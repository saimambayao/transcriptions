## What Makes This Guidebook Different

All previous guidebooks are **internal** — for people already inside BARMM government. This guidebook is **external** — for people who want to **join**. The reader may not know what "BTA" stands for.

### Primary Audience
**Fresh graduates** — people who have completed college but have not yet been employed, or are in their first job search. They have academic knowledge but zero government experience.

### Secondary Audience
**Job seekers with experience** — professionals who have worked in the private sector, NGOs, or other government agencies and want to join or transfer to BARMM government. They have work experience but may not know BARMM-specific systems.

## Part I: Understanding the Bangsamoro Government (Chapters 1-5)

Before you apply, understand what you're joining. These 5 chapters give you the foundation.

### Chapter 1 — Why the Bangsamoro Government Needs You

**Opening**: Start with the development gap and the opportunity — not history. "The Bangsamoro government has 50,467 authorized positions — and it needs more. It has the largest land area in the Philippines, the youngest population, and the widest development gaps. If you want to build something from the ground up, this is the last frontier."

**1.1 The Development Gap — In Numbers**

The gap between where BARMM is and where the BDP 2023-2028 says it should be is the reason you are needed:

| Indicator | BARMM (Current) | National Average | BDP Target (2028) | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Poverty incidence | 29.80% (2021) | ~16% | 20-25% | ~5-10 percentage points to close |
| Functional literacy rate | 71.6% | 91.6% | Near national | 20 percentage points below national |
| Dropout rate (G1-G6) | 8.36% | 3.76% | Below national | More than double the national rate |
| WASH access (safe water) | Lowest in PH | — | Universal | Ranked 17th of 17 regions |
| Maternal mortality rate | 73.83% (2020) | 70.66% | Below national | Above national average |
| GRDP contribution to national | 1.4% | — | Proportional | Lowest of all regions despite largest land area |
| Unemployment rate | 3.8% | 9.0% (2020) | 3-5% | On target — but underemployment at 9% |

- **Education**: 104 unserved/underserved barangays out of 2,590. Only 7% of 1,679 madrasahs are accredited. Teacher shortage in Grades 1-12. Completion rate (G1-G6) at 63.98% — lowest nationally.
- **Health**: Insufficient doctors, nurses, midwives. Stunting, wasting, and underweight rates highest in the Philippines. Limited psychosocial and mental health services.
- **Infrastructure**: Basic water and sanitation access ranked last nationally across all indicators.
- **Economy**: BARMM is the #1 producer of seaweed (48.42% national share) and cassava (41.70%) but contributes only 1.4% to national GDP — the lowest of any region.

- **600,000+ job applications** received through the Bangsamoro Job Portal — but many applicants lack required qualifications or eligibility
- **15 ministries** need staff across all functional areas: policy, planning, budget, admin, IT, legal, technical
- **10 OCM attached agencies** (BPDA, BBOI, BIO, BICTO, OSC, OOBC, BPA, DAB, BDI, CSEA) — many are newly created and still building their teams
- **4 commissions** (BWC, BYC, BSC, BCPCH) need specialized youth, gender, sports, and culture staff
- **116 municipalities and 2,590 barangays** under BARMM LGUs need frontline service delivery workers

**Where staff are needed most urgently:**

| Office/Ministry | Why | Example positions |
|---|---|---|
| MBHTE (Education) | Lowest literacy, dropout, and completion rates nationally | Teachers, curriculum developers, education planners |
| MOH (Health) | Maternal mortality above national, stunting highest | Nurses, midwives, health program officers, nutritionists |

**1.3 What Makes BARMM Different from Any Other Government Job**

- **Autonomous governance**: BARMM is the ONLY region in the Philippines with its own parliament, its own civil service code (BAA 17), its own administrative code (BAA 13), its own budget system (BAA 84), and its own education code (BAA 18). You are not joining a field office — you are joining a government.
- **Moral governance**: The 12 Pillars of Moral Governance (integrity, accountability, transparency, patriotism, excellence, cultural understanding, loyalty to God, justice, inclusivity, objectivity, dialogue, balance) are not aspirational values on a wall poster. They are cited in performance evaluations, referenced in legislation, and expected in daily conduct.
- **Parliamentary system**: BARMM is the only region with a parliamentary system. The Chief Minister leads the executive. The Parliament legislates AND exercises oversight. This means legislative staff and executive staff work differently than anywhere else in Philippine government.
- **The peace process**: You are not joining a regular bureaucracy. You are implementing the Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (CAB) — a peace agreement that ended decades of armed conflict. Every program you run, every policy you draft, every barangay you serve is peace dividend.
- **Cultural diversity**: 13 ethnolinguistic groups (Meranaw, Maguindanaon, Tausug, Sama, Yakan, Iranun, Kalibugan, Palawanon, Molbog, Jama Mapun, Badjao, Sangil, Teduray), plus settler communities, Other Bangsamoro Communities, and indigenous peoples. You will work with colleagues and communities whose languages, customs, and perspectives differ from yours.

**1.4 What "Founder" Means**

In a mature government, you inherit systems. In BARMM, you build them. Here is what "founder" looks like in practice:

- **The first**: You may be the first person to hold your position. Your job description may be a paragraph, not a manual. You will define the role as you fill it.
- **The systems designer**: Many offices do not yet have Manuals of Procedure (MOPs). You may be asked to create the filing system, the tracking sheet, the approval workflow, the template that everyone after you will use.
- **The standard-setter**: Your output quality becomes the benchmark. If you write excellent memos, your successors will follow your format. If you write poor ones, they will too.
- **The bridge**: You are bridging the gap between the BTA (transitional, appointed) and the elected government. The systems you build during transition become the inheritance of the first elected Bangsamoro government.
- **Real example**: When the Bangsamoro Parliament began operations in 2019, there were no committee staffing templates, no standard legislative briefer format, no standardized bill-drafting manual. Staff who joined early created these from scratch — and those templates are still in use today.

### Chapter 4 — Your Employment Framework: The Bangsamoro Civil Service Code (BAA 17)

**Opening**: "BAA 17 governs your hiring, your evaluation, your promotion, and your dismissal. Know it."

- **Types of appointments**:
  - Permanent (plantilla) — security of tenure, full benefits
  - Temporary — specified period, renewable
  - Coterminous — tied to the appointing authority's term

- **Qualification standards** — education, experience, training, eligibility requirements per position
- **The CSC and BARMM** — how the national Civil Service Commission relates to BARMM's civil service

- **Performance management** — Strategic Performance Management System (SPMS)

  - Rating period, target-setting, mid-year review, year-end evaluation
- **Rewards and recognition** — Program on Awards and Incentives for Service Excellence (PRAISE)
  - Performance-Based Bonus (PBB)
  - Step increment, loyalty award, length of service
- **Discipline** — Revised Rules on Administrative Cases in the Civil Service (RRACCS)

- **Leave benefits** — vacation, sick, maternity/paternity, solo parent, study, special
- **Government systems and compliance**:
  - SPMS (Strategic Performance Management System) — the backbone of government accountability
  - SALN — annual asset declaration (constitutional requirement)
  - OHRAORA — attendance and timekeeping

### Chapter 6 — Choosing Your Path: Parliament vs. Ministry vs. Office vs. LGU

**Opening**: "BARMM has four career tracks. Each leads somewhere different. Choose wisely."

**6.1 The Four Tracks — Overview**

| Track | What you'll do daily | Key competencies | Entry positions | Career ceiling |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Parliament** | Draft bills, write briefers, prepare speeches, support committees | Investigation, policy analysis, legal competence, CSW | Legislative Staff Officer I (SG 11) | SLSO I → Legislative Chief → Director |
| **Ministry** | Implement programs, deliver services, manage budgets, write policy | Technical expertise, project management, budget analysis | SG 6-15 (clerical SG 6-9, professional SG 11-15) | Staff → Division Chief → Director → Undersecretary |

**6.2 Day-in-the-Life: Parliament Track**

*You are a Legislative Staff Officer II (SG 13) assigned to an MP who chairs the Committee on Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform.*

**7:30 AM** — You arrive at the Parliament building in Cotabato City. You check your email and the committee's shared folder for any new referrals from the Secretary General's office. Overnight, a new bill has been referred to your committee — "An Act Establishing the Bangsamoro Fisheries Code."

**8:00 AM** — Your MP asks you to prepare a legislative briefer on the bill before Thursday's committee hearing. You open the ADDRESS IT framework (Step 1: Analyze Context). You read the bill's explanatory note, cross-reference it against BOL Article V Section 2 (enumerated powers — fisheries falls under BARMM competence), and check whether any existing BAA already covers the subject.

**9:30 AM** — You attend the weekly staff meeting with the MP's team: two PAOs (Political Affairs Officers) who handle constituency concerns, one PIMAO (Public Information and Media Affairs Officer) who manages the MP's social media, and you (the LSO handling legislative work). The MP assigns priorities for the week.

**10:30 AM** — Research phase (Step 3). You pull data from MAFAR's fisheries reports (1,010,804 metric tons total production, Tawi-Tawi at 44% share), review the national Fisheries Code (RA 8550 as amended by RA 10654), and call the committee secretary to confirm hearing logistics.

**1:00 PM** — You draft the legislative briefer: background, key provisions of the proposed bill, constitutional and BOL basis, comparison with national law, fiscal impact estimate, stakeholder positions, and recommended committee actions. This is CSW — the briefer must be so complete that your MP only needs to read it, agree or disagree, and walk into the hearing prepared.

**3:00 PM** — Your MP reviews your draft, asks you to add a section on how the bill affects small fisherfolk in Tawi-Tawi and Basilan (252,758 registered fisherfolk in BARMM — highest in the country). You revise.

**4:30 PM** — A PAO brings in a constituent complaint about delayed agrarian reform in Maguindanao (49,745 ha undistributed under CARP). You help draft a letter to MAFAR requesting status update — this is constituent service, which Parliament staff handle alongside legislation.

**5:00 PM** — You finalize the briefer, run it through the quality checklist (accuracy, completeness, formatting, citations), and submit through the transmittal chain: your desk → MP's Chief of Staff → MP.

*What this day tells you*: Parliament work is cerebral — reading, writing, analyzing, researching. You are the intellectual engine behind the MP's legislative work. You rarely interact with communities directly (that's the PAO's role). Your output is documents: briefers, committee reports, bill drafts, speeches, policy papers.

**6.3 Day-in-the-Life: Ministry Track**

*You are an Administrative Officer III (SG 14) at the Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD), assigned to the Social Protection Division.*

**7:30 AM** — You arrive at the MSSD regional office. Your first task: review the weekly disbursement report for the BANGON (sustainable livelihood) program. Three municipalities in Maguindanao have not submitted their liquidation reports — without those, the next tranche cannot be released.

**8:30 AM** — Team meeting with your Division Chief and 6 colleagues. The division is preparing for the mid-year OPCR review. Your Division Chief asks each staff member to report progress against their IPCR targets. You report: 45 of 80 BANGON beneficiaries in your assigned municipalities have received starter kits. You are behind schedule. Your Division Chief asks why and you explain: LGU coordination delays in two island barangays in Tawi-Tawi.

**10:00 AM** — You draft a memorandum to MILG requesting coordination support for the Tawi-Tawi barangays. This is a routine inter-ministry memo — To, From, Date, Subject, body explaining the situation, specific action requested, deadline. You route it through your Division Chief for clearance before it goes to the Minister's office.

**11:00 AM** — Field report comes in from a municipal social worker: a family displaced by flooding in Lanao del Sur needs BHATID (humanitarian assistance). You process the emergency assistance request — checking eligibility, preparing the voucher, getting supervisor approval.

**1:00 PM** — You participate in a video call with a DSWD national office counterpart about the Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program (4Ps) transition to MSSD. Intergovernmental coordination — BARMM handles its own social services, but some national programs are still being devolved.

**2:30 PM** — You work on a project proposal for a feeding program expansion in Basilan (stunting rates in BARMM are the highest nationally). This requires BDP alignment (Goal 4: Inclusive, Responsive, and Quality Social Services), budget justification under BAA 84, and endorsement from the provincial government.

**4:00 PM** — You update the beneficiary tracking database — 12 new entries from last week's validation exercise. Data management is unglamorous but critical — COA will audit these records.

*What this day tells you*: Ministry work is operational — you implement programs, manage budgets, coordinate with LGUs, process paperwork, and track results. Your output is service delivery. You see the direct impact of your work on communities, but you also spend significant time on compliance, reporting, and coordination.

**6.4 Day-in-the-Life: Office/Agency Track**

*You are a Development Management Officer II (SG 13) at the Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA).*

**8:00 AM** — You review the latest regional poverty statistics from PSA. BARMM poverty incidence dropped from 54.20% (2018) to 29.80% (2021) — a 24.4 percentage point reduction. But you need to verify whether this trend is holding for the latest data. You draft a data request to PSA-BARMM.

**9:00 AM** — Your section chief assigns you to support the BDP mid-term review. You are responsible for collecting progress reports from 4 ministries (MBHTE, MOH, MSSD, MIPA) against BDP Goal 4 targets. This means preparing data collection templates, scheduling interviews with ministry focal persons, and consolidating findings.

**10:30 AM** — A development partner (UNDP) requests a meeting to discuss alignment of their governance support program with BDP priorities. You prepare talking points for your Executive Director, pulling relevant BDP targets and BARMM performance data.

**1:00 PM** — You attend an inter-agency planning workshop for the Annual Investment Program (AIP). Representatives from 8 ministries present their priority projects. Your role: ensure each proposed project aligns with BDP goals and the 12-point priority agenda. You flag a PHP 200M infrastructure proposal that lacks BDP alignment documentation.

**3:00 PM** — You work on a policy brief analyzing BARMM's macroeconomic performance against BDP targets: GRDP growth at 7.5% (target: 8-9%), capital formation at 16.91% (target: 18-20%). You recommend specific interventions.

*What this day tells you*: Office/agency work is strategic — you are planning, analyzing, coordinating, and monitoring. You interact with many ministries but belong to none. Your output is plans, frameworks, assessments, and policy recommendations. You see the big picture but may feel distant from frontline implementation.

**6.5 Day-in-the-Life: LGU Track**

*You are a Municipal Planning and Development Coordinator designate (SG 11) in a 4th-class municipality in Lanao del Sur with a population of 18,000.*

**7:00 AM** — You arrive at the municipal hall. The mayor asks you to prepare the Comprehensive Development Plan (CDP) update — a BAA 49 (Bangsamoro Local Governance Code) requirement. The municipality's current CDP expired last year and has not been updated.

**8:00 AM** — You attend a barangay assembly in one of 15 barangays. Community members raise concerns: the road to the school is impassable during rain, the barangay health station has no midwife, and the water system broke 3 months ago. You take notes — these feed into the CDP.

**10:00 AM** — Back at the municipal hall, you process a request from MILG for the municipality's Statement of Receipts and Expenditures (SRE). The municipal accountant needs your help formatting the report correctly.

**11:00 AM** — A provincial government representative arrives to discuss the municipality's compliance with the 7 Elements of LGU Functionality (assessed by MILG). Your municipality is non-compliant on Element 5 (approved mandated plans) because the CDP is expired. This is your problem to solve.

**1:00 PM** — You draft a project proposal for a Level II water system for 3 barangays, to be submitted to MPW for funding. You need engineering specs (which you do not have — you will request from MPW's provincial office), a site survey (which requires travel to remote barangays), and a barangay council resolution of endorsement (which requires coordinating with 3 barangay captains).

**3:00 PM** — A walk-in citizen asks about a land title issue. This is not your job — it belongs to the Municipal Assessor — but you help direct the citizen because you are the most accessible staff member in the building.

*What this day tells you*: LGU work is frontline — you are closest to the people. You do everything: planning, compliance, coordination, community engagement, even tasks outside your job description. Resources are thin, staff are few, and you are often the most educated person in the building. The work is grounding but demanding.

**6.7 Decision Flowchart — Which Track Is Right for You?**

Answer these questions in order:

1. **Do you enjoy reading, writing, and research more than fieldwork and community engagement?**
   - Yes → Parliament or Office/Agency
   - No → Ministry or LGU

2. **Do you want to write laws and policies, or implement programs and services?**
   - Write → Parliament
   - Implement → Ministry
   - Plan and coordinate → Office/Agency
   - Deliver directly to people → LGU

3. **Are you comfortable working in Cotabato City (the seat of government), or do you want to work in a province or municipality?**
   - Cotabato City → Parliament, Ministry HQ, Office/Agency
   - Province → Ministry field office, LGU
   - Municipality/Barangay → LGU

4. **Do you have a specialized degree (law, engineering, agriculture, health, education, finance)?**
   - Yes → Ministry matching your specialization, or Parliament (for law graduates)
   - No (general degree: public admin, polisci, management) → Parliament, Office/Agency, or any Ministry admin track

5. **Do you want your career ceiling to be Director/Undersecretary level, or are you OK with Department Head level?**
   - Director+ → Parliament, Ministry, or Office/Agency (SG 25-28 positions exist)
   - Department Head is fine → LGU (ceiling is typically SG 22, but you can transfer to ministry later)

6. **How do you feel about working in a small team with limited resources?**
   - I prefer larger organizations with more structure → Ministry or Parliament
   - I thrive in lean environments where I wear many hats → Office/Agency or LGU

**6.9 Switching Tracks**

Yes, you can switch. BARMM government employees move between tracks through:

- **Transfer**: Moving from one MOA to another. Requires a vacancy in the receiving office, meeting the QS, and release from the current office. Timeline: 1-3 months. You keep your salary grade.
- **Detailing**: Temporary assignment to another office (30-90 days, extendable). Good for exploring a different track before committing. No change in salary or appointment status.
- **Secondment**: Extended assignment to another government entity (6 months to 1 year). Common for inter-agency projects. You remain employed by your original office.
- **Reapplication**: Resigning from one position and applying to another through the regular competitive process. Most permanent — but you lose seniority.
- **Promotion to a different track**: If a higher-SG position opens in a different track and you meet the QS, you can apply through the regular HRMPSB process. Common path: LGU staff → Ministry, or Office/Agency staff → Parliament.

**Common switching patterns**:
- LGU to Ministry: After 2-3 years of frontline experience, apply to the ministry that supervises your LGU sector (e.g., Municipal Health Officer → MOH regional office)
- Ministry to Parliament: After building technical expertise, move to a committee that covers your sector (e.g., MBHTE staff → Committee on Education staff)
- Office/Agency to Ministry: After developing planning skills, move to a ministry's planning division
- Parliament to Office/Agency: After building policy analysis skills, move to BPDA or a commission

**6.10 The Generalist vs. Specialist Question**

| Path | Generalist | Specialist |
|---|---|---|
| **What it means** | Broad skills across multiple functions (admin, budget, planning, coordination) | Deep expertise in one domain (health policy, agricultural economics, legislative drafting, environmental law) |
| **Best tracks** | LGU (you do everything), Office/Agency | Parliament (legislative drafting), Ministry (technical division) |
| **Career advantage** | Flexibility — you can fill any role | Depth — you become the go-to expert and are harder to replace |
| **Career risk** | You may lack the depth required for senior technical positions | You may be pigeonholed and miss broader leadership opportunities |
| **Fresh graduate advice** | Start generalist if you don't know your specialization yet | Start specialist if your degree is technical (law, engineering, health, agriculture) |

### Chapter 8 — The Application Process

**Opening**: "Getting hired in BARMM government is a structured process with specific steps and scoring criteria. No guessing. Here's exactly what happens."

**8.1 The Application Process — Step by Step**:

```
Step 1: Find the vacancy posting (Ch 7)
Step 2: Check if you meet the Qualification Standards (QS)
Step 3: Prepare your application package
Step 4: Submit before the deadline
Step 5: Wait for the HRMPSB to screen your application
Step 6: Receive notice of interview/exam (if shortlisted)
Step 7: Attend interview and assessment (Ch 9)
Step 8: Wait for the Comparative Assessment results
Step 9: Receive appointment or regret letter
```

**8.2 Understanding Qualification Standards (QS)**:
- Every position has 4 requirements: Education, Experience, Training, Eligibility
- Where to find QS: CSC Qualification Standards Manual, vacancy posting, or ask the HR office
- **For fresh graduates**: Most entry-level positions (SG 11-15) require a bachelor's degree + CSC Professional/Sub-Professional eligibility but minimal experience and training. This is your sweet spot.
- **CSC eligibility**: How to get it — Career Service Professional (CSP) exam, Career Service Sub-Professional (CSSP) exam, or RA 1080 board exam passers (lawyers, CPAs, engineers, etc. are automatically eligible)
- **The Bar/Board passer advantage** — RA 1080 automatic eligibility and how it affects your competitiveness

**8.3 Your Application Package** (complete checklist):
1. **Application letter / Cover letter** — addressed to the head of office or HRMPSB Chair
   - What to include: position applied for, why you want to serve in BARMM (connect to BDP), your relevant qualifications
   - What NOT to include: salary expectations, personal problems, desperation
   - **For fresh graduates**: Lead with your thesis, capstone, or academic achievements — not your lack of experience
2. **Personal Data Sheet (CS Form 212)** — the government's standard CV format
   - Revised 2017 version (4 pages)
   - **Critical fields fresh graduates miss**: Work Experience section (leave blank if none — don't fabricate), Training section (include seminars, workshops, even university-sponsored ones), Voluntary Work (THIS is where fresh graduates shine — OJT, volunteer work, student org activities)
   - **Common PDS mistakes**: unsigned pages, missing passport photo, inconsistent dates, blank Civil Service Eligibility section when you have RA 1080
3. **Work Experience Sheet (CS Form 212 Attachment)** — only if you have prior employment
4. **Transcript of Records (TOR)** — certified true copy from your university
5. **Diploma** — certified true copy
6. **CSC Eligibility proof** — Certificate of Eligibility, PRC Board Certificate, or Bar Certificate
7. **Training certificates** — any relevant seminars, workshops, online courses (include Coursera, edX if applicable)
8. **NBI Clearance** — current, not expired
9. **Barangay Clearance** — from your place of residence
10. **Community Tax Certificate (Cedula)** — current year
11. **2x2 and passport-size photos** — white background, recent
12. **Birth Certificate (PSA-authenticated)** — for appointment processing

**8.4 The Comparative Assessment — How You're Scored**:

The Human Resource Merit Promotion and Selection Board (HRMPSB) scores applicants on 5 criteria:

| Criteria | Weight | What's evaluated | Fresh graduate impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Education** | 25% | Degree relevance, honors, graduate units | Your strongest area — maximize it |
| **Experience** | 25% | Years and relevance of work experience | Your weakest area — COS/JO/volunteer work counts |
| **Training** | 15% | Relevant seminars, workshops, certifications | Build this NOW — attend free government webinars |
| **Eligibility** | 10% | Type and level of CS eligibility | Pass the CSP exam or use RA 1080 |
| **Interview** | 25% | CBI performance, fit assessment | Prepare thoroughly — this is where you recover points (Ch 9) |

- **The math for fresh graduates**: You'll likely score high on Education (25%) and Eligibility (10%), low on Experience (25%). That means Interview (25%) and Training (15%) are your swing factors. Invest heavily in interview preparation and accumulate training hours.
- **How to build experience without a job**: OJT, volunteer work in LGUs, mosque/church community programs, CSO project assistance — all count as "relevant experience" on your PDS if documented properly

**8.5 Posting and Timeline**:
- Vacancy posted for minimum 10 calendar days (CSC rule)
- Screening by HRMPSB: 2-4 weeks after posting closes
- Interview scheduling: 1-2 weeks after screening
- Results and appointment: 2-8 weeks after interview (varies by office)
- **Total timeline**: Expect 2-4 months from posting to appointment
- **Important**: You can follow up with HR after 30 days. A polite inquiry is professional, not pushy.

**8.6 Common Application Mistakes** (and how to avoid them):
1. Submitting after the deadline (government is strict — even 1 day late = disqualified)
2. Incomplete PDS (unsigned, missing pages, blank required fields)
3. Not including a cover letter (some offices require it, some don't — always include one)
4. Applying for positions above your qualification standard (SG 24 requires 4 years experience — don't apply with zero)
5. Sending generic applications to every office (tailor your cover letter to each office's mandate from BAA 13)
6. Not keeping copies of everything you submitted (you'll need these if asked for verification)

### Chapter 9 — The Interview and Assessment

**Opening**: "The interview is 25% of your score. For a fresh graduate with limited experience, it might be the difference between getting hired and getting a regret letter. Prepare like it matters — because it does."

**9.1 What to Expect: The Interview Format**
- **Panel interview**: Typically 3-5 members of the HRMPSB (HR head, division chief, subject matter expert, employee representative)
- **Duration**: 20-45 minutes depending on position level
- **Format**: Competency-Based Interview (CBI) — structured questions designed to assess specific competencies from the position description
- **Scoring**: Each panelist scores independently using a standardized rubric
- **Recording**: Some offices record interviews; you may be asked to sign a consent form

**9.2 Competency-Based Interviews (CBI) — The STAR Method**:
Every CBI question asks you to describe a PAST situation where you demonstrated a competency. Answer using STAR:

| Element | What it is | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **S**ituation | Set the scene — where, when, what was happening | "During my thesis research on agricultural cooperatives in Maguindanao..." |
| **T**ask | What was your specific role or responsibility | "I was responsible for conducting interviews with 30 cooperative leaders across 5 municipalities..." |
| **A**ction | What YOU specifically did (not your team) | "I designed a bilingual questionnaire in Filipino and Maguindanaon, coordinated with barangay captains for introductions, and..." |
| **R**esult | What happened — quantify if possible | "We completed all 30 interviews in 3 weeks, the thesis earned a grade of 1.25, and the cooperative federation adopted two of our recommendations." |

**For fresh graduates**: Your "situations" come from:
- Thesis/capstone research
- Student organization leadership (president, treasurer, event organizer)
- Volunteer work and community service
- Part-time jobs, OJT, or internships
- Family responsibilities (managing a household IS project management)
- Personal challenges overcome (resilience, problem-solving)

**9.3 Common Interview Questions for BARMM Positions**:

**Governance Awareness** (expect these in every BARMM interview):
- "What do you know about the Bangsamoro Organic Law?"
- "Can you name the 6 goals of the Bangsamoro Development Plan?"
- "What is the mandate of [this office] under BAA 13?"
- "What does moral governance mean to you?"
- "How does BARMM differ from ARMM? Why did ARMM fail?"

**Competency Questions** (STAR format):
- "Tell me about a time you had to analyze a complex problem and propose a solution." (Critical thinking)
- "Describe a situation where you worked with people from different backgrounds." (Cultural competence)
- "Give an example of when you had to meet a tight deadline with limited resources." (Results orientation)
- "Tell me about a time you received negative feedback. How did you respond?" (Openness to feedback)
- "Describe a project you led from start to finish." (Initiative, project management)

**BARMM-Specific Questions** (unique to this region):
- "Why do you want to work in BARMM government specifically?" (Motivation — connect to peace process, development, personal stake)
- "How would you handle working with colleagues from different ethnolinguistic groups?" (Cultural competence)
- "What role does Islamic governance play in public service?" (Moral governance awareness — answer respectfully regardless of faith)
- "What contribution can you make to the Bangsamoro transition?" (Vision, commitment)

**9.4 For Fresh Graduates: Compensating for Limited Experience**:
- **Lead with preparation**: "I may not have government experience, but I've studied your office's mandate under BAA 13, read the relevant BDP chapter, and prepared a draft [output relevant to the position] to show what I can contribute."
- **Reframe academic work**: Your thesis IS research. Your org presidency IS leadership. Your volunteer work IS stakeholder engagement.
- **Show learning speed**: "In my OJT at [organization], I was given [task] with no training. Within [timeframe], I [result]."
- **Demonstrate BOL/BDP knowledge**: Most applicants — even experienced ones — haven't read the BOL or BDP. You will have (if you followed Chapters 2 and 5).
- **Bring a portfolio**: A folder with your best academic output (thesis abstract, research paper, event documentation, organizational report). This is unusual and impressive.

**9.5 Written Exams and Skills Tests**:
- Some positions require written exams (analytical writing, policy analysis, technical knowledge)
- Format varies by office — essay, multiple choice, or practical exercise
- For legislative positions: you may be asked to draft a sample briefer or memo
- For technical positions: subject matter test (health, agriculture, education, finance)
- **How to prepare**: Review the office's recent outputs, read their published reports, study the relevant BAA

**9.6 Cultural Appropriateness**:
- **Dress code**: Business formal. For men: long-sleeved polo or barong, slacks, closed shoes. For women: modest business attire (consider that many offices observe Islamic dress norms — avoid sleeveless, wear knee-length or longer)
- **Greetings**: "Assalamu Alaikum" is the standard greeting in BARMM offices (appropriate regardless of your faith — it means "peace be upon you"). If unsure, "Good morning/afternoon" is always acceptable.
- **Addressing panelists**: "Sir/Ma'am" or by title ("Director," "Attorney," "Engineer"). Use "Honorable" for Members of Parliament.
- **Demeanor**: Respectful confidence. Look panelists in the eye when speaking. Don't interrupt. Say "Thank you for the question" before answering — it buys you thinking time.
- **Punctuality**: Arrive 30 minutes early. Being late is disqualifying in most offices.
- **Phone**: Silent mode, not vibrate. Do not check it during the interview.

**9.7 After the Interview — What Happens Next**:
1. **HRMPSB deliberation** — the panel consolidates scores, ranks candidates
2. **Shortlist submission** — top 3-5 candidates forwarded to the appointing authority (Minister, Secretary General, Director)
3. **Appointing authority decision** — final selection (may or may not follow the ranking)
4. **Appointment letter** — issued if selected. Specifies: position, salary grade, effectivity date, type of appointment
5. **Oath of office** — administered before you start (usually by the appointing authority or authorized officer)
6. **Timeline**: 2-8 weeks from interview to appointment. Some offices take longer.
7. **If not selected**: You will receive a regret letter. You may request feedback from HRMPSB (professional development, not complaint). Apply again — most successful government workers weren't hired on their first attempt.

**9.8 The Appointment Types You May Receive**:

| Type | What it means | Security | Benefits |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Permanent** | Plantilla position, met all QS | Full tenure | Full (GSIS, PhilHealth, Pag-IBIG, leave, PBB) |
| **Temporary** | Met QS except eligibility — given 2 years to pass CSC exam | Limited tenure | Full while appointed |
| **Coterminous** | Tied to appointing authority's term | Ends when they leave | Full while appointed |
| **COS** | Contract of Service — project-based | None | Limited (no GSIS/leave) |
| **JO** | Job Order — task-based | None | None |

- Section 1: "My STAR Stories" — 5 blank STAR templates to prepare before the interview (one per competency: thinking, communication, collaboration, values, Bangsamoro-specific)
- Section 2: "Office Research Checklist" — mandate (BAA 13), recent outputs, key leadership, relevant BDP goal, current programs

**11.2 Communication Skills**:

  - Formal presentations (committee hearings, budget defense, program review)

  - Public engagement (community consultations, press interactions)
  - Multilingual context: Filipino, English, Arabic, regional languages (Meranaw, Maguindanaon, Tausug, etc.)
- **How fresh graduates demonstrate communication**: Class presentations, thesis defense, student journalism, debate, social media management, tutoring

**11.3 Collaboration and Teamwork Skills**:
- **Working in teams** — Government is never solo work. Every output involves multiple people, offices, and stakeholders.
- **Cross-functional coordination** — Your office coordinates with other MOAs, Parliament committees, LGUs, national agencies, development partners.
- **Stakeholder engagement** — Consulting communities, CSOs, private sector, academe. Especially important in BARMM: engaging across 13 ethnolinguistic groups with different languages and customs.
- **Conflict resolution** — Disagreements happen. How to navigate them professionally and culturally.
- **Shura (consultation)** — The Islamic principle of collective decision-making. In BARMM, this isn't just a value — it's a governance practice.
- **How fresh graduates demonstrate collaboration**: Group projects, student organizations, volunteer work, community service, sports teams, family responsibilities

**11.4 Values-Based Competencies** (from CSC + BARMM moral governance):
- **Integrity and Ethics** — Honesty, transparency, anti-corruption. In BARMM: amanah (trustworthiness) and the 12 moral governance pillars.
- **Professionalism and Service Orientation** — Putting the public first. In BARMM: serving the Bangsamoro people with dedication, especially marginalized communities.
- **Results Orientation** — Delivering outputs on time and to standard. Connected to BDP targets and SPMS ratings.
- **Accountability** — Taking responsibility for your work. In BARMM: adl (justice) and transparency in governance.

**11.5 The Bangsamoro Dimension** — competencies NOT in the CSC Dictionary:
- **BOL Literacy** — Understanding the law that created BARMM (you work for an autonomous government — know what that means)

- **Moral Governance Practice** — Living the 12 pillars, not just reciting them
- **Peace Process Awareness** — Understanding that your work implements the Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro

**11.6 Functional Competencies by Track**:

### Chapter 13 — Government Communication: Writing That Gets Read

**Opening**: "In government, writing IS working. If you can't write a proper memo, you can't do the job. Your supervisor will judge your competence primarily through your written output. A staff member who writes well is trusted with bigger assignments. A staff member who writes poorly will spend their career doing data entry."

**13.1 The Memorandum — The Workhorse of Government**

The memorandum (memo) is the most common document you will produce. Every request, report, recommendation, and notification moves through memos.

**Standard memo structure (annotated):**

```
REPUBLIC OF THE PHILIPPINES
BANGSAMORO AUTONOMOUS REGION IN MUSLIM MINDANAO
[MINISTRY/OFFICE NAME]
[Address]

MEMORANDUM

TO      : [Full name and title of recipient — e.g., "HON. RAISSA H. JAJURIE, Minister, MSSD"]
FROM    : [Full name and title of sender — e.g., "AHMAD M. SANTOS, Administrative Officer III"]
DATE    : [Date of memo — e.g., "26 March 2026"]
SUBJECT : [Clear, specific subject — NOT "Request" but "Request for Approval of BANGON
           Beneficiary List for Municipality of Paglat, Maguindanao del Sur"]

---

[Body — organized in numbered paragraphs]

1. [Purpose paragraph: Why you are writing. State the action needed upfront.
   Example: "This is to request your approval of the attached BANGON beneficiary
   list for the Municipality of Paglat, Maguindanao del Sur, covering 45 qualified
   beneficiaries for FY 2026 Batch 1."]

2. [Background paragraph: Context the reader needs.
   Example: "Per MSSD Memorandum Order No. 2026-003, all BANGON beneficiary lists
   must be validated by the Municipal Social Welfare Office, endorsed by the Municipal
   Mayor, and approved by the Minister before disbursement."]

3. [Details paragraph: Specifics — data, findings, attachments referenced.
   Example: "The attached list has been validated against the following criteria:
   (a) residency in conflict-affected barangay, (b) income below poverty threshold,
   (c) no duplication with other livelihood programs. Supporting documents are attached
   as Annexes A-C."]

4. [Action paragraph: What you need the reader to do, by when.
   Example: "In view of the above, approval of the attached beneficiary list is
   respectfully requested to enable disbursement before the end of Q1 FY 2026."]

[Signature block]
[Name]
[Position]
```

**Key rules for fresh graduates:**
- The SUBJECT line is the most important line. Your supervisor may receive 20+ memos per day. If the subject is vague, it gets buried.
- One memo = one subject. Do not combine unrelated requests.
- Number your paragraphs. Government readers scan — numbered paragraphs let them reference specific points ("See paragraph 3 of your memo dated...").
- State the action requested in BOTH the first paragraph AND the last paragraph. Decision-makers often read only the beginning and end.
- Attach supporting documents rather than embedding lengthy data in the body.

**13.2 The Transmittal Memorandum**

A transmittal memo accompanies a CSW output (briefer, policy recommendation, project proposal, report) as it moves from your desk to the decision-maker. It is NOT the CSW itself — it is the cover page that says "here is the work, here is what I need you to do with it."

**Transmittal memo structure (annotated):**

```
MEMORANDUM

TO      : [Decision-maker]
FROM    : [You]
DATE    : [Date]
SUBJECT : Transmittal of [Name of Document] re: [Topic]

---

1. Transmitted herewith is the [Legislative Briefer / Policy Recommendation / Project
   Proposal / Budget Analysis] on [topic], prepared pursuant to [your assignment source —
   e.g., "your directive during the March 15, 2026 staff meeting" or "Committee Resolution
   No. 2026-001"].

2. [1-2 sentence summary of the CSW output's key finding or recommendation.
   Example: "The briefer recommends approval of the proposed Bangsamoro Fisheries Code
   with three amendments to align Section 12 with BOL Article V, Section 2, Item 14
   (fisheries competence)."]

3. [Mention attachments and how they are organized.
   Example: "The following documents are attached: (a) Legislative Briefer (10 pages),
   (b) Bill Comparison Matrix — Proposed vs. National Fisheries Code, (c) Stakeholder
   Position Summary."]

4. For your [approval / information / appropriate action].

[Signature block]
```

**When to use a transmittal memo vs. a regular memo:**
- Regular memo = standalone request, report, or notification
- Transmittal memo = you are forwarding a substantive document that needs action
- Rule of thumb: if your memo has attachments that are more important than the memo itself, it is a transmittal

**13.3 The Endorsement Chain**

Endorsements are how documents move between offices. When Office A sends a document to Office B for action, and Office B forwards it to Office C, each forwarding is an "endorsement."

**How it works:**

```
ORIGINAL MEMO: Municipality of Paglat → MILG (requesting technical assistance)

1st ENDORSEMENT: MILG → BPDA
"Respectfully endorsed to the Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority for
appropriate action. The Municipality of Paglat requests technical assistance in
preparing its Comprehensive Development Plan update."
[Signed by MILG Director]

2nd ENDORSEMENT: BPDA → Planning Division
"Referred to the Planning Division for evaluation and recommendation."
[Signed by BPDA Executive Director]

3rd ENDORSEMENT: Planning Division → back to BPDA Executive Director
"Respectfully returned with the recommendation that BPDA deploy a planning
team to Paglat municipality for a 5-day CDP workshop..."
[Signed by Division Chief]
```

**Key endorsement phrases and what they mean:**
| Phrase | Meaning | Who uses it |
|---|---|---|
| "Respectfully endorsed for appropriate action" | "Please handle this" — you have authority to decide | Superior forwarding to subordinate office |
| "Respectfully endorsed for information" | "FYI only — no action needed from you" | Lateral or upward endorsement |
| "Respectfully endorsed for approval" | "I've reviewed this and I'm recommending approval — please sign" | Subordinate forwarding to superior |
| "Respectfully endorsed for comment" | "Please review and give your input" | Any direction |
| "Respectfully returned with recommendation" | "I've done my review — here are my findings" | Subordinate returning to superior |

**Fresh graduate tip:** When you receive a document with endorsements, READ ALL THE ENDORSEMENTS. They tell you the document's journey — who sent it, who reviewed it, what they said, and what is expected of you.

**Email rules in government:**
- **CC**: Copy those who need to know but do not need to act. Always CC your immediate supervisor on substantive emails.
- **BCC**: Use sparingly. Appropriate only when sending to a large distribution list where recipients don't need to see each other's addresses.
- **"Noted"**: Means "I have read and acknowledged" — it does NOT mean "approved."
- **"Approved"**: Means the decision-maker agrees — this is an official action.
- **"For compliance"**: Means you MUST do what the email says — it is a directive, not a suggestion.
- **Response time**: Acknowledge within 24 hours (even if you cannot fully respond yet). Substantive response within 3-5 working days unless urgency is specified.
- **Never**: Use informal language (text speak, emojis, all caps). Remember that government emails can be FOI'd (Freedom of Information) and subpoenaed.

**13.7 "For Information" vs. "For Appropriate Action" vs. "For Approval"**

These three phrases control what happens to your document. Using the wrong one creates confusion:

| Action Phrase | What it means | When to use | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **"For information"** | Read and note — no response or action needed | Sharing data, reports, or updates that the recipient should know about but does not need to act on | "The attached FY 2025 Year-End Report is forwarded for your information." |
| **"For appropriate action"** | The recipient should decide what to do and act on it — YOU are not prescribing the action | Routing a complaint, referral, or request to the office responsible for handling it | "The attached constituent complaint is endorsed for your appropriate action." |
| **"For approval"** | You have done the work, you are recommending an action, and the decision-maker needs to sign off | Submitting a completed CSW (briefer, proposal, budget, beneficiary list) to a superior | "The attached project proposal is submitted for your approval." |
| **"For comment"** | You want the recipient's input before finalizing | Circulating a draft document for review | "The attached draft Administrative Order is forwarded for your comment." |
| **"For compliance"** | This is a directive — the recipient must do what is stated | Issuing instructions, citing regulations, or following up on unmet requirements | "Per CSC MC No. 14, s. 2018, compliance with posting requirements is directed." |

**13.8 Government Writing Voice — Rules for Fresh Graduates**

Government writing is NOT academic writing. Here are the differences:

| Academic writing | Government writing |
|---|---|
| Long sentences, complex syntax | Short sentences, simple structure |
| Passive voice ("It was determined that...") | Active voice ("The committee determined...") |
| Hedging ("It appears that there may be...") | Direct ("There is a shortage of...") |
| Citations in footnotes | Citations inline or in attachments |
| Builds to a conclusion at the end | States the conclusion FIRST, then supports it |
| Demonstrates the writer's knowledge | Enables the reader's decision |

**Common government writing mistakes fresh graduates make:**

| Mistake | Example | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Using "the undersigned" | "The undersigned respectfully requests..." | "I respectfully request..." (CSC has explicitly discouraged "the undersigned") |
| "Respectfully submitted" vs. "Respectfully transmitted" | Using them interchangeably | "Submitted" = original work you created. "Transmitted" = forwarding someone else's document |
| "Thru" | "Thru the Secretary General" | "Through the Secretary General" (thru is informal) |
| Unnecessary preambles | "In connection with the above-captioned subject matter and pursuant to..." | State the purpose directly in the first sentence |
| Acronyms without definition | "The HRMPSB reviewed the QS per CSC MC 14" | Spell out on first use: "The Human Resource Merit Promotion and Selection Board (HRMPSB)..." |
| Run-on paragraphs | One paragraph covering 3 different topics | One paragraph per point. Number them. |

### Chapter 15 — Career Growth and Development

**Opening**: "Government is one of the few employers in the Philippines where your promotion path is legally defined, your salary increases are guaranteed by law, and your training is a right — not a privilege. But you have to know the system to use it."

**15.1 How Promotion Works — The Mechanics**

Promotion in government is NOT automatic. It requires a vacancy, meeting the Qualification Standards (QS), and competing through the HRMPSB process (same as hiring — see Chapter 8). Here is how it works:

**The promotion process (step by step):**

1. **A vacancy opens** — either someone resigns, retires, gets promoted, or a new position is created through the plantilla
2. **The vacancy is posted** — minimum 10 calendar days (CSC rule), posted internally AND externally
3. **"Next-in-rank" principle** — employees one level below the vacant position get priority consideration. But "next-in-rank" is a preference, not a guarantee. The HRMPSB evaluates ALL qualified applicants.
4. **Comparative Assessment** — same 5 criteria as hiring: Education (25%), Experience (25%), Training (15%), Eligibility (10%), Interview (25%)
5. **HRMPSB recommendation** — top 3-5 candidates submitted to the appointing authority
6. **Appointment** — the appointing authority (Minister, Secretary General, Director) selects from the shortlist

**Realistic promotion timelines:**

| From → To | Typical timeline | What you need | Key blockers |
|---|---|---|---|
| SG 11 → SG 13 | 2-3 years | SPMS rating of VS or higher, basic training hours | No vacancy in your office |
| SG 13 → SG 15 | 2-3 years | Accumulated experience, intermediate training | CSC eligibility (if you don't have it yet) |
| SG 15 → SG 18 | 3-5 years | Specialized training, demonstrated CSW proficiency | Competition from lateral entrants |
| SG 18 → SG 22 | 4-6 years | Supervisory training, leadership competencies | Fewer positions — pyramid narrows |
| SG 22 → SG 24 | 3-5 years | Division Chief readiness, management experience | Very competitive — may require transfer |
| SG 24 → SG 27 | 5-10 years | Director-level competencies, strategic track record | Often requires transfer to a different MOA |

**Important reality check**: Promotion speed depends heavily on (a) whether vacancies exist in your office and (b) whether you have competitors. In a small office with 10 staff and no turnover, you might wait 5 years for a promotion. In a rapidly growing ministry with many new positions, you might get promoted in 18 months. Choose your office strategically (see Chapter 6).

Your total monthly take-home (after deductions for GSIS, PhilHealth, Pag-IBIG) will be approximately PHP 25,000-28,000. This is lower than many private sector entry positions — but government provides job security, comprehensive benefits, and a pension (GSIS) that private sector does not match.

**15.3 The Three Competency Frameworks You'll Encounter**

Your career development is guided by competency frameworks — structured descriptions of the knowledge, skills, and attitudes required at each level. BARMM uses three interconnected frameworks:

**Framework 1: Parliament Staff Competencies (25 competencies)**
- **Who it applies to**: All staff working in an MP's office or the Parliament Secretariat
- **Structure**: 3 tiers — Core (5 competencies for all staff), Leadership (5 for supervisors), Functional (15: 5 per role type — PAO, LSO, PIMAO)
- **Core competencies**: Critical/Creative/Strategic Thinking, Effective Communication, Synergistic Collaboration, Complete Staff Work, Public Service and Cultural Competence
- **Assessment scale**: 4 levels — Needs Improvement, Developing, Proficient, Exemplary
- **Your target as entry staff**: Reach "Developing" in all Core competencies within Year 1, "Proficient" by Year 3

**Framework 2: ACSCCRaPATFEE (12 competencies)**
- **Who it applies to**: Any BARMM government worker who produces Complete Staff Work — which is everyone
- **The 12 competencies**: Analytical & Critical Thinking, Creativity & Innovation, Strategic Thinking, Communication, Collaboration & Teamwork, Research & Analysis, Problem-Solving & Decision Making, Attention to Details & Quality, Time & Attention Management, Flexibility & Adaptability, Emotional Intelligence & Accountability, Ethics & Integrity
- **Assessment scale**: 5 levels — Limited, Developing, Proficient, Advanced, Expert
- **Your target as entry staff**: Reach "Developing" across all 12 within Year 1, "Proficient" in at least 6 by Year 3
- **How it connects to CSW**: Each competency maps to specific ADDRESS IT steps. For example, Analytical Thinking maps to Step 1 (Analyze Context) and Step 3 (Research).

**Framework 3: 12 Supervisory Competencies**
- **Who it applies to**: Supervisors and division chiefs — you will encounter this when you reach SG 18-22
- **The 12 competencies**: Foundations of Supervision, Assessing Performance/Potential/Readiness, Giving Effective Feedback, Coaching for Performance, Setting Goals & Expectations, Conducting Effective Check-Ins, Managing Underperformance, Developing Talent, Leading Teams, Managing Change, Delegation & Empowerment, Building Accountability Culture
- **When you need it**: When you are promoted to a position with direct reports (typically SG 18+)

**15.7 The Path from Staff to Supervisor to Director**

| Level | SG Range | Role | Competency requirements | Key transition |

- **Staff → Senior Staff**: "I used to ask for permission. Now people ask me for guidance."

### Chapter 16 — The Bangsamoro Public Servant

**Opening**: "This chapter is about who you become, not what you do. Government work will test your values — not your skills. You will be offered shortcuts, pressured to bend rules, and tempted to treat public resources as personal benefits. This chapter gives you the legal framework, the ethical compass, and the practical knowledge to stay clean."

**16.1 The 12 Pillars of Moral Governance — In Daily Practice**

The 12 Pillars are not abstract values. They are governance principles cited in BARMM policies, performance evaluations, and legislative proceedings. Here is what each one looks like in your daily work:

| Pillar | Principle | What it looks like in practice |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Integrity** | Avoid conflicts of interest | You do not process your cousin's application. You do not approve your friend's voucher. You declare relationships that could influence your decisions. |
| 2. **Accountability** | Open about decisions and actions | When your Division Chief asks why a project is delayed, you give the real reason — not an excuse. You own your mistakes. |
| 3. **Transparency** | Declare interests, resolve conflicts | If your family owns property affected by a government project, you declare it in writing and recuse yourself from related decisions. |
| 4. **Patriotism** | Invest in the Bangsamoro's reputation | You speak positively about BARMM to outsiders. You correct misinformation about the Bangsamoro. You take pride in your region's achievements. |
| 5. **Excellence** | Deliver highest quality | Your CSW is decision-ready. Your data is accurate. Your memo has no typos. You do not submit "good enough" work. |
| 6. **Cultural Understanding** | Respect diversity within BARMM | You learn basic greetings in your colleagues' languages. You do not assume all Bangsamoro are the same. You respect Teduray customs as much as Meranaw customs. |
| 7. **Loyalty to God** | Follow rules as devotion | You view your government service as amanah (trust) — a responsibility you will be accountable for, not just to your supervisor but to God. |
| 8. **Justice** | Serve without prejudice | The beneficiary list includes ALL qualified families — not just those from your clan or barangay. You do not play favorites. |
| 9. **Inclusivity** | No discrimination | You advocate for persons with disabilities in program design. You ensure women are represented in consultations. You include OBCs and IPs in service delivery. |
| 10. **Objectivity** | Merit-based decisions | You score job applicants on their qualifications — not on who recommended them. You evaluate proposals on their merit — not on the proponent's connections. |
| 11. **Dialogue** | Seek community input | Before finalizing the barangay project list, you consult the community. You listen to dissenting views. You do not railroad decisions. |
| 12. **Balance** | Practical and moral values | You pursue efficiency but not at the cost of fairness. You meet deadlines but not by cutting ethical corners. |

**16.2 Islamic Leadership Principles — For All Staff**

BARMM governance is rooted in Islamic values that apply to all public servants, regardless of personal faith:

| Arabic Term | English | In government practice |
|---|---|---|
| **Amanah** | Trustworthiness | You are entrusted with public resources, public information, and public authority. Amanah means treating these as sacred trusts, not personal assets. Every peso you manage, every document you handle, every decision you influence is amanah. |
| **Adl** | Justice | Serving all people fairly — across clans, tribes, religions, and political affiliations. A Tausug applicant and a Meranaw applicant receive the same standard of service. |
| **Shura** | Consultation | Deciding together rather than alone. Before recommending a policy, consult those affected. Before approving a program, involve the community. The BTA Parliament itself is a shura — collective deliberation. |
| **Rahmah** | Compassion/Mercy | Government exists to ease suffering, not to create bureaucratic obstacles. When a displaced family needs assistance, process their request with compassion — not bureaucratic indifference. |
| **Sidq** | Truthfulness | Report accurate data. Do not inflate numbers. Do not hide problems. If a program is failing, say so — so it can be fixed. |

**For non-Muslim staff**: These principles are universal governance values expressed in Islamic terms. Trustworthiness, justice, consultation, compassion, and truthfulness are not exclusive to any faith. You are not required to practice Islam — you are expected to practice these values.

**16.3 The 8 Norms of RA 6713 (Code of Conduct and Ethical Standards for Public Officials and Employees)**

RA 6713 is the national law governing the conduct of ALL government employees. Every Bangsamoro public servant is subject to it. Here are the 8 norms, explained for fresh graduates:

| # | Norm | What it means | Violation example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Commitment to public interest** | Your decisions prioritize the public — not your personal, family, or political interest | Approving a project because it benefits your barangay rather than the most underserved community |
| 2 | **Professionalism** | Perform duties with the highest degree of excellence, intelligence, and skill | Submitting CSW full of errors because you rushed; skipping training because you think you already know enough |
| 3 | **Justness and sincerity** | Do not discriminate against anyone. Comply with applicable laws. | Giving preferential treatment to an applicant because they belong to your clan |
| 4 | **Political neutrality** | Provide service regardless of party affiliation. Government resources are not campaign tools. | Using your government vehicle, staff, or office to campaign for a political candidate |
| 5 | **Responsiveness to the public** | Extend prompt, courteous, and adequate service to the public. | Making a citizen wait 3 hours for a document that takes 5 minutes because you are on your phone |
| 6 | **Nationalism and patriotism** | Be loyal to the Republic and to the Bangsamoro region. Promote the use of locally produced goods. | Publicly undermining the legitimacy of the Bangsamoro government or the peace process |
| 7 | **Commitment to democracy** | Maintain transparency in transactions. Participate in Philippine democratic processes. | Concealing public documents from FOI requests without legal basis |
| 8 | **Simple living** | Lead modest lives appropriate to their positions and income. Do not indulge in extravagance. | Driving a luxury vehicle and wearing expensive watches when your salary is SG 11. COA and Ombudsman will notice. |

**16.4 SALN (Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth) — Complete Walkthrough**

SALN is a constitutional requirement (Article XI, Section 17). Every government official and employee must file one annually. Failure to file is grounds for dismissal.

**What you must declare:**
| Category | What to include | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Real Property** | All land, houses, buildings you own (sole or co-owned) | Your family's house and lot in Cotabato City (even if titled to your parents if you are co-owner); agricultural land in Lanao del Sur |
| **Personal Property** | Vehicles, jewelry, cash, bank deposits, investments worth PHP 50,000+ | Your motorcycle, savings account balance, mutual fund investments |
| **Liabilities** | All outstanding debts | Housing loan (Pag-IBIG), car loan, personal loan from a relative, credit card balance |
| **Net Worth** | Assets minus Liabilities | Calculated automatically |
| **Business Interests** | Any business you own or have financial interest in | Sari-sari store, online business, shares in a corporation, partnership |
| **Relatives in Government** | Relatives within the 4th degree of consanguinity or affinity who are in government | Your brother who works in MFBM, your sister-in-law in MBHTE |
| **Financial Connections** | Any financial connection with anyone doing business with the government | Your cousin supplies IT equipment to your office — DECLARE THIS |

**SALN timeline and deadlines:**
- **When**: Filed every year, covering assets/liabilities as of December 31 of the previous year
- **Deadline**: April 30 of each year
- **Where**: Submit to your office's HR unit, which forwards to the CSC or the Ombudsman
- **First SALN**: Filed within 30 days of your appointment
- **Last SALN**: Filed within 30 days of separation from service

**Penalties for SALN violations:**
| Violation | Penalty |
|---|---|
| Failure to file | Suspension (first offense) → Dismissal (second offense) |
| Late filing | Reprimand (first offense) → Suspension (repeated) |
| Misdeclaration (understating assets) | Dismissal + criminal prosecution under RA 3019 (Anti-Graft) |
| Failure to disclose business interests or relatives | Suspension → Dismissal depending on gravity |
| Unexplained wealth (assets significantly exceed known income) | Investigation by Ombudsman, potential forfeiture of assets, criminal charges |

**Fresh graduate tip**: Your first SALN will be simple — you probably have few assets and no liabilities. FILE IT ANYWAY, on time, completely. Build the habit now. As your career progresses and your assets grow, the habit of accurate SALN filing will protect you.

**16.5 Anti-Graft: RA 3019 (Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act) — Practical Scenarios**

RA 3019 defines corrupt acts by public officials. As a fresh graduate, you may think corruption is about millions of pesos. It starts much smaller:

**Scenario 1: Gifts and the PHP 500 threshold**
- A contractor whose proposal you are evaluating brings you a box of pastries and a gift card worth PHP 300. Can you accept?
- **Answer**: CSC rules allow you to accept gifts of **nominal value** (generally interpreted as not exceeding PHP 500 per occasion from a single source). The pastries are likely OK. But a gift card can be considered cash equivalent — more problematic. **Best practice**: Politely decline gifts from anyone with pending business before your office. If you must accept (cultural obligation), declare it to your supervisor in writing.
- **The bright line**: Gifts exceeding PHP 500 from persons with pending business with your office must be refused. Period.

**Scenario 2: "Facilitation fee"**
- A citizen asks you to expedite their permit application and offers you PHP 2,000 "for coffee." Can you accept?
- **Answer**: NO. This is bribery. Even if you call it a "facilitation fee" or "processing fee" or "token of appreciation," accepting money to expedite a public service is a criminal offense under RA 3019, Section 3(b). Report the attempt to your supervisor.

**Scenario 3: Using government resources for personal use**
- It is Friday afternoon. You use the office printer to print 50 copies of your sister's wedding invitation. Is this a problem?
- **Answer**: Yes. RA 3019, Section 3(e) prohibits using government property for private purposes. This includes the printer, the vehicle, the internet connection, the office supplies, and even your work hours. It seems trivial, but COA flags these and repeated violations build a case.

**Scenario 4: "Ghost employees"**
- Your supervisor asks you to process the payroll for a staff member you have never seen come to work. What do you do?
- **Answer**: This is potentially a "ghost employee" — one of the most common forms of government fraud. You have a legal obligation NOT to process the payroll if you have reason to believe the employee is not reporting to work. Document your concern in writing and report it to HR or the Internal Audit unit. RA 3019, Section 3(e) covers this — causing injury to the government through negligence or bad faith.

**Scenario 5: Conflict of interest in procurement**
- Your office is procuring laptops. Your brother-in-law owns a computer shop that is one of the bidders. Can you sit on the BAC (Bids and Awards Committee)?
- **Answer**: No. You must disclose the relationship and recuse yourself from the BAC for this procurement. Failure to disclose is a violation of RA 6713 (norm 3) and potentially RA 3019.

**16.6 COA Audit Basics — How to Stay Compliant**

The Commission on Audit (COA) is the independent body that audits all government transactions. COA auditors can examine any document, interview any employee, and disallow any expenditure that violates rules.

**What triggers a COA audit observation:**
| Trigger | What it means | How to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Missing supporting documents | Disbursement voucher has no attached receipts, no approved request, no BAC resolution | Never process a disbursement without COMPLETE supporting documents |
| Unauthorized expenditure | Spending money for a purpose not covered by the approved budget | Always check your office's budget allocation before proposing expenditures |
| Unliquidated cash advances | You received a cash advance for an activity and haven't submitted liquidation | Liquidate ALL cash advances within 30 days (or the period specified in COA rules) |
| Splitting of contracts | Breaking a large procurement into smaller ones to avoid public bidding thresholds | Follow RA 9184 (Government Procurement Reform Act) thresholds strictly |
| Non-compliance with procurement rules | Direct contracting when public bidding is required; incomplete PhilGEPS posting | Always coordinate with the BAC and Procurement Office |
| Overpayment | Paying more than the contract amount or paying for work not yet completed | Verify deliverables before signing the Inspection and Acceptance Report |

**COA disallowance**: If COA disallows an expenditure, the persons who authorized, approved, and certified the transaction must refund the amount from personal funds. Yes — you can be held personally liable. This is why every signature you put on a government document matters.

**Fresh graduate tip**: When in doubt about any financial transaction, ask these questions:
1. Is there a budget for this? (Check the approved appropriation)
2. Is the procurement process correct? (Ask the BAC)
3. Are all supporting documents complete? (Use the documentary requirements checklist)
4. Who will sign and certify? (Each signature carries legal liability)

**16.7 Conflict of Interest — Practical Rules**

| Situation | What you must do |
|---|---|
| Your relative applies for a position in your office | Disclose the relationship to HR/HRMPSB in writing. Recuse yourself from the selection process. |
| Your family member's company bids on your office's procurement | Disclose to the BAC. Recuse yourself from evaluation. |

**16.8 Whistleblower Protection**

If you witness corruption, fraud, or illegal activity in your office, you have the right AND the obligation to report it.

**Where to report:**
| Channel | What to report | Confidentiality |
|---|---|---|
| **Immediate supervisor** | First resort for most concerns (unless your supervisor is involved) | Internal — may or may not be confidential |
| **Internal Audit unit (BIAB)** | Financial irregularities, misuse of funds, ghost employees | Confidential by policy |
| **Civil Service Commission (CSC)** | Personnel violations, nepotism, illegal appointments | Formal complaint — documented |
| **Office of the Ombudsman** | Graft and corruption, unexplained wealth, misconduct | Protected under RA 6770 (Ombudsman Act) |
| **Commission on Audit (COA)** | Financial irregularities, unauthorized expenditures | Audit findings are official records |
| **Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC)** | Rights violations by government officials | Protected |

**Whistleblower protections:**
- RA 6981 (Witness Protection, Security, and Benefit Act) provides protection for witnesses against government officials
- CSC rules prohibit retaliation against employees who file complaints in good faith
- The Ombudsman can issue protective orders for complainants facing harassment

**The reality**: Whistleblowing in government — especially in a region with strong clan and political networks — is difficult. But the legal protections exist. Document everything. File written complaints through official channels. Keep copies. If you fear retaliation, the Ombudsman is the most independent channel.

**16.10 The Peace Process and Your Role**

Every day you work in BARMM government, you are implementing the Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (CAB) — signed March 27, 2014, between the Government of the Republic of the Philippines (GRP) and the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF). The BOL (RA 11054) is the legislative product of that agreement.

- When you process a BANGON livelihood grant, you are delivering a peace dividend
- When you draft a legislative briefer, you are exercising the Bangsamoro's right to self-governance
- When you audit a project, you are ensuring public trust in the new government
- When you serve a community in Tawi-Tawi or Basilan, you are proving that the autonomous government works

The transition period is when credibility is built or lost. If the government delivers, the people's trust deepens. If it fails, skeptics who opposed autonomy will say they were right. Your daily work is the frontline of this test.

**16.11 Actionable Tool: "Ethics Decision Tree"**

```

   → NO → Refuse. Document. Report if needed.
   → YES → Continue to 2.
   → NOT SURE → Ask your supervisor or legal office BEFORE acting.
   ↓
2. Does it comply with government rules? (CSC, COA, procurement, HR)
   → NO → Refuse. Cite the specific rule.
   → YES → Continue to 3.
   ↓
3. Would you be comfortable if this appeared in the newspaper?
   → NO → Don't do it. Even if technically legal, reputational damage matters.
   → YES → Continue to 4.
   ↓
4. Does it align with the 12 Pillars of Moral Governance?
   → NO → Reconsider. Discuss with your supervisor.
   → YES → Proceed with documentation.
   ↓
5. ALWAYS: Document your decision in writing. Keep copies.
   If someone pressures you, write a memo to your supervisor:
   "For the record, I was asked to [action] by [person].
   I believe this [complies with / violates] [specific rule].
   I have [proceeded / declined]. Please advise."
```

## Part IV: Reference (Chapter 17 + Appendices)

### Chapter 17 — Frequently Asked Questions

**Opening**: "These are the questions we hear most — from fresh graduates at career fairs, university talks, and social media. If your question isn't here, email [address] or visit the CSC BARMM regional office."

**17.1 Application and Hiring FAQs**

**Q1: Do I need Civil Service eligibility to work in BARMM government?**
A: For **permanent plantilla positions**, yes — you need CSC Professional or Sub-Professional eligibility. But there are entry points that do NOT require eligibility: Contract of Service (COS), Job Order (JO), consultant, and internship positions. Many fresh graduates start with COS/JO while preparing for the CSC exam. If you are a board/bar passer (RA 1080), you automatically have CSC Professional eligibility.

**Q2: Where do I find job openings?**
A: The official channels: CSC Job Portal (csc.gov.ph/career — filter by BARMM), the BARMM Official Gazette, and individual ministry/office websites and Facebook pages. Vacancies must be posted for a minimum of 10 calendar days. See Chapter 7 for the complete job search strategy.

**Q3: What documents do I need to apply?**
A: Application letter, Personal Data Sheet (CS Form 212), Transcript of Records, Diploma, CSC eligibility proof, training certificates, NBI Clearance, Barangay Clearance, Community Tax Certificate, passport/2x2 photos, and PSA-authenticated Birth Certificate. See Chapter 8, Section 8.3 for the complete checklist.

**Q4: How long does the hiring process take?**
A: From posting to appointment: typically 2-4 months. Breakdown: 10-day posting period → 2-4 weeks HRMPSB screening → 1-2 weeks interview scheduling → 2-8 weeks results and appointment. Some offices are faster; some take longer. You can follow up with HR after 30 days.

**Q5: I graduated with a general degree (BS Public Administration, AB Political Science). What positions can I apply for?**
A: General degrees qualify you for a wide range of entry positions: Administrative Officer, Planning Officer, Legislative Staff Officer, Project Development Officer, Management and Audit Analyst, and others at SG 11-15. Your degree becomes a competitive advantage when paired with CSC eligibility and relevant volunteer/intern experience.

**Q6: Can I apply to multiple positions at the same time?**
A: Yes — and you should. There is no rule limiting the number of applications. Apply to every position you qualify for. Track them systematically using the Job Search Tracker (Chapter 7).

**Q7: I don't have work experience. Can I still get hired?**
A: Yes. Entry-level positions (SG 11-15) typically require a bachelor's degree and CSC eligibility but minimal or no experience. In the Comparative Assessment, experience is 25% of the total score — but Education (25%), Training (15%), Eligibility (10%), and Interview (25%) are the other 75%. A well-prepared fresh graduate with strong interview performance can beat a less-prepared candidate with experience. See Chapter 9 for interview preparation.

**Q8: What is a "next-in-rank" position and does it affect external applicants?**
A: "Next-in-rank" means employees one level below a vacant position get priority consideration. However, it is NOT exclusive — external applicants can and do get hired for positions even when internal candidates exist, as long as they score higher in the Comparative Assessment. Do not be discouraged from applying.

**17.2 Salary and Benefits FAQs**

**Q9: How much will I earn as an entry-level government employee?**
A: Entry-level professional positions start at SG 11 (approximately PHP 27,000/month base). With allowances (PERA, ACA), your total compensation is approximately PHP 29,000-31,000/month. After deductions (GSIS, PhilHealth, Pag-IBIG), take-home is approximately PHP 25,000-28,000/month. See Chapter 15 for the full salary grade table.

**Q10: Is government pay lower than private sector?**
A: For entry-level positions, yes — government base pay is typically 15-30% lower than comparable private sector roles. However, the total compensation package is competitive when you factor in: job security (plantilla positions are permanent), comprehensive health insurance (PhilHealth + GSIS), pension (GSIS retirement benefits), housing loan (Pag-IBIG), leave benefits (30 days VL + SL annually), Performance-Based Bonus, and 13th month pay + cash gift. Private sector rarely matches this benefits package.

**Q11: When do I get a raise?**
A: Three ways: (1) **Step increment** — automatic salary increase within your SG every 3 years of continuous satisfactory service OR upon meeting length-of-service + performance criteria. (2) **Promotion** — competitive selection to a higher-SG position. (3) **SSL tranche adjustment** — periodic government-wide salary increases mandated by the Salary Standardization Law. You do NOT get an annual raise just for showing up — step increments require satisfactory SPMS ratings.

**Q12: What are my leave benefits?**
A: Permanent employees: 15 days vacation leave + 15 days sick leave per year (earned monthly at 1.25 days each). Special leaves: maternity leave (105 days), paternity leave (7 days), solo parent leave (7 days), VAWC leave (10 days), study leave (up to 6 months after 7 years of service), special privilege leave (3 days), and rehabilitation privilege. COS/JO employees do NOT receive leave benefits — this is a major reason to pursue permanent appointment.

**Q13: What is the Performance-Based Bonus (PBB) and how do I qualify?**
A: PBB is an annual bonus for employees whose office meets its OPCR targets AND who individually meet their IPCR targets. Amount varies (typically PHP 10,000-35,000 depending on performance tier). To qualify: your office must achieve at least "Satisfactory" on the OPCR, AND you must individually achieve at least "Satisfactory" on your IPCR. Offices that fail their OPCR — no PBB for anyone in that office.

**17.3 Work Culture FAQs**

**Q14: What are government working hours?**
A: Standard: 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM, Monday to Friday, with 1 hour lunch break (12:00-1:00 PM). Some offices offer flexible work arrangements (e.g., 7:00 AM - 4:00 PM). During Ramadan, BARMM offices typically adjust hours (e.g., 7:30 AM - 3:30 PM) to accommodate fasting employees. Attendance is tracked through biometrics or daily time records (DTR).

**Q15: What is the dress code?**
A: Business formal on regular days: for men — long-sleeved polo or barong, slacks, closed shoes; for women — modest professional attire (many offices observe Islamic dress norms). Casual dress is typically allowed on Fridays ("wash day"). Some offices have specific uniform days. When in doubt, overdress rather than underdress, especially in your first month.

**Q16: Is there a prayer room in government offices?**
A: Yes. All BARMM offices are required to provide prayer spaces. Prayer times (5 daily prayers) are respected — you will not be penalized for performing prayers during work hours, but coordinate with your supervisor so work continuity is maintained. During Ramadan, the entire office culture adjusts — expect lighter work schedules, iftar gatherings, and heightened spiritual observance.

**Q17: I am not Muslim. Will I be required to participate in Islamic practices?**
A: No. You will NOT be required to pray, fast, or perform any Islamic religious obligation. You WILL be expected to respect Islamic practices: maintain quiet during prayer times, avoid eating conspicuously in front of fasting colleagues during Ramadan, and use the greeting "Assalamu Alaikum" (which simply means "peace be upon you" — appropriate for anyone). BARMM values inclusivity (Pillar 9). Non-Muslim staff serve across all ministries and offices.

**Q18: What languages will I need?**
A: Government business is conducted primarily in **English** (for official documents, memos, briefers) and **Filipino** (for meetings and informal communication). Knowledge of a Bangsamoro language (Meranaw, Maguindanaon, Tausug, Sinama, Yakan) is a significant advantage — especially for field work and community engagement — but is NOT required for most positions. Arabic is used in formal Islamic contexts (prayers, Quranic references) but is not required for daily government work.

**Q19: How hierarchical is BARMM government?**
A: Very. The approval chain is strict — you do not go over your supervisor's head, you do not send memos directly to the Minister without going through the chain of command, and you address senior officials with appropriate honorifics (Sir/Ma'am, Director, Honorable). This is not just government culture — it is also Bangsamoro culture, which deeply respects seniority and authority. As a fresh graduate, observe and follow the hierarchy. Earn trust through consistent quality work before you offer unsolicited opinions to senior leadership.

**17.4 Career Development FAQs**

**Q20: How fast can I get promoted?**
A: Realistically: first promotion (SG 11 → SG 13) in 2-3 years if vacancies exist and your performance is strong. Reaching Division Chief (SG 24) typically takes 10-15 years. See Chapter 15 for the full promotion timeline and salary grade progression table. Speed depends on: vacancy availability, your SPMS ratings, training hours accumulated, and competition for positions.

**Q21: Can I transfer between ministries?**
A: Yes. Transfer requires: a vacant position in the receiving office that you qualify for, clearance from your current office, and approval of the receiving office. You keep your salary grade (or get a higher one if the new position is higher-SG). Timeline: 1-3 months. Some offices are easier to transfer from than others — discuss with your supervisor. See Chapter 15, Section 15.6 for details on transfer, detailing, and secondment.

**Q22: Is a master's degree necessary for promotion?**
A: Not always — but it becomes increasingly important at higher levels. Positions at SG 22+ (Division Chief) often require "master's degree" or "completion of academic requirements for a master's degree" in their QS. If you plan a long government career, pursue a graduate degree within your first 5 years. Government scholarship programs (CSC, CHED, foreign scholarships) are available. See Chapter 15, Section 15.5.

**Q23: What training will I receive?**
A: Mandatory training: Government Orientation, Values Orientation Workshop, SPMS Orientation. Recommended: CSW Training (ADDRESS IT methodology), Records Management, IT Skills. As you advance: Basic Supervisory Course, Supervisory Development Program, Leadership and Management courses. You can also request functional training specific to your role. See Chapter 15, Section 15.4 for the complete training catalog.

**17.5 BARMM-Specific and Identity FAQs**

**Q24: I am not Muslim — can I still work in BARMM government?**
A: Absolutely yes. BARMM is an autonomous government for the Bangsamoro region — not an Islamic theocracy. The BOL (Article IX, Section 3 on indigenous peoples' rights and Section 15 on settler communities) explicitly guarantees the rights of non-Moro and non-Muslim communities within the Bangsamoro territory. Non-Muslim staff serve in every ministry, commission, and office — including the Parliament Secretariat. You will be expected to respect Islamic governance values (moral governance, amanah, shura), but these are universal principles of good governance, not religious requirements. Your competence, not your faith, determines your career.

**Q25: I am not Bangsamoro — I am from Manila / Visayas / Luzon. Will I fit in?**
A: Yes — if you approach the work with humility, respect, and genuine commitment to the Bangsamoro people's development. BARMM actively welcomes Filipino professionals from other regions, especially in technical fields (engineering, IT, health, finance) where local talent supply may be limited. What matters is your attitude: learn the culture, respect the traditions, study the BOL, and demonstrate that you are here to serve — not to "fix" the Bangsamoro. Several senior government officials and technical staff are from outside the region. Build relationships, learn a few Bangsamoro phrases, and show up with humility.

**Practical tips for non-Bangsamoro staff:**
- Learn the greeting "Assalamu Alaikum" and the response "Wa Alaikum Assalam"
- Respect prayer times and fasting during Ramadan
- Do not assume that the way things work in your home region is the way they should work here
- Ask questions about local customs — colleagues appreciate genuine interest
- Be patient with processes that may be unfamiliar — BARMM has its own laws (BAA 13, BAA 17, BAA 49, BAA 84) that differ from national rules
- Your outsider perspective IS valuable — fresh eyes see things insiders miss — but offer it respectfully

**Q26: What happens when the BTA transition ends and elections happen?**
A: The BTA (Bangsamoro Transition Authority) is transitional — it was created to set up the permanent government. When elections happen:
- **Parliament positions**: The 77+ appointed BTA Members of Parliament will be replaced by elected MPs. The Parliament continues — the staff continues. If you work in the Secretariat or a committee, your position is not tied to a specific MP's appointment (unlike coterminous positions in the executive).
- **Ministry positions**: Permanent plantilla positions are NOT affected by elections. Ministers may change (they are appointed by the Chief Minister), but career staff (you) remain. Your job is protected by BAA 17 (security of tenure for permanent appointees).
- **Coterminous positions**: If your appointment is coterminous with the appointing authority, your position ends when they leave. Plan for this — apply for plantilla positions or another coterminous appointment.
- **COS/JO positions**: These are project-based and have no security of tenure regardless of elections. The contract determines your end date, not the election.
- **Bottom line**: If you have a permanent appointment, the transition to an elected government does NOT threaten your job. The government continues. The mandate continues. Your work continues.

**Q27: I am from a clan/family with political affiliations. Will that help or hurt me?**
A: BARMM — like all Philippine government — has a complex relationship between merit and political networks. The honest answer:
- **Legally**: BAA 17 (Bangsamoro Civil Service Code) mandates merit and fitness. The HRMPSB process, the Comparative Assessment, and CSC oversight are designed to prevent pure patronage hiring.
- **Practically**: Connections help you HEAR about openings and get your application noticed — but you still have to pass the QS and score well in the Comparative Assessment. A well-connected applicant who cannot perform the job will struggle.
- **What you should do**: Use your network to learn about opportunities (Chapter 7), but never assume you will be hired on connections alone. Prepare as if you have no connections. Build competence that speaks for itself. The best protection against political volatility is being so good at your job that every supervisor wants to keep you.

**Q28: I am interested in Shari'ah law. Are there positions for that?**
A: Yes. The BOL (Article X) establishes the Bangsamoro justice system, including Shari'ah courts. Positions include: Shari'ah judge, court clerk, Shari'ah lawyer, and court staff. Requirements: for judicial positions, a law degree (with Shari'ah law specialization or training) and Philippine Bar membership. For court staff: relevant education and CSC eligibility. The Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' (BDI), headed by the Grand Mufti, also employs Islamic scholars and researchers. If you have Islamic law education, explore both the courts and the BDI.

**Q29: Is it safe to work in BARMM?**
A: Safety varies by location. Cotabato City (the seat of government) is generally safe for government workers. Provincial capitals are also generally safe during daylight hours. Some areas have security considerations — your office will brief you on local conditions. The peace process has significantly improved the security situation compared to the pre-BOL era. Government workers typically face more risk from natural disasters (flooding in Maguindanao, earthquakes) than from armed conflict. Your office will provide security orientation and protocols.

**Q30: Can I work from home / is there a remote work policy?**
A: During the COVID-19 pandemic, BARMM offices adopted alternative work arrangements (AWA) including work-from-home. Post-pandemic policies vary by office. Some offices allow flexible arrangements (skeleton workforce, compressed workweek). Most offices have returned to full on-site work. As an entry-level staff member, expect to report on-site daily for at least your first year — you need face-to-face interaction to learn the office culture, build relationships, and receive hands-on coaching from your supervisor.

**Q31: I am a person with a disability. Can I work in BARMM government?**
A: Yes. RA 10524 (Expanded Positions for Persons with Disability Act) requires government agencies to reserve at least 1% of positions for PWDs. BARMM offices are subject to this requirement. Your disability does not disqualify you — reasonable accommodation must be provided. If you experience discrimination during the application process, file a complaint with the CSC.

**Q32: I am a woman. Are there gender-specific programs or barriers?**
A: The Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC, created by BAA 8) promotes gender equality across BARMM. Women hold leadership positions in BARMM — including the current MSSD Minister, Deputy Speakers, and committee chairs. There are no legal barriers to women in any government position. Maternity leave (105 days under RA 11210), VAWC leave, and solo parent leave are legally guaranteed. The GAD (Gender and Development) budget ensures each office allocates resources for gender-responsive programming.

**Q33: What if I get assigned to a remote or island municipality?**
A: Some positions — especially in MOH, MBHTE, MAFAR, and MILG — require deployment to geographically isolated and disadvantaged areas (GIDAs). This means island municipalities in Tawi-Tawi or Basilan, interior barangays in Lanao del Sur, or conflict-affected communities in Maguindanao. Challenges: limited connectivity, difficult transportation, basic living conditions. Advantages: you will have the most direct impact of anyone in government, you will develop resilience and resourcefulness that urban staff never gain, and GIDA assignments are valued in your service record for future promotions. If you are assigned to a remote area, negotiate hardship allowance and transportation support.
