# (cc) Housing and Human Settlements

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(cc)** — ***"Housing and human settlements;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(cc) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national housing law. Art. IX, Sec. 9 mandates a housing and human settlements agency without any "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier referencing national law. However, Art. V, Sec. 2 is ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** and ***"this Organic Law"*** — establishing a constitutional floor (see Section B (Constitutional Considerations)). |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (MHSD)** — established under BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Public Works (MOPW); Ministry of Interior and Local Government (MILG); Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD); Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 11 (Infrastructure) — Target 6.5.1: equitable access to affordable and decent housing; Chapter 9 (Social Services) — Housing and Resettlement Program |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | **Housing and human settlements agency** (mandated by Art. IX, Sec. 9 — fulfilled through BAA 13). Note: Art. IX, Sec. 9 mandates ***"a law creating a housing and human settlements agency"*** — BAA 13 establishes MHSD institutionally but no standalone housing code has been enacted. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over housing and human settlements?**

A. Article V, Section 2(cc) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Housing and human settlements"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** The chapeau of Art. V, Sec. 2 qualifies this: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This is a broad grant covering **housing policy**, **program development**, **regulation of housing and subdivisions**, **human settlements planning**, **urban and rural development**, and **resettlement** within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Six provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. IX, Sec. 9 is the **dedicated housing provision**. It states: ***"The Parliament shall pass a law creating a housing and human settlements agency to address the lack of shelters, settlements, and livelihood for the disadvantaged and homeless, especially those victims of conflicts and atrocities. The agency may directly solicit and receive assistance, donations, aids, and grants from donors for its housing, settlements, and livelihood programs and development."*** The second paragraph adds: ***"The Bangsamoro Government, in cooperation with the private sector, shall develop its own housing and human settlement programs."***[^2]

   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 8 establishes the **basic service obligation**: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall provide, maintain, and ensure the delivery of...affordable and progressive housing projects...to the Bangsamoro people and other inhabitants of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** This makes housing a **mandatory government service**, not a discretionary program.[^3]

   (3) Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) provides **borrowing authority** for housing: ***"The Bangsamoro Government may contract loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness to finance the construction, installation, improvement, expansion, operation or maintenance of...housing projects..."***[^4]

   (4) Art. IV, Sec. 7 establishes the **social justice framework**: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall ensure every Filipino citizen in its territorial jurisdiction the provision of the basic necessities and equal opportunities in life."*** Housing is a basic necessity under this mandate.[^5]

   (5) Art. XIV, Sec. 1-2 creates the **Special Development Fund** (P50B over 10 years) for rehabilitation and development — a potential funding source for housing programs in conflict-affected areas.[^6]

   (6) Art. XVI, Sec. 8(l) names ***"Human Settlements and Development"*** as one of the **15 primary interim ministries**, confirming that housing warranted ministry-level status from the outset.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. IX, Sec. 9 contains an express legislative mandate: ***"The Parliament shall pass a law creating a housing and human settlements agency."*** This is a **named institutional mandate** — the BOL directs Parliament to create a specific type of agency. The mandate has been **partially fulfilled** through BAA 13, which establishes MHSD. However, BAA 13 is a general administrative code, not the standalone housing law that Art. IX, Sec. 9 contemplates. A **Bangsamoro Housing Code** or **Housing and Human Settlements Act** would fully satisfy this mandate by providing a comprehensive regulatory framework.[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL directs Parliament to create a ***"housing and human settlements agency"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 9) but does not name it. Art. XVI, Sec. 8(l) designates ***"Human Settlements and Development"*** as an interim ministry. MHSD was established through **BAA 13**, Book VI, Title VI, Sec. 1-12, and receives budget allocation under **BAA 85**, Chapter XIV. The BOL further authorizes the agency to ***"directly solicit and receive assistance, donations, aids, and grants from donors"*** — a fiscal power built into the institutional mandate.[^2][^7][^8][^9]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places three types of conditions:

   (1) **Constitutional floor**: Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law."*** Art. X, Sec. 15 of the Constitution requires autonomous regions to operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."***[^10]

   (2) **Target population specification**: Art. IX, Sec. 9 specifies that the housing agency must address ***"the lack of shelters, settlements, and livelihood for the disadvantaged and homeless, especially those victims of conflicts and atrocities."*** This is not merely a general housing mandate — it has a **social justice orientation** prioritizing conflict-affected populations.[^2]

   (3) **Borrowing conditions**: Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) permits housing borrowing ***"[s]ubject to acceptable credit worthiness, and in compliance with the Constitution, relevant laws and regulations"*** — and loans requiring sovereign guaranty need National Government approval.[^4]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to housing and human settlements?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** This is the **universal constitutional boundary** on all 55 powers.[^10]

   (2) **Urban Land Reform and Housing (Art. XIII, Sec. 9)**: ***"The State shall, by law, and for the common good, undertake, in cooperation with the public sector, a continuing program of urban land reform and housing which will make available at affordable cost decent housing and basic services to underprivileged and homeless citizens in urban centers and resettlements areas. It shall also promote adequate employment opportunities to such citizens. In the implementation of such program the State shall respect the rights of small property owners."*** This is the **constitutional mandate** for housing programs — both national and Bangsamoro.[^11]

   (3) **Anti-Eviction Protection (Art. XIII, Sec. 10)**: ***"Urban or rural poor dwellers shall not be evicted nor their dwellings demolished, except in accordance with law and in a just and humane manner."*** And: ***"No resettlement of urban or rural dwellers shall be undertaken without adequate consultation with them and the communities where they are to be relocated."*** These are **constitutional minimum protections** that any Bangsamoro housing law must meet or exceed.[^12]

   (4) **Due Process and Equal Protection (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, nor shall any person be denied the equal protection of the laws."*** Housing regulation affecting property rights — eviction, demolition, land acquisition, zoning — must satisfy both procedural and substantive due process.[^13]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee for housing?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Right to affordable housing** — Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandates decent housing at affordable cost. This is an **affirmative state obligation**, not a mere policy aspiration.

   (2) **Protection against eviction** — Art. XIII, Sec. 10 prohibits eviction and demolition except ***"in accordance with law and in a just and humane manner."*** Any Bangsamoro housing law must comply with this floor.

   (3) **Right to adequate consultation before resettlement** — Art. XIII, Sec. 10 requires ***"adequate consultation"*** with affected communities. This is a **procedural right** that cannot be waived by legislation.

   (4) **Property rights** — Art. III, Sec. 1 protects property against deprivation without due process. Housing regulation involving land acquisition or eminent domain must provide just compensation.

   (5) **Small property owner protection** — Art. XIII, Sec. 9 requires the State to ***"respect the rights of small property owners"*** in housing programs.

**Q. What constitutional mandates for housing legislation or institutions exist?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandates the State to undertake a ***"continuing program of urban land reform and housing."*** This was fulfilled nationally by RA 7279 (UDHA) and RA 11201 (DHSUD). For BARMM, **MHSD** serves as the counterpart institution, and the BOL's Art. IX, Sec. 9 creates a parallel mandate. The constitutional mandate implies that BARMM's housing framework must provide **comparable protections** to the national framework — particularly the eviction and resettlement safeguards of RA 7279, which implement Art. XIII, Sec. 10.[^11][^12]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for housing?**

A. The national framework establishes a comprehensive system: **RA 7279** (UDHA, as amended by RA 10884 and RA 9397) provides the primary housing and urban development framework — including socialized housing requirements, balanced housing mandates, eviction and demolition safeguards, and resettlement standards. **RA 11201** creates the **DHSUD** as the primary national policy, planning, and regulatory body. **RA 12216** rationalizes NHA's powers for socialized housing production. **RA 7835** establishes the National Shelter Program financing framework. **RA 6846** creates the Social Housing Support Fund. Together, these laws provide: a **regulatory framework** for housing development, **institutional infrastructure** (DHSUD, NHA, SHFC, NHMFC, HDMF/Pag-IBIG), **financing mechanisms**, **eviction safeguards**, and **resettlement standards**.[^14][^15][^16]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national housing laws apply in the absence of a Bangsamoro Housing Code:

   (1) **RA 7279** (as amended) — governs housing standards, eviction procedures, and resettlement within BARMM. Applies suppletorily.[^14]
   (2) **RA 11201** — DHSUD retains regulatory authority over subdivision and housing development in BARMM. Applies directly.[^15]
   (3) **RA 12216** — NHA continues implementing socialized housing and resettlement projects in BARMM. Applies directly.[^16]
   (4) **RA 7835** — National Shelter Program financing provisions apply to housing projects in BARMM. Suppletory.
   (5) **RA 6846** — SHFC community mortgage and socialized housing finance programs operate in BARMM. Suppletory.
   (6) **RA 10884** — balanced housing requirements apply to BARMM developers. Suppletory.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for housing?**

A. Three national agencies exercise authority: (a) **DHSUD** — regulatory authority over housing and land use development, subdivision approvals, and condominium registration; (b) **NHA** — direct provider of socialized housing and resettlement projects, including **Marawi rehabilitation housing**; (c) **HDMF/Pag-IBIG Fund** — housing finance through member contributions and housing loans. When a Bangsamoro Housing Code is enacted, the question is whether MHSD replaces DHSUD's regulatory functions within BARMM, or whether DHSUD retains certain functions (e.g., cross-territorial developers, Pag-IBIG coordination). NHA's project implementation role is likely to continue for nationally-funded programs.[^15][^16]

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (MHSD)** is the lead agency. See the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A) for MHSD's institutional basis in the BOL. BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI, Sec. 1-12 defines MHSD's institutional status, mandate, powers, functions, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter XIV allocates its operational and program budget [UNVERIFIED: exact budget figures must be verified against BAA 85 Chapter XIV source text].[^8][^9]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for housing and human settlements as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through MHSD's **BAA 13 general mandate** and **BAA 85 budget allocation**.

**Q. What are MHSD's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI, Sec. 1-12 defines MHSD comprehensively:

   **Declaration of Policies** (Sec. 1): The Bangsamoro Government shall address ***"the lack of shelters, settlements, and livelihood for the disadvantaged and homeless, especially those victims of conflicts and atrocities, and Internally Displaced Persons (IDPs), pursuant to Section 9, Article IX of the Bangsamoro Organic Law."*** The policy further commits to ***"formulate, adopt, execute, and promote comprehensive, integrated, and sustainable regional housing, urban and rural development policies, plans, programs, and projects."*** It mandates ***"a continuing program on housing and urban and rural development which shall provide adequate and decent housing facilities at affordable cost and sustainable physical and social infrastructure."*** It also provides that ***"poor dwellers in urban and rural areas shall not be evicted nor their dwelling demolished, except in accordance with law."***[^17]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): MHSD shall act as ***"the primary agency for the management of housing, human settlement, and urban development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It shall be the main planning and policy-making, regulatory, program coordination, and performance monitoring entity for all housing, human settlements, and urban development concerns."***[^18]

   **Powers and functions** (Sec. 3) — **19 enumerated functions**:

   - (a) Develop and implement comprehensive and integrated housing, human settlements, and urban and rural economic development plans and programs;
   - (b) Institute mechanisms for proper management of growth and development of urban and rural communities;
   - (c) Coordinate and monitor urban and rural economic development undertakings;
   - (d) Manage and oversee the development of proclaimed socialized and economic housing sites, including using land assets for resource mobilization;
   - (e) Enter into contracts, joint venture agreements, public-private partnerships, and MOAs;
   - (f) Receive grants, gifts, and donations from foreign and domestic sources;
   - (g) Implement a single regulatory system for socialized housing and urban development projects;
   - (h) Conduct continuing studies and researches for housing and development;
   - (i) Formulate housing policies and programs for the homeless, underprivileged, and IDPs;
   - (j) Formulate a framework for resilient housing and post-disaster housing;
   - (k) Determine, fix, and collect fees and charges; impose fines and penalties;
   - (l) Recommend new legislation and amendments;
   - (m) Promote, accredit, and regulate the use of indigenous materials and technologies;
   - (n) Open roads of subdivisions to the public when the general welfare requires;
   - (o) Develop sector performance monitoring and assessment mechanism for LGUs;
   - (p) Develop People's Plans in partnership with beneficiaries;
   - (q) Establish **Housing One-Stop Processing Centers (HOPCs)** in the provinces;
   - (r) Identify and acquire government and private lands through donation, expropriation, or just compensation for housing development;
   - (s) Perform such other functions as necessary.[^19]

   **Organizational structure** (Sec. 4, 9): Ministry Proper headed by the Minister (Sec. 5-6) with Deputy Minister (Sec. 7) and Bangsamoro Director-General (Sec. 8). Two services:

   (a) **Operations Management Services** — headed by a Director II, with four divisions: Administrative and Finance Division; Policy Development and Coordination Division; Housing and Human Settlements Division; and Planning and Design Division.

   (b) **Technical Regulatory Services** — headed by a Director II, with two divisions: Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division; and Monitoring and Evaluation Division.[^20]

   **Field Offices** (Sec. 10-12): **Provincial Human Settlements and Development Offices** in every province plus one Special Geographic Areas Office for the 63 barangays. The Minister exercises technical and administrative control over field offices.[^21]

**Q. What can MHSD NOT do without a standalone Bangsamoro Housing Code?**

A. While BAA 13 gives MHSD broad functions — including regulatory authority (Sec. 3(g)), fee collection (Sec. 3(k)), and land acquisition (Sec. 3(r)) — it does not provide: a **comprehensive housing regulatory code** with detailed subdivision and condominium standards; **eviction and demolition procedures** specific to BARMM's conflict-affected context; **Shari'ah-compliant housing finance models** (*murabaha*, *ijarah*, *musharakah mutanaqisah*) with legal definitions and requirements; **socialized housing price ceilings and lot area standards** adapted to BARMM economic conditions; a **clear jurisdictional boundary** with DHSUD and NHA for housing regulation and project implementation; **penalty provisions** for housing law violations; or a **Bangsamoro shelter financing framework** integrating the Special Development Fund (Art. XIV) with housing programs. BAA 13 creates the institution but not the **regulatory code** it needs to fully exercise its authority.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. Three resolutions signal parliamentary attention:

   (1) **Res. No. 356** urges BARMM ministries and LGUs to provide housing for Typhoon Paeng victims — demonstrating demand for MHSD's post-disaster housing mandate.

   (2) **Res. No. 405** directs BPDA to apprise Parliament on the housing program's status relative to BDP targets — an **oversight mechanism** signaling parliamentary interest in housing performance.

   (3) **Res. No. 476** urges funding for Marawi IDP shelter lease extensions — highlighting the ongoing **Marawi displacement crisis** as a housing priority.

   Additionally, **PR 167** (proposed) calls on MHSD to formulate guidelines for CLUP and zoning ordinance review — an area where MHSD's regulatory authority under BAA 13 needs standalone legislative backing.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. MHSD's BAA 13 mandate covers the **full scope** of Art. V, Sec. 2(cc). The mandate explicitly references Art. IX, Sec. 9, covers housing program development, human settlements, urban and rural development, land use regulation, socialized housing, and IDP resettlement. There is no mismatch between the power's scope and the ministry's mandate. MHSD is properly designated as the lead ministry.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. The following institutions exist:

   - **Lead ministry**: MHSD — BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI, Sec. 1-12; BAA 85, Chapter XIV
   - **Supporting ministries**: MOPW (infrastructure construction), MILG (LGU coordination, land identification), MSSD (welfare of displaced families), MENRE (land use, environmental clearances)
   - **Planning body**: BPDA — coordinates housing targets with BDP implementation
   - **LGU-level**: Provincial Human Settlements and Development Offices (BAA 13 Sec. 10) and constituent LGU planning offices

   All named institutions are operational.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Art. IX, Sec. 9 mandates ***"a housing and human settlements agency."*** MHSD fulfills this mandate institutionally. However, the BOL's language (***"The Parliament shall pass a law creating"***) contemplates a standalone law defining the agency — BAA 13's general administrative code provisions may not fully satisfy the legislative intent of Art. IX, Sec. 9. No separate housing finance institution (comparable to SHFC or NHMFC at the national level) has been created.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Yes, two institutional gaps exist:

   (1) A **Bangsamoro Housing Finance Entity** — a mechanism for housing loan origination, socialized housing finance, and Shari'ah-compliant housing finance (*murabaha*, *ijarah*, *musharakah mutanaqisah*). Nationally, SHFC, NHMFC, and Pag-IBIG fill this role. No Bangsamoro counterpart exists.

   (2) A **Bangsamoro Land Bank for Housing** or land acquisition mechanism — MHSD has the function to identify and acquire lands (BAA 13 Sec. 3(r)), but no dedicated land banking institution or fund exists.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. Three national agencies operate in BARMM: (a) **DHSUD** exercises regulatory authority — subdivision and housing development approvals, land use regulatory functions (inherited from HLURB), and policy coordination; (b) **NHA** directly implements socialized housing and resettlement programs, including the significant **Task Force Bangon Marawi** housing component for Marawi IDPs; (c) **Pag-IBIG Fund (HDMF)** provides housing loans to BARMM-based members through its regional service offices.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for housing?**

A. (1) **NHA Marawi Rehabilitation Housing** — construction of permanent housing for Marawi IDPs displaced by the 2017 siege; (2) **NHA resettlement projects** — socialized housing in conflict-affected and disaster-prone areas; (3) **Pag-IBIG housing loans** — mortgage financing for fund members in BARMM; (4) **SHFC Community Mortgage Program** — socialized housing finance for organized communities; (5) **DHSUD regulation** — subdivision approvals, housing development licensing, land use oversight.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **incomplete**. MHSD exists as an operational ministry with provincial field offices, but **DHSUD's regulatory functions** (subdivision approval, housing development licensing, CLUP review) have not been formally transferred. NHA continues to directly implement housing projects in BARMM — particularly the Marawi rehabilitation program — without a formal coordination framework with MHSD. No executive order, memorandum of agreement, or transition plan governing the devolution of DHSUD/NHA functions to MHSD has been identified. The **single biggest devolution gap** is regulatory authority over housing development — DHSUD continues to exercise HLURB-inherited functions that should reside with MHSD under the BOL's enumerated power.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes, in three areas: (1) **housing project implementation** — both NHA and MHSD implement housing programs in BARMM, with no formal delineation of which agency takes the lead for which type of project; (2) **housing regulation** — DHSUD retains subdivision and housing development regulatory authority while MHSD has a BAA 13 mandate for ***"a single regulatory system"*** (Sec. 3(g)); (3) **land use regulation** — DHSUD's inherited HLURB functions overlap with MHSD's Technical Regulatory Services (Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division). These overlaps exist **by default** (devolution incomplete), not by design.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MHSD has been **institutionally established** with a defined organizational structure, provincial field offices, and an operational budget of P782.5M under BAA 85 (including P733.6M for the Housing and Human Settlement Development Program). It has begun exercising programmatic functions — developing housing plans, coordinating with LGUs on land use, formulating People's Plans for beneficiary communities, and planning Housing One-Stop Processing Centers. BAA 85 requires MHSD to geo-tag all infrastructure projects and submit quarterly status reports. No standalone legislation has been enacted, but three PBs have been filed (PB 92, PB 68, PB 141) and three parliamentary resolutions have signaled legislative attention.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three potential advantages:

   (1) **Conflict-sensitive design** — BAA 13 Sec. 1 explicitly targets ***"victims of conflicts and atrocities, and Internally Displaced Persons (IDPs)"*** — a mandate the national framework does not carry. MHSD's housing programs are designed for a **post-conflict context** with populations that include Marawi IDPs, clan-conflict displaced families, and communities in formerly contested areas.

   (2) **Cultural and linguistic fit** — MHSD can deliver services in Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, and Yakan, and design housing models that reflect **Bangsamoro cultural patterns** (extended family compounds, community layout preferences, proximity to *masjid*).

   (3) **Shari'ah-compliant housing finance potential** — MHSD can develop *murabaha*, *ijarah*, and *musharakah mutanaqisah* housing finance models that Pag-IBIG and SHFC do not offer. This requires a standalone code to operationalize.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the **absence of a standalone Bangsamoro Housing Code**. Without it:

   - MHSD cannot exercise **independent regulatory authority** over housing development and subdivision — DHSUD retains this function.
   - **Eviction and demolition procedures** suited to BARMM's post-conflict context cannot be codified.
   - **Shari'ah-compliant housing finance** has no legal framework.
   - **IDP resettlement standards** specific to BARMM's displacement context cannot be enacted.
   - The **jurisdictional boundary** with DHSUD and NHA remains undefined.
   - **Socialized housing standards** cannot be adapted to BARMM's economic conditions (price ceilings, lot areas, building standards).

   Enactment of a **Bangsamoro Housing and Human Settlements Code** would remove this constraint.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a **comprehensive housing infrastructure** over three decades:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 7279 (UDHA) with multiple amendments (RA 10884, RA 9397) provides a complete regulatory framework. RA 7835 establishes shelter financing. RA 11201 creates the departmental structure.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: DHSUD (policy and regulation), NHA (direct implementation), SHFC (community mortgage), NHMFC (secondary mortgage), Pag-IBIG/HDMF (housing loans) — a multi-agency ecosystem with decades of operational experience.
   - **Regulatory systems**: subdivision and condominium approval processes, housing license-to-sell issuance, compliance monitoring, balanced housing enforcement, eviction safeguard procedures.
   - **Housing finance**: Pag-IBIG administers the largest housing loan portfolio in the Philippines, with online application systems and nationwide service offices [UNVERIFIED: fund base figure needs verification against HDMF annual report].

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in four critical areas:

   (1) **No Shari'ah-compliant housing finance**: Pag-IBIG, SHFC, and NHMFC charge interest on all housing loans. Muslim beneficiaries in BARMM must either accept interest-bearing loans (violating the *riba* prohibition) or forego government housing finance entirely. No national housing finance institution offers *murabaha*, *ijarah*, or *musharakah mutanaqisah* alternatives.

   (2) **No conflict-sensitive housing standards**: The national framework assumes stable communities with functioning land titling systems. BARMM faces **contested lands**, **clan-owned territories without formal titles**, **IDP populations in prolonged displacement**, and **communities in areas with no cadastral surveys**. RA 7279's eviction and resettlement standards do not account for these realities.

   (3) **Underserviced region**: NHA's presence in BARMM has historically been limited compared to Luzon and Visayas regions. DHSUD regulatory services are concentrated in metropolitan areas. Housing development in BARMM's island provinces (Basilan, Tawi-Tawi) receives minimal national attention.

   (4) **Cultural misalignment**: National housing models (medium-rise buildings, row housing) may not fit Bangsamoro cultural preferences for community layouts, extended-family compound arrangements, and proximity to communal and religious spaces.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: **eviction and demolition safeguards** (Art. XIII, Sec. 10 requirements implemented by RA 7279); **building standards** (National Building Code compliance); **basic housing quality standards** (structural safety, sanitation, access).
   - **Adapt**: **socialized housing price ceilings and lot areas** — adapt to BARMM's lower income levels and land availability; **balanced housing requirements** — adjust ratios for BARMM's development context; **regulatory approval system** — take DHSUD's framework and add MHSD's Housing One-Stop Processing Centers (BAA 13 Sec. 3(q)); **resettlement standards** — add conflict-specific provisions (clan mediation, IDP priority, contested land resolution).
   - **Replace**: **housing finance models** — build Shari'ah-compliant alternatives (*murabaha*, *ijarah*, *musharakah mutanaqisah*) entirely distinct from Pag-IBIG's interest-based system; **institutional framework** — MHSD replaces DHSUD's regulatory role within BARMM; **People's Plan methodology** — MHSD's BAA 13 Sec. 3(p) already provides for this, but a standalone code should make it the required planning approach rather than an optional one.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Housing has a **significant Shari'ah dimension** primarily through **housing finance**. The conventional housing finance system — mortgage loans with interest payments — constitutes *riba* under Islamic law. In BARMM, where the majority population is Muslim, a housing program that only offers interest-bearing financing effectively excludes observant Muslims from government housing assistance. Shelter is recognized as a **necessity** (*daruriyyat*) in Islamic jurisprudence — one of the five essential interests (*maqasid al-shari'ah*: life, religion, intellect, lineage, property) that the law must protect. A housing framework that fails to provide Shari'ah-compliant access to shelter fails this fundamental obligation.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. IX, Sec. 9 or Art. V, Sec. 2(cc). However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact laws on Shari'ah matters, including commercial law: ***"The Parliament has the power to enact laws governing commercial and other civil actions not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083, as amended."*** Housing finance transactions (sale, lease, mortgage, partnership) are commercial in nature and are not covered by PD 1083 (which governs personal status, marriage, divorce, succession, and property). Parliament therefore has express authority to legislate Shari'ah-compliant housing finance under this provision.[^22]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Five Islamic legal principles directly govern housing legislation:

   (1) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — housing provision for the homeless and displaced serves the common good. Legislation creating housing programs is justified under this principle.

   (2) **Prohibition of *riba*** — the central Shari'ah constraint on housing finance. Conventional mortgage lending is *riba* regardless of the rate. A Bangsamoro Housing Code must provide alternatives.

   (3) ***Murabaha*** (cost-plus financing) — the government purchases/constructs housing and resells at a disclosed markup, payable in installments. The most common Islamic home financing model globally.

   (4) ***Ijarah muntahia bi al-tamlik*** (lease-to-own) — the government retains ownership, leases to beneficiaries at affordable rates, and transfers title upon completion. Avoids interest entirely.

   (5) ***Musharakah mutanaqisah*** (diminishing partnership) — the government and beneficiary co-own the home; the beneficiary gradually buys out the government's share. No interest is charged.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) governs property rights for Muslims but does not specifically address housing finance or settlement programs. Its relevance is **limited** to property disputes between Muslim parties. No Bangsamoro legislation on Shari'ah-compliant housing finance has been enacted. No known *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address housing finance models, though Islamic banking and finance opinions on *murabaha* and *ijarah* are directly analogous.[^23]

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro housing legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is a **dual-track system**: a Bangsamoro Housing Code should provide **both conventional and Shari'ah-compliant housing finance models** as distinct options. Beneficiaries choose their track. The **Shari'ah-compliant track** would require: (a) financing structures limited to *murabaha*, *ijarah*, and *musharakah mutanaqisah*; (b) prohibition of *riba*-bearing transactions for the Shari'ah track; (c) a **Shari'ah compliance review mechanism** — through the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* or a Shari'ah advisory panel within MHSD or the housing finance entity. Compliance should be **optional**, not mandatory — not all BARMM residents are Muslim, and even Muslim beneficiaries may elect conventional financing.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national housing law and policy?**

A. BARMM has **broad legislative authority** to diverge because: (1) the BOL **expressly mandates** a housing agency (Art. IX, Sec. 9) — this is congressional authorization for a distinct institutional framework; (2) **no consistency requirement** with national housing law is imposed — Art. IX, Sec. 9 lacks any "consistent with," "in accordance with," or "subject to" qualifier referencing RA 7279 or DHSUD; (3) the BOL is itself a **congressional act** (RA 11054). As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL places no national law consistency requirement on housing. As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), the constitutional floor is Art. XIII, Sec. 9-10 (housing and anti-eviction) and Art. III, Sec. 1 (due process/equal protection).

   The **limits of divergence** are defined by the **constitutional floor** established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B):
   - Art. XIII, Sec. 9's mandate for ***"decent housing and basic services...at affordable cost"*** cannot be undermined. BARMM can go **above** this floor (more protective, more affordable) but not **below** it.
   - Art. XIII, Sec. 10's anti-eviction protections are **absolute minimums** — a Bangsamoro Housing Code cannot permit eviction or demolition in circumstances where the Constitution prohibits it.
   - Due process (Art. III, Sec. 1) requires any housing regulatory action to provide **notice, hearing, and appeal**.

   The Shari'ah dimension from the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) provides an **additional basis for divergence** — Shari'ah-compliant housing finance models (*murabaha*, *ijarah*, *musharakah mutanaqisah*) are a distinctly Bangsamoro innovation that the national system does not provide and that the BOL's authority supports.

   In practice, BARMM can: establish an **independent housing regulatory system** under MHSD replacing DHSUD functions; create **Shari'ah-compliant housing finance models**; set **different socialized housing standards** adapted to BARMM economic conditions; establish **conflict-specific resettlement procedures** for IDPs; and develop a **Bangsamoro shelter financing framework** that integrates the Special Development Fund.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For housing beneficiaries within BARMM**: They could gain rights not available under RA 7279 — particularly **Shari'ah-compliant housing finance** and **conflict-sensitive resettlement standards**. The risk is if the Bangsamoro code provides **fewer protections** (e.g., weaker eviction safeguards) — this could face constitutional challenge under Art. XIII, Sec. 10.

   (2) **For housing developers**: Developers operating in **both BARMM and non-BARMM areas** face **dual compliance** — MHSD standards within BARMM, DHSUD standards outside. The Bangsamoro code should include **mutual recognition** provisions for developer licensing.

   (3) **For Pag-IBIG members**: Pag-IBIG operates nationally. BARMM members should retain full access to Pag-IBIG benefits. A Bangsamoro housing finance entity should **complement**, not replace, Pag-IBIG — adding Shari'ah-compliant options alongside conventional Pag-IBIG loans.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. The BAA governs within BARMM; national law applies suppletorily where the BAA is silent. RA 7279's **eviction and demolition safeguards** carry **constitutional weight** (implementing Art. XIII, Sec. 10) — the BAA cannot provide lesser protections. DHSUD's regulatory functions within BARMM would transfer to MHSD for housing regulation. NHA would likely continue nationally-funded projects (Marawi rehabilitation) under coordination with MHSD.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Three primary overlaps exist: (1) **housing regulation** — DHSUD retains subdivision approval, housing development licensing, and CLUP review authority that should reside with MHSD under Art. V, Sec. 2(cc); (2) **housing project implementation** — NHA implements projects in BARMM (particularly Marawi rehabilitation) without a formal coordination framework with MHSD; (3) **housing finance** — Pag-IBIG operates nationally but has no Shari'ah-compliant track for BARMM members. Legislative or intergovernmental action is needed to: (a) transfer DHSUD's regulatory functions to MHSD through a BAA and implementing MOA; (b) establish an NHA-MHSD coordination protocol for nationally-funded projects; (c) create a Bangsamoro housing finance mechanism.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Three pending PBs address portions of the gap: **PB No. 92** (Bangsamoro Housing Office) addresses institutional creation but may be superseded by BAA 13's MHSD establishment. **PB No. 68** (solar energy in socialized housing) addresses energy sustainability but not the core regulatory gap. **PB No. 141** (IDP resettlement framework) addresses conflict-specific housing needs but not the comprehensive housing regulatory code. **No comprehensive Bangsamoro Housing Code has been filed.** The critical gap — a single comprehensive housing and human settlements code covering regulation, finance, standards, and institutional powers — remains entirely unaddressed by pending legislation.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 11 (Infrastructure), Target 6.5.1 states: ***"Equitable access to affordable and decent housing for vulnerable sector and low-income families improved."*** Chapter 9 (Social Services) identifies the **Housing and Resettlement Program** as a priority project. BDP data shows BARMM has a **38.88% housing backlog** — 281,686 households without access to affordable and decent housing (2020 PSA data). The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 4 (Responsive Social Services)**, depends on housing provision as a basic social service for conflict-affected and disaster-displaced populations.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **Bangsamoro Housing Code**: MHSD cannot **independently regulate** housing development — developers must still comply with DHSUD standards not calibrated for BARMM. The **38.88% housing backlog** (281,686 households) cannot be addressed through MHSD's BAA 13 general mandate alone — the regulatory framework for socialized housing production, developer obligations, and financing mechanisms requires standalone legislation. **Shari'ah-compliant housing finance** cannot be offered — excluding observant Muslim families from government housing assistance. **IDP resettlement** for Marawi and other conflict-affected populations lacks a BARMM-specific legal framework. The **BDP Target 6.5.1** for equitable housing access depends on institutional infrastructure that BAA 13's general mandate alone cannot provide. The **Marawi IDP crisis** — the most visible housing challenge in BARMM — operates under national frameworks (NHA, Task Force Bangon Marawi) without an integrated Bangsamoro housing law to ensure MHSD's role in long-term resettlement planning.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does the Marawi siege displacement affect this power's exercise?**

A. The 2017 Marawi siege created the **largest internal displacement crisis** in BARMM history — a large-scale displacement [UNVERIFIED: exact displacement figures must be verified against DSWD/UNHCR records]. Art. IX, Sec. 9's express reference to ***"victims of conflicts and atrocities"*** was drafted with this reality in mind. The Marawi rehabilitation housing program is primarily implemented by **NHA** through **Task Force Bangon Marawi** — a nationally-led effort, in part because no Bangsamoro Housing Code defines its authority over resettlement and rehabilitation. **Res. No. 476** (urging shelter lease extensions for Marawi IDPs) demonstrates that Parliament views this as an unresolved crisis. A comprehensive housing code should include **Marawi-specific transition provisions** — ensuring MHSD assumes primary responsibility for long-term housing and resettlement in rehabilitated areas.

**Q. How does this power interact with power (bbb) Urban land reform and land use?**

A. Power (bbb) — ***"urban land reform and land use"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(bbb)) — is closely related. Housing development requires land. Land use regulation determines where housing can be built. MHSD's Technical Regulatory Services include an **Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division** (BAA 13 Sec. 9) — indicating that land use regulatory functions are within MHSD's institutional scope. The relationship between power (cc) (housing) and power (bbb) (land use) should be clarified in a standalone code: MHSD should be the lead agency for **housing-related land use** (socialized housing zones, resettlement sites, housing development regulation), while broader land use planning and agricultural land conversion may involve other agencies (BPDA, MENRE, MAFAR).

**Q. What is the relationship between MHSD and DHSUD — will one replace the other within BARMM?**

A. BAA 13 Sec. 2 designates MHSD as ***"the primary agency for the management of housing, human settlement, and urban development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** DHSUD under RA 11201 is the primary **national** housing policy and regulatory body. When a Bangsamoro Housing Code is enacted, **MHSD should replace DHSUD within BARMM** for housing regulation, subdivision approval, and housing development licensing. DHSUD would retain authority only over: (a) developers operating across BARMM and non-BARMM areas, (b) nationally-funded programs requiring DHSUD oversight, and (c) policy coordination between BARMM and the national housing framework. A **transition MOA** between MHSD and DHSUD should govern the transfer of regulatory authority, pending cases, and data.

**Q. How does Art. IX, Sec. 9's authorization to "directly solicit and receive assistance" from donors affect MHSD's fiscal independence?**

A. This provision gives MHSD a **unique fiscal power** that most BARMM ministries do not have. Art. IX, Sec. 9 authorizes the housing agency to ***"directly solicit and receive assistance, donations, aids, and grants from donors for its housing, settlements, and livelihood programs."*** This bypasses the normal budgetary process for donor assistance — MHSD can directly engage with foreign and domestic donors (subject to Art. XII, Sec. 26 requirements for foreign grants needing Presidential clearance). This is critical for housing because: (a) the scale of BARMM's housing backlog (281,686 households) exceeds what the annual block grant can address; (b) international donors (UNHCR, UNDP, World Bank, JICA) have active programs in BARMM targeting displacement and housing; and (c) the **Special Development Fund** (Art. XIV) is time-limited. MHSD's donor solicitation authority positions it to **supplement** government appropriations with development assistance — but a standalone code should establish accountability standards for donor-funded housing programs.

**Q. What housing finance options does the Bangsamoro Government have beyond conventional mortgage lending?**

A. As described in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), three Shari'ah-compliant models are available: *murabaha* (cost-plus sale), *ijarah muntahia bi al-tamlik* (lease-to-own), and *musharakah mutanaqisah* (diminishing partnership). Beyond Shari'ah-compliant models, additional options include: (a) **community mortgage** (adapted from SHFC's model) — organized communities collectively secure financing for land acquisition and housing; (b) **sweat equity programs** — beneficiaries contribute labor to housing construction, reducing costs; (c) **land banking** — MHSD acquires land in advance of development (authorized by BAA 13 Sec. 3(r)) and develops it for socialized housing; (d) **public-private partnerships** (authorized by BAA 13 Sec. 3(e)) — private developers construct housing under MHSD standards with government land or financing support; (e) **Special Development Fund** (Art. XIV) — housing as a rehabilitation and development expenditure. A Bangsamoro Housing Code should define the legal framework for all these options.
