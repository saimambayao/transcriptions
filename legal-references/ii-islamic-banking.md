# (ii) Islamic Banking and Finance

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ii)** — "Islamic banking and finance;"

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over Islamic banking and finance as an enumerated power under Article V, Section 2(ii).[^1] This power receives extensive elaboration in Article XIII, Sections 31 through 34, making it one of the more detailed financial sector provisions in the organic law. Section 31 directs the Bangsamoro Government to encourage the establishment of banks and financial institutions — both conventional and Islamic — including "an Islamic window in domestic and foreign conventional banks" and "offshore banking units of foreign banks within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, and in accordance with the principles of the Islamic banking system."[^2]

Section 32 establishes the framework for developing the Islamic banking and finance system. It requires the Bangsamoro Government, the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP), the Department of Finance (DOF), and the National Commission on Muslim Filipinos (NCMF) to "jointly promote the development of an Islamic banking and finance system, to include, among others, the establishment of a Shari'ah Supervisory Board and the promotion and development of Shari'ah-compliant financial institutions."[^3] The BSP determines the type of organizational structure for the Shari'ah Supervisory Board. Crucially, Section 32 imposes a national law consistency requirement: "The operation of Islamic banks, Shari'ah-compliant financial institutions and other institutions performing similar functions shall be subject to the power of supervision of the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas."[^4]

Section 33 provides for an Islamic banking unit in the BSP, "headed and staffed by qualified Islamic banking experts."[^5] Section 34 defines the functions of the Shari'ah Supervisory Board — monitoring Shari'ah compliance in banking and finance transactions, issuing Shari'ah products, and issuing fatwas regarding products and practices of banks and other institutions.[^6] Board members must have knowledge of both Islamic jurisprudence and conventional banking and finance. The BOL also directs the Parliament to "enact laws that promote the growth of Islamic finance such as those that promote tax incentives and ensure tax neutrality of Islamic finance transactions."[^7]

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

No standalone BAA for Islamic banking and finance has been enacted. The MFBM exercises the financial policy dimension of this power through its BAA 13 mandate, but no dedicated institutional framework for Islamic banking regulation exists within the Bangsamoro Government.[^8] The power listed in the BAA-to-Power map shows zero BAAs implementing Islamic banking and finance, making it one of the completely unlegislated enumerated powers.

At the national level, Republic Act No. 11439 (Islamic Banking Act of 2019) establishes the regulatory framework for Islamic banks in the Philippines, with the BSP serving as the primary regulator.[^9] The Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank of the Philippines, originally created by Republic Act No. 6848 (1990), operates as the country's lone Islamic bank. BOL Article XII, Section 37(e) charges the Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board with determining "the participation of the Bangsamoro Government in the Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank of the Philippines."[^10]

Parliamentary resolutions signal engagement with Islamic finance. Resolution 24 commended the passage of the Islamic Banking bill (which became RA 11439 at the national level).[^11] Resolution 130 directed the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta to issue a fatwa on halal conduct of public officials, touching on Shari'ah governance. Resolution 200 encouraged adoption of the Bangsamoro Halal Ecosystem Program. Resolution 470 congratulated the appointee as CEO of Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank, indicating BARMM's interest in the institution's leadership.

Pending bills include Bill 337 (establishing a mechanism for Waqf management, creating a Bangsamoro Waqf Commission) and Bill 383 (establishing a Bangsamoro Social Equity Investment Fund that is Shari'ah-compliant). Bill 164 proposes requiring halal labeling in fast food restaurants, and Bill 268 provides for a Halal Consumers Act — both touching on the halal economy that supports Islamic finance development.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The national law relationship is the most explicitly defined among the financial sector powers. Article XIII, Section 32 places Islamic banks under BSP supervision — this is not merely a consistency requirement but a direct subordination of Islamic banking regulation to the national central bank.[^12] The BSP determines the organizational structure of the Shari'ah Supervisory Board. The BSP's regulatory authority over Islamic banks is reinforced by RA 11439, which creates the comprehensive national framework for Islamic banking including licensing, governance, capital requirements, and Shari'ah compliance mechanisms.

Section 33's provision for an Islamic banking unit within the BSP means the specialized regulatory capacity is housed at the national level, not within the Bangsamoro Government. The Bangsamoro Government's role under the BOL is primarily promotional and developmental — joint promotion with BSP, DOF, and NCMF; reviewing market environment policies; promoting investor awareness; and enacting tax incentives. The regulatory function (licensing, supervision, prudential standards) belongs to the BSP.

The Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board (Art. VI, Sec. 4; Art. XII, Sec. 37) serves as the coordination mechanism for financial sector matters, including the Bangsamoro Government's participation in Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank and the determination of BARMM's share in financial operations within its jurisdiction.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

Despite the BOL's detailed provisions on Islamic banking and finance, no Bangsamoro legislation has been enacted for this power. This is a significant gap given that the BOL explicitly directs the Parliament to "enact laws that promote the growth of Islamic finance such as those that promote tax incentives and ensure tax neutrality of Islamic finance transactions."[^13]

A Bangsamoro Islamic Finance Promotion Act would implement this BOL mandate. It could establish: tax incentives for Islamic finance transactions in BARMM (ensuring that the double-transfer structure of many Islamic finance products does not result in double taxation); a Shari'ah Supervisory Board for BARMM financial transactions (complementing the national board under BSP); regulatory incentives for conventional banks to open Islamic windows in BARMM; an institutional framework for promoting Shari'ah-compliant microfinance, particularly for agriculture and small enterprises; and a consumer protection framework for Islamic finance products.

Bill 337's Waqf Commission proposal addresses a critical gap in Islamic endowment management. Waqf (Islamic endowment) is a major vehicle for community-directed investment in Muslim societies, and the absence of a regulated Waqf framework in BARMM means endowment assets are managed informally without governance standards, transparency requirements, or dispute resolution mechanisms. A Bangsamoro Waqf Act would codify Waqf registration, management standards, and the institutional mandate of a Waqf Commission.

Bill 383's Shari'ah-compliant social equity investment fund represents another legislative opportunity — a government investment vehicle structured in accordance with Islamic finance principles that could channel public investment toward BDP development priorities while maintaining Shari'ah compliance.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

Islamic banking and finance are addressed in the BDP 2023-2028 Chapter 7 (Economic Development), which aims to develop the financial sector in BARMM, promote Shari'ah-compliant financial products, and expand access to banking services in underserved areas.[^14] Without Islamic finance legislation providing tax incentives and regulatory clarity, the BDP's financial inclusion targets remain constrained by the limited availability of Islamic banking products in the region. The BDP's emphasis on microfinance and cooperative development also depends on Shari'ah-compliant financial products that allow Muslim constituents to access credit without violating Islamic prohibitions on interest (riba).
