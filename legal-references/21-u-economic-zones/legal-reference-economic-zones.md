# (u) Economic Zones, Industrial Centers, and Free Ports

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(u)** — ***"Economic zones, industrial centers, and free ports;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(u) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Intergovernmental coordination — Art. XIII, Sec. 28: ***"the Bangsamoro Government and the National Government shall cooperate on customs, immigration, and quarantine services including the attendant international commitments thereto."*** Goods consumed outside zones subject to ***"all relevant national taxes."*** No express "consistent with" or "in accordance with" national law qualifier for the zone-creation power itself. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT)** — with **Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA)** as attached agency (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 6, Sec. 22-32) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bureau of Customs (national), Bureau of Immigration (national), PEZA (national — transitional until BEZA is fully operational), Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA), Ministry of Finance and Budget and Management (MFBM), Ministry of Labor and Employment (MOLE), MENRE, MAFAR, MOPW, MTC |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 3 (Spatial Development Strategy); Chapter 13 (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None by specific title. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 28 mandates the establishment of a ***"Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority"*** and authorizes Parliament to provide ***"such additional powers and functions"*** as needed. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over economic zones, industrial centers, and free ports?**

A. Article V, Section 2(u) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"economic zones, industrial centers, and free ports"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This grant is elaborated in extraordinary detail by Art. XIII, Sec. 28, which dedicates four paragraphs to defining the scope, institutional mechanism, intergovernmental relationship, incentive framework, and territorial requirements for this power. The BOL's treatment of economic zones is among the most architecturally complete of any enumerated power — it provides not merely authority but a detailed implementation blueprint.[^1][^2]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 28 is the primary elaborating provision. It establishes five components:

   (1) **Zone-creation authority and BEZA mandate** (para. 1): The Bangsamoro Government ***"may establish economic zones, industrial estates, and free ports"*** and ***"a Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority which shall have similar powers as those of the Philippine Economic Zone Authority."*** Parliament may grant BEZA ***"such additional powers and functions ... as may be necessary to meet the special circumstances of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^2]

   (2) **Jurisdictional transfer** (para. 2): Once BEZA is created, PEZA ***"shall no longer create any other economic zone within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** Existing PEZA entities transfer to BEZA with grandfathered benefits.[^2]

   (3) **Intergovernmental cooperation** (para. 3): Customs, immigration, and quarantine cooperation through the intergovernmental relations mechanism, with a ***"one (1) year"*** operationalization timeline from zone establishment.[^2]

   (4) **Dual incentive regime** (para. 3 cont.): Zone enterprises are entitled to national incentives and BARMM-specific incentives, with national taxes imposed on goods consumed outside zones.[^2]

   (5) **Free port requirements and transfer** (para. 4): Free ports must be ***"contiguous or adjacent to a seaport or airport,"*** with area coverage subject to Parliament-enacted criteria. Existing ARMM free ports are ***"hereby transferred to the Bangsamoro Government."***[^2]

   Additionally, Art. XIII, Sec. 24 provides the general trade and industry framework, Art. XII, Sec. 8 authorizes tax exemptions and incentives, and Art. XII, Sec. 9 defines the taxes BARMM cannot impose — which constrains the incentive design space.[^3][^4]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. The BOL does not name a specific code (unlike the "Bangsamoro cooperative code" or "Education Code"). However, Art. XIII, Sec. 28 contains **four operative mandates** that require legislation: (1) the creation of BEZA (institutional legislation); (2) the establishment of economic zones, industrial estates, and free ports (zone-specific legislation); (3) the provision of fiscal incentives (incentives legislation); and (4) criteria for free port area coverage ***"as the Parliament may provide in a law for that purpose."***[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. XIII, Sec. 28 mandates the creation of the ***"Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority"*** with powers similar to PEZA. **BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 6, Sec. 22-32** established BEZA as an attached agency of MTIT, with a nine-member Board chaired by the MTIT Minister, an Executive Director with Director II rank, and comprehensive operational powers.[^2][^5]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Four limitations:

   (1) **Intergovernmental cooperation requirement**: Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 3 requires cooperation with the National Government on customs, immigration, and quarantine — these functions remain national but must be coordinated for zone operations.[^2]

   (2) **National tax proviso**: Goods consumed and services rendered ***"outside the established economic zones"*** are subject to ***"all relevant national taxes."*** Zone-based tax benefits do not extend beyond zone boundaries.[^2]

   (3) **Free port location**: Free ports must be ***"contiguous or adjacent to a seaport or airport"*** — a geographic restriction not applicable to economic zones or industrial estates.[^2]

   (4) **Fiscal power limitations**: Art. XII, Sec. 9 excludes 12 categories of taxes from BARMM's taxing power (income tax, customs duties, excise taxes, VAT, etc.), constraining the types of incentives the Bangsamoro Government can offer. BARMM cannot grant exemptions from taxes it does not impose.[^4]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to economic zones?**

A. Four categories:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates this universal boundary.[^7]

   (2) **National Economy and Patrimony (Art. XII)**: Art. XII, Sec. 1 establishes that the State shall ***"develop a self-reliant and independent national economy effectively controlled by Filipinos."*** Art. XII, Sec. 2 vests in the State full control over the ***"exploration, development, and utilization of natural resources."*** These provisions mean BARMM zone legislation cannot contravene Filipino-first economic policies or national sovereignty over natural resources.[^8]

   (3) **Foreign Investment Limitations (Art. XII, Sec. 10)**: ***"The Congress shall, upon recommendation of the economic and planning agency, when the national interest dictates, reserve to citizens of the Philippines or to corporations or associations at least sixty per centum of whose capital is owned by such citizens ... certain areas of investments."*** BARMM zones cannot unilaterally override the Foreign Investment Negative List — zone incentives must operate within the nationality restrictions set by national law.[^9]

   (4) **Equal Protection and Due Process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: Zone registration, regulation, incentive grant, and revocation must satisfy procedural due process (notice, hearing, right to appeal).[^10]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees: (1) **equal protection** — zone investors are entitled to fair treatment under law; (2) **due process** — regulatory action must provide notice, hearing, and appeal; (3) **non-impairment of obligations** (Art. III, Sec. 10) — zone incentive agreements, once executed, are constitutionally protected from arbitrary revocation. The BOL's grandfathering clause for PEZA entities (Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2) operationalizes this constitutional protection.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 1 mandates that economic development must be ***"effectively controlled by Filipinos"*** and that the State ***"shall promote industrialization and full employment."*** These constitutional mandates align with the BOL's economic zone authority — zones serve industrialization and employment objectives. The Congress has fulfilled its constitutional role by enacting the BOL (RA 11054), which authorizes BEZA's creation — giving BARMM zone legislation a congressional basis.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for economic zones?**

A. The national framework provides: **RA 7916** (Special Economic Zone Act) as amended by **RA 8748** creates PEZA with powers to designate, regulate, and administer special economic zones. **RA 11534** (CREATE Act) establishes the national investment incentive structure, replacing the prior regime with a tiered system administered by the Fiscal Incentives Review Board (FIRB). **RA 10863** (CMTA) governs customs procedures for zone operations. **RA 7227** and **RA 9400** provide the model for free port governance (Subic Bay, Clark). Together, these laws establish: a **zone registration system**, **investor incentive framework**, **customs territory designation**, **free port governance**, and an **institutional infrastructure** (PEZA) that BEZA is modeled upon.[^11][^12][^13]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national economic zone and trade laws apply pending standalone Bangsamoro legislation:

   (1) **RA 7916** (as amended by RA 8748) — PEZA retains zone-creation and regulatory authority in BARMM until BEZA is fully operational. Applies transitionally.[^11]
   (2) **RA 11534** (CREATE Act) — national incentive framework applies directly to BARMM zone enterprises. Art. XIII, Sec. 28 guarantees zone enterprises receive national incentives.[^12]
   (3) **RA 10863** (CMTA) — customs procedures apply directly. Art. XIII, Sec. 28 requires customs cooperation.[^13]
   (4) **RA 11659** (amended Public Service Act) — liberalized foreign ownership rules apply to zone enterprises.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. Three national institutions: (1) **PEZA** — retains zone-creation and regulatory authority until BEZA is fully operational and the jurisdictional transfer under Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2 is completed; (2) **Bureau of Customs** — retains customs authority including zone-related customs operations, subject to intergovernmental cooperation; (3) **Bureau of Immigration** — retains immigration authority, subject to intergovernmental cooperation for zone operations. PEZA's authority will **terminate** for new zone creation once BEZA is created, but Customs and Immigration authority is **ongoing** — requiring permanent intergovernmental coordination.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT)** is the lead ministry, with the **Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA)** as its attached agency. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 6, Sec. 22-32 defines BEZA's institutional status, board composition, functions, powers, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter V allocates MTIT's operational budget, which includes BEZA funding.[^5][^14]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for economic zones. BEZA was created through **BAA 13** (Administrative Code) as part of the omnibus code, not through a dedicated enabling act. No economic zones, industrial estates, or free ports have been designated through Bangsamoro legislation.

**Q. What are BEZA's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 6, Sec. 22-32 defines BEZA comprehensively:

   **Declaration of Policy** (Sec. 22): ***"It is hereby declared the policy of the Bangsamoro Government to actively encourage, promote and accelerate a sound and balanced industrial, economic and social development of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in order to provide jobs to people, increase productivity and income, and improve the quality of living condition through the establishment of special economic zones and free ports in suitable and strategic locations in the Bangsamoro and through measures that shall effectively attract legitimate and productive foreign investments."***[^5]

   **BEZA Board** (Sec. 23): A nine-member Board chaired by the MTIT Minister, with the BEZA Executive Director (Director II rank) as Vice-Chairperson, and Ministers of MOLE, MENRE, MAFAR, MOPW, MTC, MFBM, and the BPDA Director-General as members.[^5]

   **Board Functions and Powers** (Sec. 24) — seven enumerated functions:
   - (a) ***"Set the general policies on the establishment and operations of the ECOZONES, industrial estates, export processing zones, free trade zones, and the like;"***
   - (b) ***"Review proposals for the establishment of ECOZONES based on the set criteria under Section 25 below and endorse to the Chief Minister the establishment of the ECOZONES, industrial estates, export processing zones, free trade zones and the like. Thereafter, it shall facilitate and assist in the organization of said entities;"***
   - (c) ***"Regulate and undertake the establishment, operation and maintenance of ports, airfields, utilities, other services and infrastructure in the ECOZONE, such as heat, light and power, water supply, telecommunication, transport, print and media facilities, toll roads and bridges, port services, and to fix just, reasonable and competitive rates, charges and fees therefor;"***
   - (d) ***"Approve the annual budget of the BEZA and the ECOZONE development plans, with concurrence of the Chief Minister, which budget shall be appropriated by the Bangsamoro Parliament."***
   - (e) ***"Issue rules and regulations to implement the relevant provisions of this Code"***
   - (f) ***"Exercise its powers and functions as provided for in this Code"***
   - (g) ***"Render annual reports to the Chief Minister and the Bangsamoro Parliament"***[^15]

   **General Powers and Functions of the Authority** (Sec. 25) — ten enumerated powers:
   - (a) To operate, administer, manage, and develop the ECOZONE
   - (b) To **register, regulate, and supervise** enterprises in the ECOZONE
   - (c) To coordinate with LGUs and exercise **general supervision** over development, plans, activities and operations
   - (d) To **construct, acquire, own, lease, operate and maintain** facilities and infrastructure — including light/power, water, telecommunications, transportation, warehouses, roads, bridges, ports, and airport operations
   - (e) To **create, operate and/or contract** agencies and functional units
   - (f) To adopt a corporate seal; make contracts; sue and be sued
   - (g) To coordinate formulation of development plans
   - (h) To coordinate with **BPDA, MTIT, LGUs, and appropriate government agencies**
   - (i) To **monitor and evaluate** development requirements and recommend incentives
   - (j) To **raise funds, borrow, and issue bonds** within legal limitations[^16]

   **Zone Creation Criteria** (Sec. 26): Parliament creates zones based on BEZA and BPDA recommendation, using eight criteria: (1) identification in Bangsamoro Development Plan as growth center; (2) existing infrastructure; (3) water and electric power availability; (4) vacant lands for development; (5) labor force availability; (6) incremental advantage over existing zones; (7) strategic location; and (8) smuggling-control feasibility.[^17]

   **Executive Director Powers** (Sec. 27): Overall coordinator with 13 specific powers, including safeguarding properties, investor acceptance, development planning, and emergency measures.[^18]

   **ECOZONE Administration** (Sec. 28-30): Each zone administered by an Executive Committee (Executive Director representative, Administrator, Deputy Administrator), with an Advisory Council chaired by the Provincial Governor. Privately-owned ECOZONEs retain autonomy but are monitored by BEZA.[^19]

   **Personnel** (Sec. 31): The BEZA Board provides for organization and staffing, with exclusive and final authority over promotions, transfers, assignments, and reassignments of BEZA officers.

   **Investigation and Inquiries** (Sec. 32): Upon written formal complaint under oath, BEZA or the ECOZONE Administrator may ***"inquire into the conduct of firms or employees of the ECOZONE and to conduct investigations, and for that purpose may subpoena witnesses, administer oaths, and compel the production of books, papers, and other evidences."***[^20]

**Q. What can BEZA NOT do without standalone legislation beyond BAA 13?**

A. While BAA 13 provides comprehensive institutional provisions, it does not provide:
   - A **Bangsamoro Investment Incentives Code** defining the specific fiscal incentives BARMM offers zone enterprises (tax holidays, income tax rates, duty-free importation, etc.)
   - **Customs territory designation** for free ports — creating a distinct customs territory requires coordination with the Bureau of Customs and specific legislation
   - **Free port establishment** — the BOL's criteria (adjacent to seaport/airport, Parliament-enacted area criteria) require a specific enabling law for each free port
   - **Intergovernmental cooperation protocols** with the Bureau of Customs and Bureau of Immigration for zone operations
   - **PEZA jurisdictional transfer protocol** — the automatic transfer under Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2 requires implementing legislation defining how transferred entities, grandfathered benefits, and regulatory transition are handled

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No enacted parliamentary resolutions directly address economic zone legislation. This is a notable absence given the BOL's detailed mandate in Art. XIII, Sec. 28.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. MTIT is the appropriate lead ministry for economic zones, and BEZA's attachment to MTIT is consistent with the national model (PEZA is attached to the Department of Trade and Industry). BAA 13 explicitly establishes this relationship (Sec. 5: ***"The following agency and board are attached to the Ministry: 1.) Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority"***). The BEZA Board's multi-ministry composition (MOLE, MENRE, MAFAR, MOPW, MTC, MFBM, BPDA) ensures cross-sectoral coordination for zone development.[^5]

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Two institutions:
   - **BEZA** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 6, Sec. 22-32; BAA 85, Chapter V) — created and funded, though no zones have been designated
   - **Bangsamoro Halal Board** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 7, Sec. 33-36) — relevant as the certifying body for halal industrial zones

   **Not yet created**: No ECOZONE Executive Committee, Advisory Council, or zone-specific administration exists because no zones have been designated.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 28 mandates: (1) **BEZA** — created through BAA 13 (fulfilled); (2) **economic zones, industrial estates, and free ports** — none designated through legislation (unfulfilled); (3) **intergovernmental cooperation mechanism** for customs, immigration, and quarantine — not formally established (unfulfilled). BEZA exists institutionally but has no zones to administer.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Beyond BEZA (which exists), two additional institutional needs emerge: (1) a **BEZA-Bureau of Customs Liaison Office** — to operationalize the Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 3 customs cooperation mandate within each future zone; and (2) **ECOZONE Executive Committees and Advisory Councils** for each designated zone (these are defined by BAA 13 Sec. 28-30 but cannot be created until zones exist). A standalone BEZA enabling act should also consider a **Bangsamoro Investment Promotion Board** or equivalent mechanism for marketing zones to foreign investors — particularly within BIMP-EAGA.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **PEZA** is the primary national agency. PEZA retains authority to create and regulate economic zones within BARMM because BEZA has not yet been operationalized to the point where the Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2 jurisdictional transfer has been completed in practice. The **Bureau of Customs** maintains customs operations, and the **Bureau of Immigration** handles immigration services. No formal intergovernmental cooperation mechanism under Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 3 has been established.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for economic zones?**

A. No PEZA-designated special economic zones currently operate within BARMM's territorial scope. PEZA's operational presence in the region is therefore minimal. The Bureau of Customs operates through the Collection District covering BARMM ports (Polloc, Jolo, Zamboanga) — these customs operations would need to be coordinated for any future zone establishment. The **Board of Investments** (BOI) under DTI administers investment incentive registration for enterprises in BARMM, and the **Fiscal Incentives Review Board** (FIRB) administers CREATE Act incentives — both would need to coordinate with BEZA once zones are established.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **incomplete in law and non-operative in practice**. Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2 mandates jurisdictional transfer from PEZA to BEZA upon BEZA's creation. BAA 13 created BEZA institutionally, which technically triggers the transfer prohibition (PEZA may no longer create new zones in BARMM). However, the **practical transfer** of any existing PEZA entities and the full operationalization of BEZA's regulatory functions have not occurred. Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 4 provides a **self-executing transfer** of existing ARMM free ports to the Bangsamoro Government — but no implementing legislation defines how this transfer is administered.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. The concurrent operation area is **latent** rather than active: (1) if PEZA were to attempt to create new zones in BARMM, a jurisdictional dispute would arise under Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2; (2) the Bureau of Customs and Bureau of Immigration will necessarily operate concurrently with BEZA within any future zones — this is by design (Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 3 mandates cooperation, not replacement); (3) the FIRB under the CREATE Act will retain authority over national incentive registration even for BARMM zone enterprises — potentially requiring dual registration with FIRB and BEZA.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. BEZA has been **institutionally created** through BAA 13 with a defined organizational structure, Board composition, Executive Director position, and operational framework. It has received **budget allocation** through BAA 85. However, **no economic zones, industrial estates, or free ports have been designated or established** through Bangsamoro legislation. The institutional framework exists; the operational exercise of the power has not begun.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three unique **potential** advantages (unrealized pending legislation):

   (1) **Halal industrial zones**: BAA 13 creates both BEZA (Sec. 22-32) and the Bangsamoro Halal Board (Sec. 33-36) under MTIT. This institutional co-location enables halal-certified production zones — a category PEZA does not offer. Given BARMM's location within BIMP-EAGA and proximity to Muslim-majority markets (Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei), halal zones could be a significant competitive advantage.

   (2) **BIMP-EAGA gateway positioning**: BARMM's geographic location makes it the natural gateway for cross-border trade with BIMP-EAGA partners. Art. XIII, Sec. 26 mandates regulation of barter trade with BIMP-EAGA. Zones at BARMM seaports and airports could integrate barter trade, halal production, and Islamic finance in ways no national zone can.

   (3) **Post-conflict development mandate**: BAA 13, Sec. 22 frames zone policy explicitly around ***"providing jobs to people, increase productivity and income, and improve the quality of living condition"*** — a post-conflict economic development mandate absent from PEZA's primarily export-oriented framework.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. Three interconnected constraints:

   (1) **No standalone enabling legislation**: BAA 13 creates BEZA but does not establish any zones or define the investment incentive package. A dedicated BEZA enabling act or Bangsamoro Investment Incentives Code is needed.

   (2) **No fiscal incentive framework**: Without a codified incentive package, BEZA cannot compete with PEZA-designated zones elsewhere in the Philippines. Zone enterprises need certainty about tax holidays, income tax rates, and duty-free importation.

   (3) **No intergovernmental cooperation protocol**: The Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 3 cooperation mandate for customs, immigration, and quarantine has not been formalized. Without this protocol, zones cannot be made ***"fully operational within one (1) year from their establishment."***

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a **decades-old, fully operational zone ecosystem**:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 7916 (1995, as amended by RA 8748) provides the comprehensive framework — zone creation, PEZA powers, enterprise registration, incentive grants, administrative structure. This has been supplemented by the CREATE Act (RA 11534), the CMTA (RA 10863), and individual zone-specific enabling laws (RA 7227 for Subic, RA 9728 for Bataan, RA 7903 for Zamboanga, etc.).
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: PEZA has been operational for over 30 years with a portfolio of over 400 registered economic zones nationwide, thousands of registered enterprises, specialized divisions for zone development, investor services, compliance monitoring, and incentive administration.
   - **Regulatory systems**: PEZA maintains an enterprise registration system, zone performance monitoring, compliance examination, environmental management frameworks, and incentive verification — coordinated with FIRB under the CREATE Act.
   - **Free port governance models**: Subic Bay (SBMA), Clark (CDC/CEZA), and Bataan (AFAB) provide three distinct governance models for free port operations — each with decades of operational experience.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM in three critical areas:

   (1) **No Islamic finance integration**: PEZA's investment promotion framework is entirely conventional. There are no provisions for Shari'ah-compliant investment financing, Islamic banking services within zones, or halal industrial zone categories.

   (2) **No conflict-sensitive zone design**: PEZA zones are designed for economically stable areas with existing industrial infrastructure. BARMM's post-conflict context — IDP populations, ex-combatant communities, contested lands, destroyed infrastructure — requires a zone model focused on economic reconstruction, not just export competitiveness.

   (3) **Minimal BARMM presence**: PEZA has no designated zones within BARMM territory. The national zone system has effectively bypassed the region — reinforcing the economic marginalization that the BOL's zone authority is designed to reverse.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: Enterprise **registration procedures** (documentation, compliance requirements, monitoring), **zone governance standards** (Board structure, advisory councils, administrator functions — already largely replicated in BAA 13), and **environmental management** frameworks for zone operations.
   - **Adapt**: The **fiscal incentive structure** — take the CREATE Act's tiered system as a baseline and layer BARMM-specific incentives (halal zone premiums, Islamic finance access, BIMP-EAGA trade facilitation). Adapt the **zone creation criteria** — BAA 13 Sec. 26 already provides eight criteria but should incorporate post-conflict development needs and BIMP-EAGA gateway positioning.
   - **Replace**: The **investment promotion model** — build a BARMM-specific investment promotion mechanism targeting Muslim-majority markets (BIMP-EAGA, Gulf states, Turkey, Malaysia) with Islamic finance options and halal certification. Replace the **purely export-oriented** model with a **post-conflict development** model that prioritizes employment, community benefit-sharing, and infrastructure rebuilding.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. The Shari'ah dimension is **indirect but strategically significant**. Economic zone operations are primarily commercial and regulatory in nature, and classical Islamic law does not specifically address special economic zones. However, three Shari'ah principles inform zone development: (1) ***maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) supports zone creation as a means of generating employment and reducing poverty; (2) the prohibition of ***riba*** (interest/usury) is relevant to zone financing structures and the Islamic banking services that should be available within zones; and (3) the ***halal*** industry integration dimension, where zones provide platforms for halal-certified production facilities, connecting economic zone power (u) with the halal program mandate under Art. XIII, Sec. 30.[^6]

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. XIII, Sec. 28 (the economic zone provision). However, the BOL's Islamic banking and finance provisions (Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34) are directly relevant to zone operations — Islamic banking services within economic zones and Shari'ah-compliant investment financing for zone enterprises. Art. XIII, Sec. 30 mandates halal program development, which creates the basis for **halal industrial zones** — a BARMM-specific zone category absent from the national PEZA framework.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Three principles apply:

   (1) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — zone establishment serves the common good by creating employment, attracting investment, and enabling post-conflict economic development.

   (2) **Prohibition of *riba*** — zone financing and investment services should include Shari'ah-compliant options (see power (ii) Islamic banking and finance). A BEZA enabling act or Bangsamoro Investment Incentives Code should require Islamic banking services within zones.

   (3) ***'Adl*** (justice/equity) — zone benefits must be distributed equitably, with surrounding communities receiving employment, infrastructure, and social service benefits, not just zone enterprises and external investors.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. No Shari'ah legislation directly governs economic zones. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) does not address commercial zones. However, the BOL's Islamic banking provisions (Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34) provide the framework for Shari'ah-compliant financial services within zones. The Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 7, Sec. 33-36) provides the institutional framework for halal zone certification.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro economic zone legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is **principled integration**, not mandatory compliance: (1) require **Islamic banking services** to be available within every economic zone, alongside conventional banking; (2) designate certain zones as **halal industrial zones** where all production meets halal certification standards, coordinated with the Bangsamoro Halal Board; (3) ensure zone investment promotion materials address **Shari'ah-compliant investment structures** to attract investors from Muslim-majority countries, particularly within BIMP-EAGA. Mandatory Shari'ah compliance for all zone enterprises would be impractical and would deter non-Muslim investors.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can BARMM diverge from national economic zone law?**

A. BARMM has **significant divergence authority** because: (1) the BOL **expressly authorizes** Parliament to grant BEZA ***"such additional powers and functions ... as may be necessary to meet the special circumstances of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** — this is explicit congressional authorization for divergence; (2) no "consistent with" or "in accordance with" national law qualifier is imposed on the zone-creation power itself; (3) the BOL creates a **separate institutional framework** (BEZA replacing PEZA) rather than subordinating BARMM to the national system.

   The **limits of divergence** are:
   - **Customs, immigration, and quarantine**: These remain national functions requiring cooperation, not replacement. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), BARMM cannot establish independent customs operations.
   - **National tax proviso**: As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), goods consumed outside zones are subject to national taxes. Zone-based tax benefits do not extend extraterritorially.
   - **Fiscal power limitations**: As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XII, Sec. 9 excludes 12 categories of taxes from BARMM's taxing power.
   - **Constitutional floor**: As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), foreign investment nationality restrictions and the Filipino-first economic policy apply within zones.

   In practice, BARMM can: establish zones with **different criteria** than PEZA zones; create **halal industrial zones** (a category PEZA does not have); offer **BARMM-specific incentives** layered on top of national incentives; grant BEZA **broader powers** than PEZA; and create free ports with **Parliament-defined area criteria** distinct from national models.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) **For zone investors**: They could gain a **dual incentive layer** — national CREATE Act incentives plus BARMM-specific incentives — making BARMM zones potentially more attractive than PEZA zones elsewhere in the Philippines.

   (2) **For existing PEZA entities**: Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2 guarantees they ***"shall continue to enjoy the benefits granted ... by the Philippine Economic Zone Authority"*** — a grandfathering clause that protects vested rights during transition.

   (3) **For constitutional vulnerability**: A challenge is unlikely to succeed because the BOL is a congressional act that expressly authorizes the differentiation.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. A standalone BEZA enabling act or Bangsamoro Investment Incentives Code would govern within BARMM. RA 7916 (PEZA Act) would cease to apply for new zone creation in BARMM per the self-executing Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2 transfer. RA 11534 (CREATE Act) would continue to apply — Art. XIII, Sec. 28 guarantees zone enterprises receive national incentives as a **floor**. RA 10863 (CMTA) would continue to apply for customs operations. Constitutional provisions always apply.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Three areas of jurisdictional uncertainty:

   (1) **PEZA-BEZA transition**: As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2 triggers PEZA's exit when BEZA is "created." BAA 13 created BEZA in 2021. Technically, the prohibition on new PEZA zone creation in BARMM was triggered at that point. However, BEZA is not yet operational enough to receive transferred entities or exercise regulatory authority independently. A standalone BEZA enabling act must include **transitional provisions** defining the effective date of transfer and grandfathering mechanism.

   (2) **Customs operations**: The BOL requires cooperation but does not define how customs procedures within zones will be administered. Will the Bureau of Customs operate a branch within each zone? Will BEZA have delegated customs authority? This requires an intergovernmental agreement.

   (3) **Incentive overlap**: Zone enterprises may be eligible for both national CREATE Act incentives (from FIRB) and BARMM incentives (from BEZA) — creating potential administrative complexity or double-counting. An IGR protocol should define how the two incentive layers are administered.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. No pending PBs specifically dedicated to this power have been identified. This is the most significant gap — not even a filed bill exists for a BEZA enabling act, a Bangsamoro Investment Incentives Code, or specific zone designation legislation.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) identifies economic zones as **critical infrastructure for investment attraction** and economic diversification. Chapter 3 (Spatial Development Strategy) positions zones as **anchors of spatial development** near strategic seaports and airports. Chapter 13 (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) identifies zone revenues as a pathway to **fiscal self-reliance** beyond block grant dependence. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, depends on private sector-led investment through zones to build an independent economic base that reduces dependence on national government transfers.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without standalone zone legislation: BEZA **cannot designate or establish** any economic zone, industrial estate, or free port. No investor incentive package exists to attract investment. The PEZA jurisdictional transfer cannot be operationalized. No free port has been established despite the self-executing transfer of existing ARMM free ports. The BDP's economic development targets — particularly investment attraction, employment generation, and fiscal diversification — depend on zone infrastructure that **does not yet exist**. BARMM's strategic location within **BIMP-EAGA** (Brunei Darussalam-Indonesia-Malaysia-Philippines East ASEAN Growth Area) offers natural advantages for zone development that are being forfeited every year without enabling legislation. This is among the highest-impact legislative gaps in the entire BOL.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the relationship between BEZA and PEZA — when does the jurisdictional transfer take effect?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 2 provides that ***"once the Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority is created,"*** PEZA ***"shall no longer create any other economic zone within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** BAA 13 created BEZA in 2021. Technically, the jurisdictional transfer for **new zone creation** was triggered at that point — PEZA should no longer create new zones in BARMM. However, the **practical transfer** of existing PEZA entities and regulatory functions has not occurred because BEZA lacks the operational infrastructure, incentive framework, and intergovernmental agreements needed to receive transferred entities and exercise regulatory authority. A standalone BEZA enabling act should include **transitional provisions** defining: (a) the effective date of transfer for existing PEZA entities; (b) the grandfathering mechanism for PEZA-granted benefits; (c) the protocol for regulatory transition (how BEZA assumes PEZA's registration, compliance, and examination functions); and (d) a timeline for completing the transition.[^2][^5]

**Q. What happened to the existing ARMM free ports that the BOL transfers?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 4 provides that ***"the administration of existing free ports in the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao are hereby transferred to the Bangsamoro Government."*** This is a **self-executing** provision — the transfer occurred upon the BOL's effectivity. However, no Bangsamoro legislation has been enacted to define how the Bangsamoro Government administers these transferred free ports, what governance structures apply, or how the transition is managed. [UNVERIFIED: the operational existence and current status of specific ARMM-era free ports within BARMM territory has not been confirmed from source files.]

**Q. What makes BARMM economic zones potentially distinct from national PEZA zones?**

A. BARMM zones have four unique characteristics absent from the national framework:

   (1) **Halal industrial zones**: BAA 13 creates both BEZA (Sec. 22-32) and the Bangsamoro Halal Board (Sec. 33-36) under the same ministry (MTIT). This institutional co-location enables **halal-certified production zones** — a category PEZA does not offer. Given BARMM's location within BIMP-EAGA and proximity to Muslim-majority markets, halal zones could be a significant competitive advantage.

   (2) **BIMP-EAGA positioning**: Art. XIII, Sec. 26 mandates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"regulate barter trade with the member-countries of the Brunei Darussalam-Indonesia-Malaysia-Philippines East ASEAN Growth Area."*** Economic zones positioned at BARMM seaports and airports serve as gateways for **cross-border trade** with BIMP-EAGA partners.

   (3) **Post-conflict development focus**: BAA 13, Sec. 22 frames zone policy around providing ***"jobs to people, increase productivity and income, and improve the quality of living condition"*** — an explicitly post-conflict economic development mandate absent from PEZA's primarily export-oriented framework.

   (4) **Islamic finance integration**: BARMM's Islamic banking and finance framework (Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34) could enable **Shari'ah-compliant investment financing** within zones, attracting investors from Muslim-majority countries who prefer Islamic finance structures. See the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) for details on Islamic finance integration.

**Q. How does this power interact with other BOL powers?**

A. Economic zones intersect with multiple powers:
   - **(e) Barter trade and countertrade** — zones at seaports facilitate BIMP-EAGA barter trade per Art. XIII, Sec. 26
   - **(n) Creation of sources of revenues** — zone revenues contribute to fiscal autonomy per Art. XII
   - **(oo) Power sector investments** — zone operations require reliable power supply per Art. XIII, Sec. 36
   - **(pp) Public utilities** — zone transportation and telecommunications per Art. XIII, Sec. 38-39
   - **(qq) Public works and infrastructure** — zone infrastructure development
   - **(rr) Quarantine** — zone quarantine services per Art. XIII, Sec. 28, para. 3
   - **(zz) Trade and industry** — zones serve the broader trade and industry power per Art. XIII, Sec. 24
   - **(ii) Islamic banking and finance** — Shari'ah-compliant investment financing within zones per Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34

**Q. How does the BOL's zone-creation mechanism compare to the national mechanism?**

A. Under the national framework (RA 7916), the President creates economic zones upon PEZA recommendation, with proclamation authority. Under the BOL and BAA 13, the **Bangsamoro Parliament** creates zones ***"based on the evaluation and recommendation of the Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority and the Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority's feasibility and engineering study"*** (Sec. 26). This means zone creation in BARMM is a **legislative act**, not an executive proclamation — requiring greater deliberation and democratic accountability but also a potentially slower process. The eight-criteria evaluation framework (Sec. 26) provides objective standards that parallel PEZA's criteria while adding BARMM-specific considerations (Bangsamoro Development Plan alignment, strategic location within the region).[^17]
