# (d) Ancestral Domain and Natural Resources

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(d)** — ***"Ancestral domain and natural resources;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(d) — enumerated power; Art. X, Sec. 20(3) of the 1987 Constitution expressly lists ***"Ancestral domain and natural resources"*** as one of the legislative powers the organic act may provide for autonomous regions |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XIII, Sec. 8 (***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution"***); Art. ## Legal Briefer

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over ancestral domain and natural resources?**

A. Article V, Section 2(d) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"ancestral domain and natural resources"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is one of the broadest powers in the BOL, encompassing: **natural resource exploration, development, and utilization** (Art. XIII, Sec. 8); **protected area management** (Art. XIII, Sec. 8-9); **fossil fuel and uranium co-management** (Art. XIII, Sec. 10); **mining regulation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 13-16); **ancestral domain recognition and native titles** (Art. IX, Sec. 1-3); and **natural resource revenue** (Art. XII, Sec. 34-36).[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Eleven provisions elaborate this power:

   (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 8** — the core natural resources grant, subject to the Constitution. Includes surface and subsurface rights, inland and coastal waters, renewable and nonrenewable resources. Also mandates protected area declarations.[^2]

   (2) **Art. XIII, Sec. 9** — transfer of existing protected area management from DENR to the Bangsamoro Government within two years.[^2]

   (3) **Art. XIII, Sec. 10** — joint exercise with National Government (DOE) for fossil fuels and uranium. Competitive and transparent process required; bona fide residents receive preferential rating.[^2]

   (4) **Art. XIII, Sec. 11** — preferential rights of bona fide residents for natural resource access; existing rights respected until expiration.[^2]

   (5) **Art. XIII, Sec. 12** — express legislative mandate for IP natural resource rights, including revenue share and FPIC. **Fulfilled through BAA 64.**[^2]

   (6) **Art. XIII, Sec. 13-16** — mines and minerals authority, FTAA endorsement, small-scale mining regulation, and Bangsamoro mining policy.[^2]

   (7) **Art. IX, Sec. 1** — transitional justice mechanism addressing unjust dispossession of territorial rights. **Addressed through BAA 89.**[^3]

   (8) **Art. IX, Sec. 3** — eight enumerated IP rights including native titles (*fusaka inged*), revenue share, and MIAP creation mandate.[^3]

   (9) **Art. XII, Sec. 34-36** — revenue sharing from natural resources: full pertinence to BARMM for non-fossil-fuel resources; equal sharing for fossil fuels/uranium; LGU and IP community shares.[^4]

   (10) **Art. VI, Sec. 8** — Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board creation mandate. **Fulfilled through BAA 31.**[^5]

   (11) **Art. XIII, Sec. 1** — general economic framework requiring the Bangsamoro economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution."***[^6]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **multiple legislative mandates**:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 8 — Parliament ***"shall pass a law to establish protected areas"*** — **not yet fulfilled.**

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 12 — Parliament ***"shall enact a law recognizing the rights of indigenous peoples ... in relation to natural resources"*** — **fulfilled through BAA 64.**

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 16 — mining policies ***"shall be established by the Parliament"*** — **not yet fulfilled** (no Bangsamoro Mining Code enacted).

   (4) Art. XII, Sec. 36 — IP share in natural resource revenues to be detailed ***"by a law to be passed by the Parliament"*** — **not yet fulfilled** as a standalone law (BAA 64 addresses IP rights generally but does not detail the revenue sharing mechanism and percentages).

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Five categories of limitations:

   (1) **Constitutional limitation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 8): ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution"*** — the Regalian doctrine (1987 Constitution, Art. XII, Sec. 2) remains supreme; natural resources are owned by the State.

   (2) **Joint exercise requirement** (Art. XIII, Sec. 10): fossil fuels and uranium require joint exercise with the National Government through DOE. Neither government acts alone.

   (3) **Constitutional and national law limitation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 13): mines and minerals authority is ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution and national laws."***

   (4) **FTAA endorsement** (Art. XIII, Sec. 14): the Bangsamoro Government ***"may endorse to the President"*** FTAAs — Presidential approval required.

   (5) **IPRA floor** (Art. IX, Sec. 3, last paragraph): ***"This Organic Law shall not in any manner diminish the rights and benefits of the non-Moro indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region under the Constitution, national laws, particularly Republic Act No. 8371."***

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to ancestral domain and natural resources?**

A. Five constitutional provisions define the boundaries:

   (1) **Regalian Doctrine (Art. XII, Sec. 2)**: ***"All lands of the public domain, waters, minerals, coal, petroleum, and other mineral oils, all forces of potential energy, fisheries, forests or timber, wildlife, flora and fauna, and other natural resources are owned by the State."*** The State retains ownership; the BOL grants authority to explore, develop, and utilize — not to transfer ownership.

   (2) **Land Classification (Art. XII, Sec. 3)**: ***"Lands of the public domain are classified into agricultural, forest or timber, mineral lands, and national parks."*** Only Congress can classify public lands — the BOL's Sec. 17 allows Bangsamoro to ***"recommend"*** classification to the President (see power (j)).

   (3) **IP Ancestral Lands (Art. XII, Sec. 5)**: ***"The State ... shall protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities to their ancestral lands."***

   (4) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: Expressly includes ***"Ancestral domain and natural resources"*** as a legislative power for autonomous regions.

   (5) **Presidential Prerogative (Art. XII, Sec. 2)**: The President may enter FTAAs with foreign corporations — Bangsamoro endorsement power under Art. XIII, Sec. 14 operates within this presidential authority.

**Q. What is the constitutional floor for this power?**

A. The constitutional floor comprises: (a) **state ownership** of natural resources — the Bangsamoro Government exercises management and utilization authority but does not acquire ownership; (b) **IPRA protection** — indigenous rights cannot be diminished; (c) **60-40 Filipino ownership rule** for natural resource utilization corporations; (d) **Congressional prerogative** over land classification and FTAA framework. Any Bangsamoro legislation must operate above this floor.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for this area?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 5 mandates the State to protect IP ancestral lands. Art. XII, Sec. 4 mandates Congress to determine forest land and national park limits. Art. X, Sec. 20(3) expressly authorizes the organic act to provide legislative powers over ***"Ancestral domain and natural resources."***

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide?**

A. The national framework establishes comprehensive systems:
   - **RA 8371 (IPRA)** — ancestral domain recognition, CADT/CALT issuance, FPIC, NCIP authority.
   - **RA 7942 (Mining Act)** — mineral agreements (MPSAs, FTAAs, EPSAs), environmental compliance, Mines and Geosciences Bureau (MGB).
   - **RA 7076 (Small-Scale Mining Act)** — community-based small-scale mining, Minahang Bayan.
   - **RA 7586/RA 11038 (NIPAS/Expanded NIPAS)** — protected area management, PAMBs.
   - **EO 79 (2012)** — mining sector reforms, new mineral agreement moratorium.
   - **EO 192 (1987)** — DENR as lead national agency for environment, natural resources, and land management.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM?**

A. Three national agencies operate:
   - **NCIP** — retains functions over ancestral domain delineation and CADT processes, creating jurisdictional tension with MIAP (see Res. No. 38).
   - **DENR/MGB** — manages mining permits and environmental compliance pending Bangsamoro Mining Code.
   - **DOE** — joint exercise partner for fossil fuels and uranium per Art. XIII, Sec. 10.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM for this power?**

A. All national laws listed above apply suppletorily pending standalone Bangsamoro legislation, except: (a) **IPRA** applies directly as a constitutional-level floor that cannot be diminished; (b) **RA 7942** applies for mining subject to ***"the provisions of the Constitution and national laws"*** qualifier in Art. XIII, Sec. 13.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible?**

A. **MENRE** is the lead ministry for natural resources. See the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A) for the BOL basis. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III defines MENRE's mandate, powers, and functions.[^7]

**Q. What are MENRE's functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III, Sec. 3 enumerates **19 powers and functions** including:
   - (a) ***"Recommend the enactment of regional laws relative to the development, utilization, regulation and conservation of the natural resources and energy"***
   - (d) ***"Exercise supervision and control over forest lands, agricultural lands, mineral resources, and national parks"***
   - (f) ***"Undertake exploration, assessment, classification and inventory"*** of natural resources
   - (h) ***"Undertake geological surveys of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region including its territorial waters"***
   - (j)(1) ***"Accelerate cadastral surveys, land use planning and public land titling"***
   - (j)(3) ***"Support forest-based industries"***
   - (j)(4) ***"Expedite mineral resources surveys, promote the production of metallic and non-metallic minerals"***
   - (k) ***"Regulate the development, disposition, exploration and utilization of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region's forests, land, mineral and energy resources"***
   - (l) ***"Exercise administration and supervision of all lands of the public domain"***
   - (n) ***"Recommend policies governing the grant of rights, privileges, and concessions over the exploration, development and utilization of fossil fuels ... and uranium"***[^7]

MENRE's organizational structure includes: **Land Management Services** (Surveys Division; Land Utilization Development Division); **Mines and Geosciences Services** (Geosciences Division; Mines and Technology Management Division; Mines Safety Environment and Social Development Division); **Forest Management Services**; **Environment Management Services**; **Biodiversity Ecosystems Research and Development Services**; and **Energy Management and Development Services**.[^7]

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Which office within MENRE handles ancestral domain specifically?**

A. The **Land Management Services** through its Surveys Division handles land surveys relevant to ancestral domain delineation. However, **ancestral domain recognition and CADT issuance** are primarily handled by **MIAP** — specifically the **Bureau of Ancestral Domains/Native Title** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII, Sec. 10). BAA 13 gives MIAP the power to ***"Issue Certificates of Ancestral Domain Title (CADTs) to IPs in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region and register such CADTs in the appropriate Register of Deeds, as may be provided by law passed by the Bangsamoro Parliament"*** (Sec. 3(c)).[^8]

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. Three national agencies maintain operational presence:
   - **NCIP** — continues to process ancestral domain claims and CADT applications within BARMM, creating overlap with MIAP's mandate. Resolution No. 38 protested NCIP's delineation process in Maguindanao.
   - **DENR (through MGB)** — manages mining permits, mineral agreements, and environmental compliance certificates (ECCs) within BARMM pending Bangsamoro Mining Code.
   - **DOE** — exercises joint authority over fossil fuel and uranium exploration per Art. XIII, Sec. 10.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has BARMM accomplished?**

A. BARMM has: (a) **institutionally established** MENRE and MIAP under BAA 13; (b) created the **Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board** through BAA 31; (c) enacted **BAA 64 (IP Act)** fulfilling the Art. XIII, Sec. 12 mandate; (d) enacted **BAA 89 (Transitional Justice)** addressing ancestral domain restitution; (e) MENRE operates field offices (PENREOs and CENREOs) in each province. However, **no standalone Mining Code, Environmental Code, or Protected Areas Act** has been enacted.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back?**

A. Three constraints: (1) **No Bangsamoro Mining Code** — mining regulation depends on national law (RA 7942) and MENRE's general mandate; (2) **No Protected Areas Act** — the BOL mandates this legislation but it has not been enacted; (3) **NCIP-MIAP jurisdictional overlap** on ancestral domain — no formal protocol established for transferring NCIP functions to MIAP. Additionally, the **joint exercise protocol with DOE** for fossil fuels has not been formalized.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national system has: (a) **MGB** — a specialized bureau with regional offices, geological survey capacity, and mining permit processing infrastructure; (b) **NCIP** — institutional infrastructure for ancestral domain delineation including survey teams and CADT processing systems; (c) **DENR's NIPAS system** — established protected area management boards (PAMBs) with operational budgets; (d) **DOE's petroleum service contract system** — a mature framework for fossil fuel exploration.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. (1) **NCIP's Bangsamoro disconnect** — NCIP was designed for non-Moro indigenous communities; its processes do not account for Bangsamoro Moro ancestral domain claims, *fusaka inged* (native titles under customary Moro law), or the political dimensions of land tenure in formerly conflict-affected areas. (2) **Mining permit bottleneck** — MGB processing does not account for Bangsamoro Government input or FPIC requirements specific to BARMM IPs. (3) **Protected area management** — existing PAMBs do not integrate Bangsamoro sustainable development priorities.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Islamic law contains well-developed principles on **natural resource stewardship** and **land tenure**, though none are enacted in current BARMM legislation. The concept of *khalifah* (stewardship) positions humans as trustees of natural resources — a framework that aligns with the BOL's sustainable development emphasis. The *hima* (protected reserve) concept provides Islamic precedent for community-managed conservation areas. The prohibition of *israf* (waste/excess) supports environmental protection mandates. These remain *fiqh* principles applicable as persuasive authority or policy guidance, not enacted law.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four *fiqh* principles are directly relevant:

   (1) ***Khalifah*** (stewardship) — natural resources are an *amanah* (trust) from God, to be managed for the benefit of all, including future generations.

   (2) ***Hima*** (protected reserve) — land or resources set aside by governing authority for communal benefit. Provides an Islamic model for protected areas.

   (3) ***Ihya al-mawat*** (revival of dead land) — potential basis for ancestral domain claims through long-standing cultivation. Must be reconciled with the Regalian doctrine and IPRA native title framework.

   (4) **Prohibition of *israf*** (waste/excess) — supports ecological balance requirements in mining and resource extraction.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) does not address natural resources or ancestral domain. **BOL Art. X, Sec. 4** authorizes Shari'ah-based commercial legislation, which could encompass natural resource contracts, but no such law has been enacted. The Shari'ah dimension of this power remains at the *fiqh* principle level — relevant for policy design but not for current legal obligations.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can BARMM diverge from national natural resource law?**

A. The power to diverge varies by sub-area:
   - **General natural resources** (Art. XIII, Sec. 8): ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution"*** only — no "national laws" qualifier. BARMM has **broad** authority to create distinct frameworks for forest management, non-mineral natural resources, and protected areas, constrained only by the constitutional floor (Regalian doctrine, Art. XII, Sec. 2-5).
   - **Mines and minerals** (Art. XIII, Sec. 13): ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution and national laws"*** — **narrower** authority. A Bangsamoro Mining Code must be consistent with the national Mining Act framework, though it may add additional protections.
   - **Fossil fuels and uranium** (Art. XIII, Sec. 10): ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution and national laws"*** plus **joint exercise** — the **most constrained** sub-area. BARMM cannot act unilaterally.
   - **IP rights** (Art. IX, Sec. 3): the IPRA floor ***"shall not ... be diminished."*** BAA 64 can go **above** IPRA protections but not below.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) For a **Bangsamoro Mining Code**: it must meet the constitutional and national law consistency requirement (Art. XIII, Sec. 13) but can impose **additional** environmental protections, community benefit requirements, and Shari'ah-compliant resource-sharing mechanisms. (2) For **ancestral domain**: BAA 64 can provide **stronger** protections than IPRA — broader FPIC requirements, more generous revenue sharing, faster CADT processing — because the constitutional floor only prevents diminishment.

**Q. What cross-references from other themes constrain divergence?**

A. The constitutional limitations established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) (Regalian doctrine, IPRA floor, Presidential FTAA authority) set the outer boundary. The national law consistency requirement established in the **National Law Considerations** section (Section C) (RA 7942 for mining, RA 8371 for IP rights) narrows BARMM's legislative space for mining specifically. The joint exercise requirement in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A) (Art. XIII, Sec. 10) completely constrains fossil fuel authority.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions?**

A. Yes. Three overlaps:
   (1) **NCIP vs. MIAP** on ancestral domain — NCIP (national) and MIAP (BARMM) both claim authority over CADT issuance within BARMM. Resolution No. 38 demonstrates active conflict. No formal jurisdictional protocol has been established.
   (2) **MGB vs. MENRE** on mining — DENR-MGB retains mining permit authority pending Bangsamoro Mining Code.
   (3) **DOE joint exercise** — Art. XIII, Sec. 10 requires joint exercise for fossil fuels but no implementing protocol has been agreed upon.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB No. 310** (Liguasan Marsh Conservation) and **PB No. 352** (Lake Lanao Sustainable Management) address specific natural resource conservation needs. No pending PB addresses a comprehensive Bangsamoro Mining Code, Environmental Code, or Protected Areas Act.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 12** (Environment and Natural Resources) depends directly on this power for sustainable management, biodiversity conservation, and protected area management. **BDP Chapter 7** (Economic Development) depends on mining revenue generation under Art. XII, Sec. 34-36 and resource-based economic diversification. **BDP Chapter 10** (Peace and Security) depends on ancestral domain recognition and conflict resolution.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without standalone legislation: (1) **Mining revenue** cannot be maximized — BARMM lacks its own mining policy framework to attract investment under Art. XII, Sec. 34-36. (2) **Protected areas** lack a Bangsamoro-specific management framework, undermining environmental conservation targets. (3) **IP revenue sharing** under Art. XII, Sec. 36 cannot be operationalized without an implementing law detailing percentages and mechanisms. (4) **NCIP-MIAP overlap** creates administrative confusion that delays CADT processing, undermining BDP Chapter 10 peace and security goals.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does the "joint exercise" requirement for fossil fuels work in practice?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 10 creates a **co-management model**: DOE and the Bangsamoro Government must **jointly** identify prospective contract areas, **jointly** evaluate proponents (with bona fide resident preferential rating), and **jointly** award service contracts. The BOL requires a ***"competitive and transparent process."*** In practice, this means neither government can unilaterally grant fossil fuel exploration rights within BARMM territory — an arrangement without precedent in Philippine governance. No implementing protocol has been agreed upon, leaving this provision unoperationalized.

**Q. What is the relationship between this power and power (ff) Indigenous Peoples' Rights?**

A. Power (d) — ancestral domain and natural resources — and power (ff) — indigenous peoples' rights — are **intertwined but distinct**. Power (d) covers the **resource dimension** (exploration, development, utilization, revenue) while power (ff) covers the **rights dimension** (native titles, self-determination, cultural protection, political participation). BAA 64 bridges both powers, addressing IP rights *in relation to* natural resources. MENRE leads on the resource dimension; MIAP leads on the rights dimension.

**Q. What is the significance of the Art. XII, Sec. 34 revenue provision?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 34 provides that natural resource revenues ***"shall pertain fully to the Bangsamoro Government"*** — this is one of the strongest fiscal provisions in the BOL. For non-fossil-fuel resources (minerals, forests, quarry resources), **100% of government revenues** stay in BARMM. For fossil fuels and uranium, revenues are **shared equally** with the National Government. This creates a powerful incentive for BARMM to develop a mining policy framework — the revenue potential is substantial but cannot be realized without the institutional capacity to attract and regulate investment.

**Q. Why does the BOL require "authority of the President" for cadastral surveys but not for natural resource management?**

A. The distinction reflects the different constitutional bases. Natural resource **management** falls under Art. X, Sec. 20(3) of the Constitution, which expressly lists ***"Ancestral domain and natural resources"*** as an autonomous region legislative power. **Cadastral surveys** relate to land classification, which under the Regalian doctrine remains a Presidential prerogative (see power (h)). The BOL carefully separates what the Bangsamoro Government can do autonomously (manage, explore, develop, utilize natural resources) from what requires Presidential participation (classify public lands, conduct cadastral surveys).
