# (ss) Registration of Births, Marriages, and Deaths

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ss)** — "Registration of births, marriages, and deaths, copies to be furnished the Philippine Statistics Authority;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over the registration of births, marriages, and deaths through Article V, Section 2(ss). Uniquely among the 55 enumerated powers, this provision contains an explicit operational requirement embedded in its text: "copies to be furnished the Philippine Statistics Authority." This built-in intergovernmental coordination requirement makes the civil registration power simultaneously a Bangsamoro function and one that feeds into the national statistical system. The Bangsamoro Government has full authority to establish and manage its own civil registration system — including the forms, procedures, offices, and personnel — but must ensure that the national PSA receives copies of all registrations.[^1]

The scope of this power encompasses the entire civil registration lifecycle: registration of live births, stillbirths, marriages (civil, religious, and Muslim), deaths, and foundlings; issuance of civil registry documents (birth certificates, marriage certificates, death certificates); correction and amendment of civil registry entries; annotation of court orders, legitimation, adoption, and changes of name; and maintenance of civil registry records and archives. For the Bangsamoro, this power is particularly significant because of the Muslim personal law dimension: marriages and divorces under Presidential Decree No. 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) require registration, and the proper recording of Muslim divorces has been identified as a persistent challenge.[^2]

The BOL does not elaborate further on this power in other articles — Article V, Section 2(ss) is both the grant and the sole provision. This makes it one of the most concise BOL power grants, relying on the broad authority of the text itself and the Parliament's general legislative power under Article VII, Section 3 to enact laws within Bangsamoro competencies.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

No standalone BAA exists for civil registration. The power is exercised through the general mandates of MILG under BAA No. 13 (Administrative Code) and through local civil registrars at the municipal and city level under BAA No. 49 (Local Governance Code). Local civil registrars are LGU officials who perform the day-to-day functions of civil registration — accepting documents, registering vital events, issuing certificates, and transmitting copies to the PSA. MILG provides oversight and coordination of the civil registration system across BARMM LGUs.[^3]

The absence of standalone legislation means that the national civil registration framework — primarily RA 3753 (Civil Registry Law) and its implementing rules, RA 9048 (as amended by RA 10172, on administrative correction of civil registry entries), and the PSA's operational guidelines — continues to govern civil registration in the Bangsamoro. Local civil registrars in BARMM follow the same forms, procedures, and reporting requirements as their counterparts in the rest of the Philippines. The Bangsamoro Government has not yet exercised its power to establish a distinct regional civil registration system.

Two parliamentary resolutions signal interest in this area. Resolution 191 directs MILG to implement Muslim Personal Law in civil registry services, recognizing that civil registration in BARMM must accommodate the distinctive requirements of Muslim family law. Resolution 456 urges strict adherence to registration of Muslim divorces, highlighting a specific implementation challenge where divorce decrees issued by Shari'ah courts are not consistently registered in the civil registry.[^4]

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The national law interaction is embedded in the power's text: copies of all civil registration records must be furnished to the PSA. This is not a consistency requirement (the Bangsamoro need not follow national law in designing its registration system) but an information-sharing mandate that ensures the national vital statistics system is complete. The PSA is a constitutional statistical body under the Philippine Statistical Act of 2013 (RA 10625), and maintaining complete civil registration records is a national imperative for population statistics, voter registration, and identity documentation.

RA 3753 (Civil Registry Law of 1930) and RA 9048 (as amended by RA 10172) govern civil registration nationally. In the absence of a Bangsamoro civil registration act, these national laws apply suppletorily in their entirety. Once the Bangsamoro enacts its own civil registration legislation, the national laws would apply only to the extent not covered by the Bangsamoro framework — except for the PSA copy-furnishing requirement, which is a BOL mandate that persists regardless. PD 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) also intersects significantly, as Muslim marriages, divorces, and conversions require civil registration with specific documentation requirements that general civil registry procedures do not always accommodate.[^5]

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The most significant gap is the absence of a Bangsamoro Civil Registration Act. This power has zero standalone legislation — it is exercised entirely through national law applied suppletorily and general mandates under BAA 13 and BAA 49. A dedicated Bangsamoro Civil Registration Act could: establish a unified regional civil registration framework with standards tailored to the Bangsamoro context; integrate Muslim Personal Law registration requirements (marriage, divorce, conversion, revocation of divorce) into the standard civil registration process rather than treating them as exceptions; create a Bangsamoro Civil Registrar General or regional civil registration office under MILG to coordinate, standardize, and digitize civil registration across all LGUs; establish a Bangsamoro civil registry database with digital interconnection to the PSA system; mandate late registration programs targeting the significant unregistered population in conflict-affected and remote areas of BARMM; and establish correction and amendment procedures specific to common issues in the Bangsamoro (transliteration of Arabic names, dual naming conventions, inconsistent recording of Muslim family law events).[^6]

A second gap involves the specific problem of Muslim divorce registration identified in Resolution 456. Shari'ah court divorce decrees are not consistently reflected in the civil registry, creating legal complications for individuals seeking to exercise their rights as single persons (remarriage, property transactions, government documents). A targeted legislative intervention — either within a comprehensive civil registration act or as a standalone Muslim Family Law Registration Act — could establish mandatory reporting protocols between Shari'ah courts and civil registrars, standard forms for Muslim family law events, and timelines for registration.

A third gap concerns the unregistered population. BARMM includes some of the Philippines' most underserved areas in terms of civil registration coverage. Conflict, displacement, geographic isolation, and poverty have created a significant population without birth certificates or other vital documents. A Bangsamoro Civil Registration Outreach and Late Registration Program, mandated by law, could address this through mobile registration units, fee waivers, and simplified procedures for late registration.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 5 (Governance and Institutional Development), targets improved governance systems and public service delivery. Civil registration is a foundational governance function — without accurate vital records, government cannot effectively deliver social services, plan infrastructure, allocate resources, or conduct elections. The absence of a regional civil registration framework means that the BDP's governance targets depend on a system that the Bangsamoro Government does not fully control or manage. The unregistered population represents a governance gap that undermines every other development intervention — individuals without birth certificates cannot access education, healthcare, social protection, or employment in the formal sector.
