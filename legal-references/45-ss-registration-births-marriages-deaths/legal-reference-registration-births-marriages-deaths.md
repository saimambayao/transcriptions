# (ss) Registration of Births, Marriages, and Deaths

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ss)** — ***"Registration of births, marriages, and deaths, copies to be furnished the Philippine Statistics Authority;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(ss) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — the BOL itself embeds a **national reporting obligation** within the grant: copies must be ***"furnished the Philippine Statistics Authority."*** This is one of only a few enumerated powers where the BOL expressly names a national agency within the grant itself, creating a **structural intergovernmental linkage** rather than a mere coordination requirement. The Civil Registry Law (Act No. 3753), the Civil Code (Arts. 407-413), and RA 10625 (PSA Act) establish the national civil registration framework that applies suppletorily. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG)** — exercises general supervision over constituent LGUs, which operate the local civil registries (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VIII). The **local civil registrars** (municipal/city level) are the front-line implementing officers (BAA 49, Art. IX, Sec. 533). |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) — national Civil Registrar-General; Shari'ah Courts — jurisdiction over Muslim personal status (marriage, divorce); Office of the Bangsamoro Attorney General (BAGO) — legal opinions on civil status; LGU offices — barangay secretaries assist registration (BAA 49, Sec. 421) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 5 (Governance and Administration) — local governance systems and service delivery |
| **Priority Code** | Bangsamoro Local Governance Code (BAA 49) — enacted, contains civil registrar provisions |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(ss) grants the Bangsamoro Government the power over ***"Registration of births, marriages, and deaths, copies to be furnished the Philippine Statistics Authority."*** This is an **enumerated power** — one of 55 powers expressly granted to the Bangsamoro Government. The power covers the **administration** of the registration function: receiving, recording, processing, maintaining, and issuing certificates for the three core vital events (births, marriages, deaths). The grant is distinctive among the 55 powers because it **embeds a national reporting obligation within the grant itself** — copies must be furnished to the PSA. This is not a separate intergovernmental coordination requirement (as in Art. VI bodies); it is built into the power's definition.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**
A. Three provisions elaborate on the registration power:

- **Art. VI, Sec. 10** authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to ***"regulate the affairs of its constituent local government units"*** through a Bangsamoro Local Government Code. Since civil registrars are LGU officers, this provision gives the Bangsamoro Government the authority to define the qualifications, powers, and functions of civil registrars — which BAA 49 has now implemented.[^2]
- **Art. X, Sec. 4** grants the Parliament power to ***"enact laws on personal, family, and property law jurisdiction."*** Muslim marriages and divorces — which must be registered — fall within this authority. Parliament may enact legislation governing the registration procedures for Muslim personal status events.[^3]
- **Art. XVIII, Sec. 4(d)** amended specific PD 1083 provisions on Shari'ah court jurisdiction (Arts. 140, 143, 152-154, 164-168) but did **not amend** the PD 1083 marriage solemnization and registration provisions (Arts. 13-26). These remain in full force within BARMM.[^4]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**
A. No. The BOL does not contain an express mandate stating "The Parliament shall enact a civil registration code" or similar. The mandate is **implicit** — Art. VI, Sec. 10 requires a Bangsamoro Local Government Code (enacted as BAA 49), which includes civil registrar provisions. There is no named legislative mandate equivalent to the "Bangsamoro cooperative code" (Art. XIII, Sec. 24) for civil registration.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**
A. No. The BOL does not create a dedicated civil registration agency. The power is exercised through **existing LGU structures** — local civil registrars at the municipal and city level — supervised by the MILG.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**
A. One express condition: copies of registrations must be ***"furnished the Philippine Statistics Authority."***[^1] This creates a **structural constraint** — the Bangsamoro Government cannot operate a closed, independent registration system. All registration data must flow to the PSA as Civil Registrar-General. The PSA's authority is a **retained national function** — the BOL did not devolve the Civil Registrar-General function to any BARMM ministry.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**
A. The universal constraint applies: **Art. X, Sec. 15** — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** Subject-specific provisions include:

- **Art. III, Sec. 1** (Due Process and Equal Protection) — all persons have equal right to civil registration regardless of religion, ethnicity, or legitimacy status. Registration procedures cannot discriminate.
- **Art. III, Sec. 3** (Privacy of Communication) — civil registration records contain sensitive personal information; privacy safeguards are constitutionally required.
- **Art. II, Sec. 12** — the State shall ***"protect and strengthen the family as a basic autonomous social institution"*** and ***"equally protect the life of the mother and the life of the unborn from conception."*** Birth registration gives legal identity and protects family rights.
- **Art. XV, Sec. 1** — the family is ***"the foundation of the nation"*** and marriage is ***"an inviolable social institution."*** Marriage registration gives legal effect to the constitutional protection.
- **Art. XV, Sec. 3** — the State shall ***"defend the right of children to assistance, including proper care and nutrition, and special protection from all forms of neglect, abuse, cruelty, exploitation, and other conditions prejudicial to their development."*** Birth registration is the gateway to child protection services.

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**
A. Every person has the right to **legal identity** — established through birth registration. Every marriage has the right to **legal recognition** — established through marriage registration. Every death must be recorded to settle estate rights and protect survivors. The Constitution does not enumerate a "right to civil registration" as such, but civil registration is the **mechanism** through which constitutional family rights, succession rights, and identity rights are realized. Without registration, constitutional rights are effectively unenforceable.[^5]

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**
A. The Constitution does not mandate specific civil registration legislation. However, **Art. IX-B, Sec. 3** establishes the Civil Service Commission; **Art. IX-D** establishes the Commission on Audit — both apply to BARMM LGU civil registrars as government employees. Critically, the PSA's authority derives from **RA 10625**, which implements the statistical mandate under the constitutional framework for national planning. The PSA's Civil Registrar-General function is a **national institutional function** — not subject to devolution.[^6]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**
A. The national civil registration framework is built on multiple layers:

- **Act No. 3753 (1930)** — the Civil Registry Law: establishes local civil registrars in every municipality, defines registrable events, requires registration within prescribed periods (births: 30 days, marriages: 15 days, deaths: 48 hours), and mandates transmission of duplicate copies to the Civil Registrar-General.[^7]
- **Civil Code of the Philippines (RA 386)**, Arts. 407-413 — specifies what events must be registered in the civil register: births, marriages, deaths, legal separations, annulments, adoptions, changes of name, emancipation, and judicial decrees affecting civil status.[^8]
- **RA 10625 (2013)** — created the PSA as the Civil Registrar-General responsible for maintaining the national civil registration system, including the **Civil Registration and Vital Statistics (CRVS)** system.[^6]
- **RA 9048 (2001) and RA 10172 (2012)** — administrative correction mechanisms allowing civil registrars to correct errors without judicial proceedings.[^9][^10]
- **RA 11055 (2018)** — the Philippine Identification System (PhilSys) Act: relies on civil registration data as foundational identity documents.[^11]
- **RA 11909 (2022)** — provides permanent validity for PSA-issued certificates of live birth, death, and marriage.[^12]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**
A. All of the above national laws apply **directly** in BARMM. BAA 49 has enacted BARMM-specific civil registrar provisions (Sec. 533), but these operate **within** the national framework — not as a replacement. Act No. 3753 and the Civil Code set the registrable events and timelines; RA 10625 governs the PSA's Civil Registrar-General authority; RA 9048/10172 govern administrative corrections. **PD 1083** applies directly for Muslim marriages and divorces, creating the dual-track system.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**
A. The **PSA** exercises authority as Civil Registrar-General — receiving duplicate copies, maintaining the national database (CRS), issuing PSA-authenticated certificates (birth, marriage, death), conducting the CRVS system, and setting standards for registration forms and procedures. PSA also operates **civil registration outlets** (Serbilis centers) in BARMM for certificate issuance. The PSA's authority is **retained** — not devolved to BARMM.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**
A. The **Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG)** exercises general supervision over constituent LGUs, which operate the local civil registries. MILG's mandate under BAA 13, Book VI, Title VIII, Sec. 2 is to ***"exercise general supervision over the constituent local government units of the Bangsamoro Government, and ensure public safety and disaster preparedness, local autonomy, decentralization, and community empowerment."***[^13] Civil registration is implemented at the **LGU level** by municipal/city civil registrars under MILG's supervisory authority.

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**
A. No standalone civil registration code has been enacted. However, **BAA 49** (Bangsamoro Local Governance Code, 2023) contains the most comprehensive BARMM-specific civil registration provisions in Art. IX, Sec. 533. This is the primary enacted legislation governing how BARMM exercises this power.[^14]

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title VIII, Sec. 3 enumerates MILG's powers and functions:

a. ***"Advise the Chief Minister on the Government of the Day's policies, rules, regulations, and other issuances relative to the general supervision of local government units;"***
b. ***"Establish and prescribe rules, regulations, and other issuances implementing laws on general supervision of local government units and on the promotion of local autonomy, and monitor compliance thereof by the said units;"***
c. ***"Provide technical assistance in the preparation of regional legislation affecting local governments units;"***
d. ***"Establish and prescribe plans, policies, programs, and projects to strengthen the administrative, technical, and fiscal capabilities of local government offices and personnel;"***
e. ***"Implement plans, policies, programs, and project to promote public order and safety and disaster preparedness within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao;"***
f. ***"Formulate plans, policies, and programs which will meet regional and local emergencies arising from natural and human-made calamities;"***
g. ***"Enforce its disciplinary authority over elective officials in accordance with the Bangsamoro Local Governance Code enacted by the Parliament;"***
h. ***"Perform other functions as maybe provided by law."***[^13]

Civil registration is not **expressly named** among MILG's functions. The connection is **structural**: civil registrars are LGU officers → MILG supervises LGUs → MILG has indirect authority over civil registration standards and compliance. Function (b) — "establish rules and monitor compliance" — is the basis for MILG involvement in civil registration improvement programs, as recognized by Res. 191.

MILG's organizational structure (BAA 13, Sec. 9) includes the **Operation Management Services** with a **Local Government Supervision Division** and **Local Government Development Division** — these divisions are the logical home for civil registration oversight within MILG.[^15]

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**
A. MILG and the LGUs **cannot**:
- Establish a **Bangsamoro-specific CRVS system** separate from PSA's national system
- Set **different registration timelines or procedures** from those in Act 3753 and the Civil Code
- Create **digital-first registration systems** with legal equivalence to paper-based records (requires legislative authority)
- Establish **penalties** for non-registration or late registration beyond what national law provides
- Create a **Bangsamoro Civil Registration Academy** or mandatory training standards for civil registrars
- Establish **Shari'ah-integrated registration procedures** that harmonize PD 1083 requirements with the general registration system
- Mandate **mobile registration** services for remote, conflict-affected, or IDP communities

BAA 49 provides the institutional structure (civil registrar position, duties), but does not create a comprehensive regulatory framework for how BARMM exercises the full scope of the Art. V, Sec. 2(ss) power.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**
A. Yes. **Res. No. 191** directed the MILG to implement programs strengthening civil registry services in LGUs, with emphasis on Muslim personal law implementation. **Res. No. 456** urged the PSA, the Office of the Civil Registrar-General, and the Shari'ah Courts to enforce strict compliance with Muslim divorce registration procedures. Together, these resolutions identify two critical problems: (1) weak LGU civil registration capacity, and (2) breakdown in the Shari'ah court-to-civil registrar coordination for Muslim personal status events.[^16][^17]

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**
A. **Partially.** MILG is the correct lead for the **LGU supervision** dimension — civil registrars are LGU officers under MILG's general supervision. However, this power involves **three institutional layers** that no single MILG division fully coordinates:

1. **LGU civil registrars** (MILG supervision) — front-line registration
2. **PSA** (national authority) — Civil Registrar-General, national database, certificate authentication
3. **Shari'ah Courts** (judiciary) — issue decrees affecting Muslim personal status (marriage dissolution, divorce)

MILG can supervise the LGU layer but has **no authority** over the PSA or Shari'ah Courts. A fully functional civil registration system in BARMM requires **interagency coordination** that transcends MILG's mandate.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**
A.
- **Lead ministry**: MILG (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VIII) — general supervision over LGUs
- **LGU civil registrars**: mandatory in all municipalities and cities (BAA 49, Sec. 533)
- **Barangay secretaries**: assist in registration of births, deaths, and marriages (BAA 49, Sec. 421(5))
- **Shari'ah Courts**: Circuit Courts and District Courts — issue marriage confirmations and divorce decrees that must be registered
- **National (retained)**: PSA — Civil Registrar-General with Serbilis outlets in BARMM

No Bangsamoro-level civil registration oversight office, quality assurance unit, or vital statistics coordination body exists.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**
A. The BOL does not mandate specific civil registration institutions. However, Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes the Parliament to ***"create offices"*** for various purposes — a Bangsamoro Civil Registration Coordination Office could be created under this general authority. No such office has been created.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**
A. Yes. Effective exercise of this power would benefit from:
- A **BARMM Civil Registration Coordination Office** (under MILG or the Office of the Chief Minister) to serve as the single point of contact between LGU civil registrars, PSA, and Shari'ah Courts
- A **CRVS Quality Assurance Unit** to audit registration completeness, accuracy, and timeliness across BARMM LGUs
- **Mobile registration teams** for conflict-affected areas, IDP communities, and geographically isolated barangays

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**
A. The **PSA** operates as the Civil Registrar-General nationwide, including in BARMM. PSA receives duplicate copies of all registered vital events from local civil registrars, maintains the national **Civil Registration System (CRS)** database, issues authenticated copies of certificates, and sets the forms, procedures, and standards for civil registration nationwide. PSA also conducts periodic **censuses** (population, housing) that depend on civil registration completeness. PSA operates **Serbilis centers** in BARMM for on-the-spot issuance of PSA-authenticated certificates.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**
A.
- **PSA Serbilis outlets** — certificate issuance services
- **PhilSys (national ID) registration** — relies on birth certificates; PSA conducts registration drives in BARMM
- **UNICEF-supported birth registration campaigns** — international development partner programs operating through PSA and LGUs
- **PSA mobile civil registration outreach** — periodic campaigns in underserved areas

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**
A. **Partial.** The BOL devolved the **administration** of civil registration to the Bangsamoro Government through the LGU civil registrar system. BAA 49 now governs civil registrar qualifications and duties in BARMM LGUs. However, the **Civil Registrar-General function** (PSA) is **not devolved** — the PSA retains authority over the national database, certificate authentication, registration standards, and forms. Devolution is therefore **functional** (LGU civil registrars operate under BARMM law) but **architecturally connected** to the national system (PSA remains the apex).

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**
A. Yes:
- **Certificate issuance**: both LGU civil registrars (local copies) and PSA Serbilis centers (authenticated copies) issue birth/marriage/death certificates
- **Registration standards**: PSA sets national forms and procedures; BAA 49 defines BARMM civil registrar duties — potential for misalignment
- **Divorce registration**: Shari'ah courts (Bangsamoro judiciary) issue decrees; local civil registrars (BARMM LGU) process registration; PSA (national) maintains the database — a three-agency chain with documented failures (Res. 456)

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**
A. The BTA Parliament has:
1. **Enacted BAA 49** (Bangsamoro Local Governance Code) with Art. IX establishing the civil registrar as a mandatory LGU position with defined qualifications, powers, and duties — including coordination with PSA
2. **Adopted Res. 191** directing MILG to strengthen civil registry services, recognizing the challenge
3. **Adopted Res. 456** urging compliance with Muslim divorce registration requirements
4. Through MILG, supervised LGU civil registrar operations across BARMM's constituent municipalities and cities

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**
A. BARMM's institutional proximity to the **dual-track registration reality** — PD 1083 for Muslims, Civil Code for others — is a potential strength. BARMM understands the cultural and religious context of Bangsamoro communities where marriages may be solemnized by traditional religious authorities, where Arabic names create transliteration challenges, and where conflict displacement disrupts documentation. **No national agency has this contextual expertise.** However, this strength is **latent** — it has not yet been translated into operational advantage through legislation or programs.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**
A. The **single biggest constraint** is the **absence of a standalone Bangsamoro Civil Registration Act**. Without it:
- MILG cannot prescribe BARMM-specific registration procedures adapted to Muslim personal law
- No penalty framework exists for non-registration or late registration under BARMM law
- No mechanism forces coordination between Shari'ah courts and civil registrars
- No legal basis exists for digital/mobile registration systems
- The dual-track problem (PD 1083 vs. Civil Code) remains unresolved at the operational level

A secondary constraint is **incomplete devolution** — PSA has not fully integrated BARMM-specific protocols into the national CRS, and data-sharing between PSA and BARMM planning bodies is ad hoc rather than systematic.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**
A. The national system has:
- **PSA's Civil Registration System (CRS)** — a digital national database of all registered vital events, operational since the NSO era, now under PSA. Decades of data.
- **Serbilis network** — over 100 outlets nationwide for one-stop certificate issuance
- **Comprehensive legal framework** — Act 3753 + Civil Code + RA 9048 + RA 10172 + RA 10625 + RA 11909 — layers of legislative refinement over 90+ years
- **PhilSys integration** — birth certificates are the foundational document for national ID issuance
- **Administrative correction mechanism** (RA 9048/10172) — allows civil registrars to fix errors without going to court

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**
A.
- **No Shari'ah track integration** — the national CRS does not have built-in workflows for PD 1083 marriages, Shari'ah court divorce decrees, or Muslim-specific documentation requirements
- **Transliteration challenges** — Arabic/Bangsamoro names are often mangled in the national system, leading to mismatches between local and PSA records
- **Underregistration** — BARMM has among the **highest rates of unregistered births** in the Philippines, driven by conflict displacement, geographic isolation, poverty, and the cultural practice of registering births only when needed (school enrollment, marriage)
- **No conflict-sensitive registration** — the national system has no protocol for registering vital events of IDPs, returnees, or persons whose records were destroyed (e.g., during the Marawi siege)

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**
A.
- **Replicate**: the PSA's digital CRS framework — BARMM should build a compatible (not separate) digital registration system that feeds into the national database
- **Adapt**: registration procedures to accommodate the **dual-track system** (PD 1083 + Civil Code), including Arabic name handling, Shari'ah court decree registration workflows, and mobile registration for remote areas
- **Adapt**: the administrative correction mechanism (RA 9048/10172) with BARMM-specific guidelines for Arabic/Muslim name corrections
- **Replace**: nothing — the national framework is sound; the problem is insufficient adaptation for BARMM's unique context, not structural deficiency

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**
A. **Yes — significant.** Civil registration of Muslim marriages and divorces has a direct Shari'ah dimension. Marriage (*nikah*) is a fundamental Islamic institution with specific contractual requirements under both Shari'ah and PD 1083. Divorce (*talaq*, *khul'*, *li'an*) is recognized under Islamic law and PD 1083 but not under the general Civil Code — creating a registration category that exists only for Muslims. The registration of these events is where **Islamic personal law meets the civil registration system**.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**
A. **Art. X, Sec. 4** grants Parliament power to enact laws on ***"personal, family, and property law jurisdiction"*** — directly covering Muslim marriage and divorce registration. **Art. X, Sec. 5(b)** grants Shari'ah Circuit Courts exclusive original jurisdiction over Muslim ***"Marriage"*** and ***"Divorce"*** disputes. The BOL's amendment of PD 1083 court jurisdiction provisions (Art. XVIII, Sec. 4(d)) while **preserving** PD 1083's marriage and registration provisions confirms that the Shari'ah-civil registration interface is a live legal system that Parliament may update but has not yet done so.[^3][^4]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**
A.
- ***Hifz al-nasl*** (preservation of lineage/progeny) — birth registration protects **nasab** (lineage), which is critical in Islamic law for inheritance, marriage prohibition degrees, and identity. Unregistered births threaten this fundamental Shari'ah objective.
- ***'Adl*** (justice) — equal access to registration services, regardless of geographic location or conflict status
- ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — civil registrars hold a trust obligation over vital records; accuracy and integrity are religious as well as legal duties
- ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — complete registration enables governance, resource allocation, and social protection — all serving the general welfare

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**
A. **PD 1083**, Arts. 13-26 (Muslim marriage) and Arts. 45-54 (Muslim divorce) — these are the operative Shari'ah-based provisions governing the events that must be registered. PD 1083 Art. 18 requires the marriage contract to be ***"registered in the local civil registrar's office"*** — creating the bridge between Islamic solemnization and civil registration. Shari'ah court divorce decrees must similarly be registered per AO No. 1, Series of 2005, though Res. 456 documents non-compliance.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**
A. Through an **adapted dual-track system**: the Bangsamoro Civil Registration Act should include a **Muslim personal status registration chapter** that: (1) codifies PD 1083 marriage registration requirements with updated procedures, (2) establishes a mandatory **Shari'ah court-to-civil registrar transmission protocol** for divorce decrees, (3) creates a standardized **marriage contract form** (*aqd al-nikah*) that satisfies both PD 1083 and civil registration requirements, and (4) designates the **Shari'ah Registrar** function — a specialized role within the civil registrar's office or Shari'ah court system for handling Muslim personal status documents. The Shari'ah Legislative Review Board (SLRB) should review this legislation for compliance.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**
A. **Limited divergence — constrained by the BOL itself.** The Art. V, Sec. 2(ss) grant contains an embedded national reporting obligation: copies to be furnished to PSA. This structurally ties BARMM's registration system to the national CRS. Per the constitutional floor established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), all registration must comply with constitutional rights (equal protection, due process, privacy). Per the **National Law Considerations** section (Section C), the PSA's Civil Registrar-General authority is a retained national function.

The divergence tier for this power is **Tier 3 (Moderate — framework alignment)**:
- BARMM **cannot** displace the PSA as Civil Registrar-General
- BARMM **cannot** create a closed registration system that does not transmit data to PSA
- BARMM **can** establish its own registration procedures and standards, provided they are compatible with the national CRS
- BARMM **can** create specialized registration tracks (Muslim personal status) that the national system does not provide
- BARMM **can** mandate higher standards (e.g., shorter registration deadlines, digital-first registration, mobile registration teams) than the national baseline
- The **Shari'ah dimension** (Section E) provides a unique basis for divergence: BARMM can create registration procedures for Muslim marriages and divorces that the national system does not offer, grounded in Art. X, Sec. 4 authority

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**
A. Rights holders benefit from a **BARMM-adapted system** — particularly Muslims whose marriages and divorces are currently underregistered due to the procedural gap between PD 1083 and the general registration system. For cross-boundary operations: as long as BARMM registration data flows to the PSA's CRS (as the BOL requires), certificates issued by BARMM civil registrars have **national validity** — persons moving from BARMM to other regions can obtain PSA-authenticated copies. No mutual recognition problem arises because the BOL itself ensures data integration.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**
A. A Bangsamoro Civil Registration Act would govern **registration procedures** within BARMM — who registers, how, when, what forms, what penalties for non-compliance, and what Shari'ah-specific procedures apply. National law continues to apply for: (1) PSA's Civil Registrar-General authority and national database management, (2) certificate authentication standards, (3) PhilSys integration, and (4) the basic registrable events defined in the Civil Code. The BAA would **supplement and adapt**, not supersede, the national framework.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**
A. Yes — three overlaps:

1. **Registration standards**: BAA 49 defines civil registrar duties in general terms; the PSA sets specific forms, procedures, and timelines. No formal coordination mechanism ensures BARMM-specific adaptations are recognized by the PSA — and vice versa.
2. **Muslim divorce registration**: Shari'ah courts (Bangsamoro judiciary) issue divorce decrees; local civil registrars (BARMM LGU) process registration; PSA (national) maintains the database. Res. 456 documents that this chain is broken. No legislation assigns responsibility for monitoring compliance or penalizes failure to transmit.
3. **Certificate authentication**: both LGU civil registrars and PSA outlets issue certificates. The relationship between BARMM-issued certificates and PSA-authenticated certificates needs legislative clarification — particularly whether a BARMM digital certificate could be legally equivalent to a PSA-authenticated copy.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**
A. No pending Parliament Bill specifically addresses civil registration as of the current legislative archive. The gaps remain unaddressed by filed legislation. The Parliament has expressed intent through Resolutions 191 and 456, but these are **non-binding** — they direct and urge, but do not legislate.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**
A. **BDP Chapter 5 (Governance and Administration)** depends on civil registration for:
- Population data driving **resource allocation** and IRA/block grant computation
- Electoral roll accuracy (voter registration depends on birth certificates)
- Social protection targeting (Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino, PhilHealth, social pension — all require birth certificates for enrollment)
- Planning and development data (BDP indicators require vital statistics)

**Mas Matatag Pillar 1 (Moral Governance)** — effective civil registration demonstrates government capacity and reach. **Pillar 3 (Basic Services)** — birth registration is the gateway to accessing basic services (education enrollment, health insurance, social protection). BDP Chapter 9 (Social Services) identifies access to social services as a priority — which depends on legal identity through civil registration.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**
A.
- **Unregistered births** remain high — BARMM continues to have some of the lowest birth registration rates in the Philippines, excluding children from school enrollment, PhilHealth, Pantawid, and PhilSys
- **Unregistered Muslim divorces** leave women in legal limbo — unable to remarry legally, unable to establish custody, unable to claim property rights
- **Inaccurate population data** undermines BDP implementation, IRA computation, and evidence-based policy
- **PhilSys enrollment** cannot reach universal coverage in BARMM without addressing the unregistered birth backlog
- **Shari'ah court effectiveness** is undermined when divorce decrees are not registered — the court's work has no civil legal effect until registered

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the dual-track registration system, and why does it matter for BARMM?**
A. BARMM has a **dual-track** civil registration system unique among Philippine regions:

- **Track 1 (Civil Code)**: marriages solemnized under the Family Code/Civil Code, registered through standard civil registrar procedures
- **Track 2 (PD 1083)**: Muslim marriages solemnized under PD 1083 (Art. 13-26), requiring distinct documentation (*aqd al-nikah*, marriage contract with specific PD 1083 elements)

Both tracks must register with the same local civil registrar and flow to the same PSA database. But the **requirements, documentation, and solemnizing authorities** differ. Many Muslim marriages are solemnized by religious authorities (*imam*, *wali*) but never registered with the civil registrar — creating a large population of **religiously valid but civilly unregistered** marriages. This is the core problem that legislation must solve.

**Q. What specific problems does the unregistered birth/marriage/death backlog create?**
A.
- **Children without birth certificates** cannot enroll in school (DepEd/MBHTE requires birth certificates), cannot be issued national IDs (PhilSys), cannot access Pantawid cash transfers, and are effectively invisible to the government
- **Women in unregistered marriages** have no legal claim to conjugal property, no legal basis for custody, and no access to the Shari'ah court system for divorce (because the court may require proof of a registered marriage)
- **Unregistered deaths** prevent settlement of estates, may result in ghost voters, and leave survivors unable to claim insurance, pensions, or survivor benefits
- **Unregistered Muslim divorces** mean women cannot legally remarry — creating a significant population of women in legal limbo

**Q. What role should the Shari'ah courts play in civil registration?**
A. Shari'ah courts are **producers of registrable events** — their decrees (divorce, annulment, marriage confirmation) must be registered to have civil legal effect. The current system relies on the parties or the court to transmit the decree to the local civil registrar, with **no enforcement mechanism**. A standalone BAA should: (1) mandate Shari'ah courts to transmit all decrees affecting civil status directly to the civil registrar within a prescribed period, (2) create a dedicated **Shari'ah Registry Liaison** function, and (3) impose consequences for non-transmission.

**Q. How does the PSA relationship work, and what are its limits?**
A. The BOL embeds the PSA relationship in the power grant itself: copies must be ***"furnished the Philippine Statistics Authority."***[^1] This means:
- Local civil registrars in BARMM **transmit duplicate copies** of all registered events to the PSA
- PSA **maintains the national database** and issues authenticated certificates
- PSA **sets registration forms and standards** nationwide
- BARMM **cannot** refuse to transmit data or operate a separate database

What BARMM **can** do: establish supplementary BARMM-level data systems (e.g., a regional CRVS dashboard), create additional registration procedures (e.g., Shari'ah track), set higher registration standards, and deploy mobile registration systems — all while maintaining the PSA data flow.

**Q. What would a Bangsamoro Civil Registration Act contain?**
A. A comprehensive act should cover:
1. **Institutional framework** — BARMM Civil Registration Coordination Office, relationship with MILG and LGUs
2. **Registration procedures** — updated timelines, digital-first options, mobile registration authorization
3. **Muslim personal status registration chapter** — PD 1083-compliant marriage and divorce registration procedures, Shari'ah court transmission protocols
4. **Data management** — BARMM-level vital statistics system, interoperability with PSA's CRS, privacy safeguards
5. **Penalty provisions** — consequences for non-registration, late registration, and failure to transmit Shari'ah court decrees
6. **Backlog resolution** — delayed registration program for unregistered births, marriages, and deaths, including those displaced by the Marawi siege
7. **Arabic/Bangsamoro name standards** — transliteration rules to prevent name-mismatch errors between local and PSA records
8. **Coordination mechanism** — formal MILG-PSA-Shari'ah Courts coordination protocol
