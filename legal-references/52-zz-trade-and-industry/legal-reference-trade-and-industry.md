# (zz) Trade and Industry

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(zz)** — ***"Trade and industry;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(zz) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national trade/industry law. Art. XIII, Sec. 24-27 contain no "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier referencing national law. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 1 requires the Bangsamoro economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution"*** and Art. V, Sec. 2 is ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law"*** — establishing a constitutional floor. Art. XIII, Sec. 26 requires barter goods sold elsewhere in the country to pay ***"appropriate customs or import duties in accordance with existing national laws."*** |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA); Bangsamoro Halal Board; Bangsamoro Barter Trade Council (BBTC); Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI, under OCM); Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA); Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR); Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 4 (Development Framework) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | **Bangsamoro cooperative code** (mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 24 — not yet enacted). Social enterprise laws (mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 27 — not yet enacted). Halal development laws (mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 30 — not yet enacted). Economic zone establishment laws (mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 28 — not yet enacted as standalone). |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over trade and industry?**

A. Article V, Section 2(zz) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"trade and industry"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is one of the broadest economic powers — encompassing **trade regulation**, **industrial development**, **investment promotion**, **consumer protection**, **halal industry development**, **MSME support**, **business name registration**, **barter trade and countertrade regulation**, **economic zone establishment**, and **private sector development** within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The chapeau subjects this power to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law"*** and operates ***"without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines."***[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Eight provisions elaborate on this power — more than almost any other enumerated power:

   (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 24** (three paragraphs) establishes: (a) the ***"private sector as a mover of trade, commerce, and industry"***; (b) the obligation to build ***"entrepreneurial capability"*** and support ***"cooperatives and other medium, small, and micro enterprises"***; (c) international trade promotion through ***"trade missions, trade fairs, and other promotional activities"***; and (d) ***"domestic trade preference"*** for locally produced goods with priority for trading partners with ***"historic and cultural ties to the Bangsamoro people."***[^2]

   (2) **Art. XIII, Sec. 25** grants the power to ***"register business names which shall be listed in the Philippine Business Registry."***[^3]

   (3) **Art. XIII, Sec. 26** authorizes regulation of ***"traditional barter trade"*** with BIMP-EAGA and ***"countertrade"*** with ASEAN, subject to national customs law for goods sold elsewhere.[^4]

   (4) **Art. XIII, Sec. 27** mandates recognition, protection, and promotion of ***"cooperative enterprises"*** and ***"social entrepreneurship"*** — with a legislative mandate for social enterprise laws.[^5]

   (5) **Art. XIII, Sec. 28** authorizes economic zones, industrial estates, and free ports, including the creation of ***"a Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority."***[^6]

   (6) **Art. XIII, Sec. 30** grants ***"the power to accredit halal-certifying bodies"*** and mandates Parliament to ***"enact laws to further strengthen its policy and programs on halal development."***[^7]

   (7) **Art. XIII, Sec. 1** requires the Bangsamoro economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution."***[^8]

   (8) **Art. XIII, Sec. 2** mandates ***"economic development that facilitates growth and full employment, human development, and social justice."***[^9]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **four legislative mandates** within the trade and industry scope:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 24: ***"through the enactment of a Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** — a named legislative mandate. (Not one of the 7 priority codes in Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a).) See power (k) for detailed analysis.[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 27: ***"The Parliament shall pass laws to strengthen the growth and development of social enterprises"*** — a separate mandate for social enterprise legislation. See power (k).[^5]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 30: ***"The Parliament shall enact laws to further strengthen its policy and programs on halal development."***[^7]

   (4) Art. XIII, Sec. 28 contemplates economic zone establishment through legislation — the Parliament may ***"provide such additional powers and functions to the Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority as may be necessary."*** See power (u).[^6]

   None of these four mandates has been fulfilled through standalone legislation as of BAA 89.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places five types of limitations:

   (1) **Constitutional floor**: Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law"*** — all trade and industry legislation must operate within the constitutional framework.[^1]

   (2) **Coordination requirement for international trade**: Art. XIII, Sec. 24 authorizes trade missions ***"observing the necessary coordination with the relevant government agencies."***[^2]

   (3) **National customs law compliance for barter goods**: Art. XIII, Sec. 26 requires goods traded through barter not to be ***"sold elsewhere in the country without the payment of appropriate customs or import duties in accordance with existing national laws."***[^4]

   (4) **ASEAN alignment for countertrade**: Art. XIII, Sec. 26 requires countertrade to be ***"in accordance with the spirit, intent, goals, processes, modalities, and arrangements in the ASEAN economic integration agreements."***[^4]

   (5) **Philippine Business Registry listing**: Art. XIII, Sec. 25 requires BARMM-registered business names to be ***"listed in the Philippine Business Registry."***[^3]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to trade and industry?**

A. Five categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This is the universal constitutional boundary on all 55 powers. The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."***[^10]

   (2) **National Economy Goals (Art. XII, Sec. 1)**: ***"The goals of the national economy are a more equitable distribution of opportunities, income, and wealth; a sustained increase in the amount of goods and services produced by the nation for the benefit of the people; and an expanding productivity as the key to raising the quality of life for all, especially the underprivileged."*** The State shall ***"protect Filipino enterprises against unfair foreign competition and trade practices."*** This constitutional goal aligns with and constrains Bangsamoro trade policy.[^11]

   (3) **Trade Policy (Art. XII, Sec. 13)**: ***"The State shall pursue a trade policy that serves the general welfare and utilizes all forms and arrangements of exchange on the basis of equality and reciprocity."*** Bangsamoro trade policy — including barter and countertrade — must serve the general welfare and cannot create discriminatory trade arrangements.[^12]

   (4) **Competition Policy (Art. XII, Sec. 19)**: ***"The State shall regulate or prohibit monopolies when the public interest so requires. No combinations in restraint of trade or unfair competition shall be allowed."*** This is the constitutional basis for the Philippine Competition Act (RA 10667), which applies directly in BARMM.[^13]

   (5) **Domestic Preference (Art. XII, Sec. 12)**: ***"The State shall promote the preferential use of Filipino labor, domestic materials and locally produced goods, and adopt measures that help make them competitive."*** This aligns with and supports the BOL's ***"domestic trade preference"*** mandate in Art. XIII, Sec. 24 — but the constitutional domestic preference is Filipino-wide, not region-specific.[^14]

**Q. What constitutional limitations constrain Bangsamoro trade and industry authority?**

A. Three structural limitations:

   (1) **Foreign investment nationalization requirements**: Art. XII, Sec. 10 reserves to Congress the power to ***"reserve to citizens of the Philippines or to corporations or associations at least sixty per centum of whose capital is owned by such citizens"*** certain areas of investment. The Foreign Investment Negative List is a Congressional/Presidential prerogative — BARMM cannot independently liberalize or restrict foreign investment beyond what national law permits.[^15]

   (2) **Public utility franchises**: Art. XII, Sec. 11 limits public utility franchises to ***"citizens of the Philippines or to corporations or associations organized under the laws of the Philippines at least sixty per centum of whose capital is owned by such citizens."*** BARMM economic zones and industrial estates cannot override this constitutional requirement.[^16]

   (3) **Customs and tariff authority**: The BOL does not grant customs or tariff authority to the Bangsamoro Government. Art. XIII, Sec. 26 requires compliance with ***"existing national laws"*** for customs and import duties. Art. XIII, Sec. 28 provides for ***"cooperation and coordination"*** on ***"customs, immigration, and quarantine services"*** for economic zones. Customs authority remains with the Bureau of Customs under the national government.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for trade and industry?**

A. The national framework establishes a comprehensive system across multiple domains:

   - **Consumer protection**: **RA 7394** (Consumer Act) provides product quality, safety, labeling, fair trade, and consumer rights standards.
   - **MSME development**: **RA 9501** (Magna Carta for MSMEs) creates SMEDC, mandatory lending allocations, and Negosyo Centers. **RA 9178** (BMBEs Act) provides barangay-level micro enterprise incentives. **RA 11337** (Innovative Startup Act) creates the startup ecosystem framework. **RA 11293** (Philippine Innovation Act) promotes innovation-driven competitiveness.
   - **Foreign investment**: **RA 7042** as amended by **RA 8179** and **RA 11647** (Foreign Investments Act) establishes the FINL and investment registration framework.
   - **Retail trade**: **RA 8762** as amended by **RA 11595** (Retail Trade Liberalization Act) regulates foreign retail participation.
   - **Competition**: **RA 10667** (Philippine Competition Act) prohibits anti-competitive conduct and creates PCC.
   - **Business registration**: **RA 11032** (Ease of Doing Business Act) streamlines government services. **EO 587** establishes the Philippine Business Registry.
   - **Fiscal incentives**: **RA 11534** (CREATE Act) reforms corporate taxation and investment incentives.
   - **Procurement**: **RA 9184** (Government Procurement Reform Act) governs all public procurement.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM for trade and industry?**

A. All national trade and industry laws apply — most of them **directly** because they are national-level frameworks:

   (1) **RA 7394** — consumer protection, suppletory pending a Bangsamoro Consumer Protection Act.
   (2) **RA 9501**, **RA 9178**, **RA 11337**, **RA 11293** — MSME frameworks, suppletory.
   (3) **RA 7042/RA 11647** — foreign investment regulation, applies directly.
   (4) **RA 8762/RA 11595** — retail trade liberalization, applies directly.
   (5) **RA 10667** — competition policy, applies directly (PCC has nationwide jurisdiction).
   (6) **RA 11032** — ease of doing business, applies directly.
   (7) **RA 11534** — corporate tax and incentives, applies directly for national taxes.
   (8) **RA 9184** — government procurement, applies directly.
   (9) **EO 587** — Philippine Business Registry, applies directly per BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 25.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for trade and industry?**

A. Four principal national agencies:

   (1) **Department of Trade and Industry (DTI)** — operates **Negosyo Centers** in BARMM LGUs, maintains the Philippine Business Registry, conducts consumer protection enforcement, implements MSME programs, and operates provincial offices.

   (2) **Board of Investments (BOI)** — national investment promotion agency. The **Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI)** was created under the Office of the Chief Minister to promote investments within BARMM, but BOI retains national investment registration authority.

   (3) **Philippine Economic Zone Authority (PEZA)** — per BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 28, PEZA ***"shall no longer create any other economic zone within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** once BEZA is created. Existing PEZA-created entities transfer to BEZA jurisdiction.

   (4) **Philippine Competition Commission (PCC)** — nationwide jurisdiction over anti-competitive conduct; applies directly in BARMM.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV (Sec. 1-41) defines MTIT's institutional status, mandate, powers, functions, organizational structure, and attached bodies. BAA 85 allocates its operational budget.[^17][^18]

**Q. What is MTIT's mandate under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Sec. 2 designates MTIT as ***"the primary government agency mandated to implement laws, programs and projects on trade, investments, and tourism."*** Its mandate is to ***"promote and develop an industrialization program effectively controlled by inhabitants of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** through: (a) ***"comprehensive industrial growth strategy"***; (b) ***"progressive and socially responsible liberalization program"***; and (c) ***"policies designed for the expansion and diversification of trade."***[^19]

#### D.1.A MTIT Functions under BAA 13

**Q. What are MTIT's 26 enumerated powers and functions?**

A. BAA 13, Sec. 3 enumerates 26 powers and functions (a-z) — the most extensive function list of any BARMM ministry:

   - (a) Formulate policies, plans, programs for trade, investments, and tourism
   - (b) Coordinate implementation and enforcement of trade, investment, and tourism rules
   - (c) Create stable market and responsive investment policies
   - (d) Undertake investment promotion initiatives
   - (e) Design integrated marketing to attract investors and tourists
   - (f) Formulate standards for tourism-oriented establishments
   - (g) Promulgate rules governing tourism activities
   - (h) Provide standards for hotel/resort accreditation consistent with national laws
   - (i) Develop programs for private sector tourism investment
   - (j) Promote protection of historical, cultural, and natural assets
   - (k) Assist in enforcement of tourist protection laws
   - (l) Coordinate tourism zone infrastructure development
   - (m) Coordinate social infrastructure for tourism zones
   - **(n) Promote, develop, and regulate halal industry; accredit halal certifying bodies and auditors**
   - **(o) Coordinate halal industry development with MAFAR and other agencies**
   - **(p) Coordinate with MILG to facilitate LGU-level local economic development**
   - **(q) Promote, develop, regulate, and accredit repair and service enterprises**
   - **(r) Encourage formation of Small and Medium Enterprise Development Council (SMEDC)**
   - **(s) Formulate programs to strengthen industries affected by economic crisis**
   - **(t) Formulate plans for industrial dispersal to rural areas, manufactured exports, and MSME development**
   - **(u) Coordinate long-term industry sectoral plans with private sector**
   - **(v) Promote domestic trade, marketing, and distribution**
   - **(w) Propose Shari'ah-compliant commercial lending programs to the Monetary Board**
   - **(x) Create One-Stop-Shop Business Development Support Center for MSMEs**
   - **(y) Administratively adjudicate trade, investment, tourism, and cooperative development law violations**
   - (z) Exercise other necessary powers[^20]

**Q. How is MTIT organized?**

A. BAA 13, Sec. 4-20 establishes a comprehensive organizational structure:

   **Ministry Proper** (Sec. 6-9): Minister, Deputy Minister, Bangsamoro Director-General

   **Services** (Sec. 10): Legal and Legislative Liaison Division; Internal Audit Division; Administrative and Finance Services (Administrative Division + Finance Division); Technical Services (Policy and Planning Division + Policy Research, Development and Monitoring Division + ICT Division)

   **Four Bureaus** (Sec. 11-18):
   - **Bureau of Trade and Industry** (Sec. 11-12) — divisions: Trade Regulation and Consumer Protection; Enterprise and Trade Development; Industry Development and Investment Promotion
   - **Bureau of Investments** (Sec. 13-14) — divisions: Technical and Regulatory; Research and Support
   - **Bureau of Tourism** (Sec. 15-16) — divisions: Product Development and Standards; Tourism Promotions and Marketing
   - **Bureau of Product Standards** (Sec. 17-18) — divisions: Standards Development and Conformity; Product Testing

   **Seven Field Offices** (Sec. 19-21): Maguindanao; Cotabato City; Lanao del Sur/Marawi City; Special Geographic Area; Basilan/Lamitan City; Sulu; Tawi-Tawi[^21]

   **Attached Bodies**:
   - **Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA)** — Ch. 6 (Sec. 22-32)
   - **Bangsamoro Halal Board** — Ch. 7 (Sec. 33-36)
   - **Bangsamoro Barter Trade Council (BBTC)** — Ch. 8 (Sec. 37-41)

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in BARMM for trade and industry?**

A. **DTI** operates provincial offices and Negosyo Centers in BARMM LGUs, processes business name registrations for the Philippine Business Registry, conducts consumer protection enforcement, and implements MSME development programs. **BOI** processes investment registrations for enterprises seeking national fiscal incentives within BARMM. **PEZA** manages any existing PEZA-created zones — though Art. XIII, Sec. 28 transfers these to BEZA jurisdiction. **PCC** exercises nationwide competition enforcement. The **Bureau of Customs** handles all customs and tariff functions, including barter trade goods entering the general market. **DOST** implements national science and technology programs relevant to industrial development. **NCC** (National Competitiveness Council) coordinates ease of doing business reforms.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **partial and uneven**:

   - **Institutionally devolved**: MTIT is fully established with 4 bureaus, 7 field offices, and 3 attached bodies — the most comprehensively organized BARMM ministry for trade and industry.
   - **Functionally incomplete**: MTIT operates under BAA 13's general mandate without standalone trade, investment, consumer protection, halal, or economic zone legislation. This means MTIT exercises programmatic functions but lacks the **regulatory codes** that define standards, penalties, and adjudicatory procedures.
   - **Registration authority shared**: Business name registration per Art. XIII, Sec. 25 must be listed in the PBR — creating a coordination requirement with DTI rather than full devolution.
   - **Customs/tariff not devolved**: Customs authority remains entirely national.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MTIT has been **fully institutionalized** under BAA 13 with the most extensive organizational structure of any BARMM ministry for trade functions — 4 bureaus, 7 field offices, and 3 attached bodies (BEZA, Halal Board, BBTC). The **Bureau of Trade and Industry** operates trade regulation and consumer protection, enterprise development, and investment promotion divisions. The **Bureau of Product Standards** has begun developing Bangsamoro Regional Product Standards. The **Halal Board** has been constituted with 10 members (Sec. 35) and a secretariat (Sec. 36). The **BBTC** has been created with regulatory authority over barter trade. MTIT field offices operate in all 7 provincial areas. Multiple resolutions signal parliamentary intent for halal development (Res. 200), domestic trade preference (Res. 189), and consumer protection (Res. 358-359, 540-541).

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has four unique advantages:

   (1) **Halal industry authority** — the Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13, Sec. 33-36) has **halal accreditation authority** under BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 30 that no national agency possesses at the same level. The national government has no single dedicated halal regulatory body with accreditation powers comparable to the Bangsamoro Halal Board.

   (2) **BIMP-EAGA and ASEAN barter/countertrade authority** — the BBTC (BAA 13, Sec. 37-41) is the only Philippine government body specifically mandated to regulate traditional barter trade with BIMP-EAGA members. DTI has no equivalent specialized body.

   (3) **Shari'ah-compliant trade financing mandate** — BAA 13, Sec. 3(w) mandates MTIT to ***"propose, for the consideration of the Monetary Board, programs in the commercial banking sector for directing Shari'ah-compliant commercial lending facilities towards priority areas of commercial and industrial development."*** No national trade agency has an equivalent mandate.

   (4) **Integrated institutional design** — MTIT houses trade, investment, tourism, halal industry, economic zones, and barter trade under one ministry with four specialized bureaus and three attached bodies. Nationally, these functions are split across DTI, BOI, DOT, PEZA, Bureau of Customs, and multiple other agencies.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built regulatory depth that BARMM lacks:

   - **Consumer protection code**: RA 7394 provides a comprehensive consumer protection framework with 181 sections — product quality, safety, labeling, warranties, price regulation, and consumer credit. BARMM has no equivalent. PB No. 401 (Consumer Rights Protection) would begin filling this gap.
   - **MSME ecosystem**: RA 9501 with SMEDC, mandatory bank lending quotas (8% for micro, 2% for small enterprises), Negosyo Centers in LGUs, and the Go Negosyo Act (RA 10644). BARMM has the institutional mandate (BAA 13, Sec. 3(r)(t)(x)) but no standalone code.
   - **Competition enforcement**: PCC with quasi-judicial powers, merger review authority, and investigative capacity. BARMM has no equivalent — PCC covers BARMM directly.
   - **Investment incentive system**: CREATE Act (RA 11534) with FIRB oversight, strategic and enhanced deduction incentives, and income tax holidays. BEZA can provide zone-specific incentives but the national incentive system is far more developed.
   - **IT systems**: DTI's online business registration, BOI's investment tracking system, PEZA's zone management databases. MTIT operates primarily through manual processes.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: consumer protection standards (RA 7394 product quality and safety requirements); procurement reform processes (RA 9184 bidding procedures); MSME classification thresholds.
   - **Adapt**: business registration to integrate with the PBR while serving BARMM-specific needs (halal certification bundled with registration); MSME development programs for conflict-affected communities and predominantly Muslim entrepreneurial culture; investment incentives calibrated to BARMM's priority sectors (halal, agri-processing, maritime trade).
   - **Replace**: halal certification — BARMM builds an entirely distinct halal accreditation system with no national equivalent; Shari'ah-compliant trade financing mechanisms that the conventional national framework does not provide; barter trade regulation through BBTC that DTI/Bureau of Customs do not handle.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Trade and industry has a **profound Shari'ah dimension** — Islamic commercial law (*fiqh al-mu'amalat*) provides one of the most developed bodies of jurisprudence governing commercial transactions, contracts, trade practices, and consumer protection. For a predominantly Muslim region, ensuring that trade and industry regulation aligns with Shari'ah principles is not merely aspirational — it is a governance imperative. The BOL recognizes this through the **halal accreditation mandate** (Art. XIII, Sec. 30), the **Shari'ah-compliant lending mandate** (operationalized in BAA 13, Sec. 3(w)), and the broader Islamic banking and finance framework (Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34).[^7]

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to trade and industry?**

A. The BOL provides three express connections:

   (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 30** mandates halal accreditation — a direct application of the Shari'ah concept of *halal* (permissible) and *haram* (prohibited) to trade regulation and consumer protection.[^7]

   (2) **Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34** establishes the Islamic banking and finance framework — including a Shari'ah Supervisory Board with authority to issue *fatwas* on financial products and practices. While these provisions primarily fall under power (ii) Islamic Banking, they directly affect trade financing for BARMM enterprises.[^22]

   (3) **Art. X, Sec. 4** authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah-based commercial laws: ***"The Parliament has the power to enact laws governing commercial and other civil actions not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083, as amended."*** Trade and industry regulation is commercial in nature and is not provided for under PD 1083 — giving Parliament express authority for Shari'ah-informed trade legislation.[^23]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to trade and industry legislation?**

A. Six principles directly govern trade and industry:

   (1) ***Halal*** — all commercial transactions must involve permissible goods and services. The Bangsamoro Halal Board operationalizes this through accreditation of certifying bodies.

   (2) **Prohibition of *riba*** — trade financing cannot involve interest-bearing instruments. BAA 13, Sec. 3(w) addresses this by mandating Shari'ah-compliant lending proposals.

   (3) ***Mudarabah*** and ***musharakah*** — profit-sharing and joint venture structures that provide Shari'ah-compliant alternatives to conventional business lending and investment.

   (4) **Prohibition of *gharar*** — contracts must be clear, transparent, and free from excessive uncertainty. This aligns with consumer protection, product standards, and fair trade enforcement.

   (5) ***Hisbah*** (market supervision) — the Islamic concept of market regulation and consumer protection, where a *muhtasib* (market inspector) ensures fair dealing, honest weights and measures, and product quality. The Bureau of Product Standards and the Trade Regulation and Consumer Protection Division perform a modern *hisbah* function.

   (6) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — justifies broad trade regulation, consumer protection, and industrial policy as serving the welfare of the Bangsamoro community.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national trade and industry law?**

A. BARMM has **moderate to broad** legislative authority to diverge, depending on the specific trade function:

   **Broad divergence authority** exists for:
   - **Halal regulation** — no national equivalent framework exists; BARMM builds from scratch under Art. XIII, Sec. 30.
   - **Barter/countertrade regulation** — Art. XIII, Sec. 26 grants specific regulatory authority; BBTC has no DTI counterpart.
   - **Shari'ah-compliant trade financing** — BAA 13, Sec. 3(w) creates a mandate that the conventional national framework does not address.
   - **MSME support and enterprise development** — Art. XIII, Sec. 24's mandate to build ***"entrepreneurial capability"*** authorizes BARMM-specific programs, thresholds, and incentives.

   **Limited divergence** exists for:
   - **Foreign investment** — the FINL is a national/presidential prerogative under Art. XII, Sec. 10 of the Constitution.
   - **Competition policy** — PCC's nationwide jurisdiction under RA 10667 is constitutionally grounded in Art. XII, Sec. 19.
   - **Customs and tariffs** — not devolved; Art. XIII, Sec. 26 expressly requires compliance with national customs laws.
   - **Government procurement** — RA 9184 applies directly; BARMM can add preferences (per PB No. 424) but cannot circumvent the national framework.

   The **limits of divergence** are the constitutional floor established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B): Art. XII, Sec. 1 (national economy goals), Art. XII, Sec. 13 (trade policy serving general welfare), Art. XII, Sec. 19 (anti-monopoly), and Art. X, Sec. 15 (autonomous region framework).

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) **For enterprises within BARMM**: They could gain access to Shari'ah-compliant trade financing, halal certification bundled with business registration, and enterprise development programs tailored to BARMM's economic context. The risk is if BARMM standards create **additional compliance burdens** without corresponding benefits — enterprises might prefer the national pathway.

   (2) **For cross-boundary trade**: Goods and services moving between BARMM and the rest of the Philippines must comply with national standards (RA 7394 for consumer protection, RA 10667 for competition, customs laws for barter goods). BARMM product standards (through the Bureau of Product Standards) must be harmonized with or meet the Philippine National Standards (PNS) — BAA 13, Sec. 17(b) already contemplates this by authorizing adoption of PNS.

   (3) **For investors**: BEZA can offer zone-specific fiscal incentives that complement the national CREATE Act incentive regime. Divergent incentive structures could attract investment — or create confusion if not clearly communicated.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Four principal overlaps:

   (1) **MTIT vs. DTI**: DTI operates provincial offices and Negosyo Centers within BARMM alongside MTIT's own field offices and Bureau of Trade and Industry. Business name registration requires coordination with DTI's PBR. Consumer protection enforcement has dual channels — MTIT under BAA 13 Sec. 3(y) and DTI under RA 7394. A comprehensive trade code should clarify jurisdictional boundaries.

   (2) **BBOI vs. BOI**: The Bangsamoro Board of Investments (under OCM) promotes investments within BARMM, but national BOI retains authority over investment registration for enterprises seeking national incentives. PB No. 409 (additional BBOI positions) signals intent to strengthen the Bangsamoro institution.

   (3) **BEZA vs. PEZA**: Art. XIII, Sec. 28 provides clear jurisdictional separation — PEZA cannot create new zones once BEZA is created, and existing PEZA entities transfer to BEZA. The transition of existing zones needs implementing legislation.

   (4) **BBTC vs. Bureau of Customs**: BBTC regulates barter trade, but customs authority (including tariff assessment on goods sold outside barter zones) remains national. BAA 13, Sec. 38(g) provides for coordination with the Bureau of Customs.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Nine pending PBs address various aspects of this power:

   - **PB No. 401** (Consumer Rights Protection) — would create a standalone consumer protection framework and Bangsamoro Consumer Protection Council, reducing dependence on DTI enforcement of RA 7394.
   - **PB No. 243/329** (Public-Private Partnerships) — would create the legal framework for investment partnerships.
   - **PB No. 268** (Halal Consumers Act) — would fulfill the Art. XIII, Sec. 30 legislative mandate for halal development laws.
   - **PB No. 346** (Trade and Tourism Centers) — would create physical infrastructure for trade promotion.
   - **PB No. 376** (Bangsamoro Enterprise Support Corporation) — would create an enterprise development GOCC.
   - **PB No. 409** (BBOI Plantilla) — would strengthen investment promotion institutional capacity.
   - **PB No. 424** (Locally Produced Goods Procurement) — would operationalize the domestic trade preference mandate.
   - **PB No. 164** (Halal Labeling) — would strengthen halal consumer protection.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) positions trade and industry as the **core strategy for regional economic growth**. Key development objectives include: MSME development and expansion; investment promotion and private sector growth; halal industry development as a competitive advantage; market access for Bangsamoro products to BIMP-EAGA and ASEAN markets; and industrial dispersal to rural and conflict-affected areas. Chapter 4 (Development Framework) establishes the private sector as the mover of economic development — directly reflecting Art. XIII, Sec. 24's language. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, depends on trade and industry development to strengthen regional economic self-sufficiency. **Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without standalone trade and industry legislation:

   (1) **Consumer protection** operates under the national framework (RA 7394) without halal-specific or BARMM-specific provisions — consumers lack recourse for halal fraud through Bangsamoro institutions.

   (2) **MSME development** lacks a BARMM-specific code with appropriate thresholds, Shari'ah-compliant financing mechanisms, and conflict-sensitive enterprise support programs. National MSME thresholds may not reflect BARMM's economic reality.

   (3) **Halal industry development** — the Halal Board operates under BAA 13's general mandate without the standalone legislation that Art. XIII, Sec. 30 requires. Accreditation standards, enforcement mechanisms, and penalty provisions are not codified.

   (4) **Investment promotion** operates through BBOI and MTIT without a comprehensive investment code defining registration procedures, incentive structures, and investor protections specific to BARMM's priority sectors.

   (5) **Domestic trade preference** — Art. XIII, Sec. 24's mandate for preferential treatment of locally produced goods cannot be fully operationalized without procurement-level implementation (addressed by PB No. 424).

   (6) **Economic zone development** — BEZA operates under BAA 13's enabling provisions but no standalone economic zone act has been enacted defining zone establishment criteria, incentive structures, and governance frameworks beyond the general provisions in BAA 13, Ch. 6.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why is trade and industry one of the broadest enumerated powers?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 24-28 and Sec. 30 together dedicate **six full sections** of the BOL to trade and industry functions — more than any other single enumerated power except possibly budgeting (Art. XII) and education (Art. IX, Sec. 16-19). The breadth reflects the BOL's design of **economic self-determination** for the Bangsamoro — recognizing that genuine autonomy requires not only political self-governance but economic capability. The power encompasses: trade regulation, industrial development, investment promotion, MSME support, cooperative development, barter and countertrade, business name registration, economic zones, halal industry, consumer protection, product standards, and Shari'ah-compliant trade financing. Several of these functions are also enumerated as standalone powers — **(e)** barter trade, **(g)** business name registration, **(k)** cooperatives, **(u)** economic zones — reflecting the overlap between trade and industry as the umbrella power and its component parts.

**Q. How does MTIT relate to the Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI)?**

A. The BBOI is not created by BAA 13. It operates under the **Office of the Chief Minister** as a separate investment promotion body. BAA 13 creates the **Bureau of Investments** within MTIT (Sec. 13-14), which handles matters ***"not otherwise exercised by the Bangsamoro Board of Investments."*** This creates a dual institutional arrangement: BBOI handles high-level investment promotion and the Investments Priorities Plan, while MTIT's Bureau of Investments handles day-to-day investment regulation, registration supervision, and enterprise monitoring. The coordination between these two bodies is not fully defined in legislation — PB No. 409 (additional BBOI positions) signals intent to strengthen the OCM-based body.[^24]

**Q. What is the significance of the Bangsamoro Halal Board?**

A. The Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13, Sec. 33-36) is **unique in Philippine governance** — no other local or regional government body has dedicated halal accreditation authority comparable to what the BOL grants under Art. XIII, Sec. 30. The Board is the **policy-making body** for halal industry development, with powers to: (a) grant or deny halal accreditation certificates; (b) formulate the Bangsamoro Halal Development and Promotion Program; (c) coordinate with regulatory agencies on halal standards; (d) investigate complaints on halal implementation; and (e) create technical panels and working groups. It is chaired by the MTIT Minister and includes ministers from MAFAR, MOST, MOH, MOLE, MOTC, the Mufti of the Darul-Ifta, an MSU representative, and two Bangsamoro professionals. The halal program bridges **trade regulation and Shari'ah compliance** — it is both an economic development strategy (halal industry as a competitive advantage in BIMP-EAGA and global markets) and a religious obligation (ensuring Muslim consumers have access to permissible goods).[^25][^26]

**Q. How does this power overlap with other enumerated powers?**

A. Trade and industry is the umbrella economic power with five direct overlaps:

   (1) **Power (e) — Barter trade and countertrade**: Art. XIII, Sec. 26 provides the specific mandate; BBTC (BAA 13, Ch. 8) is the institutional mechanism. The barter trade power is a **subset** of trade and industry focused on BIMP-EAGA and ASEAN traditional trade.

   (2) **Power (g) — Business name registration**: Art. XIII, Sec. 25 is a trade regulation function housed within MTIT's Bureau of Trade and Industry. Registration must feed into the Philippine Business Registry.

   (3) **Power (k) — Cooperatives and social entrepreneurship**: Art. XIII, Sec. 24 and 27 establish cooperative development within the "Trade and Industry and Tourism" subsection of the BOL. CSEA is the lead agency (attached to OCM), but MTIT is a supporting agency. The cooperative code mandate is embedded within a trade and industry provision.

   (4) **Power (u) �� Economic zones, industrial centers, and free ports**: Art. XIII, Sec. 28 authorizes BEZA, which is attached to MTIT (BAA 13, Ch. 6). Economic zones are an instrument of trade and industrial development.

   (5) **Power (yy) — Tourism development**: Tourism is housed within MTIT's mandate and Bureau of Tourism. Art. XIII, Sec. 35 provides the specific tourism provision. Tourism promotion and trade promotion often overlap in institutional design.

**Q. What distinguishes MTIT's trade regulation from consumer protection specifically?**

A. MTIT exercises **both** trade regulation and consumer protection functions under BAA 13. The Bureau of Trade and Industry's **Trade Regulation and Consumer Protection Division** (Sec. 12) handles fair trade enforcement, price regulation, and consumer complaints. The **Bureau of Product Standards** (Sec. 17-18) handles product safety, quality certification, and standards. BAA 13 Sec. 3(y) gives MTIT power to ***"administratively adjudicate and impose reasonable fines and penalties for violation of existing trade, investments, tourism, and cooperative development laws."*** However, **PB No. 401** proposes a separate **Bangsamoro Consumer Protection Council** — which would create a dedicated consumer protection body distinct from MTIT's trade regulation function. This mirrors the national pattern where DTI houses both trade and consumer protection but PCC (for competition) operates independently.
