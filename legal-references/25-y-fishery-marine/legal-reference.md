# (y) Fishery, Marine, and Aquatic Resources

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(y)** — "Fishery, marine, and aquatic resources;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The Bangsamoro Organic Law grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over fishery, marine, and aquatic resources as one of 55 enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2(y). This power covers commercial, municipal, and aquaculture fisheries; marine resource conservation; aquatic resource management; and the regulation of fishing activities within Bangsamoro waters.

The BOL elaborates this power extensively. Article XIII, Section 23 is the primary provision, requiring that Bangsamoro fisheries policies be "consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550, otherwise known as 'The Philippine Fisheries Code of 1998,' as amended, and other relevant national laws." Despite this consistency requirement, Section 23 carves out a significant benefit: Bangsamoro people, indigenous peoples, and resident marginal fisherfolk enjoy preferential fishing rights in Bangsamoro regional waters. Article III, Sections 3-4 define the geographic scope — inland waters (lakes, rivers, streams) form part of the Bangsamoro territory, and Bangsamoro municipal and regional waters extend up to 15 and 19 kilometers respectively from the low-water mark. The Zones of Joint Cooperation provisions (Art. XIII, Sec. 18-21) create a framework for joint management of the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf, where the Joint Body co-chaired by the Secretary of Agriculture and a Bangsamoro Minister formulates policies for protection of traditional fishing grounds, equitable benefit-sharing, and island-mainland interconnectivity.

Article XIII, Section 22 grants the Bangsamoro Government "exclusive powers over inland waters, including lakes, marshes, rivers, and tributaries within its territorial jurisdiction," which directly affects freshwater fisheries management, including the critical Lake Lanao and Liguasan Marsh ecosystems.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

MAFAR is the lead agency, with fisheries falling under its mandate alongside agriculture and agrarian reform. MAFAR has a budget allocation under BAA 85 (General Appropriations Act, FY 2026), Chapter XVI. It operates under its BAA 13 (Administrative Code) general mandate, which includes fisheries development, marine resource management, and support for fisherfolk.

BAA 59 created the Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute (BAFTI), which provides training programs that include fisheries skills development for fisherfolk communities. This is the only standalone BAA that directly touches the fisheries power. Without a dedicated Bangsamoro Fisheries Code, MAFAR cannot independently establish fishing regulations, create a region-specific licensing system for commercial fishing, or enforce conservation measures with the legal authority of standalone legislation. The national Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources (BFAR) continues to exercise regulatory authority over fisheries within BARMM under RA 8550.

Parliament has demonstrated interest in the fisheries sector through filed bills. PB No. 180 (Integrated Coastal Management) and PB No. 289 (Social Protection for Agri-Fishery Workers) address aspects of the fisheries sector. Resolutions 23 and 370 have called for the constitution and finalization of the Joint Body for Zones of Joint Cooperation and Bangsamoro waters delineation, respectively — critical prerequisites for exercising fisheries authority in marine areas.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL imposes one of its most explicit national law consistency requirements on this power. Article XIII, Section 23 requires fisheries policies to be "consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws." This means any Bangsamoro Fisheries Code must operate within the framework of the Philippine Fisheries Code, which governs municipal and commercial fishing zones, fishing licenses, conservation, and penalties. RA 10654 (2014) amended RA 8550 to strengthen penalties for illegal fishing, and these amendments apply within BARMM.

The Joint Body for Zones of Joint Cooperation (Art. VI, Sec. 5; Art. XIII, Sec. 19) is a formal intergovernmental mechanism for marine governance. It is co-chaired by the Secretary of Agriculture and a Bangsamoro Minister, with representation from DENR, DOTr, and LGUs. Resolutions 23 and 370 indicate this body has not been fully operationalized, creating a governance gap for the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf zones. Additionally, the Philippine Coast Guard retains responsibility for maritime law enforcement in coordination with the Bangsamoro Government (Art. XIII, Sec. 22, last paragraph).

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The most critical gap is the absence of a Bangsamoro Fisheries Code. The BOL grants authority over fisheries but requires consistency with RA 8550 — this creates an opportunity for a Bangsamoro law that adapts the national fisheries framework to regional conditions while adding region-specific provisions. Such a code would establish: a Bangsamoro fishing licensing and registration system (operating in coordination with BFAR), municipal and regional water zoning specific to BARMM's unique archipelagic geography, conservation and sustainable use standards for critical ecosystems like Liguasan Marsh and the Sulu Sea coral reefs, aquaculture development regulations, post-harvest and value chain standards for fisheries products, a mariculture framework for island provinces (Tawi-Tawi, Sulu, Basilan), and social protection mechanisms for marginal fisherfolk.

The non-operationalization of the Joint Body for Zones of Joint Cooperation remains a structural gap. Without this body, the Bangsamoro Government cannot fully exercise its maritime fisheries authority in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf. The delineation of Bangsamoro waters — called for in Resolution 370 — is a prerequisite for any effective marine fisheries regulation.

### Development and Policy Connection

BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) prioritizes fisheries as a key economic sector, targeting increased fish production and improved livelihoods for fisherfolk communities. Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) addresses marine ecosystem conservation and sustainable fisheries. The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028) includes food security and marine resource management under its economic resilience pillar. The island provinces of Tawi-Tawi, Sulu, and Basilan depend overwhelmingly on fisheries — without dedicated legislation, these communities lack a regulatory framework that protects their fishing rights while ensuring sustainable resource use.
