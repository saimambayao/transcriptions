# (y) Fishery, Marine, and Aquatic Resources

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(y)** — ***"Fishery, marine, and aquatic resources;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(y) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XIII, Sec. 23 requires policies and laws on fisheries to be ***"Consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550, otherwise known as 'The Philippine Fisheries Code of 1998,' as amended, and other relevant national laws."*** |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR)** — specifically the Office of the Bangsamoro Director-General for Fisheries (BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. I, Ch. 4, Sec. 10) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources, and Energy (MENRE); Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute (BAFTI, BAA 59); Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31); Joint Body for the Zones of Joint Cooperation (Art. VI, Sec. 5) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development — agriculture, fisheries, and agrarian reform subsector); Chapter 3 (Spatial Strategy — BSAFDA and island province development) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | No named code. But Art. XIII, Sec. 23 contemplates comprehensive fisheries policies and laws, and Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates Parliament to ***"enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection"*** of inland waters including fisheries. |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over fishery, marine, and aquatic resources?**

A. Article V, Section 2(y) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Fishery, marine, and aquatic resources"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a broad grant covering the full scope of **fishery regulation**, **marine resource management**, **aquatic resource conservation**, and **fishery development** within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region — encompassing both **marine fisheries** (coastal and offshore) and **inland fisheries** (lakes, rivers, marshes).[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Eight provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 23 is the **primary fisheries provision**. It states: ***"Consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550, otherwise known as 'The Philippine Fisheries Code of 1998,' as amended, and other relevant national laws, the policies and laws of the Bangsamoro Government on agriculture, fisheries, and aquatic resources shall advance agriculture as a key development strategy, promote productivity measures, and provide support for small farmers, landholders, and marginal fisherfolk."*** It further grants: ***"The Bangsamoro people, indigenous peoples, and resident marginal fisherfolk shall enjoy preferential fishing rights in the Bangsamoro regional waters, without prejudice to the fishing rights of other citizens of the Philippines, whether residents or nonresidents of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 8 establishes the general natural resources authority: ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall have the power, authority, and right to explore, develop, and utilize the natural resources, including surface and subsurface rights, inland waters, coastal waters, and renewable and nonrenewable resources."*** It mandates that ***"protection, conservation, rehabilitation, and development of forests, coastal, and marine resources"*** be given **priority** for ecological balance and biodiversity.[^3]

   (3) Art. III, Sec. 3 defines inland water jurisdiction: ***"All inland waters such as lakes, rivers, river systems, and streams within its territorial jurisdiction shall form part of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region inland waters."***[^4]

   (4) Art. III, Sec. 4 establishes the spatial extent of Bangsamoro waters: municipal waters up to **15 kilometers** and regional waters up to **19 kilometers** from the low-water mark.[^5]

   (5) Art. XIII, Sec. 18-19 create the **Zones of Joint Cooperation** in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf and establish the Joint Body to formulate policies for ***"protection of the traditional fishing grounds"*** and ***"equitable sharing of the benefits from the resources therein."***[^6]

   (6) Art. XIII, Sec. 22 grants ***"exclusive powers over inland waters"*** and mandates Parliament to ***"enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection of these resources."***[^7]

   (7) Art. XIII, Sec. 11 grants **preferential rights** for bona fide residents over natural resource utilization.[^8]

   (8) Art. VI, Sec. 5 creates the **Joint Body for the Zones of Joint Cooperation** as an intergovernmental institution under Art. VI.[^9]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. The BOL does not name a specific ***"Bangsamoro fisheries code"*** by title. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 22 expressly mandates Parliament to ***"enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection"*** of inland waters — which includes inland fisheries. Art. XIII, Sec. 23's consistency requirement with RA 8550 contemplates that the Bangsamoro Government will adopt its own ***"policies and laws"*** on fisheries. Art. XIII, Sec. 8 mandates Parliament to ***"pass a law to establish protected areas"*** — which includes marine protected areas. Taken together, these provisions create a **composite legislative mandate** for comprehensive fishery legislation, though no single code is named.[^2][^3][^7]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL creates the **Joint Body for the Zones of Joint Cooperation** (Art. VI, Sec. 5; Art. XIII, Sec. 19) — an intergovernmental institution co-chaired by the Secretary of Agriculture and a Bangsamoro Minister. Additionally, Art. XIII, Sec. 22 authorizes Parliament to ***"create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water that shall exercise management and regulatory powers over these bodies of water."*** Neither MAFAR nor the Office of the Director-General for Fisheries is named in the BOL — these were established through **BAA 13** (Administrative Code).[^6][^7][^9]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places four categories of conditions on this power:

   (1) **Express national law consistency requirement**: Art. XIII, Sec. 23 mandates that Bangsamoro fisheries policies be ***"Consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws."*** This is one of the strongest consistency requirements in the entire BOL — naming a specific national law by number and title.[^2]

   (2) **Constitutional subjection**: Art. XIII, Sec. 8 subjects natural resource authority to ***"the provisions of the Constitution."*** Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."***[^3][^10]

   (3) **Intergovernmental joint management**: Art. XIII, Sec. 18-19 require **joint cooperation** with the National Government in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf zones — fisheries in these offshore areas cannot be managed unilaterally.[^6]

   (4) **Non-prejudice to other Filipino citizens**: Art. XIII, Sec. 23 grants preferential fishing rights ***"without prejudice to the fishing rights of other citizens of the Philippines, whether residents or nonresidents."*** The Bangsamoro Government cannot exclude non-Bangsamoro Filipino citizens from fishing in Bangsamoro waters — only grant preferential treatment to residents.[^2]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to fishery, marine, and aquatic resources?**

A. Five categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: Autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."***[^10]

   (2) **Regalian Doctrine (Art. XII, Sec. 2)**: ***"All lands of the public domain, waters, minerals, coal, petroleum, and other mineral oils, all forces of potential energy, fisheries, forests or timber, wildlife, flora and fauna, and other natural resources are owned by the State."*** Fisheries are **State-owned**. The Bangsamoro Government exercises delegated authority — not ownership — over fishery resources.[^11]

   (3) **Marine wealth protection (Art. XII, Sec. 2, para. 2)**: ***"The State shall protect the nation's marine wealth in its archipelagic waters, territorial sea, and exclusive economic zone, and reserve its use and enjoyment exclusively to Filipino citizens."*** Only Filipino citizens may access marine resources — no foreign fishing in Philippine waters without congressional authorization.[^11]

   (4) **Subsistence fishermen's rights (Art. XIII, Sec. 7)**: ***"The State shall protect the rights of subsistence fishermen, especially of local communities, to the preferential use of local marine and fishing resources, both inland and offshore. It shall provide support to such fishermen through appropriate technology and research, adequate financial, production, and marketing assistance, and other services."*** This is a **constitutional mandate** for fishermen's protection that both the national and Bangsamoro governments must fulfill.[^12]

   (5) **Cooperative fish farming (Art. XII, Sec. 2, para. 3)**: ***"The Congress may, by law, allow small-scale utilization of natural resources by Filipino citizens, as well as cooperative fish farming, with priority to subsistence fishermen and fishworkers in rivers, lakes, bays, and lagoons."*** This authorizes cooperative-based fishery utilization with preferential treatment for subsistence fishers.[^11]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee for fisheries?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Preferential use for subsistence fishermen** (Art. XIII, Sec. 7) — the strongest constitutional protection for fisherfolk. Local communities have priority over commercial operators.

   (2) **Support from the State** (Art. XIII, Sec. 7) — technology, research, financial assistance, production and marketing support. This is an **affirmative obligation**, not merely a permission.

   (3) **Just share for fishworkers** (Art. XIII, Sec. 7) — ***"Fishworkers shall receive a just share from their labor in the utilization of marine and fishing resources."***

   (4) **Protection from foreign intrusion** (Art. XIII, Sec. 7) — the State must protect offshore fishing grounds of subsistence fishermen against foreign intrusion.

   (5) **Equal protection and due process** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — fishery regulation, licensing, and enforcement must satisfy procedural and substantive due process.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for fisheries?**

A. The national framework establishes a comprehensive system: **RA 8550** (Philippine Fisheries Code of 1998), as amended by **RA 10654** (2014), is the primary law — covering fishery management, conservation, utilization, licensing and permitting, penalties for illegal fishing, and institutional mechanisms including the **Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources (BFAR)**. **RA 8435** (AFMA) provides modernization and infrastructure support for the fishery sector. **RA 7160** (Local Government Code) assigns municipal water management to LGUs. **RA 9147** (Wildlife Act) and **RA 7586/11038** (NIPAS/E-NIPAS) protect marine wildlife and establish marine protected areas. **RA 9275** (Clean Water Act) manages water quality affecting fishery habitats. **RA 11901** provides fisheries financing. **EO 240** creates the FARMC system for participatory fishery governance. **EO 533** adopts Integrated Coastal Management as a national strategy. Together, these laws provide: a **licensing and permitting system**, **conservation and management framework**, **enforcement and penalty regime**, **institutional infrastructure** (BFAR), **participatory governance** (FARMCs), **financing**, and **environmental protection** for marine and aquatic resources.[^13][^14][^15]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national fisheries laws apply, with RA 8550 having the strongest application:

   (1) **RA 8550**, as amended by **RA 10654** — applies **directly** per the BOL's express consistency requirement. This is not merely suppletory — the BOL demands conformity.[^13][^14]
   (2) **RA 8435** (AFMA) — suppletory for fisheries modernization programs.[^15]
   (3) **RA 7160** — governs LGU fishery management in municipal waters; modified within BARMM by BAA 49 (Local Governance Code).
   (4) **RA 9147**, **RA 7586/11038**, **RA 9275** — suppletory for marine wildlife, protected areas, and water quality.
   (5) **RA 11901** — suppletory for fisheries financing.
   (6) **EO 240**, **EO 533** — suppletory for FARMC structures and coastal management.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for fisheries?**

A. The **Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources (BFAR)** is the primary national agency — exercising fishery licensing, resource management, enforcement, and technical services within BARMM through a regional field office. The **Philippine Coast Guard (PCG)** exercises maritime law enforcement including fishery violations in Bangsamoro waters. The **Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR)** manages marine protected areas pending the Art. XIII, Sec. 9 transfer. The **Philippine Navy** has maritime security responsibilities overlapping with fishery enforcement in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf. The **National Mapping and Resource Information Authority (NAMRIA)** is a member of the ad hoc Joint Body for delineating Bangsamoro waters (Art. XIII, Sec. 18).

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title I establishes MAFAR with its full mandate. Within MAFAR, the **Office of the Bangsamoro Director-General for Fisheries** (BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. I, Ch. 4, Sec. 10) is the specific institutional unit for fisheries. BAA 85, Chapter XVI allocates MAFAR's operational budget.[^16][^17]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. One standalone BAA has been enacted: **BAA 59** (Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute Act) creates BAFTI as the training and extension institution for agriculture and fisheries, attached to MAFAR. No standalone **Bangsamoro Fisheries Code** or **Fishery Management Act** has been enacted as of BAA 89.[^18]

**Q. What are MAFAR's mandate and fisheries functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title I defines MAFAR comprehensively:

   **Declaration of Policy** (Sec. 1): ***"It is the policy of the Bangsamoro Government to ensure resiliency and productivity for food security, income and rural employment in an enabling environment for farmers, fisherfolks, and agrarian reform beneficiaries."***[^16]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): ***"The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform and its attached agencies and bureaus shall promote sustainable Agri-fishery growth and development..."*** It shall ensure ***"the availability, adequacy, accessibility, and affordability of food supplies at all times."***[^16]

   **Powers and Functions** (Sec. 3) — 28 enumerated functions, of which the following are directly fishery-related:

   - (a) ***"Provide integrated services to farmers, fishermen, and other food producers on production, utilization, conservation, and disposition of agricultural livestock and fishery resources;"***
   - (c) ***"Promulgate and enforce all laws, rules and regulations governing the conservation and proper utilization of agricultural, livestock, and fishery resources;"***
   - (f) ***"Conduct, coordinate, and disseminate research studies on appropriate technologies for the improvement and development of agricultural crops, livestock, fisheries, and other allied commodities;"***
   - (g) ***"Provide the mechanisms for the participation of farmers, fishermen and agricultural entrepreneurs at all levels of policy-making, planning, and program formulation;"***
   - (j) ***"Approve charter contracts, issue permits for Special Commercial Fishing Boats and Licenses for Commercial Fishing Vessels and Gears to operate within the territorial waters of BARMM in accordance with national and regional laws;"***
   - (k) ***"Determine and designate fish landing points for all commercial fishing boats within its territorial limits;"***
   - (l) ***"Assist LGUs in the management and conservation of their municipal waters;"***
   - (m) ***"Regulate and supervise the production, capture, and gathering of fish and fishery/aquatic products;"***
   - (n) ***"Designate areas in the BARMM as fishery reserves for the exclusive use of the Bangsamoro Government or any of its political subdivisions, agencies or instrumentalities, or of the inhabitants therein, for the culture of fish and other aquatic animals for educational, research and scientific purposes in accordance with existing laws;"***[^19]

   **Director-General for Fisheries** (Sec. 7, Sec. 10): One of three Bangsamoro Directors-General within MAFAR — the highest career official for fisheries. The Office of the Bangsamoro Director-General for Fisheries has two services:

   (a) **Fisheries Operations Services** (Sec. 10-A) — headed by a Director II, with three divisions: (i) Fisheries Production Division; (ii) Fisheries Resource Management Division; (iii) Fisheries Post-Harvest and Marketing Division.

   (b) **Fisheries Research and Regulatory Services** (Sec. 10-B) — headed by a Director II, with two divisions: (i) Research Division; (ii) Fisheries Regulations and Law Enforcement Division.[^20]

**Q. What can MAFAR NOT do without a standalone Bangsamoro Fisheries Code?**

A. While BAA 13 gives MAFAR broad operational functions — including commercial fishing permits (Sec. 3(j)), fish landing point designation (Sec. 3(k)), municipal water management assistance (Sec. 3(l)), and fishery reserve designation (Sec. 3(n)) — it does not provide: a **comprehensive fishery management framework** with closed seasons, catch limits, and gear restrictions specific to BARMM waters; **penalties and sanctions** for illegal fishing offenses under Bangsamoro law (currently reliant on RA 8550/10654 penalties); **municipal vs. commercial fishery boundary definitions** adapted to BARMM's geographic context; **preferential fishing rights implementation** — Art. XIII, Sec. 23's preference for Bangsamoro people, IPs, and resident marginal fisherfolk requires implementing legislation defining registration, licensing priority, and preference mechanisms; **inland water management framework** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates legislation but none exists; **marine protected area declaration and management** under Art. XIII, Sec. 8; or **fishery revenue-sharing** between the Bangsamoro Government and LGUs for fishery licenses and permits.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. **BTA Resolution No. 23** called for the constitution of the Joint Body for the Sulu Sea Zones of Joint Cooperation. **BTA Resolution No. 370** urged finalization of Bangsamoro waters delineation. **BTA Resolution No. 122** asserted BARMM rights over Lake Lanao's inland waters. These resolutions signal Parliament's recognition that the **territorial and institutional prerequisites** for full fishery governance — water delineation, joint body establishment, and inland water authority — remain incomplete.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. MAFAR's mandate under BAA 13 explicitly covers fisheries — including commercial fishing permits, fishery reserves, and fishery regulation. The Office of the Bangsamoro Director-General for Fisheries provides a dedicated institutional unit within MAFAR. The assignment mirrors the national structure where fisheries falls under the Department of Agriculture (DA-BFAR). However, the power's scope is broader than MAFAR alone: **marine resource conservation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 8) overlaps with MENRE's environmental mandate, and **Zones of Joint Cooperation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 18-19) require intergovernmental coordination beyond any single ministry.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. The following institutions exist:

   - **Lead ministry**: MAFAR — BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. I; BAA 85, Ch. XVI. Operational.
   - **Fisheries office**: Office of the Bangsamoro Director-General for Fisheries — BAA 13, Sec. 10. Operational within MAFAR.
   - **Training institute**: BAFTI — BAA 59, attached to MAFAR. Operationalized.
   - **Supporting agencies**: MENRE (coastal/marine environmental management); Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31, environmental oversight).
   - **LGU-level**: Municipal fishery management under BAA 49 and RA 7160.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Two BOL-mandated institutions remain unestablished:

   (1) **Joint Body for the Zones of Joint Cooperation** (Art. VI, Sec. 5; Art. XIII, Sec. 19) — the intergovernmental body for Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf fishery and maritime governance. BTA Resolution No. 23 called for its constitution, but it has not been operationalized.

   (2) **Bangsamoro Authority for specific inland bodies of water** (Art. XIII, Sec. 22) — Parliament is authorized to ***"create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water."*** No such authority has been created — most critically for Lake Lanao and the Liguasan Marsh system, the two most significant inland fishery areas in BARMM.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Yes. Fully exercising this power would benefit from:

   (1) **Bangsamoro Fisheries and Aquatic Resources Management Council (B-FARMC)** — adapting the national FARMC concept (EO 240) for BARMM. A participatory co-management body at regional, provincial, and municipal levels to advise on fishery policy and resource management.

   (2) **Maritime enforcement unit within MAFAR** — dedicated fishery law enforcement capacity for Bangsamoro waters. Currently, enforcement depends on coordination with the PCG and PNP Maritime Group, which may not prioritize BARMM fishery enforcement.

   (3) **Lake Lanao Fishery Management Authority** and **Liguasan Marsh Management Authority** — dedicated bodies for the two largest inland fishery systems, as contemplated by Art. XIII, Sec. 22.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **BFAR** is the primary national agency operating within BARMM. BFAR maintains a regional office (BFAR-BARMM) that performs: **fishery licensing** for commercial fishing vessels; **resource assessment** (stock assessment, catch monitoring); **law enforcement** (fishery patrols, anti-IUU operations); **post-harvest and technology** support; and **aquaculture development** programs. The **Philippine Coast Guard** exercises maritime law enforcement in Bangsamoro waters — including apprehension of illegal fishing vessels. **DENR** manages existing marine protected areas pending transfer under Art. XIII, Sec. 9. The **Philippine Fisheries Development Authority (PFDA)** operates fish ports and post-harvest facilities.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. National programs include: (1) BFAR's **Fisheries Livelihood Program** — provision of fishing gear, boats, and post-harvest equipment to marginal fisherfolk; (2) **Fisheries Resource Management** — enforcement of closed seasons, gear restrictions, and fishing ground delineation; (3) **Aquaculture Development** — freshwater and mariculture technology dissemination; (4) **FARMC organization** — establishment and support of Fisheries and Aquatic Resource Management Councils in coastal and lakeside LGUs; (5) PFDA fish port operations; (6) DA-BFAR **post-harvest and marketing** support.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **incomplete**. MAFAR exercises some fishery functions under BAA 13 — including commercial fishing permits (Sec. 3(j)) and fishery reserve designation (Sec. 3(n)). However, **BFAR retains significant operational presence** within BARMM — particularly in commercial fishing vessel licensing, resource assessment, and enforcement. The **transfer of marine protected area management** under Art. XIII, Sec. 9 has not been completed — DENR retains management authority over existing protected areas in BARMM waters. The **Bangsamoro waters delineation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 18) has not been finalized — BTA Resolution No. 370 urges completion. Without this delineation, MAFAR's spatial jurisdiction for fishery management remains partially undefined.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes, across multiple functions: (1) **commercial fishing licensing** — both MAFAR (BAA 13, Sec. 3(j)) and BFAR issue permits; (2) **fishery law enforcement** — MAFAR's Fisheries Regulations and Law Enforcement Division operates alongside BFAR enforcement and the PCG; (3) **marine protected area management** — DENR retains management pending the Art. XIII, Sec. 9 transfer, while MENRE and MAFAR exercise Bangsamoro environmental authority; (4) **resource assessment and research** — both BFAR and MAFAR conduct fishery research. These overlaps exist primarily because devolution is incomplete and no standalone Bangsamoro Fisheries Code clarifies jurisdictional boundaries.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MAFAR has been **institutionally established** under BAA 13 with the Office of the Bangsamoro Director-General for Fisheries (two services, five divisions), an operational budget under BAA 85, and provincial and municipal offices. **BAFTI** (BAA 59) has been created to deliver fishery training and extension services. MAFAR exercises **commercial fishing permitting** under Sec. 3(j), has designated **fish landing points** under Sec. 3(k), and assists LGUs with **municipal water management** under Sec. 3(l). The Parliament has adopted **five resolutions** signaling intent on fishery governance — including calling for the Joint Body's constitution (Res. 23), Bangsamoro waters delineation (Res. 370), and inland water rights (Res. 122). **PB Nos. 180, 221, 289, and 291** have been filed addressing coastal management, fishery infrastructure, fisherfolk welfare, and fishery education. No standalone fisheries code has been enacted.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three potential advantages:

   (1) **Preferential fishing rights** — Art. XIII, Sec. 23 grants a BOL-level preferential fishing right to Bangsamoro people, IPs, and resident marginal fisherfolk that has no equivalent in the national Fisheries Code. This creates a legal basis for prioritizing local fishers over commercial operators from outside the region.

   (2) **Halal fishery development** — BAA 59, Sec. 10 mandates BAFTI to ***"prioritize the development of the Halal industry"*** in all training modules, including fishery post-harvest processing. The national framework has no equivalent mandate for Halal-certified fishery products — giving BARMM a potential market advantage for Halal fishery exports to BIMP-EAGA and Muslim-majority countries.

   (3) **Conflict-sensitive fishery management** — BARMM's fishery framework operates in a post-conflict context where fishing communities in Basilan, Tawi-Tawi, and the Maguindanao marshlands have been affected by armed conflict. MAFAR can design livelihood programs for conflict-affected fisherfolk — a context the national BFAR framework does not address.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. Three binding constraints:

   (1) **No standalone Bangsamoro Fisheries Code** — MAFAR operates under BAA 13's general mandate and the national Fisheries Code (RA 8550/10654). Without a Bangsamoro fisheries law, MAFAR cannot: implement Art. XIII, Sec. 23's preferential fishing rights; establish BARMM-specific closed seasons and catch limits; create a distinct penalty regime; or define the jurisdictional boundary with BFAR.

   (2) **Incomplete Bangsamoro waters delineation** — the ad hoc Joint Body under Art. XIII, Sec. 18 has not completed the delineation of Bangsamoro municipal waters, regional waters, and Zones of Joint Cooperation. Without spatial boundaries, MAFAR cannot exercise full jurisdiction over marine fisheries.

   (3) **Joint Body not operationalized** — the Joint Body for the Zones of Joint Cooperation (Art. VI, Sec. 5; Art. XIII, Sec. 19) has not been constituted despite BTA Resolution No. 23. Fishery management in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf — critical for Basilan and Tawi-Tawi's island communities — cannot proceed through the BOL's envisioned intergovernmental mechanism.

   The **single biggest constraint** is the incomplete waters delineation — without knowing where Bangsamoro waters begin and end, no fishery law or institutional arrangement can be fully operationalized.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a **comprehensive fishery governance system** over three decades:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 8550 (1998), as amended by RA 10654 (2014), provides a complete regulatory framework with provisions on municipal fisheries, commercial fisheries, aquaculture, post-harvest, penalties (including vessel confiscation), and institutional mechanisms. Supplemented by AFMA (RA 8435), Wildlife Act (RA 9147), NIPAS (RA 7586/11038), and multiple implementing rules.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: BFAR — established under the Department of Agriculture, with regional offices in all 17 regions, specialized divisions (fishery management, enforcement, aquaculture, research), research stations, and a nationwide enforcement arm. PFDA operates 12 fish ports.
   - **Regulatory systems**: Commercial fishing vessel licensing, Comprehensive National Fisheries Industry Development Plan, stock assessment programs, Vessel Monitoring System (VMS) for commercial fishing vessels, catch documentation, and FARMC co-management system at barangay, municipal, and national levels.
   - **IT and data**: BFAR maintains fishery registration databases, the Boat Registration System (BoatR), and fishery statistics systems.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in four areas:

   (1) **No preferential fishing rights mechanism**: The national Fisheries Code (RA 8550) provides preferential use for municipal fisherfolk in municipal waters (Sec. 17) but does not have the BOL's additional layer of preference for Bangsamoro people and IPs in Bangsamoro regional waters.

   (2) **No Halal fishery certification**: BFAR's regulatory framework does not include Halal certification for fishery products — a critical gap for BARMM's predominantly Muslim fishing communities seeking access to Halal markets.

   (3) **Underserved enforcement**: BFAR-BARMM has historically been one of the least-resourced regional offices — fewer patrol vessels, less enforcement staff, and weaker surveillance coverage compared to regions in Luzon and Visayas. IUU fishing in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf remains a persistent problem.

   (4) **No conflict-sensitive design**: The national framework does not account for post-conflict realities — displaced fishing communities, destroyed fishing infrastructure, and disrupted market chains in conflict-affected areas of Basilan, Tawi-Tawi, and mainland Maguindanao.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: **fishery conservation standards** (closed seasons for commercially important species, gear restrictions, marine protected area management protocols), **VMS requirements** for commercial vessels, and **post-harvest quality standards**. These are technically sound and aligned with international obligations.
   - **Adapt**: **licensing and permitting system** — take BFAR's framework and add the Art. XIII, Sec. 23 **preferential fishing rights** mechanism. Adapt the **FARMC structure** into a BARMM-specific participatory fishery governance system. Adapt **enforcement protocols** for BARMM's geographic context (island provinces, limited patrol capacity).
   - **Replace**: **institutional framework** — MAFAR's Office of the Director-General for Fisheries should replace BFAR within BARMM for fishery licensing, regulation, and enforcement. Build a **Halal fishery value chain** framework that the national system does not provide. Create **conflict-sensitive fishery livelihood programs** tailored to BARMM's post-conflict context.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes, but it is **moderate** compared to powers like Islamic banking or cooperatives. The primary Shari'ah dimensions are: (1) **Halal fishery products** — seafood is generally halal under all Islamic schools of thought, but post-harvest processing, storage, and handling must maintain Halal standards (no cross-contamination with haram substances); (2) **environmental stewardship** (*amanah*) — Islamic principles of resource stewardship prohibit wastefulness (*israf*) and require sustainable use of natural resources; (3) **prohibition of *israf*** — overfishing and destructive fishing methods are prohibited under the Islamic principle against waste and excess.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. XIII, Sec. 23 or the fisheries-related provisions. However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact laws on Shari'ah matters including commercial law — fishery trade and marketing fall within this scope. BAA 59, Sec. 10 mandates BAFTI to ***"prioritize the development of the Halal industry and shall include the relevance of promoting Halal standards and its incentives in all training modules"*** — this is the most direct link between Islamic standards and fisheries in enacted legislation.[^18]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four Islamic principles govern fisheries legislation:

   (1) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — marine and aquatic resources are a public trust. Fishery managers and regulators bear a Shari'ah obligation to manage these resources sustainably for current and future generations.

   (2) **Prohibition of *israf*** (waste/excess) — overfishing, bycatch discarding, and destruction of marine habitats constitute *israf*. Conservation measures have Shari'ah grounding.

   (3) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — fishery regulation serving food security and livelihood protection is justified under this principle.

   (4) **Halal compliance** — fishery post-harvest processing, trade, and export should comply with Halal standards to serve the predominantly Muslim population and access Halal export markets.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) does not specifically address fisheries or marine resource management. No enacted Shari'ah legislation specifically governs fisheries in BARMM. BAA 13's Halal Board provisions (Bk. VI, Tit. IV) include coordination with MAFAR on Halal industry development — relevant to fishery product Halal certification. No known *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address fishery management, though general pronouncements on environmental stewardship (*amanah*) and waste prevention (*israf*) apply.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro fisheries legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is **principled integration** — fishery legislation guided by Islamic values of stewardship, waste prevention, and Halal compliance without mandating Shari'ah-specific regulatory tracks. Specifically: (a) **Halal certification** for fishery post-harvest processing should be integrated into MAFAR's regulatory framework through coordination with the Bangsamoro Halal Board; (b) **conservation rationale** should cite *amanah* and *israf* prohibition alongside secular environmental justifications; (c) **no mandatory Shari'ah compliance track** for fishery operations — unlike cooperatives (where *mudarabah*/*musharakah* structures create a dual-track need), fisheries regulation does not require structurally different Shari'ah-compliant alternatives.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national fisheries law?**

A. BARMM has **limited legislative autonomy** compared to most other Art. V, Sec. 2 powers because of the **express consistency requirement**. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XIII, Sec. 23 mandates that Bangsamoro fisheries policies be ***"Consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws."*** This is among the strongest consistency requirements in the BOL — naming a specific national law by number. As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), the Regalian Doctrine (Art. XII, Sec. 2) vests State ownership of fisheries, and Art. XIII, Sec. 7 mandates subsistence fishermen's protection.

   The **permissible scope of divergence** is:

   (1) **Additions above the national floor**: BARMM can enact fishery regulations **stricter than** RA 8550 — additional closed seasons, lower catch limits, more gear restrictions, additional marine protected areas — because the consistency requirement means conformity with the national framework, not prohibition of higher standards. *Tano v. Socrates* confirms that local governments can enact stricter fishery measures.

   (2) **Preferential fishing rights**: Art. XIII, Sec. 23's preference for Bangsamoro people, IPs, and resident marginal fisherfolk is a **BOL-granted right** that does not exist in RA 8550. BARMM must implement this through legislation — creating a licensing preference that goes beyond the national framework without contradicting it.

   (3) **Halal fishery standards**: The national framework is silent on Halal certification — BARMM can build this dimension without any consistency conflict.

   (4) **Institutional design**: BARMM can establish its own fishery governance institutions (as it has with MAFAR and BAFTI) without contradicting RA 8550 — the consistency requirement applies to **substantive fishery management standards**, not institutional design.

   BARMM **cannot** diverge by: lowering fishery conservation standards below RA 8550; exempting fishing operations from RA 10654's IUU prevention requirements; or excluding Filipino citizens from Bangsamoro waters (Art. XIII, Sec. 23 non-prejudice clause).

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For Bangsamoro fisherfolk**: They gain **preferential fishing rights** that the national framework does not provide — BOL-level preference in licensing and access within Bangsamoro regional waters. They may benefit from **Halal certification** for fishery products accessing export markets.

   (2) **For non-BARMM Filipino fisherfolk**: They retain their constitutional right to fish in Bangsamoro waters (Art. XIII, Sec. 23 non-prejudice clause) but may face **lower priority** in licensing and allocation. This is constitutionally permissible because the preference rests on the BOL (a congressional act) and does not **exclude** — only **deprioritizes**.

   (3) **For commercial fishing operators**: Operators licensed nationally (BFAR) and operating in Bangsamoro waters must also comply with MAFAR regulations — creating a **dual compliance obligation** until jurisdictional boundaries are clarified.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. Unlike most other powers, national fisheries law (RA 8550/10654) **continues to apply directly** — not merely suppletorily — because of the express consistency requirement. A Bangsamoro Fisheries Code would operate as a **supplementary and implementing framework** — adding preferential rights, Halal standards, institutional design, and stricter conservation measures on top of the national floor, rather than replacing it.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Three critical jurisdictional overlaps exist:

   (1) **MAFAR vs. BFAR**: Both agencies exercise fishery licensing, regulation, and enforcement within BARMM. BAA 13, Sec. 3(j) authorizes MAFAR to issue commercial fishing permits, while BFAR under RA 8550 also exercises this function nationally. No legislation or MOA clarifies the division of authority — whether MAFAR replaces BFAR within BARMM or operates alongside it.

   (2) **Marine protected areas**: DENR retains management of existing marine protected areas pending the Art. XIII, Sec. 9 transfer. No implementing legislation or transfer protocol has been completed. MENRE and MAFAR exercise Bangsamoro environmental authority without clarity on which agency assumes marine protected area management.

   (3) **Bangsamoro waters boundaries**: The ad hoc Joint Body (Art. XIII, Sec. 18) has not completed delineation. Without defined boundaries, the spatial extent of MAFAR's fishery jurisdiction versus BFAR's national jurisdiction is undefined — particularly in waters between BARMM coastal LGUs and non-BARMM adjacent LGUs.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB No. 180** (Integrated Coastal Management Policy) would address the coastal management gap but not the fisheries code gap. **PB No. 221** (Bureau of Agricultural and Fisheries Engineering) would address fishery infrastructure but not regulatory authority. **PB No. 289** (Agri-Fishery Workers Social Protection) would address fisherfolk welfare. **PB No. 291** (Agri-Fishery Education) would address human capacity.

   **Critical gap remaining**: No pending PB has been identified that would enact a **comprehensive Bangsamoro Fisheries Code** implementing Art. XIII, Sec. 23's preferential fishing rights, establishing the inland water management framework under Art. XIII, Sec. 22, or clarifying the MAFAR-BFAR jurisdictional boundary. This is the most significant legislative gap.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) identifies agriculture and fisheries as the **largest economic sector** in BARMM — accounting for 38.94% of the region's economic output. The BDP targets sustainable agri-fishery production, **Bangsamoro Strategic Agricultural and Fisheries Development Areas (BSAFDA)** covering 125,562 hectares of inland fishery areas, and fisheries modernization. BDP Chapter 3 (Spatial Strategy) positions island provinces — Basilan as the ***"Agri-Fishery Industrial Island"*** and Tawi-Tawi as the ***"aqua-marine and eco-cultourism island"*** — with fisheries as the primary economic driver. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, depends on fishery development for food security and coastal livelihood resilience. **Pillar 4 (Environmental Stewardship)** depends on marine and coastal resource conservation — sustainable fishery management and marine protected areas.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **Bangsamoro Fisheries Code** and completed **waters delineation**: (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 23's **preferential fishing rights** for Bangsamoro people and marginal fisherfolk cannot be implemented — the licensing priority mechanism has no legal basis; (2) **IUU fishing** in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf continues unabated without the Joint Body and coordinated enforcement; (3) island province economies (Basilan, Tawi-Tawi) — where fisheries is the primary livelihood — cannot transition from artisanal to modernized fisheries without a regional regulatory framework; (4) **inland fisheries** in Lake Lanao, Liguasan Marsh, and other inland water bodies operate under national law alone — the Art. XIII, Sec. 22 exclusive authority remains unexercised; (5) **BSAFDA development** cannot reach its inland fishery targets without a management framework for the 125,562 hectares designated for inland fisheries; (6) **Halal fishery export potential** — access to BIMP-EAGA and Middle East markets for Halal-certified fishery products — cannot be systematized without a Halal fishery certification framework; (7) the **BDP spatial strategy** positioning Basilan and Tawi-Tawi as fishery hubs cannot materialize without the legal, institutional, and territorial foundations that remain incomplete.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why is Art. XIII, Sec. 23's consistency requirement with RA 8550 significant — and how does it differ from other powers?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 23's requirement that Bangsamoro fisheries policies be ***"Consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws"*** is one of the **most restrictive consistency clauses** in the entire BOL. Most enumerated powers under Art. V, Sec. 2 have no such consistency requirement — the BOL grants authority and leaves implementation to the Bangsamoro Parliament. Only a handful of powers — fisheries (Art. XIII, Sec. 23), civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39: ***"in accordance with national laws"***), GOCCs (Art. XII, Sec. 30: ***"compliance with Republic Act No. 10149"***), and cadastral surveys (Art. XIII, Sec. 17: ***"upon authority of the President"***) — have express consistency or coordination requirements. This means fisheries is the power where BARMM has the **least legislative autonomy** to diverge from the national framework. Bangsamoro fisheries legislation must work **within and on top of** RA 8550, not replace it.

**Q. What is the relationship between this power and the Zones of Joint Cooperation?**

A. The Zones of Joint Cooperation (Art. XIII, Sec. 18-19) are the **most distinctive feature** of this power — no other Art. V, Sec. 2 power has an equivalent intergovernmental joint management mechanism. The Zones cover waters in the **Sulu Sea** and **Moro Gulf** beyond the 19 km Bangsamoro regional waters. Within these zones, fishery management is not unilateral — the BOL requires the Bangsamoro Government and National Government to jointly: (a) **protect traditional fishing grounds**; (b) **equitably share benefits**; and (c) **ensure island interconnectivity**. The Joint Body — co-chaired by the Secretary of Agriculture and a Bangsamoro Minister, with representatives from LGUs adjacent to both the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf — is the institutional mechanism. BTA Resolution No. 23 called for its constitution and Resolution No. 370 urged finalization of the waters delineation. Neither has been completed. Until the Joint Body is operational and the zones delineated, fishery management in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf remains in a **governance vacuum** — neither fully national nor Bangsamoro.

**Q. How does Art. III, Sec. 4's definition of Bangsamoro waters interact with fishery jurisdiction?**

A. Art. III, Sec. 4 establishes a **two-tier water classification**: Bangsamoro **municipal waters** extend up to **15 kilometers** and Bangsamoro **regional waters** extend up to **19 kilometers** from the low-water mark. This creates three fishery management zones: (a) **municipal waters** (0-15 km) — managed by LGUs under BAA 49 and RA 7160, primarily for artisanal and municipal fisheries; (b) **regional waters** (15-19 km) — managed by the Bangsamoro Government (MAFAR), primarily for commercial fisheries; (c) **Zones of Joint Cooperation** (beyond 19 km) — jointly managed with the National Government under Art. XIII, Sec. 18-19. A Bangsamoro Fisheries Code must define the licensing, gear, and access rules for each zone — and the demarcation provisions where BARMM coastal LGUs are adjacent to non-BARMM LGUs (Art. III, Sec. 4 provides the equidistant line rule for adjacent shores less than 30 km apart).

**Q. How does this power interact with power (c) Agriculture, (d) Ancestral domain, and (gg) Inland waters?**

A. Four related powers intersect:

   (1) **Power (c) — Agriculture, livestock, and food security**: Fisheries and agriculture are institutionally merged under MAFAR. Art. XIII, Sec. 23 addresses both agriculture and fisheries in a single provision. BAFTI (BAA 59) covers both. The powers are **operationally inseparable** within MAFAR's mandate.

   (2) **Power (d) — Ancestral domain and natural resources**: Art. XIII, Sec. 8's natural resources authority covers marine and coastal resources broadly. Art. XIII, Sec. 12 protects IP rights to natural resources including fisheries within ancestral domains — requiring free, prior, and informed consent for development activities.

   (3) **Power (gg) — Inland waters**: Art. XIII, Sec. 22 grants exclusive authority over inland waters. Inland fisheries — Lake Lanao tilapia and carp, Liguasan Marsh freshwater fisheries, river fisheries throughout mainland BARMM — fall under this related power.

   (4) **Power (x) — Environment, parks, forest management**: Marine protected areas, coral reef conservation, and mangrove management overlap with fishery habitat protection. MENRE is the lead for environmental management; MAFAR for fishery resource management — requiring coordination.

**Q. What would a Bangsamoro Fisheries Code need to cover?**

A. A comprehensive Bangsamoro Fisheries Code, consistent with RA 8550 as required by Art. XIII, Sec. 23, should address:

   (1) **Preferential fishing rights** — implementing Art. XIII, Sec. 23's preference for Bangsamoro people, IPs, and resident marginal fisherfolk through a licensing priority system.
   (2) **Fishery zoning** — defining rules for municipal waters (0-15 km), regional waters (15-19 km), and coordination with the Joint Body for the Zones of Joint Cooperation.
   (3) **Inland water fishery management** — fulfilling Art. XIII, Sec. 22's legislative mandate for Lake Lanao, Liguasan Marsh, and other inland water bodies.
   (4) **MAFAR-BFAR jurisdictional boundary** — clarifying which functions transfer to MAFAR and which remain with BFAR within BARMM.
   (5) **Enforcement and penalties** — BARMM-specific sanctions for illegal fishing, operating above RA 8550/10654's floor.
   (6) **Halal fishery framework** — certification, post-harvest processing standards, and market access for Halal fishery products.
   (7) **Marine protected area management** — implementing Art. XIII, Sec. 8's authority and the Art. XIII, Sec. 9 transfer.
   (8) **Participatory governance** — BARMM-specific FARMC or equivalent co-management mechanism.
   (9) **Climate adaptation** — fishery resilience measures for island communities vulnerable to climate change impacts on marine resources.
