# (pp) Public Utilities' Operations

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(pp)** — ***"Public utilities' operations;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(pp) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Partial — Art. XIII, Sec. 36 requires power generation/distribution interconnection ***"subject to limitations under Republic Act No. 9136"*** (EPIRA); Art. XIII, Sec. 39 preserves Congress's franchise power over telecommunications; Art. XIII, Sec. 37 requires ***"cooperation and coordination"*** with national government on water supply connecting outside BARMM. No blanket "consistent with national law" qualifier applies to the full scope of this power. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Transportation and Communications (MOTC)** — for transportation and telecommunications franchises (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 1-20); **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources, and Energy (MENRE)** — for power generation and distribution utilities (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 1-3 of the MENRE chapter); **Ministry of Public Works (MPW)** — for water supply infrastructure (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 1-3 of the MPW chapter) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Intergovernmental Energy Board (Art. VI, Sec. 7); Bangsamoro Land Transportation Franchising and Regulatory Board (BLTFRB); Bangsamoro Telecommunications Commission; Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 11 (Infrastructure); Chapter 7 (Economic Development) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None expressly named. However, MENRE's BAA 13 mandate is ***"subject to laws to be enacted by Parliament"*** for electric power industry restructuring (Sec. 3(o)) and power generation/distribution utilities (Sec. 3(p)). |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. ### BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(pp) enumerates ***"Public utilities' operations"*** as a power of the Bangsamoro Government. This is a broad grant covering the operational regulation of all public utilities within BARMM — encompassing energy generation and distribution, transportation franchising, telecommunications licensing, and water supply services. The BOL elaborates this power across four dedicated sections: Sec. 36 (energy and power generation), Sec. 37 (water supply as part of public works), Sec. 38 (transportation), and Sec. 39 (telecommunications), all in Art. XIII.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**
A. The BOL provides extensive elaboration:

- **Energy and power** (Art. XIII, Sec. 36) — authority to promote investment in the power sector; authority to ***"build power generation and distribution utilities operating exclusively in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** through a Ministry of Energy; 25-year mandatory divestment; electric cooperative assistance; Agus Hydropower Complex preferential rights; Intergovernmental Energy Board coordination.[^2]
- **Transportation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 38) — authority to ***"grant regional franchises, licenses, and permits to land, sea, and air transportation"*** within BARMM; registration of transportation operating exclusively within BARMM; quasi-judicial powers over land and water transportation operations. Airside operations of airports remain with the National Government.[^3]
- **Telecommunications** (Art. XIII, Sec. 39) — authority to ***"grant regional franchises, licenses, and permits to telecommunication utilities whose frequencies are confined to and whose main offices are located within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** Congress retains the power to grant national franchises within BARMM.[^4]
- **Water supply** (Art. XIII, Sec. 37) — the National Government funds and implements water supply construction/maintenance, with ***"cooperation and coordination"*** between governments for systems connecting outside BARMM.[^5]
- **Intergovernmental coordination** (Art. VI, Sec. 7) — creates the Intergovernmental Energy Board to ***"resolve all matters specified in Section 36, Article XIII"*** and other energy issues. Composed of representatives from both governments' energy regulatory commissions and electrification administrations.[^6]
- **Intra-regional routes** (Art. XIII, Sec. 21) — transportation routes through Zones of Joint Cooperation are classified as intra-regional, expanding the scope of BARMM's franchise authority.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**
A. There is no expressly named legislative mandate (no "Parliament shall enact a Public Utilities Code"). However, BAA 13 conditions two MENRE powers on future legislation: electric power industry restructuring shall be ***"subject to laws to be enacted by Parliament"*** (Sec. 3(o)), and power generation/distribution utility supervision shall be ***"as may be provided in a law passed by Parliament"*** (Sec. 3(p)).[^8] This creates a de facto legislative mandate — MENRE cannot fully exercise energy regulatory functions without a standalone energy act.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**
A. Yes. Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) directs the Bangsamoro Government to exercise power generation/distribution authority ***"through the Ministry of Energy it shall create."*** This is a BOL-mandated institution that has **not** been established as a separate ministry. Energy functions are currently housed within the Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources, and Energy (MENRE). Art. VI, Sec. 7 creates the **Intergovernmental Energy Board** for coordination with the National Government on energy matters.[^9]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**
A. The BOL imposes several significant constraints:

1. **"Operating exclusively" limitation** — BARMM's authority over power utilities (Sec. 36(a)), electric cooperatives (Sec. 36(a)), and transportation registration (Sec. 38(b)) is limited to entities operating ***"exclusively in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***
2. **EPIRA compliance** — power grid interconnection is ***"subject to limitations under Republic Act No. 9136"*** (Sec. 36(a)).
3. **25-year mandatory divestment** — BARMM-built power utilities must be divested within 25 years (Sec. 36(a)).
4. **National Government notification** — the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall notify the National Government insofar as power generation investments are concerned"*** (Sec. 36).
5. **Congressional franchise power** — ***"nothing herein shall be construed as limiting in any way the power of the Congress of the Philippines to grant national franchises in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** (Sec. 39).
6. **Airside operations** — the National Government ***"shall regulate the airside operations of all existing airports"*** (Sec. 38).
7. **Cross-boundary water supply** — requires ***"cooperation and coordination"*** with national/local government bodies (Sec. 37).
8. **Local supply priority** — power plants ***"may only sell power over the national transmission grid once the power supply needs of its customers have been completely met"*** (Sec. 36(a)).[^10]

### Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**
A. The constitutional framework for public utilities is among the most specific in the 1987 Constitution:

- **Universal constraint (Art. X, Sec. 15)** — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***[^11]
- **Art. XII, Sec. 11** — ***"No franchise, certificate, or any other form of authorization for the operation of a public utility shall be granted except to citizens of the Philippines or to corporations or associations organized under the laws of the Philippines at least sixty per centum of whose capital is owned by such citizens, nor shall such franchise, certificate, or authorization be exclusive in character or for a longer period than fifty years."*** This is the primary constitutional constraint — all public utility franchises granted by BARMM must comply with these **citizenship**, **exclusivity**, and **duration** restrictions.[^12]
- **Art. XII, Sec. 11 (cont.)** — franchises are ***"subject to amendment, alteration, or repeal by the Congress when the common good so requires."*** Congress retains ultimate authority over franchise terms.
- **Art. II, Sec. 20** — the State recognizes the ***"indispensable role of the private sector"*** — relevant to BARMM's mandate to promote private investment in power generation.

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**
A. Art. XII, Sec. 11 guarantees the **right of equity participation** by the general public in public utilities. The Constitution also implicitly protects consumers through the equal protection clause (Art. III, Sec. 1) — public utility rates and service standards must apply without arbitrary discrimination. The **50-year maximum franchise period** and **non-exclusivity requirement** protect the public against monopolistic entrenchment.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**
A. Art. XII, Sec. 11 is self-executing in its ownership restrictions but requires legislation for implementation of franchise-granting procedures. Importantly, RA 11659 (2022) **narrowed the definition of "public utility"** under Art. XII, Sec. 11 — only distribution of electricity, transmission of electricity, water pipeline distribution/sewerage, and seaports/airports are now classified as "public utilities" subject to the 60% Filipino ownership requirement. Transportation and telecommunications are reclassified as "public services" with different (or no) foreign ownership caps.[^13]

### National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**
A. The national framework is segmented by utility type:

- **Power**: RA 9136 (EPIRA, 2001) — restructured the electric power industry into generation, transmission, distribution, and supply; created the Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC) as the primary regulator; mandated privatization of NPC assets through PSALM; established the Wholesale Electricity Spot Market (WESM).
- **Telecommunications**: RA 7925 (1995) — national telecommunications policy; NTC as regulator; national franchise-granting by Congress.
- **Water**: PD 198 (1973), as amended by RA 9286 (2004) — Provincial Water Utilities Act; LWUA as supervisory agency for water districts.
- **Transportation**: Various national franchise laws for specific operators; LTO, LTFRB, MARINA, CAAP under the Department of Transportation (DOTr).
- **General**: RA 11659 (2022) — Amended Public Service Act; redefined "public utility" for ownership purposes.[^14]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**
A. RA 9136 (EPIRA) applies **directly** — the BOL expressly references it. RA 11659 (Amended Public Service Act) applies **directly** — it is a law of general application redefining public utility classifications. RA 7925 (Telecommunications) applies **suppletorily** — the BOL grants BARMM regional franchise authority but NTC retains jurisdiction over nationally-franchised operators. PD 198/RA 9286 (Water Districts) applies **suppletorily** — LWUA retains supervisory functions pending BARMM legislation. RA 10531 (NEA Reform Act) applies **suppletorily** — NEA retains supervisory authority over electric cooperatives in BARMM.[^15]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**
A. Multiple national agencies retain functions within BARMM: **Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC)** — rate regulation, WESM oversight; **National Electrification Administration (NEA)** — electric cooperative supervision; **National Telecommunications Commission (NTC)** — national franchise operators, spectrum management; **Local Water Utilities Administration (LWUA)** — water district supervision; **Department of Transportation (DOTr)** — airside airport operations, national transportation routes. These agencies' functions would not fully transfer upon BAA enactment — the BOL specifically preserves several national jurisdictions (airside operations, congressional franchises, EPIRA grid rules).[^16]

### Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**
A. This power is split across three ministries:

- **Ministry of Transportation and Communications (MOTC)** — primary for transportation franchises and telecommunications (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 1-20 of the MOTC chapter).
- **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources, and Energy (MENRE)** — primary for energy and power generation/distribution (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 1-3 of the MENRE chapter).
- **Ministry of Public Works (MPW)** — primary for water supply infrastructure (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 1-3 of the MPW chapter).[^17]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**
A. None. No standalone public utilities code, transportation code, telecommunications code, energy code, or water supply act has been enacted as of BAA 89. All three ministries operate under their BAA 13 mandates only.

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**
A. **MOTC** (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 1-3): The Ministry is mandated as ***"the primary policy, planning, programming, coordinating, implementing, regulating, and administrative entity of the Bangsamoro Government in the promotion, development, and regulation of dependable and coordinated networks of transportation and communications systems."*** Its 16 enumerated functions include: (a) administer and enforce transportation/communications laws; (b) formulate policies; (c) ***"grant regional franchises, licenses, and permits to and exercise quasi-judicial powers over land, sea, and air transportation"***; (d) register transportation; (e) manage inland waterway navigation; (f) manage landside port/airport operations; (g) ***"grant regional franchises, licenses, and permits to telecommunication utilities"***; (h) issue CPCNs for telecommunications; (i) register and regulate telecommunications; (j) register cargo/courier services; (k) monitor postal services; (l) generate revenues and determine utility rates; (m) impose duties on vessels/wharves; (n) ensure free movement in Zones of Joint Cooperation; (o-p) devolved functions and other functions.[^18]

The MOTC has 8 sectoral offices: Bangsamoro Telecommunications Commission, Bangsamoro Land Transportation Office (BLTO), Bangsamoro Land Transportation Franchising and Regulatory Board (BLTFRB), Bangsamoro Ports Management Authority (BPMA), Bangsamoro Maritime Industry Authority (BMARINA), Civil Aviation Authority of the Bangsamoro (CAAB), Civil Aeronautics Board of the Bangsamoro (CABB), and other sectoral offices as created by law.[^19]

**MENRE** (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 3(o)-(p)): Among MENRE's enumerated functions: (o) ***"subject to laws to be enacted by Parliament, supervise the implementation of electric power industry restructuring to establish a competitive, market-based environment, and encourage private sector participation"***; (p) ***"build and exercise supervision and control over energy-projects relative to power generation and distribution utilities, as may be provided in a law passed by Parliament."***[^20]

**MPW** (BAA 13, Book VI, Sec. 2): Mandated to undertake ***"the planning, design and construction of infrastructures, such as, but not limited to roads and bridges, flood control, water supply projects and other public works facilities."***[^21]

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**
A. Critical gaps:

- **MOTC**: Cannot establish a comprehensive regulatory framework for transportation and telecommunications rates, service standards, consumer protection, or penalty provisions. The franchise-granting function exists but the regulatory depth — standard franchise terms, service quality metrics, enforcement mechanisms — requires standalone legislation.
- **MENRE**: **Explicitly cannot** fully exercise energy regulatory functions — BAA 13 conditions both electric power industry restructuring (Sec. 3(o)) and power generation/distribution utility supervision (Sec. 3(p)) on ***"laws to be enacted by Parliament."*** Without a standalone energy act, MENRE cannot: create a competitive electricity market structure, set regional electricity rates, establish a Bangsamoro energy regulatory body, or define the framework for the 25-year divestment mandate.
- **MPW**: Cannot establish water supply service standards, water quality requirements, or a Bangsamoro water utility regulatory framework distinct from the national LWUA system.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**
A. Public utilities operations is a **multi-ministry power** — no single MOA covers its full scope. MOTC is correctly assigned for transportation and telecommunications franchise operations. MENRE is correctly assigned for energy operations. MPW is correctly assigned for water supply infrastructure. However, the BOL mandates creation of a separate ***"Ministry of Energy"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a)) that has not been established — energy functions remain bundled within MENRE, creating a mismatch between the BOL's institutional design and the current organizational structure.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**
A.
- **Lead ministry (transportation/telecom)**: MOTC — BAA 13, Book VI; BAA 85
- **Lead ministry (energy)**: MENRE — BAA 13, Book VI; BAA 85
- **Lead ministry (water supply)**: MPW — BAA 13, Book VI; BAA 85
- **Sectoral offices under MOTC**: BLTO, BLTFRB, BPMA, BMARINA, CAAB, CABB, Bangsamoro Telecommunications Commission — all established by BAA 13, Sec. 4 and Sec. 9-15 of the MOTC chapter
- **Intergovernmental body**: Intergovernmental Energy Board — created by BOL Art. VI, Sec. 7; operational status [UNVERIFIED]
- **Intergovernmental body**: Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board — created by BOL Art. VI, Sec. 6; relevant for cross-boundary infrastructure coordination

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**
A. The BOL's most significant unfulfilled institutional mandate for this power is the **Ministry of Energy** — Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) directs that power generation/distribution authority be exercised ***"through the Ministry of Energy it shall create."*** Energy functions currently reside within MENRE rather than a standalone ministry. Additionally, the BOL envisions BARMM energy regulatory commission representation on the Intergovernmental Energy Board (Art. VI, Sec. 7) — no separate Bangsamoro energy regulatory commission has been established.[^22]

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**
A. Yes. Full exercise of this power would benefit from:
- **Bangsamoro Energy Regulatory Commission** — to set regional electricity rates, monitor service quality, and enforce consumer protection standards (analogous to ERC)
- **Bangsamoro Water Utilities Authority** — to supervise water districts and water supply service providers within BARMM (analogous to LWUA)
- A **unified public utilities consumer protection body** or ombudsman — cross-cutting entity for complaint resolution across all utility types

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**
A. The BARMM has established the institutional framework — MOTC with its 8 sectoral offices (BLTO, BLTFRB, BPMA, BMARINA, CAAB, CABB, Bangsamoro Telecommunications Commission, and Land/Water/Air Transportation Services). BLTFRB exercises franchise-granting authority for land transportation within BARMM. However, no standalone legislation codifying comprehensive regulatory standards has been enacted. **Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**
A. BARMM's primary advantage is **localized knowledge of connectivity needs** — particularly for the island provinces (Sulu, Basilan, Tawi-Tawi) where inter-island maritime transportation is essential and where the national system has historically underserved the population. The BOL's classification of inter-island routes through Zones of Joint Cooperation as "intra-regional" (Art. XIII, Sec. 21) gives BARMM a regulatory advantage over these critical transport links. Additionally, BARMM can integrate **Shari'ah-compliant financing** for utility development — an option unavailable in the national system.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**
A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of standalone legislation — particularly an **energy act** that BAA 13 itself conditions MENRE's power functions upon. Without it: MENRE cannot supervise electric power industry restructuring or exercise full authority over power generation/distribution utilities. Secondary constraints include: incomplete devolution of national agency functions (especially in telecommunications spectrum management and electric cooperative supervision); limited technical capacity for utility regulation (rate-setting, service quality monitoring); and the gap between the BOL-mandated Ministry of Energy and the current MENRE arrangement.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**
A.
- **ERC**: Decades of electricity rate-setting experience, approved rate methodologies, enforcement mechanisms, consumer protection protocols, and the Wholesale Electricity Spot Market (WESM)
- **NEA**: Electric cooperative supervision framework, performance benchmarking, financial rehabilitation programs, and technical assistance infrastructure developed over 50+ years since PD 269 (1969)
- **NTC**: Spectrum management system, type-approval testing laboratories, interconnection dispute resolution, and a comprehensive national telecommunications database
- **LWUA**: Water district performance monitoring, financial advisory services, and loan guarantee programs
- **LTFRB/LTO**: Integrated vehicle registration and franchising databases

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**
A. The national system's critical shortfalls for BARMM include: (1) **electrification gap** — BARMM provinces have among the lowest electrification rates in the Philippines, with many island barangays lacking grid connection; the national missionary electrification approach has been insufficient; (2) **maritime transportation** — the national framework prioritizes trunk routes, leaving inter-island routes in Sulu/Tawi-Tawi/Basilan underserved; (3) **telecommunications** — cellular coverage and broadband penetration in BARMM significantly trail national averages, particularly in island municipalities; (4) **water supply** — many BARMM municipalities lack organized water districts, and LWUA's presence has been limited in conflict-affected areas.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**
A.
- **Replicate**: ERC rate-setting methodology (transparent, formula-based); NEA performance benchmarking for electric cooperatives; safety standards for all utility types
- **Adapt**: Franchise-granting procedures to include Shari'ah-compliant financing options and conflict-sensitive service delivery requirements; water district formation process to account for BARMM's municipal governance under BAA 49 (Local Governance Code)
- **Replace**: The national approach to island electrification with distributed, renewable energy models suited to BARMM's archipelagic geography; the conventional financing model with Shari'ah-compliant alternatives where appropriate

### Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**
A. Yes, though indirectly. Public utilities are essential services — access to clean water, electricity, and transportation are necessities (*daruriyyat*) under Islamic jurisprudence. The Shari'ah dimension is strongest in two areas: (1) **financing** — where BARMM builds power utilities (Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a)), Shari'ah-compliant financing mechanisms should be available; (2) **consumer protection** — the Islamic principles of *'adl* (justice) and *hifz al-mal* (protection of property/wealth) inform rate regulation and anti-monopoly measures.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**
A. The BOL does not explicitly reference Shari'ah in the public utilities provisions (Art. XIII, Sec. 36-39). However, the general framework of Art. X, Sec. 4 — Parliament's power to enact Shari'ah laws on ***"commercial"*** matters — could extend to Shari'ah-compliant public utility financing and franchise terms. The Shari'ah Legislative Review Board can review any proposed public utilities legislation for Shari'ah compliance.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**
A. Key principles are detailed in the Shari'ah Sources table above. Beyond those: *istisna'* (manufacturing contract) is the appropriate Shari'ah-compliant mechanism for infrastructure construction; *ijarah* (leasing) for equipment and facilities; *murabaha* (cost-plus financing) for utility equipment procurement; and the general principle that necessities (*daruriyyat*) must be accessible to all members of society at fair prices.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**
A. No existing Shari'ah legislation directly governs public utility operations. PD 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) does not cover commercial utility operations. Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34 (Islamic banking and finance) provides a related but distinct framework — Shari'ah-compliant banking products could finance utility development.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**
A. A **principled approach** is most appropriate — legislation guided by Islamic values of fair pricing, public welfare, and non-exploitative commerce without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance for all utility operators. A Shari'ah-compliant track should be **available** for financing (allowing utility developers and consumers to choose between conventional and Shari'ah-compliant options) rather than **mandatory**.

### Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**
A. The divergence space for public utilities is **moderate but segmented** by utility type:

- **Transportation franchising** — **Broad divergence**. The BOL grants direct franchise authority (Art. XIII, Sec. 38) with no "consistent with national law" qualifier. BARMM can set its own franchise terms, service standards, and rate structures for regional transportation. Classification: **Tier 4 (constitutional floor only)** for regional routes. The BOL's express Art. X, Sec. 15 framework applies, and *Ali v. BTA Parliament* (GR-02219, 2025) confirms legislative authority must operate ***"within the bounds of the Constitution and national statutes."***
- **Telecommunications** — **Constrained divergence**. BARMM can grant regional franchises but is limited to utilities whose frequencies and main offices are within BARMM. Congress retains national franchise power (Art. XIII, Sec. 39 proviso). Classification: **Tier 3 (framework alignment)**.
- **Energy/power** — **Constrained by EPIRA**. Grid interconnection is expressly subject to RA 9136. BARMM can create its own power utilities but must comply with national grid rules. Classification: **Tier 3 (framework alignment)** for grid-connected operations; **Tier 4** for off-grid distributed generation.
- **Water supply** — **Cooperation required for cross-boundary**. Where water systems connect outside BARMM, cooperation is mandatory (Art. XIII, Sec. 37). For purely internal water supply, BARMM has **broad authority** — no national law consistency requirement. Classification: **Tier 4** for internal; **Tier 3** for cross-boundary.[^23]

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**
A. Key implications: (1) **Utility operators** holding both national and BARMM franchises would need to comply with dual regulatory regimes — e.g., a telecommunications company with a congressional franchise AND a BARMM regional franchise; (2) **Electric cooperatives** supervised by both NEA and MENRE face overlapping regulatory expectations; (3) **Consumers** could benefit from BARMM-specific consumer protection standards that exceed national minimums; (4) **Cross-boundary water supply** systems require intergovernmental agreements to avoid regulatory fragmentation.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**
A. The BAA governs within BARMM for matters within its scope. However, several national laws will continue to apply even after BAA enactment: RA 9136 (EPIRA) for grid interconnection (expressly referenced in BOL); RA 11659 (Amended Public Service Act) for ownership classifications (constitutional implementation); RA 11361 for electricity theft prosecution (criminal law). National law applies suppletorily where the BAA is silent. Constitutional Art. XII, Sec. 11 restrictions on public utility ownership (citizenship, exclusivity, duration) apply absolutely and cannot be overridden by any BAA.[^24]

### Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**
A. Yes — several significant overlaps:

1. **Electric cooperative supervision** — NEA retains supervisory authority while MENRE has assistance authority; no clear protocol for when these conflict
2. **Telecommunications spectrum** — BARMM can grant regional franchises but NTC manages spectrum allocation nationally; a regional franchise is meaningless without spectrum access, which NTC controls
3. **Airport operations** — CAAB manages landside while CAAP manages airside; the boundary between "landside" and "airside" is not defined in the BOL or BAA 13
4. **Water district creation/supervision** — LWUA exercises authority nationally; it is unclear whether BARMM can create water districts under a different framework or must follow PD 198

Resolution requires either standalone legislation defining jurisdictional boundaries or intergovernmental agreements (through the Intergovernmental Energy Board for energy, and bilateral MOAs for other utility types).

### Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**
A. **BDP Chapter 11 (Infrastructure)** — connectivity between BARMM's island and mainland territories depends directly on transportation franchise authority and maritime/air transportation regulation. Electrification targets require the energy authority under Art. XIII, Sec. 36. Water supply expansion requires exercise of both the water supply function and coordination with the National Government under Art. XIII, Sec. 37. **BDP Chapter 7 (Economic Development)** — private sector investment and trade development require reliable electricity, telecommunications, and transportation services. **Mas Matatag Pillar 3 (Socioeconomic Development)** — expansion of basic services (water, electricity) to underserved communities is a core commitment.[^25]

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**
A. Without standalone legislation: (1) **electrification** cannot accelerate beyond what NEA's national program delivers — MENRE's own mandate is conditioned on Parliament legislation; (2) **maritime transportation** safety and service standards for the inter-island routes critical to Sulu/Basilan/Tawi-Tawi remain governed by ad hoc regulation rather than a comprehensive code; (3) **telecommunications** expansion depends entirely on national operators' commercial decisions, since BARMM cannot effectively grant regional franchises without a regulatory framework; (4) **water supply** expansion lacks a regional institutional framework for water district creation, supervision, and service quality assurance; (5) the BOL-mandated **Ministry of Energy** remains unestablished, leaving BARMM's energy governance fragmented within MENRE.

### Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does power (pp) Public utilities' operations relate to (oo) Power sector investments and (ccc) Water supply and services?**
A. These three powers are closely related but distinct: (oo) power sector investments covers the **investment and generation** side — promoting investment, building power plants, the Agus Hydropower Complex; (pp) public utilities' operations covers the **operational regulation** — franchising, rate-setting, service delivery, consumer protection for all public utilities including energy distribution; (ccc) water supply covers the **infrastructure** side — construction and maintenance of water supply systems, flood control, and irrigation. In practice, Art. XIII, Sec. 36 elaborates both (oo) and (pp) for energy. Art. XIII, Sec. 37 elaborates both (pp) and (ccc) for water. The legislative treatment should be coordinated — an energy code would implement both (oo) and the energy component of (pp).[^26]

**Q. What is the significance of RA 11659 (Amended Public Service Act) for BARMM public utility franchises?**
A. RA 11659 (2022) fundamentally changed the landscape. By **narrowing** the definition of "public utility" under Art. XII, Sec. 11, it means: (1) **telecommunications** companies operating in BARMM are no longer subject to the 60% Filipino ownership requirement — 100% foreign ownership is constitutionally permissible; (2) **transportation** companies are similarly reclassified as "public services" with relaxed ownership rules; (3) only **electricity distribution, electricity transmission, water pipeline distribution, and airports/seaports** remain "public utilities" subject to Art. XII, Sec. 11's citizenship requirements. BARMM-granted regional franchises must comply with these national reclassifications.

**Q. What does the mandatory 25-year divestment mean in practice?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) requires the Bangsamoro Government to ***"divest its ownership"*** of power utilities 25 years after creation. This means BARMM-built power plants and distribution systems must be privatized within the 25-year window. The provision aligns with the national EPIRA privatization policy but with a fixed timeline. Key implementation questions that require legislation: (a) what constitutes "divestment" — full sale, partial equity retention, management contract; (b) whether BARMM residents or entities get preferential purchase rights (similar to the Agus Hydropower Complex preferential right); (c) the regulatory framework that applies after divestment.

**Q. Can the Bangsamoro Parliament grant telecommunications franchises that compete with congressionally-granted national franchises?**
A. Yes, but with significant practical limitations. The BOL authorizes BARMM to grant ***"regional franchises, licenses, and permits to telecommunication utilities whose frequencies are confined to and whose main offices are located within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 39). However, the proviso expressly states that ***"nothing herein shall be construed as limiting in any way the power of the Congress of the Philippines to grant national franchises."*** In practice, a BARMM regional telecommunications franchise holder would compete with nationally-franchised operators (Globe, Smart/PLDT, DITO) that already serve the region. The practical challenge is **spectrum access** — since NTC manages frequency allocation nationally, a BARMM-franchised telecom entity would need NTC cooperation for spectrum, which the BOL does not guarantee. This is a jurisdictional gap that the Intergovernmental Energy Board (for energy) has no counterpart for (no Intergovernmental Telecommunications Board exists).[^27]
