# (qq) Public Works and Infrastructure

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(qq)** — ***"Public works and infrastructure;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(qq) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national public works law in Art. V, Sec. 2. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 37 assigns **national roads, bridges, airports, seaports, and wharves** to the National Government for funding and implementation — creating a **functional division** rather than a consistency clause. Art. VI, Sec. 6 mandates an **Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board** for coordination. The constitutional floor (Art. X, Sec. 15) applies. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Public Works (MPW)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XI, Sec. 1-12 |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Infrastructure Modernization and Public Asset Construction and Transformation (IMPACT) — BAA 85, Chapter XXXVI; Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) — irrigation and farm-to-market roads; Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA) — infrastructure planning and programming |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 11 (Scaling Up Strategic, Adequate, and Climate-Resilient Infrastructure) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None — the BOL does not name a specific "public works code" or equivalent. Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)(i) identifies **"Public Works"** as one of the ministries the BTA shall establish, confirming institutional importance without mandating a standalone code. |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**

A. Art. V, Sec. 2(qq) grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over ***"Public works and infrastructure."*** This is a broad enumerated power encompassing the planning, design, construction, maintenance, and rehabilitation of physical infrastructure within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The power is exercised ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law"*** and ***"without prejudice to the general supervision of the President"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2 chapeau).[^1]

   The scope is immediately qualified by Art. XIII, Sec. 37, which creates a **functional division** between national and Bangsamoro infrastructure authority (see below).

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Three provisions elaborate the grant:

   (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 37 (Public Works and Infrastructure)**: This is the primary elaborating provision. It assigns **national roads, bridges, water supply and services, flood control, irrigation systems, airports, seaports, and wharves** to the National Government for ***"fund[ing] and implement[ation of] the construction and maintenance."*** Three critical rules emerge: (a) ***"Nationally-funded infrastructure projects shall be implemented by the National Government"*** — meaning the Bangsamoro Government cannot implement infrastructure that the national government funds; (b) cross-boundary water supply, flood control, and irrigation require ***"cooperation and coordination between the Bangsamoro Government and the appropriate national or local government bodies"***; (c) ***"All national roads and bridges in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall be included in the National Road Network Information System."***[^2]

   (2) **Art. VI, Sec. 6 (Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board)**: Creates a mandatory coordination body ***"responsible for coordinating and synchronizing national and Bangsamoro infrastructure development plans."*** Composed of BARMM ministry heads and the DPWH Secretary. This is one of six intergovernmental mechanisms established by the BOL — its existence signals the complexity and dual-authority nature of this power.[^3]

   (3) **Art. XII, Sec. 24(a)(a)**: Authorizes BARMM to ***"contract loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness to finance the construction, installation, improvement, expansion, operation or maintenance of public utilities, infrastructure facilities"*** — providing the fiscal mechanism for BARMM-funded infrastructure beyond annual appropriations.[^4]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. Unlike cooperatives (Art. XIII, Sec. 24 — ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"***), the BOL does not name a specific public works code or infrastructure development act. However, Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)(i) identifies ***"Public Works"*** as one of the ministries the BTA shall establish — confirming the BOL's expectation of a dedicated institutional mechanism. The absence of a named code does not prevent Parliament from legislating; it means there is no express **unfulfilled legislative mandate** for this power.[^5]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL creates two institutions:

   (1) **Ministry of Public Works** — named in Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)(i) as one of 15 ministries. Established through BAA 13. See the **Implementation** section (Section D) for institutional details.

   (2) **Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board** — created by Art. VI, Sec. 6. This is a BOL-mandated body (not merely a BAA-created one), meaning it cannot be abolished by the Parliament and its coordination function is constitutionally embedded.[^3]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places three significant limitations:

   (1) **National Government retention of national infrastructure** (Art. XIII, Sec. 37): The National Government retains authority over national roads, bridges, airports, seaports, wharves, and cross-boundary water/flood control/irrigation. This is not a "consistency requirement" — it is a **jurisdictional partition**. BARMM's public works authority covers **regional and local infrastructure only**.[^2]

   (2) **National implementation of nationally-funded projects** (Art. XIII, Sec. 37): ***"Nationally-funded infrastructure projects shall be implemented by the National Government."*** Even within BARMM's territorial jurisdiction, the implementing agency for nationally-funded projects is the national government (DPWH), not MPW.[^2]

   (3) **Mandatory coordination** (Art. VI, Sec. 6): The Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board must ***"coordinat[e] and synchroniz[e] national and Bangsamoro infrastructure development plans"*** — BARMM cannot develop infrastructure plans in isolation from the national system.[^3]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to public works and infrastructure?**

A. Three categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This is the universal constitutional boundary. The Supreme Court in *Ali v. BTA Parliament* (GR-02219, 2025) described this as ***"clear and categorical."***[^6][^7]

   (2) **State Policy on Infrastructure (Art. II, Sec. 20)**: ***"The State recognizes the indispensable role of the private sector, encourages private enterprise, and provides incentives to needed investments."*** This supports PPP and BOT arrangements for infrastructure development — the private sector's role in infrastructure is constitutionally recognized.[^8]

   (3) **Social Justice (Art. XIII, Sec. 1)**: ***"The Congress shall give highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity, reduce social, economic, and political inequalities, and remove cultural inequities by equitably diffusing wealth and political power for the common good."*** Infrastructure that connects isolated communities, provides clean water, and enables economic participation serves this constitutional purpose.[^8]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. Public works is primarily a **government function**, not a rights-bearing area like education or labor. However, two constitutional protections apply:

   (1) **Just compensation for eminent domain** (Art. III, Sec. 9): ***"Private property shall not be taken for public use without just compensation."*** Every infrastructure project requiring land acquisition — roads, bridges, public buildings — must satisfy this requirement. This is the constitutional basis for right-of-way acquisition laws (RA 10752/12289).[^8]

   (2) **Equal protection** (Art. III, Sec. 1): Infrastructure investment decisions must not discriminate. The distribution of public works projects across provinces and municipalities must have a rational basis — not arbitrary favoritism. This applies at the **rational basis** level of scrutiny (not strict scrutiny), meaning a BARMM infrastructure allocation formula that prioritizes underserved areas over urban centers would survive constitutional challenge because it serves a legitimate government purpose.[^8]

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. No specific constitutional mandate requires the creation of a public works ministry or the enactment of a public works code. The constitutional framework is permissive: Art. X, Sec. 15 creates the space for autonomous regions; the BOL (itself a congressional act) exercises this space by enumerating public works as an autonomous power. The Constitution does not prescribe how the Bangsamoro Government must organize its infrastructure function.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for public works?**

A. The national framework provides a comprehensive system:

   - **Institutional structure**: DPWH (EO 124, s. 1987) serves as the national engineering and construction arm — planning, design, construction, and maintenance of national infrastructure. DPWH operates through Regional Offices and District Engineering Offices (DEOs) nationwide, including within BARMM.
   - **Procurement**: RA 9184 (as revised by RA 12009) governs all government infrastructure procurement — competitive bidding, alternative methods, contract implementation, and monitoring.
   - **Building standards**: PD 1096 (National Building Code) sets minimum design and construction standards for all buildings and structures. Professional regulation under RA 544 (civil engineering) and RA 545 (architecture) ensures design competence.
   - **Right-of-way**: RA 10752 (as amended by RA 12289) provides procedures for land acquisition for infrastructure projects — negotiated sale and expropriation.
   - **Private sector participation**: RA 7718 (BOT Law) enables PPP arrangements for infrastructure financing, construction, and operation.
   - **Safety standards**: PD 1096 (building code), RA 9514 (fire code), and BP 344 (accessibility law) provide the safety and accessibility framework for all structures.[^9][^10][^11][^12][^13]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national infrastructure laws apply in BARMM:

   (1) **RA 9184/12009** (Procurement) — applies **directly** to all BARMM infrastructure procurement. This is a law of general application that the Bangsamoro Government cannot override.[^9]
   (2) **PD 1096** (Building Code) — applies **directly** as minimum standards. MPW can set higher standards but not lower.[^10]
   (3) **RA 10752/12289** (Right-of-Way) — applies **directly** for nationally-funded projects; suppletorily for BARMM-funded projects.[^11]
   (4) **RA 544 and RA 545** (Professional regulation) — apply **directly**. Licensed civil engineers and architects are required for all projects.[^14]
   (5) **RA 7718** (BOT Law) — **suppletory** pending any Bangsamoro-specific PPP legislation.[^12]
   (6) **RA 9514** (Fire Code) and **BP 344** (Accessibility) — apply **directly** as laws of general application.[^15]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. Three national institutions exercise direct authority:

   (1) **DPWH** — maintains District Engineering Offices in BARMM for national road and bridge construction/maintenance. DPWH implements nationally-funded projects per Art. XIII, Sec. 37.
   (2) **Government Procurement Policy Board (GPPB)** — oversees procurement compliance for all government entities including BARMM.
   (3) **Professional Regulation Commission (PRC)** — regulates civil engineers, architects, and other professionals involved in infrastructure projects.

   DPWH's presence is the most significant. Unlike some national agencies that withdraw when BARMM establishes a counterpart ministry, DPWH retains a **permanent operational role** because the BOL expressly assigns national roads and bridges to the National Government.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Public Works (MPW)** is the lead agency. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XI, Sections 1-12 defines MPW's institutional status, mandate, powers, functions, organizational structure, and district offices. BAA 85 allocates operational and capital budget to MPW. **IMPACT** (Infrastructure Modernization and Public Asset Construction and Transformation) — BAA 85, Chapter XXXVI — serves as a separate infrastructure construction body.[^16][^17]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for public works and infrastructure as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through MPW's **BAA 13 general mandate** and **BAA 85 budget allocation**. There is no Bangsamoro building code, infrastructure development act, or public works regulatory framework beyond BAA 13.

**Q. What are MPW's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XI defines MPW comprehensively:

   **Declaration of Policy** (Sec. 1): ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall maintain an engineering and construction arm and continuously develop its technology, for the purpose of ensuring the safety of all infrastructure facilities and securing for all public works the highest efficiency and the most appropriate quality in construction."***[^16]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): MPW is ***"the Bangsamoro Government's engineering and construction arm"*** mandated to undertake: (a) ***"the planning, design and construction of infrastructures, such as, but not limited to roads and bridges, flood control, water supply projects and other public works facilities within the jurisdiction of the Bangsamoro Government"***; and (b) ***"the construction, rehabilitation and maintenance of infrastructure facilities of the Autonomous Region."***[^16]

   **Powers and Functions** (Sec. 3) — 11 enumerated functions:

   - (a) ***"Undertake and/or evaluate the planning, design, construction and supervision for all infrastructure located within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"***
   - (b) ***"Undertake maintenance and repairs of infrastructure and facilities within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region and as may be authorized or delegated by the National Government"***
   - (c) Ensure necessary property, plant, and equipment are available
   - (d) ***"Undertake the submission of proposals to the appropriate National Government Agency for funding and technical assistance on infrastructure projects, whenever applicable"***
   - (e) ***"Administer or establish implementation codes, standards, and guidelines to ensure the safety of all public and private structures in the region and ensure efficiency and proper quality in the construction of public works"***
   - (f) ***"Ensure the implementation of laws, policies, programs, rules and regulations regarding infrastructure projects as well as all public and private physical structures within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"***
   - (g) ***"Supervise all public works construction and ensure that actual constructions are done in accordance with approved government plans and specifications"***
   - (h) Provide technical assistance to other agencies and LGUs
   - (i) Coordinate with national and regional agencies and LGUs on infrastructure planning and implementation
   - (j) Conduct continuing consultations with local communities
   - (k) Perform other functions as assigned by the Chief Minister or by law[^16]

   **Organizational Structure** (Sec. 4, 9): Ministry Proper (Minister, Deputy Minister, Director-General) with three service divisions:
   - **(1) Administrative and Finance Services** — Administrative Division, Finance Division, Procurement Division
   - **(2) Technical Services** — Planning and Programming Division, Survey and Design Division, Special Projects Division
   - **(3) Operations and Infrastructure Services** — Construction Division, Maintenance Division, Quality Assurance and Hydrology Division, Equipment Management Division
   - Plus Legal and Legislative Liaison Division, Internal Audit Division, Information Communication Division[^16]

   **District Offices** (Sec. 10-12): A **District Office** in each legislative district, headed by a **District Engineer**. Functions include: implementing infrastructure policies; inspecting, monitoring, and supervising projects; preparing programs of work and estimates; and managing construction equipment.[^16]

   **Minister's Functions** (Sec. 6): Include: ***"Establish policies and standards consistent with the National Government policies and standards for the efficient administration and operation of the Ministry."*** This is the only express consistency requirement within BAA 13 for MPW — the Minister's policy-setting must be ***"consistent with National Government policies and standards."***[^16]

**Q. What can MPW NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 provides **institutional existence** and **general operational authority** but lacks:

   (1) **A Bangsamoro Building Code** — Sec. 3(e) authorizes MPW to ***"administer or establish implementation codes, standards, and guidelines"*** but this is a general mandate, not a comprehensive regulatory code with specific standards, classifications, penalties, and enforcement mechanisms. PD 1096 fills this gap suppletorily.
   (2) **A regulatory framework with penalties** — BAA 13 does not prescribe penalties for violations of infrastructure standards (e.g., unauthorized construction, substandard materials, illegal encroachment on road easements). Without a standalone BAA, MPW has no enforcement teeth.
   (3) **Infrastructure financing framework** — no BAA establishes a Bangsamoro Infrastructure Fund, PPP framework, or Shari'ah-compliant infrastructure financing mechanisms beyond the general borrowing authority in Art. XII, Sec. 24(a).
   (4) **Right-of-way acquisition procedures** — MPW relies on national law (RA 10752/12289) for land acquisition. A Bangsamoro right-of-way law could streamline this for regional projects.
   (5) **Quality assurance and construction standards** — beyond PD 1096 minimums, no BAA codifies BARMM-specific construction standards for climate resilience, seismic design, or flood-resistant infrastructure.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes — extensively. Ten resolutions address infrastructure: Res. Nos. 17, 37, 251, 305, 346, 385, 398, 425, 504, and 511 (see Resolutions table above). The most significant are **Res. No. 37** (asserting institutional prerogative against DPWH) and **Res. No. 346** (later accepting DPWH DEO presence) — together revealing Parliament's evolving position on the MPW-DPWH jurisdictional boundary. Ten filed PBs also address infrastructure gaps, with **PB 343** (Promoting Infrastructure and Connectivity Development) potentially being the most comprehensive pending proposal.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. MPW's BAA 13 mandate covers the **full scope** of Art. V, Sec. 2(qq) — planning, design, construction, supervision, maintenance, and standard-setting for infrastructure within BARMM. The mandate's language (***"all infrastructure located within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"***) is comprehensive. However, **IMPACT** (BAA 85, Ch. XXXVI) operates as a **separate construction body** — creating a two-institution structure for implementation that requires internal coordination. MAFAR handles agricultural infrastructure (irrigation, farm-to-market roads) that overlaps with MPW's general mandate. BPDA handles infrastructure planning and programming at the strategic level.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Four institutions exercise public works functions:

   - **Ministry of Public Works (MPW)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XI; BAA 85, Ch. XXIII. The lead ministry for infrastructure planning, design, construction, supervision, maintenance, and standards.
   - **IMPACT** — BAA 85, Ch. XXXVI. A separate infrastructure construction body focused on project implementation and asset construction.
   - **MAFAR** — for agricultural infrastructure (irrigation, farm-to-market roads, post-harvest facilities).
   - **BPDA** — for strategic infrastructure planning, programming, and development plan alignment.

   All are operational. MPW and IMPACT both perform construction functions, requiring coordination to avoid duplication.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The **Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board** (Art. VI, Sec. 6) — while BOL-mandated, its operational status is unclear. The BOL creates this body ***"hereby"*** (self-executing creation), but whether it meets regularly, has adopted rules, and effectively coordinates DPWH-MPW planning is not documented in any enacted legislation. [UNVERIFIED — operational status of the Board requires executive confirmation]

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Full exercise of this power would benefit from:

   (1) **A Bangsamoro Building Code Authority** — a specialized regulatory body for building permits, inspections, and enforcement of construction standards within BARMM. Currently, building officials operate under PD 1096's national framework.
   (2) **An Infrastructure Coordination Office** — to harmonize MPW, IMPACT, MAFAR, and DPWH infrastructure activities. The Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board is limited to national-Bangsamoro coordination; an internal BARMM coordination mechanism is also needed.
   (3) **A Bangsamoro PPP Center** — to manage public-private partnership arrangements for infrastructure. No such body currently exists; PPP opportunities depend on the national PPP Center framework.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **DPWH** is the principal national agency. It operates **District Engineering Offices (DEOs)** within BARMM's legislative districts — each headed by a District Engineer responsible for national road and bridge construction, maintenance, and rehabilitation. DPWH DEOs implement all nationally-funded projects within BARMM per Art. XIII, Sec. 37. DPWH also maintains BARMM's national roads in its **National Road Network Information System** (required by Art. XIII, Sec. 37).

   The **GPPB** oversees procurement compliance for BARMM infrastructure projects. The **PRC** regulates professionals involved in infrastructure design and construction.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. DPWH implements several national programs within BARMM:
   - **National Road Construction and Maintenance** — annual GAA-funded road and bridge projects
   - **Flood Control Programs** — for cross-boundary water systems
   - **National Infrastructure Pipeline** — large-scale projects under the Build, Better, More program and its successors
   - **Convergence programs** — DPWH coordinates with DSWD, DepEd, and DOH on school buildings, health facilities, and social welfare infrastructure within BARMM

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. Devolution is **partial and complex**:

   (1) **Regional and local infrastructure**: MPW exercises authority over regional roads, local infrastructure, and government buildings. This was devolved from the former ARMM-DPWH structure.
   (2) **National infrastructure**: DPWH retains full authority per Art. XIII, Sec. 37. This is **not subject to devolution** — the BOL expressly assigns it to the National Government.
   (3) **Equipment and assets**: The transition of equipment, facilities, and personnel from the former ARMM public works function to the new MPW is the operational dimension of devolution.
   (4) **Building permit authority**: Unclear whether building officials within BARMM report to MPW or continue under the DPWH/LGU framework established by PD 1096.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes — this is the **central jurisdictional challenge** for this power:

   (1) **Road maintenance**: DPWH DEOs maintain national roads; MPW maintains regional roads. But where a road transitions from "national" to "regional" — or where a regional road connects to a national road — jurisdiction is unclear.
   (2) **Building permits and inspections**: PD 1096 assigns building officials to LGUs under DPWH coordination. MPW claims authority to ***"ensure the safety of all public and private structures in the region"*** (BAA 13, Sec. 3(e)). The boundary between MPW's standard-setting authority and PD 1096's LGU-based permit system is not legislatively resolved.
   (3) **Infrastructure planning**: DPWH's national infrastructure plan and MPW's regional plan must be ***"coordinated and synchronized"*** (Art. VI, Sec. 6) — but without clear primacy rules when plans conflict.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MPW has accomplished:
   - Establishment of a fully staffed ministry with District Engineering Offices across BARMM's legislative districts
   - Planning, design, and construction of regional road, bridge, and public building projects using BARMM funds
   - IMPACT has implemented major construction projects as a separate implementation arm
   - BDP Chapter 11 data shows MPW has inventory data on BARMM's 11,544 km local road network (59% unpaved, 41% paved)

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has potential advantages in:
   - **Local knowledge**: MPW understands local terrain, community needs, and conflict-sensitive construction requirements better than DPWH central planning
   - **Conflict-sensitive design**: Infrastructure in post-conflict areas requires community consultation and sensitivity to contested lands — MPW's BAA 13 mandate includes ***"conduct[ing] continuing consultations with the local communities"*** (Sec. 3(j))
   - **Integrated planning**: MPW can align infrastructure with BDP goals and Matatag Agenda priorities in ways that DPWH's national programming cannot

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the **absence of a standalone infrastructure code or public works act**. Without it:
   - MPW cannot enforce construction standards with penalties
   - No Bangsamoro building code exists to adapt PD 1096 for local conditions
   - No BARMM-specific right-of-way procedures exist to expedite land acquisition
   - No infrastructure financing framework (PPP, Shari'ah-compliant bonds) exists
   - The MPW-DPWH jurisdictional boundary remains undefined beyond Art. XIII, Sec. 37's broad allocation

   The secondary constraint is **infrastructure financing** — BARMM's block grant and annual appropriations limit the scale of infrastructure investment. Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) authorizes borrowing, but no BAA operationalizes this for infrastructure-specific financing.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national system provides:
   - **Legislative depth**: PD 1096 (Building Code, 1977) has nearly five decades of implementation experience, IRRs, and jurisprudence. RA 9184/12009 (Procurement) provides a mature, tested procurement framework.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: DPWH operates 16 Regional Offices and over 200 District Engineering Offices with specialized engineering staff, testing laboratories, and equipment pools.
   - **IT systems**: The National Road Network Information System, the Philippine Infrastructure Transparency Portal, and e-procurement (PhilGEPS) provide nationwide infrastructure monitoring and transparency.
   - **Professional ecosystem**: A deep pool of licensed civil engineers, architects, and construction firms accredited under PRC — concentrated in urban centers but extending to BARMM through DPWH DEOs.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM in three ways:
   - **Underinvestment**: BARMM has only **3% of national roads** despite its large land area (BDP Chapter 11). The national road allocation system has historically underserved the region.
   - **No Shari'ah-compliant infrastructure financing**: National BOT and PPP frameworks assume conventional interest-bearing financing. No national framework provides *istisna'* (construction contracts), *sukuk* (infrastructure bonds), or other Shari'ah-compliant instruments.
   - **No conflict-sensitive construction protocols**: DPWH's standard construction specifications do not account for BARMM's post-conflict realities — disputed land ownership, IDP settlement areas, or former conflict zones requiring community consent processes.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:
   - **Replicate**: Procurement procedures (RA 9184/12009) — these are laws of general application and should be adopted as-is. Professional licensing requirements (RA 544/545) — no basis for different standards.
   - **Adapt**: Building code (PD 1096) — adopt as the **floor** and add BARMM-specific standards for climate resilience (typhoon/flood), seismic design requirements for island provinces, and culturally appropriate public building design. Right-of-way procedures — adapt RA 10752/12289 for BARMM-specific land tenure complexities (ancestral domain, Torrens/non-Torrens overlap).
   - **Replace**: Infrastructure financing framework — build a BARMM-specific framework with Shari'ah-compliant options (*istisna'*, *sukuk*, *ijarah*) alongside conventional instruments. Community consultation protocols — replace DPWH's standard process with a conflict-sensitive, culturally appropriate approach incorporating *shura* (consultation) principles.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Public works is primarily an **engineering and governance function** — Shari'ah does not prescribe specific road widths, bridge load capacities, or building heights. However, two dimensions are relevant:

   (1) **Infrastructure financing**: The BOL authorizes BARMM to borrow for infrastructure (Art. XII, Sec. 24(a)). For a population where interest (*riba*) is prohibited, Shari'ah-compliant financing instruments are essential. *Istisna'* (construction/manufacturing contracts), *sukuk* (Islamic bonds), and *ijarah* (lease financing) provide alternatives to conventional loans.

   (2) **Governance values**: *Maslahah* (public welfare), *amanah* (trust/stewardship over public funds), and *'adl* (equitable distribution) inform how infrastructure decisions should be made — prioritizing underserved communities, ensuring transparent procurement, and distributing projects based on need rather than political patronage.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not expressly link Shari'ah to public works. Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact ***"Shari'ah-based laws in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** covering ***"personal, family, and property law, as well as commercial, and criminal law"*** — commercial Shari'ah law could encompass Shari'ah-compliant infrastructure contracts and financing instruments, but this requires legislative action.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four principles apply (see Shari'ah Sources table for details):
   - ***Maslahah*** — infrastructure serves the common good
   - ***Amanah*** — procurement integrity and fund stewardship
   - ***'Adl*** — equitable distribution of infrastructure investment
   - **Prohibition of *riba*** — Shari'ah-compliant financing for infrastructure projects

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. No existing Shari'ah legislation (PD 1083 or Shari'ah court rulings) directly governs public works. The Shari'ah dimension is **prospective** — relevant to future legislation on infrastructure financing, not current regulation.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. Shari'ah compliance for public works should be **optional and financing-focused**: (1) a Bangsamoro infrastructure financing act should include a Shari'ah-compliant financing track (*istisna'*, *sukuk*, *ijarah*) alongside conventional instruments; (2) procurement integrity standards should reference *amanah* principles without creating separate compliance requirements. Mandatory Shari'ah review of engineering and construction standards is not warranted — the Shari'ah dimension is in **governance and financing**, not technical specifications.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. Public works sits in a **unique position** on the divergence spectrum — neither fully autonomous (like cooperatives) nor fully constrained (like civil service). The 5-layer constraint framework from the constitutional analysis yields:

   - **Layer 1 (Constitutional)**: The constitutional floor applies — just compensation for eminent domain (Art. III, Sec. 9), equal protection (Art. III, Sec. 1), and the Art. X, Sec. 15 framework. None of these prevent divergence in engineering standards or infrastructure programming.
   - **Layer 2 ("Shall prevail" clauses)**: No express supremacy clause applies to public works. Art. VII, Sec. 39's civil service supremacy is not triggered by infrastructure legislation.
   - **Layer 3 (Minimum standards)**: No BOL provision requires BARMM infrastructure to ***"conform to minimum standards set by the National Government."*** However, PD 1096 (National Building Code) functions as a **de facto floor** — a law of general application that applies until a BAA establishes higher BARMM-specific standards.
   - **Layer 4 (Institutional retention)**: DPWH retains jurisdiction over **national roads, bridges, airports, seaports, and wharves** (Art. XIII, Sec. 37). This is the most significant constraint — not a divergence issue but a **jurisdictional partition**. Within its partition (regional/local infrastructure), BARMM has broad authority.
   - **Layer 5 ("Shall not diminish")**: No existing infrastructure rights or privileges are locked by the BOL.

   **Divergence classification**: **Tier 3 — Moderate** (framework alignment). BARMM can set its own construction standards, infrastructure programming priorities, and institutional arrangements for regional/local infrastructure. It cannot absorb national road functions, alter procurement law (RA 9184/12009), or lower building safety standards below PD 1096. The mandatory Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board (Art. VI, Sec. 6) ensures ongoing coordination.

   **Shari'ah as a basis for divergence** (as established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E)): Shari'ah-compliant infrastructure financing is a legitimate basis for distinctive legislation that no national law currently provides. This is uniquely available to BARMM and represents the strongest justification for a standalone infrastructure financing BAA.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. Two scenarios require attention:

   (1) **Higher building standards**: If BARMM enacts a building code with stricter seismic or flood-resistance standards than PD 1096, construction firms must comply with the higher standard within BARMM. This creates a **compliance differential** for firms operating across BARMM and non-BARMM jurisdictions — manageable because higher standards are generally permissible and firms routinely adapt to local building codes.

   (2) **Infrastructure connecting BARMM and non-BARMM**: Roads and water systems that cross jurisdictional boundaries must meet **both** BARMM and national standards. Art. XIII, Sec. 37 already addresses this for water supply and flood control by requiring ***"cooperation and coordination."***

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. The BAA would govern within BARMM for regional/local infrastructure. National law (PD 1096, RA 9184/12009, RA 544/545) continues to apply: (a) **directly** for procurement (law of general application); (b) **directly** for professional regulation (national function); (c) **suppletorily** where the BAA is silent on specific standards; (d) **as the floor** where the BAA sets higher standards. National law continues to govern national roads and bridges (Art. XIII, Sec. 37) regardless of any BAA.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Three overlapping jurisdictions require legislative or intergovernmental clarification:

   (1) **MPW vs. DPWH DEOs**: Both operate within BARMM's territory. The BOL's allocation (national roads to DPWH; regional/local infrastructure to MPW) is clear in principle but unclear at road classification boundaries. Which entity classifies a road as "national" vs. "regional"? What happens when BARMM wants to upgrade a regional road to national standards?

   (2) **Building permits**: PD 1096 assigns building permit authority to local building officials under DPWH coordination. BAA 13 assigns MPW authority over ***"all public and private structures in the region."*** The permit-issuing authority and inspection protocols are not legislatively reconciled.

   (3) **MPW vs. IMPACT**: Both are BARMM entities implementing infrastructure construction. Their respective mandates and project allocation criteria are not defined in any enacted BAA — creating potential overlap and duplication.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB 343** (Promoting Infrastructure and Connectivity Development) is the most potentially comprehensive pending bill. **PB 183** (Enabling Acceleration of Infrastructure Development) addresses procedural bottlenecks. **PB 364** (Right-of-Way Indemnification) addresses land acquisition. **PB 232** (Prohibiting Unauthorized Construction on Roads/Bridges) addresses enforcement. **PB 219** (Geo-Tagging System) addresses monitoring and transparency.

   Even if all pending PBs pass, gaps remain in: (a) a comprehensive Bangsamoro building code; (b) infrastructure financing (Shari'ah-compliant and conventional); (c) the MPW-DPWH jurisdictional boundary definition; and (d) the MPW-IMPACT internal coordination framework.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 11** (Infrastructure) directly depends on this power. It covers five sub-sectors: transport (roads, bridges, airports, seaports), water resources (potable water, irrigation, flood control, drainage), energy, ICT, and socioeconomic infrastructure (government facilities, education, health, housing, solid waste). The BDP documents that BARMM has only **3% of national roads**, **59% of local roads unpaved**, and significant gaps in water, energy, and socioeconomic infrastructure.[^18]

   **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda**, Pillar 3 (Mas Matatag na Pamayanan — Communities) explicitly includes **infrastructure** alongside education, health, and social justice. Infrastructure development is positioned as a **community development** priority — not isolated engineering activity.[^19]

   Public works also enables: BDP Chapter 7 (Economic Development — market access requires roads); Chapter 9 (Social Services — health and education facilities require construction); Chapter 12 (Environment — climate-resilient infrastructure).

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without standalone legislation:

   (1) **Road connectivity**: The 59% unpaved local road rate cannot be addressed at scale without an infrastructure development framework that goes beyond annual GAA appropriations. Shari'ah-compliant financing instruments could unlock Islamic Development Bank and Gulf capital — but no BAA provides the framework.

   (2) **Construction quality**: Without a Bangsamoro building code adapted for local conditions (seismic activity in island provinces, flooding in Cotabato Basin, typhoon exposure in Sulu/Tawi-Tawi), construction defaults to PD 1096's national standards that may not address BARMM-specific risks.

   (3) **Enforcement gap**: MPW cannot penalize unauthorized construction, substandard materials, or road encroachment without a regulatory BAA. BAA 13's general mandate provides no enforcement mechanism.

   (4) **Financing bottleneck**: Without an infrastructure financing BAA, BARMM is limited to block grant allocations and national budget requests — insufficient for the scale of infrastructure deficit documented in BDP Chapter 11.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does the road classification system work between DPWH and MPW?**

A. The BOL creates a **two-track** system: national roads and bridges fall under DPWH; regional and local roads under MPW. Art. XIII, Sec. 37 requires ***"All national roads and bridges in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall be included in the National Road Network Information System."*** However, the BOL does not define the criteria for classifying a road as "national" vs. "regional" — this classification determines which agency has jurisdiction. Currently, the DPWH road classification system (following the highway engineering framework) determines which roads are "national." BARMM has no independent road classification authority, meaning MPW's jurisdiction depends on DPWH's classification decisions. A Bangsamoro infrastructure act should establish criteria for regional road classification and a mechanism for upgrading regional roads to national network standards.

**Q. What is the legal basis for IMPACT as a separate infrastructure body?**

A. IMPACT appears only in **BAA 85** (General Appropriations Act, Chapter XXXVI) as a budget allocation. It is not established by BAA 13 or any standalone BAA. Its legal basis is therefore the annual appropriations act — meaning its institutional existence depends on annual budget renewal rather than permanent legislation. A standalone infrastructure BAA should define IMPACT's mandate, relationship with MPW, and project allocation criteria to provide permanent institutional status.

**Q. Can the Bangsamoro Government adopt Shari'ah-compliant infrastructure financing?**

A. Yes. Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) authorizes BARMM to ***"contract loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness to finance... infrastructure facilities."*** The term ***"other forms of indebtedness"*** is broad enough to encompass Shari'ah-compliant instruments — *istisna'* (construction contracts where the financier commissions construction and transfers upon completion), *sukuk* (asset-backed certificates that provide returns from infrastructure project revenues), and *ijarah* (lease-to-own arrangements for equipment and facilities). Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah-based commercial law, which could include an Islamic infrastructure finance framework. No national law prohibits this — it is an area where BARMM can legislate distinctively without conflicting with any national statute. See the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) for the Shari'ah dimension.

**Q. What happens to infrastructure projects when a road is reclassified from regional to national or vice versa?**

A. The BOL does not address this scenario. If DPWH reclassifies a BARMM regional road as a national road, jurisdiction shifts from MPW to DPWH — including construction, maintenance, and budget allocation. Conversely, if a national road is downgraded to regional status, MPW assumes responsibility without guaranteed national funding. This transfer mechanism is not defined in any BAA. The Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board (Art. VI, Sec. 6) is the intended forum for resolving such transitions, but operational protocols have not been legislatively established.

**Q. How does the Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board relate to the MPW-DPWH operational boundary?**

A. Art. VI, Sec. 6 creates the Board to ***"coordinat[e] and synchroniz[e] national and Bangsamoro infrastructure development plans."*** The Board's composition — BARMM ministry heads and the DPWH Secretary — positions it as the **highest-level coordination mechanism** for resolving MPW-DPWH conflicts. However, the BOL defines the Board's function as **coordination**, not **adjudication** — meaning it facilitates agreement but has no express power to impose decisions when DPWH and MPW disagree on jurisdiction, project priority, or road classification. A standalone infrastructure BAA should define the Board's decision-making procedures, voting rules, and dispute resolution mechanisms.
