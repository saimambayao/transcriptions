# (uu) Science and Technology, Research Councils, and Scholarship Programs

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(uu)** — "Science and technology, research councils, and scholarship programs;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over science and technology, research councils, and scholarship programs as an enumerated power under Article V, Section 2(uu).[^1] This power is elaborated in Article IX, Section 21, which states that the "Bangsamoro Government recognizes science and technology as essential to national and regional progress."[^2] The provision directs the Bangsamoro Government to "give priority to science, research, inventions, technology education, and their development" and to "ensure the full and effective participation of all sectors in the planning, programming, coordination, and implementation of scientific and technological researches." The BOL further directs the Bangsamoro Government to "prioritize the acquisition, adoption, innovation, and application of science and technology for development."

This is a broad mandate covering four interlinked domains: (1) scientific research and development, (2) technology adoption and innovation, (3) research councils as institutional mechanisms, and (4) scholarship programs for human capital development. The BOL places no explicit national law consistency requirement on this power — the Bangsamoro Government has full authority to formulate its own science and technology policy, establish research institutions, and design scholarship programs. However, the power naturally intersects with education (v) through science education and with technical cooperation (xx) through human resource development for technology transfer.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST) is the lead ministry, established under BAA 13 and funded through BAA 85, Chapter XV.[^3] MOST is responsible for formulating and implementing science and technology policies, coordinating research, and managing scholarship programs in BARMM. The Parliament has enacted one BAA that directly implements this power: BAA 40, the Bangsamoro Science High School System Act, which creates a system of science-oriented secondary schools across the region.[^4] BAA 40 is listed under both the education power (v) and the science and technology power (uu), reflecting its dual nature as both an educational institution and a science development mechanism.

Beyond BAA 40, MOST exercises its mandate through its BAA 13 general authority, which provides the organizational basis but not the detailed standards, research funding mechanisms, or institutional framework that standalone legislation would establish. The Development Academy of the Bangsamoro (DAB), funded through BAA 85, Chapter XXVII, supports human resource development and capacity building, touching on the scholarship dimension of this power.[^5]

Parliamentary resolutions reflect interest in science and technology outcomes. Resolution 482 commended MOST's scholarship program and its graduates, indicating the ministry's active role in scholarship administration. Resolution 389 congratulated a Chemistry Olympiad bronze medalist, and Resolution 485 congratulated a Nuclear Science Olympiad gold medalist — both highlighting BARMM's emerging profile in science competitions. No dedicated parliamentary resolutions specifically direct science and technology policy development.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not require this power to be exercised in accordance with national law. Article IX, Section 21 contains no consistency clause, meaning the Bangsamoro Government has autonomous authority to set its own science and technology priorities, establish research institutions, and design scholarship programs. In the absence of a comprehensive Bangsamoro Science and Technology Code, the national framework — primarily Republic Act No. 2067 (Science Act of 1958) as amended by RA 3589, and Republic Act No. 8439 (Magna Carta for Scientists, Engineers, Researchers, and other S&T Personnel) — applies suppletorily.[^6]

RA 11035 (Balik Scientist Act) and RA 10612 (Fast-Tracked S&T Scholarship Act) also apply suppletorily in BARMM for returning scientists and government-funded science scholarships. The Department of Science and Technology (DOST) continues to operate programs in BARMM, including regional scholarship programs. Once the Parliament enacts its own science and technology code, Bangsamoro policy would govern, with national law applying only suppletorily on matters not covered by the BAA.

There is no intergovernmental body under Article VI specifically for science and technology. Coordination with DOST occurs through operational arrangements rather than a BOL-mandated mechanism.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The science and technology power has one of the largest legislative gaps in the BOL's enumerated powers. No comprehensive Bangsamoro Science and Technology Code exists. MOST operates entirely through its BAA 13 mandate, without a dedicated legislative framework for research funding, technology development, innovation policy, or intellectual property incentives. The only enacted legislation (BAA 40) addresses science education at the secondary level, leaving the full breadth of the BOL's science and technology mandate — research councils, technology adoption, innovation support, scholarship programs — unlegislated.

A Bangsamoro Science and Technology Code would codify: the establishment and governance of research councils (for agriculture, health, marine sciences, Islamic studies, and indigenous knowledge systems); a regional research fund with transparent allocation criteria; technology transfer and adoption mechanisms, particularly for agriculture and fisheries; innovation incentives for Bangsamoro enterprises; and a framework for protecting traditional knowledge and indigenous innovations. The code could also establish a Bangsamoro equivalent of the Magna Carta for Scientists, providing career incentives to attract and retain science professionals in the region.

The scholarship dimension also lacks comprehensive legislation. While MOST administers scholarships through its general mandate, no unified Bangsamoro Scholarship Act codifies eligibility criteria, funding mechanisms, return-service obligations, or priority fields of study aligned with BDP development targets. A standalone scholarship act could integrate MOST scholarships with MBHTE education programs and DAB capacity building, creating a coherent human capital development pipeline.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028 addresses this power primarily in Chapter 8 (Technology and Innovation), which aims to build regional capacity for technology adoption, promote research and development, and develop a science-oriented workforce.[^7] Without a science and technology code, the BDP's Chapter 8 targets lack the institutional and legal infrastructure for systematic implementation. Chapter 7 (Economic Development) also depends on technology and innovation to drive productivity improvements in agriculture, fisheries, and trade — all of which require the science and technology power to be fully exercised.
