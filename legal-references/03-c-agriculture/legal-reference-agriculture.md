# (c) Agriculture, Livestock, and Food Security

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(c)** — ***"Agriculture, livestock, and food security;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(c) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XIII, Sec. 23 requires agriculture and fisheries policies to be ***"consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws"*** |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR)** — promotes sustainable agri-fishery growth and equitable land distribution (BAA 13, Book V, Title I, Sec. 2-15) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT); Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE); Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute (BAFTI, BAA 59); Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI, BAA 13) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) |
| **Priority Code** | None named in BOL — but the scope warrants a comprehensive **Bangsamoro Agriculture and Food Security Code** |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over agriculture?**

A. Article V, Section 2(c) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"agriculture, livestock, and food security"*** as one of the 55 powers. This is a broad grant covering **crop production**, **livestock management**, **food security** programs, and related agricultural governance within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Four provisions elaborate:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 23 is the primary elaboration. It directs the Bangsamoro Government's agriculture and fisheries policies to ***"advance agriculture as a key development strategy, promote productivity measures, and provide support for small farmers, landholders, and marginal fisherfolk."*** It also establishes **preferential fishing rights** for Bangsamoro people, indigenous peoples, and resident marginal fisherfolk in regional waters. Critically, it imposes a **national law consistency requirement**: ***"Consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws."***[^2]

   (2) Art. VII, Sec. 5(f) authorizes Parliament to ***"enact a law ... to regulate the grant of regional franchises and concessions, and empower the Chief Minister to grant leases, permits, and licenses over agricultural lands."*** This links the agriculture power to **land allocation authority**.[^3]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 19 establishes the **Joint Body for Zones of Joint Cooperation** co-chaired by the **Secretary of Agriculture** and a Bangsamoro Minister — addressing fishing grounds and resource-sharing in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf.[^4]

   (4) Art. XII, Sec. 9(d) provides **fiscal protection** by excluding from the Bangsamoro taxing power: ***"Taxes, fees, or charges on agricultural and aquatic products when sold by marginal farmers or fisherfolk."***[^5]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation?**

A. The BOL does not name a specific "agriculture code" by title. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 23 contemplates ***"policies and laws of the Bangsamoro Government on agriculture, fisheries, and aquatic resources"*** — implying legislative action. Art. VII, Sec. 5(f) explicitly mandates Parliament to ***"enact a law"*** on agricultural land leases and permits. The combined effect is a strong legislative obligation — though no named code is mandated.[^2][^3]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body for this power?**

A. The BOL creates the **Joint Body for Zones of Joint Cooperation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 19) co-chaired by the Secretary of Agriculture and a Bangsamoro Minister. MAFAR itself was established through **BAA 13** (Administrative Code) as the lead ministry, receiving budget allocation under **BAA 85**, Chapter XVI. **BAFTI** (BAA 59) is the dedicated training institution.[^4][^6][^7]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three express limitations:

   (1) **National law consistency**: Art. XIII, Sec. 23 requires consistency with ***"Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws"*** — one of the BOL's most explicit consistency requirements.[^2]

   (2) **Constitutional floor**: Art. XIII, Sec. 1 requires the economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution"*** — including the constitutional mandate for agrarian reform (Art. XIII, Sec. 4-8 of the Constitution).[^8]

   (3) **Fiscal limitation**: Art. XII, Sec. 9(d) prohibits taxing agricultural products sold by marginal farmers or fisherfolk.[^5]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to agriculture?**

A. Four categories:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: The universal constitutional boundary.[^10]

   (2) **Agrarian Reform (Art. XIII, Sec. 4-8)**: The Constitution mandates ***"a comprehensive agrarian reform program"*** to promote social justice. Art. XIII, Sec. 4 states: ***"The State shall, by law, undertake an agrarian reform program founded on the right of farmers and regular farmworkers ... to own directly or collectively the lands they till."*** This is a constitutional mandate that applies in BARMM — any Bangsamoro agrarian reform must meet this floor.[^11]

   (3) **Social Justice (Art. XIII, Sec. 1)**: ***"The Congress shall give highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity, reduce social, economic, and political inequalities."*** Agriculture is a primary vehicle for social justice in BARMM.[^12]

   (4) **Right to Food and Basic Services**: While the Constitution does not expressly guarantee a "right to food," Art. II, Sec. 9 (social justice), Art. XIII, Sec. 1-2 (social justice and human rights), and Art. II, Sec. 18 (labor and employment) collectively support food security as a state obligation.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for agriculture?**

A. The national framework is comprehensive: **RA 8550** (Fisheries Code) governs fisheries management; **RA 8435** (AFMA) provides the agricultural modernization framework — infrastructure, credit, R&D, trade, and extension services; **RA 6657/9700** (CARL/CARPER) governs agrarian reform; **RA 10068/11511** (Organic Agriculture Act) promotes organic farming; **RA 10611** (Food Safety Act) establishes food safety standards; **RA 11321** (Sagip Saka Act) links farmers to institutional markets; **RA 11203** (Rice Tariffication Law) liberalizes rice trade and funds the Rice Competitiveness Enhancement Fund; **RA 11901** strengthens agricultural financing. Together, these provide comprehensive **regulatory standards**, **institutional frameworks**, **financing mechanisms**, and **market linkage** systems.[^13][^14][^15][^16][^17][^18][^19][^20]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national agricultural laws apply in the absence of a comprehensive Bangsamoro agriculture code:

   (1) **RA 8550** — applies **directly** by express BOL mandate (Art. XIII, Sec. 23).[^13]
   (2) **RA 8435** — **suppletory**: agricultural modernization framework.[^14]
   (3) **RA 6657/9700** — **suppletory**: agrarian reform. DAR retains national authority; MAFAR exercises delegated functions within BARMM.[^15]
   (4) **RA 10068/11511** — **suppletory**: organic agriculture framework.[^16]
   (5) **RA 10611** — **suppletory**: food safety standards.[^17]
   (6) **RA 11203** — **directly applicable**: rice farmers eligible for RCEF.[^19]
   (7) **RA 11901** — **suppletory**: agricultural financing.[^20]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM?**

A. (1) **Department of Agriculture (DA)** — retains regulatory authority over quarantine, plant health, and animal disease control; co-chairs the Joint Body for Zones of Joint Cooperation with MAFAR; implements nationally-funded agricultural programs. (2) **Department of Agrarian Reform (DAR)** — retains authority over agrarian reform case adjudication and national policy; MAFAR exercises delegated functions per BAA 13. (3) **Bureau of Fisheries and Aquatic Resources (BFAR)** — national fisheries regulatory functions apply under RA 8550 consistency requirement.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry is primarily responsible?**

A. **MAFAR** is the lead agency. BAA 13, Book V, Title I, Chapter 1, Sec. 2 defines its mandate: ***"The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform and its attached agencies and bureaus shall promote sustainable Agri-fishery growth and development, and equitable land distribution and tenurial security for the benefit of the landless farmers in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** It further mandates MAFAR to ***"ensure the availability, adequacy, accessibility, and affordability of food supplies at all times in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** BAA 85, Chapter XVI allocates its operational budget.[^6][^7]

**Q. What standalone legislation has Parliament enacted?**

A. **BAA 59** (BAFTI) is the only standalone BAA directly implementing the agriculture power. It creates the Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute for specialized farmer and fisherfolk training. No comprehensive agriculture code has been enacted.

**Q. What are MAFAR's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book V, Title I, Chapter 1, Sec. 3 enumerates **28 functions** organized across agriculture, fisheries, and agrarian reform:

   **Agriculture functions** (Sec. 3(a)-(h)):
   - (a) ***"Provide integrated services to farmers, fishermen, and other food producers on production, utilization, conservation, and disposition of agricultural livestock and fishery resources;"***
   - (b) ***"Plan, formulate, execute, regulate and monitor programs and activities relating to agriculture, food production, and supply;"***
   - (c) ***"Promulgate and enforce all laws, rules and regulations governing the conservation and proper utilization of agricultural, livestock, and fishery resources;"***
   - (d) ***"Establish central and regional information systems to serve the production, marketing, and financing data requirements of the farmers;"***
   - (e) ***"Provide comprehensive and effective extension services and training to farmers;"***
   - (f) ***"Conduct, coordinate, and disseminate research studies on appropriate technologies;"***
   - (g) ***"Provide the mechanisms for the participation of farmers, fishermen and agricultural entrepreneurs at all levels of policy-making;"***
   - (h) ***"Coordinate with and enlist other public and private agencies for cooperation and assistance"***[^6]

   **Fisheries functions** (Sec. 3(i)-(n)):
   - (i) ***"Issue clearances and permits for the importation and exportation of agricultural and fishery commodities;"***
   - (j) ***"Approve charter contracts, issue permits for Special Commercial Fishing Boats and Licenses for Commercial Fishing Vessels;"***
   - (k) ***"Determine and designate fish landing points;"***
   - (l) ***"Assist LGUs in the management and conservation of their municipal waters;"***
   - (m) ***"Regulate and supervise the production, capture, and gathering of fish and fishery/aquatic products;"***
   - (n) ***"Designate areas in the BARMM as fishery reserves"***[^6]

   **Agrarian reform functions** (Sec. 3(o)-(ab)):
   - (o) ***"Implement all agrarian laws"*** with subpoena and contempt authority;
   - (p)-(q) Advise on agrarian reform policy and establish operational rules;
   - (r)-(t) Coordinate with LBP; acquire, administer, distribute agricultural lands; conduct surveys;
   - (u) ***"Issue Certificates of Land Ownership Awards (CLOA) and emancipation patents;"***
   - (v) ***"Provide free legal services to agrarian reform beneficiaries;"***
   - (w)-(y) Promote cooperatives; conduct continuing education; institutionalize farmer participation;
   - (z) ***"Exercise exclusive authority to approve or disapprove conversion of agricultural lands;"***[^6]

   **Organizational structure** (Sec. 7-15): Three **Directors-General** (Agriculture, Fisheries, Agrarian Reform), each with specialized services. The **Bangsamoro Agrarian Reform Adjudication Board** (Sec. 12) adjudicates agrarian reform cases. Provincial and Municipal offices extend MAFAR's reach to the field level (Sec. 13-14).[^6]

**Q. What can MAFAR NOT do without a standalone agriculture code?**

A. Without a comprehensive code, MAFAR cannot: (1) establish **Bangsamoro-specific agricultural regulatory standards** — pesticide/fertilizer regulations, agricultural product quality standards, livestock health codes with legal enforcement mechanisms; (2) create a **Bangsamoro agrarian reform framework** addressing the unique intersection of CARP, ancestral domain, and Islamic land tenure (*waqf*, *muzara'ah*); (3) establish **crop insurance and Shari'ah-compliant agricultural credit** programs with statutory authority; (4) create **food security institutional mechanisms** with enforceable targets and accountability; (5) define the **jurisdictional boundary with DA and DAR** — particularly on regulatory functions and agrarian reform adjudication.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is MAFAR the correct lead institution?**

A. Yes. MAFAR's mandate is comprehensive — covering agriculture, fisheries, AND agrarian reform in a single ministry. This integrated structure is an advantage over the national system where DA, DAR, and BFAR are separate agencies. However, the scope is enormous — three Directors-General, provincial and municipal offices, an adjudication board — making institutional capacity a significant challenge.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist?**

A. (1) **MAFAR** — lead ministry (BAA 13, BAA 85 Ch. XVI); (2) **BAFTI** — training institute (BAA 59); (3) **Bangsamoro Agrarian Reform Adjudication Board** — adjudicates agrarian cases (BAA 13, Sec. 12); (4) **Bangsamoro Halal Board** — MAFAR participates in halal certification (BAA 13); (5) **Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI)** — MAFAR Minister is an *ex officio* member (BAA 13, Sec. 28); (6) **Joint Body for Zones of Joint Cooperation** — intergovernmental body co-chaired by DA Secretary and MAFAR Minister (Art. XIII, Sec. 19).

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. Three national agencies maintain significant presence: (1) **DA** — implements nationally-funded agricultural programs (RCEF under RA 11203, AFMA infrastructure projects); regulatory authority over plant quarantine, animal disease control, and veterinary drug regulation; co-chairs the Zones of Joint Cooperation Joint Body; (2) **DAR** — retains national agrarian reform policy authority; MAFAR exercises delegated functions but DAR maintains oversight of CLOA issuance and land valuation; (3) **BFAR** — national fisheries regulation under RA 8550, which BARMM must comply with.

**Q. What is the status of devolution?**

A. Devolution is **partially complete**. MAFAR exercises agricultural extension, fisheries management, and agrarian reform functions within BARMM. However, DA regulatory functions (quarantine, plant health, animal disease control) have not been fully devolved. DAR retains policy authority over agrarian reform nationally. The devolution of specific functions — particularly regulatory and adjudicatory — depends on BARMM enacting comprehensive legislation that can replace the suppletory application of national laws.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has BARMM accomplished?**

A. (1) **MAFAR operational** with three Directors-General, provincial offices across all BARMM provinces, and municipal offices; (2) **BAFTI established** (BAA 59) for farmer and fisherfolk training; (3) Agricultural extension services delivered — input distribution, livestock development, crop production programs; (4) Agrarian reform functions exercised — CLOA issuance, land surveys, beneficiary development; (5) Fisheries regulation — licensing of commercial fishing vessels, management of municipal waters; (6) Parliament has signaled legislative intent through 7 filed bills (PB 184, 221, 244, 249, 260, 289, 291) and 2 resolutions (Res. 221, 222).

**Q. Where is BARMM stronger than the national government?**

A. BARMM has two unique advantages: (1) **Integrated agriculture-fisheries-agrarian reform ministry** — MAFAR combines functions that are split among DA, DAR, and BFAR nationally, enabling coordinated policy; (2) **Conflict-sensitive agricultural programming** — MAFAR can design programs for IDP farming communities, ex-combatant livelihood cooperatives, and disaster-recovery agriculture that the national system cannot.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back?**

A. The **primary constraint** is the absence of a **comprehensive Bangsamoro Agriculture and Food Security Code**. Without it, MAFAR cannot independently regulate agricultural standards, establish Shari'ah-compliant agricultural credit, address the *waqf*-CARP tension, or create enforceable food security targets. The secondary constraint is **incomplete devolution** — DA regulatory functions and DAR policy authority have not been fully transferred.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. (1) **Legislative depth** — RA 8435 (AFMA), RA 8550 (Fisheries Code), RA 6657/9700 (CARP/CARPER), and 10+ supporting laws provide a comprehensive regulatory framework with decades of implementing rules and jurisprudence; (2) **Institutional infrastructure** — DA has regional offices, research centers (PhilRice, PCAARRD), and field stations nationwide; DAR has provincial agrarian reform officers in every province; (3) **Regulatory systems** — quarantine protocols, plant variety protection database, livestock disease surveillance, food safety inspection; (4) **Financing mechanisms** — RCEF (RA 11203), agricultural credit guarantee (RA 11901), crop insurance (PCIC).

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. (1) **No Shari'ah-compliant agricultural financing** — all national agricultural credit programs use conventional interest-bearing instruments; (2) **No recognition of Islamic land tenure** — CARP does not account for *waqf*, *muzara'ah*, or Islamic inheritance patterns; (3) **No conflict-sensitive agricultural framework** — national programs are designed for stable agricultural communities, not post-conflict environments; (4) **Language and cultural barriers** — DA extension materials are primarily in Filipino and English, not in Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, or Yakan.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Agriculture in BARMM has a **significant Shari'ah dimension** in three areas: (1) **Islamic land tenure** — *muzara'ah* (sharecropping), *musaqah* (irrigation partnership), and *waqf* (endowment) are traditional agricultural arrangements that coexist with and sometimes conflict with the national agrarian reform framework; (2) **Agricultural finance** — the prohibition of *riba* (interest) means conventional agricultural credit is not Shari'ah-compliant; alternatives such as *salam* (forward sale) and *murabaha* (cost-plus) financing are needed; (3) **Halal standards** — BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 30 establishes a halal program; BAA 13 creates the Bangsamoro Halal Board with MAFAR participation; livestock production must accommodate *halal* slaughter requirements.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Five principles directly apply:

   (1) ***Muzara'ah*** — the predominant traditional farming arrangement. A Bangsamoro agrarian reform act must recognize this alongside CARP-based tenure.

   (2) ***Waqf*** — agricultural land dedicated as *waqf* cannot be sold or redistributed under agrarian reform. This creates a direct tension with CARP compulsory acquisition.

   (3) **Prohibition of *riba*** — agricultural credit must include Shari'ah-compliant alternatives (*salam*, *murabaha*).

   (4) ***Halal*** standards — livestock production, food processing, and the food supply chain must accommodate Islamic dietary requirements.

   (5) ***Maslahah*** — food security is a fundamental public welfare obligation.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) governs property relations for Muslims — including inheritance of agricultural land, *waqf* endowments, and gifts (*hibah*). Its property provisions directly affect how agricultural land is held, transferred, and distributed among Muslim landholders. Shari'ah courts have jurisdiction over property disputes among Muslims — agricultural land disputes between Muslim parties could fall within this jurisdiction. No specific *fatawa* on agricultural contracts have been identified from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'*, though the principles of *muzara'ah* and *musaqah* are well-established in classical Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*).[^9]

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can BARMM diverge from national agricultural law?**

A. BARMM's divergence is **moderately constrained** by the express consistency requirement. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XIII, Sec. 23 requires consistency with ***"Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws."*** This is broader than most BOL consistency requirements — it references "other relevant national laws" generally, not just RA 8550. As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), the constitutional agrarian reform mandate (Art. XIII, Sec. 4-8) sets the floor. BARMM **cannot**: enact fisheries regulations that contradict RA 8550; exempt agricultural lands from agrarian reform coverage (constitutional mandate); or lower food safety standards below national minimums. BARMM **can**: add Shari'ah-compliant agricultural credit as an alternative; recognize Islamic land tenure alongside CARP; establish region-specific crop insurance; create halal-specific livestock and food processing standards; and design agricultural programs for conflict-affected communities.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. Agricultural products from BARMM must meet national food safety standards (RA 10611) to access national markets. BARMM halal standards would be additional requirements, not replacements for national standards. Farmers holding CLOAs under national CARP continue to be protected by RA 6657 regardless of any Bangsamoro agrarian reform act. Cross-boundary implications are significant for fisheries — the Zones of Joint Cooperation (Art. XIII, Sec. 18-21) require coordinated regulation between BARMM and the national government.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions?**

A. Yes. Three overlaps: (1) **MAFAR vs. DA** — regulatory authority over quarantine, plant health, and animal disease control within BARMM is unclear; (2) **MAFAR vs. DAR** — agrarian reform adjudication authority is shared between the Bangsamoro Agrarian Reform Adjudication Board (BAA 13, Sec. 12) and DAR's national adjudication system; (3) **Fisheries regulation** — MAFAR regulates fishing within BARMM waters, but the RA 8550 consistency requirement means BFAR standards apply.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Seven bills address parts of the gap: PB 260 (agricultural modernization), PB 184 (agricultural microfinance), PB 289 (agri-fishery worker social protection), PB 249 (veterinary services), PB 291 (agricultural education), PB 244 (fruit-bearing trees), PB 221 (agricultural engineering bureau). However, **no comprehensive agriculture and food security code** has been filed — these are sectoral bills that address fragments of the gap.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP goals and Matatag pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) identifies agriculture as the **backbone of the Bangsamoro economy** — the sector employing the majority of the region's workforce. Targets include increased agricultural productivity, crop diversification, and value-chain development. Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) addresses sustainable agriculture, climate-resilient farming, and integrated watershed management. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, prioritizes food security and agricultural productivity as foundations of economic resilience — reducing dependence on imported food.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if gaps remain?**

A. Without a comprehensive agriculture code: MAFAR lacks the **regulatory authority** to enforce agricultural standards, regulate markets, or create institutional architecture that transforms subsistence farming into a productive sector. **Agricultural cooperatives** — critical for food security in conflict-affected areas — operate without a region-specific support framework. **Shari'ah-compliant agricultural credit** remains unavailable, excluding observant Muslim farmers from formal financing. The **waqf-CARP intersection** remains unresolved, creating legal uncertainty for Muslim landholders. The **BDP targets** for agricultural productivity depend on institutional infrastructure that BAA 13's general mandate alone cannot provide.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does this power interact with power (y) Fishery, marine, and aquatic resources?**

A. The BOL treats agriculture and fisheries together in Art. XIII, Sec. 23. MAFAR has jurisdiction over both (BAA 13 includes a Bangsamoro Director-General for Fisheries). The consistency requirement with RA 8550 technically applies to fisheries, but Art. XIII, Sec. 23 also covers agricultural policy. The **Zones of Joint Cooperation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 18-21) primarily affect fisheries but indirectly impact agricultural fisherfolk communities.

**Q. How does the waqf-CARP conflict affect agrarian reform in BARMM?**

A. *Waqf* agricultural land is **inalienable** under Islamic law — it cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited as personal property. CARP (RA 6657) authorizes **compulsory acquisition** of agricultural land for redistribution. The conflict arises when *waqf* agricultural land falls within CARP coverage — can the government compel redistribution of *waqf*? PD 1083 recognizes *waqf* under Muslim personal law. The BOL does not expressly resolve this conflict. A Bangsamoro Agrarian Reform Act should address this by: (a) recognizing *waqf* as an exemption or special category under BARMM agrarian reform; (b) providing alternative mechanisms for *waqf* beneficiaries (e.g., stewardship leases rather than ownership transfer); (c) coordinating with DAR on cases where *waqf* designation is contested.[^9]

**Q. What is the significance of halal standards for this power?**

A. BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 30 establishes a **halal program** and accreditation of halal-certifying bodies. BAA 13 creates the **Bangsamoro Halal Board** (Sec. 35-36) with MAFAR as a key participant — the MAFAR Minister serves as Vice Chairperson (Sec. 35(b)). *Halal* standards directly affect livestock production (slaughter methods), food processing (ingredient compliance), and the food supply chain. A comprehensive agriculture code should integrate halal standards into the livestock and food safety regulatory framework, rather than treating halal as a separate program.
