# (c) Agriculture, Livestock, and Food Security

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(c)** — "Agriculture, livestock, and food security;"

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(c) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XIII, Sec. 23 requires agriculture and fisheries policies to be "consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550 ... as amended, and other relevant national laws" |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT); Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) |

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The Bangsamoro Organic Law grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over agriculture, livestock, and food security as one of 55 enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2(c). This power encompasses the full range of agricultural governance — from crop production and livestock management to food supply chain regulation and food security programs.

Article XIII, Section 23 elaborates on this power by directing the Bangsamoro Government to "advance agriculture as a key development strategy, promote productivity measures, and provide support for small farmers, landholders, and marginal fisherfolk." This provision treats agriculture and fisheries together, recognizing their intertwined role in the Bangsamoro economy. Importantly, Section 23 grants Bangsamoro people, indigenous peoples, and resident marginal fisherfolk preferential rights in Bangsamoro regional waters, though without prejudice to the fishing rights of other Filipino citizens. Article VII, Section 5(f) further empowers the Parliament to "enact a law within the competencies of the Bangsamoro Government to regulate the grant of regional franchises and concessions, and empower the Chief Minister to grant leases, permits, and licenses over agricultural lands and for forest management." This provision directly links the agriculture power to land allocation authority. The Zones of Joint Cooperation provisions in Article XIII, Sections 18-21 also protect traditional fishing grounds and regulate resource-sharing in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf, which directly affect agricultural fisherfolk communities.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) is the lead agency, with a budget allocation under BAA 85 (General Appropriations Act, FY 2026), Chapter XVI. MAFAR operates under its BAA 13 (Administrative Code) mandate, which assigns it responsibility for agriculture, fisheries, agrarian reform, and food security within BARMM. MAFAR delivers extension services, distributes agricultural inputs, manages livestock development programs, and implements food production initiatives across the region.

BAA 59 created the Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute (BAFTI), which provides specialized training for farmers and fisherfolk. This is the only standalone BAA directly implementing this power. Without a comprehensive agriculture code, MAFAR's regulatory authority remains limited to what the Administrative Code's general mandate covers — program delivery, technical assistance, and coordination. It cannot independently establish agricultural standards, create a region-specific regulatory framework for pesticide and fertilizer use, or implement a Bangsamoro-specific agrarian reform program with the legal force of dedicated legislation.

Parliament has signaled awareness of the sector's importance through several filed bills, including PB No. 260 (Agricultural Modernization Strategies), PB No. 184 (Agricultural Microfinance), PB No. 289 (Social Protection for Agri-Fishery Workers), and PB No. 249 (Veterinary Services Augmentation). Resolutions 221 and 222 directed MAFAR to report on the coconut industry and modify procurement of motorized bancas for fisherfolk.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL explicitly requires agriculture and fisheries policies to be "consistent with the provisions of Republic Act No. 8550, otherwise known as 'The Philippine Fisheries Code of 1998,' as amended, and other relevant national laws" (Art. XIII, Sec. 23). This is one of the clearest national law consistency requirements in the BOL. The Bangsamoro Government's agricultural legislation must therefore operate within the framework established by national agricultural laws, including RA 8435 (Agriculture and Fisheries Modernization Act), RA 10068 (Organic Agriculture Act), RA 7900 (High-Value Crops Development Act), and RA 11321 (Sagip Saka Act). The Department of Agriculture's regulatory standards — on quarantine, plant health, and animal disease control — also apply within BARMM, with MAFAR serving as the regional implementing arm.

No intergovernmental body is specifically created for agriculture alone, but the Joint Body for the Zones of Joint Cooperation (Art. VI, Sec. 5; Art. XIII, Sec. 19) co-chaired by the Secretary of Agriculture and a Bangsamoro Minister handles policies for fishing grounds and resource-sharing in the Sulu Sea and Moro Gulf. This body directly affects the agricultural-fisheries sector.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

Despite agriculture being the primary livelihood of the Bangsamoro people, there is no comprehensive Bangsamoro Agriculture and Food Security Code. MAFAR operates entirely under its BAA 13 general mandate and the standalone BAFTI law (BAA 59). The absence of dedicated legislation means the region lacks: a Bangsamoro-specific agrarian reform framework that accounts for the region's unique land tenure patterns (including ancestral domain and Torrens title overlap), regulatory standards for agricultural inputs and products, a food security institutional framework with enforceable targets and accountability mechanisms, and a livestock and animal health regulatory system.

A Bangsamoro Agriculture and Food Security Act would codify: agricultural modernization strategies adapted to BARMM's island and mainland geography, crop insurance and agricultural credit mechanisms, livestock development and animal health standards, food security programs with measurable targets, market linkage and value chain development mechanisms, and MAFAR's regulatory authority over the sector. PB No. 260 (Agricultural Modernization Strategies) addresses part of this gap but has not been enacted.

A separate Bangsamoro Agrarian Reform Act would address the region's land distribution challenges, particularly the interplay between ancestral domain claims, public land classification, and agricultural land reform — an area where national agrarian reform law (RA 6657) may not adequately address the Bangsamoro context.

### Development and Policy Connection

BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) identifies agriculture as the backbone of the Bangsamoro economy, with the sector employing the majority of the region's workforce. The BDP targets increased agricultural productivity, crop diversification, and value-chain development. Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) addresses the sustainable use of agricultural resources and climate-resilient farming practices. The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028) prioritizes food security under its economic resilience pillar. Without comprehensive agriculture legislation, MAFAR lacks the regulatory authority and institutional framework to achieve these targets — the ministry can deliver programs but cannot enforce standards, regulate markets, or create the institutional architecture that transforms subsistence farming into a productive agricultural sector.
