# (hh) Inland Waterways for Navigation

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(hh)** — ***"Inland waterways for navigation;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(hh) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Intergovernmental coordination — Art. XIII, Sec. 22 (last paragraph) requires the Bangsamoro Government to ***"coordinate and cooperate with the Philippine Coast Guard concerning inland waterways for navigation."*** No express "consistent with national law" clause, but PCG coordination is mandatory. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Transportation and Communications (MTC)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XV; BAA 85, Chapter VII |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Maritime Industry Authority (BMARINA); Bangsamoro Ports Management Authority (BPMA); Philippine Coast Guard (PCG) — BARMM Regional Office; Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE); Ministry of Public Works (MPW) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development — Water Transport); Chapter 3 (Spatial Development Strategy) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None — the BOL does not name a specific code for inland waterways. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates the Parliament to ***"enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection"*** of inland waters generally (power (gg)), which includes but is not limited to navigation. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant — constitutional floor — national law — implementation — Shari'ah dimension — divergence — gaps — development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(hh) enumerates ***"Inland waterways for navigation"*** as a Bangsamoro Government power. This is a **navigation-specific grant** — authority over the **transport use** of inland waterways (rivers, river systems, lakes, and streams within BARMM). It is distinct from power (gg) — ***"Inland waters"*** — which covers the preservation, management, and conservation of the water bodies themselves. Power (hh) governs what moves **on** the water; power (gg) governs the water **itself**.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**
A. The BOL provides one direct elaboration and several supporting provisions:

1. **Art. XIII, Sec. 22 (last paragraph)** — the sole provision that expressly addresses power (hh): ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall coordinate and cooperate with the Philippine Coast Guard concerning inland waterways for navigation."*** This establishes a **mandatory intergovernmental coordination requirement** — the Bangsamoro Government cannot exercise navigation authority in isolation from the PCG.[^2]

2. **Art. XIII, Sec. 22 (first paragraph)** — while primarily elaborating power (gg), this provision defines the territorial scope of inland waters over which navigation authority applies: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall have exclusive powers over inland waters, including lakes, marshes, rivers, and tributaries within its territorial jurisdiction."*** The Parliament's mandate to ***"enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection of these resources"*** encompasses navigation regulation as one dimension of managing these waters.[^3]

3. **Art. III, Sec. 3** — defines ***"All inland waters such as lakes, rivers, river systems, and streams within its territorial jurisdiction"*** as forming part of the Bangsamoro territory — establishing the geographic scope of navigation jurisdiction.[^4]

4. **Art. XIII, Sec. 38** — grants authority to ***"grant regional franchises, licenses, and permits to land, sea, and air transportation plying routes in the provinces or cities within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** — which includes vessels plying inland waterways.[^5]

5. **Art. XI, Sec. 7** — establishes that the PCG ***"shall establish a regional office in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** under ***"the direct operational and administrative control and supervision of the Philippine Coast Guard."*** This defines the nature of the coordination partner: a national agency not under Bangsamoro control.[^6]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**
A. No. The BOL does not name a specific inland waterway navigation code. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates the Parliament to ***"enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection of these resources"*** — a broad legislative mandate covering inland waters that encompasses navigation regulation as one component. The mandate to create ***"a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water"*** could include navigation regulatory functions within such an authority.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**
A. Not for navigation specifically. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 directs the Parliament to ***"create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water"*** — this is a general inland waters mandate under power (gg) that could house navigation functions. The BOL does not create a dedicated inland waterway navigation authority.[^8]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**
A. Two limitations:

1. **Mandatory PCG coordination** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall coordinate and cooperate with the Philippine Coast Guard concerning inland waterways for navigation."*** This is an express coordination requirement, not merely permissive.[^9]

2. **Energy exception** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22: inland waters ***"that provide energy to power generating plants"*** are excluded from Bangsamoro authority. Where an inland waterway used for navigation also serves as an energy source for areas outside BARMM (e.g., Lake Lanao/Agus Hydropower Complex), the **co-management principle** of Art. XIII, Sec. 10 and Art. XII, Sec. 34 applies — limiting unilateral Bangsamoro control over navigation in those waters.[^10]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**
A. Three layers of constitutional constraint apply:

1. **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)** — all autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law."*** This is the **universal constitutional boundary** on all 55 powers — Bangsamoro navigation authority operates within this framework.[^11]

2. **National patrimony** — Art. XII, Sec. 2: ***"All lands of the public domain, waters, minerals, coal, petroleum, and other mineral oils, all forces of potential energy, fisheries, forests or timber, wildlife, flora and fauna, and other natural resources are owned by the State."*** Inland waters are State-owned natural resources. BARMM exercises management and navigation authority over these waters under the BOL, but ownership remains with the State. This means navigation legislation cannot treat inland waterways as private or regional property.[^12]

3. **State policies** — Art. II, Sec. 19: ***"The State shall develop a self-reliant and independent national economy effectively controlled by Filipinos"*** — supporting domestic transport infrastructure including inland waterways.[^13]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**
A. Art. III (Bill of Rights) protections apply to all persons using inland waterways — due process (Sec. 1) in regulation and enforcement, equal protection (Sec. 1) in access to waterway transport, and protection against unreasonable searches and seizures (Sec. 2) in vessel inspections. Navigation regulation must provide procedural due process before imposing penalties on vessel operators.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**
A. No constitutional provision specifically mandates an inland waterway navigation institution. The general mandate for autonomous regions (Art. X, Sec. 20) provides the constitutional basis for the BOL's grant of this power. Art. X, Sec. 15 creates the framework: autonomous regions shall be established ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines"*** — meaning navigation authority is a delegated power, not inherent sovereignty over waterways.[^14]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**
A. The national framework provides:

1. **Maritime safety enforcement** — RA 9993 (Philippine Coast Guard Law) assigns the PCG authority over ***maritime safety, law enforcement, and search and rescue*** in all navigable waters, including inland waterways. PCG enforces vessel safety standards, conducts inspections, and manages search-and-rescue operations.[^15]

2. **Maritime industry regulation** — RA 10635 establishes MARINA as the ***"single maritime administration"*** responsible for implementing international maritime conventions and setting standards for seafarer training and vessel certification.[^16]

3. **Protection of navigable waterways** — RA 2056 prohibits the ***construction of dams, dikes or any other works in public navigable waters or waterways*** — protecting navigation channels from obstruction.[^17]

4. **Transportation franchising** — the national DOTC framework (EO 125 and successor legislation) provides the institutional model for transportation regulation that BARMM's MTC mirrors.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**
A. **RA 9993** (PCG Law) applies **directly** — the PCG's BARMM regional office operates under national PCG control per BOL Art. XI, Sec. 7. **RA 10635** (MARINA) applies **partially** — BMARINA exercises delegated functions but national MARINA retains standards-setting authority. **RA 2056** applies **suppletorily** — no BAA regulates obstruction of navigable waterways, so the national law fills the gap. **EO 477** determines the PCG-DOTr reporting relationship that structures intergovernmental coordination.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**
A. Two national institutions operate in BARMM:

1. **Philippine Coast Guard (PCG) — BARMM Regional Office** — established per BOL Art. XI, Sec. 7. Exercises law enforcement over maritime and inland waterway safety. Under direct national PCG operational control.

2. **Maritime Industry Authority (MARINA)** — national standards-setting authority for vessel certification and seafarer training. BMARINA exercises devolved functions but coordinates with national MARINA on standards compliance.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**
A. The **Ministry of Transportation and Communications (MTC)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XV, Sec. 2 defines MTC's mandate as ***"the primary policy, planning, programming, coordinating, implementing, regulating, and administrative entity of the Bangsamoro Government in the promotion, development, and regulation of dependable and coordinated networks of transportation and communications systems."*** BAA 85, Chapter VII allocates MTC's operational budget.[^18]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**
A. None. No standalone BAA addresses inland waterway navigation as of BAA 89. The sole legislative framework is BAA 13 (Administrative Code), which assigns the function to MTC.

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XV, Sec. 3 enumerates **16 powers and functions** of MTC. The function directly relevant to inland waterway navigation is:

- **Sec. 3(e)**: ***"Manage navigation in inland waterways within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** — the express statutory basis for BARMM's exercise of power (hh).[^19]

Additional MTC functions supporting this power:

- **Sec. 3(c)**: ***"Grant regional franchises, licenses, and permits to and exercise quasi-judicial powers over land, sea, and air transportation plying routes within the Bangsamoro Region"*** — includes vessels on inland waterways.[^20]
- **Sec. 3(d)**: ***"Register land, sea, and air transportation operating exclusively within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** — covers vessel registration for inland waterway operators.[^21]
- **Sec. 3(f)**: ***"Manage or supervise the landside operation of ports and airports in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, except those in economic zones"*** — includes inland ports and boat landings.[^22]
- **Sec. 3(m)**: ***"Impose duties and fees on vessels registered with the Ministry and on wharves constructed and maintained by the Bangsamoro Government"*** — revenue authority for inland waterway vessels.[^23]
- **Sec. 3(n)**: ***"Ensure, with the National Government, the free movement of vessels, goods, and people in Zones of Joint Cooperation and the interconnectivity of the islands and mainland areas comprising the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** — connectivity mandate.[^24]

**Organizational structure** (BAA 13, Title XV, Sec. 4): MTC has 8 sectoral offices, of which the following are relevant to inland waterway navigation:

- **Bangsamoro Maritime Industry Authority (BMARINA)** — Sec. 25: issues Certificates of Public Convenience, Special Permits, registers vessels, conducts safety inspections, issues Bay and River Licenses, and determines fares for maritime transportation within BARMM.[^25]
- **Bangsamoro Ports Management Authority (BPMA)** — Sec. 29-33: manages all public ports in BARMM, prescribes rules for port construction and operation, provides berthing and cargo-handling services.[^26]
- **Water Transportation Services** — Sec. 11: headed by a Director II, coordinates the programs, projects, activities, strategic communication, and capacity-building of BMARINA and BPMA.[^27]

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13 grants MTC general navigation management authority but lacks:

- **Comprehensive inland waterway classification system** — no framework distinguishes navigable from non-navigable waterways, or classifies waterways by vessel type and tonnage capacity
- **Safety and engineering standards** — no regional vessel safety standards specific to inland waterway conditions (shallow draft requirements, flood-season protocols, night navigation rules)
- **Inland port and boat landing regulatory framework** — BPMA manages major ports but no code governs the dozens of informal boat landings along rivers and marshes
- **Environmental-navigation integration** — no mechanism for coordinating navigation permits with environmental management of the same water body
- **Penalty provisions** — BAA 13 assigns functions but does not prescribe penalties for violations of navigation rules on inland waterways
- **PCG coordination protocol** — no formalized agreement or protocol implements the Art. XIII, Sec. 22 coordination requirement

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**
A. Yes. **Resolution No. 251** directly addresses this power — urging MTC to conduct a feasibility study on establishing an **Inland Waterway Transport System (IWTS)** for the Ligawasan Marsh and other waterways within Maguindanao Province. **Resolution No. 465** signals broader maritime transport investment priorities for island provinces.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**
A. Yes. MTC's BAA 13 mandate expressly includes ***"Manage navigation in inland waterways"*** (Sec. 3(e)). The institutional alignment is precise — unlike some powers where the assigned MOA's mandate only partially covers the power's scope. However, the scope of power (hh) also overlaps with:

- **MENRE** — environmental management of the same water bodies (power (gg))
- **MPW** — infrastructure construction including boat landings and river improvements
- **PCG** — safety enforcement on navigable waters

MTC is correctly the **lead coordinator**, but a standalone BAA would need to define inter-ministry roles to prevent jurisdictional gaps.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**
A. The following institutions exercise functions related to inland waterway navigation:

- **Lead ministry**: MTC — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XV; BAA 85, Chapter VII. Operational.
- **BMARINA** — BAA 13, Title XV, Sec. 25. Exercises vessel registration, franchising, safety inspection, and fare-setting for maritime transport within BARMM. Operational — issues CPCs, SPs, and Bay and River Licenses.
- **BPMA** — BAA 13, Title XV, Sec. 29-33. Manages public ports. Operational — manages 12 major ports under BPMA administration [UNVERIFIED — exact count per BDP Table 11.1].
- **Water Transportation Services** — BAA 13, Title XV, Sec. 11. Coordinates BMARINA and BPMA activities. Operational.
- **PCG — BARMM Regional Office** — per BOL Art. XI, Sec. 7. National agency under national PCG control. Exercises maritime safety enforcement and law enforcement in BARMM navigable waters.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates the Parliament to ***"create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water"*** — this has not been created. While this mandate is primarily under power (gg), such an authority would logically house navigation regulatory functions for specific inland waterways (e.g., a Ligawasan Marsh Authority, a Lake Lanao Management Authority).

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**
A. Yes. Fully exercising power (hh) would benefit from:

- **Inland Waterway Navigation Bureau** (within MTC or as a stand-alone body) — to manage navigation channels, designate navigable routes, set vessel standards for shallow-draft inland conditions, and coordinate with the PCG
- **BARMM-PCG Coordination Committee** — a formalized intergovernmental body to implement the Art. XIII, Sec. 22 coordination requirement, rather than relying on ad hoc coordination
- **Ligawasan Marsh Transport Authority** — as envisioned by Resolution 251 and the BDP Mindanao River Basin Masterplan — to manage the specific water transport system for the marsh area

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**
A. The national government exercises authority through two agencies:

1. **PCG — BARMM Regional Office** — conducts vessel safety inspections, maritime law enforcement, search and rescue, and navigational safety in all BARMM navigable waters including inland waterways. The PCG sets safety standards, enforces compliance, and investigates maritime incidents.

2. **MARINA** — national office retains standards-setting for seafarer certification (STCW Convention compliance) and vessel construction standards. BMARINA exercises devolved registration and franchising functions but refers to national MARINA for international-standard compliance.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**
A. PCG's operational services (vessel inspections, search and rescue, maritime safety enforcement) are the primary national programs. MARINA's seafarer certification and vessel standards programs apply to BARMM operators.[UNVERIFIED — specific PCG-BARMM operational data not available in local sources]

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**
A. Devolution is **partial**:

- **MTC/BMARINA** exercises vessel registration, franchising, and maritime regulation for vessels operating exclusively within BARMM — this is functional devolution under BAA 13.
- **PCG** retains full operational control of its BARMM regional office — no devolution. The BOL expressly keeps the PCG under ***"the direct operational and administrative control and supervision of the Philippine Coast Guard"*** (Art. XI, Sec. 7).
- **Navigation management** — BAA 13 Sec. 3(e) assigns MTC the function to manage navigation in inland waterways, but the PCG coordination requirement means MTC cannot act unilaterally on navigation safety.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**
A. Yes. The primary overlap is between **MTC (navigation management)** and **PCG (navigation safety enforcement)** on the same inland waterways. MTC manages routes, franchising, and vessel registration; PCG enforces safety standards and conducts inspections. Without a formalized coordination protocol, this dual presence creates potential for conflicting directions, duplicative inspections, or enforcement gaps.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**
A. BARMM has:

- Established MTC with an express inland waterway navigation mandate (BAA 13, Sec. 3(e))
- Created BMARINA as a functional sectoral office issuing CPCs, Special Permits, Bay and River Licenses, and conducting safety inspections for maritime vessels within BARMM
- Created BPMA managing public ports including those that serve inland waterway and coastal transport
- Adopted Resolution 251 signaling intent to develop the Ligawasan Marsh Inland Waterway Transport System
- Adopted Resolution 465 urging maritime transport investment in island provinces
- No standalone BAA or comprehensive inland waterway regulation has been enacted

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**
A. BARMM has two potential advantages:

- **Conflict-sensitive design** — BARMM can design inland waterway transport systems that account for post-conflict realities, IDP populations, and communities that rely on water transport because road infrastructure was destroyed or never built
- **Local knowledge** — the Ligawasan Marsh, Lake Lanao, Pulangi River, and Rio Grande de Mindanao waterway systems serve communities with unique transport patterns that national agencies have not prioritized. BARMM can design navigation services tailored to local conditions (shallow-draft *banca* operations, flood-season routing, market-day schedules)

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**
A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a standalone BAA providing a comprehensive inland waterway navigation framework. BAA 13 assigns the function to MTC but does not provide the regulatory tools — waterway classification, vessel standards, penalty provisions, environmental integration, or a formalized PCG coordination protocol. Without these, MTC's navigation management authority is limited to general administrative oversight. The **Resolution 251 feasibility study** has not yet produced a legislative proposal.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**
A. The national framework provides:

- **Legislative depth** — RA 9993 (PCG Law) is a comprehensive maritime safety statute with enforcement mechanisms, penalty provisions, and institutional structure. RA 10635 (MARINA) provides the maritime administration framework. RA 2056 protects navigable waterways from obstruction. BARMM has no equivalent comprehensive inland waterway legislation.
- **Institutional infrastructure** — the PCG has an established nationwide presence with specialized maritime safety expertise, search-and-rescue capability, and vessel inspection protocols. MARINA has seafarer certification systems and vessel construction standards.
- **Regulatory systems** — vessel registration databases, safety inspection regimes, and navigational aid placement are national systems that BARMM's BMARINA must interface with.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**
A. The national system has not invested in **inland waterway transport infrastructure** specific to BARMM's needs:

- No national program has developed the Ligawasan Marsh as a navigable transport corridor despite its centrality to Maguindanao communities
- PCG resources are concentrated on coastal and sea operations — inland waterway safety enforcement on rivers and marshes is underresourced
- National MARINA standards are designed for seagoing vessels — they do not adequately address the small *banca* operations that dominate BARMM's inland waterways
- No intermodal transport connectivity framework links inland waterway transport to road networks in BARMM

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**
A. For inland waterway navigation:

- **Replicate** — PCG safety inspection protocols and search-and-rescue procedures. These are effective and should be maintained through coordination.
- **Adapt** — MARINA's vessel registration and franchising framework for inland waterway conditions. BMARINA already does this but needs a BAA to codify standards for shallow-draft vessels, *banca* operators, and river transport.
- **Replace** — the absence of an inland waterway transport development program. The national system has no equivalent of what the BDP envisions for the Ligawasan Marsh. BARMM must build this from scratch through standalone legislation.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**
A. No specific Shari'ah principles directly govern inland waterway navigation. However, the general Islamic values of ***maslahah*** (public welfare), ***'adl*** (justice/equity), and ***amanah*** (trust/stewardship) apply. Inland waterway transport serves *maslahah* by connecting isolated communities; it addresses *'adl* by providing equitable transport access to underserved populations; and navigation management carries an *amanah* obligation to preserve waterways while enabling their transport use.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**
A. The BOL is silent on Shari'ah in relation to inland waterway navigation. Art. X, Sec. 4 — which empowers Parliament to enact Shari'ah laws on personal, family, property, commercial, and criminal matters — does not directly extend to navigation regulation.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**
A. No specific Shari'ah principles beyond the general values of *maslahah*, *'adl*, and *amanah* directly govern this power area. Where navigation fees or transport charges are imposed, they should avoid exploitative pricing (*ghaban fahish*) that would burden poor communities dependent on water transport.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**
A. None. PD 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) does not address navigation or transport.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**
A. Shari'ah compliance is not a primary concern for navigation legislation. The principled approach is sufficient — legislation guided by Islamic values of public welfare and equity without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance mechanisms. Any inland waterway authority should ensure that transport fees do not impose undue burden on the poor, consistent with *maslahah*.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**
A. BARMM has **broad divergence capacity**:

- **Strongest basis for divergence**: the BOL does not require national law consistency for this power. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 requires only ***"coordination and cooperation"*** with the PCG — not consistency with PCG standards or national maritime law. BARMM can establish its own navigation rules, waterway classification system, vessel standards, and inland port regulations.
- **Constraint from Section A (BOL Grant and Scope)**: the mandatory PCG coordination requirement means BARMM cannot diverge on **safety enforcement** — the PCG retains operational authority over maritime safety in all navigable waters.
- **Constitutional floor from Section B (Constitutional Considerations)**: inland waters are State-owned under Art. XII, Sec. 2 — BARMM manages but does not own these waterways. Navigation legislation must respect this ownership framework.
- **Shari'ah from Section E (Shari'ah Considerations)**: no specific Shari'ah basis for divergence on navigation; general Islamic values support but do not compel distinct navigation standards.

In practice, BARMM can create an **entirely distinct inland waterway navigation regulatory framework** — waterway classification, vessel standards, route designation, franchising criteria, inland port management — so long as it maintains PCG coordination for safety enforcement.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**
A. The primary concern is vessels and operators that navigate waterways crossing BARMM boundaries (e.g., the Rio Grande de Mindanao system, which extends into non-BARMM areas). A BARMM inland waterway code would need **mutual recognition provisions** for vessel registrations and operator licenses issued by national agencies, and vice versa.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**
A. The BAA would govern navigation on inland waterways within BARMM. National laws (RA 2056, etc.) would apply **suppletorily** where the BAA is silent. RA 9993 (PCG Law) would continue to apply directly for safety enforcement functions, as the PCG operates under national control per BOL Art. XI, Sec. 7.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**
A. The primary jurisdictional ambiguity is between **MTC's navigation management** function and **PCG's safety enforcement** function on the same inland waterways. BAA 13 assigns MTC the power to ***"Manage navigation in inland waterways"*** but does not define the boundary between navigation management (BARMM) and navigation safety enforcement (PCG). Questions include:

- Who designates navigable routes — MTC or PCG?
- Who sets vessel safety standards for inland waterway operations — BMARINA or PCG?
- Who investigates navigation incidents on inland waterways — MTC or PCG?
- How are conflicting regulatory requirements reconciled?

A formalized **MTC-PCG Coordination Protocol** (either by BAA, executive order, or MOA) is needed to operationalize the Art. XIII, Sec. 22 requirement.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**
A. No pending Parliament Bill directly addresses inland waterway navigation. **PB 211** (Bureau of ICT under MTC) restructures MTC but does not create navigation-specific institutional capacity. **Resolution 251** signals feasibility study intent for the Ligawasan Marsh IWTS but has not produced legislation. The legislative gap remains fully open.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

- **Water transport** as one of the common means of transportation in BARMM, documenting 17 major ports and the need for expanded water transport infrastructure
- The **Mindanao River Basin Integrated Management and Development Masterplan** includes a specific programme component: ***"Water transport system including boat landings in the strategic locations around the marsh and along the Pulangi river"*** — calling for water transport route improvement, boat landing construction, and intermodal connectivity
- The **Ligawasan Marsh Water Transport System Development Programme** — a three-part programme: (i) water transport route improvement, (ii) boat landing construction, (iii) water transport safety improvement

**BDP Chapter 3** (Spatial Development Strategy) addresses connectivity of island and mainland BARMM areas through water transport corridors.

The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** Pillar 3 (Economic Prosperity) depends on transport infrastructure connectivity — inland waterway transport is critical for communities in Maguindanao, Lanao del Sur, and island provinces where road networks are insufficient.

- The **Ligawasan Marsh IWTS** envisioned by Resolution 251 and the BDP cannot be established — no legal framework exists for designating navigation routes, constructing boat landings, licensing operators, or setting safety standards for marsh transport
- **Pulangi River and Rio Grande de Mindanao** transport corridors cannot be developed as formal intermodal transport links — the absence of waterway classification prevents route designation
- **Community access** — isolated communities in the Ligawasan Marsh [UNVERIFIED — approximately 400,000 residents in surrounding municipalities] who depend on water transport lack regulated, safe, and reliable navigation services
- **MTC-PCG coordination** remains informal — risk of enforcement gaps or duplicative requirements that burden small operators
- **Investment** in inland waterway infrastructure (boat landings, navigation aids, channel maintenance) lacks a regulatory foundation to attract public and private funding

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the relationship between power (hh) — inland waterways for navigation — and power (gg) — inland waters?**
A. The two powers cover the **same geographic space** but address **different functions**. Power (gg) covers the **preservation, conservation, management, and protection** of inland waters as natural resources — the ecological and resource dimension. Power (hh) covers the **navigation and transport use** of the same waters — the transportation dimension. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 elaborates both powers in a single provision, with the first three paragraphs addressing (gg) and the last paragraph addressing (hh). Legislation for either power must account for the other — navigation infrastructure affects water quality, and water management rules affect navigability.

**Q. How does the energy exception in Art. XIII, Sec. 22 affect navigation authority?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 excludes inland waters ***"that provide energy to power generating plants"*** from Bangsamoro authority. This primarily affects **Lake Lanao**, which feeds the Agus Hydropower Complex. Navigation on Lake Lanao is subject to the co-management principle (Art. XIII, Sec. 10 and Art. XII, Sec. 34) rather than unilateral Bangsamoro authority. This means BARMM cannot regulate navigation on Lake Lanao without coordination with the national government — beyond the PCG coordination already required for all inland waterways.

**Q. What specific inland waterways in BARMM are candidates for navigation development?**
A. Based on the BDP and Resolution 251:

- **Ligawasan Marsh** — identified by both the BDP Mindanao River Basin Masterplan and Resolution 251 as the priority inland waterway transport corridor. Communities around the marsh lack road access during flood seasons and depend on water transport.
- **Pulangi River** — the BDP Masterplan specifically identifies ***"boat landings in the strategic locations around the marsh and along the Pulangi river"*** as part of the water transport programme.
- **Rio Grande de Mindanao** — the major river system through Maguindanao; historically used for transport and commerce.
- **Lake Lanao** — subject to the energy exception and co-management requirement, but used for local transport around the lake's littoral communities. [UNVERIFIED — current navigation use and volume data not available in local sources]

**Q. What vessel types operate on BARMM inland waterways?**
A. The dominant vessel type is the **motorized *banca*** — small, shallow-draft boats used for passenger and cargo transport on rivers, marshes, and lakes. BMARINA has authority to accredit *banca* operators (BAA 13, Sec. 25(d)) and issue Motorboat Operator's Licenses for vessels of 3 GRT and below (Sec. 25(h)). A standalone BAA should establish vessel standards specific to these operations — hull integrity requirements, engine safety, passenger capacity limits, and personal flotation device requirements — distinct from the seagoing vessel standards that national MARINA prescribes.

**Q. What intermodal connectivity is needed for inland waterway transport?**
A. The BDP Masterplan identifies the critical need: ***"All boat landings shall be connected to the trunk road to realize smooth flow of people and goods (intermodal transport) from origin (community) to final destination (e.g., market at the municipality)."*** Inland waterway navigation is only effective when boat landings connect to road networks. This requires coordination between MTC (navigation and port management), MPW (road construction), and LGUs (local road networks). A standalone BAA should include intermodal planning provisions requiring MTC-MPW coordination for each designated navigation route.
