# (r) Development Programs and Laws for Women, Labor, the Youth, the Elderly, the Differently-Abled, and Indigenous Peoples

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(r)** — "Development programs and laws for women, labor, the youth, the elderly, the differently-abled, and indigenous peoples;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

Article V, Section 2(r) grants the Bangsamoro Government authority to enact development programs and laws for six specific population groups: women, labor, the youth, the elderly, the differently-abled, and indigenous peoples. This is an umbrella power that cuts across multiple BOL provisions. For women, Article IX, Sections 11-12 mandate at least one woman in the Bangsamoro Cabinet, require the Parliament to create a commission on women, and direct the protection of women's fundamental rights including under the CEDAW. Article XIII, Section 5 establishes the 5% gender and development (GAD) budget requirement and mandates consultation mechanisms with women.[^1]

For youth, Article IX, Section 13 directs the Parliament to create a commission on youth and recognizes the vital role of the youth in nation-building. For children, Article IX, Section 14 mandates protection of children's rights including orphans, requiring policies that promote survival and development with the "best interest of children" standard. For indigenous peoples, Article IX, Section 3 enumerates eight protected rights and mandates the creation of a ministry for indigenous peoples. Article IX, Section 23 requires the establishment of a special agency, support facilities, and livelihood or skills training for persons with special needs, senior citizens, and other disadvantaged groups.[^2]

The BOL also requires Parliament to enact specific laws in several of these areas: a law for the commission on women (Art. IX, Sec. 11), a law for the commission on youth (Art. IX, Sec. 13), laws for the implementation of women's protection (Art. IX, Sec. 12), and an office for settler communities (Art. IX, Sec. 15). This power thus carries some of the heaviest legislative mandates in the BOL, requiring the Parliament to build an entire social protection legislative framework.[^3]

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Bangsamoro Government has enacted two dedicated commissions under this power. BAA No. 8 created the Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC), which serves as the policy-making and coordinating body for gender and development, implementing the 5% GAD budget mandate and the consultation mechanisms required by Article XIII, Section 5. BAA No. 10 created the Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC), fulfilling the Article IX, Section 13 mandate. Both commissions have dedicated chapters in BAA No. 85 (FY 2026 GAA) with annual appropriations and staffing.[^4]

For indigenous peoples, BAA No. 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act) provides a comprehensive legislative framework, and the Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIAP) operates under BAA 13 with a dedicated chapter in BAA 85. For labor, MOLE operates under BAA 13, and BAA No. 82 (Bangsamoro Labor and Employment Code), enacted in January 2026, now provides a comprehensive labor code for the region. BAA No. 72 (Bereavement Leave Act) addresses one specific labor welfare area. For overseas workers, BAA No. 9 regulates recruitment agencies and BAA No. 19 provides protections for overseas Bangsamoro workers.[^5]

However, no standalone legislation exists specifically for the elderly (senior citizens) or the differently-abled within the Bangsamoro. The special agency mandated by Article IX, Section 23 for persons with special needs, senior citizens, and disadvantaged groups has not been established through standalone legislation. The Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD) exercises general responsibility for these populations through its BAA 13 mandate.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

This power does not carry a blanket national law consistency requirement, but several component areas do. Labor programs must be "in accordance with the Constitution and the Labor Code of the Philippines" per Article IX, Section 10, though the Parliament may pass labor laws that expand or enhance these rights — which it has done through BAA 82. Indigenous peoples' programs must not diminish rights under RA 8371 (IPRA) per Article IX, Section 3. No specific national law consistency is required for women, youth, elderly, or disability programs, but several national laws apply suppletorily: RA 7277 (Magna Carta for Disabled Persons), RA 9994 (Expanded Senior Citizens Act), RA 10354 (Responsible Parenthood and Reproductive Health Act), RA 9262 (Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act), RA 9231 (Anti-Child Labor), and RA 9344 (Juvenile Justice and Welfare Act).[^6]

No intergovernmental coordination body exists specifically for this power, though the general Intergovernmental Relations Body under Article VI, Section 2 could address overlapping mandates. The Philippine Commission on Women, Council for the Welfare of Children, National Youth Commission, and NCIP continue to operate in BARMM alongside their Bangsamoro counterparts.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The most significant gaps involve the elderly and persons with disabilities. Article IX, Section 23 mandates a "special agency, support facilities and livelihood or skills training for persons with special needs, and other disadvantaged persons." No standalone BAA establishes this agency. A Bangsamoro Persons with Disabilities Act could create a Bangsamoro Disability Affairs Office (as proposed in PB No. 250), establish standards for accessible public facilities, provide for livelihood and skills training programs, and mandate inclusion in government employment. Similarly, a Bangsamoro Senior Citizens Act could establish a regional framework for elderly welfare, expanding upon the national RA 9994 framework with Bangsamoro-specific provisions for senior citizens' welfare, pension supplements, and healthcare access.[^7]

For women, while BWC exists under BAA 8, there is no comprehensive Bangsamoro Women's Development Code that consolidates gender mainstreaming requirements, anti-discrimination measures, protection from violence, and economic empowerment into a single legislative framework. Several proposed bills address fragments of this — PB No. 301 (haven for women victims of gender-based violence), PB No. 292 (economic empowerment for former women combatants), PB No. 382 (women's welfare centers) — but no overarching code exists.

For children, the BOL mandate in Article IX, Section 14 for comprehensive children's rights protection has not been fulfilled through standalone legislation. A Bangsamoro Children's Code could consolidate child protection, education access, anti-child labor enforcement, and juvenile justice provisions specific to the Bangsamoro context, including protection of children affected by armed conflict (as proposed in PB No. 406).

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 9 (Social Services), targets comprehensive social protection systems covering all vulnerable populations — women, youth, elderly, persons with disabilities, and indigenous peoples. These targets depend on the legislative framework that this power enables. Without standalone legislation for the elderly and persons with disabilities, BDP goals for inclusive social protection remain aspirational rather than enforceable. The absence of a comprehensive women's code limits the ability to systematize the 5% GAD budget into measurable development outcomes.

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type A**: No standalone legislation | Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandates a special agency for persons with special needs, senior citizens, and disadvantaged groups. No BAA establishes this agency. MSSD exercises general mandate through BAA 13 | Enact a Bangsamoro Persons with Disabilities and Senior Citizens Act establishing the mandated special agency with dedicated programs and standards |
| **Type A**: No standalone legislation | No comprehensive Bangsamoro Women's Development Code exists. BAA 8 creates BWC but does not consolidate anti-discrimination, protection from violence, economic empowerment, and GAD implementation into a single framework | Draft a Bangsamoro Women's Development Code integrating CEDAW obligations, GAD budget enforcement, and women's economic empowerment |
| **Type A**: No standalone legislation | Art. IX, Sec. 14 mandates comprehensive children's rights protection.
