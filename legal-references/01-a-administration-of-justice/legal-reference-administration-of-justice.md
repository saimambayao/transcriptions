# (a) Administration of Justice

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(a)** --- ***"Administration of justice;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(a) --- enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes --- Art. X, Sec. 1 (***"in consonance with the Constitution"***); Art. ## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis in the style of Philippine bar review materials (Bernas, Golden Notes). All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over administration of justice?**

A. Article V, Section 2(a) enumerates ***"Administration of justice"*** as the first of the 55 powers. This encompasses a **multi-layered justice system**: (1) the **Shari'ah court system** (Art. X, Sec. 2-16), (2) **traditional or tribal justice systems** (Art. X, Sec. 17-18), and (3) **alternative dispute resolution** (Art. X, Sec. 20). The entire Article X --- 20 sections --- elaborates this power.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power?**

A. Article X contains 20 sections organized into four clusters:

   (1) **Framework** (Sec. 1-4): multi-source justice system, Shari'ah courts integrated into national judiciary under Supreme Court supervision, four sources of Shari'ah codified, Parliament's legislative authority over Shari'ah personal/family/property/commercial/minor criminal matters.[^2][^3]

   (2) **Court Structure** (Sec. 5-15): three-tier Shari'ah court system --- Circuit Courts, District Courts, Shari'ah High Court --- with defined jurisdiction, qualifications, compensation parity, Shari'ah Rules of Court, special bar examinations, and the Shari'ah Integrated Bar.[^4]

   (3) **Supporting Institutions** (Sec. 16-18): permissive authority for 4 supporting institutions (prosecution service, public assistance office, academy, Jurisconsult); mandatory mandate for traditional/tribal justice laws; direct creation of the Office for Tribal Justice System.[^5][^6]

   (4) **Regular Courts and ADR** (Sec. 19-20): regular courts continue under national law; Parliament must institutionalize ADR.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate specific legislation?**

A. Yes. Two **mandatory** mandates: (1) Art. X, Sec. 17: ***"The Parliament shall enact laws to promote and support the traditional or tribal justice systems"***; (2) Art. X, Sec. 20: Parliament must enact ADR legislation. One **permissive** authority: Art. X, Sec. 16 (4 supporting institutions). Neither mandatory mandate has been fulfilled as of BAA 89.[^5][^6][^7]

**Q. What bodies does the BOL create?**

A. (1) **Shari'ah High Court** (Art. X, Sec. 7) --- not yet fully organized; (2) **Office for Tribal Justice System** (Art. X, Sec. 18) --- established under BAA 13; (3) **BAGO** (BAA 5 per Art. XVI, Sec. 8); (4) **BTJRC** (BAA 89).

**Q. What limitations does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Five constraints: (1) constitutional consonance (Art. X, Sec. 1); (2) Supreme Court supervision of Shari'ah courts (Art. X, Sec. 2); (3) Congress creates courts (Art. X, Sec. 2); (4) criminal jurisdiction ceiling --- *arresto menor*/*ta'zir* only (Art. X, Sec. 4); (5) regular courts untouched (Art. X, Sec. 19).[^2][^3][^7][^8]

### B. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes --- the **most significant Shari'ah dimension** of any BOL power. The entire Shari'ah court system is a Shari'ah institution, and Parliament's Art. X, Sec. 4 legislative authority is the broadest Shari'ah law-making power in Philippine history.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant?**

A. (1) ***'Adl*** (justice/equity); (2) ***Sulh*** (mediation/settlement) --- Islamic basis for ADR; (3) ***Ta'zir*** (discretionary punishment) --- the only Shari'ah criminal penalty authorized; (4) ***Shura*** (consultation) --- the legislative process for Shari'ah laws should involve *ulama* and the *Darul Ifta'*.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) is the primary substantive Shari'ah legislation. Parliament may legislate on matters **not provided for** under PD 1083 --- principally commercial law and minor criminal offenses.[^9]

### C. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply?**

A. (1) **Art. X, Sec. 20**: autonomous region constitutional framework; (2) **Art. VIII** (Judicial Power): Supreme Court has plenary supervisory authority over all courts; (3) **Art. III, Sec. 1**: due process and equal protection apply to all justice proceedings; (4) **Art. III, Sec. 12, 14, 16**: right to counsel, presumption of innocence, speedy trial.[^10][^11][^12]

**Q. What constitutional mandates for justice institutions exist?**

A. Art. VIII, Sec. 5(5) grants the Supreme Court the power to promulgate rules of procedure --- the Supreme Court governs procedure in Shari'ah courts; Parliament legislates substantive Shari'ah law.

### D. National Law Considerations

**Q. What national laws apply in BARMM?**

A. All apply **directly**: (1) **PD 1083**; (2) **BP 129** (court organization); (3) **RA 12018** (additional Shari'ah courts); (4) **RA 9285** (ADR --- suppletory); (5) **RA 7160** (*Katarungang Pambarangay*).[^9][^13]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority?**

A. **Supreme Court** (plenary supervisory authority over all courts); **DOJ** prosecution service (Shari'ah criminal cases pending prosecution service creation); **JBC** (judicial appointments). These retain authority **permanently**.

### E. Implementation

#### E.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which office is primarily responsible?**

A. **BAGO** --- BAA 5, BAA 13 Book IV, Title II, Ch. 2 (Sec. 10-24).[^14][^15]

**Q. What are BAGO's functions under BAA 13?**

A. **Functions** (Sec. 13): (a) chief legal counsel to the Bangsamoro Government; (b) review/vet/interpret documents and contracts for the Chief Minister; (c) recommend actions on administrative complaints; (d) assist the Intergovernmental Relations Body; (e) other functions assigned by law.[^16]

   **Powers** (Sec. 14): require information from officers; summon officers; administer oaths; propose organizational changes; supervise policy formulation; appear in Parliament committees.[^17]

   **Divisions** (Sec. 17): (a) **Litigation Division** --- represent government in court, initiate contract enforcement, deputize legal officers; (b) **LROD** --- review executive issuances, draft contracts, ***"formulate and review policies on Shari'ah matters to ensure compliance with Shari'ah rules and principles"***; (c) **IRD**; (d) **AFD**.[^18]

   **Additional**: Legal Liaison Network (Sec. 18); all ministries notify BAGO of litigation within 3 days (Sec. 22); BAGO settles disputes involving government corporations (Book V, Sec. 29); protection from personal liability (Sec. 24).[^19]

#### E.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government exercise authority?**

A. **Supreme Court** supervises all courts, appoints judges, promulgates Rules of Court, administers bar exams. **DOJ** prosecution service handles Shari'ah criminal cases. **Congress** creates additional Shari'ah courts (exercised through RA 12018). This power is **not devolved** in the traditional sense --- the judiciary is constitutionally independent.

#### E.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has BARMM accomplished?**

A. (1) BAGO fully operationalized; (2) BAA 89 (Transitional Justice) enacted; (3) Office for Tribal Justice System established; (4) Shari'ah Public Assistance Office established; (5) Multiple resolutions urging Shari'ah court expansion and judge appointments; (6) Support Fund to Judiciary institutionalized.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back?**

A. (1) Shari'ah High Court not yet fully organized; (2) no Shari'ah substantive legislation enacted under Art. X, Sec. 4; (3) no traditional/tribal justice law or ADR law despite mandatory mandates.

#### E.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. (1) Insufficient Shari'ah courts despite RA 12018; (2) no Shari'ah legislation beyond PD 1083 (1977); (3) no recognition of tribal justice at the national level.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace?**

A. **Replicate**: procedural due process standards, bar examination rigor, judicial compensation parity. **Adapt**: *Katarungang Pambarangay* to integrate tribal dispute resolution and *sulh*. **Replace**: substantive law gap by enacting Shari'ah commercial/civil legislation and a Bangsamoro ADR framework integrating *sulh*.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can BARMM diverge?**

A. **Broad but structurally constrained**. Parliament has express authority to enact Shari'ah substantive laws (Art. X, Sec. 4). Structural constraints from **Theme A**: Supreme Court supervision; Congress creates courts; criminal jurisdiction capped at *arresto menor*; regular courts under national law. Constitutional floor from **Theme C**: due process, equal protection, Art. VIII judicial power.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping jurisdictions?**

A. (1) Shari'ah court administration (Supreme Court supervises; Parliament legislates; BAGO provides services); (2) prosecution (DOJ handles Shari'ah criminal cases pending prosecution service).

**Q. What pending bills address gaps?**

A. PB Nos. 170, 173, 174 (barangay justice); PB 304 (Shari'ah scholarship); PB 363 (Shari'ah academy). No comprehensive traditional justice code or ADR act has been filed.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk?**

A. Without traditional/tribal justice legislation: IP dispute resolution lacks legal recognition. Without ADR legislation: Art. X, Sec. 20 remains unfulfilled. Without Shari'ah legislation under Art. X, Sec. 4: the broadest Shari'ah legislative authority in Philippine history goes unexercised. Without the Shari'ah prosecution service: DOJ handles cases it is not trained for.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the jurisdiction of each Shari'ah court level?**

A. (1) **Circuit Courts** (Sec. 5): PD 1083 offenses; family disputes; communal property; *ta'zir*; civil claims up to P200,000/real property up to P400,000. (2) **District Courts** (Sec. 6): custody/guardianship; estate/probate; Muslim Registry; Shari'ah contracts; civil claims above Circuit thresholds; appellate jurisdiction. (3) **High Court** (Sec. 7): auxiliary writs; annulment of District Court judgments; appellate jurisdiction; final except questions of law to SC.

**Q. Who appoints Shari'ah judges?**

A. The **Supreme Court** (Art. X, Sec. 14). Qualifications (Sec. 8): Philippine citizen, Muslim, Philippine Bar member, 2+ years Shari'ah/Islamic Jurisprudence. Circuit: 30 years, 5 years practice. District: 35 years, 10 years. High Court: natural-born citizen, 40 years, 15 years.

**Q. What is the scope of Parliament's *ta'zir* authority?**

A. Limited to ***"minor offenses punishable by arresto menor or ta'zir which must be equivalent to arresto menor or fines commensurate to the offense"*** --- 1-30 days imprisonment maximum. No *hudud* penalties are within Parliament's authority.[^8]

**Q. What is the Bangsamoro Support Fund to Judiciary (BAA 85, Ch. XXXIV)?**

A. Because Shari'ah courts are under Supreme Court supervision, the Bangsamoro Government cannot directly fund court operations. The Support Fund provides **supplemental financial support** without administrative encroachment on the Supreme Court's domain.
