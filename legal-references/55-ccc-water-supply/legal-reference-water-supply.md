# (ccc) Water Supply and Services, Flood Control, and Irrigation Systems

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ccc)** — ***"Water supply and services, flood control, and irrigation systems."***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(ccc) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national water supply law. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 37 establishes a **dual-authority model**: the National Government funds and implements construction and maintenance of water supply and services, flood control, and irrigation systems in BARMM; the Bangsamoro Government exercises coordination authority where systems connect to or from facilities outside BARMM. Art. V, Sec. 2 chapeau subjects all 55 powers to Art. X, Sec. 15 of the Constitution.[^1] |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Public Works (MPW)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XI, Sec. 1-9[^2] |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE) — watershed management; Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) — irrigation for agriculture; Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development) — Sectoral Objective 6.2: Equitable and sustainable access to potable water and irrigation systems ensured and vulnerability risk to disaster reduced[^3] |
| **Priority Code** | None. No standalone Bangsamoro Water Supply Code, Flood Control Act, or Irrigation Act has been enacted as of BAA 89. |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(ccc) enumerates ***"Water supply and services, flood control, and irrigation systems"*** as one of the 55 powers of the Bangsamoro Government.[^1] This is a composite power covering three interconnected infrastructure domains: (1) **water supply and services** — the provision, distribution, and management of potable water; (2) **flood control** — engineering structures and systems to manage water flow and prevent flooding; and (3) **irrigation systems** — water infrastructure for agricultural production. The scope encompasses the full water infrastructure cycle from source development through distribution to flood management and agricultural water delivery.

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 37 is the central elaborating provision. It establishes a **dual-authority model** unique among the 55 enumerated powers: the National Government ***"shall fund and implement the construction and maintenance of national roads, bridges, water supply and services, and flood control and irrigation systems"*** in BARMM.[^7] A proviso requires ***"cooperation and coordination between the Bangsamoro Government and the appropriate national or local government bodies"*** for systems ***"that connect to or from facilities outside the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^7] Art. XIII, Sec. 37, para. 2 creates a **budget mechanism** whereby the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall submit proposals to the appropriate national government agency for the inclusion of the cost of such maintenance"*** in the national GAA.[^8] Art. IX, Sec. 8 adds a **social justice dimension** by listing water supply as a basic service the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall provide, maintain, and ensure the delivery of"*** to the Bangsamoro people.[^9] Art. XIII, Sec. 22 grants ***"exclusive powers over inland waters"*** — the source water bodies from which supply systems draw.[^10] Art. XIII, Sec. 8 authorizes exploration and utilization of ***"surface and subsurface rights"*** for natural resources development, including groundwater.[^11]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**
A. There is no express mandate in the BOL stating "The Parliament shall enact" a water supply code, flood control act, or irrigation act. Unlike the (gg) inland waters power — where Art. XIII, Sec. 22 expressly states the Parliament ***"shall enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection"*** — the water supply power has no named legislative mandate. However, Art. V, Sec. 3's general welfare clause and the affirmative duty in Art. IX, Sec. 8 provide the constitutional and organic law basis for enacting comprehensive water supply legislation.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**
A. The BOL does not create or direct the creation of a specific water supply authority, water district framework, flood control agency, or irrigation authority. The institutional framework operates through the **Ministry of Public Works (MPW)** as established under BAA 13. For source water management, Art. XIII, Sec. 22 directs Parliament to ***"create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water"*** — which would overlap with the water supply power where those bodies of water serve as water sources.[^10]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**
A. Three significant qualifications: (1) **National Government implementation role** — Art. XIII, Sec. 37 assigns the National Government responsibility for funding and implementing construction and maintenance of water supply, flood control, and irrigation infrastructure, creating a dual-authority relationship rather than full Bangsamoro autonomy; (2) **cross-boundary coordination** — the proviso in Art. XIII, Sec. 37 requires cooperation where systems connect to or from facilities outside BARMM; (3) **budget pathway through national GAA** — Art. XIII, Sec. 37, para. 2 channels maintenance funding through the national appropriations process rather than directly to the Bangsamoro Government. These qualifications make this power one of the most constrained among the 55 enumerated powers — the Bangsamoro Government has the power but the National Government retains the implementation and funding mechanism for major infrastructure.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**
A. The **universal constraint** applies: Art. X, Sec. 15 of the Constitution provides that the creation of autonomous regions shall be ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***[^12] Subject-specific provisions include: **Art. II, Sec. 9** — the State shall promote a just and dynamic social order to ensure the prosperity and independence of the nation, which supports equitable water access; **Art. XII, Sec. 1** — national economy and patrimony provisions, as water resources are part of the national patrimony; **Art. XII, Sec. 2** — all natural resources are owned by the State, including water resources; **Art. XIII, Sec. 1** — Congress shall give highest priority to social justice measures, which includes access to basic services like water; **Art. XVI, Sec. 1** — consumer protection, relevant to water utility rate regulation.

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**
A. The Constitution establishes water access as part of the social justice framework. **Art. XIII, Sec. 1** directs Congress to ***"give highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity."*** Access to clean water is integral to human dignity. **Art. II, Sec. 15** provides that the State shall protect the right of the people to health — contaminated water supply directly threatens this right. **Art. III, Sec. 1** guarantees **equal protection** — water supply services must be equitably distributed regardless of ethnicity, religion, or geographic location within BARMM.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**
A. **Art. XII, Sec. 2** declares that all natural resources, including water, are owned by the State and their exploration, development, and utilization are under its full control and supervision. This means BARMM's authority over water resources — while broad under the BOL — operates within the State ownership framework. Any Bangsamoro water legislation cannot contravene the constitutional principle that water resources belong to the people. **Art. XVI, Sec. 1** mandates Congress to establish an independent economic and planning agency for the country, which has implications for water resource planning coordination between BARMM and national agencies.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**
A. The national framework is layered across multiple laws: **PD 1067** (Water Code) provides the comprehensive framework for water appropriation, utilization, and conservation — declaring all waters as property of the State. **PD 198** as amended by **RA 9286** (Provincial Water Utilities Act) creates the framework for **local water districts** as GOCCs and establishes **LWUA** as the regulatory-financing body. **RA 9275** (Clean Water Act) sets water quality standards and management frameworks. **RA 8041** (National Water Crisis Act) addresses water conservation and supply rationalization. **RA 6716** mandates construction of water wells and rainwater collectors in all barangays. For flood control, **RA 10121** (DRRM Act) integrates flood management into the national disaster risk framework. For irrigation, **RA 3601** (NIA Charter) governs national irrigation system development, and **RA 8435** (AFMA) provides the agricultural modernization framework including irrigation.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**
A. All the above national laws apply **suppletorily** in BARMM — they govern pending enactment of BAAs on the subject. No BAA has been enacted that expressly supersedes any of these national laws for the water supply, flood control, or irrigation domains. PD 1067 (Water Code) governs water permits and appropriation. PD 198 governs water district creation and operation — LWUA continues to exercise regulatory authority over any water districts in BARMM. RA 10121 applies for flood control within the DRRM framework, with Art. XIII, Sec. 7 of the BOL expressly requiring consistency. NIA operates irrigation systems in BARMM under RA 3601.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**
A. Multiple national agencies operate in BARMM: (1) **NWRB** — issues water permits for appropriation and use in BARMM; (2) **LWUA** — regulates and finances local water districts in BARMM provinces; (3) **DPWH** — implements nationally-funded water supply and flood control projects per Art. XIII, Sec. 37; (4) **NIA** — constructs and operates national irrigation systems; (5) **DENR** — manages watershed areas and groundwater resources. Authority transfer depends on specific BAA enactment — currently, these national agencies retain their full jurisdictions within BARMM for water supply, flood control, and irrigation functions.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**
A. The **Ministry of Public Works (MPW)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XI, Sec. 2 states the MPW's mandate: ***"The Ministry of Public Works shall be the Bangsamoro Government's engineering and construction arm, and is mandated to undertake (a) the planning, design and construction of infrastructures, such as, but not limited to roads and bridges, flood control, water supply projects and other public works facilities within the jurisdiction of the Bangsamoro Government; and (b) the construction, rehabilitation and maintenance of infrastructure facilities of the Autonomous Region."***[^2]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**
A. No standalone water supply code, flood control act, irrigation act, or water district act has been enacted as of BAA 89. The power is exercised exclusively through MPW's general BAA 13 mandate and annual appropriations acts (BAA 3, BAA 32, BAA 63, BAA 65) that allocate specific budgets for the Water Supply Program and flood control projects.

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XI, Sec. 1 declares the policy: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall maintain an engineering and construction arm and continuously develop its technology, for the purpose of ensuring the safety of all infrastructure facilities and securing for all public works the highest efficiency and the most appropriate quality in construction. The planning, design, construction and maintenance of infrastructure facilities, especially roads and bridges, flood control and water resources development within the jurisdiction of the Bangsamoro Government, shall be the responsibility of such an engineering and construction arm."***[^13]

Sec. 3 enumerates 11 powers and functions:[^14]
- (a) Planning, design, construction, and supervision for all infrastructure in BARMM
- (b) Maintenance and repairs of infrastructure and facilities
- (c) Ensuring property, plant, and equipment availability
- (d) Submission of proposals to national agencies for funding and technical assistance
- (e) Administration of implementation codes, standards, and guidelines for safety
- (f) Implementation of laws, policies, and programs for infrastructure and structures
- (g) Supervision of public works construction per approved plans and specifications
- (h) Technical assistance to agencies and LGUs
- (i) Coordination with national and regional agencies on infrastructure projects
- (j) Continuing consultations with local communities
- (k) Other functions assigned by the Chief Minister or provided by law

The organizational structure under Sec. 9 includes: (1) Administrative and Finance Services; (2) Technical Services (Planning and Programming Division, Survey and Design Division, Special Projects Division); (3) Operations and Infrastructure Services (Construction Division, Maintenance Division, **Quality Assurance and Hydrology Division**, Equipment Management Division); (4) Legal and Legislative Liaison Division; (5) Internal Audit Division; (6) Information Communication Division.[^15]

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13 creates MPW as the engineering and construction arm — it does **not** provide: (1) a comprehensive **water quality and distribution regulatory framework** — standards for potable water quality, distribution system requirements, water safety planning; (2) a **water district or water utility framework** — the legal basis for creating, regulating, and financing Bangsamoro water districts or water utilities as GOCCs; (3) **rate-setting authority** — regulatory powers to approve water tariffs and ensure affordability; (4) a **flood control master plan mandate** — authority to prepare binding flood control plans with development restrictions in flood-prone areas; (5) an **irrigation management framework** — devolution of NIA functions, creation of irrigators' associations, or communal irrigation system governance; (6) **penalty provisions** — sanctions for water supply contamination, illegal connections, or non-compliance with water quality standards; (7) **intergovernmental coordination protocols** — formal mechanisms for the Art. XIII, Sec. 37 cooperation requirement with national agencies; (8) **Shari'ah-compliant water governance principles** — stewardship and equitable access principles grounded in Islamic law.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**
A. No resolutions specifically directing the filing of a comprehensive water supply bill have been identified. However, Res. No. 122 (Lake Lanao rights) and Res. No. 509 (Mindanao River Basin Master Plan) signal Parliament's awareness of the critical water resource and flood control challenges facing BARMM.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**
A. **MPW is correctly assigned as the lead for the infrastructure delivery components** — construction of water supply systems, flood control structures, and irrigation infrastructure. However, the (ccc) power encompasses aspects beyond engineering and construction that fall outside MPW's mandate: (1) **water resource management** — source water protection, water allocation, and conservation fall under **MENRE** through its environment and natural resources mandate (BAA 13, Book VI, Title III); (2) **agricultural irrigation** — crop-specific irrigation planning and irrigators' association governance fall under **MAFAR** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title I); (3) **water quality regulation** — drinking water quality standards involve **MOH** (Ministry of Health) for public health dimensions. MPW is the primary coordinator for infrastructure delivery, but full exercise of this power requires a multi-ministry approach.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**
A.
- **Lead ministry**: Ministry of Public Works (MPW) — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XI; BAA 85
- **Supporting agencies**: MENRE — watershed management, water resource conservation (BAA 13, Book VI, Title III); MAFAR — agricultural irrigation coordination; BPDA — infrastructure planning and development coordination
- **Special bodies**: Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31) — policy coordination for sustainable water resource use
- **LGU-level**: Municipal/city engineering offices — local water supply and drainage maintenance per the Local Government Code

No Bangsamoro Water Authority, Water District Regulatory Body, Irrigation Authority, or Flood Control Management Office has been created.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates Parliament to ***"create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water"***[^10] — this has not been fulfilled. While this mandate relates directly to the (gg) inland waters power, such authorities would exercise management over the source water bodies from which water supply systems draw. A Lake Lanao Management Authority (proposed in PB 352) would be one such body.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**
A. Yes, three institutional gaps exist: (1) a **Bangsamoro Water Supply Regulatory Authority** or equivalent — to regulate water utilities, set tariffs, enforce water quality standards, and replace LWUA/NWRB functions within BARMM; (2) a **Bangsamoro Irrigation Authority** or irrigation management office — to manage communal and small-scale irrigation systems, replacing or coordinating with NIA functions; (3) a **Flood Control and Water Resources Management Office** — to prepare flood control master plans, enforce development restrictions in flood-prone areas, and coordinate with DPWH on nationally-funded flood control projects.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**
A. The national government's role in BARMM is unusually strong for this power due to Art. XIII, Sec. 37's dual-authority model. **DPWH** implements nationally-funded water supply and flood control projects through its regional engineering districts. **NIA** constructs and operates national irrigation systems, including the large-scale communal irrigation projects in Maguindanao and Lanao del Sur. **LWUA** regulates existing water districts and provides technical and financial assistance. **NWRB** issues water permits for appropriation of water resources. **DENR** manages watershed reservations that protect water supply sources.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**
A. Key programs include: (1) DPWH's **Flood Control and Drainage Program** — construction of flood control structures in flood-prone municipalities, particularly in Maguindanao; (2) DPWH's **Water Supply Program** — construction of Level II and Level III water supply systems; (3) NIA's **Communal Irrigation Development Program** — construction and rehabilitation of communal irrigation systems; (4) LWUA's **Water District Development Program** — technical assistance and loan financing for LWDs; (5) the **Mindanao River Basin Integrated Management and Development Masterplan** (MRBIMDMP) — a cross-regional flood control and development planning initiative involving DPWH, MinDA, and BARMM.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**
A. Devolution is **partial and fragmented**. MPW exercises independent authority over BARMM-funded water supply and flood control projects. However, nationally-funded infrastructure projects — which Art. XIII, Sec. 37 assigns to the National Government — continue to be implemented by DPWH and NIA. LWUA and NWRB retain their full regulatory and permitting functions within BARMM. No formal devolution agreement or executive order has been issued transferring DPWH, NIA, LWUA, or NWRB functions to the Bangsamoro Government for this power area.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**
A. Extensive overlap exists: (1) MPW and DPWH both implement water supply and flood control projects in BARMM — MPW using regional funds, DPWH using national appropriations per Art. XIII, Sec. 37; (2) MAFAR and NIA both work on irrigation — MAFAR coordinates agricultural water needs while NIA manages the physical irrigation systems; (3) MENRE and DENR exercise overlapping environmental management functions over watersheds. This overlap is **by design** — Art. XIII, Sec. 37 creates the dual-authority model — but the absence of formal coordination protocols means duplication and gaps coexist.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**
A. MPW has delivered measurable results: (1) **Water Supply Program** — annual appropriations ranging from Php 140-152 million for construction of Level II water supply systems across all BARMM provinces; (2) **Flood Control** — 159 flood control projects including drainage systems implemented across 8 districts and Cotabato City from 2019-2022; 97 flood control structures targeted for construction/rehabilitation in FY 2026; (3) **Rainwater Collection Systems** — BAA 3, Sec. 20 mandates installation of RWCS in public markets, school sites, and government buildings per MPW design; (4) **Water Access Target** — FY 2026 target of 65% household access to basic safe water supply (581,598 households).[^16]

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**
A. BARMM's advantages are **limited** in this power area compared to other enumerated powers. The dual-authority model means the National Government retains significant implementation responsibility. However, BARMM's unique advantages include: (1) **local responsiveness** — MPW can prioritize water supply for conflict-affected and geographically isolated communities that national agencies underserve; (2) **cultural appropriateness** — community engagement in local languages and culturally sensitive approaches to water infrastructure siting; (3) **Shari'ah-grounded governance** — potential to embed *amanah* (stewardship) principles in water utility management, which the national framework does not incorporate.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**
A. The **single biggest constraint is the absence of standalone legislation** — no Bangsamoro Water Supply Code, Water District Act, Flood Control Act, or Irrigation Act exists. Without standalone legislation, MPW cannot: (a) create and regulate Bangsamoro water districts; (b) set water tariff standards; (c) enforce drinking water quality standards; (d) prepare binding flood control master plans; (e) establish irrigation management frameworks. The second constraint is the **Art. XIII, Sec. 37 dual-authority model** itself — major infrastructure funding flows through the national GAA rather than directly to BARMM, creating dependency on national budget processes. The third constraint is **incomplete devolution** — LWUA, NWRB, and NIA retain full jurisdiction within BARMM.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**
A. The national framework is far more developed: (1) **LWUA** has been operational since 1973 (PD 198), regulating over 500 local water districts nationwide, with technical staff, financing programs, and a rate-setting methodology refined over five decades; (2) **NIA** has operated national irrigation systems since 1963 (RA 3601), with 245+ national irrigation systems and thousands of communal irrigation systems nationwide; (3) **NWRB** administers a comprehensive water permitting system for all water appropriation; (4) **DPWH** maintains a national flood control master plan with district engineering offices, design standards, and construction oversight capacity that MPW is still building; (5) the **Clean Water Act** (RA 9275) provides an enforcement framework with penalties, monitoring, and remediation requirements that BARMM lacks.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**
A. The national system is inadequate for BARMM in several areas: (1) **water access gap** — despite decades of DPWH and LWUA presence, BARMM's 74.6% potable water access rate is 18.3 percentage points below the national average (92.9%); (2) **flood control underinvestment** — the MRBIMDMP completed in 2014 has seen limited implementation of its recommended flood control projects, leaving Maguindanao municipalities perpetually flood-prone; (3) **no Shari'ah integration** — the national framework treats water supply purely as a technical-utility matter without integrating Islamic governance principles; (4) **conflict-sensitive gaps** — national systems do not adequately account for water infrastructure destroyed by armed conflict, displacement-related water access, or post-conflict reconstruction needs; (5) **language barriers** — national agency services and regulatory processes are conducted in English and Filipino, with limited accommodation for Maguindanaon, Tausug, Maranao, and other Bangsamoro languages.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**
A.
- **Replicate**: Water quality standards for potable water (RA 9275 / Philippine National Standards for Drinking Water) — adopt national standards as the minimum floor for Bangsamoro water supply systems.
- **Adapt**: Water district framework (PD 198) — create a Bangsamoro Water District Act modeled on PD 198 but with: Bangsamoro regulatory authority replacing LWUA, *amanah*-based governance requirements, conflict-sensitive siting provisions, and Bangsamoro civil service rules for water district employees.
- **Adapt**: Irrigation management framework (RA 3601/RA 8435) — develop a Bangsamoro irrigation code that integrates with MAFAR's agricultural program and accounts for small-scale communal systems suited to BARMM's agricultural patterns.
- **Replace**: Flood control governance — instead of relying solely on DPWH's project-by-project approach, establish a Bangsamoro flood control management framework with binding flood hazard zoning, development restrictions, and a regional flood control master plan for the Mindanao River Basin portions within BARMM.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**
A. Yes. Water is one of the most fundamental resources in Islamic law. The Shari'ah dimension is **indirect but substantive** — water supply is not governed by specific Islamic jurisprudence on *halal/haram* (as Islamic banking or marriage would be), but Islamic principles of stewardship, equity, and public welfare directly inform how the Bangsamoro Government should exercise this power.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**
A. The BOL does not specifically reference Shari'ah for water supply, flood control, or irrigation. However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to ***"enact laws governing property and commercial matters"***[^17] — which could extend to water as property and water utility operations as commercial matters. BAA 13, Book I, Sec. 17 defines public office as a ***"sacred public trust"*** and requires officers to ***"serve as 'khalifa'"*** — the stewardship concept applies directly to management of water resources and utilities.[^18]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**
A. Four principles are directly relevant: (1) ***hifz al-nafs*** (preservation of life) — access to clean water is a *daruriyyat* (necessity), the highest category of need in *Maqasid al-Shari'ah*; (2) water as **communal resource** — the Prophetic tradition that Muslims share water, pasture, and fire supports policies ensuring equitable access and preventing monopolization; (3) ***amanah*** (stewardship/trust) — government officials managing water supply bear sacred trust, supporting accountability and transparency requirements; (4) ***maslahah*** (public interest) — water infrastructure investment serves *maslahah 'ammah* (general public interest), justifying public expenditure and even eminent domain for water infrastructure.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**
A. A **principled approach** is most appropriate — legislation guided by Islamic values without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance for technical water operations. Specifically: (a) preamble language grounding water governance in *amanah* and *maslahah*; (b) governance requirements reflecting the *khalifa* concept — accountability, transparency, and service orientation for water utility managers; (c) equity provisions reflecting the communal resource principle — subsidized access for the poor, prohibition on water supply monopolization, community participation in water governance; (d) Shari'ah Legislative Review Board (SLRB) review of any water legislation to ensure consistency with Islamic principles.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**
A. BARMM's ability to diverge is **moderate but constrained by the dual-authority model**. Unlike most other enumerated powers where the BOL grants primary authority to the Bangsamoro Government, Art. XIII, Sec. 37 assigns the National Government an affirmative implementation role for water supply, flood control, and irrigation infrastructure. BARMM can: (1) enact its own water supply regulatory framework — establishing Bangsamoro water districts, water quality standards, and rate-setting authority; (2) create Bangsamoro-specific flood control governance with flood hazard zoning and development restrictions; (3) establish irrigation management frameworks adapted to BARMM agriculture. BARMM **cannot** unilaterally exclude national agencies from implementation — Art. XIII, Sec. 37 requires the dual-authority arrangement to continue. However, the Shari'ah dimension identified in Section E provides a unique basis for distinctiveness that no national framework offers — embedding *amanah*-based governance and equitable access principles in water legislation.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**
A. Water systems connecting to or from facilities outside BARMM require the Art. XIII, Sec. 37 proviso cooperation mechanism. If BARMM enacts different water quality standards, cross-boundary systems must comply with the higher standard at the point of interconnection. For water districts operating near BARMM boundaries (e.g., Cotabato City water systems serving surrounding municipalities), regulatory clarity on which framework applies is essential. The constitutional equal protection guarantee requires that any Bangsamoro water standards must not result in lower service quality than what national law provides.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**
A. The BAA governs within BARMM for matters covered by the legislation. National laws — PD 1067, PD 198, RA 9275 — apply suppletorily where the BAA is silent. However, the Art. XIII, Sec. 37 dual-authority model means that even with a BAA, the National Government retains its funding and implementation role for nationally-funded infrastructure. Constitutional provisions — State ownership of natural resources (Art. XII, Sec. 2), social justice (Art. XIII), equal protection (Art. III, Sec. 1) — always apply.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**
A. Three critical overlaps: (1) **MPW vs. DPWH** — both implement water supply and flood control projects in BARMM without a formal coordination protocol. Art. XIII, Sec. 37 assigns DPWH the nationally-funded projects and MPW the regionally-funded ones, but no intergovernmental agreement defines the demarcation, creating potential for duplication or gaps. (2) **MENRE vs. NWRB** — both exercise authority over water resources, but no BAA has superseded NWRB's water permitting jurisdiction within BARMM. (3) **MAFAR vs. NIA** — irrigation planning involves both MAFAR (agricultural productivity) and NIA (irrigation infrastructure), but NIA's operational presence in BARMM has not been formally coordinated with MAFAR's agricultural programs.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**
A. **PB 385** is the most comprehensive — it would establish a regional framework for water sustainability in BARMM. **PB 352** addresses the Lake Lanao source water management gap through a specific Lake Lanao Management Authority. **PB 348** addresses school water supply and sanitation. **PB 239** introduces solar-powered water distribution technology. **PB 238** expands rainwater harvesting requirements. **PB 182** mandates flood hazard mapping. Even if all six bills pass, significant gaps remain: no water district regulatory framework, no irrigation management act, and no comprehensive flood control governance code.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**
A. **BDP Chapter 11**, Sectoral Objective 6.2: ***"Equitable and sustainable access to potable water and irrigation systems ensured and the vulnerability risk to disaster reduced."***[^3] Three sub-objectives depend directly on this power: (1) households with equitable access to potable water increased; (2) vulnerability and exposure to flooding and coastal disaster decreased; (3) rice and other high-value crops productivity areas with access to irrigation system increased. **BDP Chapter 12** (Environment) identifies 13 watersheds with potential for domestic water production totaling 52,804.24 hectares.[^6] The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), Pillar 3 (Accelerating Economic Growth and Infrastructure), depends on water supply infrastructure for community development and agricultural productivity.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**
A. Three development outcomes are at risk: (1) **potable water access** — the 25.4% household water access gap cannot be closed through project-by-project construction alone. Without a water district framework and regulatory authority, BARMM cannot establish self-sustaining water utility operations that generate revenue for system expansion and maintenance. The FY 2026 target of 65% household access (still below the national average) requires institutional capacity that BAA 13 alone cannot deliver. (2) **Flood resilience** — Maguindanao's perennial flooding affects 99,250 families (first semester 2022) and damages 3,115.8 hectares of agricultural land annually. Without a flood control governance framework with binding hazard zoning and development restrictions, MPW's flood control projects address symptoms rather than systemic causes. (3) **Agricultural productivity** — irrigation access directly determines rice and high-value crop yields. Without a Bangsamoro irrigation management framework, NIA's national programs remain the primary mechanism — and NIA's BARMM coverage has been historically inadequate.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does this power relate to the (gg) inland waters power and the former (pp) public utilities power?**
A. The (ccc) water supply power overlaps with two other enumerated powers. With **(gg) inland waters** — the inland waters power governs the source water bodies (lakes, rivers, streams), while the (ccc) power governs the infrastructure that extracts, treats, distributes, and manages water from those sources. Art. XIII, Sec. 22's "exclusive powers over inland waters" grants authority over the resource; Art. XIII, Sec. 37's dual-authority model governs the infrastructure. A coherent water governance framework must integrate both powers. With the former **(pp) public utilities** — water supply systems operated by water districts or private utilities are public utilities. The regulatory framework for water utilities intersects with general public utility regulation — franchise grants, rate-setting, service quality standards, and consumer protection.

**Q. What is the significance of the Art. XIII, Sec. 37 dual-authority model?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 37 makes this power unique among the 55 enumerated powers. While Art. V, Sec. 2(ccc) enumerates water supply, flood control, and irrigation as Bangsamoro powers, Art. XIII, Sec. 37 simultaneously assigns the **National Government** the role of funding and implementing construction and maintenance. This creates a **shared implementation space** — not a shared power in the doctrinal sense (the BOL does not use the terms "shared" or "concurrent"), but a practical arrangement where both governments exercise functions. The Bangsamoro Government exercises the power through policy, regulation, and regionally-funded projects; the National Government exercises implementation through nationally-funded infrastructure. This model recognizes that major water and flood control infrastructure requires resources and technical capacity that may exceed BARMM's current capacity — but it also means that BARMM's autonomy in this area is constrained by national budget and implementation decisions.

**Q. What is the cross-boundary cooperation requirement and how should it be operationalized?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 37's proviso requires ***"cooperation and coordination between the Bangsamoro Government and the appropriate national or local government bodies"*** for systems ***"that connect to or from facilities outside the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^7] This applies to: (1) the **Mindanao River Basin** flood control — the Rio Grande de Mindanao flows through Regions X, XII, and BARMM, requiring multi-regional coordination; (2) **irrigation systems** drawing from rivers that originate outside BARMM; (3) **water supply systems** serving populations across the BARMM boundary (e.g., Cotabato City and surrounding municipalities). Operationalizing this requires formal intergovernmental agreements — potentially through the Art. VI intergovernmental relations framework — defining roles, cost-sharing, and decision-making protocols for cross-boundary water infrastructure.

**Q. Why does BARMM have only 74.6% potable water access despite having vast water resources?**
A. The BDP 2023-2028 identifies BARMM's water access paradox: despite abundant water resources — 13 major watersheds, Lake Lanao, and extensive river systems — 25.4% of households lack access to potable water.[^4] The gap is institutional, not hydrological. Contributing factors include: (1) **no water district framework** — BARMM lacks the legal mechanism to create and regulate community water supply systems; (2) **underinvestment** — historically low water supply infrastructure spending relative to need; (3) **geographic isolation** — island provinces (Tawi-Tawi, Sulu, Basilan) face unique water distribution challenges; (4) **conflict damage** — armed conflict destroyed water infrastructure in affected municipalities; (5) **incomplete devolution** — national agencies that should have transferred water supply functions to BARMM have not fully done so.
