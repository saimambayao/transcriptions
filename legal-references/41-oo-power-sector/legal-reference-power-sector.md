> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(oo)** — ***"Power sector investments;"***

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over the power sector?**

A. Article V, Section 2(oo) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"power sector investments"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** The term "investments" signals a focus on **investment promotion and facilitation** rather than full regulatory authority over the entire electrical industry. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 36 expands the scope significantly — granting the Bangsamoro Government authority to ***"promote investments in the energy and power generation sector, domestic and international, public and private"*** and to ***"build power generation and distribution utilities operating exclusively in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^1][^2]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Seven provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 36** (main body) is the primary elaborating provision, establishing the Bangsamoro Government's investment promotion authority over the power sector with two conditions: ***"as far as practicable, promote low carbon sustainable power generation"*** and ***"notify the National Government insofar as power generation investments are concerned."***[^2]

   (2) **Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a)** grants authority to ***"build power generation and distribution utilities operating exclusively in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region through the Ministry of Energy it shall create"*** with a **25-year divestment** requirement. It authorizes grid interconnection ***"subject to limitations under Republic Act No. 9136"*** and mandates that grid sales occur only ***"once the power supply needs of its customers have been completely met."*** It also authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to ***"assist electric cooperatives operating exclusively in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in accessing funds and technology."***[^3]

   (3) **Art. XIII, Sec. 36(b)** addresses the **Agus Hydropower Complex** — granting ***"preferential rights to acquire the hydroelectric plants situated within its territorial jurisdiction"*** upon privatization, requiring coordination through the ***"Intergovernmental Energy Board"*** for Lake Lanao water utilization, and preserving ***"the obligation of National Power Corporation or Power Sector Assets and Liabilities Management Corporation"*** to the Bangsamoro Government.[^4]

   (4) **Art. VI, Sec. 7** creates the **Intergovernmental Energy Board (IEB)** to ***"resolve all matters specified in Section 36, Article XIII"*** and ***"other energy issues referred to it by the Intergovernmental Relations Body."*** Its composition includes ***"representatives of the National Government and the Bangsamoro Government from their respective energy regulatory commissions and electrification administrations."***[^5]

   (5) **Art. XIII, Sec. 10** establishes the **joint exercise** framework for fossil fuel exploration and development — the Bangsamoro Government and the National Government ***"shall jointly exercise the power to grant rights, privileges, and concessions over the exploration, development, and utilization of uranium and fossil fuels."***[^6]

   (6) **Art. XIII, Sec. 22** extends the co-management principle to inland waters that are ***"a source of energy for areas outside of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** — applying ***"the principle of co-management of fossil fuels under Section 10."*** This is the legal basis for joint management of hydroelectric resources from Lake Lanao.[^7]

   (7) **Art. XII, Sec. 34** establishes revenue sharing — revenues from ***"all natural resources"*** pertain ***"fully to the Bangsamoro Government"*** except fossil fuels, which are ***"shared equally between the National Government and Bangsamoro Government."*** This creates a fiscal incentive structure favoring renewable energy.[^8]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) directs the Bangsamoro Government to exercise power sector authority ***"through the Ministry of Energy it shall create"*** — an express institutional mandate to establish a standalone Ministry of Energy. The phrase "it shall create" is directive. Additionally, BAA 13, Sec. 3(o) references power industry restructuring ***"subject to laws to be enacted by Parliament"*** and Sec. 3(p) references energy projects ***"as may be provided in a law passed by Parliament"*** — indicating that comprehensive energy legislation is contemplated.[^3][^9]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL creates two institutions:

   (1) The **Ministry of Energy** — Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) directs the Bangsamoro Government to build utilities ***"through the Ministry of Energy it shall create."*** This has not been established as a standalone ministry. Energy functions are currently exercised by MENRE under BAA 13.[^3]

   (2) The **Intergovernmental Energy Board** — Art. VI, Sec. 7 creates this body with representatives from both the National Government and Bangsamoro Government's ***"energy regulatory commissions and electrification administrations."***[^5]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places six specific limitations:

   (1) **EPIRA compliance**: grid interconnection and power sales are ***"subject to limitations under Republic Act No. 9136"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a)).[^3]

   (2) **National Government notification**: ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall notify the National Government insofar as power generation investments are concerned"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 36).[^2]

   (3) **Exclusivity requirement**: BARMM authority covers only utilities ***"operating exclusively in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a)).[^3]

   (4) **Mandatory divestment**: ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall divest its ownership of the utilities twenty-five (25) years after their creation"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a)).[^3]

   (5) **Supply-first rule**: power may be sold over the national grid only ***"once the power supply needs of its customers have been completely met"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a)).[^3]

   (6) **Joint exercise for fossil fuels**: the Bangsamoro Government and National Government ***"shall jointly exercise"*** authority over fossil fuel exploration (Art. XIII, Sec. 10).[^6]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to the power sector?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** This is the universal constitutional boundary.[^10]

   (2) **National Economy and Patrimony (Art. XII, Sec. 2)**: The State has ***"full control and supervision"*** over the exploration, development, and utilization of natural resources. Fossil fuels and uranium are constitutional patrimony — the National Government cannot fully relinquish control, which is why the BOL requires joint exercise for these resources.[^11]

   (3) **State Policy on Self-Reliant Economy (Art. II, Sec. 19)**: The State shall ***"develop a self-reliant and independent national economy effectively controlled by Filipinos."*** Energy security is a component of this constitutional policy — BARMM energy legislation must support, not undermine, national energy independence.[^12]

   (4) **Equal Protection and Due Process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, nor shall any person be denied the equal protection of the laws."*** Rate-setting, franchise revocation, and regulatory enforcement against power sector participants must satisfy procedural and substantive due process. Equal protection ensures that similarly situated consumers and investors receive comparable treatment.[^13]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee for the power sector?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Non-impairment of contracts** (Art. III, Sec. 10) — existing power supply agreements, service contracts, and franchise grants cannot be retroactively impaired by BARMM legislation.

   (2) **Due process in rate regulation** — consumers and utilities are entitled to notice and hearing before rate changes. The ERC's regulatory due process standards apply to EPIRA-covered utilities.

   (3) **Equal protection** — BARMM cannot discriminate among power sector investors without rational basis. Foreign investment limitations under Art. XII, Sec. 2 apply uniformly.

**Q. What constitutional mandates for energy legislation or institutions exist?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 2 mandates that the ***"exploration, development, and utilization of natural resources shall be under the full control and supervision of the State."*** This constitutional mandate is the basis for DOE's authority and constrains how far the BOL can devolve fossil fuel authority to BARMM — hence the joint exercise model in Art. XIII, Sec. 10.[^11]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for the power sector?**

A. The national framework is comprehensive and highly structured. **RA 9136** (EPIRA) is the foundational law — it unbundled the electricity industry into generation, transmission, distribution, and supply; created the **Wholesale Electricity Spot Market (WESM)**; established the **Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC)** as the independent regulator; mandated the privatization of NPC generation assets through **PSALM**; and set the framework for competition in generation and supply. **RA 9513** (Renewable Energy Act) adds the renewable energy framework — feed-in tariffs, net metering, renewable portfolio standards, and fiscal incentives. **RA 7638** (DOE Act) establishes DOE's planning and policy authority. **RA 10531** strengthens NEA's supervision of electric cooperatives. Together, these laws provide a complete regulatory architecture covering investment, generation, transmission, distribution, retail competition, rate regulation, consumer protection, renewable energy, and rural electrification.[^14][^15][^16][^17]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national energy laws apply in the absence of comprehensive Bangsamoro energy legislation:

   (1) **RA 9136** (EPIRA) — applies **directly** as expressly referenced in BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a). Cannot be displaced by BAA for grid-connected activities.[^14]
   (2) **RA 9513** (Renewable Energy Act) — applies **suppletorily** for renewable energy development. DOE certification required for RE incentives.[^15]
   (3) **RA 7638** (DOE Act) — applies **directly** for fossil fuel exploration (joint exercise) and national energy planning.[^16]
   (4) **RA 10531** (NEA Strengthening Act) — applies **directly** for electric cooperative supervision.[^17]
   (5) **RA 11234**, **RA 11285**, **RA 11552**, **RA 11571**, **RA 12179** — general application laws governing permitting, energy efficiency, lifeline rates, congressional oversight, and PSALM operations respectively.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for the power sector?**

A. Five national institutions exercise active authority:

   (1) **Department of Energy (DOE)** — energy planning, fossil fuel service contracts (joint exercise), renewable energy service contracts, energy data and statistics, international energy cooperation.
   (2) **Energy Regulatory Commission (ERC)** — rate-setting for distribution utilities, franchise-granting for grid-connected utilities, WESM oversight, consumer protection.
   (3) **National Electrification Administration (NEA)** — electric cooperative supervision, financial assistance, governance standards, intervention in distressed cooperatives.
   (4) **National Grid Corporation of the Philippines (NGCP)** — transmission services including within BARMM, grid connection, transmission development planning.
   (5) **Power Sector Assets and Liabilities Management Corporation (PSALM)** — manages NPC's generation assets including Agus Hydropower Complex plants, processes privatization.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III establishes MENRE with an express **energy mandate** alongside its environment and natural resources responsibilities. BAA 85, Chapter XIII allocates its operational budget, including the Energy Management and Development Services.[^9][^18]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for the power sector as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through MENRE's BAA 13 general mandate and BAA 85 budget allocation.

**Q. What are MENRE's mandate and functions under BAA 13 for energy?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III defines MENRE's energy-related powers and functions:

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): ***"The Ministry shall be primarily responsible for the exploration, utilization, management, conservation, protection and sustainable development of the region's environment, natural resources and energy sources."***[^9]

   **Policy** (Sec. 1(b)): ***"Ensure continuous, adequate and economic supply of energy and likewise promote the use of renewable energy for power generation to achieve the sustainable development goals and as far as practicable, promote low-carbon energy generation policies."***[^19]

   **Energy-specific powers and functions** (Sec. 3):

   - (a) ***"Recommend the enactment of regional laws relative to the development, utilization, regulation and conservation of the natural resources and energy"***
   - (b) ***"Formulate, supervise, and implement policies, plans and programs pertaining to the management, conservation, protection, development and utilization of the region's environment, natural resources, and energy"***
   - (c) ***"Promulgate rules and regulation in accordance with regional law governing the exploration, development, conservation, disposition, utilization"*** of energy
   - (f) ***"Undertake exploration, assessment, classification and inventory of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region's environment, natural resources, and energy"***
   - (g) ***"Promote proper and mutual consultation with the private sector involving natural resources and energy development, utilization and conservation"***
   - (k) ***"Regulate the development, disposition, exploration and utilization of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region's forests, land, mineral and energy resources"***
   - (n) ***"Recommend policies governing the grant of rights, privileges, and concession over the exploration, development and utilization of fossil fuels (such as petroleum, natural gas and coal) and uranium"***
   - (o) ***"Subject to laws to be enacted by Parliament, supervise the implementation of electric power industry restructuring to establish a competitive, market-based environment, and encourage private sector participation"***
   - (p) ***"Build and exercise supervision and control over energy-projects relative to power generation and distribution utilities, as may be provided in a law passed by Parliament"***[^9]

   **Energy organizational structure** (Sec. 8-9): MENRE has two **Bangsamoro Directors-General** — one for (a) Environment and Natural Resources and one for (b) **Energy**. The **Energy Management and Development Services** (Sec. 9(11)) is headed by a Director II and includes two divisions: (a) **Energy Resource Development Division** and (b) **Energy Utilization Management Division**.[^20][^21]

**Q. What can MENRE NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 provides MENRE with **institutional existence**, **general policy-making authority**, and **basic organizational structure** for energy. However, two critical functions are expressly conditioned on Parliament enacting dedicated energy legislation:

   (1) **Electric power industry restructuring** — Sec. 3(o) is ***"subject to laws to be enacted by Parliament"***
   (2) **Power generation and distribution utility supervision** — Sec. 3(p) operates ***"as may be provided in a law passed by Parliament"***

   Without standalone legislation, MENRE cannot: establish a **Bangsamoro-specific rate-setting mechanism** for off-grid utilities; create a **competitive power market** within BARMM; exercise **regulatory enforcement** (penalties, franchise revocation, license suspension) over power sector participants; establish a **Bangsamoro renewable energy incentive framework** distinct from RA 9513; or define the **jurisdictional boundary** between MENRE and national agencies (DOE, ERC, NEA) for specific power sector functions.[^9]

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No enacted resolutions specifically addressing power sector legislation or energy policy have been identified. However, two pending Parliament Bills — PB No. 263 (Bangsamoro Electrification Administration) and PB No. 339 (Micro-Grid Solar Systems) — demonstrate legislative intent to address the institutional and infrastructure gaps.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. MENRE is the correct lead — but with a significant qualification. The BOL envisions a standalone **Ministry of Energy** (Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a)), not an energy division within an environment ministry. The current arrangement bundles energy with environment and natural resources, which is logical (energy extraction is a natural resource issue) but creates an institutional tension: MENRE's primary orientation is **conservation and environmental protection**, while the energy mandate requires **investment promotion and industrial development**. A standalone Ministry of Energy — or at minimum, a significantly elevated energy bureau within MENRE — would resolve this tension.[^3]

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Current institutional landscape:

   - **Lead ministry**: MENRE (BAA 13, Book VI, Title III; BAA 85, Chapter XIII) — with Energy Management and Development Services (2 divisions: Energy Resource Development, Energy Utilization Management)
   - **Bangsamoro Director-General for Energy**: career official within MENRE (BAA 13, Sec. 8) overseeing the energy services
   - **Intergovernmental Energy Board**: created by BOL Art. VI, Sec. 7 — operational status unclear [UNVERIFIED]
   - **Supporting agencies**: BPDA (development planning), MFBM (fiscal management of energy investments)

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Two BOL-mandated institutions have not been established:

   (1) **Ministry of Energy** — Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) directs the creation of a standalone ministry. Energy functions are currently absorbed within MENRE.[^3]

   (2) **Intergovernmental Energy Board** — Art. VI, Sec. 7 creates this body. Whether it has been operationalized and convened is unclear. [UNVERIFIED]

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Yes. Fully exercising this power would require:

   (1) A **Bangsamoro Energy Regulatory Commission** — a regional counterpart to ERC for off-grid and exclusively-BARMM utilities not connected to the national grid. Art. VI, Sec. 7 contemplates BARMM having its own ***"energy regulatory commission."***

   (2) A **Bangsamoro Electrification Administration** — a regional counterpart to NEA for electric cooperative supervision and rural electrification. Art. VI, Sec. 7 contemplates BARMM having its own ***"electrification administration."*** PB No. 263 addresses this gap.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The national government exercises extensive authority:

   - **DOE** conducts energy planning for the Mindanao grid (including BARMM), processes service contract applications for fossil fuel exploration within BARMM (joint exercise), and administers renewable energy contracts.
   - **ERC** sets distribution rates for electric cooperatives and utilities serving BARMM consumers connected to the Mindanao grid, grants certificates of compliance, and adjudicates consumer complaints.
   - **NEA** supervises the 5 electric cooperatives operating in BARMM (MAGELCO in Maguindanao, LASURECO in Lanao del Sur, SULECO in Sulu, TAWELCO in Tawi-Tawi, BASELCO in Basilan) [UNVERIFIED — cooperative count], provides financial and technical assistance, and can intervene in governance failures.
   - **NGCP** operates the Mindanao transmission grid including lines within BARMM and manages interconnection of BARMM-located power plants.
   - **PSALM** manages the Agus Hydropower Complex privatization process and NPC's obligations to the Bangsamoro Government.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. Devolution is **minimal**. Unlike other powers where MENRE has assumed operational functions from DENR, the energy sector remains almost entirely under national agency control. DOE, ERC, NEA, and NGCP continue to exercise their full mandates within BARMM. The BOL's design contemplates **parallel institutional development** (BARMM creates its own energy ministry, regulatory commission, and electrification administration) rather than transfer of existing national agency functions. The Intergovernmental Energy Board is the intended mechanism for managing this parallel structure.[^5]

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. BARMM's accomplishments are limited to institutional foundation-building:

   - MENRE's **Energy Management and Development Services** has been established with two divisions
   - A **Bangsamoro Director-General for Energy** position has been created
   - MENRE has been assigned energy policy recommendation functions

   No standalone energy legislation has been enacted, no Bangsamoro-specific power generation facility has been built under BARMM authority, no regional energy regulatory framework exists, and the Ministry of Energy mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) has not been created.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of standalone energy legislation. BAA 13 expressly conditions two critical MENRE functions — electric power industry restructuring (Sec. 3(o)) and energy project supervision (Sec. 3(p)) — on Parliament enacting energy laws. Without this legislation, MENRE cannot exercise regulatory authority, and the power sector remains entirely under national agency jurisdiction. The institutional gap (no standalone Ministry of Energy, no Bangsamoro energy regulatory commission, no electrification administration) is a secondary constraint that the enabling legislation would address.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has over two decades of post-EPIRA institutional infrastructure:

   - **ERC** — independent quasi-judicial body with rate-setting, franchise-granting, and enforcement authority; specialized staff; published methodologies and jurisprudence on rate cases
   - **DOE** — comprehensive energy planning capability; energy data systems; renewable energy management bureau; petroleum and coal management
   - **NEA** — nationwide electric cooperative supervision network; financial intervention mechanisms; cooperative performance monitoring systems
   - **WESM** — operational wholesale electricity spot market with clearing and settlement infrastructure
   - **NGCP** — transmission development plan; grid connection standards; open access regime

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national system fails BARMM in three critical areas:

   (1) **Electrification gap**: BARMM has the lowest electrification rate in the Philippines. Island provinces (Sulu, Tawi-Tawi, Basilan) and conflict-affected areas remain largely unelectrified. National programs have not achieved universal access.

   (2) **Shari'ah-compliant financing**: National energy financing is entirely conventional (interest-based). Muslim communities and investors seeking Shari'ah-compliant investment vehicles for power projects have no framework.

   (3) **Conflict-sensitive planning**: National energy planning does not account for post-conflict realities — security constraints on infrastructure, displaced communities, contested lands, and the need for decentralized solutions in areas where centralized grid extension is impractical.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: grid technical standards, safety standards, WESM rules for grid-connected facilities, transmission interconnection protocols
   - **Adapt**: rate-setting methodology (adjusted for BARMM cost structures and subsidy needs), rural electrification strategy (micro-grid and off-grid focus rather than grid extension), renewable energy incentive framework (aligned with BARMM's solar and hydro potential)
   - **Replace**: cooperative supervision model (BARMM-specific approach through a Bangsamoro Electrification Administration instead of NEA), project financing framework (Shari'ah-compliant options alongside conventional), energy planning process (integrated with BDP rather than national energy plan)

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes — primarily in three areas: (1) **project financing** — the prohibition of *riba* means that conventional interest-based project finance for power plants and distribution infrastructure must have Shari'ah-compliant alternatives; (2) **environmental stewardship** — the principles of *khalifah* (stewardship) and *la darar wa la dirar* (no harm) reinforce the BOL's sustainability mandate and constrain environmentally destructive power generation; (3) **public utility governance** — the principle of *amanah* (trust) applies to the management of publicly-owned power utilities during the 25-year pre-divestment period.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not expressly reference Shari'ah in Art. XIII, Sec. 36. However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact ***"Shari'ah-based laws"*** on ***"commercial"*** matters — which encompasses Shari'ah-compliant energy financing and commercial energy transactions. Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34 on Islamic banking and finance provides the broader framework for Shari'ah-compliant financial instruments (*sukuk*, *ijarah*, *istisna'*) that can be applied to power sector project finance.[^22]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Five principles apply as detailed in the Shari'ah Sources table above: *maslahah* (public interest — justifies universal electrification as a public good), *amanah* (trust — governs management of public energy assets), *la darar wa la dirar* (no harm — constrains environmentally destructive projects), prohibition of *riba* (necessitates Shari'ah-compliant financing options), and *khalifah* (stewardship — requires sustainable resource management for Lake Lanao and other energy resources).

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro energy legislation?**

A. Integration should be **optional** (Shari'ah-compliant track available alongside conventional) rather than mandatory. A Bangsamoro energy law should: (1) authorize **Islamic project financing instruments** — *istisna'* for construction, *ijarah* for equipment leasing, *sukuk* for infrastructure bonds — as alternatives to conventional debt; (2) require the **Shari'ah Legislative Review Board** to certify that the energy law's provisions do not violate Shari'ah principles; (3) ensure that BARMM-owned power utilities during the 25-year period operate under governance standards incorporating *amanah* principles.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in the power sector?**

A. This power falls within **Tier 3 — Moderate** on the 5-layer constraint framework established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B). The analysis:

   **Express EPIRA compliance** — the BOL's direct reference to ***"subject to limitations under Republic Act No. 9136"*** in Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) means BARMM cannot diverge from EPIRA's core architecture for grid-connected activities. This is a **Layer 4 institutional jurisdiction retention** — ERC, NGCP, and WESM retain their national mandates within BARMM.

   **Joint exercise constraint** — fossil fuel exploration requires National Government co-exercise through DOE (Art. XIII, Sec. 10). This is another **Layer 4 retention** — DOE's authority is constitutionally grounded in Art. XII, Sec. 2 (State control over natural resources).

   **Off-grid and exclusively-BARMM space** — for power generation and distribution utilities operating **exclusively within BARMM** and **not connected to the national grid**, BARMM has the broadest divergence space. No EPIRA constraint applies to purely internal, off-grid systems. BARMM can establish its own rate-setting, licensing, and regulatory framework for these utilities.

   **Renewable energy** — BARMM retains full revenue (Art. XII, Sec. 34) and can develop a Bangsamoro-specific renewable energy framework. BARMM can diverge from RA 9513's incentive structure by creating different or additional incentives for renewable energy within the region.

   **Electric cooperatives** — the BOL authorizes BARMM to "assist" cooperatives but does not displace NEA's supervisory authority. BARMM can provide parallel support programs but cannot unilaterally assume NEA's regulatory functions without intergovernmental agreement.

   As the Supreme Court held in *Ali v. BTA Parliament* (GR-02219, 2025), BARMM legislative authority ***"must always operate within the bounds of the Constitution and national statutes."*** For the power sector, this means EPIRA compliance is non-negotiable for grid-connected operations, while off-grid and exclusively-BARMM operations offer the primary space for legislative innovation.[^23]

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. Three categories of cross-boundary implications:

   (1) **Grid-connected utilities** — must comply with both BARMM regulations (if enacted) and EPIRA/ERC rules. Dual compliance creates complexity. The Intergovernmental Energy Board is the intended mechanism for harmonization.

   (2) **Electric cooperatives** — currently under NEA supervision. If BARMM creates a Bangsamoro Electrification Administration, cooperatives may face dual regulation. A clear jurisdictional delineation — either through the Intergovernmental Energy Board or a specific BAA provision — is essential.

   (3) **Investors** — different regulatory standards between BARMM and the rest of the Philippines may create investment uncertainty. A transparent, predictable regulatory framework is necessary to attract the domestic and international power sector investments that Art. XIII, Sec. 36 envisions.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on the power sector?**

A. The BAA would govern within BARMM for matters within BARMM's legislative competence — specifically, power generation and distribution utilities operating exclusively within the region. **EPIRA continues to apply** for grid-connected activities, WESM participation, and transmission. National law applies suppletorily where the BAA is silent. Constitutional provisions (Art. XII, Sec. 2 on natural resources; Art. X, Sec. 15/20 on autonomous region framework) always apply.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Three overlapping jurisdictions require resolution:

   (1) **Electric cooperative supervision** — NEA currently exercises full authority. BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) authorizes BARMM to "assist" cooperatives. What constitutes "assistance" versus "supervision"? Can BARMM issue regulations binding on cooperatives that are also subject to NEA regulations? PB No. 263 (Bangsamoro Electrification Administration) would address this, but requires coordination with NEA through the Intergovernmental Energy Board.

   (2) **Renewable energy service contracts** — DOE currently grants all RE service contracts. Art. XIII, Sec. 36 grants BARMM power sector investment authority. Can BARMM grant its own renewable energy contracts for projects operating exclusively within the region? The BOL's joint exercise provision (Art. XIII, Sec. 10) applies expressly to ***"fossil fuels and uranium"*** — renewable energy is not mentioned, suggesting BARMM may have broader unilateral authority over renewable energy.

   (3) **Rate regulation** — ERC regulates rates for grid-connected distribution utilities. For off-grid BARMM utilities not connected to the national grid, who regulates rates? The BOL does not specify. A Bangsamoro energy law must create a rate-setting mechanism for these utilities.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Two pending bills partially address the gaps:

   - **PB No. 263** (Bangsamoro Electrification Administration) — would address the electric cooperative supervision overlap and create an institutional counterpart to NEA.
   - **PB No. 339** (Micro-Grid Solar Systems) — would address off-grid renewable energy infrastructure but is narrower than a comprehensive energy law.

   Even if both pass, significant gaps remain: no comprehensive **Bangsamoro Energy Code** establishing the regulatory framework, no **Bangsamoro rate-setting mechanism** for off-grid utilities, no **renewable energy incentive framework** specific to BARMM, and no legislation creating the **Ministry of Energy** mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a).

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. Three BDP chapters and one Matatag pillar depend directly on power sector authority:

   - **BDP Chapter 7** (Economic Development) — industrial development, economic zone activation, and agro-processing all require reliable, affordable electricity. The BDP targets expansion of productive capacity that cannot occur without energy infrastructure.
   - **BDP Chapter 11** (Infrastructure Development) — identifies power generation and distribution as critical infrastructure. Targets electrification of unserved areas, particularly island provinces and conflict-affected communities.
   - **BDP Chapter 12** (Environment and Natural Resources) — renewable energy promotion, sustainable management of Lake Lanao/Agus hydropower resources, and low-carbon development pathways.
   - **Mas Matatag Pillar 2** (Sustainable Economic Growth) — manufacturing, agro-processing, and economic zone development all depend on power sector investments.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Four development outcomes are directly at risk:

   (1) **Electrification** — without a Bangsamoro Electrification Administration or energy law, BARMM cannot deploy a targeted rural electrification program for its unique geography (island provinces, mountainous areas, conflict-affected communities). NEA's nationwide approach has not achieved universal access in BARMM.

   (2) **Renewable energy development** — BARMM's significant solar, hydro, and biomass potential remains undeveloped because no Bangsamoro-specific incentive framework exists to attract investors. The 100% revenue retention for renewable resources (Art. XII, Sec. 34) is a powerful fiscal incentive that cannot be operationalized without enabling legislation.

   (3) **Industrial development** — economic zones, agro-processing facilities, and manufacturing all require reliable power. Without the authority to build and regulate power utilities under Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a), BARMM cannot guarantee the energy supply that industrial investors need.

   (4) **Agus/Lake Lanao management** — the NPC/PSALM obligations to the Bangsamoro Government and the preferential acquisition rights upon privatization require an institutional framework to assert and exercise. Without energy legislation, BARMM's position in Agus privatization is weakened.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the Agus Hydropower Complex and why is it significant for BARMM?**

A. The Agus Hydropower Complex is a cascade of hydroelectric power plants along the Agus River, which flows from **Lake Lanao** in Lanao del Sur to Iligan Bay. The complex is one of Mindanao's largest power generation sources, producing approximately 730 MW of installed capacity [UNVERIFIED]. Lake Lanao — the largest lake in Mindanao and the second-largest in the Philippines — is entirely within BARMM territory (Lanao del Sur). The Agus plants straddle BARMM and non-BARMM territory, creating the inter-jurisdictional complexity that Art. XIII, Sec. 36(b) and the Intergovernmental Energy Board address. NPC built the complex; PSALM manages its privatization. The BOL grants BARMM ***"preferential rights to acquire the hydroelectric plants situated within its territorial jurisdiction"*** upon privatization.[^4]

**Q. What is the 25-year divestment requirement and what does it mean?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) requires the Bangsamoro Government to ***"divest its ownership of the utilities twenty-five (25) years after their creation."*** This means BARMM-owned power generation and distribution utilities are **temporary public enterprises** — the Government may build them to address electrification gaps, but must eventually transfer them to private ownership. This mirrors EPIRA's philosophy of reducing government participation in the power sector to promote competition. The 25-year clock starts from the **creation** of each utility, not from the BOL's effectivity.[^3]

**Q. How does the "distributed power generation" mandate work?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 36(a) states that ***"distributed power generation shall be aggressively promoted as part of the Bangsamoro Government's power development plan."*** Distributed generation refers to small-scale power production close to the point of consumption — solar rooftop systems, micro-hydro plants, biomass generators, diesel gensets, and micro-grids. This mandate is particularly important for BARMM's island provinces (Sulu, Tawi-Tawi, Basilan) where extending the Mindanao grid is impractical and expensive. PB No. 339 (Micro-Grid Solar Systems) directly addresses this mandate.[^3]

**Q. What is the fiscal incentive structure for BARMM energy development?**

A. The BOL creates a strong fiscal incentive favoring **renewable energy**:

   - **Renewable energy revenues**: pertain ***"fully to the Bangsamoro Government"*** (Art. XII, Sec. 34) — 100% BARMM retention.[^8]
   - **Fossil fuel revenues**: ***"shared equally between the National Government and Bangsamoro Government"*** (Art. XII, Sec. 34) — 50-50 split.[^8]

   This 50-percentage-point difference in revenue retention creates a structural fiscal incentive for BARMM to prioritize solar, wind, hydro, biomass, and geothermal over petroleum, natural gas, and coal — aligning fiscal self-interest with the BOL's low-carbon mandate.

**Q. What electric cooperatives operate in BARMM and what is their status?**

A. Electric cooperatives are the primary distribution utilities in BARMM. Each province typically has one cooperative: MAGELCO (Maguindanao), LASURECO (Lanao del Sur), SULECO (Sulu), TAWELCO (Tawi-Tawi), and BASELCO (Basilan) [UNVERIFIED — cooperative names and coverage areas]. Many of these cooperatives face financial distress, governance challenges, and low collection efficiency — common issues in conflict-affected areas. The BOL authorizes BARMM to ***"assist electric cooperatives operating exclusively in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in accessing funds and technology to ensure their financial and operational viability"*** through ***"restructuring of debts, with rehabilitation and efficiency improvement measures based on a set of clear time-bound operational reform programs."***[^3]
