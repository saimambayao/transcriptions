# (v) Education and Skills Training

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(v)** — "Education and skills training;"

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|

| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. IX, Sec. 16 ("the Bangsamoro Government shall conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government"); Art. IX, Sec. 17 (vocational/technical/nonformal education "shall conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government") |

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over education and skills training as an enumerated power under Article V, Section 2(v).[^1] This grant is among the most extensively elaborated in the entire organic law. Article IX, Sections 14 through 20, dedicates seven full sections to defining the scope and architecture of Bangsamoro education. The core provision, Section 16, directs the Bangsamoro Government to "establish, maintain, and support a complete and integrated system of quality education, which shall be a subsystem of the national education system."[^2] The Bangsamoro Government must develop an educational framework "relevant and responsive to the needs, ideals, and aspirations of the Bangsamoro people," and it is mandated to institutionalize peace education at all levels.

The BOL further requires the Bangsamoro Government to establish, maintain, and supervise the Madaris educational system in coordination with the Department of Education, the Commission on Higher Education, and the Technical Education and Skills Development Authority.[^3] Section 17 extends the power to vocational, technical, nonformal, and special education, targeting the poor, illiterate, out-of-school youth, persons with special needs, the disadvantaged, and senior citizens.[^4] Section 19 mandates the Parliament to create a tribal university system to address the higher educational needs of non-Moro indigenous peoples.[^5] Section 20 requires a comprehensive physical education program and the creation of a Bangsamoro Sports Commission, linking education to sports development.[^6] The BOL also authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to supervise and regulate private schools, including sectarian and nonsectarian institutions at all levels, and grants private schools three representatives to participate in deliberations of the appropriate ministry.

Crucially, the BOL imposes a constitutional limit: the Bangsamoro educational system must conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government.[^7] This national law consistency requirement applies to both basic education (Section 16) and vocational, technical, and nonformal education (Section 17). The BOL also mandates that state universities and colleges in BARMM continue to be governed by their respective charters, and that the National Government shall continue to support the Mindanao State University System.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE) serves as the lead ministry for education, as established under BAA 13 (Bangsamoro Administrative Code) and funded through BAA 85, Chapter VIII.[^8] MBHTE exercises authority over basic education, higher education, and technical-vocational education within the region. The Bangsamoro Parliament has enacted three standalone education BAAs: BAA 18 (Bangsamoro Education Code), BAA 40 (Bangsamoro Science High School System), and BAA 50 (Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies).[^9]

BAA 18, the Bangsamoro Education Code, is the most comprehensive of the three. It establishes the integrated system of quality education required by the BOL, covering basic education, higher education, technical-vocational education, Madaris education, and Islamic and Arabic studies. BAA 40 creates the Bangsamoro Science High School System, implementing the science education dimension and cross-referencing the science and technology power under Article V, Section 2(uu).[^10] BAA 50 establishes the Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies (BKIS), a dedicated institution for Islamic higher education that fulfills the BOL's mandate on Madaris educational development.[^11]

Parliamentary resolutions signal continuing legislative interest in education. Resolution 101 mandated inclusion of Prophet Muhammad's teachings in the curriculum. Resolution 481 urged MBHTE to allow teaching of religion in public schools. Resolution 503 called for establishing a Madrasah and Markadz Database Management System. Resolutions 532-534 requested MBHTE reports on education performance, textbook distribution, and scholarship programs, reflecting robust parliamentary oversight. Multiple bills remain pending, including bills for a Bangsamoro State University (Bill 359), a Tawi-Tawi Maritime School (Bill 356), and comprehensive SPED centers in every province (Bill 367).

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The national law relationship is explicit and unambiguous. Article IX, Section 16 requires the Bangsamoro educational system to "conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government."[^12] Section 17 imposes the same requirement on vocational, technical, nonformal, and special education. This means the Bangsamoro Government may exceed national standards but may not fall below them. In practical terms, DepEd's K-12 curriculum standards, CHED's minimum course requirements for higher education, and TESDA's training regulations apply as floors that BARMM education must meet.

The Madaris system operates in coordination with DepEd, CHED, and TESDA, with the Civil Service Commission promulgating rules for Madaris teacher qualifications. The National Government continues to support the MSU System, and state universities retain their individual charters. No intergovernmental body under Article VI is specifically created for education, but coordination is built into the operational framework through the Bangsamoro's status as a subsystem of the national education system. Article XII, Section 19 further reinforces education's importance by directing that "the highest priority shall be given by the Bangsamoro Government for education, health services, and social services" in block grant allocation.[^13]

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

Despite having three standalone BAAs, significant gaps remain in Bangsamoro education legislation. The Education Code (BAA 18) provides the general framework but does not address several specific BOL mandates. The tribal university system mandated by Article IX, Section 19 has not been established through a standalone BAA, though Bill 274 (converting Upi Agricultural School into Bangsamoro Tribal University) remains pending.[^14] The BOL's mandate to recognize and support indigenous peoples' educational systems for integration into the Bangsamoro educational system (Section 19, last paragraph) also remains unlegislated.

Higher education governance lacks a dedicated regulatory body. Bill 417 proposes creating a Bangsamoro Commission on Higher Education, which would give BARMM its own higher education regulatory authority parallel to CHED at the national level. This would address the current situation where higher education regulation operates through MBHTE's general mandate under BAA 13, without the specialized institutional structure the sector requires. Special education also lacks comprehensive legislation — Bills 367 and 386 propose SPED centers and services but have not been enacted. The scholarship framework remains fragmented across individual programs rather than being codified in a unified scholarship act.

The Madaris educational system, while addressed in BAA 18, could benefit from a dedicated Madaris Development Act that codifies funding mechanisms, teacher development pathways, curriculum standards, and the integration framework with the formal education system. Resolution 503's call for a Madrasah database signals Parliament's awareness of the need for better institutional infrastructure in this area.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

Education is a centerpiece of the BDP 2023-2028, addressed primarily in Chapter 9 (Social Services) with the goal of improving access to quality education, reducing functional illiteracy, and expanding technical-vocational training to address unemployment.[^15] The BDP's human capital development strategy depends on the full exercise of this power — without comprehensive higher education regulation, a tribal university system, and expanded SPED coverage, the BDP's education targets for 2028 will remain partially unmet. Chapter 8 (Technology and Innovation) also depends on a robust education system to produce the skilled workforce needed for Bangsamoro technological development.

## Legislative Gaps

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type A**: No tribal university legislation | Art. IX, Sec. 19 mandates the Parliament to create a tribal university system for non-Moro indigenous peoples — this BOL mandate remains unfulfilled | Enact Bangsamoro Tribal University Act (Bill 274 pending) |
| **Type A**: No higher education commission | No standalone regulatory body for higher education; MBHTE manages through its general BAA 13 mandate | Enact Bangsamoro Commission on Higher Education Act (Bill 417 pending) |
| **Type B**: Partial SPED implementation | BAA 18 addresses special education in general terms but no dedicated SPED institutional framework exists | Enact Bangsamoro Special Education Act establishing SPED centers per province (Bills 367, 386 pending) |
| **Type B**: Partial Madaris framework | BAA 18 covers Madaris education but lacks dedicated funding mechanisms, teacher development pathways, and institutional database | Enact standalone Madaris Development Act with dedicated funding and teacher professionalization provisions |
| **Type B**: No unified scholarship code | Scholarships are fragmented across individual programs without a codified framework | Enact Bangsamoro Scholarship Act unifying scholarship criteria, funding, and return-service obligations |
