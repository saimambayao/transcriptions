# (v) Education and Skills Training

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(v)** — ***"Education and skills training;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(v) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. IX, Sec. 16 (***"the Bangsamoro Government shall conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government"***); Art. IX, Sec. 17 (vocational, technical, nonformal, and special education ***"shall conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government"***) |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title II |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies (BAA 50); Bangsamoro Science High School System (BAA 40); Bangsamoro Sports Commission (BAA 12); Development Academy of the Bangsamoro (DAB) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 9 — Social Services (Education, Health) |
| **Priority Code** | Bangsamoro Education Code (BAA 18) — one of the 7 priority codes mandated by Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) |

<tr>
  <td>Art. V, Sec. 2(v)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"Education and skills training;"</strong></em></td>

<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 16</td>
  <td><em><strong>"It shall be a top priority of the Bangsamoro Government to establish, maintain, and support a complete and integrated system of quality education, which shall be a subsystem of the national education system. The Bangsamoro Government shall develop an educational framework relevant and responsive to the needs, ideals, and aspirations of the Bangsamoro people. For this purpose, the Bangsamoro Government shall conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Core education provision. Three mandates: (1) establish a <strong>complete and integrated</strong> system of quality education; (2) develop an educational framework <strong>responsive to Bangsamoro needs</strong>; (3) <strong>conform to minimum national standards</strong>. The education system is a <strong>subsystem</strong> of the national system — not separate from it.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 16 (para. 2)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall institutionalize peace education in all levels of education."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Mandatory <strong>peace education</strong> at all levels — a distinct Bangsamoro requirement with no national equivalent.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 16 (para. 3)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"Any school, college or university existing in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region as of the effectivity of this Organic Law, and such other schools and institutions that may be established hereinafter, shall be deemed integral components of the educational system of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, and shall be governed by their respective charters. The National Government shall continue to support, enhance, and strengthen the Mindanao State University System."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Existing schools retain their <strong>charters</strong>. MSU System retains <strong>National Government support</strong> — a constitutional-level protection for the MSU system within BARMM.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 16 (para. 4-5)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall supervise and regulate private schools, including sectarian and nonsectarian institutions of learning in any level. Three (3) representatives of private schools shall have the right to participate in the deliberations of the appropriate Bangsamoro Government ministry, and Bangsamoro offices of the Commission on Higher Education, Technical Education and Skills Development Authority, and other government agencies on matters dealing with private schools."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Grants <strong>supervisory and regulatory authority</strong> over private schools at all levels. Guarantees private school <strong>representation</strong> (3 seats) in MBHTE and BARMM offices of CHED and TESDA deliberations.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 16 (para. 6)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The right of sectarian and educational institutions to propagate their religious beliefs shall not be curtailed. The regional educational system shall not discriminate against the sectarian educational institutions in any manner or form."</strong></em></td>
  <td><strong>Non-discrimination</strong> guarantee for sectarian schools — their right to propagate religious beliefs is constitutionally protected within the Bangsamoro education system.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 17</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The thrusts and programs of vocational, technical, nonformal, and special education of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region educational system for the poor, illiterate, out-of-school youth, persons with special needs, disadvantaged, and senior citizens shall be supportive and relevant to the human resource requirements of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, which shall conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Extends education power to <strong>TVET, nonformal, and special education</strong> for six target populations. Imposes <strong>national minimum standards</strong> — the same consistency requirement as Sec. 16.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 18</td>
  <td><em><strong>"In coordination with the Department of Education, Commission on Higher Education, and Technical Education and Skills Development Authority, the Bangsamoro Government shall establish, maintain, and supervise Madaris education in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. In the case of Madaris teachers, the Civil Service Commission shall promulgate rules and regulations to set the standards for their qualifications, appointments, and promotions."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Mandates <strong>Madaris education system</strong> in coordination with three national agencies. <strong>CSC</strong> retains authority over Madaris teacher qualification standards — not MBHTE alone.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 18 (para. 2-3)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall ensure the integration in the elementary and high school education curricula the teaching of Islamic and Arabic studies for Muslim pupils and students in public schools. The Parliament shall enact legislation to develop and strengthen the Madaris educational system in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Two mandates: (1) <strong>Islamic and Arabic studies</strong> must be integrated into elementary and high school curricula for Muslim students in public schools; (2) Parliament must <strong>enact legislation</strong> for Madaris development — a legislative mandate.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 19</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Parliament shall create a tribal university system in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region to address the higher educational needs of the non-Moro indigenous peoples."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Express <strong>legislative mandate</strong> to create a <strong>tribal university system</strong>. <strong>Unfulfilled as of BAA 89.</strong></td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 19 (para. 2)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Parliament shall pass a law to recognize and support the indigenous peoples' educational system for its integration in the Bangsamoro educational system."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Second unfulfilled mandate — <strong>indigenous peoples' educational system</strong> must be recognized and integrated. Distinct from the tribal university mandate.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 20</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro educational system shall develop and maintain an integrated and comprehensive physical education program."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Links education to <strong>physical education and sports development</strong>. Cross-implements power (ww) sports and recreation. BAA 12 (Bangsamoro Sports Commission) was enacted pursuant to this section.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 14</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall respect, protect, and promote the rights of children, especially orphans of tender age. [...] Their education and development, both physical and mental, shall be fully addressed."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Children's <strong>right to education</strong> — imposes an obligation on the Bangsamoro Government to fully address children's educational development.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XII, Sec. 19</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Parliament shall assign the highest budgetary priority to education, health, and social services as may be provided in its appropriations law."</strong></em></td>
  <td><strong>Block grant allocation</strong> must prioritize education above all other sectors — a fiscal mandate.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"[The BTA shall] enact the priority legislation of the Bangsamoro: Bangsamoro Administrative Code, [...] Bangsamoro Education Code [...]"</strong></em></td>
  <td>The <strong>Education Code</strong> is one of the 7 priority codes mandated during the transition. <strong>Fulfilled by BAA 18.</strong></td>
</tr>

<tr>
  <td><em>Talab al-'Ilm</em> (Obligation to Seek Knowledge)</td>
  <td>Prophetic tradition: <em>"Seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim"</em> (Ibn Majah). Islamic tradition treats education as a religious <strong>obligation</strong>, not merely a right.</td>
  <td>Provides the Islamic basis for the Bangsamoro Government's education mandate. The BOL's designation of education as <em><strong>"a top priority"</strong></em> (Art. IX, Sec. 16) aligns with this principle.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td><em>Maqasid al-Shari'ah</em> (Objectives of Islamic Law)</td>
  <td>The five objectives include <strong>preservation of intellect</strong> (<em>hifz al-'aql</em>) — education is the primary means of fulfilling this objective.</td>
  <td>Legislation promoting education serves a <strong>foundational Shari'ah objective</strong>. Policies that restrict or undermine education access conflict with this principle.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>BOL Art. IX, Sec. 18</td>
  <td>Mandates the <strong>Madaris educational system</strong> and integration of <em><strong>"Islamic and Arabic studies for Muslim pupils and students in public schools."</strong></em></td>
  <td>The BOL itself embeds Shari'ah educational principles by requiring Islamic and Arabic studies in the public school curriculum. The Madaris system provides a <strong>parallel educational track</strong> rooted in Islamic educational tradition.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td><em>Waqf</em> (Islamic Endowment)</td>
  <td>The traditional Islamic mechanism for funding educational institutions. A <em>waqf</em> is a permanent charitable endowment whose income funds specified beneficiaries — historically, <em>madaris</em> and schools were primary waqf beneficiaries.</td>
  <td>A Madaris Development Act could codify <em>waqf</em> as a funding mechanism for Islamic educational institutions, providing sustainable financing beyond government appropriations.</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over education and skills training?**

A. Article V, Section 2(v) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"education and skills training"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is among the **most extensively elaborated** powers in the entire organic law — Article IX, Sections 14 through 20, dedicates seven sections to defining the scope and architecture of Bangsamoro education, covering basic education, higher education, vocational and technical education, nonformal and special education, Madaris education, physical education, indigenous peoples' education, and tribal university systems.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Eight provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. IX, Sec. 16 is the core provision. It states: ***"It shall be a top priority of the Bangsamoro Government to establish, maintain, and support a complete and integrated system of quality education, which shall be a subsystem of the national education system. The Bangsamoro Government shall develop an educational framework relevant and responsive to the needs, ideals, and aspirations of the Bangsamoro people. For this purpose, the Bangsamoro Government shall conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government."*** This section also mandates **peace education** at all levels, **supervision of private schools** with guaranteed representation for sectarian institutions, and continued support for the **Mindanao State University System**.[^2]

   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 17 extends the power to ***"vocational, technical, nonformal, and special education"*** targeting six populations — ***"the poor, illiterate, out-of-school youth, persons with special needs, disadvantaged, and senior citizens"*** — with the same national minimum standards requirement.[^3]

   (3) Art. IX, Sec. 18 mandates the **Madaris educational system** — ***"In coordination with the Department of Education, Commission on Higher Education, and Technical Education and Skills Development Authority, the Bangsamoro Government shall establish, maintain, and supervise Madaris education."*** The **CSC** retains authority over Madaris teacher qualifications. Parliament must ***"enact legislation to develop and strengthen the Madaris educational system."***[^4]

   (4) Art. IX, Sec. 19 contains **two legislative mandates**: create a ***"tribal university system"*** for non-Moro indigenous peoples, and pass a law to ***"recognize and support the indigenous peoples' educational system."*** **Both remain unfulfilled as of BAA 89.**[^5]

   (5) Art. IX, Sec. 20 mandates an ***"integrated and comprehensive physical education program"*** and the creation of a **Bangsamoro Sports Commission** (fulfilled by BAA 12).[^6]

   (6) Art. IX, Sec. 14 protects children's right to education: ***"Their education and development, both physical and mental, shall be fully addressed."***[^7]

   (7) Art. XII, Sec. 19 mandates the ***"highest budgetary priority to education, health, and social services"*** in the block grant allocation.[^8]

   (8) Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) designates the ***"Bangsamoro Education Code"*** as one of the 7 priority codes — fulfilled by BAA 18.[^9]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **four express legislative mandates**:

   (1) Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a): ***"Bangsamoro Education Code"*** — named priority code. **Fulfilled by BAA 18.**

   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 18: ***"The Parliament shall enact legislation to develop and strengthen the Madaris educational system"*** — a standalone legislative mandate for Madaris education, distinct from the Education Code.

   (3) Art. IX, Sec. 19 (para. 1): ***"The Parliament shall create a tribal university system"*** — **unfulfilled.**

   (4) Art. IX, Sec. 19 (para. 2): ***"The Parliament shall pass a law to recognize and support the indigenous peoples' educational system"*** — **unfulfilled.**

   Additionally, Art. IX, Sec. 20 mandates creation of a **Bangsamoro Sports Commission** — **fulfilled by BAA 12.**

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places five types of limitations:

   (1) **National minimum standards** (Art. IX, Sec. 16 and 17): The Bangsamoro education system must ***"conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government."*** This applies to both basic education and vocational/technical/nonformal/special education. BARMM may exceed national standards but may not fall below them.

   (2) **Subsystem status** (Art. IX, Sec. 16): The education system is ***"a subsystem of the national education system"*** — not an independent system. This means DepEd, CHED, and TESDA standards provide the floor.

   (3) **Interagency coordination** (Art. IX, Sec. 18): Madaris education must be established ***"in coordination with the Department of Education, Commission on Higher Education, and Technical Education and Skills Development Authority."***

   (4) **CSC authority over Madaris teachers** (Art. IX, Sec. 18): ***"The Civil Service Commission shall promulgate rules and regulations to set the standards for their qualifications, appointments, and promotions"*** — MBHTE does not have sole authority over Madaris teacher qualifications.

   (5) **Charter protection** (Art. IX, Sec. 16): Existing schools ***"shall be governed by their respective charters"*** — the Bangsamoro Government cannot unilaterally alter the charters of SUCs and other institutions existing at the BOL's effectivity.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to education and skills training?**

A. Five categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."***[^10]

   (2) **Right to Education (Art. XIV, Sec. 1)**: ***"The State shall protect and promote the right of all citizens to quality education at all levels and shall take appropriate steps to make such education accessible to all."*** This is the foundational constitutional right that the BOL's education power serves.[^11]

   (3) **Education System (Art. XIV, Sec. 2)**: The State shall: ***"(1) Establish, maintain, and support a complete, adequate, and integrated system of education relevant to the needs of the people and society; (2) Establish and maintain a system of free public education in the elementary and high school levels [...] (3) Establish and maintain a system of scholarship grants, student loan programs, subsidies [...] (4) Encourage non-formal, informal, and indigenous learning systems [...] (5) Provide adult citizens, the disabled, and out-of-school youth with training in civics, vocational efficiency, and other skills."***[^12]

   (4) **Budgetary Priority (Art. XIV, Sec. 5(5))**: ***"The State shall assign the highest budgetary priority to education."*** Both the Constitution and the BOL (Art. XII, Sec. 19) mandate education as the highest budgetary priority — this is a dual constitutional-organic law requirement.[^13]

   (5) **Religious Instruction (Art. XIV, Sec. 3(3))**: ***"At the option expressed in writing by the parents or guardians, religion shall be allowed to be taught to their children or wards in public elementary and high schools within the regular class hours."*** This provision interacts with the BOL's mandate for Islamic and Arabic studies in public schools (Art. IX, Sec. 18 para. 2) and Res. 481 (urging MBHTE to allow teaching of religion).[^14]

**Q. What constitutional mandates for education legislation or institutions exist?**

A. Art. XIV, Sec. 2 mandates a **complete, adequate, and integrated system of education** — this is the national obligation that the BOL's education power implements at the regional level. Art. XIV, Sec. 4(1) recognizes ***"the complementary roles of public and private institutions"*** and mandates ***"reasonable supervision and regulation of all educational institutions"*** — MBHTE's regulatory authority over private schools (BOL Art. IX, Sec. 16 para. 4-5) is an exercise of this constitutional power. Art. XIV, Sec. 5(2) guarantees **academic freedom** in all institutions of higher learning — any Bangsamoro higher education legislation must protect this guarantee.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for education?**

A. The national framework establishes a comprehensive system: **RA 10533** (Enhanced Basic Education Act/K-12) sets the curriculum framework; **RA 7722** (Higher Education Act) creates CHED; **RA 7796** (TESDA Act) governs technical-vocational education; **RA 9155** (Governance of Basic Education Act) establishes DepEd's governance framework; **RA 10931** (Universal Access to Quality Tertiary Education Act) provides free tuition in SUCs; **RA 11650** (Inclusive Education Act) mandates services for learners with disabilities; **RA 4670** (Magna Carta for Public School Teachers) protects teacher rights; **RA 10157** (Kindergarten Education Act) institutionalizes kindergarten; and **RA 9293** (Teachers Professionalization Act) governs teacher licensing through PRC. Together, these laws establish the **minimum standards floor** that the BOL requires BARMM to meet.[^15][^16][^17][^18][^19][^20][^21][^22][^23]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national education laws apply within BARMM as minimum standards:

   (1) **RA 10533** (K-12) — sets the basic education curriculum framework. BARMM conforms and adds Islamic/Arabic studies.
   (2) **RA 7722** (CHED) — CHED minimum standards for higher education apply. BARMM offices of CHED operate within the region.
   (3) **RA 7796** (TESDA) — TESDA training regulations apply as the TVET floor.
   (4) **RA 10931** (Universal Access) — free tuition applies directly to SUCs in BARMM.
   (5) **RA 11650** (Inclusive Education) — applies directly per Res. 372 and 480.
   (6) **RA 4670** (Magna Carta for Teachers) — applies directly.
   (7) **RA 9293** (Teacher Professionalization) — PRC retains teacher licensing authority nationwide.
   (8) **EO 174** (Expanded Career Progression for Teachers) — applies directly.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for education?**

A. Five national agencies retain authority within BARMM: (1) **DepEd** — sets the national K-12 standards that BARMM must conform to, and coordinates on Madaris education per Art. IX, Sec. 18; (2) **CHED** — sets higher education minimum standards; BOL gives private school representatives participation rights in BARMM CHED offices; (3) **TESDA** — sets technical-vocational training regulations and coordinates on Madaris education; (4) **CSC** — promulgates rules for Madaris teacher qualifications per Art. IX, Sec. 18; (5) **PRC** — retains teacher licensure authority. No intergovernmental body under Art. VI is specifically created for education, but coordination is built into the subsystem relationship.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title II establishes MBHTE's mandate, powers, functions, and organizational structure.[^24]

**Q. What are MBHTE's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title II defines MBHTE comprehensively:

   **Declaration of Policies** (Sec. 1): The Bangsamoro Government shall ***"promote and protect the constitutional right of all to quality and accessible education at all levels and to establish, maintain and support a complete, adequate, and integrated system of education relevant and responsive to the needs, ideals and aspirations of the Bangsamoro People."*** Twelve policy objectives are enumerated, including: (a) promoting the Bangsamoro peoples' distinct historical identity; (b-g) establishing, maintaining, and supervising education systems across childhood, basic, alternative learning, higher, technical, and Madrasah tracks; (h) encouraging lifelong learning; (i) developing qualifications frameworks; (j) establishing indigenous education; (k) mobilizing resources and financing; and (l) ensuring moral governance in education.[^24]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): MBHTE is ***"primarily responsible for the formulation, planning, implementation, and coordination of the policies, plans, programs, and projects in the areas of formal and non-formal education at all levels"*** and shall ***"supervise all educational institutions, both public and private."***[^25]

   **Powers and Functions** (Sec. 3) — 15 enumerated functions:

   - (a) Formulate, plan, implement, and coordinate policies for: (1) elementary and secondary, physical education and sports, international education, private education, in both school and *Madrasah* systems; (2) non-formal education in school and *Madrasah* systems; (3) higher education, including Islamic higher education; (4) technical education and skills development; (5) indigenous people's education and tribal university system; (6) foreign and locally assisted projects
   - (b) Establish, maintain, and support a complete and integrated system of quality education as a subsystem of the national education system
   - (c) Develop an educational framework relevant and responsive to the needs, ideals, and aspirations of the Bangsamoro people
   - (d) Institutionalize peace education in all levels of education
   - (e) Supervise and regulate private schools, including sectarian and non-sectarian institutions at any level
   - (f) Establish, maintain, and supervise *Madaris* education in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region
   - (g) Ensure the integration in elementary and high school curricula the teaching of Islamic and Arabic studies for Muslim pupils and students in public schools
   - (h) Develop and maintain an integrated and comprehensive physical education program
   - (i) Develop appropriate curricula, quality textbooks, and teaching materials
   - (j) Establish linkages with institutions with track records of excellence for pre-service and in-service teacher training
   - (k) Exercise powers of selection, recruitment, appointment, and promotion of teaching and non-teaching personnel
   - (l) Determine organizational components and approve staffing patterns of divisions, districts, and schools
   - (m) Promulgate rules and regulations for administration, control, supervision, and regulation of basic, higher, and technical educational institutions, including *Madaris*
   - (n) Evaluate all schools division superintendents and assistant division superintendents
   - (o) Perform such other functions as may be provided by law[^26]

   **Organizational Structure** (Sec. 5-10): MBHTE consists of the Ministry Proper (Minister + Deputy Minister), **four Directorate-Generals** — (a) Basic Education; (b) Higher Education; (c) Technical Education; (d) *Madaris* Education — and support services including Legal and Legislative Liaison Division, Internal Audit Division, Information and Communications Division, Planning Division, and Administrative and Finance Services.[^27]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **Four standalone BAAs** have been enacted:

   (1) **BAA 18** (Bangsamoro Education Code) — the comprehensive education framework fulfilling the Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) priority code mandate.

   (2) **BAA 40** (Bangsamoro Science High School System) — creates the BSHS system for science-oriented secondary education, cross-implementing power (uu) science and technology.

   (3) **BAA 50** (Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies) — establishes BKIS as a dedicated institution for Islamic higher education, implementing the Madaris higher education dimension.

   (4) **BAA 12** (Bangsamoro Sports Commission) — creates BSC pursuant to Art. IX, Sec. 20, implementing the physical education and sports dimension.

**Q. Have parliamentary resolutions signaled continuing legislative interest?**

A. Yes. **Eighteen resolutions** demonstrate robust parliamentary oversight and legislative interest: Res. 81 (COVID impact assessment), Res. 101 (Prophet Muhammad's teachings in curriculum), Res. 102 (SPC conversion to state university), Res. 342 (BARMMAA Meet), Res. 372 and 480 (inclusive education/RA 11650), Res. 466 (MSU-Marawi recognition), Res. 478 (anti-hazing for HEIs), Res. 479 (MSU chancellor-regents), Res. 481 (teaching religion in public schools), Res. 482 (MOST scholarship), Res. 501 (MSU College of Law), Res. 502 (incentives for families with multiple students), Res. 503 (Madrasah database), Res. 506 (hygiene kits for public high schools), and Res. 532-534 (MBHTE reports on performance, textbooks, scholarships). This is among the **highest resolution counts** for any single power.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How do national education agencies currently exercise authority within BARMM?**

A. National agencies operate in BARMM through the **subsystem relationship** established by the BOL:

   (1) **DepEd** — maintains its organizational presence in BARMM through schools division offices. Sets the K-12 curriculum standards that MBHTE must conform to. Coordinates with MBHTE on Madaris education per Art. IX, Sec. 18.

   (2) **CHED** — maintains BARMM offices for higher education regulation. Oversees SUCs, CHEIs, and private HEIs in BARMM. Exercises quality assurance and accreditation functions. BOL provides that private school representatives participate in BARMM CHED deliberations.

   (3) **TESDA** — maintains BARMM offices for TVET regulation. Sets training regulations that apply as minimum standards. Coordinates with MBHTE on Madaris education per Art. IX, Sec. 18.

   (4) **CSC** — promulgates rules for Madaris teacher qualifications, appointments, and promotions per Art. IX, Sec. 18.

   (5) **PRC** — retains teacher licensure authority through the Licensure Examination for Teachers (LET) nationwide, including BARMM.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished in exercising this power?**

A. Education is the **most heavily legislated** power in the Bangsamoro Parliament. BARMM has:

   - Enacted the **Bangsamoro Education Code** (BAA 18) — a priority code fulfilling Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)
   - Created the **Bangsamoro Science High School System** (BAA 40) — a dedicated science education track
   - Established the **Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies** (BAA 50) — Islamic higher education
   - Created the **Bangsamoro Sports Commission** (BAA 12) — fulfilling Art. IX, Sec. 20
   - Established MBHTE under **BAA 13** with a comprehensive organizational structure including four Directorate-Generals
   - Maintained active parliamentary oversight through **18 resolutions** on education topics
   - Filed **18 pending bills** addressing education access, special education, higher education governance, Madaris development, and the tribal university system

**Q. Where is BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three structural advantages:

   (1) **Unified ministry**: MBHTE integrates basic, higher, technical, and *Madaris* education under one ministry with four Directorate-Generals. Nationally, education is split across three separate agencies (DepEd, CHED, TESDA) with no unified governance.

   (2) **Madaris integration**: The *Madaris* educational system is a **core component** of BARMM education, not an add-on. MBHTE has a dedicated Directorate-General for *Madaris* Education and functions (f) and (g) that place Islamic and Arabic studies at the center of the curriculum for Muslim students.

   (3) **Peace education mandate**: The BOL's requirement to ***"institutionalize peace education in all levels of education"*** gives BARMM a distinct educational dimension with no equivalent in the national system.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. Despite having the most comprehensive education legislative portfolio of any power, significant gaps remain: (1) the **tribal university system** mandated by Art. IX, Sec. 19 has not been established; (2) **indigenous peoples' educational system** recognition and integration (Art. IX, Sec. 19 para. 2) remains unlegislated; (3) higher education governance lacks a **dedicated regulatory body** — Bill 417 proposes a Bangsamoro Commission on Higher Education; (4) **special education** lacks comprehensive standalone legislation; (5) the **Madaris educational system** could benefit from a standalone development act with dedicated funding mechanisms beyond what BAA 18 provides.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Education has a **profound Shari'ah dimension**. In Islamic tradition, seeking knowledge (*talab al-'ilm*) is a religious obligation — the Prophet Muhammad stated that ***"seeking knowledge is an obligation upon every Muslim."*** The *maqasid al-shari'ah* (objectives of Islamic law) include **preservation of intellect** (*hifz al-'aql*), which Islamic jurisprudence treats as one of five foundational values. The BOL directly embeds this dimension through Art. IX, Sec. 18, which mandates the **Madaris educational system** and integration of ***"Islamic and Arabic studies for Muslim pupils and students in public schools."*** This is not supplementary — it is a core obligation of the Bangsamoro education system.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Three principles are directly relevant:

   (1) ***Talab al-'Ilm*** (obligation to seek knowledge) — provides the theological basis for the BOL's designation of education as ***"a top priority."*** Any legislation that restricts education access for Bangsamoro residents would conflict with this principle.

   (2) ***Hifz al-'Aql*** (preservation of intellect) — one of the five *maqasid al-shari'ah*. Education legislation serves this foundational objective. The Bangsamoro Education Code (BAA 18) and Madaris system are instruments for fulfilling this objective.

   (3) ***Waqf*** (Islamic endowment) — the traditional mechanism for funding *madaris* and Islamic educational institutions. A Madaris Development Act could codify *waqf* as a supplementary funding mechanism for Islamic educational institutions, providing sustainable financing beyond annual government appropriations.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) does not specifically address education. However, **BOL Art. X, Sec. 4** authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah-based laws on matters not covered by PD 1083. Since education governance is not addressed in PD 1083, Parliament has authority to incorporate Shari'ah educational principles into legislation under this provision. The Madaris mandate (Art. IX, Sec. 18) is the primary Shari'ah-education intersection in the BOL.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can BARMM diverge from national education law?**

A. BARMM's divergence is **explicitly constrained** by the national minimum standards requirement. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. IX, Sec. 16 and 17 both require the Bangsamoro education system to ***"conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government."*** This is a **floor, not a ceiling** — BARMM can go above national standards but not below them.

   The **permissible divergence** is substantial despite this constraint:

   (1) **Curriculum additions**: BARMM can and does add Islamic and Arabic studies, peace education, and Bangsamoro history and identity education to the national K-12 framework — these exceed rather than contradict national standards.

   (2) **Institutional design**: BARMM's unified MBHTE (integrating basic, higher, technical, and Madaris education) diverges from the national three-agency model (DepEd/CHED/TESDA). The BOL authorizes this through Art. IX, Sec. 16 and BAA 13.

   (3) **Madaris system**: The parallel Madaris educational system has no national equivalent at this scale. BARMM can develop Madaris standards, curricula, and teacher development pathways that go well beyond the national framework.

   (4) **Tribal university system**: Art. IX, Sec. 19 mandates a distinct institutional form — a tribal university — that does not exist in the national system.

   (5) **Higher education commission**: PB 417 proposes a Bangsamoro Commission on Higher Education — creating a regional counterpart to CHED. This would diverge from the centralized national higher education regulatory model.

**Q. What are the limits of divergence?**

A. The limits are defined by: (1) the **national minimum standards** floor (Art. IX, Sec. 16 and 17); (2) the **subsystem status** — BARMM education remains part of the national system, not separate from it; (3) **CSC authority** over Madaris teacher qualifications (Art. IX, Sec. 18); (4) **charter protection** for existing schools (Art. IX, Sec. 16 para. 3); (5) **PRC teacher licensing** — a national function not devolved to BARMM; and (6) the **constitutional floor** (Art. XIV) as established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), particularly academic freedom (Art. XIV, Sec. 5(2)) and the right to education (Art. XIV, Sec. 1).

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Three areas of jurisdictional overlap exist:

   (1) **Higher education regulation**: CHED retains national regulatory authority while MBHTE exercises supervisory and regulatory power under BAA 13. PB 417 (Bangsamoro Commission on Higher Education) would create a dedicated BARMM higher education regulatory body, but the jurisdictional relationship with CHED — who accredits, who sets standards, who regulates — needs explicit delineation.

   (2) **Madaris teacher qualifications**: Art. IX, Sec. 18 assigns the CSC to promulgate rules for Madaris teacher qualifications, while MBHTE exercises general powers over teacher selection, recruitment, and appointment (BAA 13, Sec. 3(k)). The boundary between CSC standard-setting and MBHTE appointment is not clearly drawn.

   (3) **Teacher licensing**: PRC retains national teacher licensing authority. MBHTE appoints and promotes teachers but cannot issue professional licenses. This dual system may create challenges for Madaris teachers who hold Islamic educational credentials but may not satisfy PRC licensing requirements.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Eighteen pending PBs address various gaps (see Pending Bills table above). The most significant gap-closing PBs are: **PB 274** (tribal university — would fulfill Art. IX, Sec. 19); **PB 417** (Bangsamoro Commission on Higher Education — would address the higher education governance gap); **PB 367 and PB 386** (SPED centers — would address the special education gap); **PB 168 and PB 420** (Madrasah Fund and Boarding Madrasah — would strengthen Madaris infrastructure); and **PB 253** (TESD Centers in every municipality — would expand TVET access).

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 9 (Social Services) identifies education as a centerpiece of social development — targeting improved access to quality basic education, reduction of functional illiteracy, and expansion of TVET to address unemployment. Chapter 8 (Technology and Innovation) depends on a robust education system to produce the skilled workforce for Bangsamoro technological development. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 2 (Human Capital Development)**, depends directly on full exercise of this power — education is the primary vehicle for building the human capital that all other development pillars require.[^28]

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without the **tribal university system** (Art. IX, Sec. 19), non-Moro indigenous peoples in BARMM lack access to higher education institutions designed for their needs — a BOL mandate that directly serves inclusive development. Without a **dedicated higher education commission** (PB 417), BARMM higher education governance operates through MBHTE's general mandate without the specialized institutional structure needed to expand quality higher education. Without comprehensive **special education legislation** (PBs 255, 367, 386), learners with disabilities lack the institutional infrastructure mandated by both the BOL (Art. IX, Sec. 17) and national law (RA 11650). Without a **standalone Madaris Development Act** with dedicated funding, the Madaris system — which serves a unique and essential function in Bangsamoro education — depends on general appropriations without the institutional depth that a standalone BAA would provide. The BDP's human capital development targets for 2028 depend on addressing these gaps during the remaining transition period.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why is education one of the most extensively legislated powers in the BOL?**

A. Education receives more detailed treatment in the BOL than almost any other power because: (1) it is designated as ***"a top priority"*** of the Bangsamoro Government (Art. IX, Sec. 16) — language no other power receives; (2) the Constitution (Art. XIV, Sec. 5(5)) independently mandates ***"the highest budgetary priority to education"*** — creating a dual constitutional-organic law priority; (3) the BOL dedicates **seven sections** (Art. IX, Sec. 14-20) to education and skills training — more than any other power; (4) the Education Code is one of only **7 priority codes** mandated during the transition (Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)); and (5) education is central to the Bangsamoro people's aspiration for self-determination — the ability to develop an educational framework ***"relevant and responsive to the needs, ideals, and aspirations of the Bangsamoro people"*** is among the most identity-affirming powers in the entire BOL.

**Q. How does the MBHTE's unified structure compare with the national three-agency model?**

A. The national government splits education governance across three independent agencies — **DepEd** (basic education), **CHED** (higher education), and **TESDA** (technical-vocational) — each with its own charter, budget, and regulatory authority. BARMM chose a **unified model**: MBHTE integrates all three functions under a single ministry with four Directorate-Generals (Basic Education, Higher Education, Technical Education, and *Madaris* Education). The unified model has advantages: (a) coherent education policy across all levels; (b) integrated curriculum development from basic through higher education; (c) the Madaris system as a fourth pillar equal in institutional standing to the other three. However, PB 417 (Bangsamoro Commission on Higher Education) signals that the **higher education function may need institutional separation** — mirroring the national model's recognition that higher education regulation requires a specialized body distinct from basic education administration.

**Q. What is the significance of the tribal university mandate (Art. IX, Sec. 19)?**

A. Art. IX, Sec. 19 is a **mandatory legislative directive** — ***"The Parliament shall create a tribal university system."*** The use of "shall" makes this obligatory, not discretionary. It represents the BOL's commitment to the **non-Moro indigenous peoples** within BARMM — Teduray, Lambangian, B'laan, Manobo, Higaonon, and other IP communities whose educational needs differ from the Bangsamoro Moro majority. PB 274 (converting Upi Agricultural School into Bangsamoro Tribal University) would partially fulfill this mandate, but the BOL envisions a "system" (implying multiple institutions or campuses), not a single converted school. The second mandate — recognizing ***"the indigenous peoples' educational system"*** — is broader, requiring integration of IP customary education into the Bangsamoro educational framework. Both mandates remain unfulfilled as of BAA 89, making this the most significant unaddressed legislative obligation within this power.

**Q. How does the Madaris system interact with the broader education system?**

A. The BOL creates a **dual-track** education model — the formal school system and the *Madaris* system operate in parallel. Art. IX, Sec. 18 requires the Bangsamoro Government to ***"establish, maintain, and supervise Madaris education"*** while also requiring ***"the integration in the elementary and high school education curricula the teaching of Islamic and Arabic studies."*** This means Islamic education operates at two levels: (a) the **Madaris system** as a standalone educational track with its own Directorate-General within MBHTE; and (b) **Islamic and Arabic studies integrated** into the regular public school curriculum for Muslim students. The BAA 13 mandate (Sec. 3(a)) confirms this dual structure by placing Madaris in both the formulation/coordination function (items 1-2) and as a dedicated supervision function (item f). BAA 50 (Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies) extends the Madaris track into higher education. The challenge is ensuring articulation between the two tracks — students should be able to move between Madaris and formal education without losing credit or qualification recognition. BAA 13, Sec. 1(i) addresses this through ***"standards for qualification outcomes"*** and ***"pathways and equivalencies."***
