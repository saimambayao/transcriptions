> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(qq)** — "Public works and infrastructure;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The Bangsamoro Organic Law grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over public works and infrastructure as one of 55 enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2(qq). This power covers the planning, design, construction, and maintenance of roads, bridges, buildings, and other physical infrastructure within the Bangsamoro region — but with a significant qualifier that distinguishes it from most other BOL powers.

Article XIII, Section 37 is the primary elaboration, and it contains the BOL's most explicit division of responsibility between the Bangsamoro Government and the National Government for any single power area. The provision states: "The National Government shall fund and implement the construction and maintenance of national roads, bridges, water supply and services, and flood control and irrigation systems and for the maintenance of existing airports, seaports, and wharves in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region." It further declares: "Nationally-funded infrastructure projects shall be implemented by the National Government." This means the Department of Public Works and Highways (DPWH) retains primary authority over national road construction and maintenance within BARMM, while the Ministry of Public Works (MOPW) handles regional and local infrastructure. For water supply, flood control, and irrigation connecting to facilities outside BARMM, the BOL requires "cooperation and coordination between the Bangsamoro Government and the appropriate national or local government bodies."

Article VI, Section 6 creates the Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board, tasked with "coordinating and synchronizing national and Bangsamoro infrastructure development plans." This board includes representatives from BARMM ministries and national agencies, with the Secretary of Public Works and Highways as a mandatory member.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Ministry of Public Works (MOPW) is the lead agency, with a budget allocation under BAA 85 (General Appropriations Act, FY 2026), Chapter XI. MOPW operates under its BAA 13 (Administrative Code) general mandate for planning, design, construction, and maintenance of public infrastructure within BARMM. BAA 85 also includes Chapter XXXVI — the Infrastructure Modernization and Public Asset Construction and Transformation (IMPACT) fund — a dedicated special purpose fund for infrastructure development.

No standalone BAA has been enacted specifically for public works and infrastructure. MOPW operates entirely under its Administrative Code mandate and the annual appropriations acts. In practice, the division of labor with DPWH is a constant governance challenge: DPWH implements nationally-funded projects (including national roads and bridges within BARMM), while MOPW handles regionally-funded infrastructure. This dual-authority arrangement has generated friction, as evidenced by Resolution 37 (objecting to a House Resolution creating a DPWH in BARMM) and Resolution 346 (supporting creation of DPWH District Engineering Offices in BARMM). Resolution 547 further urged the creation of a DPWH Regional Office for BARMM, indicating ongoing institutional coordination challenges.

Parliament has filed multiple infrastructure-related bills, including PB No. 183 (Acceleration of Infrastructure Development Projects), PB No. 343 (Infrastructure and Connectivity Development), PB No. 296 (Emergency Runway Infrastructure), and PB No. 412 (Cotabato Airport Upgrade and Modernization). Resolution 526 called for formulation of a long-term 15-year infrastructure development plan.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

This power has the most direct National Government involvement of any BOL power. Article XIII, Section 37 expressly reserves nationally-funded infrastructure to the National Government. DPWH implements national road and bridge construction within BARMM under its own appropriations through the General Appropriations Act. The Bangsamoro Government "shall submit proposals to the appropriate national government agency for the inclusion of the cost of such maintenance in the latter's budget." This creates a unique dependency: even though BARMM has the power over public works, major national infrastructure within its territory is funded and implemented by the National Government.

The Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board (Art. VI, Sec. 6) is the coordination mechanism. RA 10752 (Right-of-Way Act) applies to infrastructure projects requiring land acquisition. PD 1096 (National Building Code) and PD 1185 (Fire Code) provide construction and safety standards that apply nationwide, including in BARMM. RA 9184 (Government Procurement Reform Act) governs procurement for all government infrastructure projects, including those of the Bangsamoro Government.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The absence of a Bangsamoro Public Works and Infrastructure Code leaves MOPW without a comprehensive legal framework for regional infrastructure governance. Key gaps include: no codified standards for infrastructure design and construction specific to BARMM's geography (island provinces, marshlands, conflict-affected areas), no clear regulatory framework for the division of responsibilities between MOPW and DPWH, no regional infrastructure master plan with legislative force, and no mechanism to ensure that nationally-funded projects align with Bangsamoro development priorities.

A Bangsamoro Public Works and Infrastructure Act would codify: MOPW's regulatory authority and organizational structure, regional infrastructure classification (what constitutes a "regional" versus "national" road), procurement and project management standards adapted to BARMM conditions, a long-term infrastructure development plan with legislative mandate, coordination protocols with DPWH and other national agencies, and standards for infrastructure in conflict-affected and disaster-prone areas.

PB No. 364 (Right-of-Way Indemnification in Infrastructure Projects) addresses one specific gap in infrastructure land acquisition. The broader infrastructure governance framework remains unlegislated.

### Development and Policy Connection

BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development) identifies infrastructure as a prerequisite for all other development goals, targeting expanded road networks, improved port and airport facilities, and increased access to basic services. Chapter 3 (Spatial Development Strategy) frames infrastructure within the region's spatial development plan. The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028) prioritizes infrastructure under its development pillar, recognizing that poor roads, bridges, and port facilities remain the primary barrier to economic integration of BARMM's island and mainland areas. Without a comprehensive infrastructure law, MOPW cannot enforce regional standards, and the coordination gap with DPWH continues to result in infrastructure projects that do not align with Bangsamoro development priorities.
