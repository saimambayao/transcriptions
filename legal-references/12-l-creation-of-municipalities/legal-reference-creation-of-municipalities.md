# (l) Creation, Division, Merger, Abolition or Alteration of Boundaries of Municipalities and Barangays

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(l)** — ***"Creation, division, merger, abolition or alteration of boundaries of municipalities and barangays;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(l) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. VI, Sec. 10 requires ***"the criteria laid down in Republic Act No. 7160, as amended, and other national laws shall be satisfied"***; plebiscite required; express prohibition on creating legislative districts |

| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 1 (BARMM Extended Transition); Chapter 5 (Governance and Institutional Development) |
| **Priority Code** | Local Government Code — BAA 49 enacted (one of the 7 priority codes in Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)) |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over municipality and barangay creation?**

A. Article V, Section 2(l) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"creation, division, merger, abolition or alteration of boundaries of municipalities and barangays"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This covers the full range of LGU restructuring at the **municipal and barangay levels** — creating new units, splitting existing ones, combining them, eliminating them, or redrawing their boundaries.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Art. VI, Sec. 10 provides the detailed operational framework:

   (1) ***"The Parliament may create, divide, merge, abolish, or substantially alter boundaries of municipalities or barangays in accordance with a law enacted by the Parliament."*** This vests the power in the **Parliament** as a legislative function — each action requires a separate enacted law.[^2]

   (2) Two mandatory requirements: ***"the criteria laid down in Republic Act No. 7160, as amended, and other national laws shall be satisfied"*** and ***"it shall be approved by a majority of the votes cast in a plebiscite in the political units directly affected."*** These are the **RA 7160 criteria** and the **plebiscite requirement** — neither of which can be waived by Parliament.[^2]

   (3) Congressional coordination for legislative districts: ***"When such acts require the creation of a legislative district, the Bangsamoro Government shall cooperate and coordinate with the National Government through the Philippine Congress-Bangsamoro Parliament Forum."***[^2]

   (4) Express prohibition: ***"Nothing in this Organic Law shall be construed to allow the Bangsamoro Government to create legislative districts."***[^2]

   (5) LGU privilege protection: ***"The privileges already enjoyed by local government units under Republic Act No. 7160 ... shall not be diminished."***[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. VI, Sec. 10 (para. 1) mandates ***"a Bangsamoro local government code to be enacted by the Parliament."*** This is one of the 7 priority codes listed in **Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)**. This mandate has been fulfilled by **BAA 49** (Local Governance Code).[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not create a specific body solely for municipality/barangay creation. However, it mandates the **Bangsamoro local government code** which provides the institutional framework. BAA 13 establishes **MILG** as the ministry exercising general supervision over LGUs, and BAA 13, Sec. 14-28 creates the **Special Geographic Area Development Authority** to manage the 63 SGA barangays — which eventually became the 8 new SGA municipalities (BAA 41-48).[^3][^4]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Four express limitations:

   (1) **RA 7160 criteria**: income, population, and land area thresholds must be satisfied — this is among the BOL's most explicit national law consistency requirements.[^2]

   (2) **Plebiscite requirement**: majority vote approval in directly affected political units.[^2]

   (3) **Legislative district prohibition**: BARMM cannot create legislative districts under any circumstance.[^2]

   (4) **Non-diminution of LGU privileges**: existing LGU privileges under RA 7160 cannot be reduced.[^2]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to municipality and barangay creation?**

A. Three categories:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates this. This is the universal constitutional boundary.[^5]

   (2) **LGU Creation (Art. X, Sec. 10)**: ***"No province, city, municipality, or barangay may be created, divided, merged, abolished, or its boundary substantially altered, except in accordance with the criteria established in the local government code and subject to approval by a majority of the votes cast in a plebiscite in the political units directly affected."*** The BOL's requirements in Art. VI, Sec. 10 directly mirror this constitutional mandate.[^6]

   (3) **Congressional District Creation (Art. VI, Sec. 5)**: Legislative district apportionment is a congressional power. The BOL's prohibition on BARMM creating legislative districts directly implements this constitutional allocation.[^7]

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for this area?**

A. Art. X, Sec. 10 establishes the constitutional mandate that all LGU creation requires (a) compliance with **local government code criteria** and (b) **plebiscite approval**. Both are non-waivable. The BOL faithfully reproduces these requirements in Art. VI, Sec. 10.[^6]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for LGU creation?**

A. **RA 7160** (Local Government Code, 1991) sets the criteria: for **municipality creation** — at least PhP 2.5 million average annual income for the last two consecutive years (Sec. 442), population of at least 25,000, and a contiguous land area of at least 50 square kilometers (with exceptions for islands and cities); for **barangay creation** — at least 2,000 inhabitants for a metropolitan city/city barangay or 5,000 for a municipal barangay (Sec. 386). RA 7160 also governs mergers, divisions, and boundary alterations, requiring compliance with these thresholds and plebiscite approval.[^8]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. (1) **RA 7160** — applies **directly** by express BOL mandate (Art. VI, Sec. 10). Its criteria are the binding standard for all BARMM municipality and barangay creation. (2) **RA 11550** — the national law dividing Maguindanao into two provinces. Applies directly as a congressional act. Demonstrates that **province-level** restructuring within BARMM requires congressional action, not just Parliament legislation.[^8][^9]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. The **Commission on Elections (COMELEC)** — through its Bangsamoro Electoral Office — conducts the required **plebiscites** for municipality and barangay creation. COMELEC's authority over plebiscites is constitutionally vested and cannot be transferred to BARMM. The **Department of the Interior and Local Government (DILG)** provides technical assistance on RA 7160 compliance verification, though MILG performs the primary supervisory function within BARMM.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG)** is the lead agency. BAA 13, Book V, Title I, Chapter 1, Sec. 2 defines its mandate: ***"The Ministry of the Interior and Local Government shall exercise general supervision over the constituent local governments units of the Bangsamoro Government, and ensure public safety and disaster preparedness, local autonomy, decentralization, and community empowerment."*** BAA 85, Chapter XII allocates its operational budget.[^3][^10]

**Q. What standalone legislation has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. This is one of the **most actively exercised** enumerated powers. Parliament has enacted **13 municipality creation/amendment BAAs** (BAA 41-48, 53-55, 70-71) plus the comprehensive **BAA 49** (Local Governance Code). Additional enacted bills include PB 247 (Bonggo), PB 271 (Pilintangan), PB 272 (Bayantol), and renaming bills (PB 284, 328, 380).

**Q. What are MILG's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book V, Title I, Chapter 1, Sec. 3 enumerates **8 functions**:

   - (a) ***"Advise the Chief Minister on the Government of the Day's policies, rules, regulations, and other issuances relative to the general supervision of local government units;"***
   - (b) ***"Establish and prescribe rules, regulations, and other issuances implementing laws on general supervision of local government units and on the promotion of local autonomy, and monitor compliance thereof;"***
   - (c) ***"Provide technical assistance in the preparation of regional legislation affecting local governments units;"***
   - (d) ***"Establish and prescribe plans, policies, programs, and projects to strengthen the administrative, technical, and fiscal capabilities of local government offices and personnel;"***
   - (e) ***"Implement plans, policies, programs, and project to promote public order and safety and disaster preparedness;"***
   - (f) ***"Formulate plans, policies, and programs which will meet regional and local emergencies arising from natural and human-made calamities;"***
   - (g) ***"Enforce its disciplinary authority over elective officials in accordance with the Bangsamoro Local Governance Code;"***
   - (h) ***"Perform other functions as maybe provided by law."***[^3]

   MILG's organizational structure (Sec. 9) includes: **Operation Management Services** (with Local Government Development Division, Local Government Supervision Division, Barangay and Community Affairs Division); **Interior Affairs Services** (Public Safety Division, DRRM Division); and Provincial/City field offices (Sec. 10-12).[^3]

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is MILG the correct lead institution for this power?**

A. Yes, but with a qualification. MILG exercises **general supervision** over LGUs and provides **technical assistance** on LGU creation matters. However, the actual **creation power** is legislative — it is exercised by the **Bangsamoro Parliament** through enactment of specific creation laws. MILG's role is pre-legislative (technical assistance, compliance verification with RA 7160 criteria) and post-legislative (supervision of newly created municipalities). The **SGA Development Authority** (BAA 13, Sec. 14-28), placed under MILG, manages the transition of the 63 SGA barangays.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. (1) **MILG** — lead ministry (BAA 13, BAA 85 Ch. XII); (2) **Bangsamoro Parliament** — exercises the legislative creation power; (3) **SGA Development Authority** — manages SGA barangay transition under MILG supervision (BAA 13, Sec. 15); (4) **SAMLMG** (Sustainable Assistance Mechanism for Local Moral Governance) — BAA 85, Ch. XXXIII, provides transition support funding for LGUs; (5) **Regional Chapter of Leagues of Municipalities and Barangays** — BAA 13, Sec. 13, coordinates LGU interests.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The national government retains three functions: (1) **COMELEC** conducts plebiscites for all municipality and barangay creation — this is a constitutionally-vested function that cannot be devolved; (2) **Congress** retains the power to create **provinces, cities, and legislative districts** — the BOL expressly prohibits BARMM from creating legislative districts, and province creation requires congressional action (as demonstrated by RA 11550); (3) **DILG** provides technical standards and certification on RA 7160 compliance criteria.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. BARMM has been **highly active** in this area: (1) Created **8 new municipalities** from SGA barangays (BAA 41-48), converting the 63 barangays that voted for inclusion in the 2019 plebiscite into formally constituted municipalities; (2) Created **3 additional municipalities** from existing BARMM territories (BAA 53-55); (3) Successfully conducted **plebiscites** for the SGA municipalities (commended by Res. 462); (4) Enacted **BAA 49** (Local Governance Code), fulfilling the Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) priority code mandate; (5) Renamed municipalities (Sultan Mastura to Subpangen, Datu Blah T. Sinsuat to West Upi, South Upi to Teduray Municipality); (6) Amended creation laws where needed (BAA 70-71); (7) Signaled intent to coordinate province creation through Congress (Res. 499 — Kutawato Province).

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **primary constraints** are: (1) **RA 7160 criteria** — the income, population, and land area thresholds may not reflect BARMM's unique circumstances (island barangays, conflict-affected communities with undercounted populations, LGUs with limited income due to delayed IRA transfers); (2) **No barangay creation framework** — while municipality creation has been active, no BAA or specific provisions address barangay creation or boundary alteration procedures; (3) **IRR development** — the implementing rules for BAA 49's LGU creation provisions are still being developed; (4) **Capacity building** — newly created municipalities need institutional support, staffing, and infrastructure during their transition period.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built that BARMM uses?**

A. The national system provides: **RA 7160's LGU creation criteria** — which BARMM must comply with and which serve as the objective standard; **COMELEC's plebiscite machinery** — which BARMM depends on for all creation/alteration votes; and the **DILG's LGU capacity-building programs** which complement MILG's work.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The RA 7160 criteria apply uniformly nationwide — they do not account for (1) **island and remote barangays** where populations are dispersed across maritime territories; (2) **conflict-affected communities** with undercounted populations due to displacement; (3) **IRA/ICA dependency** — newly created municipalities in BARMM depend heavily on block grant shares rather than locally generated income, making the income threshold harder to meet. The national framework also provides no guidance on how the **SGA** — a unique BARMM institutional arrangement — should transition into permanent LGUs.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. The Shari'ah dimension is **limited but relevant**. Municipality and barangay creation is primarily an administrative-territorial function. However, the *maslahah* (public interest/welfare) principle supports creating new LGUs when doing so brings government services closer to communities. The *shura* (consultation) principle aligns with the plebiscite requirement — the community's voice determines the outcome. No specific Shari'ah rules govern territorial subdivision. The BOL does not reference Shari'ah in Art. VI, Sec. 10.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Two general principles apply: (1) ***Maslahah*** — creating new municipalities serves the common good by decentralizing governance and improving public service access. (2) ***Shura*** — the plebiscite requirement ensures the directly affected population is consulted, consistent with Islamic governance principles of participatory decision-making. No subject-specific Shari'ah rules exist for territorial subdivision.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law in this area?**

A. BARMM's divergence is **significantly constrained** — this power is tightly bounded by express national criteria requirements. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL expressly requires compliance with ***"the criteria laid down in Republic Act No. 7160"*** and plebiscite approval. As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), Art. X, Sec. 10 of the Constitution independently requires the same. BARMM cannot: lower RA 7160 income, population, or land area thresholds; waive the plebiscite; or create legislative districts. BARMM **can**: determine the process for applying the criteria (through BAA 49 and its IRR), establish additional requirements beyond RA 7160's minimums, create the SGA institutional framework (no national equivalent), and adopt its own organizational framework for newly created LGUs.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different procedural standards, what are the implications?**

A. BARMM may establish its own **procedural framework** for processing municipality/barangay creation proposals (how applications are filed, who conducts the compliance review, what documentation is required) — but the **substantive criteria** (income, population, land area) must match or exceed RA 7160. Any municipality created without meeting RA 7160 criteria could face a legal challenge. Cross-boundary implications are limited since municipalities are entirely within BARMM territory — but municipalities near the SGA boundary may affect non-BARMM LGUs, requiring coordination.

**Q. What happens to national law when Parliament enacts a creation BAA?**

A. The specific creation BAA governs that municipality's establishment. RA 7160 remains the binding standard for the criteria. BAA 49 provides the governance framework for the new municipality. National laws on LGU operations (budgeting, procurement, civil service) apply suppletorily where BAA 49 is silent.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Three overlaps: (1) **Province and city creation** — the BOL grants power over municipalities and barangays but is silent on provinces and cities. RA 11550 (Maguindanao division) was enacted by Congress, suggesting province creation is congressional. But BAA 49 may address provincial governance within BARMM. (2) **Legislative district implications** — when municipality creation triggers new district needs, coordination with Congress is required but no formal protocol exists beyond the Congress-Parliament Forum. (3) **RA 7160 certification** — who certifies compliance with RA 7160 criteria (MILG or DILG?) is procedurally unclear.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. No major gaps remain in the municipality creation pipeline — this power is actively exercised. The primary gap is in **barangay creation** procedures and **IRR development** for BAA 49's LGU creation provisions.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 1 (BARMM Extended Transition) depends on municipality creation to complete the **territorial and institutional setup** of BARMM — particularly the integration of SGA communities into the formal governance framework. Chapter 5 (Governance and Institutional Development) targets strengthened LGU governance and decentralized service delivery. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 1 (Good Governance)**, depends on properly constituted LGUs to deliver responsive governance at the community level.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if remaining gaps are unaddressed?**

A. Without **IRR completion** for BAA 49's LGU creation provisions, future municipality and barangay creation proposals lack a clear procedural pathway. Without a **barangay creation framework**, communities that have outgrown their current barangay structures cannot be reorganized. Without **transition support legislation** for newly created municipalities, the 11+ new municipalities risk being under-resourced during their formative years — undermining the governance gains that motivated their creation.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why is this power limited to municipalities and barangays — not provinces and cities?**

A. Art. V, Sec. 2(l) expressly names only ***"municipalities and barangays."*** Province and city creation requires congressional action because it directly affects **legislative district apportionment** (Art. VI, Sec. 5 of the Constitution) and the **IRA/ICA formula** (national tax distribution). RA 11550 — Congress dividing Maguindanao — confirms this limitation. The BOL's express prohibition on creating legislative districts reinforces that BARMM cannot take actions that would automatically trigger new congressional districts.[^2]

**Q. How do the 8 SGA municipalities relate to this power?**

A. The 63 barangays in the Special Geographic Area (from Pikit, Pigkawayan, Carmen, Kabacan, Midsayap, and Aleosan) voted for inclusion in BARMM during the 2019 plebiscite. BAA 13, Sec. 14 constituted them as a **Special Geographic Area** under MILG supervision. The SGA Authority (BAA 13, Sec. 15) managed their transition governance. Parliament then exercised Art. V, Sec. 2(l) to create 8 permanent municipalities (BAA 41-48: Pahamuddin, Kadayangan, Nabalawag, Old Kaabakan, Kapalawan, Malidegao, Tugunan, Ligawasan). This was the **largest single exercise** of the municipality creation power, converting what was a transitional governance arrangement into permanent local government units. The plebiscites were successfully conducted and commended by Res. 462.[^4]

**Q. Can Parliament rename municipalities under this power?**

A. The BOL names five actions: ***"creation, division, merger, abolition or alteration of boundaries."*** Renaming is not expressly listed. However, Parliament has exercised renaming authority through enacted bills (PB 284 — Sultan Mastura to Subpangen; PB 328 — Datu Blah T. Sinsuat to West Upi; PB 380 — South Upi to Teduray Municipality). This is plausibly grounded in the **general legislative power** of Parliament (Art. VII, Sec. 2) and the power over ***"administrative organization"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(b)), rather than Art. V, Sec. 2(l) specifically.
