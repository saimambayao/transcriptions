# (ll) Loans, Credits, and Other Forms of Indebtedness

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ll)** — ***"Loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(ll) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) requires ***"compliance with the Constitution, relevant laws and regulations"***; sovereign-guaranty loans require ***"approval of the National Government"***; ODA requires review ***"in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182"*** (Art. ### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness?**

A. Article V, Section 2(ll) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness"*** as one of the 55 powers over which the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise authority. This is a broad grant covering: the power to **contract loans** from domestic and foreign sources; the power to **issue debt instruments** (bonds, debentures, securities, notes, obligations); the power to **manage and service** public debt; and the power to **access official development assistance**. The chapeau of Art. V, Sec. 2 provides: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines."***[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Six provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) is the primary borrowing authority. It states: ***"The Bangsamoro Government may contract loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness to finance the construction, installation, improvement, expansion, operation or maintenance of public utilities, infrastructure facilities, housing projects, acquisition of real property, implementation of other capital investment projects, and for the development and welfare of the people of the Bangsamoro."*** It further provides: ***"Subject to acceptable credit worthiness, and in compliance with the Constitution, relevant laws and regulations, such loans may be secured from domestic and foreign lending institutions, except foreign and domestic loans requiring sovereign guaranty, whether explicit or implicit, which would require the approval of the National Government."***[^2]

   (2) Art. XII, Sec. 24(b) authorizes debt instruments: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall have the authority to issue bonds, debentures, securities, collaterals, notes, obligations, and other debt papers or documents, as well as redeem or retire the same, pursuant to a law enacted by the Parliament, to finance self-liquidating and income-producing development or livelihood projects pursuant to the priorities established in its development plan."***[^3]

   (3) Art. XII, Sec. 24(c) establishes debt service ceilings: ***"the amount of appropriations for debt servicing shall not exceed twenty percent (20%) of the regular sources of revenue, excluding those coming from grants, endowments, donations, loans, foreign assistance and official development assistance"*** and ***"not more than twenty percent (20%) of the block grant shall be utilized for loan payments."***[^4]

   (4) Art. XII, Sec. 24(d) imposes a transparency requirement: ***"Any domestic or foreign-assisted loan and the purpose thereof shall be published once a week for two (2) consecutive weeks in at least one (1) newspaper of general circulation in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^5]

   (5) Art. XII, Sec. 25 governs ODA: ***"The Bangsamoro Government may avail of official development assistance, upon review and approval of the National Government in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182."***[^6]

   (6) Art. XII, Sec. 6(i) lists ***"Loans and official development assistance"*** as one of the twelve revenue sources of the Bangsamoro Government, confirming borrowing as a structural component of the fiscal framework.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. The BOL contains **one express legislative prerequisite** and **one implied mandate**:

   (1) Art. XII, Sec. 24(b) requires bond issuance to be ***"pursuant to a law enacted by the Parliament."*** This means the Bangsamoro Government **cannot issue bonds, debentures, securities, or other debt instruments** until Parliament enacts an enabling law. This is not a named code (unlike the ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** in Art. XIII, Sec. 24), but it is a functional legislative prerequisite — without it, an entire dimension of the borrowing power is locked.[^3]

   (2) Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) provides that ***"The Parliament by a vote of the majority of all its members may authorize the Chief Minister to contract such domestic or foreign loans."*** This implies that major loan contracting requires **parliamentary authorization** through a resolution or act — though the BOL does not prescribe the form of such authorization beyond the majority vote requirement.[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not create a specific body for debt management. However, Art. XII, Sec. 37-41 establishes the **Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board (IFPB)**, which oversees fiscal relations between the Bangsamoro and National governments — including the framework within which sovereign-guaranty borrowing and ODA access operate. MFBM and the Bangsamoro Treasury Office were established through **BAA 13** as the operational institutions for debt management (see Section D (Implementation)).[^8]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places five specific limitations:

   (1) **Creditworthiness and legal compliance**: Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) — ***"Subject to acceptable credit worthiness, and in compliance with the Constitution, relevant laws and regulations."***[^2]

   (2) **Sovereign guaranty restriction**: Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) — loans requiring sovereign guaranty (explicit or implicit) require ***"the approval of the National Government."***[^2]

   (3) **Debt service ceiling (revenue)**: Art. XII, Sec. 24(c) — debt servicing cannot exceed ***"twenty percent (20%) of the regular sources of revenue"*** (excluding grants, donations, loans, foreign/ODA assistance).[^4]

   (4) **Debt service ceiling (block grant)**: Art. XII, Sec. 24(c) — ***"not more than twenty percent (20%) of the block grant shall be utilized for loan payments."***[^4]

   (5) **Parliamentary authorization**: Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) — contracting domestic or foreign loans requires ***"a vote of the majority of all its members"*** of Parliament to authorize the Chief Minister. Bond issuance requires an enabling law (Art. XII, Sec. 24(b)).[^2][^3]

   Additionally, Art. VII, Sec. 37 prohibits the government from contracting loans during the **holdover period** after a no-confidence vote.[^9]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to loans, credits, and indebtedness?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** This is the **universal constitutional boundary** on all 55 powers.[^10]

   (2) **Public Debt Control (Art. VI, Sec. 24)**: ***"All appropriation, revenue or tariff bills, bills authorizing increase of the public debt, bills of local application, and private bills shall originate exclusively in the House of Representatives."*** While this applies to Congress, it reflects the constitutional principle that **public debt requires legislative authorization** — the BOL mirrors this principle by requiring parliamentary authorization for borrowing and an enabling law for bond issuance.[^11]

   (3) **No Money Clause (Art. VI, Sec. 29(1))**: ***"No money shall be paid out of the Treasury except in pursuance of an appropriation made by law."*** Debt service payments require appropriation. The BOL codifies this through Art. XII, Sec. 24(c)'s requirement that the government ***"appropriate in its annual budget the amounts sufficient to pay its incurred loans and indebtedness."***[^12]

   (4) **Due Process and Equal Protection (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, nor shall any person be denied the equal protection of the laws."*** Government borrowing that creates obligations for constituents (through taxation to service debt) must serve legitimate public purposes. Arbitrary or discriminatory debt incurrence could face constitutional challenge.[^13]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution does not guarantee a "right to borrow" — it protects **against abusive debt incurrence** by requiring legislative authorization for public debt and appropriation for expenditures. The **fiscal autonomy** of autonomous regions (Art. X, Sec. 15-20) includes the power to borrow, but this is subject to the constitutional framework, not independent of it. Citizens have a right to **transparency** in government financial operations and a right not to be burdened by debt incurred without proper authorization.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. No specific constitutional mandate requires the creation of a sub-national debt management institution. However, the constitutional principle that public debt requires legislative authorization (Art. VI, Sec. 24) and that treasury disbursements require appropriation (Art. VI, Sec. 29(1)) means that any government borrowing system must include **legislative oversight** and **budgetary control** mechanisms — both of which the BOL provides through Art. XII, Sec. 24.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for government borrowing?**

A. The national framework establishes a multi-layered system: **RA 4860** (Foreign Borrowings Act) governs sovereign borrowing and guarantees — the President is authorized to obtain foreign loans for economic development. **RA 8182** (ODA Act) creates the framework for accessing concessional development assistance. **RA 7653/RA 11211** (BSP Charter) governs monetary policy, banking regulation, and credit standards that affect the lending environment within which government borrowing occurs. **RA 7160** (Local Government Code), Sec. 296-302, provides the LGU borrowing framework — credit financing, bonds, and a 20% debt service ceiling. **RA 9474** (Lending Company Regulation Act) and **RA 9510** (Credit Information System Act) regulate private lending. Together, these laws provide: **sovereign borrowing controls**, **ODA access procedures**, **monetary and credit regulation**, **LGU borrowing standards**, and **private lending oversight** — the baseline against which Bangsamoro borrowing legislation operates.[^14][^15][^16][^17][^18]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All major national financial laws apply:

   (1) **RA 4860** — governs sovereign-guaranty borrowing. Applies **directly** by virtue of BOL Art. XII, Sec. 24(a)'s requirement of National Government approval for such loans.[^14]
   (2) **RA 8182** — governs ODA access. Applies **directly** by express BOL mandate (Art. XII, Sec. 25).[^15]
   (3) **RA 7653/RA 11211** — BSP monetary policy and banking regulation. Applies **directly** — BSP jurisdiction is nationwide and not affected by the BOL.[^16]
   (4) **RA 7160**, Sec. 296-302 — LGU borrowing framework. Applies to BARMM LGUs **suppletorily** unless modified by BAA 49.[^17]
   (5) **RA 3765** (Truth in Lending), **RA 9474** (Lending Company Regulation), **RA 9510** (Credit Information System), **RA 10693** (Microfinance NGOs), **RA 10744** (Credit Surety Fund), **RA 11901** (Agricultural Financing) — all apply **suppletorily** within BARMM.
   (6) **RA 6848** (Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank) — applies **directly** as the legislative charter of a government-owned Islamic bank with operations in BARMM.[^19]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. Three national institutions exercise authority: (1) The **Department of Finance (DOF)** manages sovereign guarantees and foreign borrowing on behalf of the National Government — any Bangsamoro Government application for sovereign guaranty under Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) must go through DOF. (2) The **Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP)** regulates all banks and lending institutions in BARMM — monetary policy, reserve requirements, and lending standards are set nationally. (3) The **National Economic and Development Authority (NEDA)** reviews ODA proposals as the national planning agency for development assistance — Bangsamoro ODA requests must pass NEDA's evaluation process under RA 8182. None of these national functions transfer to BARMM; they are inherently national in scope (sovereign guarantee, monetary policy, ODA coordination).

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management (MFBM)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title IV establishes MFBM with express authority to ***"Manage public debt"*** (Sec. 3(f)). The **Bangsamoro Treasury Office**, within MFBM (BAA 13, Sec. 10-14), is the operational unit for borrowing. BAA 85 allocates MFBM's operational budget.[^20][^21]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone borrowing code or debt management act** has been enacted as of BAA 89. However, **BAA 84** (Budget System Act) partially implements debt-related provisions — including fiscal responsibility principles requiring sustainable public debt (Sec. 5(c)), BBCC authority to recommend borrowing measures (Sec. 7(c)-(d)), and automatic appropriation for debt service interest payments with the 20% ceiling (Sec. 30(b)).[^22]

**Q. What are MFBM's mandate and functions under BAA 13 relevant to borrowing?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title IV defines MFBM comprehensively:

   **Ministry mandate** (Sec. 2): MFBM is ***"primarily mandated to evolve sound, efficient and responsive fiscal management and utilization of resources."***[^20]

   **Powers and functions** (Sec. 3) — 15 enumerated functions, of which the following are directly relevant to borrowing:
   - (f) ***"Manage public debt;"***
   - (e) ***"Act as custodian of, and manage all financial resources of Bangsamoro Government;"***
   - (j) ***"Coordinate with other Bangsamoro Ministries and Offices on matters concerning fiscal and monetary policies, credit, economic development, international finance, trade and investment;"***[^20]

   **Bangsamoro Treasury Office** (Sec. 10-14) — headed by a Bangsamoro Treasurer (SG-27) with a six-year term, consisting of four divisions: (a) Management and Reporting Division; (b) Fund Receipt and Fiscal Division; (c) Fund Transfer Division; (d) Fund Management Division. Functions (Sec. 12) include:
   - (f) ***"Formulate annual projections of revenue needs, cash position and borrowing capacity of the Bangsamoro Government;"***
   - (g) ***"Formulate and execute policies on financial management, public borrowings and capital market development;"***
   - (h) ***"Assist in the management, control and servicing of public debts from domestic or foreign sources;"***[^21]

   **Bangsamoro Finance Office** (Sec. 15-16) — functions include:
   - (f) ***"Coordinate with other Ministries and Offices on matters concerning fiscal and monetary policies, credit, economic development, international finance, trade investment;"***[^23]

**Q. What can MFBM NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. While BAA 13 gives MFBM general authority to manage public debt and the Treasury Office express functions on borrowing capacity and capital market development, the following require dedicated legislation:

   - **Bond and debt instrument issuance** — Art. XII, Sec. 24(b) expressly requires ***"a law enacted by the Parliament."*** Without this enabling law, the entire bond market dimension of borrowing is legally blocked.
   - **Sukuk (Islamic bonds)** framework — no legal definition, structure requirements, or Shari'ah-compliance standards exist in Bangsamoro law.
   - **Debt management standards** — risk limits, maturity profiles, currency composition, and refinancing guidelines are not codified.
   - **Borrowing procedures** — the process for parliamentary authorization of loans (Art. XII, Sec. 24(a)), application for sovereign guaranty, and ODA proposals is not specified beyond the BOL's bare framework.
   - **Accountability and reporting** — while BAA 84 provides some fiscal reporting, comprehensive debt disclosure and performance reporting for borrowed funds is not codified.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No adopted resolutions specifically addressing borrowing authority, debt management, bond issuance, or ODA policy have been identified.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. MFBM is the correct lead — public debt management is an inherent function of the finance ministry. BAA 13 Sec. 3(f) expressly assigns the ***"Manage public debt"*** function to MFBM. The Bangsamoro Treasury Office (BAA 13, Sec. 10-14) is the operational arm specifically designed for borrowing, investment, and debt servicing. No other ministry has a competing mandate.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. The following institutions exercise functions related to borrowing:
   - **Lead ministry**: MFBM (BAA 13, Book VI, Title IV; BAA 85 budget allocation)
   - **Operational unit**: Bangsamoro Treasury Office (BAA 13, Sec. 10-14) — borrowing capacity projections, public borrowing policy, debt servicing
   - **Fiscal coordination**: Bangsamoro Finance Office (BAA 13, Sec. 15-16) — credit policy coordination
   - **Budget coordination**: Bangsamoro Budget Coordinating Committee (BAA 84, Sec. 6-7) — recommends borrowing measures
   - **Planning coordination**: Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA) — aligns borrowing with BDP priorities
   - **Intergovernmental**: Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board (BOL Art. XII, Sec. 37-41) — oversees fiscal relations including sovereign guaranty framework

All institutions are **operational** with BAA 85 budget allocations.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not mandate a specific debt management institution beyond what BAA 13 and BAA 84 have created. However, if Parliament enacts a bond issuance enabling law (as required by Art. XII, Sec. 24(b)), it would likely need to designate a **bond issuance authority or committee** — potentially within the Treasury Office — with the technical capacity to structure, price, market, and manage government debt instruments.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Full exercise of this power would benefit from:
   - A **Debt Management Office** or dedicated unit within the Treasury Office with specialized staff for debt portfolio management, risk assessment, and capital market operations.
   - A **Shari'ah Advisory Committee for Public Finance** — to review and certify Shari'ah-compliant borrowing structures (sukuk, murabaha, ijarah) if the Bangsamoro Government pursues Islamic finance alternatives (see Section E (Shari'ah Considerations)).

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The national government exercises authority through three channels: (1) **DOF** processes sovereign guaranty applications from the Bangsamoro Government under Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) — the 30-day response requirement reflects the BOL's intent to expedite this process. (2) **BSP** regulates all banks and financial institutions in BARMM — lending terms, interest rates, capital adequacy, and reserve requirements are nationally determined. (3) **NEDA** evaluates and approves ODA proposals under RA 8182 — the Bangsamoro Government cannot access concessional foreign financing without NEDA's review.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. National programs relevant to credit and lending within BARMM include: (1) **BSP financial inclusion programs** — promoting access to formal financial services in underserved areas including BARMM; (2) **Landbank and DBP lending programs** — government-owned banks extending credit for agriculture, SMEs, and infrastructure in BARMM; (3) **Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank (AAIIBP)** — the sole government-owned Islamic bank, with historical operations in Muslim Mindanao; (4) **NEDA-facilitated ODA** — development assistance from bilateral (Japan, Australia, Korea) and multilateral (World Bank, ADB, IsDB) institutions flowing to BARMM through national government coordination.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. Devolution is **structurally limited** for this power because the key national functions — sovereign guarantee authority (DOF), monetary policy (BSP), and ODA coordination (NEDA) — are **inherently national** and not subject to devolution. The BOL does not contemplate transferring these functions to BARMM; instead, it creates a **parallel borrowing authority** for the Bangsamoro Government that operates within and alongside the national framework. What has devolved is the **decision-making authority** on what to borrow for, how much to borrow, and from what sources (within the BOL's limitations) — the Bangsamoro Government exercises this autonomously.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes, in two areas: (1) **ODA management** — the Bangsamoro Government identifies priority projects and negotiates with development partners, but NEDA reviews and the National Government approves under RA 8182. This creates a **concurrent planning and approval** process. (2) **LGU borrowing** — BARMM constituent LGUs borrow under RA 7160 (national framework) while being subject to Bangsamoro fiscal policy. BAA 49 (Local Governance Code) may modify LGU borrowing provisions, potentially creating a distinct BARMM framework alongside the national one.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. The Bangsamoro Government has: (1) **established the institutional framework** — MFBM, Treasury Office, Finance Office, and BBCC are all operational with defined borrowing-related functions under BAA 13 and BAA 84; (2) **enacted the Budget System Act (BAA 84)** which codifies fiscal responsibility principles, debt service ceilings, and borrowing program evaluation within the budget process; (3) **accessed ODA** through National Government-facilitated programs — development assistance from the World Bank, ADB, Islamic Development Bank, and bilateral partners flows to BARMM development programs; (4) **established fiscal reporting** through the GAAB process, including disclosure of outstanding debt in the Budget of Expenditures and Sources of Financing (BAA 84, Sec. 24(c)). However, the Bangsamoro Government has **not yet issued bonds or other debt instruments** because no enabling law has been enacted.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has two potential advantages that have not yet been realized:

   (1) **Islamic finance potential** — BARMM is uniquely positioned to pioneer **sukuk issuance** and Shari'ah-compliant government borrowing in the Philippines. No Philippine government entity — national or local — has issued sukuk or structured government debt using Islamic finance principles. The Islamic Development Bank (IsDB) and other Islamic finance institutions are natural partners for BARMM. This represents an **untapped capital market** that the national government has not accessed.

   (2) **Development partner alignment** — BARMM's post-conflict, development-focused context attracts concessional financing from international partners specifically interested in peace-building, post-conflict recovery, and Muslim-majority governance. This **targeted ODA access** is a comparative advantage.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a **bond issuance enabling law** as required by Art. XII, Sec. 24(b). Without it:
   - The Bangsamoro Government cannot access the **domestic capital market** through bonds, notes, or other debt instruments.
   - **Sukuk issuance** — which could tap Islamic capital markets — has no legal framework.
   - The government is limited to **direct loans** from banks and institutions, which are more expensive and less flexible than capital market borrowing.
   - **Debt management standards** are not codified, creating risk as the Bangsamoro Government's borrowing capacity grows with the transition to full fiscal autonomy.

   A secondary constraint is the **sovereign guaranty process** — while the BOL requires a 30-day National Government response, the actual process for obtaining sovereign guarantees for Bangsamoro borrowing is not well-defined in practice.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a mature debt management infrastructure:
   - **Legislative depth**: RA 4860 (Foreign Borrowings Act, as amended), RA 8182 (ODA Act), and the annual General Appropriations Act's debt service provisions. The national debt management framework has been operating since the 1960s with continuous amendments.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: The **Bureau of the Treasury (BTr)** manages national government debt with specialized staff for domestic and foreign borrowing, bond auctions, and debt portfolio management. The **DOF** handles sovereign risk assessment and guarantee issuance. **NEDA's** Investment Coordination Committee reviews all government borrowing proposals.
   - **Capital market operations**: BTr conducts **regular bond auctions** (Treasury bills and bonds) through an established dealer network. The Philippines has a functioning government securities market with secondary trading.
   - **Credit ratings**: The Philippines maintains investment-grade sovereign credit ratings from international rating agencies — this enables access to international capital markets at competitive rates.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in three areas:

   (1) **No Islamic finance instruments**: The national government has never issued sukuk or structured government debt using Shari'ah-compliant instruments. The Philippine capital market is entirely conventional. For a region where a significant portion of the population observes Islamic finance principles, this is a structural gap.

   (2) **One-size-fits-all ODA process**: The NEDA review process for ODA does not account for BARMM's unique post-conflict development priorities. ODA proposals compete with all other national priorities through a centralized review, potentially delaying critical peace-building and development investments.

   (3) **Sovereign guaranty bottleneck**: While the BOL provides a 30-day response timeline, the actual sovereign guaranty process depends on the national government's assessment of its own contingent liability exposure — BARMM's borrowing needs may be deprioritized relative to other national borrowing requirements.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:
   - **Replicate**: **Debt service ceilings** (already mirrored in BOL Art. XII, Sec. 24(c)); **publication and transparency requirements** (already required by Art. XII, Sec. 24(d)); **fiscal reporting standards** for debt disclosure (partially implemented through BAA 84).
   - **Adapt**: **Bond issuance framework** — take the national BTr model and adapt it for a smaller-scale regional government, potentially starting with short-term Treasury bills before expanding to longer-term bonds. Add a **sukuk track** as a Shari'ah-compliant alternative. Adapt the **ODA coordination process** to give BARMM more agency in identifying and negotiating with development partners, within the RA 8182 framework.
   - **Replace**: **Borrowing entirely through the national system** — the BOL creates an autonomous borrowing authority. The goal is a Bangsamoro Government that can access capital markets independently (for non-sovereign-guaranty borrowing), not one that channels all borrowing through national agencies.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Government borrowing has a **significant Shari'ah dimension**. Conventional loans involve **interest payments** — the very definition of *riba* (usury/interest), which is among the most strictly prohibited transactions in Islamic law. A Bangsamoro Government that borrows exclusively through conventional interest-bearing instruments operates in tension with the Islamic principles that animate its governance. While the BOL does not require Shari'ah compliance in borrowing, and *maslahah* (public interest) may justify conventional borrowing where alternatives are unavailable, the Bangsamoro Government has a unique opportunity — and arguably an obligation to its constituents — to develop **Shari'ah-compliant borrowing alternatives** alongside conventional instruments.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. XII, Sec. 24-25 (the borrowing provisions). However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact laws on Shari'ah matters, including commercial law: ***"The Parliament has the power to enact laws governing commercial and other civil actions not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083, as amended."*** Government borrowing and debt instrument issuance are commercial in nature. Since PD 1083 does not address government borrowing, Parliament has express authority to enact Shari'ah-compliant public finance legislation under Art. X, Sec. 4.[^24]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Five Islamic legal principles directly govern government borrowing:

   (1) **Prohibition of *riba*** (interest/usury) — the central constraint. Conventional loan interest is *riba* regardless of rate or label. Shari'ah-compliant alternatives structure returns through asset ownership, lease payments, or profit-sharing — not interest on money lent.

   (2) ***Sukuk*** (Islamic bonds) — the primary instrument for Shari'ah-compliant government borrowing. Unlike conventional bonds that pay interest, sukuk represent proportional ownership of a tangible asset, usufruct, or services. Returns are generated from the underlying asset — not from lending money. *Ijarah sukuk* (lease-based), *murabaha sukuk* (trade-based), and *musharakah sukuk* (partnership-based) are the main structures used by sovereign and sub-sovereign issuers globally.

   (3) ***Murabaha*** (cost-plus financing) — applicable to procurement financing. The government structures a purchase as a sale from the financier at a disclosed markup rather than borrowing at interest.

   (4) ***Ijarah*** (lease financing) — applicable to infrastructure and equipment. The financier purchases and leases the asset to the government; ownership transfers upon final payment.

   (5) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — government officials managing public debt are trustees. Borrowing must serve the public good, be transparent, and be managed with accountability. The BOL's debt ceilings and publication requirements are consistent with this principle.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **RA 6848** (Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank Charter, 1990) is the most directly relevant existing legislation. It authorizes AAIIBP to conduct banking ***"without interest"*** using Shari'ah-compliant instruments. AAIIBP could serve as an intermediary for Shari'ah-compliant government borrowing. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) does not address government borrowing or public finance. No Shari'ah court jurisdiction extends to government debt transactions. No known *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address government borrowing, though global Islamic finance opinions on sovereign sukuk issuance provide applicable guidance.[^19][^25]

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro borrowing legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is an **optional dual-track system**: a bond issuance enabling law should provide **both conventional bonds and sukuk** as distinct legal instruments. The government would choose which instrument to use based on the transaction and investor base. The **sukuk track** would require: (a) asset-backed structure — sukuk must be linked to tangible assets, usufructs, or services; (b) prohibition of interest — returns derived from asset performance, not money lending; (c) Shari'ah certification — either through AAIIBP, the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'*, or a designated Shari'ah advisory committee within MFBM; and (d) compliance with international Islamic finance standards (AAOIFI or IFSB). Shari'ah compliance should be **optional** — not all Bangsamoro borrowing needs to be Shari'ah-compliant, but the option must exist.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM has **limited divergence space** for this power because the BOL itself requires compliance with national law. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) requires borrowing to be ***"in compliance with the Constitution, relevant laws and regulations"*** — a direct national law consistency requirement. Art. XII, Sec. 25 requires ODA to comply with **RA 8182** by name. This means:

   - **Sovereign-guaranty borrowing**: No divergence — National Government approval is required, and the process operates under national law (RA 4860).
   - **ODA access**: No divergence — RA 8182 governs, by express BOL mandate.
   - **Direct borrowing (non-sovereign-guaranty)**: **Moderate divergence** — the Bangsamoro Government can independently contract loans from domestic and foreign institutions without sovereign guaranty, subject to creditworthiness and constitutional compliance. The choice of lender, terms, purposes, and procedures can differ from national practice.
   - **Bond and debt instrument issuance**: **Significant divergence potential** — Art. XII, Sec. 24(b) authorizes Parliament to enact the enabling law, with no "consistent with national law" qualifier for the framework itself. A Bangsamoro bond law could include **sukuk** as a distinct instrument category that does not exist in national law.

   As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), the constitutional floor is Art. X, Sec. 15 (autonomous regions operate within the constitutional framework) and Art. VI, Sec. 24/29 (legislative authorization for public debt, appropriation for expenditures).

   The **unique basis for divergence** comes from the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) — Shari'ah-compliant instruments (sukuk, murabaha, ijarah) represent a form of differentiation that the national framework does not provide and that BARMM's Islamic governance mandate supports.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For BARMM constituents**: They could benefit from additional transparency requirements, Shari'ah-compliant government financing, and potentially more targeted borrowing for post-conflict development. The risk is if BARMM's debt management is less rigorous than the national framework, potentially leading to fiscal stress.

   (2) **For investors**: A Bangsamoro bond or sukuk market would create a **new asset class** in the Philippine capital market. Investors would need clarity on the legal framework, payment guarantees, and dispute resolution mechanisms. Cross-boundary recognition of Bangsamoro debt instruments would need to be established.

   (3) **For ODA partners**: Development partners already accustomed to working with BARMM through national government channels would need to understand the Bangsamoro Government's autonomous borrowing authority — particularly for concessional loans that do not require sovereign guaranty.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. National borrowing laws **continue to apply** in most areas because the BOL expressly requires compliance. RA 4860 and RA 8182 apply directly regardless of any BAA. A bond issuance enabling BAA would create a **parallel framework** for Bangsamoro-specific debt instruments — national law governs what the BOL requires it to govern; the BAA fills the spaces where the BOL grants autonomous authority (non-sovereign-guaranty borrowing, bond issuance structure, sukuk framework).

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes, in two areas: (1) **ODA coordination** — the BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority to avail of ODA (Art. XII, Sec. 25) but requires National Government review under RA 8182. The process for BARMM to propose, negotiate, and implement ODA projects is not clearly delineated — does BARMM negotiate directly with development partners and submit to NEDA for approval, or does NEDA manage the entire process? The 2nd BDP (Chapter 13) notes that the Bangsamoro Government will work with donor institutions, but the operational protocol is undefined. (2) **Sovereign guaranty process** — Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) requires a 30-day National Government response, but the criteria for approval, the application process, and the institutional pathway (DOF? NEDA? Both?) are not specified in the BOL or in national implementing rules.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. No pending Parliament Bills specifically addressing government borrowing, bond issuance, or debt management have been identified. The enabling law required by Art. XII, Sec. 24(b) — without which **no bond or debt instrument can be issued** — has not been filed.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 13 (Plan Financing) lists ***"Loans and official development assistance"*** as one of 12 revenue sources for financing the plan's implementation. The BDP envisions **collaborative financing** with the national government, LGUs, donor institutions, and development partners. LGUs are expected to access ***"loans directly negotiated by the LGUs"*** as part of their financing strategy. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 2 (Responsive and Relevant Services)**, depends on capital financing for infrastructure expansion — hospitals, schools, roads, and water systems that cannot be fully funded from the annual block grant alone. **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)** requires productive investment in economic infrastructure — agricultural facilities, industrial zones, and transportation networks — that capital market borrowing can finance.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **bond issuance enabling law**: the Bangsamoro Government cannot access the **domestic capital market** for long-term, large-scale infrastructure financing. This forces reliance on **direct bank loans** (higher cost, shorter tenor, less flexible) or **ODA** (subject to National Government approval, conditionality, and availability). The BDP's infrastructure targets — road networks connecting conflict-affected communities, hospital upgrades across five provinces, school construction — require capital outlays that exceed annual appropriations from the block grant. Without capital market access, these investments must be either **deferred** or financed through more expensive channels. Additionally, the **Islamic capital market opportunity** — sukuk issuance that could attract investment from Islamic Development Bank, Gulf capital, and Southeast Asian Islamic finance institutions — remains untapped. Every year without enabling legislation is a year of foregone infrastructure investment, foregone economic growth, and foregone demonstration of BARMM's fiscal capacity and governance maturity.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why does Art. XII, Sec. 24 impose TWO separate debt service ceilings — and how do they interact?**

A. The two ceilings serve **different fiscal protection purposes**:

   (1) The **20% of regular revenue** ceiling (Art. XII, Sec. 24(c), first proviso) limits debt service relative to the Bangsamoro Government's **own revenue capacity**. It excludes grants, donations, loans, foreign assistance, and ODA from the base — meaning only taxes, fees, charges, and operational revenues count. This prevents the government from borrowing beyond what its **recurring income** can sustain.

   (2) The **20% of block grant** ceiling (Art. XII, Sec. 24(c), second proviso) limits debt service relative to the **largest single revenue source** — the annual block grant from the national government. This prevents the government from mortgaging the block grant to creditors, ensuring that at least 80% remains available for operations and development.

   The two ceilings operate **independently and cumulatively** — both must be satisfied. In practice, the binding constraint depends on the Bangsamoro Government's revenue mix. If own-source revenue is small relative to the block grant (as is currently the case), the 20% of regular revenue ceiling is likely the binding constraint. As own-source revenue grows, the 20% of block grant ceiling may become more constraining. A Bangsamoro debt management framework should model both ceilings and set operational borrowing limits below the lower of the two.

**Q. What is the significance of the "self-liquidating and income-producing" requirement for bonds?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 24(b) restricts bond issuance to financing ***"self-liquidating and income-producing development or livelihood projects pursuant to the priorities established in its development plan."*** This is a **narrower purpose** than the general borrowing authority in Art. XII, Sec. 24(a), which covers public utilities, infrastructure, housing, real property, capital investments, and general development. The restriction means:

   - Bonds cannot finance **general government operations** or **current expenditures** — only projects that generate revenue or recover their own costs.
   - **Revenue-generating infrastructure** (toll roads, market facilities, water systems, power plants) and **livelihood projects** qualify.
   - **Social infrastructure** (schools, hospitals, government buildings) may not qualify unless structured as income-producing (e.g., hospital with paying wards, school with tuition recovery).
   - All bond-financed projects must be **aligned with the BDP** — ensuring that borrowed capital serves the region's development priorities.

   This restriction reflects a **fiscal prudence** principle — bonds create long-term obligations that should be matched by long-term revenue streams. A bond issuance enabling law must include mechanisms for **project evaluation** to verify the self-liquidating and income-producing character of proposed projects.

**Q. How does this power interact with power (ii) Islamic banking and finance?**

A. Power (ii) — ***"Islamic banking and finance"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(ii)) — is the companion power that governs the **institutional framework** for Shari'ah-compliant financial services, while power (ll) governs the **government's own borrowing authority**. The two intersect at **sukuk issuance** and **Shari'ah-compliant government borrowing** — instruments that require both a borrowing legal framework (power (ll)) and an Islamic finance regulatory framework (power (ii)). A bond issuance enabling law that includes a sukuk track should be coordinated with any Islamic banking and finance legislation to ensure consistent Shari'ah compliance standards. **RA 6848** (AAIIBP Charter) bridges the two powers — it provides an existing institutional mechanism through which Shari'ah-compliant government borrowing could be channeled.[^19]

**Q. What role does the Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board play in borrowing?**

A. The **IFPB** (BOL Art. XII, Sec. 37-41) is the intergovernmental body overseeing fiscal relations between the Bangsamoro and National governments. While the BOL does not assign the IFPB a specific role in approving Bangsamoro borrowing, the IFPB's functions include: reviewing the block grant formula (which determines one of the debt service ceiling bases); monitoring fiscal performance; and coordinating fiscal policy. The IFPB's relevance to borrowing is **indirect but significant** — it shapes the fiscal environment within which borrowing decisions are made. The sovereign guaranty process under Art. XII, Sec. 24(a) would benefit from IFPB coordination, as the National Government's assessment of guaranty applications intersects with broader fiscal relations.[^8]

**Q. How does the holdover loan prohibition (Art. VII, Sec. 37) protect fiscal governance?**

A. Art. VII, Sec. 37 provides that during the holdover period after a no-confidence vote, the caretaker government ***"shall not contract loans, approve new contracts, or disburse public funds except those for payment of salaries and wages and expenses for the regular operations of the government."*** This prohibition serves three purposes: (1) prevents an **outgoing government** from incurring debt obligations that bind its successor without electoral mandate; (2) protects the incoming government's **fiscal space** — preventing the previous administration from exhausting borrowing capacity; (3) ensures **democratic accountability** — debt incurrence, which commits future taxpayers, should only occur under a government that retains Parliament's confidence. This is a fiscal governance safeguard with no parallel in the national government framework (there is no equivalent holdover loan prohibition for the President).[^9]
