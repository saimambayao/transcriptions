# (ll) Loans, Credits, and Other Forms of Indebtedness

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ll)** — "Loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The Bangsamoro Organic Law grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness as one of 55 enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2(ll). This power enables the Bangsamoro Government to borrow funds for development — a critical fiscal tool for a resource-constrained autonomous region.

Article XII, Sections 24-25 provide the detailed framework, making this one of the most elaborated powers in the BOL. Section 24(a) authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to "contract loans, credits, and other forms of indebtedness to finance the construction, installation, improvement, expansion, operation or maintenance of public utilities, infrastructure facilities, housing projects, acquisition of real property, implementation of other capital investment projects, and for the development and welfare of the people of the Bangsamoro." The authorized purposes are broad, covering virtually all capital expenditure needs.

However, the power comes with significant conditions. Sovereign-guaranteed loans — "foreign and domestic loans requiring sovereign guaranty, whether explicit or implicit" — require National Government approval. The Parliament must authorize the Chief Minister to contract domestic or foreign loans by a majority vote of all members. A strict fiscal discipline provision limits debt service to no more than 20% of regular revenue sources (excluding grants, donations, loans, and foreign assistance), and no more than 20% of the block grant may be used for loan payments. Section 24(b) further authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to issue bonds, debentures, securities, and other debt instruments to finance self-liquidating and income-producing projects. Section 24(d) requires publication of all loans. Section 25 addresses Official Development Assistance loans, which require "review and approval of the National Government in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182."

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

MFBM manages fiscal policy including debt management, while BPDA coordinates development financing and project prioritization. No standalone BAA has been enacted specifically for loans and debt management. The power is exercised under BAA 13 (Administrative Code) general fiscal mandate and BAA 84 (Budget System Act), which provides the broader fiscal management framework.

In practice, the Bangsamoro Government's borrowing capacity has been limited during the transition period. The BTA has focused on establishing institutional foundations, and most development financing has come through the block grant, Special Development Fund (Art. XIV, Sec. 2), and grant-based ODA rather than loans. The 20% debt service ceiling and the sovereign guaranty requirement provide built-in fiscal discipline, but the absence of a comprehensive debt management framework means there are no codified policies for: when to borrow versus use block grant funds, debt portfolio management, risk assessment for proposed loans, or Islamic (Shari'ah-compliant) financing instruments for government borrowing.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

This power has multiple points of National Government control. First, sovereign-guaranteed loans require National Government approval (Art. XII, Sec. 24(a)). The National Government is also required to "assist the Bangsamoro Government in complying with the requirements for a speedy issuance of the sovereign guaranty" — within 30 days of application, the National Government must inform BARMM of actions taken. Second, ODA loans require "review and approval of the National Government in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182" (Art. XII, Sec. 25). Third, Article XII, Section 2 mandates the Commission on Audit as the exclusive auditor of the Bangsamoro Government, meaning all loan expenditures are subject to COA audit. Fourth, compliance with the Constitution and relevant national laws is expressly required (Art. XII, Sec. 24(a)).

The Department of Finance, Bureau of the Treasury, and the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas are all involved in the national loan approval pipeline. For ODA loans, the NEDA Investment Coordination Committee reviews project proposals. These national requirements ensure that Bangsamoro borrowing aligns with national fiscal and monetary policy.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

While the BOL's loan provisions are relatively self-executing (the basic authority exists without implementing legislation), a comprehensive debt management framework would strengthen fiscal governance. Key gaps include: no codified debt management policy or strategy, no risk assessment framework for proposed loans, no mechanism for Islamic (Shari'ah-compliant) government borrowing instruments (sukuk bonds, Islamic financing structures), no clear institutional framework for which office negotiates, approves, and manages loans, and no guidelines for bond issuance under Section 24(b).

A Bangsamoro Debt Management and Government Securities Act would codify: debt management policy and strategy, institutional roles (MFBM as lead, OCM for negotiations, BPDA for project prioritization), a loan evaluation and approval framework, bond issuance procedures under Art. XII, Sec. 24(b), Islamic financing instruments for government borrowing (sukuk, murabaha-based financing), risk management and debt sustainability analysis, and coordination protocols with DOF, BTr, and BSP for sovereign-guaranteed loans. This legislation becomes increasingly important as BARMM transitions from the BTA to an elected government and begins to exercise its borrowing authority more actively for capital investment.

### Development and Policy Connection

BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 13 (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) identifies loans and debt instruments as potential financing sources for the Bangsamoro Development Plan, particularly for large-scale infrastructure and economic development projects that cannot be funded from the block grant alone. The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028) requires significant infrastructure investment that may necessitate borrowing. Without a debt management framework, the Bangsamoro Government risks either: under-borrowing (failing to leverage its borrowing authority for development) or, if borrowing increases, inadequate fiscal risk management. The 20% debt service ceiling provides a constitutional safeguard, but codified debt management policy would ensure borrowing is strategic and sustainable.
