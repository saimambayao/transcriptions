## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over indigenous peoples' rights through Article V, Section 2(ff). This is one of the most elaborated powers in the BOL, with provisions spanning multiple articles. Article IX, Section 3 is the primary provision, enumerating eight protected rights of indigenous peoples: (a) native titles or fusaka inged; (b) indigenous customs and traditions; (c) justice systems and indigenous political structures; (d) equitable share in revenues from utilization of resources in their ancestral lands; (e) free, prior and informed consent (FPIC); (f) political participation in the Bangsamoro Government including reserved seats for non-Moro indigenous peoples in Parliament; (g) basic services; and (h) freedom of choice as to their identity. The same section mandates the creation of a ministry for indigenous peoples with primary responsibility for formulating and implementing IP policies, plans, and programs.[^1]

The power extends into political representation. Article VII, Section 7(c) reserves seats for non-Moro indigenous peoples in Parliament, and Article VII, Section 8 establishes that these seats shall be filled through customary laws and indigenous processes — a distinctive feature that makes the Bangsamoro Parliament one of the few legislatures in the world that constitutionally mandates indigenous selection methods for parliamentary representation. Article IV, Sections 9-10 address the rights of non-Moro indigenous peoples more broadly, guaranteeing their rights within the framework of the Constitution and national laws, and protecting their freedom of choice regarding identity.[^2]

The power also connects to natural resources. Article XIII, Section 12 provides that indigenous peoples shall have the right to the benefits of natural resource utilization in their ancestral lands, subject to FPIC. Article XII, Section 36 mandates an equitable share of revenues from natural resource exploration and development for indigenous communities, with the sharing mechanism to be provided by Parliament. These provisions create a direct link between IP rights and the region's resource governance framework.[^3]

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

BAA No. 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act), enacted in 2024 from PB No. 273, is the comprehensive legislation implementing this power. It establishes the legal framework for recognizing and protecting indigenous peoples' rights within the Bangsamoro, covering native titles, customary law systems, FPIC requirements, and the institutional framework for IP governance. BAA 64 gives legislative effect to the eight enumerated rights in Article IX, Section 3 and addresses the relationship between Bangsamoro indigenous peoples' legislation and the national IPRA framework.[^4]

The Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIAP), established under BAA No. 13 (Administrative Code) and funded through BAA No. 85 (Chapter IX), serves as the lead ministry. MIAP's mandate covers policy formulation, program implementation, and coordination for indigenous peoples' welfare, including ancestral domain management, cultural preservation, and basic services delivery. The Bangsamoro Parliament maintains reserved seats for non-Moro indigenous peoples (Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, B'laan, and Higaonon), with the selection process per Article VII, Section 8 following customary laws and indigenous processes.[^5]

BAA No. 89 (Transitional Justice and Reconciliation) also touches this power by addressing historical injustices affecting indigenous peoples, including unjust dispossession of ancestral lands. The Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BAA 4) monitors human rights conditions affecting all populations including indigenous peoples.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

This power carries the strongest national law constraint among the eight powers in this cluster. Article IX, Section 3 explicitly states that the BOL "shall not in any manner diminish the rights and benefits of the non-Moro indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region under the Constitution, national laws, particularly Republic Act No. 8371, otherwise known as the 'Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act of 1997.'" This means IPRA serves as a floor — the Bangsamoro Government may grant additional rights and protections beyond IPRA but cannot reduce what IPRA already provides. Article IV, Section 9 reinforces this by requiring measures for non-Moro indigenous peoples to be "within the framework of the Constitution and national laws."[^6]

The National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP) continues to operate in the Bangsamoro region alongside MIAP. The delineation of responsibilities between NCIP and MIAP — particularly regarding Certificate of Ancestral Domain Title (CADT) processes, FPIC certification, and ancestral domain management — has been a source of tension, as evidenced by Resolution 38 (protesting NCIP delineation processes in Maguindanao). BAA 64 provides the Bangsamoro framework, but the interaction between NCIP's national mandate and MIAP's regional mandate requires ongoing coordination. No intergovernmental body exists specifically for indigenous peoples' affairs, though the general Intergovernmental Relations Body could address jurisdictional disputes.[^7]

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

While BAA 64 provides a comprehensive framework, several implementation gaps remain. First, the revenue sharing mechanism mandated by Article XII, Section 36 — which requires Parliament to pass a law "detailing the sharing mechanism and percentages" for indigenous communities' share of natural resource revenues — has not been enacted as standalone implementing legislation. Without this, the equitable share guarantee remains dependent on ad hoc arrangements rather than a transparent, codified formula.

Second, BAA 64's FPIC provisions may need implementing rules or supplementary legislation to address practical challenges. The FPIC process in the Bangsamoro involves communities where both NCIP and MIAP may assert regulatory authority, creating potential for conflicting FPIC certifications or procedural requirements. A Bangsamoro FPIC Implementing Framework codified in legislation would establish clear procedures, timelines, and dispute resolution for consent processes.

Third, while reserved seats for non-Moro indigenous peoples in Parliament are constitutionally guaranteed, the specific mechanisms for customary selection processes in Article VII, Section 8 require ongoing legislative attention to ensure they remain genuine expressions of indigenous political self-determination rather than being captured by external political interests. The BTA Electoral Code (BAA 35) addresses parliamentary elections generally, but the customary processes for IP seats may benefit from dedicated provisions developed in consultation with IP communities.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage) and Chapter 10 (Peace and Security), target the full recognition of indigenous peoples' rights as essential to both cultural preservation and the broader peace process. Chapter 6 envisions the preservation and promotion of IP cultural heritage, while Chapter 10 addresses the historical injustices that have affected indigenous communities. BAA 64 provides the legislative foundation, but the absence of the natural resource revenue sharing mechanism and formalized NCIP-MIAP coordination means that economic justice for indigenous communities — the material dimension of IP rights — remains incomplete.
