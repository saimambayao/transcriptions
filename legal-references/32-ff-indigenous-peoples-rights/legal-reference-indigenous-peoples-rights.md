# (ff) Indigenous Peoples' Rights

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ff)** — ***"Indigenous peoples' rights;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(ff) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. IX, Sec. 3 (last paragraph) expressly provides: ***"This Organic Law shall not in any manner diminish the rights and benefits of the non-Moro indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region under the Constitution, national laws, particularly Republic Act No. 8371."*** Art. IV, Sec. 9 further requires the Bangsamoro Government to ***"recognize and promote the rights of non-Moro indigenous peoples within the framework of the Constitution and national laws."*** |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over indigenous peoples' rights?**

A. Article V, Section 2(ff) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"indigenous peoples' rights"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a broad grant covering the recognition, protection, promotion, and preservation of the rights of indigenous cultural communities and indigenous peoples (ICCs/IPs) within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region — including ancestral domain rights, self-governance, customary laws, cultural integrity, FPIC, political participation, equitable resource sharing, and access to basic services.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. This power has extensive BOL elaboration — more than most enumerated powers — spanning at least 12 provisions across 7 articles:

   (1) **Art. IX, Sec. 3** is the core provision. It enumerates **8 specific IP rights** the Bangsamoro Government must protect: (a) native titles or *fusaka inged*; (b) indigenous customs and traditions; (c) justice systems and indigenous political structures; (d) equitable share in revenues from natural resource utilization; (e) free, prior and informed consent; (f) political participation including reserved parliamentary seats; (g) basic services; and (h) freedom of choice as to identity. It further mandates: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall create a ministry for indigenous peoples"*** and provides the **non-diminution clause**: ***"This Organic Law shall not in any manner diminish the rights and benefits of the non-Moro indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region under the Constitution, national laws, particularly Republic Act No. 8371."***[^2]

   (2) **Art. IV, Sec. 9** provides the general framework: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall recognize and promote the rights of non-Moro indigenous peoples within the framework of the Constitution and national laws."***[^3]

   (3) **Art. IV, Sec. 10** guarantees **freedom of choice** and **non-discrimination**: IPs ***"shall have the freedom to retain their distinct indigenous and ethnic identity in addition to their Bangsamoro political identity."***[^4]

   (4) **Art. VII, Sec. 7(c)** guarantees **two reserved parliamentary seats** for non-Moro IPs. Art. VII, Sec. 8 provides that these seats shall be filled in accordance with ***"customary laws and indigenous processes"*** and names the 5 non-Moro IP groups: **Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, B'laan, and Higaonon**.[^5]

   (5) **Art. IX, Sec. 2** mandates **reparation** for unjust dispossession of territorial and proprietary rights — applicable to IPs whose ancestral domains were unjustly taken.[^6]

   (6) **Art. IX, Sec. 19** mandates creation of a **tribal university system** for the higher educational needs of non-Moro IPs.[^7]

   (7) **Art. X, Sec. 17** mandates Parliament to ***"enact laws to promote and support the traditional or tribal justice systems."*** Art. X, Sec. 18 creates the **Office for Traditional or Tribal Justice System**.[^8]

   (8) **Art. XIII, Sec. 9** requires **FPIC** before protected areas are declared within ancestral domains.[^9]

   (9) **Art. XIII, Sec. 12** mandates a law recognizing ***"the rights of indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in relation to natural resources within the areas covered by a native title, including their share in revenues."***[^10]

   (10) **Art. XII, Sec. 21** protects IP revenue shares from being deducted from the block grant.[^11]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **multiple express legislative mandates**:

   (1) **Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a), third paragraph**: ***"The Bangsamoro Transition Authority shall enact a law to recognize, protect, promote, and preserve the rights of the indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** This is a **transition priority** — **fulfilled by BAA 64**.[^12]

   (2) **Art. IX, Sec. 19**: Parliament shall create a **tribal university system** — partially addressed by BAA 64, Sec. 53, but a **Tribal University Charter** has not yet been enacted (pending as PB 274).[^7]

   (3) **Art. X, Sec. 17**: Parliament shall enact laws to promote and support **tribal justice systems** — addressed by BAA 64, Chapter VI (Sec. 34-37).[^8]

   (4) **Art. XIII, Sec. 12**: Parliament shall enact a law recognizing **IP rights to natural resources** — addressed by BAA 64, Chapter XII (Sec. 77-83).[^10]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. Two BOL-mandated institutions:

   (1) **Ministry for Indigenous Peoples** — Art. IX, Sec. 3 mandates: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall create a ministry for indigenous peoples."*** Created as MIPA by BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII; strengthened by BAA 64, Chapter X.[^2]

   (2) **Office for Traditional or Tribal Justice System** — Art. X, Sec. 18: ***"There is hereby created an Office for Tribal Justice System that shall be responsible for overseeing the study, preservation, and development of the tribal justice system."***[^8]

   BAA 64 created additional institutions: the **Fusaka Ingëd Development Office (FIDO)** (Sec. 20), the **Indigenous Peoples Council** (Sec. 22), and the **Regional Adjudicators Bureau** (Sec. 89).

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places three significant conditions:

   (1) **Non-diminution clause** (Art. IX, Sec. 3, last paragraph): ***"This Organic Law shall not in any manner diminish the rights and benefits of the non-Moro indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region under the Constitution, national laws, particularly Republic Act No. 8371."*** This means any Bangsamoro IP legislation must meet or exceed IPRA standards.[^2]

   (2) **Constitutional and national law framework** (Art. IV, Sec. 9): ***"within the framework of the Constitution and national laws."***[^3]

   (3) **UNDRIP alignment** (Art. IX, Sec. 3, second-to-last paragraph): ***"Any measure enacted by the Parliament shall in no way diminish the rights and privileges granted to indigenous peoples by virtue of the United Nations Declaration of the Rights of Indigenous Peoples."***[^2]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to indigenous peoples' rights?**

A. Five categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Universal constraint (applies to all 55 powers)**: Art. X, Sec. 15 — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***[^13]

   (2) **State policy on IPs (Art. II, Sec. 22)**: ***"The State recognizes and promotes the rights of indigenous cultural communities within the framework of national unity and development."*** This is the foundational State policy — it mandates recognition and promotion but within national unity.[^14]

   (3) **Ancestral lands (Art. XII, Sec. 5)**: ***"The State, subject to the provisions of this Constitution and national development policies and programs, shall protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities to their ancestral lands to ensure their economic, social, and cultural well-being."*** This is the constitutional anchor for ancestral domain protection.[^15]

   (4) **Customary property laws (Art. XII, Sec. 5, second paragraph)**: ***"The Congress may provide for the applicability of customary laws governing property rights or relations in determining the ownership and extent of ancestral domain."*** Congress has exercised this through RA 8371; Parliament has exercised this within BARMM through BAA 64.[^15]

   (5) **IP cultural rights (Art. XIV, Sec. 17)**: ***"The State shall recognize, respect, and protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities to preserve and develop their cultures, traditions, and institutions. It shall consider these rights in the formulation of national plans and policies."***[^16]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees: (a) **equal protection** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — IPs cannot be discriminated against; (b) **due process** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — IP land rights cannot be taken without due process; (c) **ancestral land protection** (Art. XII, Sec. 5) — affirmative duty to protect; (d) **cultural self-determination** (Art. XIV, Sec. 17) — right to preserve and develop cultures, traditions, and institutions; and (e) **indigenous education** (Art. XIV, Sec. 4(4)) — the State shall ***"encourage non-formal, informal, and indigenous learning systems."***[^17]

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 5 authorizes Congress to legislate on customary property laws for ancestral domains — exercised through IPRA. Art. XVI, Sec. 12 authorizes Congress to ***"create a consultative body to advise the President on policies affecting indigenous cultural communities."*** The constitutional framework gives both Congress and Parliament (via the BOL delegation) authority to legislate on IP rights — Parliament's authority derives from Art. X, Sec. 15 (organic act scope) read with Art. V, Sec. 2(ff).[^15][^18]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for indigenous peoples?**

A. The national framework is anchored on **RA 8371 (IPRA of 1997)** — the comprehensive IP rights statute. IPRA establishes: (a) ancestral domain and ancestral land rights, including the CADT/CALT system; (b) self-governance and empowerment through indigenous political structures; (c) social justice and human rights protections; (d) cultural integrity guarantees; (e) the FPIC mechanism for any project affecting ancestral domains; and (f) the **National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP)** as the primary national agency. Additional laws include RA 10689 (IP Day), RA 10908 (IP history in education), and EO 139 (IPMR benefits).[^19]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. **RA 8371 (IPRA)** applies directly and is expressly preserved by the BOL's non-diminution clause. BAA 64 does not repeal IPRA — it builds a parallel regional framework. Where BAA 64 provides equal or greater protections, it governs within BARMM. Where BAA 64 is silent, IPRA provisions continue to apply suppletorily. National laws of general application (RA 10689, RA 10908) also apply directly.[^2][^19]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. **NCIP** is the primary national agency. Under IPRA, NCIP has jurisdiction over ancestral domain delineation, CADT/CALT issuance, FPIC certification, and dispute resolution nationwide. Within BARMM, the jurisdictional boundary is contested: **BAA 64, Sec. 102** provides that pending ancestral domain applications processed by NCIP (including the Teduray-Lambangian Ancestral Domain Claim) shall be ***"turned over to the MIPA for immediate action."*** **Resolution No. 38** formally protested NCIP delineation activities in Maguindanao. The practical effect is that **MIPA exercises regional functions** that NCIP exercises nationally — but no formal intergovernmental agreement (MOA) has been concluded to govern the handover.[^19][^20]

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIPA)** — established by BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII, Sec. 1-13; strengthened and expanded by BAA 64, Chapter X. MIPA receives its operational budget under BAA 85, Chapter IX.[^21][^22]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **BAA 64** (Bangsamoro Indigenous Peoples' Act of 2024) is the **comprehensive standalone legislation** — 106 sections across 19 chapters covering ancestral domain rights, self-governance, IPMR representation, tribal justice, social justice, women and children, IKSP, MIPA powers, delineation procedures, natural resource rights, FPIC, enforcement mechanisms, penalties, and appropriations. This is one of the most fully legislated powers among the 55 enumerated powers.[^20]

**Q. What are MIPA's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII, Sec. 2 provides the mandate: ***"The Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs is mandated to protect, promote, and preserve the interest and well-being of IPs within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region with due regard to their beliefs, customs, traditions, and institutions."***[^21]

Sec. 3 enumerates **10 powers and functions**: (a) serve as primary ministry for IPs; (b) review and assess IP conditions, propose relevant laws; (c) issue CADTs and register them with the Registry of Deeds; (d) formulate and implement IP policies, plans, programs, and projects; (e) request services and engage experts; (f) negotiate for and accept grants and donations for IP benefit; (g) coordinate development programs for IP advancement; (h) convene periodic IP conventions, congresses, and assemblies; (i) issue Certificates of Accreditation and Certificates of Dissolution of Marriages per IP customs; (j) perform related functions as provided by law.[^21]

The organizational structure (Sec. 4, 9) includes: Legal and Legislative Liaison Section; Internal Audit Unit; Information and Communications Section; Planning Section; Administrative and Finance Division. The **Bureau of Ancestral Domains/Native Title** (Sec. 10-11) handles ancestral domain assessment and coordination. The **Special Public Assistance and Legal Aid Division** (Sec. 12) provides free legal assistance to indigent IP litigants. **Community Service Centers** (Sec. 13) serve municipalities with significant IP populations.[^21]

BAA 64, Sec. 105 provides that BAA 13 Title VII provisions ***"insofar as they are in conflict or inconsistent with the provisions of this Act, are hereby repealed or amended"*** — BAA 64 supersedes and expands the BAA 13 mandate.

**Q. What can MIPA NOT do without additional legislation beyond BAA 64?**

A. BAA 64 is comprehensive, but certain matters still require additional legislation: (a) a **Tribal University Charter** (BAA 64, Sec. 53 explicitly states ***"the Bangsamoro Parliament shall enact a law providing for the Charter of the Tribal University"***); (b) detailed **implementing rules and regulations** (BAA 64, Sec. 100 requires IRR within 90 days); (c) specific **revenue-sharing formulas** operationalizing the "not less than 10%" IP share (BAA 64, Sec. 77); and (d) any **Bangsamoro Ancestral Domain Code** that would provide a comprehensive regulatory framework for land management — BAA 64 covers rights and procedures but not detailed land use regulation within ancestral domains.[^20]

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. Resolution No. 38 protested NCIP delineation within BARMM, signaling jurisdictional assertion. Multiple proposed resolutions address ongoing IP concerns: PR 327 (civil registration for Sama Dilaut/Badjao), PR 461 (mainstreaming IPEd), PR 462 (tribal university development TWG), PR 643 (inter-community dialogue), PR 658 (rape case handling in IP communities), PR 712 (peace sanctuary), and PR 743 (security in IP communities).

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is MIPA the correct lead MOA for this power?**

A. Yes. MIPA's mandate directly and fully covers the scope of Art. V, Sec. 2(ff). The BOL itself mandates ***"a ministry for indigenous peoples"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 3), and BAA 64 strengthens and expands MIPA's functions well beyond the initial BAA 13 assignment. Unlike some powers where the lead MOA covers only part of the power's scope, MIPA is a **single-purpose ministry** dedicated entirely to IP rights — there is no mismatch between the power's scope and the MOA's mandate.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Multiple institutions:

   - **Lead ministry**: MIPA (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII; BAA 64, Chapter X; BAA 85, Chapter IX) — **operational**
   - **Fusaka Ingëd Development Office (FIDO)**: attached agency of MIPA with fiscal autonomy (BAA 64, Sec. 20-21) — ancestral domain development coordination — **created by law, operational status to be confirmed**
   - **Indigenous Peoples Council**: advisory body to MIPA (BAA 64, Sec. 22-27) — representatives from recognized IP tribes — **created by law**
   - **Regional Adjudicators Bureau**: quasi-judicial body under MIPA (BAA 64, Sec. 89-91) — hears ancestral domain disputes, FPIC violations, IPMR disputes — **created by law**
   - **Bureau of Ancestral Domains/Native Title**: within MIPA (BAA 13, Sec. 10-11; BAA 64, Sec. 65-70) — delineation and CADT/CALT issuance — **operational**
   - **Special Public Assistance and Legal Aid Division**: within MIPA (BAA 13, Sec. 12) — free legal aid to indigent IP litigants — **operational**
   - **Office for Traditional or Tribal Justice System**: BOL Art. X, Sec. 18 — **created by BOL; operational status to be confirmed**
   - **BTJRC** (BAA 89): addresses historical injustices including IP land dispossession — **created by law**

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The **tribal university system** (Art. IX, Sec. 19) — BAA 64, Sec. 53 calls for a Tribal University Charter to be enacted by Parliament. PB 274 proposes converting Upi Agricultural School into the Bangsamoro Tribal University, but has not been enacted.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. BAA 64 has already created the institutions needed for comprehensive IP governance (FIDO, IPs Council, Regional Adjudicators Bureau). However, full operationalization may require: (a) **Community Service Centers** in each ancestral domain area (BAA 64, Sec. 64); (b) **Provincial and city offices** of MIPA (BAA 64, Sec. 63); (c) formalization of the **Indigenous Cultural Guardians** system (BAA 64, Sec. 56) as an operational structure.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **NCIP** maintains a regional presence in BARMM. NCIP historically exercised all IP-related functions: ancestral domain delineation, CADT/CALT issuance, FPIC certification, dispute resolution, and IP program delivery. The jurisdictional contest between NCIP and MIPA is the **defining implementation issue** — BAA 64, Sec. 102 orders NCIP to turn over pending applications, and Resolution No. 38 protested NCIP delineation activities in Maguindanao.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. Devolution is **legislatively mandated but not fully operationalized**. BAA 64, Sec. 102 provides for turnover of pending ancestral domain applications from NCIP to MIPA. BAA 64, Sec. 67 recognizes ancestral domains already delineated under national law. BAA 64, Sec. 68 addresses cross-boundary ancestral domains. However, no formal **MOA between NCIP and MIPA** has been publicly documented to govern the transfer of records, databases, ongoing cases, and personnel — making the devolution incomplete in practice.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes — this is the core jurisdictional tension. NCIP retains national authority over all IPs nationwide, including those in BARMM. MIPA exercises regional authority over the same populations. Specific concurrent areas include: (a) **CADT/CALT issuance** — both agencies claim authority; (b) **FPIC certification** — both issue certifications; (c) **dispute resolution** — both have quasi-judicial functions; (d) **IP program delivery** — both implement development programs. BAA 64, Sec. 68 provides a practical resolution for cross-boundary claims: where ancestral domains span BARMM and non-BARMM areas, NCIP processes the portion outside BARMM while MIPA issues CALT for the BARMM portion.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. This is one of the most legislatively advanced powers:

   (a) **BAA 64** enacted — comprehensive IP rights law with 106 sections, one of the longest and most detailed BAAs
   (b) **MIPA operational** — staffed, budgeted under BAA 85 Chapter IX, with Bureau of Ancestral Domains, Legal Aid Division, and Community Service Centers
   (c) **Two reserved parliamentary seats** filled for non-Moro IPs — political representation operational since the BTA's inception
   (d) **IPMR system** operating in constituent LGUs
   (e) **FIDO and IPs Council** created by law
   (f) **Tribal justice** provisions codified in BAA 64, Chapter VI
   (g) **Natural resource revenue sharing** established with minimum 10% IP share (BAA 64, Sec. 77)
   (h) **Ancestral Domain Fund** created with initial PHP 200 million appropriation (BAA 64, Sec. 98)

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has structural advantages:

   - **Moro-IP kinship tradition**: BAA 64 uniquely recognizes the ***"Moro-IP kinship as a living tradition"*** rooted in the Mamalu-Tabunaway ancestry — a cultural framework absent from national law
   - **Revenue sharing floor**: BAA 64 sets a **minimum 10% share** of natural resource revenues for IPs (Sec. 77), providing a concrete floor
   - **FIDO**: an institution with fiscal autonomy dedicated to ancestral domain development — NCIP has no equivalent focused development arm
   - **Regional Adjudicators Bureau**: a dedicated quasi-judicial body for IP disputes within BARMM — faster and more accessible than NCIP's national adjudication
   - **Cultural specificity**: BAA 64 names specific indigenous political structures (*Council of Timuays*, *Gempa te Kalindaan ne Kamal*, *Kena Menuwa*, *Alimbubungan*) — national law uses generic terminology

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. Three binding constraints:

   (1) **NCIP-MIPA jurisdictional overlap**: no formal MOA governs the handover. NCIP retains databases, records, and institutional knowledge that MIPA needs. Pending ancestral domain claims (including the large Teduray-Lambangian Ancestral Domain Claim) are in limbo. This is the **single biggest constraint**.

   (2) **IRR development**: BAA 64, Sec. 100 requires IRR within 90 days of enactment — the IRR operationalizes critical procedures (FPIC process, delineation guidelines, CADT/CALT issuance, Adjudicators Bureau rules). Without the IRR, BAA 64's provisions remain partially inoperative.

   (3) **Tribal University Charter**: not yet enacted. PB 274 is filed but not passed.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national system through NCIP offers: (a) **27+ years of IPRA implementation** — established procedures, jurisprudence, IRRs; (b) **national ancestral domain database** — records of all delineated domains and pending applications nationwide; (c) **FPIC guidelines** (NCIP Administrative Order No. 3, s. 2012, as amended) — detailed implementation rules tested in hundreds of applications; (d) **regional offices** with trained personnel in ancestral domain surveys, delineation, and titling; (e) **dispute resolution track record** — NCIP hearing officers with case law precedents.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national NCIP framework falls short in critical ways:

   - **No recognition of Moro-IP kinship**: IPRA treats IPs uniformly nationwide without recognizing the unique Moro-IP relationship in the Bangsamoro context
   - **No Shari'ah or Islamic sensitivity**: NCIP processes do not account for the Muslim-majority context of BARMM or customary dispute resolution involving Moro and IP communities
   - **Conflict-insensitive**: NCIP's standard procedures were not designed for BARMM's post-conflict environment — contested lands, military camps within ancestral domains (BAA 64, Sec. 66), IDP populations
   - **Underserved**: NCIP's presence in BARMM has been historically limited — few staff, limited delineation activities, backlog of pending claims
   - **No FIDO equivalent**: NCIP lacks a dedicated, fiscally autonomous body for ancestral domain development coordination

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. **Replicate**: NCIP's CADT/CALT delineation methodology (survey standards, documentation requirements, publication procedures) — BAA 64, Sec. 69-70 already mirrors these. **Adapt**: NCIP's FPIC guidelines — BAA 64, Sec. 84-88 establishes the framework, but MIPA's IRR should adapt NCIP AO No. 3's detailed procedures for BARMM's context (Moro-IP relations, post-conflict sensitivity, local languages). **Replace**: NCIP's national adjudication with the Regional Adjudicators Bureau — faster, more locally accessible dispute resolution.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. The Shari'ah dimension is **indirect but significant**. Most ICCs/IPs in BARMM are non-Muslim (Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, B'laan, Higaonon practice indigenous religions or Christianity), so Shari'ah law does not directly govern their personal or property matters. However, Islamic principles inform the broader governance framework: (a) the Bangsamoro Government is rooted in Islamic governance values; (b) Moro communities frequently neighbor or overlap with IP ancestral domains, creating intersections where Shari'ah and customary law coexist; and (c) Islamic principles of justice, welfare, and stewardship provide moral grounding for IP protection.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL is largely silent on Shari'ah specifically for IP rights. Art. X, Sec. 1 provides that justice shall be dispensed ***"in consonance with the Constitution"*** and references Shari'ah courts, traditional/tribal justice systems, and alternative dispute resolution as parallel tracks. Art. X, Sec. 17 specifically addresses ***"traditional or tribal justice systems that are appropriate for the indigenous peoples"*** — these are distinct from Shari'ah courts and operate under customary law, not Islamic law. The BOL thus treats IP justice as a **separate track** from Shari'ah justice.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Three Islamic principles apply indirectly:

   (1) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — protecting IP communities, preventing dispossession, and ensuring equitable development all serve the common good.

   (2) ***'Adl*** (justice/equity) — the Quranic mandate for justice extends to all people regardless of religion. Protecting non-Muslim IP rights within a Muslim-majority autonomous region is an exercise of *'adl*.

   (3) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — the IP concept of communal, generational land ownership mirrors the Islamic concept of humans as custodians (*khalifah*) of the earth. Ancestral domain protection aligns with *amanah*.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. Shari'ah compliance for IP rights should be **principled rather than mandatory** — legislation guided by Islamic values (*'adl*, *maslahah*, *amanah*) without requiring IPs to comply with Islamic law. BAA 64 takes this approach: it does not mandate Shari'ah compliance for any IP institution or process. The Shari'ah dimension is most relevant when resolving disputes between Moro and IP communities over overlapping land claims — in such cases, both customary law and Shari'ah principles inform mediation and resolution.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. The BARMM's divergence space is **constrained but significant**. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL's non-diminution clause (Art. IX, Sec. 3, last paragraph) creates a **floor**: BARMM IP rights cannot fall below what RA 8371 (IPRA) provides. As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), the constitutional floor includes Art. XII, Sec. 5 (ancestral land protection) and Art. XIV, Sec. 17 (cultural self-determination). However, BARMM **can exceed** the national framework:

   - **Stronger revenue sharing**: BAA 64 sets a 10% minimum IP share in natural resource revenues — this may exceed the IPRA framework
   - **Unique institutions**: FIDO, IPs Council, Regional Adjudicators Bureau have no direct NCIP equivalents
   - **Moro-IP kinship recognition**: a distinctive BARMM feature with no national parallel
   - **Named indigenous political structures**: BAA 64 specifically names and recognizes local governance structures that IPRA treats generically
   - **Military camp provisions**: BAA 64, Sec. 66 addresses military camps within ancestral domains — a post-conflict reality unique to BARMM

   The key constraint is that BARMM cannot **diminish** any IPRA right. The non-diminution clause operates as a one-way ratchet: BARMM can only go higher, not lower.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. Two practical implications:

   (1) **Cross-boundary ancestral domains**: BAA 64, Sec. 68 provides that where an ancestral domain spans BARMM and non-BARMM territory, NCIP processes the portion outside BARMM while MIPA issues titles for the BARMM portion. This creates a **dual-jurisdiction reality** where a single IP community may hold both an NCIP-issued CADT and a MIPA-issued CALT for different portions of the same ancestral domain.

   (2) **Rights portability**: IPs who hold NCIP-issued CADTs within BARMM have their titles recognized by BAA 64, Sec. 67. The non-diminution clause ensures they do not lose any rights they already hold under IPRA.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. BAA 64 governs IP rights within BARMM. IPRA continues to apply suppletorily where BAA 64 is silent — the non-diminution clause ensures IPRA protections remain available. NCIP retains authority over IPs outside BARMM and over cross-boundary matters. Constitutional provisions (Art. XII, Sec. 5; Art. XIV, Sec. 17; Art. II, Sec. 22) always apply.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. The **NCIP-MIPA jurisdictional boundary** is the primary overlap. BAA 64 legislatively asserts MIPA's authority, but no intergovernmental MOA or judicial decision has formally demarcated the boundary. Specific unresolved questions include: (a) which agency issues FPIC certifications for projects within BARMM — MIPA or NCIP?; (b) which agency's Adjudicators/Hearing Officers have jurisdiction over IP disputes within BARMM?; (c) how are NCIP-issued CADTs within BARMM migrated into MIPA's system?; and (d) who maintains the authoritative ancestral domain database for BARMM?

Resolution would require either: (i) an **intergovernmental MOA between NCIP and MIPA** (recommended), or (ii) a **national legislative amendment** to IPRA explicitly recognizing MIPA's regional jurisdiction, or (iii) **Supreme Court adjudication** of the jurisdictional question.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB 274** (Bangsamoro Tribal University) would address the unfulfilled Art. IX, Sec. 19 mandate and BAA 64, Sec. 53 requirement. **PB 215** (IP Community Centers) would enhance service delivery infrastructure. **PB 171** (IP culture in education) would strengthen cultural preservation. However, the **NCIP-MIPA jurisdictional overlap** is not addressed by any pending PB — it requires an intergovernmental instrument rather than a parliamentary bill.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. Three BDP chapters directly depend on IP rights: **Chapter 6** (Cultural Diversity and Heritage) — cultural preservation and ancestral domain protection; **Chapter 10** (Peace and Security) — IP community protection, tribal justice, and conflict resolution are foundational to lasting peace; and **Chapter 12** (Environment and Natural Resources) — ancestral domains contain some of BARMM's most ecologically significant areas, and IP communities serve as custodians. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** connects through **Pillar 1** (Good Governance — inclusive IP representation) and **Pillar 5** (Peace and Security — IP community protection).

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Three outcomes at risk:

   (1) **Ancestral domain titling backlog**: without a formal NCIP-MIPA handover, pending claims (including the Teduray-Lambangian Ancestral Domain Claim) remain in limbo — denying IP communities formal recognition of their territorial rights

   (2) **Higher education access**: without a Tribal University Charter, non-Moro IPs lack the dedicated higher education institution the BOL promises — limiting human capital development in the most marginalized IP communities

   (3) **Natural resource revenue leakage**: without operationalized revenue-sharing mechanisms (BAA 64, Sec. 77 IRR), the minimum 10% IP share in natural resource revenues may not flow to communities — perpetuating economic marginalization

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the relationship between this power (ff — indigenous peoples' rights) and power (d — ancestral domain and natural resources)?**

A. These two powers are **deeply intertwined but distinct**. Power (d) — ancestral domain and natural resources — covers the broader authority over natural resource exploration, development, and utilization, including joint exercise with the National Government over fossil fuels and uranium (Art. XIII, Sec. 10). Power (ff) — indigenous peoples' rights — focuses on the **rights holders** (ICCs/IPs) and their specific entitlements within ancestral domains. In practice: power (d) governs what happens with natural resources; power (ff) governs what IPs' rights are when resources are in their ancestral domains. BAA 64 bridges both powers by establishing the FPIC requirement, revenue-sharing minimums, and priority rights for IPs in natural resource activities within ancestral domains. **MENRE** leads power (d); **MIPA** leads power (ff); they must coordinate per BAA 64, Sec. 69(d).

**Q. Which IP groups are covered by BAA 64?**

A. BAA 64, Sec. 3 provides coverage for ***"all native IPs within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** and specifically names: **Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, Erumanun Ne Menuvu, Higaonon, B'laan, Badjao, Sama Dilaut, Jama Mapun, Sama Bangingi, Sama Pangutaran**, and ***"other marginalized and minority ICCs/IPs who identify, both through self-ascription and ascription by others as IPs."*** Notably, this list includes **Moro marginalized peoples** (Sama groups) alongside non-Moro IPs — BAA 64, Sec. 5(s) defines ***"Marginalized Moro Peoples or Communities"*** as ***"historically Moro ICCs/IPs who are politically, economically, or socio-culturally marginalized."*** This is broader than the BOL's emphasis on "non-Moro indigenous peoples."[^20]

**Q. What is the FPIC framework under BAA 64?**

A. BAA 64 establishes a comprehensive FPIC regime (Chapter XIII, Sec. 84-88): (a) FPIC is the ***"consensus decision made by affected ICC/IP communities in accordance with their respective customary laws and practices"*** (Sec. 5(k)); (b) **all** public or private institutions must secure Certification Precondition through FPIC before implementing plans, programs, projects, or activities within ancestral domains (Sec. 84); (c) **all ministries and agencies** are enjoined from issuing concessions, licenses, leases, or production-sharing agreements without MIPA certification that the area does not overlap ancestral domains (Sec. 86); (d) interference with the FPIC process is both an **administrative and criminal offense** (Sec. 87); (e) FPIC guidelines to be provided in the IRR with IPS participation, reviewed every 3 years (Sec. 88). Failure to provide Certification Precondition is ***"prima facie evidence of unauthorized or unlawful intrusion"*** (Sec. 12).[^20]

**Q. How does BAA 64 handle the ancestral domain titling process?**

A. BAA 64 establishes a detailed delineation and titling process (Chapter XI, Sec. 65-76): **Self-delineation** is the guiding principle (Sec. 65). The process involves: (a) Petition for Delineation by IPS representing ICCs/IPs; (b) Delineation Proper by the Bureau of Ancestral Domains with IP participation; (c) Proof requirements including elder testimony, documentary evidence (10 categories listed); (d) coordination with MAFAR and MENRE to verify adverse claims; (e) map preparation; (f) notice and publication for 15 days; (g) endorsement to the MIPA Minister; (h) turnover of areas within ancestral domains managed by other agencies; (i) CADT issuance; (j) registration with Registry of Deeds. The process mirrors NCIP's methodology under IPRA but is administered by MIPA rather than NCIP.[^20]

**Q. What are the penal provisions in BAA 64?**

A. BAA 64, Sec. 96-97 provides: violation of the Act is punishable first by **customary laws** of the ICCs/IPs concerned (provided penalties are not cruel, degrading, or inhuman, and no death penalty or excessive fines). Alternatively, violators may be punished by **imprisonment of 9 months to 12 years** or a **fine of PHP 100,000 to PHP 500,000**, or both, plus damages to the affected ICCs/IPs. When the offender is a **public officer**, the penalty is one degree higher (Sec. 12, last paragraph). Officers of juridical entities are personally liable. This is a significant advance over IPRA's penal provisions and over BARMM's earlier reliance on general penalty frameworks.[^20]
