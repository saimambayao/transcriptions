## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis in the style of Philippine . Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over cadastral land surveys?**

A. Article V, Section 2(h) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"cadastral land survey"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This grants authority over the systematic surveying of land parcels to establish boundaries, areas, and ownership — the foundation of land administration, land registration, and property rights.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power?**

A. Two provisions elaborate:

   (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 17 (para. 2)** — the primary implementing provision. States: ***"The Bangsamoro Government may, upon authority of the President, conduct cadastral surveys, lot surveys, and isolated and special surveys in the Bangsamoro: Provided, That it shall furnish the results of these surveys to, and coordinate with, the relevant national government agencies to effect inclusion into the National Cadastral Survey."***[^2]

   (2) **Art. XIII, Sec. 17 (para. 1)** — the related land classification provision (power (j)). Classification of public lands ***"shall be recommended by the Bangsamoro Government to the President."*** Cadastral surveys produce the data that makes land classification possible.[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate specific legislation?**

A. No — the BOL does not name a specific cadastral survey code or land survey act. The authority is self-executing through MENRE's BAA 13 mandate (Sec. 3(j)(1): ***"Accelerate cadastral surveys, land use planning and public land titling"***), but effective exercise requires: (a) Presidential delegation of authority; (b) institutional capacity for survey operations; and (c) coordination protocols with national agencies. Legislation would formalize these requirements.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Two express conditions make this among the **most constrained** of the 55 powers:

   (1) **Presidential authority requirement**: ***"upon authority of the President"*** — the Bangsamoro Government cannot conduct cadastral surveys unilaterally. Each survey initiative requires Presidential authorization. This is unique — most BOL powers can be exercised autonomously or with coordination; this one requires affirmative permission from the President.[^2]

   (2) **National integration requirement**: survey results ***"shall furnish ... to, and coordinate with, the relevant national government agencies to effect inclusion into the National Cadastral Survey."*** This ensures that Bangsamoro surveys produce nationally-recognized data, not a parallel land information system.[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any body for this power?**

A. No dedicated body. MENRE's **Land Management Services** (with its Surveys Division and Land Utilization Development Division) under BAA 13 handles survey operations. No standalone Bangsamoro Land Survey Authority exists.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply?**

A. Three provisions:

   (1) **Regalian Doctrine (Art. XII, Sec. 2)**: All lands of the public domain are owned by the State. Cadastral surveys serve to identify, classify, and administer state-owned lands — the survey function operates within this constitutional framework.

   (2) **Land Classification (Art. XII, Sec. 3)**: ***"Lands of the public domain are classified into agricultural, forest or timber, mineral lands, and national parks."*** Cadastral surveys identify land boundaries that enable classification. Only Congress can determine how much alienable land can be acquired — the BOL allows Bangsamoro to ***"recommend"*** classification, not decide it.

   (3) **IP Ancestral Lands (Art. XII, Sec. 5)**: ***"The State ... shall protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities to their ancestral lands."*** Cadastral surveys must not extinguish ancestral domain claims.

**Q. What is the constitutional floor?**

A. The constitutional floor is: (a) **state ownership** of public domain lands — cadastral surveys identify and delineate state lands, they do not create private ownership; (b) **Presidential land classification prerogative** — the Bangsamoro Government recommends, the President decides; (c) **IP ancestral land protection** — surveys cannot override constitutional ancestral domain protections.

**Q. Why does the BOL require Presidential authority for cadastral surveys?**

A. Because land classification is a **Regalian function** under the Constitution. The President, as head of State, exercises authority over the disposition of public lands. Cadastral surveys directly enable land classification and patent issuance — functions that under the Regalian doctrine require presidential authority. The BOL carefully preserves this constitutional structure while granting the Bangsamoro Government the **operational capacity** to conduct surveys — but not the authority to initiate them unilaterally.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the national framework provide?**

A. The national framework is built on:
   - **CA 141 (Public Land Act)** — the fundamental law governing public land surveys, classification, and disposition.
   - **PD 239** — prescribes procedures for conducting cadastral surveys.
   - **PD 1529 (Property Registration Decree)** — governs the Torrens registration system that receives cadastral survey data.
   - **RA 9176 (Geodetic Engineering Act)** — regulates surveyor qualifications.
   - **RA 10023/RA 11573** — free patent laws that depend on cadastral survey completion.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority?**

A. Three national institutions:
   - **DENR-Land Management Bureau (LMB)** — primary national agency for land surveys, land classification, and public land administration.
   - **National Mapping and Resource Information Authority (NAMRIA)** — maintains the national geodetic framework, base maps, and geographic information systems.
   - **Land Registration Authority (LRA)** — manages the Torrens system and Registers of Deeds where survey-based titles are registered.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national laws listed above apply directly because: (a) the BOL requires Presidential authority and national agency coordination — the national land survey framework is not superseded but rather integrated; (b) survey results must be compatible with the national system (PD 239, PD 1529) to be legally valid; (c) geodetic engineers must be licensed under national law (RA 9176).

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry is primarily responsible?**

A. **MENRE** through its **Land Management Services** — specifically the **Surveys Division** and **Land Utilization Development Division** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title III, Sec. 9(7)).[^3]

**Q. What are MENRE's relevant functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III, Sec. 3 provides:
   - (j)(1) ***"Accelerate cadastral surveys, land use planning and public land titling"***
   - (f) ***"Undertake exploration, assessment, classification and inventory of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region's environment, natural resources, and energy"***
   - (h) ***"Undertake geological surveys of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"***
   - (l) ***"Exercise administration and supervision of all lands of the public domain within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"***[^3]

The Surveys Division under Land Management Services is the specific unit responsible for cadastral survey operations.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Does MENRE have the operational capacity for cadastral surveys?**

A. MENRE has the **institutional mandate** but the question of **operational capacity** — licensed geodetic engineers, survey equipment, and field teams — remains a constraint. Provincial MENRE field offices (PENREOs) and Community offices (CENREOs) exist in each province (BAA 13, Sec. 10-11) but their survey capacity is limited. Building this capacity is critical for exercising this power.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government exercise authority within BARMM?**

A. **DENR-LMB** conducts and supervises cadastral surveys nationwide, including within BARMM. **NAMRIA** provides the geodetic reference framework and base maps. In practice, BARMM provinces — particularly in conflict-affected areas — have **significant cadastral survey backlogs**. Many municipalities lack completed cadastral surveys, leaving land tenure insecure and land classification incomplete.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has BARMM accomplished?**

A. MENRE has been **institutionally established** with a Land Management Services division capable of supporting survey operations. However: (a) **no standing Presidential delegation** of cadastral survey authority has been obtained; (b) **no data-sharing agreement** with NAMRIA and DENR-LMB has been formalized; (c) **no standalone legislation** has been enacted.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back?**

A. Three constraints: (1) **Presidential authority requirement** — each survey initiative requires Presidential authorization, creating an administrative bottleneck. No standing delegation or blanket authorization exists; (2) **Technical capacity** — shortage of licensed geodetic engineers and survey equipment within MENRE; (3) **No formal coordination protocol** with NAMRIA and DENR-LMB for data integration.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. DENR-LMB has: (a) decades of institutional experience in cadastral survey operations; (b) an established cadre of geodetic engineers and survey teams; (c) the **National Cadastral Survey database** into which Bangsamoro surveys must be integrated; (d) **NAMRIA's geodetic reference framework** — the technical foundation for all survey work. BARMM must build survey capacity from a lower baseline while meeting the technical standards of this established system.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. (1) **Massive survey backlog** — BARMM provinces, particularly in formerly conflict-affected Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, Lanao del Sur, and the island provinces, have some of the **largest unsurveyed areas** in the Philippines. National survey capacity has historically been insufficient for these areas. (2) **Conflict-affected land tenure** — displacement, multiple occupancy claims, and destroyed records require survey approaches beyond standard cadastral procedures. (3) **Indigenous land tenure** — standard cadastral processes do not adequately account for *fusaka inged* and communal land holdings.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes, indirectly. Cadastral surveys in BARMM intersect with **Islamic customary land tenure** — *fusaka inged* (native titles recognized by BOL Art. IX, Sec. 3(a)) and *ihya al-mawat* (revival of dead land). In Bangsamoro communities, land rights have historically been established through customary processes — long-term occupation, communal allocation by traditional leaders, and Islamic inheritance (*mirath*). The Torrens system introduced through colonial-era laws often failed to recognize these customary claims, resulting in the **unjust dispossession** that BOL Art. IX, Sec. 2 specifically addresses.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant?**

A. Three principles intersect:

   (1) ***Fusaka inged*** — recognized in enacted law (BOL Art. IX, Sec. 3(a); BAA 64). Cadastral surveys must include protocols to identify and protect native title claims.

   (2) ***Ihya al-mawat*** — Islamic recognition of land rights through productive cultivation. Relevant to long-term cultivators without formal titles.

   (3) ***Amanah*** — land administration as a trust. Government survey processes should serve justice, not merely administrative convenience.

**Q. How should Shari'ah considerations be integrated?**

A. A Bangsamoro Land Survey Act should include: (a) mandatory **native title screening** before conducting cadastral surveys in areas with potential ancestral domain claims; (b) **community consultation** protocols recognizing Islamic and indigenous land tenure traditions; (c) **dispute resolution mechanisms** that account for customary land allocation practices.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can BARMM diverge from national cadastral survey law?**

A. BARMM has **very limited** authority to diverge. This is one of the **most constrained** powers because: (a) **Presidential authority** is required — the Bangsamoro Government cannot unilaterally change survey procedures; (b) **national integration** is mandatory — survey results must be compatible with and included in the National Cadastral Survey; (c) **technical standards** under RA 9176 and PD 239 must be met for surveys to produce legally valid data.

However, BARMM can: (a) establish **additional survey protocols** — e.g., mandatory native title screening, community consultation requirements, conflict-sensitive approaches — that **supplement** but do not contradict national standards; (b) create **institutional structures** (a dedicated Land Survey Authority or strengthened MENRE capacity) to accelerate survey work; (c) negotiate a **standing delegation** from the President, reducing the per-survey authorization bottleneck.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. The key risk is producing survey data that **fails national integration** — surveys not meeting PD 239 and NAMRIA standards would not be included in the National Cadastral Survey, rendering the work legally useless for land registration purposes. Any Bangsamoro legislation must ensure full compatibility with national technical standards while adding region-specific protocols.

**Q. What cross-references from other themes constrain divergence?**

A. The constitutional constraints from the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) (Regalian doctrine, Presidential land classification prerogative) and the national law framework from the **National Law Considerations** section (Section C) (CA 141, PD 239, PD 1529, RA 9176) are binding. The Presidential authority requirement from the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A) is the most significant constraint — it cannot be legislated away; it can only be addressed through a standing delegation agreement.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions?**

A. The primary jurisdictional question is the **relationship between MENRE's Surveys Division and DENR-LMB**. Both have authority over land surveys within BARMM — MENRE under BAA 13, DENR-LMB under national law. No formal protocol establishes which agency leads survey operations, how survey requests are initiated, or how Presidential authorization is obtained.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **No pending Parliament Bill** addresses cadastral land surveys. This power has received no legislative attention — no filed PB, no resolution, no committee action identified.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 12** (Environment and Natural Resources) depends on cadastral surveys for land administration, resource mapping, and tenure security. **BDP Chapter 3** (Spatial Development Strategy) depends entirely on cadastral data for spatial planning, land use zoning, and urban-rural development frameworks. Without completed cadastral surveys, **land use plans cannot be grounded in authoritative boundary data**.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without accelerated cadastral surveys: (1) **Land tenure remains insecure** — occupants cannot obtain titles, reducing investment incentives and increasing land conflict. (2) **Land classification cannot proceed** — the Bangsamoro Government cannot recommend classification to the President without survey data, delaying the transition of public lands to alienable/disposable status. (3) **Spatial planning lacks data foundation** — BDP Chapter 3 goals cannot be operationalized. (4) **Free patent applications stall** — RA 10023 and RA 11573 benefits cannot reach BARMM residents without completed surveys. (5) **Ancestral domain delineation slows** — CADT processing under BAA 64 depends on survey data.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why is this power uniquely dependent on Presidential authorization?**

A. Among the 55 BOL powers, cadastral survey is one of the few that requires ***"authority of the President"*** as a precondition for exercise. This reflects the **Regalian doctrine** — public land administration is a sovereign function. The President exercises general supervision over autonomous regions (1987 Constitution, Art. X, Sec. 16; BOL Art. VI, Sec. 1). Because cadastral surveys directly enable land classification and alienation of public domain lands, the constitutional framework requires presidential involvement. A **standing delegation of authority** — a general authorization from the President for BARMM to conduct specified categories of surveys — would be the most practical approach to removing this bottleneck while preserving the constitutional requirement.

**Q. How does this power relate to power (j) Classification of public lands?**

A. Power (h) — cadastral survey — and power (j) — classification of public lands — are **sequential and interdependent**. Cadastral surveys (h) produce the **spatial data** on which land classification (j) depends. The BOL treats both under the same section (Art. XIII, Sec. 17), signaling their interconnection. Both require Presidential involvement — surveys require Presidential ***"authority"***; classification requires Presidential ***"recommendation"*** approval.

**Q. How does this power relate to power (d) Ancestral domain and natural resources?**

A. Cadastral surveys must account for **ancestral domain claims** under power (d). Areas covered by CADTs or pending CADT applications require special survey protocols — the survey must recognize ancestral domain boundaries rather than treating the land as unencumbered public domain. BAA 64 (IP Act) and IPRA (RA 8371) both require FPIC for activities within ancestral domains, potentially including cadastral surveys. See the legal reference for **power (d)** for the full ancestral domain analysis.

**Q. What is the practical significance of the "National Cadastral Survey" integration requirement?**

A. The requirement to ***"furnish the results ... to effect inclusion into the National Cadastral Survey"*** ensures that Bangsamoro surveys produce **legally authoritative data** recognized across the Philippine land administration system. Without national integration: (a) land titles based on Bangsamoro surveys could be challenged as invalid; (b) survey data would not appear in national databases used by courts, banks, and government agencies; (c) the Torrens system could not process registrations based on non-integrated survey data. The integration requirement is a **technical necessity**, not merely an administrative formality.
