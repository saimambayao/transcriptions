# (q) Customary Laws

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(q)** — ***"Customary laws;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(q) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement for customary laws. However, Art. IX, Sec. 3 constrains measures affecting non-Moro IPs to not diminish rights under RA 8371 (IPRA). Art. X, Sec. 1 requires justice dispensation to be ***"in consonance with the Constitution."*** |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIAP)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII; BAA 85, Chapter IX |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH); Office for Tribal Justice System (Art. X, Sec. 18 — mandated but not yet fully established through standalone legislation) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 6 — Cultural Diversity and Heritage; Chapter 10 — Peace and Security |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | Art. X, Sec. 17: ***"The Parliament shall enact laws to promote and support the traditional or tribal justice systems."*** (Not one of the 7 priority codes in Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a).) |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over customary laws?**

A. Article V, Section 2(q) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"customary laws"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a broad grant covering the **recognition**, **codification**, **application**, and **development** of customs, beliefs, and traditions within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region — encompassing both **Bangsamoro Muslim** customary practices (*adat*) and the indigenous customs of **non-Moro peoples** (Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, B'laan, Higaonon).[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Seven provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) **Art. IX, Sec. 4** — the primary elaboration: ***"The customs, beliefs, and traditions of the Bangsamoro people are hereby recognized, protected, and guaranteed."*** It further mandates Parliament to ***"adopt measures to ensure mutual respect and protection of the distinct beliefs, customs, and traditions of the Bangsamoro people and the other inhabitants in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^2]

   (2) **Art. X, Sec. 1** — establishes customary law as a basis for justice: ***"The dispensation of justice in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall be in consonance with the Constitution, Shari'ah, traditional or tribal laws, and other relevant laws."***[^3]

   (3) **Art. X, Sec. 17** — express legislative mandate: ***"The Parliament shall enact laws to promote and support the traditional or tribal justice systems that are appropriate for the indigenous peoples."*** It further provides that ***"The traditional justice systems are the mechanisms to determine, settle, and decide controversies and enforce decisions involving disputes between members of the indigenous peoples concerned in accordance with the customary laws of these communities."***[^4]

   (4) **Art. X, Sec. 18** — institutional mandate: ***"There is hereby created an Office for Tribal Justice System"*** with Parliament to define its powers and functions. The Office must ***"ensure the full participation of indigenous peoples in the formulation, implementation, and evaluation of policies related to the strengthening of tribal justice systems."***[^5]

   (5) **Art. IX, Sec. 3** — protects ***"indigenous customs and traditions"*** as an enumerated right (item b) while establishing that Bangsamoro measures ***"shall not in any manner diminish"*** IPRA (RA 8371) rights.[^6]

   (6) **Art. VII, Sec. 8** — establishes that reserved parliamentary seats for non-Moro IPs ***"shall adhere to their customary laws and indigenous processes"*** — making customary law the basis for **political representation**.[^7]

   (7) **Art. X, Sec. 20** — links customary justice to ADR: ***"traditional or tribal adjudicatory tribunals may utilize"*** conciliation and mediation.[^8]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **three express legislative mandates**:

   (1) Art. IX, Sec. 4: Parliament ***"shall adopt measures"*** to ensure mutual respect and protection of customs — a general legislative mandate.

   (2) Art. X, Sec. 17: ***"The Parliament shall enact laws to promote and support the traditional or tribal justice systems"*** — a specific mandate for traditional justice legislation.

   (3) Art. X, Sec. 18: Parliament must define the ***"powers and functions"*** of the Office for Tribal Justice System — a specific institutional mandate.

   **None of these mandates have been fully fulfilled** through standalone legislation as of BAA 89. BAA 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act) partially addresses IP customary law but does not constitute the comprehensive traditional justice systems law contemplated by Art. X, Sec. 17.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. X, Sec. 18 ***"hereby created"*** the **Office for Tribal Justice System** — a BOL-mandated institution. Its functions (study, preservation, development of tribal justice) are defined in the BOL, but its powers and functions require further definition by Parliament. This office has not been fully established through standalone legislation, though BAA 64's IP framework provides some institutional basis.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three limitations apply:

   (1) **IPRA floor**: Art. IX, Sec. 3 provides that the BOL ***"shall not in any manner diminish the rights and benefits of the non-Moro indigenous peoples"*** under RA 8371. Bangsamoro customary law measures affecting non-Moro IPs cannot reduce IPRA protections.[^6]

   (2) **Constitutional consonance**: Art. X, Sec. 1 requires justice dispensation to be ***"in consonance with the Constitution."*** Customary law application in judicial proceedings must comply with constitutional requirements — particularly due process (Art. III, Sec. 1) and equal protection.[^3]

   (3) **Human rights compatibility**: Art. X, Sec. 17 requires indigenous justice systems to be ***"compatible with the national legal system and with internationally recognized human rights."*** This prevents customary practices that violate fundamental human rights from receiving legal recognition.[^4]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to customary laws?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."*** This is the universal constitutional boundary.[^11]

   (2) **Indigenous Peoples' Rights (Art. XIV, Sec. 17)**: ***"The State shall recognize, respect, and protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities to preserve and develop their cultures, traditions, and institutions."*** This is a **constitutional mandate** supporting customary law recognition.[^12]

   (3) **Due Process and Equal Protection (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, nor shall any person be denied the equal protection of the laws."*** Customary adjudication processes must satisfy minimum due process requirements — notice, opportunity to be heard, and impartial decision-making.[^13]

   (4) **Bill of Rights Protections (Art. III)**: Customary law practices that violate fundamental rights (e.g., discrimination based on gender, denial of equal protection) cannot receive legal recognition regardless of their customary standing.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. XIV, Sec. 17 mandates the State to ***"recognize, respect, and protect"*** indigenous cultural rights — this carries constitutional weight. Art. X, Sec. 20 permits autonomous regions to operate their own legal systems within the constitutional framework. The 1987 Constitution does not specifically mandate customary law codification, but its recognition of indigenous rights establishes the constitutional foundation for Bangsamoro customary law legislation.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for customary law?**

A. The national framework provides:

   - **RA 8371** (IPRA, 1997) — the most comprehensive national law recognizing indigenous customary law. It protects the right of indigenous peoples to their customary laws and justice systems, native title (*fusaka inged*), and ancestral domain. Chapter V establishes indigenous cultural communities' right to use their own dispute resolution mechanisms.
   - **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) — codifies Islamic customary practices for personal law matters. Art. 3 recognizes customary law as supplementary.
   - **RA 7160** (Local Government Code, 1991) — establishes the Katarungang Pambarangay system for community-level dispute resolution, which interfaces with customary practices.
   - **RA 10066** (National Cultural Heritage Act, 2009) and **RA 11961** (2023 amendment) — provide for the protection of intangible cultural heritage including customs and traditions.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. (1) **RA 8371** — applies as a **constitutional-level floor** per BOL Art. IX, Sec. 3. IPRA protections for non-Moro IPs cannot be diminished. (2) **PD 1083** — applies **suppletorily** for Muslim personal law matters; Bangsamoro may legislate beyond its scope. (3) **RA 7160** — applies **suppletorily** for barangay justice; BAA 49 may modify arrangements. (4) **RA 10066/RA 11961** — apply **suppletorily** for cultural heritage protection.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. The **National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP)** exercises authority over indigenous peoples' rights including customary law systems nationwide. NCIP continues to operate in BARMM alongside MIAP — Resolution No. 38 highlights tensions over NCIP's CADT delineation process in Maguindanao. When BAA 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act) is fully implemented, MIAP should exercise primary authority within BARMM, with NCIP retaining a coordination role for cross-regional matters.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIAP)** is the lead agency. BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII defines MIAP's institutional status, mandate, powers, functions, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter IX allocates its operational budget.[^14][^15]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **BAA 64** (Indigenous Peoples' Act, PB No. 273) is the primary enactment — a comprehensive IP rights law addressing customary law systems, native titles, and indigenous justice mechanisms for non-Moro IPs. **BAA 89** (Transitional Justice and Reconciliation, PB No. 353) incorporates customary approaches to justice for historical grievances. However, **no standalone Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code** exists, and the Art. X, Secs. 17-18 mandates for traditional/tribal justice legislation remain unfulfilled.

**Q. What are MIAP's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII, Chapter 1:

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): MIAP is ***"mandated to protect, promote, and preserve the interest and well-being of IPs within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region with due regard to their beliefs, customs, traditions, and institutions."***[^14]

   **Powers and Functions** (Sec. 3) — 10 enumerated functions:

   - (a) ***"Serves as the primary ministry of the Bangsamoro Government to assist IPs"***
   - (b) ***"Review and assess the conditions of IPs, including existing laws and policies pertinent thereto, and to propose relevant laws and policies for their welfare"***
   - (c) ***"Issue Certificates of Ancestral Domain Title (CADTs) to IPs"*** as may be provided by law
   - (d) ***"Formulate and implement policies, plans, programs, and projects for the economic, social, and cultural development, as well as the legal protection of the IPs"***
   - (e) ***"Request and engage the services and support of experts"***
   - (f) ***"Negotiate for funds and to accept grants, donations, gifts"*** for IP benefit
   - (g) ***"Coordinate development programs and projects for the advancement of the IPs"***
   - (h) ***"Convene periodic conventions, congresses, summits or assemblies of IPs"***
   - (i) ***"Issue Certificates of Accreditation and Certificates of Dissolution of Marriages contracted and solemnized in accordance with the customs, culture, and tradition of IPs"*** as may be provided by law
   - (j) ***"Perform such other related functions as may be provided by law"***[^16]

   **Organizational structure** (Secs. 4-13): Ministry Proper (Office of the Minister, Deputy Minister, Director-General); Bureau of Ancestral Domains/Native Title; Special Public Assistance and Legal Aid Division; Community Service Centers in municipalities with significant IP populations.

**Q. What can MIAP NOT do without standalone customary laws legislation?**

A. BAA 13 gives MIAP a general mandate focused on IP welfare and ancestral domain matters. Without standalone legislation, MIAP cannot: (a) **codify and register customary law practices** with legal effect; (b) **define the jurisdiction and procedures** of traditional/tribal justice systems with binding legal force; (c) **establish qualification standards** for traditional adjudicators; (d) **define the legal effect** of decisions rendered through customary dispute resolution; (e) **regulate the relationship** between *adat*, Shari'ah, and the formal legal system; (f) operate the **Office for Tribal Justice System** with defined powers per Art. X, Sec. 18. BAA 64 addresses IP customary law but does not cover **Bangsamoro Muslim *adat*** — the broader customary law landscape.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No resolution specifically calls for a Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code. However, **Res. No. 38** (protesting NCIP delineation) and **Res. No. 180** (IP rights recognition) signal Parliament's attention to IP customary law issues.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. **Partially correct**. MIAP's mandate covers **non-Moro indigenous customary laws** comprehensively. However, this power encompasses **two distinct spheres**: (1) non-Moro IP customs (Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, B'laan, Higaonon) — clearly within MIAP's mandate; and (2) **Bangsamoro Muslim *adat*** (Moro customary practices around governance, property, dispute resolution, community relations) — not clearly assigned to any single ministry. BCPCH handles cultural preservation but not legal codification. The **Office for Tribal Justice System** (Art. X, Sec. 18) would handle the justice dimension but has not been established through standalone legislation. A standalone code would need to assign institutional responsibility for Bangsamoro Muslim *adat* — potentially to a new body or to the OCM.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. - **MIAP** (BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. VII; BAA 85, Ch. IX) — lead for non-Moro IP customary laws; operational
   - **BCPCH** (BAA 13; BAA 85, Ch. XXII) — cultural heritage preservation including intangible heritage; operational
   - **Office for Tribal Justice System** — BOL-mandated (Art. X, Sec. 18) but not fully established through standalone legislation
   - **Shari'ah courts** — handle disputes where *adat* and Shari'ah overlap in Muslim personal law matters

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The **Office for Tribal Justice System** (Art. X, Sec. 18) is ***"hereby created"*** by the BOL but its powers and functions have not been defined by Parliament through standalone legislation. While some institutional basis exists through BAA 64, the comprehensive mandate for this office — study, preservation, and development of the entire tribal justice system — remains unfulfilled.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **NCIP** exercises authority over indigenous peoples' rights in BARMM, including: processing CADT applications, conducting ancestral domain delineation, enforcing FPIC requirements, and recognizing indigenous justice mechanisms. **NCIP and MIAP** have overlapping jurisdictions — Parliament Resolution No. 38 highlights a specific conflict where NCIP's delineation process in Maguindanao was contested by BARMM.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **partial**. MIAP exists as the Bangsamoro counterpart to NCIP, and BAA 64 provides a comprehensive IP framework. However, NCIP continues to exercise functions within BARMM — particularly CADT issuance and ancestral domain delineation — because the jurisdictional boundary between NCIP and MIAP has not been fully clarified through legislation or intergovernmental agreement.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MIAP has been institutionally established with organizational structure and budget. BAA 64 provides a comprehensive IP rights framework. BAA 89 incorporates customary justice approaches in transitional justice. BCPCH is operational for cultural heritage preservation. MIAP Community Service Centers operate in IP areas. MIAP issues Certificates of Accreditation for customary marriages. However, no Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code exists, and the Office for Tribal Justice System lacks standalone legislation.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of standalone legislation for traditional/tribal justice systems (the Art. X, Sec. 17 mandate) and Bangsamoro *adat* codification. Without this: customary dispute resolution decisions lack defined legal effect; the relationship between *adat*, Shari'ah, and formal law is undefined; traditional adjudicators have no statutory qualification standards; and the Office for Tribal Justice System cannot exercise defined powers. Enactment of a Bangsamoro Traditional and Tribal Justice Systems Act would remove this constraint.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national framework under **RA 8371** (IPRA) has been operational since 1997 — nearly three decades of institutional development. NCIP has: a national registration and delineation system for ancestral domains; established procedures for FPIC; recognition mechanisms for indigenous justice systems; regional and provincial offices with trained staff; and decades of jurisprudence interpreting indigenous rights. For Muslim customary law, **PD 1083** has been in force since 1977 — providing 49 years of codified Islamic personal law application.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. (1) **NCIP does not address Bangsamoro Muslim *adat***  — IPRA and NCIP are designed for indigenous cultural communities, not Bangsamoro Muslim customary practices. (2) **PD 1083 is limited to personal law** — it does not address broader *adat* practices in governance, community relations, or non-personal property. (3) **NCIP's one-size-fits-all framework** does not account for the post-conflict context in BARMM or the unique relationship between *adat*, Shari'ah, and tribal justice in the Bangsamoro region.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Customary laws have a **significant Shari'ah dimension** in the Bangsamoro context. Islamic jurisprudence recognizes ***'urf*** (custom) as a subsidiary source of law under the maxim ***"al-'adah muhakkamah"*** (custom is authoritative). Bangsamoro *adat* — covering marriage customs, property arrangements, dispute resolution (*rido* settlement), inheritance practices, and community governance — operates alongside Shari'ah. The relationship between *adat* and Shari'ah is a **defining feature** of Bangsamoro legal identity: in many communities, customary practices and Islamic law are intertwined and mutually reinforcing.[^9]

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. Art. X, Sec. 1 places customary law alongside Shari'ah as a basis for justice dispensation: ***"Shari'ah, traditional or tribal laws."*** Art. X, Sec. 4 grants Parliament authority to enact Shari'ah-based laws including those on ***"property"*** and ***"commercial"*** matters — areas where *adat* and Shari'ah may overlap. The BOL treats Shari'ah and customary law as **complementary** rather than competing systems.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Three principles directly govern:

   (1) ***'Urf*** (custom) — the central principle. Valid custom that does not contradict Shari'ah is recognized as binding. Legislation should define which customary practices receive legal recognition and the process for validating customs against Shari'ah standards.

   (2) ***Sulh*** (conciliation/settlement) — the preferred Islamic method of dispute resolution. Many Bangsamoro customary dispute practices (elder-mediated *rido* settlement, property negotiation) follow *sulh* principles.

   (3) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — codification and preservation of customary laws serves the common good by providing legal certainty and preserving cultural heritage.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) governs Muslim personal status, marriage, divorce, succession, and property. Art. 3 of PD 1083 recognizes that ***"customary law shall supplement the provisions of this Code"*** — establishing precedent for customary law as a supplementary legal source. However, PD 1083 is limited to personal law matters and does not address broader *adat* practices in governance, community relations, or non-personal property disputes. **Shari'ah courts** have jurisdiction over property and commercial disputes among Muslims — disputes involving *adat*-based arrangements could fall within this jurisdiction.[^9][^10]

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro customary law legislation?**

A. A Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code should adopt a **principled approach**: customary practices that are consistent with or neutral toward Shari'ah receive legal recognition; practices that directly contradict established Shari'ah rules (e.g., customs involving prohibited elements) require a **reconciliation mechanism**. The code should establish a **Shari'ah review process** — potentially through the *Darul Ifta'* or a designated body — to evaluate contested customs. For non-Moro indigenous customary practices, Shari'ah compliance is not applicable — these are governed by Art. IX, Sec. 3 and IPRA standards.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law in this area?**

A. BARMM has **broad legislative authority** to diverge because: (1) the BOL places **no national law consistency requirement** on customary laws — no "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier appears in Art. V, Sec. 2(q) or Art. IX, Sec. 4; (2) Art. X, Sec. 17 **expressly mandates** Parliament to enact traditional justice legislation — congressional authorization for a distinct framework; (3) the BOL itself is a congressional act (RA 11054).

   The **limits of divergence** are: the **IPRA floor** established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A) (Art. IX, Sec. 3) — cannot diminish non-Moro IP rights under RA 8371; the **constitutional consonance** requirement from Art. X, Sec. 1; the **human rights compatibility** requirement from Art. X, Sec. 17; and the **due process and equal protection** floor from the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) (Art. III, Sec. 1). In practice, BARMM can: create a **separate customary law recognition system**; define **legal effect for customary adjudication**; establish **qualification standards for traditional adjudicators**; and codify the **relationship between *adat*, Shari'ah, and formal law** — none of which the national system provides.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts legislation on this subject?**

A. A Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code would govern within BARMM; PD 1083 would continue to apply suppletorily for Muslim personal law matters not covered by the code; RA 8371 protections remain as the floor for non-Moro IPs per Art. IX, Sec. 3; and RA 7160's Katarungang Pambarangay provisions would be superseded within BARMM to the extent the Bangsamoro code establishes alternative community dispute resolution mechanisms.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between **MIAP and NCIP**. NCIP under RA 8371 exercises authority over indigenous peoples' rights nationwide, including within BARMM. MIAP under BAA 13 and BAA 64 exercises authority over IP affairs within BARMM. Res. No. 38 highlights a specific jurisdictional conflict over CADT delineation. Legislative or intergovernmental action is needed to clarify: (a) whether **MIAP replaces NCIP within BARMM** for all IP functions; (b) how **cross-regional ancestral domain claims** are processed; and (c) the **transitional process** for pending NCIP proceedings within BARMM.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB No. 174** (Barangay Justice System Traditional and Customary Laws Advisory Council) addresses the community-level integration of customary law into justice. **PB No. 204** (Cultural Heritage) addresses cultural heritage preservation including customary traditions. Neither addresses the comprehensive Art. X, Secs. 17-18 mandate for traditional/tribal justice systems or Bangsamoro *adat* codification. **Significant gaps remain even if these PBs pass**: no comprehensive customary laws code, no traditional justice systems act, and no *adat* documentation program.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage) targets the preservation of cultural heritage — which includes customary law traditions as living cultural practices. Chapter 10 (Peace and Security) envisions community-level peace and security systems that integrate traditional dispute resolution. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 2 (Social Transformation)**, depends on cultural identity preservation, including customary practices, as a foundation for social cohesion.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without customary laws legislation: (1) traditional dispute resolution has **no defined legal effect** — decisions by community elders and tribal adjudicators cannot be enforced through the formal legal system; (2) the **Office for Tribal Justice System** cannot exercise defined powers, leaving a BOL-mandated institution without an operational mandate; (3) **Bangsamoro *adat*** practices risk being lost as communities modernize — oral traditions require systematic documentation; (4) BDP Chapter 10 targets for community-level peace systems remain **aspirational** rather than institutionalized; and (5) the **MIAP-NCIP jurisdictional overlap** continues to create confusion for IP communities seeking recognition of their customary rights.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the relationship between Bangsamoro *adat* (Moro customary law) and non-Moro indigenous customary law?**

A. This power encompasses **two distinct legal traditions**: (1) **Bangsamoro Muslim *adat*** — customary practices of the Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, Yakan, Iranun, and other Bangsamoro peoples, which operate alongside and sometimes intertwined with Shari'ah; and (2) **non-Moro indigenous customs** — practices of the Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, B'laan, Higaonon, and other non-Moro indigenous peoples, governed by tribal law and traditions distinct from Islamic law. BAA 64 addresses the second category; the first has **no dedicated legislative framework**. A comprehensive Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code would need to address both traditions — with different institutional mechanisms for each, given the Shari'ah dimension in Bangsamoro Muslim *adat*.

**Q. How does customary law interact with Art. VII, Sec. 8's political representation provision?**

A. Art. VII, Sec. 8 provides that reserved parliamentary seats for non-Moro IPs ***"shall adhere to their customary laws and indigenous processes."*** This is one of the few BOL provisions where customary law has a **directly political** function — determining who represents IP communities in Parliament. The provision establishes ***"primacy of customary laws and practices"*** and ***"primacy of consensus building"*** as guiding principles. This means the selection of IP parliamentary representatives is governed by indigenous processes rather than standard electoral law — a unique application of customary law to democratic governance.

**Q. What is the scope of the unfulfilled Art. X, Secs. 17-18 mandate?**

A. Art. X, Sec. 17 mandates Parliament to ***"enact laws to promote and support the traditional or tribal justice systems."*** This is broader than what BAA 64 provides. BAA 64 addresses IP customary law in the context of IP rights generally; Art. X, Sec. 17 envisions a **justice system law** that: defines jurisdiction of traditional/tribal tribunals; establishes procedures for customary adjudication; determines the **legal effect** of decisions (binding, advisory, or subject to confirmation by formal courts); defines qualification and accountability standards for traditional adjudicators; and establishes the **relationship between customary justice, Shari'ah courts, and regular courts**. Art. X, Sec. 18 creates the **Office for Tribal Justice System** to oversee the study, preservation, and development of tribal justice — requiring a separate institutional enabling law.

**Q. How does this power interact with power (ff) Indigenous peoples' rights?**

A. Power (ff) — ***"indigenous peoples' rights"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(ff)) — is the broader IP rights power under which customary law for non-Moro IPs is situated. Power (q) encompasses customary law for **all peoples** in BARMM (both Bangsamoro Muslim and non-Moro indigenous), while power (ff) is specific to non-Moro indigenous peoples. BAA 64 implements power (ff) and addresses customary law within that context. A Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code would implement power (q) comprehensively — including the Bangsamoro Muslim *adat* dimension that power (ff) does not cover. MIAP is the institutional link between the two powers.
