## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The Bangsamoro Organic Law grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over disaster risk reduction and management (DRRM) as one of 55 enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2(s). The BOL envisions a comprehensive DRRM system led by the highest executive authority in the region — the Chief Minister.

Article XIII, Section 7 is the primary elaboration. It creates the Bangsamoro DRRM Council headed by the Chief Minister, with "powers and functions that shall be defined by the Parliament in a law providing for disaster preparedness and response." The Council is mandated to formulate the Bangsamoro DRRM Plan, which "shall complement that of the National Government." Critically, the Chief Minister is empowered to "recommend to the President the mobilization of resources of national defense in times of disaster." The provision also requires relevant national and Bangsamoro agencies to "cooperate and coordinate on disaster risk reduction and management."

On climate change, Section 7 requires the Bangsamoro Government, "consistent with Republic Act No. 9729, otherwise known as the 'Climate Change Act of 2009,'" to ensure that its LGUs formulate local climate change action plans. Two other BOL provisions reinforce this power: Article VII, Section 32(g) empowers the Chief Minister to "proclaim a state of calamity whenever typhoons, flash floods, earthquakes, tsunamis, or other natural or man-made calamities that cause widespread damage or destruction to life or property," and Article VII, Section 42 authorizes Parliament to create offices for disaster risk reduction and management.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Bangsamoro DRRM Council exists under the Office of the Chief Minister, as mandated by the BOL. However, no standalone BAA has been enacted to define its powers, functions, organizational structure, or operational framework. The Council operates under BAA 13 (Administrative Code) general mandate and its BOL constitutional basis. BAA 85 (General Appropriations Act, FY 2026) provides a Quick Response Fund (Chapter XXXV) — a dedicated special purpose fund for immediate disaster response.

Without standalone legislation, the DRRM Council can coordinate disaster response, activate emergency protocols, and deploy the Quick Response Fund. However, it lacks: codified regulatory authority to set DRRM standards for LGUs, a legally mandated institutional structure with defined roles for each ministry, enforceable requirements for DRRM plans at the provincial and municipal levels, and a formalized Bangsamoro DRRM Plan with legislative force. The Chief Minister has exercised the state of calamity proclamation power (Art. VII, Sec. 32(g)) on at least one occasion — the COVID-19 pandemic response.

Parliament has filed several DRRM-related bills: PB No. 182 (Multi-Hazard and Flood Hazard Maps), PB No. 227 (Mandatory Evacuation Centers), PB No. 242 (Bangsamoro Climate Change Commission), and PB No. 297 (Resilient Development through Mandatory Risk Assessments). Resolution 213 urged proactive DRRM measures. Multiple resolutions (282-292, 297) commended disaster relief efforts during Severe Tropical Storm Paeng, and Resolution 356 urged housing for typhoon victims.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL explicitly requires climate change adaptation to be "consistent with Republic Act No. 9729" (Climate Change Act of 2009). This means the Bangsamoro Government's climate change framework must align with the national Climate Change Commission's policies, and LGU climate change action plans must follow national guidelines. RA 10121 (Philippine Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Act of 2010) establishes the national DRRM system, including the National DRRM Council and the requirement for LGU DRRM plans. While the BOL creates a separate Bangsamoro DRRM Council, the national framework applies suppletorily for standards, protocols, and coordination mechanisms not yet covered by Bangsamoro legislation.

The BOL mandates cooperation and coordination between national and Bangsamoro agencies on DRRM. In practice, the Office of Civil Defense (OCD), Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA), and Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PHIVOLCS) continue to operate within BARMM for early warning and monitoring. The Armed Forces of the Philippines provides disaster response resources that the Chief Minister can request through the President.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The most significant gap is the unfulfilled BOL mandate in Article XIII, Section 7: the Parliament has not yet enacted the law defining the Bangsamoro DRRM Council's powers and functions. This is one of the BOL's explicit legislative mandates — the BOL creates the Council and requires Parliament to define its legal framework. Without this law, the Council lacks: a defined organizational structure, clear delineation of roles among ministries, enforceable standards for LGU DRRM plans, a legally mandated Bangsamoro DRRM Plan, and a disaster fund management framework beyond the annual Quick Response Fund.

A comprehensive Bangsamoro DRRM Act would codify: the DRRM Council's organizational structure, powers, and functions; a Bangsamoro DRRM Plan formulation process; DRRM standards for LGUs (including mandatory evacuation center requirements, hazard mapping, and early warning systems); a permanent disaster fund beyond the annual Quick Response Fund; coordination protocols with national agencies (OCD, PAGASA, PHIVOLCS, AFP); community-based DRRM programs; and post-disaster rehabilitation and reconstruction standards. PB No. 242 (Bangsamoro Climate Change Commission) addresses the climate change aspect, but the broader DRRM institutional framework remains unlegislated.

### Development and Policy Connection

BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) addresses DRRM as essential to protecting development gains. BARMM is highly vulnerable to natural disasters — the Sulu Sea provinces face typhoon and earthquake risks, Maguindanao's marshlands are flood-prone, and Marawi's post-siege reconstruction adds conflict-related displacement to natural disaster vulnerability. The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028) recognizes disaster resilience as a cross-cutting concern under its governance and sustainability pillars. Without DRRM legislation, the region cannot enforce disaster preparedness standards, ensure systematic post-disaster recovery, or build the institutional resilience that protects BDP investments from being wiped out by disasters.
