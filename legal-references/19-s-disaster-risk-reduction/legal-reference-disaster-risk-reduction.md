# (s) Disaster Risk Reduction and Management

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(s)** — ***"Disaster risk reduction and management;"***

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over disaster risk reduction and management?**

A. Article V, Section 2(s) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"disaster risk reduction and management"*** as one of the 55 powers over which the Bangsamoro Government exercises authority. The power covers the **full DRRM cycle** — prevention, mitigation, preparedness, response, recovery, and rehabilitation — for both natural and man-made disasters within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Four provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 7 is the **primary elaboration**. It creates the ***"Bangsamoro Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council headed by the Chief Minister"*** with ***"powers and functions that shall be defined by the Parliament in a law providing for disaster preparedness and response."*** The Council must formulate the ***"Bangsamoro Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Plan, which shall complement that of the National Government."*** The Chief Minister may ***"recommend to the President the mobilization of resources of national defense in times of disaster."*** Climate change adaptation must be ***"consistent with Republic Act No. 9729."*** Relevant national and Bangsamoro agencies ***"shall cooperate and coordinate."***[^2]

   (2) Art. VII, Sec. 32(g) empowers the Chief Minister to ***"proclaim a state of calamity whenever typhoons, flash floods, earthquakes, tsunamis, or other natural or man-made calamities that cause widespread damage or destruction to life or property."***[^3]

   (3) Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes Parliament to ***"create other offices such as... disaster risk reduction and management."***[^4]

   (4) Art. IX, Sec. 8 obligates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"maintain appropriate disaster-preparedness units for immediate and effective relief services"*** and ***"ensure the rehabilitation of calamity-affected areas."***[^5]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. XIII, Sec. 7 contains an **express legislative mandate** — the DRRM Council's ***"powers and functions... shall be defined by the Parliament in a law providing for disaster preparedness and response."*** This is one of the clearest legislative mandates in the BOL, naming both the institution (DRRM Council) and the type of law (providing for disaster preparedness and response). **This mandate remains unfulfilled as of BAA 89.**[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL **directly creates** the Bangsamoro DRRM Council (Art. XIII, Sec. 7) — the BOL itself says ***"There is hereby created."*** This is not a mandate to create; it is a creation by the organic law itself. The Council is headed by the Chief Minister. Parliament's mandate is to **define its powers and functions**, not to create it. Additionally, Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes Parliament to create a dedicated DRRM office — READi under BAA 13 serves this function.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three limitations apply:

   (1) **Climate change consistency**: Art. XIII, Sec. 7 requires climate change adaptation to be ***"consistent with Republic Act No. 9729."*** This is the most specific national law consistency requirement — citing the law by name and number.[^2]

   (2) **Complementary DRRM Plan**: The Bangsamoro DRRM Plan ***"shall complement that of the National Government"*** — not replace or contradict it.[^2]

   (3) **Intergovernmental cooperation**: National and Bangsamoro agencies ***"shall cooperate and coordinate on disaster risk reduction and management."*** This is mandatory cooperation, not optional.[^2]

   The Chief Minister's calamity proclamation power (Art. VII, Sec. 32(g)) requires no national government approval. However, mobilization of national defense resources requires recommendation ***"to the President"*** — the President retains authority over military resources.[^3]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to disaster risk reduction and management?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: the universal constitutional boundary.[^6]

   (2) **Right to Life (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life... without due process of law."*** The government's obligation to protect life includes disaster preparedness and response. Failure to maintain adequate DRRM systems implicates this constitutional right.

   (3) **Social Justice (Art. XIII, Sec. 1)**: The State must ***"give highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity."*** DRRM legislation protects human dignity by preventing preventable disaster deaths and homelessness.

   (4) **State Policy on Disaster (Art. II, Sec. 16)**: ***"The State shall protect and advance the right of the people to a balanced and healthful ecology."*** Environmental protection includes climate change adaptation and disaster resilience.

**Q. What constitutional mandates for DRRM legislation or institutions exist?**

A. No specific constitutional mandate for DRRM legislation exists. The constitutional basis for DRRM is derived from the **general welfare clause** and the **right to life**. The BOL itself — enacted by Congress pursuant to Art. X of the Constitution — provides the specific legislative mandate for Bangsamoro DRRM through Art. XIII, Sec. 7.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for DRRM?**

A. The national framework is comprehensive. **RA 10121** (Philippine DRRM Act of 2010) establishes the entire national DRRM system: the **National DRRM Council (NDRRMC)** chaired by the Secretary of National Defense; **Office of Civil Defense (OCD)** as the implementing arm; mandatory **LGU DRRM councils and offices** at all levels; a **5% Local DRRM Fund** allocation from the annual budget of each LGU; the four thematic areas of DRRM (disaster prevention and mitigation, disaster preparedness, disaster response, disaster rehabilitation and recovery); mandatory **DRRM plans** at national, regional, and local levels; and the **National DRRM Framework** as the policy guide. **RA 9729** (Climate Change Act, as amended by RA 10174) creates the **Climate Change Commission (CCC)** and the **People's Survival Fund** — both expressly applicable to BARMM per Art. XIII, Sec. 7.[^7][^8]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. (1) **RA 10121** — applies suppletorily. LGU DRRM councils in BARMM follow RA 10121 requirements. The Bangsamoro DRRM Council under the BOL is a **parallel regional structure** not contemplated by RA 10121 (which creates Regional DRRMCs through OCD regional offices).[^7]

   (2) **RA 9729/RA 10174** — **directly applicable** per Art. XIII, Sec. 7's express consistency requirement. LGUs must formulate local climate change action plans. The People's Survival Fund is accessible to BARMM LGUs.[^8]

   (3) **RA 11292** (Seal of Good Local Governance) — suppletory; includes DRRM performance criteria.

   (4) **RA 12019** — suppletory; establishes mechanisms for climate loss and damage response.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for DRRM?**

A. The **Office of Civil Defense (OCD)** maintains regional operations for DRRM coordination. **PAGASA** provides weather monitoring, forecasts, and warnings. **PHIVOLCS** monitors seismic and volcanic activity. **DENR-MGB** (Mines and Geosciences Bureau) provides geological hazard assessments. The **Armed Forces of the Philippines (AFP)** provides disaster response resources — the Chief Minister can request their mobilization through the President. The **Climate Change Commission (CCC)** coordinates national climate change programs applicable to BARMM. **DSWD** provides disaster relief and social protection services.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Office of the Chief Minister (OCM)** is the lead through the Bangsamoro DRRM Council, which the Chief Minister chairs. The **Bangsamoro READi** office, attached to MILG, serves as the DRRMC's executing office and secretariat (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VIII, Ch. 8, Sec. 26).[^9]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone DRRM Act** has been enacted as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through BAA 13's institutional provisions (DRRM Council and READi) and BAA 85's Quick Response Fund. The Art. XIII, Sec. 7 legislative mandate remains unfulfilled.

**Q. What are the DRRM Council and READi's mandates and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 provides two institutional layers:

   **Bangsamoro DRRM Council** (Title III, Ch. 2, Secs. 9-11):
   - Chaired by the **Chief Minister**
   - Vice-Chair for Disaster Mitigation and Prevention: **Minister of Science and Technology**
   - Vice-Chair for Disaster Preparedness: **Minister of the Interior and Local Government**
   - Vice-Chair for Disaster Response: **Minister of Social Services and Development**
   - Vice-Chair for Disaster Recovery and Rehabilitation: **Bangsamoro Director-General of BPDA**
   - Member-agencies identified by the Chief Minister
   - Functions (Sec. 10): ***"coordinate, integrate, supervise, and evaluate the activities of the Local Disaster Risk Reduction Management Councils (LDRRMCs)"***; ensure disaster-sensitive regional development plans; convene regional line agencies in emergencies; utilize READi facilities
   - State of Calamity (Sec. 11): Chief Minister may proclaim state of calamity based on criteria set by the DRRMC; declaration may warrant domestic and international humanitarian assistance[^10]

   **Bangsamoro READi** (Book VI, Title VIII, Ch. 8, Secs. 26-29):
   - Attached to **MILG**; headed by a Director II appointed by the Chief Minister
   - **10 enumerated functions**: (a) advise BDRRMC consistent with national and regional laws; (b) formulate and implement RDRRMP, ensuring LGU plans are consistent; (c) review and evaluate LDRRMPs for integration into CDPs and CLUPs; (d) formulate SOPs for assessment teams, information sharing, and coordination; (e) ensure government agencies and LGUs prioritize DRRM; (f) create enabling environment for CSO and volunteer participation; (g) provide technical assistance to LGUs, especially low-income and high-risk ones; (h) manage the Regional Disaster Risk Reduction Fund; (i) report on RDRRF utilization; (j) other functions as provided by law
   - **Three divisions**: (a) Administration and Training Division — trainings, hazard assessment, public awareness, response team supervision; (b) Research and Planning Division — DRRM planning, risk assessment, contingency planning, annual plan and budget preparation; (c) Emergency Operations Division — early warning systems, monitoring, relief operations, coordination
   - **Multi-ministry representation** (Sec. 28): MSSD, MILG, MOST, MTC, and BPDA represented in READi
   - **Emergency Operations Center** (Sec. 29): established within READi to support DRRMC and RPOC[^9]

**Q. What can the DRRM Council and READi NOT do without a standalone DRRM Act?**

A. While BAA 13 provides a substantial institutional framework, it does not provide: **codified regulatory authority** to set enforceable DRRM standards for LGUs (BAA 13, Sec. 10 uses "coordinate, integrate, supervise, and evaluate" — but without penalty provisions or enforcement mechanisms); a **legally mandated Bangsamoro DRRM Plan** with legislative force (the RDRRMP is formulated by READi but has no statutory backing beyond BAA 13's general mandate); **mandatory evacuation center standards** at the barangay level; a **permanent disaster fund** beyond the annual Quick Response Fund appropriation; **mandatory hazard mapping** requirements for all BARMM territories; formal **coordination protocols** with national agencies (OCD, PAGASA, PHIVOLCS, AFP); or a **post-disaster rehabilitation framework** with systematic standards for reconstruction.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. **Res. 213** urges proactive DRRM measures. **Res. 282-292** commended disaster relief for STS Paeng (2022). **Res. 356** urges housing for typhoon victims. **Res. 374** urges El Nino preparedness. **Res. 476** urges extension of lease for Marawi IDP shelters. These collectively demonstrate Parliament's **active engagement** with DRRM issues and recognition of gaps.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL itself places the DRRM Council under the **Chief Minister** (Art. XIII, Sec. 7), making the OCM the correct lead. BAA 13's four-Vice-Chair structure (MOST for mitigation, MILG for preparedness, MSSD for response, BPDA for recovery) appropriately distributes DRRM functions across the relevant ministries. READi's attachment to MILG aligns with the national framework where OCD operates under the Department of National Defense but LGU-level DRRM operates under DILG supervision.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Existing institutions:
   - **Bangsamoro DRRM Council** — chaired by Chief Minister (BAA 13, Sec. 9) — operational
   - **Bangsamoro READi** — attached to MILG (BAA 13, Sec. 26) — operational
   - **Emergency Operations Center** — within READi (BAA 13, Sec. 29) — operational
   - **Quick Response Fund** — special purpose fund (BAA 85, Ch. XXXV) — appropriated annually
   - **Local DRRM Councils (LDRRMCs)** — at provincial, city, and municipal levels per RA 10121 — operational

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The DRRM Council itself is **already created by the BOL** (***"There is hereby created"***). What remains unfulfilled is the **law defining its powers and functions**. Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes a dedicated DRRM office — READi under BAA 13 partially fulfills this, but a standalone DRRM Act could establish a more robust institutional structure (e.g., a Bangsamoro DRRM Authority with regulatory powers).

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **Office of Civil Defense (OCD)** operates its Region XII (SOCCSKSARGEN) and BARMM coordination through the NDRRMC framework. **PAGASA** provides weather monitoring, typhoon warnings, and seasonal forecasts. **PHIVOLCS** monitors seismic activity — critical given the active faults near Cotabato and the Sulu archipelago. **DENR-MGB** provides geological hazard assessments and rain-induced landslide warnings. The **AFP** deploys disaster response teams when requested. **DSWD** provides relief goods, financial assistance, and evacuation center management. The **Climate Change Commission** coordinates national climate change programs and supervises LGU climate change action plan compliance.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for DRRM?**

A. Key programs include: (1) **OCD's Regional Disaster Preparedness** — coordination, training, and capacity-building for LGU DRRM offices; (2) **PAGASA early warning systems** — weather monitoring stations and forecast dissemination; (3) **DSWD Disaster Response Operations Monitoring and Information Center (DROMIC)** — real-time disaster response tracking; (4) **National DRRM Fund** — calamity fund for disaster response and rehabilitation; (5) **People's Survival Fund** (RA 10174) — accessible to BARMM LGUs for climate change adaptation projects.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **partial and structurally complex**. The BOL creates a **Bangsamoro DRRM Council** that operates parallel to the national NDRRMC regional structure. READi under MILG operates as the regional DRRM executing office. However, **early warning systems** (PAGASA, PHIVOLCS), **military disaster response** (AFP), and **national disaster fund** administration remain national government functions. LGU DRRM councils operate under **RA 10121's national framework** while coordinated by the Bangsamoro DRRMC — creating a dual accountability structure (national NDRRMC and regional BDRRMC).

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. The Bangsamoro DRRM Council is operational under the Chief Minister's leadership with the four-Vice-Chair structure. READi has three functional divisions and an Emergency Operations Center. The Chief Minister has exercised the state of calamity proclamation power — notably during the COVID-19 pandemic and STS Paeng (2022). The Quick Response Fund is appropriated annually under BAA 85. READi has coordinated disaster response operations for STS Paeng, which devastated Maguindanao in 2022, and ongoing Marawi IDP displacement. Five pending PBs demonstrate active legislative work toward a more comprehensive framework.

**Q. Where is BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has two unique advantages: (1) **Chief Minister-led DRRM Council** — the BOL places the **highest executive authority** as DRRM Council chair, giving DRRM political priority. Nationally, the NDRRMC is chaired by the Secretary of National Defense — a Cabinet member, not the President. BARMM's structure ensures that DRRM decisions reach the top of the executive chain immediately. (2) **Conflict-sensitive DRRM** — BARMM faces both natural disasters and **conflict-related displacement** (Marawi siege IDPs, armed conflict evacuees). The national DRRM framework under RA 10121 focuses on natural and technological hazards; BARMM's framework must integrate armed conflict displacement into DRRM, which it is uniquely positioned to do.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of the standalone **DRRM Act** mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 7. Without it: the DRRM Council's powers lack statutory definition beyond BAA 13's general mandate; there are no enforceable DRRM standards for LGUs; the Bangsamoro DRRM Plan lacks legislative force; there is no permanent disaster fund mechanism beyond annual QRF appropriations; hazard mapping is not mandatory; evacuation center standards are not codified; and the coordination relationship with national agencies (OCD, PAGASA, PHIVOLCS) is not formalized. Enactment of a comprehensive Bangsamoro DRRM Act would remove this constraint.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for DRRM that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has a **comprehensive DRRM infrastructure** built since 2010: RA 10121 provides a 40+ section code covering every aspect of DRRM with implementing rules; OCD has been operational for decades with regional offices, trained personnel, and established protocols; the NDRRMC provides a tested coordination framework with clear role assignments; LGU DRRM councils have a statutory 5% budget allocation framework; PAGASA and PHIVOLCS provide mature early warning systems with decades of institutional knowledge; and the National DRRM Fund provides a permanent funding mechanism.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework does not account for: (1) **conflict-related displacement** — RA 10121 covers natural and technological hazards but not armed conflict displacement, which is a major DRRM concern in BARMM (Marawi siege IDPs, recurring armed conflict evacuees); (2) **island and archipelagic geography** — Tawi-Tawi, Basilan, and the Sulu Sea islands face unique disaster risks (tsunamis, maritime emergencies) with limited access for rapid response; (3) **cultural and linguistic context** — early warning systems and disaster preparedness materials must be delivered in local languages (Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, Yakan); (4) **limited national agency presence** — PAGASA, PHIVOLCS, and OCD maintain minimal infrastructure in BARMM's most disaster-prone areas.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element of the national framework:

   - **Replicate**: **DRRM Plan formulation process** (national plan structure works well); **5% LDRRMF budget allocation** (proven fiscal mechanism); **four thematic pillars** (prevention/mitigation, preparedness, response, recovery/rehabilitation); **LGU DRRM council structure** (functional at all local government levels)
   - **Adapt**: **Early warning dissemination** — adapt for local languages and island communities; **coordination protocols** — formalize the BDRRMC-NDRRMC relationship that RA 10121 doesn't contemplate; **community-based DRRM** — integrate Islamic community structures (mosques, *barangay* Islamic leaders) as DRRM partners
   - **Replace**: **Hazard and displacement framework** — build a distinct framework that integrates natural disaster and **armed conflict displacement** into a single DRRM system; **regional fund mechanism** — establish a **permanent Bangsamoro DRRM Fund** beyond the annual QRF

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes, though the dimension is **principled rather than technical**. DRRM does not involve Shari'ah commercial law or personal status law. However, the **preservation of life** (*hifz al-nafs*) is the **highest of the five objectives** (*maqasid al-Shari'ah*) of Islamic law. DRRM legislation directly serves this objective. The Islamic legal maxim ***"la darar wa la dirar"*** (no harm and no reciprocal harm) provides a foundational justification for disaster prevention and mitigation measures.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Three principles directly apply:

   (1) ***Hifz al-nafs*** (preservation of life) — the first *maqsad* of the Shari'ah. DRRM legislation that protects lives from foreseeable disasters serves the highest Islamic legal objective.

   (2) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — public officials entrusted with disaster preparedness are *amin* (trustees) whose failure to prepare for foreseeable disasters constitutes a breach of trust.

   (3) ***La darar wa la dirar*** (no harm/no reciprocal harm) — the foundational maxim requiring harm prevention and mitigation.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro DRRM legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is **principled integration** — legislation should be guided by the *maqasid al-Shari'ah* (particularly *hifz al-nafs*) without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance structures. Practical considerations include: disaster relief distribution consistent with Islamic charitable principles (*zakat* and *sadaqah* as supplementary disaster funds); culturally appropriate shelter arrangements (gender-segregated evacuation facilities where needed); Islamic counseling for disaster-affected communities; and coordination with mosques and Islamic institutions as community-level DRRM partners.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national DRRM law and policy?**

A. BARMM's divergence space is **moderate but bounded**. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A): (1) climate change adaptation must be ***"consistent with Republic Act No. 9729"*** — this is a hard constraint; (2) the Bangsamoro DRRM Plan must ***"complement"*** the national plan — it cannot contradict it; (3) national and Bangsamoro agencies must ***"cooperate and coordinate."*** Within these boundaries, BARMM can: establish its own **DRRM regulatory standards** for LGUs (potentially stricter than RA 10121); create a **conflict-sensitive DRRM framework** integrating armed conflict displacement; establish a **permanent Bangsamoro DRRM Fund** beyond the annual QRF; mandate **culturally appropriate** early warning and preparedness measures; and set **unique hazard mapping requirements** for BARMM's disaster-prone areas.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) **For LGUs within BARMM**: They would be subject to **both RA 10121 and the Bangsamoro DRRM Act** — the Bangsamoro Act would govern matters it covers, with RA 10121 applying suppletorily. This creates the potential for **stricter standards** (e.g., mandatory evacuation centers per PB 227). (2) **For national agencies**: OCD, PAGASA, and PHIVOLCS would continue operating within BARMM under their national mandates but would coordinate through the formalized BDRRMC structure. (3) **For disaster response**: Clearer jurisdictional lines between the BDRRMC (regional coordination) and the NDRRMC (national coordination) would improve response efficiency.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between the **Bangsamoro DRRM Council** and the **NDRRMC regional structure**. RA 10121 creates Regional DRRMCs through OCD regional offices; the BOL creates a separate Bangsamoro DRRMC. Currently, both operate within BARMM without a clear delineation of functions. A standalone DRRM Act must clarify: (a) whether the BDRRMC **replaces** the RA 10121 Regional DRRMC within BARMM; (b) how the BDRRMC interfaces with the NDRRMC for national-level coordination; (c) how early warning information flows from PAGASA/PHIVOLCS through the BDRRMC to LGUs; and (d) how the Quick Response Fund and National DRRM Fund coordinate for disasters affecting BARMM.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Five pending PBs address specific components: **PB 242** (Climate Change Commission), **PB 182** (hazard maps), **PB 227** (evacuation centers), **PB 297** (mandatory risk assessments), and **PB 384** (public safety framework). However, **no comprehensive Bangsamoro DRRM Act** has been filed that would fulfill the Art. XIII, Sec. 7 mandate. The pending PBs address pieces of the DRRM system but not the institutional, regulatory, and fiscal framework that a standalone DRRM Act would provide.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) identifies DRRM as essential to protecting development gains — without disaster resilience, infrastructure investments, agricultural productivity, and social development achievements are vulnerable to destruction. Chapter 4 (Development Framework) positions disaster resilience as a **cross-cutting element** of all eight development strategies. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 5 (Governance and Institutional Strengthening)**, recognizes disaster resilience as a component of effective governance — DRRM capacity building for LGUs is a governance prerequisite.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a standalone DRRM Act: the DRRM Council cannot set **enforceable disaster preparedness standards** for LGUs — meaning disaster preparedness remains voluntary rather than mandatory; the Bangsamoro DRRM Plan lacks legislative force — making it aspirational rather than binding; there is no **permanent disaster fund** mechanism — annual QRF appropriations are subject to budget negotiations and may be insufficient for major disasters; **hazard mapping** cannot be mandated — leaving vulnerable communities without risk information; **evacuation center standards** cannot be enforced — as demonstrated by inadequate facilities during STS Paeng; the **conflict-displacement dimension** of DRRM remains unaddressed — leaving Marawi IDPs and armed conflict evacuees without a systematic rehabilitation framework; and **BDP infrastructure investments** are unprotected — without enforceable disaster-resilient construction standards, every new road, bridge, and building is at risk.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What makes the Art. XIII, Sec. 7 legislative mandate distinctive among the BOL's DRRM provisions?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 7 is unique because the BOL **creates the institution directly** (***"There is hereby created"***) while mandating Parliament to **define its powers and functions**. Most BOL provisions either create an institution with defined powers or mandate Parliament to create one. Here, the institution exists by constitutional-organic law force, but its operational framework requires legislation. This creates an unusual situation: the DRRM Council has **constitutional existence** but **undefined statutory authority** — a gap that BAA 13's general provisions only partially fill.

**Q. How does the Quick Response Fund interact with the DRRM framework?**

A. The Quick Response Fund (BAA 85, Ch. XXXV) is a **special purpose fund** appropriated annually for immediate disaster response. It provides the **only dedicated fiscal mechanism** for DRRM beyond regular ministry budgets. However, the QRF has three limitations: (a) it is **annual** — appropriated only through the yearly GAA, not a permanent fund; (b) it is designed for **immediate response**, not prevention, preparedness, or long-term rehabilitation; (c) its **management framework** is governed by BAA 85 appropriations law rather than a comprehensive DRRM fund management code. A standalone DRRM Act should establish a **permanent Bangsamoro DRRM Fund** with allocations for all four DRRM thematic pillars — not just response.

**Q. How should BARMM's DRRM framework address conflict-related displacement?**

A. BARMM faces a **dual displacement challenge** — natural disasters and armed conflict. The Marawi siege (2017) displaced over 350,000 people, many of whom remain in temporary shelters eight years later (Res. 476). Recurring armed conflict in Maguindanao, Basilan, and Sulu produces periodic evacuee populations. RA 10121's national framework was designed for natural and technological hazards — it does not systematically address conflict-related displacement. BAA 62 (Rights of Internally Displaced Persons) provides a legal framework for IDP rights but is not integrated into the DRRM system. A comprehensive Bangsamoro DRRM Act should **integrate conflict displacement** into the DRRM cycle — covering evacuation, shelter management, livelihood restoration, and return/resettlement for both natural disaster IDPs and armed conflict evacuees.

**Q. What is the relationship between this power and power (vv) Social services?**

A. Power (s) — DRRM — and power (vv) — social services — overlap in the **disaster response and rehabilitation** phase. Art. IX, Sec. 8 (under Basic Rights/Social Justice) mandates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"maintain appropriate disaster-preparedness units"*** and ***"ensure the rehabilitation of calamity-affected areas."*** MSSD is Vice-Chair for Disaster Response on the DRRM Council and has representatives in READi. In practice, MSSD delivers the social protection component of disaster response (relief goods, financial assistance, psychosocial support, evacuation center management) while READi coordinates the operational DRRM system. A standalone DRRM Act should formalize this division — DRRM Council for overall coordination, READi for operations, MSSD for humanitarian welfare.
