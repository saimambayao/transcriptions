# (o) Cultural Exchange, and Economic and Technical Cooperation

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(o)** — ***"Cultural exchange, and economic and technical cooperation;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(o) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XII, Sec. 28: ***"upon review and approval of the National Government"*** — all international linkages require national government clearance |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over cultural exchange and economic/technical cooperation?**

A. Article V, Section 2(o) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"cultural exchange, and economic and technical cooperation"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This power covers three dimensions of international engagement: **cultural exchange** (cross-cultural programs and activities), **economic cooperation** (trade, investment, and economic development partnerships), and **technical cooperation** (capacity building, skills transfer, and institutional development). The power is qualified by Art. XII, Sec. 28, which requires ***"review and approval of the National Government"*** for all international linkages.[^1][^2]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Six provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XII, Sec. 28 — the **core implementing provision** (quoted in full in the BOL Provisions table). It establishes three elements: scope (cultural exchange, economic/technical cooperation), condition (countries with diplomatic relations + national government approval), and personnel (embassy/consulate assignments and international delegations).[^2]

   (2) Art. XII, Sec. 29 — **benefits from international conventions**: the Bangsamoro Government is entitled to ***"equitable"*** benefits from conventions to which the National Government is a party.[^3]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 24 (last para.) — **trade missions and fairs**: the Bangsamoro Government may participate in and organize trade missions and promotional activities with ***"necessary coordination with the relevant government agencies."***[^4]

   (4) Art. XIII, Sec. 26 — **barter trade and countertrade**: regulates traditional barter trade with ***"BIMP-EAGA Member States"*** and countertrade with ***"ASEAN Member States."***[^5]

   (5) Art. XII, Sec. 26 — **grants and donations**: acceptance from **foreign governments and multilateral institutions** requires ***"prior clearance and approval by the President or his authorized representative."*** Domestic donations do not require Presidential clearance.[^6]

   (6) Art. XIII, Sec. 6 — **national development plan participation**: ensures Bangsamoro representation in national planning steering committees.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not name a specific legislation for international cooperation. The power is exercised through the **executive function** of the Chief Minister and the OCM, with national government approval. However, the Parliament could enact legislation to establish institutional mechanisms, protocols, and priorities for international engagement.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not create a dedicated international cooperation body. Art. XII, Sec. 28 vests the function in the **Bangsamoro Government** broadly. Under BAA 13, the function is exercised through: (a) the **Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila** (liaison to international offices/embassies — BAA 13, Book IV, Sec. (8)); and (b) the **Bangsamoro Barter Trade Council** (BBTC — for BIMP-EAGA/ASEAN economic cooperation — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 8).

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three significant limitations:

   (1) **National government review and approval**: Art. XII, Sec. 28 — ***"upon review and approval of the National Government."*** This is the single most important qualifier — the Bangsamoro Government cannot independently establish international linkages.[^2]

   (2) **Diplomatic relations requirement**: Art. XII, Sec. 28 — only with ***"countries having diplomatic relations with the Philippines."*** The Bangsamoro Government cannot engage with states not recognized by the Philippines.[^2]

   (3) **Presidential clearance for grants**: Art. XII, Sec. 26 — foreign grants require ***"prior clearance and approval by the President."***[^6]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Foreign affairs as national power**: The 1987 Constitution vests the **treaty-making power** exclusively in the President (Art. VII, Sec. 21) with Senate concurrence. The conduct of foreign affairs is an inherently national function. Art. II, Sec. 2 declares the Philippines ***"adheres to the policy of peace, equality, justice, freedom, cooperation, and amity with all nations."***[^8][^9]

   (2) **Autonomous region framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."*** International cooperation by a sub-national government must not infringe on national sovereignty or foreign affairs prerogatives.[^10]

   (3) **Autonomous region legislative powers (Art. X, Sec. 20(6))**: The organic act of autonomous regions may provide for ***"economic, social, and tourism development."*** This provides constitutional basis for economic cooperation activities within the autonomous region framework.[^11]

   (4) **Due process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: Government action affecting international cooperation agreements must satisfy procedural requirements.[^12]

**Q. What is the constitutional boundary on this power?**

A. The Bangsamoro Government **cannot**: (a) enter into **treaties or binding international agreements** — this is a Presidential/Senate prerogative; (b) establish diplomatic relations or recognize foreign states; (c) conduct **foreign policy** independent of the National Government; (d) engage with countries that do not have diplomatic relations with the Philippines. The Bangsamoro Government **can**: (a) establish **cooperation linkages** (MOUs, technical agreements, cultural exchanges) with national government approval; (b) participate in **trade missions and fairs**; (c) recommend **personnel for embassy assignments**; (d) receive **equitable benefits** from international conventions.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**

A. The national framework is structured around: **RA 7157** (Philippine Foreign Service Act) governing embassies and consulates; **EO 459, s. 1997** establishing the approval process for international agreements; **RA 8182** (ODA Act) governing development assistance; and the **Department of Foreign Affairs** (DFA) as the lead national agency for foreign affairs coordination. No national law specifically addresses sub-national government international cooperation — the BOL is the primary framework.[^13][^14][^15]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. (1) **RA 7157** — directly applicable for embassy/consulate assignments recommended under Art. XII, Sec. 28.[^13]
   (2) **EO 459** — directly applicable for the approval process of international cooperation agreements.[^14]
   (3) **RA 8182** — directly applicable for ODA received through international cooperation.[^15]
   (4) **Constitution, Art. VII, Sec. 21** — directly applicable as the constitutional limit on treaty-making.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. The **Department of Foreign Affairs** (DFA) exercises primary authority over international engagement. The **National Economic and Development Authority** (NEDA) coordinates ODA programming. The **Department of Trade and Industry** (DTI) coordinates trade missions and fairs. None of these functions have been devolved to the Bangsamoro Government — the BOL establishes a **coordination-based model** rather than devolution.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Office of the Chief Minister (OCM)** is the primary institution. BAA 13 assigns two specific functions: (a) the **Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila** (Book IV, Sec. (8)) serves as ***"liaison office of the Chief Minister to international offices/embassies"*** and ***"coordinating office of foreign scholarship programs"***; (b) the Chief Minister heads the **Bangsamoro Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council** which coordinates international humanitarian assistance. BAA 85, Chapter II allocates the OCM's operational budget.[^16][^17]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for international cooperation. The power is exercised through OCM's executive authority and BAA 13 institutional provisions.

**Q. What are the OCM's mandate and functions under BAA 13 relevant to international cooperation?**

A. BAA 13 assigns two relevant units to the OCM:

   **Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila** (Book IV, Sec. (8)):
   - (a) ***"Serve as liaison office of the Chief Minister to international offices/embassies, national offices/departments, agencies, and bureaus;"***
   - (b) ***"Provide administrative and technical support to the Chief Minister and BARMM officials/employees while on official business in Metro Manila;"***
   - (c) ***"Coordinate with National Government Agencies in Metro Manila on matters related to the BARMM;"***
   - (d) ***"Act as coordinating office of foreign scholarship programs;"***
   - (e) ***"Perform other functions as directed by the Chief Minister or by his/her authorized representative."***[^16]

   **Bangsamoro Barter Trade Council** (BBTC) (Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 8, Sec. 37-39):
   - Mandate: ***"to accredit, regulate, supervise, coordinate and harmonize all policies, operations, programs and activities of barter trade in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** (Sec. 37)
   - Key functions: (a) ***"Create an environment conducive to barter and counter trade development and growth in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, BIMP-EAGA and ASEAN"***; (b) issue rules and regulations on barter trader accreditation; (c) issue guidelines governing barter trade; (d) exercise regulatory powers; (e) ***"Coordinate with other appropriate government agencies/institutions...such as Coast Guard, Bureau of Customs, Bureau of Immigrations and Deportation, Department of Foreign Affairs"*** (Sec. 38)[^18]

**Q. What can the OCM NOT do without a standalone BAA?**

A. Without legislation, the OCM cannot: (a) establish a **permanent international cooperation office** with defined authority, staffing, and protocols; (b) create a **standardized approval process** for international cooperation proposals within the Bangsamoro Government before submission to the National Government; (c) define **eligible cooperation activities** and **partner country priorities**; (d) establish a **Bangsamoro International Cooperation Fund** for co-financing partnerships; (e) codify the **embassy/consulate recommendation process** for qualified Bangsamoro persons.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No enacted resolution directly calls for international cooperation legislation. The Parliament has expressed positions on international issues through solidarity resolutions (Res. 124, 233, 407, 419) and cultural recognition resolutions (Res. 396), indicating engagement with international affairs through the resolution mechanism rather than legislation.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. The OCM is the appropriate lead because international cooperation is an **executive function** exercised by the head of government. The Chief Minister is the interlocutor with the National Government for approval of international linkages (Art. XII, Sec. 28) and has the political authority to engage with foreign counterparts. The Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila provides the operational link to embassies and national government agencies.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. - **Lead office**: OCM — Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila (BAA 13, Book IV, Sec. (8))
   - **Specialized body**: Bangsamoro Barter Trade Council (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 8) — BIMP-EAGA/ASEAN economic cooperation
   - **Supporting ministry**: MTIT — trade promotion and international trade fairs (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 1)
   - **Supporting agency**: BPDA — coordinates development plans that inform international cooperation priorities
   - **Youth dimension**: Bangsamoro Youth Commission — BAA 13 assigns ***"establish and maintain linkages with national and international youth organizations"*** (Book XVI, Ch. 1)

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **Department of Foreign Affairs** (DFA) exercises primary authority over all international engagements involving BARMM. DFA reviews and approves Bangsamoro cooperation proposals per Art. XII, Sec. 28. **NEDA** coordinates ODA programming for BARMM. **DTI** facilitates trade mission participation. In practice, the Chief Minister's Office engages directly with foreign embassies and international organizations in Metro Manila through the Liaison Office, with DFA providing the formal approval channel.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. This power is **not devolvable** in the traditional sense. International relations remain a **national prerogative** under the Constitution. The BOL creates a **coordination model** — the Bangsamoro Government initiates and recommends, the National Government reviews and approves. The function that can mature is the Bangsamoro Government's **capacity to propose, negotiate (with national supervision), and implement** international cooperation activities.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. The Bangsamoro Government has: (a) established the **Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila** as operational link to embassies and national agencies; (b) participated in **BIMP-EAGA** activities through the BBTC; (c) engaged with **OIC member states** and international organizations (UNDP, World Bank, JICA, USAID, etc.) for development cooperation; (d) participated in international conferences and delegations; (e) received foreign grants and technical assistance through the National Government channel. The international engagement has been **primarily executive-driven** rather than legislatively structured.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has a unique advantage in **Muslim-majority country engagement** — the Bangsamoro identity creates natural affinities with OIC member states (particularly Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei, Turkey, and Gulf states) that the National Government's secular diplomatic apparatus does not fully leverage. Bangsamoro cultural exchange with Muslim-majority countries strengthens both development cooperation and cultural identity preservation.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **primary constraint** is the **absence of institutional infrastructure** — no dedicated international cooperation office with clear authority, staffing, and protocols. The Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila has a broad mandate (national agencies, embassies, scholarship coordination) without the specialized capacity for systematic international cooperation management. The **secondary constraint** is the national government approval process, which can be slow without a standardized protocol.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has: (a) a **professional Foreign Service** with 117 embassies and consulates worldwide; (b) **EO 459** establishing a standardized international agreement approval process; (c) **NEDA's ODA portfolio** managing billions of pesos in development assistance; (d) **DTI's trade promotion network** including trade attaches in key markets. BARMM has none of these at the regional level.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national system: (a) does not **prioritize Bangsamoro-specific** international cooperation — BARMM's unique relationship with Muslim-majority countries is not systematically leveraged; (b) the approval process under Art. XII, Sec. 28 has no **standardized protocol** for BARMM proposals; (c) no **Bangsamoro foreign service personnel** have been formally recommended for embassy/consulate assignments under Art. XII, Sec. 28.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. The Shari'ah dimension is **principled rather than regulatory**. International cooperation is governed by diplomatic and administrative law, not Shari'ah. However, three Islamic principles inform the Bangsamoro Government's approach:

   (1) ***Ta'aruf*** (mutual acquaintance) — the Qur'anic principle in Surah 49:13 supports cultural exchange as a means of mutual understanding across peoples and cultures [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

   (2) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — international cooperation that brings development to the Bangsamoro people serves the public interest [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

   (3) ***Ummah*** — the global Muslim community provides a natural network for cultural exchange and technical cooperation with OIC member states, though the BOL channels this engagement through Philippine diplomatic relations [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL is **silent** on Shari'ah in relation to international cooperation. No provision of Art. XII, Sec. 28-29 or Art. XIII, Sec. 24-26 references Islamic law, Shari'ah institutions, or OIC engagement.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM's ability to diverge is **minimal** because international cooperation is constitutionally a **national function**. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XII, Sec. 28 requires national government approval. As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), the treaty-making power is exclusively Presidential/Senatorial. The Bangsamoro Government can diverge only in: (a) establishing **internal institutional mechanisms** for managing international cooperation; (b) setting **regional priorities** for which countries and cooperation areas to pursue; (c) developing **selection criteria** for embassy/consulate personnel recommendations. In all cases, the external-facing dimension requires national government approval.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. A Bangsamoro International Cooperation Act would govern the **internal process** — how the Bangsamoro Government identifies, evaluates, and proposes international cooperation activities. National law (EO 459, RA 7157, RA 8182) would continue to govern the **approval and implementation** processes involving the National Government.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. The principal ambiguity is in the **approval process**: Art. XII, Sec. 28 says ***"upon review and approval of the National Government"*** but does not specify which national government body approves, what the review criteria are, or the timeline for approval. Is it DFA? The Office of the President? NEDA for economic cooperation? In practice, different types of cooperation may require different approval channels — but no protocol exists.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **No pending Parliament Bill** addresses international cooperation. This is among the **least legislated** of the 55 enumerated powers.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 7** (Economic Development) targets international trade linkages and investment attraction from OIC/ASEAN countries. **BDP Chapter 13** (Plan Financing) identifies international cooperation as a channel for development financing and technical assistance. **BDP Chapter 6** (Cultural Diversity) envisions cultural exchange programs. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda**, **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, depends on international economic partnerships.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without an institutional framework: (a) **ODA and technical assistance** opportunities may be missed due to lack of systematic identification and proposal development; (b) the **embassy/consulate recommendation** provision (Art. XII, Sec. 28) remains unexercised — no Bangsamoro voice in Philippine foreign service posts; (c) **BIMP-EAGA engagement** remains limited to the BBTC's barter trade mandate without broader economic cooperation; (d) **cultural exchange** programs with Muslim-majority countries lack institutional support; (e) international cooperation depends on **ad hoc executive initiative** rather than systematic strategy.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the scope of "linkages" under Art. XII, Sec. 28 — what types of agreements can the Bangsamoro Government enter?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 28 uses the term ***"linkages"*** rather than "agreements" or "treaties." This suggests a **broad but soft** scope: memoranda of understanding, cultural exchange programs, technical cooperation arrangements, trade promotion activities, educational partnerships, and institutional cooperation agreements. These linkages are **not treaties** under Art. VII, Sec. 21 of the Constitution — they do not require Senate concurrence. They are sub-national cooperation arrangements subject to national executive approval.

**Q. How does power (o) relate to power (xx) Technical cooperation for human resource development?**

A. Power (xx) — ***"technical cooperation for human resource development"*** — is the human capital dimension of the broader power (o). Art. XII, Sec. 28 applies to both: the same ***"review and approval of the National Government"*** applies. The distinction is that power (o) covers all three dimensions (cultural, economic, technical) while power (xx) focuses specifically on human resource development through technical cooperation. The **Development Academy of the Bangsamoro (DAB)** (BAA 13, Book XVI, Ch. 7) is the lead agency for power (xx), working in coordination with OCM for power (o).

**Q. How does the BIMP-EAGA dimension work under this power?**

A. BIMP-EAGA engagement operates under two BOL provisions: (a) Art. XIII, Sec. 26 — barter trade regulation with ***"BIMP-EAGA Member States"***; and (b) Art. XII, Sec. 28 — broader economic cooperation. The **BBTC** handles barter trade regulation, while OCM handles the broader BIMP-EAGA economic cooperation framework. BIMP-EAGA is significant because it represents the **strongest international economic cooperation mandate** in the BOL — the Bangsamoro Government is authorized to regulate barter trade with BIMP-EAGA members with no express "review and approval" qualifier in Art. XIII, Sec. 26 (though the general Art. XII, Sec. 28 requirement applies to broader cooperation).

**Q. What is the practical significance of the embassy/consulate provision?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 28's provision that the Bangsamoro Government ***"may recommend to the National Government the assignment of qualified persons in Philippine embassies and consulates, and their participation in international delegations"*** is **unprecedented** in Philippine autonomous region law. It recognizes the Bangsamoro's unique international dimension — particularly relations with OIC member states where Bangsamoro cultural knowledge and language skills (Arabic, Malay) are valuable. The provision remains **largely unexercised** as of 2026. A systematic recommendation process could place Bangsamoro professionals in key embassies (Kuala Lumpur, Jakarta, Riyadh, Ankara, Islamabad) where they could serve as cultural and economic bridges.
