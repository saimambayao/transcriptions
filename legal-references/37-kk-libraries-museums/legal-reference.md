# (kk) Libraries and Museums, and Historical, Cultural, and Archaeological Sites

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(kk)** — "Libraries and museums, and historical, cultural, and archaeological sites;"

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over libraries, museums, and historical, cultural, and archaeological sites as an enumerated power under Article V, Section 2(kk).[^1] This power is closely intertwined with the culture and language power under Section 2(p), sharing the same institutional architecture. Article IX, Sections 24 through 26 elaborate on the scope. Section 25 specifically directs the BCPCH to "establish and sustain the cultural institutions, programs, and projects in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region" and to "establish libraries and museums, declare and restore historical shrines and cultural sites to preserve the Bangsamoro heritage for posterity."[^2] The Commission must also ensure that Philippine history, culture, and heritage are included in the establishment of museums and similar institutions, fostering national unity.

Section 26 provides the mechanism for consolidating this power: the National Government "shall transfer the management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites currently under the jurisdiction of the National Museum of the Philippines, National Historical Commission of the Philippines, or other agencies of the National Government to the Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage through the intergovernmental relations mechanisms."[^3] The BCPCH is further directed to coordinate with relevant national agencies on the regulation, excavation, and preservation of cultural artifacts and the recovery of lost historical and cultural heritage.

This power thus encompasses four distinct domains: (1) library systems for public access to knowledge, (2) museums as custodians of material culture, (3) historical and cultural sites including shrines and memorials, and (4) archaeological sites requiring regulation of excavation and artifact preservation. The BOL does not require national law consistency for this power, though coordination with national agencies is expected.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The BCPCH is the lead institution, established under BAA 13 and funded through BAA 85, Chapter XXII.[^4] The Parliament has enacted two BAAs that directly implement the libraries-museums-heritage power. BAA 60 declares the burial site of Hashim and Mimbantas as a memorial site, establishing the first formally legislated heritage site in BARMM.[^5] BAA 61 establishes the Camp Abubakar Memorial Marker and Eco-Park as a memorial for Bangsamoro shuhada (martyrs), combining heritage preservation with ecological stewardship.[^6]

Both BAAs focus on revolutionary heritage — memorializing leaders and martyrs of the Bangsamoro struggle. No BAA addresses library systems, museum establishment, archaeological site protection, or the broader universe of historical and cultural sites across the five provinces and two cities of BARMM. The BCPCH exercises these functions through its general BAA 13 mandate, which provides the organizational authority but not the detailed standards, funding mechanisms, or regulatory framework that standalone legislation would provide.

Several pending bills target this gap. Bill 177 proposes creating a Mujahideen Memorial Wall. Bill 299 envisions a museum exhibiting Bangsamoro history toward autonomy. Bill 327 seeks to declare the burial site of Hajji Salik Abu Ghazali Jaafar as a memorial site. Bill 335 would declare the houses of Sheikh Salamat Hashim as historical places. Bill 358 proposes establishing the Shariff Kabunsuan Shrine. These bills indicate strong parliamentary interest in expanding heritage protection, though none has been enacted.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not require this power to be exercised in accordance with national law. Instead, the relationship is structured around transfer and coordination. Article IX, Section 26 mandates the transfer of Bangsamoro heritage site management from national agencies to the BCPCH, creating a clear trajectory toward Bangsamoro self-governance over its own cultural patrimony.[^7] The BCPCH must coordinate with the National Museum, NHCP, and NCCA — but this coordination is for preservation expertise and artifact recovery, not for regulatory approval.

In the absence of a comprehensive Bangsamoro cultural heritage code, Republic Act No. 10066 (National Cultural Heritage Act of 2009) applies suppletorily.[^8] RA 10066 establishes the national framework for declaring, protecting, and managing cultural heritage sites and artifacts, including provisions for archaeological excavation permits, penalties for heritage destruction, and the registry of cultural properties. Presidential Decree No. 374 (amending RA 4846 on cultural properties) and RA 8492 (National Museum Act of 1998) also apply where no BAA fills the regulatory space. Once the Parliament enacts its own cultural heritage code, these national laws would be supplanted on matters within Bangsamoro authority.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

This power has the widest gap between its BOL mandate and its legislative implementation among the culture-related powers. Despite the BOL's explicit directive to establish libraries, museums, and heritage sites, only two memorial sites have been legislated. No Bangsamoro library system act exists. No museum establishment act has been enacted. No archaeological site protection or excavation regulation framework exists. No registry of Bangsamoro cultural properties has been legislated.

A comprehensive Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code — which could be combined with the culture and language power (p) — would address the largest gap. Such a code would establish: a classification system for cultural properties (national cultural treasures, important cultural properties, cultural heritage zones), a Bangsamoro Registry of Cultural Properties, archaeological excavation permit requirements and protocols, penalties for unauthorized destruction or removal of cultural artifacts, a public library system framework, and museum governance standards.

The BOL-mandated transfer of heritage sites from national agencies (Art. IX, Sec. 26) also lacks implementing legislation. An implementing BAA would identify the sites to be transferred, establish the transfer timeline and procedures, define post-transfer management standards, and allocate the budget needed for the BCPCH to manage the expanded portfolio. Without this, the transfer remains aspirational rather than operational.

A standalone Bangsamoro Museums Act would provide the legal basis for a regional museum system, governing museum establishment, collection management, exhibition standards, and community engagement. A Bangsamoro Public Libraries Act would similarly codify the public library system that the BCPCH is mandated to establish but currently manages only through administrative action.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028 addresses this power in Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage), which sets goals for protecting cultural heritage sites, establishing cultural institutions, and promoting Bangsamoro history and identity.[^9] Without dedicated legislation for libraries, museums, and heritage site management, the BDP's institutional development targets in this area rely entirely on the BCPCH's administrative capacity rather than a legislated mandate with dedicated funding and regulatory authority.

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type A**: No comprehensive cultural heritage code | BCPCH exercises its mandate through BAA 13 only; no systematic framework for classification, conservation, or management of heritage properties | Enact Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code covering tangible and intangible heritage, cultural property registry, archaeological regulation, and penalties |
| **Type A**: No library system legislation | No BAA establishes a public library system despite BCPCH's mandate under Art. IX, Sec. 25 to "establish libraries" | Enact Bangsamoro Public Libraries Act |
| **Type A**: No museum establishment legislation | Only two memorial sites legislated; no museum governance framework or museum establishment act | Enact Bangsamoro Museums Act (Bill 299 pending) |
| **Type B**: Incomplete heritage site transfer | Art. IX, Sec. 26 mandates transfer of Bangsamoro heritage sites from national agencies to BCPCH — no implementing legislation codifies the transfer process | Enact Heritage Site Transfer Implementation Act identifying sites, timelines, and post-transfer management standards |
