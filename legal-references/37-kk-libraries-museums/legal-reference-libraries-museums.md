# (kk) Libraries and Museums, and Historical, Cultural, and Archaeological Sites

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(kk)** — ***"Libraries and museums, and historical, cultural, and archaeological sites;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(kk) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement. Art. IX, Sec. 24 directs creation of the BCPCH ***"in coordination with the appropriate and relevant national government agencies,"*** and Art. IX, Sec. 26 mandates transfer of management ***"through the intergovernmental relations mechanisms."*** These are coordination requirements, not consistency-with-national-law requirements. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH)** — attached to the Office of the Chief Minister (BAA 13, Book V, Title XVI, Ch. 4, Sec. 46-60) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Library and Archives (BAA 13, Book III, Title II — under OCM); Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education (MBHTE); Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT — tourism/heritage sites) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 6: Cultural Diversity and Heritage |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None expressly named. The BOL mandates the creation of the BCPCH (Art. IX, Sec. 24) and the transfer of site management (Art. IX, Sec. 26) but does not name a specific code or act for libraries, museums, or heritage sites. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over libraries, museums, and heritage sites?**

A. Article V, Section 2(kk) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Libraries and museums, and historical, cultural, and archaeological sites"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a broad grant covering three distinct domains: (1) **library services** — establishment, maintenance, and regulation of public and private libraries; (2) **museum establishment and operations** — creation, management, and curation of museums; and (3) **heritage site management** — identification, preservation, restoration, and management of historical, cultural, and archaeological sites within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Three provisions in Art. IX elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. IX, Sec. 24 establishes the **preservation mandate** and the **institutional creation directive**: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall preserve the history, culture, arts, traditions, and the rich cultural heritage of the Bangsamoro people and their Sultanates, such as those of Sulu, Maguindanao, Kabuntalan, Buayan, the Royal Houses of Ranao and of the Iranun, and the non-Moro indigenous peoples of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. For this purpose, it shall create the Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage in coordination with the appropriate and relevant national government agencies."*** This provision is notable for its specificity — it names the **Sultanates and Royal Houses** whose heritage must be preserved, and it mandates coordination (not consistency) with national agencies.[^2]

   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 25 defines the BCPCH's **primary responsibilities**: ***"The Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage shall have the primary responsibility to write the history of the Bangsamoro people and to establish and sustain the cultural institutions, programs, and projects in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** It further specifies that the BCPCH ***"shall establish libraries and museums, declare and restore historical shrines and cultural sites to preserve the Bangsamoro heritage for posterity."*** Critically, this provision also imposes a **national unity obligation**: the BCPCH ***"shall ensure the inclusion of Philippine history, culture, and heritage in the establishment of museums and similar institutions, programs and projects to foster unity among Filipinos."***[^3]

   (3) Art. IX, Sec. 26 mandates the **transfer of management**: ***"The National Government shall transfer the management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites currently under the jurisdiction of the National Museum of the Philippines, National Historical Commission of the Philippines, or other agencies of the National Government to the Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage through the intergovernmental relations mechanisms."***[^4]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. Unlike other powers (e.g., Art. XIII, Sec. 24 naming a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"***), the BOL does not name a specific piece of legislation for libraries, museums, or cultural heritage. The BOL mandates the **creation of the BCPCH** (Art. IX, Sec. 24) — an institutional mandate fulfilled by BAA 13 — but does not expressly require Parliament to enact a dedicated cultural heritage code, library act, or museum act. However, PB No. 204 (comprehensive cultural heritage bill) represents legislative intent to create such a framework.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. IX, Sec. 24 expressly mandates the creation of the ***"Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage."*** This is one of the few instances where the BOL names a specific institution by title. The BCPCH was created through BAA 13, Book V, Title XVI, Chapter 4, Sec. 46-60, and receives its annual budget under BAA 85, Chapter XXII.[^5][^6]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places three qualifications:

   (1) **Coordination with national agencies**: Art. IX, Sec. 24 requires the BCPCH to be created ***"in coordination with the appropriate and relevant national government agencies."*** Art. IX, Sec. 25 requires the BCPCH to ***"coordinate and work closely with the National Commission for Culture and the Arts, National Historical Commission of the Philippines, National Museum of the Philippines, and other concerned cultural agencies."*** This is a coordination requirement — not a consistency requirement — meaning the BCPCH must work with these agencies but is not bound to follow their standards.

   (2) **National unity obligation**: Art. IX, Sec. 25 requires the BCPCH to ***"ensure the inclusion of Philippine history, culture, and heritage in the establishment of museums and similar institutions, programs and projects to foster unity among Filipinos."*** This prevents an exclusively Bangsamoro narrative — museums must include national context.

   (3) **Intergovernmental transfer mechanism**: Art. IX, Sec. 26 conditions the management transfer on ***"intergovernmental relations mechanisms"*** — meaning transfer cannot be unilateral; it requires negotiation through the IGR framework established in Art. VI.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to libraries, museums, and heritage sites?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: Autonomous regions consist of areas sharing ***"common and distinctive historical and cultural heritage"*** operating ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** Art. X, Sec. 20(8) specifically enumerates ***"Preservation and development of the cultural heritage"*** as a subject for autonomous region legislative powers — this is the **direct constitutional basis** for Art. V, Sec. 2(kk).[^7]

   (2) **Arts and Culture (Art. XIV, Sec. 14-18)**: Sec. 14 mandates the State to ***"foster the preservation, enrichment, and dynamic evolution of a Filipino national culture based on the principle of unity in diversity in a climate of free artistic and intellectual expression."*** Sec. 15 states: ***"Arts and letters shall enjoy the patronage of the State. The State shall conserve, promote, and popularize the nation's historical and cultural heritage and resources, as well as artistic creations."*** Sec. 16 declares: ***"All the country's artistic and historic wealth constitutes the cultural treasure of the nation and shall be under the protection of the State which may regulate its disposition."*** These provisions establish an **affirmative state obligation** to protect cultural heritage and a **regulatory power** over its disposition.[^8]

   (3) **Indigenous Cultural Communities (Art. XIV, Sec. 17)**: ***"The State shall recognize, respect, and protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities to preserve and develop their cultures, traditions, and institutions."*** This protects both the Bangsamoro peoples and the non-Moro indigenous peoples whose heritage the BOL (Art. IX, Sec. 24) expressly includes.

   (4) **State Policy (Art. II, Sec. 17)**: ***"The State shall give priority to education, science and technology, arts, culture, and sports to foster patriotism and nationalism, accelerate social progress, and promote total human liberation and development."*** Culture receives constitutional priority alongside education and science.

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees: (1) **State patronage** of arts and letters (Art. XIV, Sec. 15) — both the national and Bangsamoro governments have an affirmative obligation to support cultural institutions; (2) **Equal access to cultural opportunities** (Art. XIV, Sec. 18(1)): ***"The State shall ensure equal access to cultural opportunities through the educational system, public or private cultural entities, scholarships, grants and other incentives, and community cultural centers, and other public venues"*** — this mandates that library and museum services be accessible, not restricted by geography, language, or economic status; (3) **Protection of indigenous cultural rights** (Art. XIV, Sec. 17) — the non-Moro IP communities within BARMM have constitutionally-protected cultural rights that BCPCH must respect; (4) **State protection of cultural treasure** (Art. XIV, Sec. 16) — the State may regulate the disposition of cultural property, setting a floor below which BARMM legislation cannot fall.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. XIV, Sec. 14 mandates the State to ***"foster the preservation, enrichment, and dynamic evolution of a Filipino national culture."*** This is a directive principle, not a specific legislative mandate like Art. XII, Sec. 15 (cooperatives). The NCCA was created by RA 7356 to fulfill this mandate at the national level. At the Bangsamoro level, the BCPCH fulfills this function through the BOL's Art. IX, Sec. 24. Art. X, Sec. 20(8) constitutionally authorizes the autonomous region to legislate on ***"Preservation and development of the cultural heritage"*** — this gives Bangsamoro legislation in this area direct constitutional authorization.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for libraries, museums, and heritage sites?**

A. The national framework establishes a multi-agency system: **RA 10066** (National Cultural Heritage Act of 2009, as amended by **RA 11961**) provides the comprehensive framework — defining cultural property, establishing a presumption of important cultural property for items over 50 years old, creating a registry of cultural property, designating heritage zones, and imposing penalties for destruction or unauthorized alteration. **RA 11333** (National Museum Act) governs the national museum system. **RA 10087** (National Library Act) strengthens library services. **RA 10086** (NHCP Act) governs historical research, markers, and monuments. **RA 7356** (NCCA Act) creates the overall coordination body. **RA 9470** (National Archives Act) governs archival records. Together, these laws provide: a **classification system** for cultural property, **registration and registry** mechanisms, **protection standards** (penalties for destruction), an **institutional infrastructure** (NCCA, National Museum, NHCP, National Library, National Archives), and **funding mechanisms** (National Endowment Fund). This is the baseline against which BARMM legislation will be compared.[^9][^10][^11][^12][^13][^14]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national cultural heritage laws apply in the absence of a standalone Bangsamoro cultural heritage code:

   (1) **RA 10066** (as amended by **RA 11961**) — comprehensive cultural heritage protection. Applies suppletorily.[^9]
   (2) **RA 11333** — National Museum retains jurisdiction over nationally-declared properties until management transfer per BOL Art. IX, Sec. 26. Applies directly for nationally-significant items.[^10]
   (3) **RA 10087** — National Library provides book allocations and technical support to Bangsamoro Library and Archives. Suppletory.[^11]
   (4) **RA 10086** — NHCP retains authority over national markers and nationally-significant historical sites until transfer. Applies directly for national-level designations.[^12]
   (5) **RA 7356** — NCCA remains the national policy coordination body. BCPCH coordinates with NCCA per BOL Art. IX, Sec. 25.[^13]
   (6) **RA 9470** — National Archives standards apply to BARMM archival records. Suppletory.[^14]
   (7) **PD 374** (amending RA 4846) — archaeological excavation permits. Applies until BCPCH is fully empowered with excavation authority.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. Five national agencies exercise authority: (1) **NCCA** — overall cultural policy coordination; (2) **National Museum of the Philippines** — jurisdiction over nationally-declared cultural properties, archaeological excavation regulation, and natural specimens; (3) **National Historical Commission of the Philippines (NHCP)** — historical markers, monuments, shrines of national significance; (4) **National Library of the Philippines** — book allocation, regional library services, bibliographic standards; (5) **National Archives of the Philippines** — archival standards and national records management. Under BOL Art. IX, Sec. 26, management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites currently under NHCP and National Museum jurisdiction must be transferred to BCPCH — but this transfer requires intergovernmental negotiation and has not been fully completed.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH)** is the primary institution. It is not a ministry but a commission attached to the **Office of the Chief Minister**, created by BAA 13, Book V, Title XVI, Chapter 4, Sec. 46-60. It receives its budget under BAA 85, Chapter XXII (P134,575,177 for FY 2026). The **Bangsamoro Library and Archives** — a separate office under the OCM's Bangsamoro Management and Support System (BAA 13, Book III, Title II) — exercises the library and archives functions.[^5][^6]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. Two site-specific BAAs: **BAA 60** (Hashim and Mimbantas Memorial Site, PB No. 36) and **BAA 61** (Bangsamoro Memorial Marker and Eco-Park in Camp Abubakar, PB No. 35). Both create management boards chaired by the BCPCH. No **comprehensive** cultural heritage code, library act, or museum act has been enacted as of BAA 89.

**Q. What are the BCPCH's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book V, Title XVI, Chapter 4 establishes the BCPCH's complete institutional framework:

   **Mandate** (Sec. 47): The BCPCH ***"shall have the primary responsibility to write the history of the Bangsamoro people and to establish and sustain the cultural institutions, programs, and projects in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** It ***"shall coordinate and work closely with the National Commission for Culture and the Arts, National Historical Commission of the Philippines, National Museum of the Philippines, and other concerned cultural agencies for the preservation of cultural heritage, shall establish libraries and museums, declare and restore historical shrines and cultural sites to preserve the Bangsamoro heritage for posterity."***[^5]

   **Management Transfer** (Sec. 48): ***"The National Government shall transfer the management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites currently under the jurisdiction of the National Museum of the Philippines, National Historical Commission of the Philippines, and other agencies of the National Government to the BCPCH through intergovernmental relations mechanisms."***

   **Powers** (Sec. 49 — 6 enumerated):
   - (a) Identify, declare, validate, and monitor historical shrines and sites, including markers and landmarks;
   - (b) Recommend regional policies and legislation on culture, arts, and heritage;
   - (c) Establish consultative mechanisms for cultural groups;
   - (d) Accredit, register, and oversee cultural mapping initiatives and registry of cultural property;
   - (e) Solicit and accept donations, gifts, and grants;
   - (f) Perform other acts incident to its creation.

   **Functions** (Sec. 50 — 8 enumerated):
   - (a) Support retrieval and conservation of artifacts;
   - (b) Encourage preservation of endangered human cultural resources (weavers, chanters, dancers, craftsmen);
   - (c) Support establishment of cultural and historical monuments, markers, names, and sites;
   - (d) ***"Encourage and support the establishment and/or maintenance all over the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region of museums, libraries, archives, private or public"***;
   - (e) Encourage private sector to establish and maintain private museums and libraries;
   - (f) Encourage scholarly research and documentation of Bangsamoro cultural traditions;
   - (g) Encourage writing of history from the Bangsamoro perspective;
   - (h) Ensure standards of excellence through symposia, workshops, and publications.

   **Composition** (Sec. 51): 7 Commissioners — 5 representing major political subdivisions (Maguindanao, Lanao del Sur, Sulu, Basilan, Tawi-Tawi), plus 2 ex officio from MIPA and Office for Settler Communities. At least 2 must be women.

   **Organizational Structure** (Sec. 55-58): Chairperson with presiding, contracting, and appointing powers (SG 24 and below); Commissioner offices in each province; Executive Director managing day-to-day operations with supervision over Coordination and Support Services Division and Administrative and Finance Division.

   **Bangsamoro Library and Archives** (BAA 13, Book III, Title II): Separate from BCPCH. Headed by a Licensed Librarian (Director II rank). Eight functions: (a) repository of BARMM reports; (b) free library service; (c) cataloging and classification; (d) cooperation with National Library; (e) assisting LGUs in library establishment; (f) children's library; (g) linkage with government/non-government agencies; (h) other functions as provided by law.[^15]

**Q. What can the BCPCH NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 creates the institution and defines general powers and functions. Without a standalone cultural heritage code, the BCPCH cannot: (1) **impose penalties** for destruction, alteration, or unauthorized export of cultural property — BAA 13 contains no penal provisions for heritage offenses; (2) **establish a comprehensive registry system** with legal force — Sec. 49(d) allows the BCPCH to accredit and oversee cultural mapping, but a mandatory cultural property registry with registration obligations on owners requires legislative authority beyond BAA 13; (3) **create heritage zones** with land-use restrictions — designating areas where development is restricted to protect heritage requires legislation that overrides private property rights and LGU planning authority; (4) **regulate archaeological excavation** — while the BCPCH coordinates with national agencies (Sec. 48), it has no independent permitting authority for excavations; (5) **establish museum standards** — accreditation standards, collection management protocols, and museum governance requirements need a museum code.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No resolution directly calls for a comprehensive cultural heritage code. However, Res. No. 171 (preservation of boat-making tradition) signals concern for intangible heritage protection, and the filing of PB No. 204 (comprehensive cultural heritage bill) represents legislative intent.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the BCPCH the correct lead institution for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL itself names the BCPCH (Art. IX, Sec. 24-26) as the institution for this power — this is not an inferential assignment but an express BOL mandate. The BCPCH's BAA 13 mandate directly maps to all three components of Art. V, Sec. 2(kk): libraries (Sec. 50(d)-(e)), museums (Sec. 50(d)-(e)), and heritage sites (Sec. 49(a), Sec. 50(c)). However, the **Bangsamoro Library and Archives** — which is a separate office under the OCM, not under the BCPCH — handles the public library service and government archives functions. This creates a **dual-institution structure**: BCPCH for museums and heritage sites; Bangsamoro Library and Archives for library services and government records. This split is by design (BAA 13 assigns them to different organizational units), though a standalone library act could clarify the relationship.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Four institutions:
   - **BCPCH** — lead institution, BAA 13 Sec. 46-60, BAA 85 Ch. XXII. Operational with P134.6M FY 2026 budget, 5 program areas including Museum Management and Development.
   - **Bangsamoro Library and Archives** — under OCM/BMSS, BAA 13 Book III Title II. Operational as government records repository and public library.
   - **Hashim and Mimbantas Memorial Site Management Board** — created by BAA 60, chaired by BCPCH. Site-specific governance body.
   - **Bangsamoro Memorial Management Board** — created by BAA 61, chaired by BCPCH. Governs Camp Abubakar memorial marker and eco-park.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL-mandated institution (BCPCH) has been created. However, the **management transfer** mandated by Art. IX, Sec. 26 — requiring the National Government to transfer management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites from the National Museum, NHCP, and other national agencies to BCPCH — has not been fully implemented. This is not an institution gap but an **operational gap** requiring intergovernmental negotiation.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Three potential institutions merit consideration: (1) a **Bangsamoro Museum** — a flagship institution distinct from the BCPCH's administrative function; PB No. 299 proposes this; (2) a **Cultural Property Registry Authority** or specialized unit within BCPCH — to manage a mandatory registry of cultural properties within BARMM; (3) a **Cultural Heritage Fund** or *waqf*-based endowment — a dedicated funding mechanism beyond annual appropriations for heritage preservation, artifact acquisition, and museum development.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **National Museum** maintains jurisdiction over nationally-declared cultural properties and archaeological specimens within BARMM — including authority over excavation permits under PD 374. The **NHCP** maintains national historical markers within BARMM territory, including the Sheikh Karimul Makhdum Mosque in Simunul, Tawi-Tawi (declared a National Historical Landmark by RA 10573). The **NCCA** provides policy coordination and access to the National Endowment Fund for Culture and the Arts. The **National Library** provides book allocation support and bibliographic services.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred?**

A. Devolution is **partial and incomplete**. BOL Art. IX, Sec. 26 expressly mandates management transfer, but the intergovernmental mechanism for executing this transfer has not been fully activated. The BCPCH exists and operates with its own budget, but national agencies retain management of nationally-declared sites. The practical effect: BCPCH manages regionally-declared sites and implements its own programs, while nationally-significant sites remain under national agency management pending formal transfer.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes, in three areas: (1) **archaeological excavation** — the National Museum retains permitting authority while BCPCH coordinates per Art. IX, Sec. 26; (2) **heritage site management** — nationally-declared sites within BARMM are managed by national agencies, while regionally-declared sites are managed by BCPCH; (3) **cultural mapping** — both NCCA (through RA 11961) and BCPCH (through BAA 13, Sec. 49(d)) have cultural mapping mandates, creating potential duplication.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. The BCPCH is operational with a FY 2026 budget of P134.6M across 5 programs. Specific accomplishments include: enactment of two heritage site laws (BAA 60 and BAA 61); establishment of the BCPCH as a functioning commission with provincial offices; implementation of cultural preservation and conservation programs; and a dedicated **Museum Management and Development** budget line (P3.1M in BAA 85). The Bangsamoro Library and Archives operates as a government records repository.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. In two areas: (1) **Cultural specificity** — the BCPCH is designed to preserve the heritage of specific Bangsamoro Sultanates and IP communities named in the BOL, a granularity that the national cultural agencies cannot match; (2) **Integration of Islamic heritage** — the BCPCH can incorporate *waqf* traditions, Islamic scholarly traditions, and the preservation of Islamic cultural artifacts in ways that national secular agencies are not structured to do. BAA 60 and BAA 61 — honoring *shuhada* and the Bangsamoro struggle — reflect a heritage narrative that the national system cannot produce.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a standalone cultural heritage code (PB No. 204). Without it, the BCPCH cannot impose penalties for heritage destruction, cannot create a legally-binding cultural property registry, cannot establish heritage zones with land-use restrictions, and cannot independently regulate archaeological excavation. The second constraint is the **incomplete management transfer** under Art. IX, Sec. 26 — nationally-declared sites within BARMM remain under national agency jurisdiction.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national system has: (1) **Comprehensive legislation** — RA 10066 (as amended by RA 11961) provides a detailed cultural heritage protection framework with classification systems, presumptions, penalties, and heritage zones — BARMM has no equivalent; (2) **Cultural property registry** — the NCCA and affiliated agencies maintain a national registry of cultural properties with legal force; (3) **Museum infrastructure** — the National Museum system operates multiple facilities with specialized collections, conservation laboratories, and professional staff; (4) **Archaeological expertise** — the National Museum has decades of archaeological research capacity and excavation teams; (5) **National Endowment Fund** — RA 7356 established a permanent endowment for culture and the arts.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. In three critical areas: (1) **Narrative** — the national cultural heritage framework is structured around a Filipino national narrative that does not fully accommodate the Bangsamoro struggle narrative, Islamic heritage, or the distinct histories of the Sultanates. National museums and historical markers reflect a Manila-centered perspective; (2) **Cultural sensitivity** — national agencies are not structured to handle the Islamic dimensions of Bangsamoro heritage — sacred sites, Islamic manuscripts, *waqf* properties, and Islamic art require specialized curatorial and conservation approaches; (3) **Language and accessibility** — national library and museum services are primarily in Filipino and English; Bangsamoro communities speaking Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, Yakan, and other languages are underserved.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. **Replicate**: The cultural property classification system and presumption framework from RA 10066 — these are sound heritage protection standards. **Adapt**: The cultural property registry system — BCPCH should create a BARMM-specific registry that includes Islamic heritage categories (manuscripts, mosques, *waqf* properties, Islamic art) not present in the national registry. **Replace**: The heritage narrative framework — BARMM museums and heritage programs must center the Bangsamoro historical experience while including Philippine national context per Art. IX, Sec. 25.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes, significantly. Libraries, museums, and heritage sites in the Bangsamoro region preserve a civilization with deep Islamic roots. The Sultanates of Sulu and Maguindanao predate the Spanish colonial period, and their heritage is inseparable from Islamic tradition. **Islamic manuscripts**, **mosque architecture**, ***waqf* properties**, **Islamic calligraphy and decorative arts**, and **oral traditions of Islamic scholarship** are core components of the Bangsamoro cultural heritage that Art. IX, Sec. 24 mandates the BCPCH to preserve.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not expressly reference Shari'ah in Art. IX, Sec. 24-26. However, the BOL's mandate to preserve ***"the rich cultural heritage of the Bangsamoro people and their Sultanates"*** inherently encompasses Islamic heritage — the Sultanates were Islamic polities. Art. X, Sec. 4 empowers Parliament to enact Shari'ah laws on ***"property"*** matters — which could extend to *waqf* properties that constitute part of the cultural heritage.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four principles apply:

   (1) ***Hifz al-'Aql*** (Preservation of Intellect) — one of the five essential objectives (*maqasid al-Shari'ah*). Libraries serve this *maqsad* by preserving and disseminating knowledge. Islamic civilization historically placed libraries (*bayt al-hikmah*, *dar al-kutub*) at the center of learning.

   (2) ***Waqf*** (Endowment) — the Islamic institution of perpetual endowment historically funded libraries, schools, and mosques. A Bangsamoro cultural heritage code could establish a ***waqf*-based endowment fund** for museums and libraries — providing perpetual, self-sustaining funding consistent with Islamic tradition and distinct from government appropriations.

   (3) ***Amanah*** (Trust/Stewardship) — cultural heritage is an *amanah* held for present and future generations. Those managing heritage assets are *amin* (trustees) subject to accountability standards.

   (4) ***Maslahah*** (Public Interest) — heritage preservation serves the common good by strengthening community identity, supporting education, and promoting social cohesion.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. Through a **principled** approach — legislation guided by Islamic values without mandating specific compliance for all cultural activities. Specifically: (1) a *waqf*-based cultural endowment fund as an optional financing mechanism alongside government appropriations; (2) Islamic heritage categories (manuscripts, mosques, *waqf* properties) in the cultural property classification system; (3) a Shari'ah-informed curatorial ethics framework for Islamic artifacts; (4) consultation with the **Bangsamoro Darul Ifta'** on matters involving sacred sites and Islamic heritage — BAA 60 and BAA 61 already include the Darul Ifta' as a member of heritage management boards.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. The BARMM has a **strong basis for divergence** in this power area:

   (1) **Constitutional authorization**: As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), Art. X, Sec. 20(8) expressly enumerates ***"Preservation and development of the cultural heritage"*** as a subject for autonomous region legislative authority. This is a direct constitutional grant — not merely inferred from the BOL.

   (2) **BOL design**: As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL creates a specific institution (BCPCH), mandates management transfer from national agencies (Art. IX, Sec. 26), and imposes only coordination requirements — not consistency requirements. The absence of any ***"consistent with national laws"*** language for this power (compare Art. VII, Sec. 39 for civil service: ***"in accordance with the civil service laws of the National Government"***) significantly expands the legislative space.

   (3) **Shari'ah basis**: As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), the Islamic heritage dimension — *waqf* endowments, Islamic manuscripts, mosque preservation, Sultanate heritage — provides a unique justification for a distinctly Bangsamoro approach that the national framework cannot accommodate.

   The primary constraint is the **national unity obligation** (Art. IX, Sec. 25) — BARMM museums must include Philippine national history. And the **constitutional floor** (Art. XIV, Sec. 16) — cultural treasure remains under State protection, meaning BARMM cannot allow destruction or export of nationally-significant cultural property.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. Two categories of impact: (1) **Cultural property owners** — if BARMM establishes its own cultural property registry with different classification criteria, property owners may face dual registration requirements (national under RA 10066, regional under a BARMM code). A standalone BAA should clarify that BARMM registration satisfies national requirements within BARMM territory, with mutual recognition provisions for nationally-declared properties. (2) **Archaeological researchers** — if BCPCH gains independent excavation permitting authority, researchers would apply to BCPCH rather than the National Museum for BARMM sites. A transition provision should clarify existing permits.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. The BAA would govern within BARMM. RA 10066 and other national cultural heritage laws would apply suppletorily where the BAA is silent. Nationally-declared sites (e.g., RA 10573 for the Sheikh Karimul Makhdum Mosque) would be subject to the intergovernmental transfer mechanism of Art. IX, Sec. 26 — the BAA could not unilaterally strip national designation but would receive management authority upon transfer.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Three overlapping jurisdictions: (1) **Archaeological excavation** — the National Museum retains permitting authority under PD 374, while the BCPCH has a coordination mandate under Art. IX, Sec. 26. A standalone BAA should clarify that BCPCH issues permits for BARMM-territory excavations, with the National Museum providing technical support rather than primary authority. (2) **Heritage site management** — nationally-declared sites within BARMM (e.g., Sheikh Karimul Makhdum Mosque under RA 10573) remain under national jurisdiction pending transfer. An intergovernmental agreement should establish a transfer timeline. (3) **Cultural mapping** — both NCCA (RA 11961) and BCPCH (BAA 13, Sec. 49(d)) mandate cultural mapping. Coordination protocols should prevent duplication.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB No. 204** (comprehensive cultural heritage bill) would address the primary legislative gap — providing the regulatory framework, penalty provisions, registry system, and heritage zone designations that BAA 13 cannot deliver. **PB No. 299** (Bangsamoro museum) would establish a flagship institution. **PB Nos. 327, 335, and 358** (site-specific declarations) follow the BAA 60/61 model but do not address systemic gaps. Even if PB 204 passes, gaps remain in: (1) a standalone **library act** (public library system, digital library infrastructure, LGU library mandates); (2) *waqf*-based cultural endowment legislation.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage)** directly depends on this power — the chapter's goals of preserving cultural identity, documenting heritage, and promoting cultural diversity require functioning libraries, museums, and heritage site management. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda's** social development pillar relies on cultural heritage as a foundation for community identity and social cohesion. [UNVERIFIED]

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Three outcomes at risk: (1) **Heritage loss** — without penalty provisions, cultural properties destroyed during conflict (especially Marawi Siege artifacts, per PB No. 181) cannot be legally protected from further loss, looting, or unauthorized sale; (2) **Institutional weakness** — BCPCH operates on general authority without the regulatory tools (mandatory registration, heritage zones, excavation permits) that a standalone code would provide — limiting its effectiveness relative to the national agencies from which management is supposed to transfer; (3) **Tourism deficit** — heritage sites and museums are tourism infrastructure; without legal frameworks for site preservation and museum standards, BARMM cannot fully develop cultural tourism as an economic driver.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the relationship between BCPCH and the Bangsamoro Library and Archives?**

A. They are **separate institutions** under different organizational structures within the Bangsamoro Government. The **BCPCH** is a commission attached to the Office of the Chief Minister (BAA 13, Book V, Title XVI, Ch. 4) with a dedicated BAA 85 budget chapter (XXII). The **Bangsamoro Library and Archives** is part of the Bangsamoro Management and Support System (BMSS) under the OCM (BAA 13, Book III, Title II). The BCPCH's Sec. 50(d) mandates it to ***"encourage and support the establishment and/or maintenance all over the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region of museums, libraries, archives"*** — suggesting a promotional/policy role over libraries — while the Bangsamoro Library and Archives handles direct library operations. A standalone library act should clarify whether the Bangsamoro Library and Archives reports to the BCPCH for policy purposes or remains independently under the OCM.

**Q. How does the national unity obligation in Art. IX, Sec. 25 affect museum content?**

A. Art. IX, Sec. 25 requires the BCPCH to ***"ensure the inclusion of Philippine history, culture, and heritage in the establishment of museums and similar institutions, programs and projects to foster unity among Filipinos."*** This creates a **dual narrative obligation**: museums must present both the Bangsamoro historical experience (Sultanates, struggle for self-determination, Islamic civilization) and the broader Philippine national context (Filipino nationhood, shared colonial history, constitutional democracy). This does not require equal treatment — BARMM museums may center the Bangsamoro narrative while including Philippine national context — but a museum that entirely excludes the Philippine dimension would violate Art. IX, Sec. 25.

**Q. What happens to Marawi cultural heritage destroyed during the 2017 siege?**

A. PB No. 181 (database of cultural properties lost during the Marawi Siege) addresses this through documentation. The BCPCH's Sec. 50(a) function — ***"support, monitor and systematize the retrieval and conservation of artifacts of Bangsamoro culture and history and all Bangsamoro cultural treasures from all over the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region and the Philippine Archipelago"*** — provides a legal basis for artifact recovery. However, criminal prosecution for heritage destruction requires either national law (RA 10066 penalties) or a standalone Bangsamoro cultural heritage code with its own penal provisions.

**Q. Can BARMM restrict the export of cultural property from the Bangsamoro region?**

A. Under the constitutional framework established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), Art. XIV, Sec. 16 declares that ***"All the country's artistic and historic wealth constitutes the cultural treasure of the nation and shall be under the protection of the State which may regulate its disposition."*** This gives BARMM constitutional authority to regulate the disposition of cultural property within its territory. RA 10066 already restricts export of important cultural properties. A Bangsamoro cultural heritage code could impose additional or more stringent restrictions on export from BARMM, consistent with the constitutional mandate.

**Q. What role does the Bangsamoro Darul Ifta' play in cultural heritage?**

A. The Darul Ifta' is included as a member of both management boards created by BAA 60 and BAA 61 — establishing a precedent for its involvement in heritage governance. For heritage sites with Islamic significance (mosques, burial sites of Islamic scholars, Sultanate palaces), the Darul Ifta' provides religious guidance on appropriate preservation practices, ensures Shari'ah-compliant site management (e.g., burial site protocols), and validates the Islamic historical narrative. A standalone cultural heritage code should formalize the Darul Ifta's consultative role for all Islamic heritage sites.

| **Institutional** | No dedicated Bangsamoro museum | **Bangsamoro National Museum** | Flagship institution for Bangsamoro history, culture, and heritage — distinct from BCPCH's administrative function | Art. IX, Sec. 25 |
| **Operational** | Incomplete management transfer from national agencies | **Intergovernmental agreement** on heritage site transfer timeline and protocols | Transfer of nationally-declared Bangsamoro sites from National Museum, NHCP to BCPCH; technical support framework; overlapping jurisdiction for nationally-significant properties | Art.
