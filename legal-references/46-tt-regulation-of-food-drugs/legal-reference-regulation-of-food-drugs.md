# (tt) Regulation of the Manufacture and Distribution of Food, Drinks, Drugs, and Tobacco

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(tt)** — ***"Regulation of the manufacture and distribution of food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(tt) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | **Yes — the strictest consistency requirement among all 55 powers.** Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 2) requires regulation ***"in accordance with the policies, rules and regulations of the concerned national government agencies."*** This is not merely "consistent with" or "subject to" national law — it requires conformity with the **operational policies and regulations** of agencies like the FDA, not just with statutory text.[^1] |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Health (MOH)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Sec. 1-27 |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT) — Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 7); Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST) — halal quality standards (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII); Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission (BAA 83); Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) — food production and supply |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 9 (Social Development — Health, Nutrition, and Sanitation) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 2): ***"The Parliament shall enact appropriate legislation towards this end, including the regulation of the manufacture and distribution of food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco."*** |

  <td>Art. V, Sec. 2(tt)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"Regulation of the manufacture and distribution of food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco;"</strong></em></td>
  <td>Grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over the regulation of manufacturing and distribution of four product categories: food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco. This is a <strong>regulatory</strong> power — covering standards, licensing, inspection, and enforcement — not a revenue or taxing power.</td>

<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 2)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall protect and promote the right to health of the people and instill health consciousness among them. It shall also uphold the people's right to have access to essential goods, health, and other social services that promote their well-being. The Parliament shall enact appropriate legislation towards this end, including the regulation of the manufacture and distribution of food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco in accordance with the policies, rules and regulations of the concerned national government agencies."</strong></em></td>
  <td>The <strong>primary elaborating provision</strong>. Contains three elements: (1) a <strong>right to health</strong> directive; (2) an <strong>express legislative mandate</strong> — Parliament shall enact appropriate legislation; (3) the <strong>strictest consistency requirement</strong> in the entire BOL — regulation must be ***"in accordance with the policies, rules and regulations of the concerned national government agencies."*** The phrase "in accordance with" requires conformity, not merely consistency. The "concerned national government agencies" means primarily the FDA, DOH, and PDEA.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 1)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall adopt a policy on health that provides for a comprehensive and integrated health service delivery for its constituents."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Establishes the broader health policy framework within which food and drug regulation operates. Food and drug safety is a <strong>component of</strong> the comprehensive health service delivery mandate.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 8</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall provide, maintain, and ensure the delivery of basic and responsive health programs, quality education, appropriate services, livelihood opportunities, affordable and progressive housing projects, power and electricity, and water supply, among others, to the Bangsamoro people and other inhabitants of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Social justice provision that obligates the Bangsamoro Government to provide <strong>basic and responsive health programs</strong>. Access to safe food, drugs, and essential medicines is a prerequisite for this obligation.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XIII, Sec. 30</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall have the power to accredit halal-certifying bodies in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It shall promote awareness through the development and implementation of a halal campaign program. The Parliament shall enact laws to further strengthen its policy and programs on halal development."</strong></em></td>
  <td><strong>Halal accreditation power</strong>. Directly intersects food regulation because halal certification governs the manufacture, processing, and distribution of food and non-food products. Creates a <strong>second legislative mandate</strong> — Parliament shall enact halal development laws. The halal power operates alongside but is distinct from the general food/drug regulatory power.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XIII, Sec. 29</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall regulate, restrict, or prohibit the use, importation, transit, transport, deposit, disposal, and dumping of toxic or hazardous substances within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Hazardous substances regulation overlaps with drug safety insofar as pharmaceutical waste, expired medications, and toxic chemical compounds used in food processing are concerned.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XII, Sec. 9(f)</td>
  <td><em><strong>"Excise taxes on articles enumerated under the National Internal Revenue Code of 1997, as amended, and taxes, fees, or charges on petroleum products;"</strong></em></td>
  <td>Lists <strong>excise taxes</strong> — including those on tobacco and alcohol products — among the taxes the Bangsamoro Government <strong>cannot impose</strong>. Sin tax revenue collection remains a national government function. BARMM's power over tobacco is <strong>regulatory</strong> (manufacturing and distribution standards), not fiscal.</td>
</tr>

  <td><em><strong>"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall have the power to enact laws on..."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Universal constraint on all 55 powers: the Bangsamoro Government operates within the constitutional framework (Art. X, Sec. 15 — autonomous regions operate <strong>"within the framework of this Constitution"</strong>) and is subject to the BOL itself.</td>
</tr>

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant, constitutional floor, national law, implementation, Shari'ah dimension, divergence, gaps, development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**

A. Art. V, Sec. 2(tt) enumerates the power over ***"Regulation of the manufacture and distribution of food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco."***[^1] This is a **regulatory** power — it covers standard-setting, licensing, inspection, and enforcement over four product categories. The word "regulation" distinguishes this from revenue powers (excise taxes on tobacco and alcohol are excluded under Art. XII, Sec. 9(f)).[^2] The scope covers **manufacturing** (production, processing, compounding) and **distribution** (sale, transport, marketing, dispensing) — the entire supply chain from production to consumer access.

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. The primary elaborating provision is **Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 2)**, which states that ***"The Parliament shall enact appropriate legislation towards this end, including the regulation of the manufacture and distribution of food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco in accordance with the policies, rules and regulations of the concerned national government agencies."***[^3] This provision does three things: (1) creates an **express legislative mandate** — Parliament shall enact legislation; (2) identifies food/drug/tobacco regulation as part of the broader **health service delivery** obligation; and (3) imposes the **strictest consistency requirement** in the BOL — the phrase ***"in accordance with"*** requires conformity, not merely consistency.

Additionally, **Art. XIII, Sec. 30** grants the Bangsamoro Government the power to ***"accredit halal-certifying bodies in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** and mandates Parliament to ***"enact laws to further strengthen its policy and programs on halal development."***[^4] This creates a parallel regulatory track for **halal certification** of food products.

**Art. IX, Sec. 8** obligates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"provide, maintain, and ensure the delivery of basic and responsive health programs"***[^5] — of which food and drug safety is a prerequisite component.

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes — **two express mandates**: (1) Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 2): Parliament shall enact ***"appropriate legislation"*** including food/drug/tobacco regulation; (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 30: Parliament shall ***"enact laws to further strengthen its policy and programs on halal development."*** Neither mandate has been fulfilled through a standalone BAA — the power currently operates only through BAA 13 general mandates.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not create a specific food and drug regulatory body. Art. IX, Sec. 22 assigns the mandate to the Bangsamoro Government generally. The institutional implementation falls to the **Ministry of Health** under BAA 13 (Book VI, Title V) for food and drug safety, and the **Bangsamoro Halal Board** under MTIT (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 7) for halal certification. The **Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission** was subsequently created by BAA 83 for food and nutrition policy coordination.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. This is the **most constrained** of the 55 enumerated powers. Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 2) requires regulation ***"in accordance with the policies, rules and regulations of the concerned national government agencies."***[^3] This phrase establishes a **five-layer constraint framework**:

1. **"In accordance with"** — requires conformity, stronger than "consistent with" or "subject to"
2. **"Policies"** — includes not just statutes but executive policy directions of national agencies
3. **"Rules"** — includes administrative orders, circulars, and implementing rules and regulations (IRRs)
4. **"Regulations"** — includes specific technical standards, product registration requirements, and inspection protocols
5. **"Concerned national government agencies"** — plural, covering FDA (food, drugs, cosmetics), DOH (health policy), PDEA/DDB (dangerous drugs), DTI (consumer protection), DA (agricultural products), and potentially the Bureau of Customs (import regulation)

Additionally, Art. XII, Sec. 9(f) excludes **excise taxes** on tobacco and alcohol from BARMM's taxing power — limiting the power to regulation, not taxation.[^2]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**

A. The universal constraint applies: Art. X, Sec. 15 — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***[^6] Subject-specific provisions include:

- **Art. II, Sec. 15** — ***"The State shall protect and promote the right to health of the people and instill health consciousness among them."***[^7] A state policy declaration that the BOL echoes verbatim in Art. IX, Sec. 22.
- **Art. XIII, Sec. 11** — ***"The State shall adopt an integrated and comprehensive approach to health development which shall endeavor to make essential goods, health and other social services available to all the people at affordable cost."***[^8] Establishes the framework of accessible health services including essential medicines.
- **Art. XIII, Sec. 12** — ***"The State shall establish and maintain an effective food and drug regulatory system and undertake appropriate health manpower development and research, responsive to the country's health needs and problems."***[^9] The **most directly relevant constitutional provision**. This is a constitutional mandate to maintain a food and drug regulatory system — a mandate Congress fulfilled through RA 3720 and RA 9711 (creating and strengthening the FDA). Any BARMM regulation operates within this constitutionally mandated national system.

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees: (1) **right to health** (Art. II, Sec. 15); (2) access to **essential goods and health services at affordable cost** (Art. XIII, Sec. 11) — which includes affordable medicines; (3) an **effective food and drug regulatory system** (Art. XIII, Sec. 12) — meaning BARMM residents are constitutionally entitled to the same level of food and drug safety protection as all Filipinos. BARMM may not weaken these protections, though it may **add** protections (such as halal compliance requirements).

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 12 mandates the State to ***"establish and maintain an effective food and drug regulatory system."***[^9] Congress fulfilled this through the FDA (RA 3720, as strengthened by RA 9711). This constitutional mandate carries particular weight for BARMM because the BOL's "in accordance with" language was clearly drafted to ensure the constitutionally mandated national FDA system remains intact within BARMM.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**

A. The national framework is **comprehensive and mature**:

- **Food safety**: RA 3720 (Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act, 1963), RA 9711 (FDA Act, 2009), and RA 10611 (Food Safety Act, 2013) create a multi-layered food safety system covering product registration, manufacturing standards, post-market surveillance, and recall authority.
- **Drug regulation**: RA 9711 (FDA — pharmaceutical registration), RA 6675 (Generics Act), RA 9502 (Cheaper Medicines), RA 8203 (Counterfeit Drugs), RA 10918 (Pharmacy Act), and RA 9165 (Comprehensive Dangerous Drugs Act) create a parallel drug safety and drug control framework.
- **Tobacco regulation**: RA 9211 (Tobacco Regulation Act), RA 10643 (Graphic Health Warnings), and RA 11900 (Vape/Novel Tobacco Products) regulate manufacturing, packaging, sale, distribution, advertising, and use of tobacco products.
- **Halal certification**: RA 10817 (Philippine Halal Export Development and Promotion Act) creates the national Philippine Halal Board for export-oriented halal products.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. **All** major food, drug, and tobacco laws apply **directly** in BARMM because the BOL's "in accordance with" requirement means BARMM cannot establish a parallel regulatory system that deviates from national agency standards. Specifically: RA 9711 (FDA authority), RA 10611 (food safety), RA 9211 (tobacco), RA 9165 (dangerous drugs), RA 6675 and RA 9502 (generics and affordable medicines), and RA 10918 (pharmacy) all apply with full force. No BAA supersedes any of these laws.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. Multiple national agencies operate directly:

- **Food and Drug Administration (FDA)** — product registration, licensing, inspection, and enforcement for food, drugs, cosmetics, and medical devices. The primary "concerned national government agency" referenced in Art. IX, Sec. 22.
- **Department of Health (DOH)** — health policy, pharmaceutical benefits, and health facility regulation.
- **Philippine Drug Enforcement Agency (PDEA)** — dangerous drug enforcement.
- **Dangerous Drugs Board (DDB)** — policy-making on dangerous drugs.
- **Department of Agriculture (DA)** — fresh agricultural product safety.
- **Department of Trade and Industry (DTI)** — consumer product standards and labeling.
- **Philippine Halal Export Development and Promotion Board** — national halal certification for export.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Health (MOH)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Sec. 1-27.[^10] MOH is the primary regulator for food and drug safety in BARMM. The **Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT)** exercises complementary authority through the **Bangsamoro Halal Board** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 7, Sec. 33-36)[^11] for halal certification. The **Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST)** contributes through quality standards including halal for food and non-food industry (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII, Sec. 3(i)).[^12]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted specifically for food, drug, or tobacco regulation. BAA 83 (Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission) touches food policy and nutrition but is not a food safety regulatory law. The power operates entirely through BAA 13 general mandates.

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Sec. 2 establishes the MOH mandate: ***"The Ministry shall be primarily responsible for the formulation, planning, implementation and coordination of policies and programs for health. It shall promote, protect, preserve and restore the health of the people through the provision and delivery of health services through regulation and encouragement of providers of health goods and services."***[^10]

The powers and functions directly relevant to this power include (Sec. 3):

- **(d)** ***"Administer all laws, rules and regulations in the field of health, including quarantine laws and food and drug safety laws"***[^13] — the broadest food/drug mandate
- **(h)** ***"Regulate the operation and issue licenses and permits to government and private hospitals, clinics and dispensaries, laboratories, blood banks, drugstores and such other establishments which by the nature of their functions are required to be regulated by the Ministry"***[^14] — authorizes **drugstore licensing**
- **(j)** ***"Formulate policies, guidelines, regulations as well as operating procedures congruent to Islamic teachings on health and sanitation"***[^15] — unique Shari'ah-congruent health regulation authority

For halal regulation, the **Bangsamoro Halal Board** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 7, Sec. 34) has the powers to:[^11]

- **(a)** ***"Grant or deny applications for issuance of certificates of accreditation of halal standards"***
- **(b)** ***"Formulate, advocate, coordinate, oversee and assess the implementation of the Bangsamoro Halal Development and Promotion Program"***
- **(c)** ***"Coordinate with existing government regulatory agencies on matters of halal standard setting, including the application thereof on products, processes and services"***

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 provides the **institutional mandate** but cannot deliver:

1. **A comprehensive food safety code** — detailed manufacturing standards, testing protocols, inspection schedules, and penalty provisions for food safety violations within BARMM
2. **Regional pharmaceutical regulation** — a framework for drug distribution monitoring, drug quality assurance, and pharmaceutical supply chain integrity within BARMM, operating within FDA parameters
3. **Tobacco control enforcement** — BARMM-specific smoking regulations, tobacco product distribution restrictions, and enforcement mechanisms beyond the national framework
4. **Halal food regulatory code** — comprehensive halal food production, processing, distribution, and labeling standards with legal force (vs. the Halal Board's current accreditation-only mandate)
5. **A penalty framework** — BAA 13, Sec. 6(f) states the Minister may promulgate administrative issuances but these ***"shall not prescribe penalties for their violation, except when expressly authorized by law"***[^16] — without a standalone BAA, MOH cannot impose sanctions for food/drug safety violations

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. Resolutions 371 (nutritional guidelines for Muslim Filipinos), 540-541 (pork/no-pork signage), 358-359 (halal food integrity), 200 (halal ecosystem program), and 201 (Ibadah-friendly hospital services) collectively signal strong parliamentary interest in the **halal dimension** of food regulation. No resolution specifically addresses a comprehensive food and drug safety code.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. **MOH is correct as lead** for general food and drug safety regulation — its BAA 13 mandate expressly covers food and drug safety laws and drugstore licensing. However, the power has a **multi-ministry dimension** that a single lead cannot fully cover:

- **MTIT** (through the Halal Board) is the correct lead for halal food certification and halal industry development
- **MOST** contributes halal quality standards and food testing
- **MAFAR** handles agricultural food production and primary product safety
- **Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission** (BAA 83) coordinates food and nutrition policy

The power thus requires **inter-agency coordination** that does not currently have a formal mechanism.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Existing institutions:

- **Ministry of Health (MOH)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title V. Operational. Lead regulator for food/drug safety. Licenses drugstores and health facilities.
- **Bangsamoro Halal Board** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Ch. 7. Operational. Attached to MTIT. Accredits halal standards. Composed of 9 members chaired by the MTIT Minister.
- **Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission** — BAA 83. Created 2025. Attached to OCM. Coordinates food and nutrition policy. Chaired by a nutrition expert appointed by the Chief Minister.
- **Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII. Contributes halal quality standards through testing and analysis.
- **Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title IX. Handles food production and agricultural product quality.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not mandate a specific food and drug regulatory body. However, Art. IX, Sec. 22's legislative mandate for ***"appropriate legislation"*** including food/drug/tobacco regulation implies the need for an institutional framework to implement that legislation — which does not yet exist as a standalone structure.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Yes — full exercise of this power likely requires:

1. A **Bangsamoro Food and Drug Regulatory Office** (or unit within MOH) — not a standalone FDA equivalent, but a coordinating body that interfaces with the national FDA for product registration, maintains regional inspection capacity, and monitors food/drug safety within BARMM
2. A **Halal Regulatory Authority** with expanded powers beyond the current Halal Board's accreditation mandate — covering halal food production standards, halal supply chain certification, and halal enforcement
3. An **inter-agency Food Safety Coordinating Committee** — formalizing the coordination among MOH, MTIT/Halal Board, MAFAR, MOST, and the Nutrition Commission

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The FDA retains **full regulatory authority** in BARMM for product registration, manufacturing facility licensing, and post-market surveillance. No FDA function has been devolved. PDEA operates in BARMM for dangerous drug enforcement. The DOH sets health policy standards that apply in BARMM. The DA regulates fresh agricultural product safety. The DTI enforces consumer product labeling standards. The Philippine Halal Board exercises national halal certification authority for export products.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. **Devolution has not occurred.** The "in accordance with" requirement in Art. IX, Sec. 22 effectively preserves national agency authority intact. Unlike other powers where devolution is ongoing (education, health services, social services), food and drug regulation remains **entirely under national agency control** for core functions. MOH's BAA 13 mandate to administer "food and drug safety laws" operates as a **supplementary** implementation role, not a replacement for FDA authority.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes — several overlapping jurisdictions:

1. **Drugstore licensing**: MOH (BAA 13, Sec. 3(h)) licenses drugstores, while the FDA registers pharmaceutical products sold in those drugstores — parallel regulatory layers
2. **Halal certification**: The Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13) accredits halal-certifying bodies within BARMM, while the Philippine Halal Board (RA 10817) governs national halal certification for export — potential standard-setting conflict
3. **Food establishment inspection**: LGUs issue business permits with sanitary requirements, MOH exercises health regulatory oversight, and FDA regulates processed food products — three-tiered regulation

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. Concrete achievements remain limited due to the absence of standalone legislation:

- **Halal Board establishment** — operational under BAA 13, MTIT. Has begun halal accreditation processes.
- **Nutrition Commission creation** — BAA 83 (2025) created the Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission for food and nutrition policy coordination.
- **Parliamentary resolutions** — multiple resolutions on halal food integrity (358-359, 540-541), nutritional guidelines for Muslims (371), and halal ecosystem development (200).
- **Pending bills** — PB 164 (halal labeling), PB 268 (Halal Consumers Act), PB 187 (school food regulation) indicate active legislative interest.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM holds a **unique advantage in halal regulation**:

- **Halal Board with regional authority** — BARMM has a dedicated halal accreditation body that the national government lacks at the regional level
- **Shari'ah integration** — BAA 13 mandates MOH to formulate policies ***"congruent to Islamic teachings on health and sanitation"*** — no national health agency has this mandate
- **Cultural and linguistic fit** — halal food standards, pork/no-pork labeling, and Muslim dietary compliance are integral to BARMM's population — the national framework is religion-neutral and does not address these needs

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the "in accordance with" requirement itself. Unlike other powers where BARMM can legislate first and coordinate later, food and drug regulation is **pre-constrained** by national agency policies. BARMM cannot establish any standard that deviates from FDA, DOH, or PDEA regulations. This means:

- No standalone food safety code can set lower standards than FDA
- No drug regulation can conflict with FDA product registration requirements
- No tobacco regulation can be weaker than RA 9211

The secondary constraint is the **absence of standalone legislation** — without a BAA, MOH cannot impose penalties, establish inspection protocols, or create enforcement mechanisms. The Halal Board cannot compel compliance with halal standards. The third constraint is **institutional capacity** — MOH lacks dedicated food safety inspection staff and laboratory capacity.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government operates a **mature, multi-decade regulatory infrastructure**:

- **FDA** — established 1963 (RA 3720), strengthened 2009 (RA 9711). Maintains product registration databases for all food, drugs, cosmetics, and medical devices. Operates regional offices, testing laboratories, and inspection teams. Issues Certificates of Product Registration (CPR), Licenses to Operate (LTO), and conducts post-market surveillance. Over 60 years of institutional experience.
- **PDEA** — nationwide enforcement network for dangerous drugs. Intelligence, interdiction, and prosecution capabilities.
- **Food Safety Regulation Coordinating Board** — multi-agency coordinating body under RA 10611.
- **Sin tax revenue system** — national excise tax collection on tobacco and alcohol with revenues earmarked for health programs under RA 11346 and RA 11467.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in specific ways:

- **No halal-specific food safety standards** — the FDA regulates safety but does not certify halal compliance. A product can be FDA-approved but haram.
- **No Shari'ah-compliant pharmaceutical guidance** — the national drug regulatory system does not distinguish between halal and non-halal pharmaceutical ingredients (gelatin capsules, alcohol-based solvents).
- **Underserved regulatory presence** — FDA's inspection and enforcement capacity in BARMM provinces is significantly weaker than in urban centers.
- **Language and cultural barriers** — FDA labeling requirements are in English and Filipino. There is no provision for Arabic or local language labeling relevant to BARMM's predominantly Muslim population.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A.

- **Replicate**: FDA product registration and licensing system — adopt national standards as the baseline. Manufacturing standards for food and drugs should conform to FDA requirements.
- **Adapt**: Food safety inspection protocols — maintain FDA standards but establish BARMM-level inspection capacity with culturally competent inspectors. Add halal verification as a supplementary inspection criterion.
- **Adapt**: Tobacco regulation — adopt RA 9211 as the floor, but BARMM may adopt **stricter** regulations drawing on Shari'ah principles (expanded smoking bans, stronger advertising restrictions, higher age limits for tobacco purchase).
- **Replace**: Halal certification — the national Philippine Halal Board (RA 10817) focuses on export. BARMM should build a **domestic halal regulatory system** through the Halal Board that covers local food production, food service, and domestic distribution — a function the national system does not adequately serve.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. **Yes — this power has the strongest Shari'ah dimension of any regulatory power.** The concepts of *halal* (permissible) and *haram* (forbidden) apply directly to all four regulated product categories: food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco. Shari'ah principles govern what Muslims may consume, how food must be processed, which pharmaceutical ingredients are permissible, whether intoxicants may be sold, and how tobacco use is viewed. For BARMM's predominantly Muslim population, food and drug regulation without a Shari'ah dimension is incomplete.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. Three provisions connect Shari'ah to this power: (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 30** — halal accreditation power and legislative mandate for halal development; (2) **Art. X, Sec. 4** — Parliament may enact Shari'ah laws on ***"commercial and other civil actions"*** (para. 2), which covers halal food trade; (3) **BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Sec. 3(j)** — MOH shall formulate policies ***"congruent to Islamic teachings on health and sanitation."***

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. The relevant principles include:

- ***Halal*** and ***tayyib*** — food must be both permissible and wholesome (Qur'an 2:168). This dual requirement means food regulation must address both religious permissibility and safety.
- ***Haram*** prohibitions — dead animals, blood, pork, and food dedicated to other than Allah (Qur'an 2:173). Requires traceability, labeling, and segregation in food processing and distribution.
- ***Khamr*** prohibition — intoxicants are forbidden (Qur'an 5:90). Directly relevant to the regulation of alcoholic drinks and substances that impair mental function.
- ***Hifz al-nafs*** — preservation of life. Supports strict food safety, drug safety, and tobacco regulation.
- ***Hifz al-'aql*** — preservation of intellect. Supports regulation of intoxicants, drugs, and substances affecting mental health.
- ***Maslahah*** — public interest/welfare. Provides jurisprudential basis for regulatory measures that protect community health even when they restrict commercial freedom.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. A **dual-track** approach is appropriate: (1) **mandatory halal compliance** for food and pharmaceutical products sold in BARMM government facilities, schools, hospitals, and official events; (2) **mandatory halal labeling** for all food products sold in BARMM — requiring disclosure, not necessarily compliance; (3) **Shari'ah-informed standards** for tobacco and intoxicant regulation — drawing on *hifz al-nafs* and *hifz al-'aql* to justify standards stricter than the national floor. The **Bangsamoro Halal Board** and the **Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta'** should serve as the Shari'ah review mechanism — the Halal Board for commercial standards, the Darul-Ifta' for religious rulings on permissibility.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. This is the **most constrained power** in the BOL for divergence from national law. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section, Art. IX, Sec. 22 requires regulation ***"in accordance with the policies, rules and regulations of the concerned national government agencies"*** — language that permits virtually **no downward divergence**. BARMM cannot set lower food safety standards, weaker drug registration requirements, or less restrictive tobacco regulations than the national framework.

However, **upward divergence** is permitted and even encouraged:

1. **Halal requirements** — BARMM can add halal compliance as a **supplementary regulatory layer** on top of FDA safety standards. This is not a deviation from national law but an addition. Art. XIII, Sec. 30 provides the BOL basis; the Shari'ah principles discussed in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section provide the jurisprudential justification.
2. **Stricter tobacco regulation** — BARMM can adopt more restrictive smoking bans, advertising limitations, and distribution restrictions than RA 9211, drawing on *hifz al-nafs* and *hifz al-'aql*.
3. **Stricter food labeling** — BARMM can require pork/no-pork disclosure, halal labeling, and ingredient transparency beyond national DTI requirements.
4. **Islamic health standards** — BAA 13 authorizes MOH to formulate policies "congruent to Islamic teachings" — this permits health facility regulations that exceed national standards (halal pharmaceuticals, Islamic dietary compliance in hospitals).

As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section, Art. XIII, Sec. 12 mandates an effective food and drug regulatory system — BARMM's additional halal and Shari'ah-based requirements **strengthen** rather than undermine this constitutional mandate.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. Two scenarios arise: (1) Products manufactured in BARMM must comply with **both** FDA national standards and any BARMM-specific requirements (halal, stricter labeling) — increasing compliance costs for manufacturers. (2) Products from outside BARMM entering the region must comply with **BARMM labeling requirements** (halal disclosure) even if they already have FDA approval. This requires **mutual recognition provisions** — national FDA registration should be recognized in BARMM, and BARMM's halal certification should be recognized nationally.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. National law will **continue to apply** in parallel. The "in accordance with" requirement means a BARMM food and drug code would operate as a **supplementary regulatory layer** — adding BARMM-specific requirements (halal, Shari'ah-congruent standards, stricter controls) on top of the national framework. National law would apply suppletorily for any matter the BAA does not address. FDA authority would remain intact for product registration and core safety functions.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Three significant overlaps require resolution:

1. **MOH vs. FDA on drugstore and food establishment regulation** — MOH licenses drugstores (BAA 13, Sec. 3(h)), while FDA regulates the products sold in those drugstores. No MOA or protocol clarifies the boundary between regional facility licensing and national product regulation.
2. **Bangsamoro Halal Board vs. Philippine Halal Board** — the Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13) accredits halal-certifying bodies within BARMM; the Philippine Halal Board (RA 10817) governs national halal export certification. When a BARMM food producer exports, which board's certification applies? No harmonization mechanism exists.
3. **MOH/MOA food safety vs. DA/FDA food safety** — the national Food Safety Act (RA 10611) assigns fresh product safety to DA and processed food to FDA. BARMM's MAFAR and MOH do not have clearly delineated food safety roles that map to this national division.

A standalone **Bangsamoro Food and Drug Safety Act** and a **Bangsamoro Halal Act** would resolve these jurisdictional overlaps by establishing BARMM-level institutional arrangements that interface with the national framework.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. PB 164 (halal labeling in fast food restaurants) and PB 268 (Halal Consumers Act) address portions of the halal regulation gap. PB 187 (prohibition of unhealthy food sale in schools) addresses school-level food regulation. None of these bills address the comprehensive food and drug safety gap — no pending bill proposes a **Bangsamoro Food and Drug Safety Code** or a comprehensive **tobacco control act** for BARMM.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 9** (Social Development — Health, Nutrition, and Sanitation) depends on food and drug safety for health outcome improvement. Malnutrition indicators cannot improve without food quality standards. Maternal and child health targets require pharmaceutical access and drug safety. **BDP Chapter 7** (Economic Development) includes halal industry development as an economic driver — this requires credible halal food regulation and certification to attract investment and access halal export markets.

The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** links through **Pillar 3** (Accelerated Social Services Delivery) — food and drug safety is a precondition for health service quality — and **Pillar 2** (Economic Transformation) — halal industry development requires a regulatory framework that international halal markets recognize.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without standalone legislation: (1) **Malnutrition** — food quality and safety standards cannot be enforced regionally; the Nutrition Commission (BAA 83) coordinates policy but has no enforcement tools for food safety. (2) **Halal industry development** — without a halal regulatory code, BARMM's halal certification lacks legal force, deterring investment and undermining export credibility. (3) **Pharmaceutical access** — without regional drug distribution monitoring, pharmaceutical supply chain gaps persist in remote BARMM provinces. (4) **Tobacco-related health burden** — without a regional tobacco control framework, BARMM cannot implement tobacco prevention programs calibrated to its unique Shari'ah-informed health policy.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why is this the most constrained of the 55 enumerated powers?**

A. Three factors make this power uniquely constrained: (1) The BOL uses the phrase ***"in accordance with the policies, rules and regulations of the concerned national government agencies"*** — the strongest conformity language in the entire BOL. Compare with "consistent with" (used for education, civil service) or "subject to" (used for natural resources) — "in accordance with" leaves the least room for deviation. (2) The constitutional mandate (Art. XIII, Sec. 12) for an "effective food and drug regulatory system" was implemented through the FDA — a centralized national agency whose authority has not been devolved to any region. (3) Excise taxes on tobacco and alcohol are expressly excluded from BARMM's taxing power (Art. XII, Sec. 9(f)), removing the fiscal dimension entirely.

**Q. Has the FDA's regulatory authority been devolved to BARMM?**

A. **No.** FDA authority has not been devolved. Unlike health service delivery (where DOH hospitals transfer to MOH), or education (where DepEd functions transfer to MBHTE), the FDA retains full regulatory jurisdiction nationwide. Product registration, manufacturing facility licensing, quality testing, and post-market surveillance remain national FDA functions. BARMM's role under BAA 13 is to **administer** food and drug safety laws — not to replace the national regulatory system. This is by design: Art. IX, Sec. 22's "in accordance with" language preserves FDA authority.

**Q. What is the unique halal angle and why does it matter?**

A. Halal regulation is BARMM's **primary basis for adding value** beyond the national framework. The national FDA regulates safety but not religious permissibility. For BARMM's predominantly Muslim population, a product that is safe but not halal fails to meet the standard established by Shari'ah (the *halal* and *tayyib* requirement). The Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13) has accreditation authority, Art. XIII, Sec. 30 provides the BOL mandate, and the Shari'ah principles of *hifz al-nafs* and *hifz al-'aql* provide the jurisprudential basis. Halal regulation is where BARMM can exercise genuine regulatory autonomy — it adds a **Shari'ah layer** to the national safety layer without conflicting with FDA standards.

**Q. Can BARMM regulate alcoholic beverages and intoxicants more strictly than national law?**

A. Yes — within constitutional limits. The BOL grants power over the regulation of "drinks," which includes alcoholic beverages. While BARMM cannot impose excise taxes (Art. XII, Sec. 9(f)), it can regulate **distribution and sale** — including zoning restrictions, age limits, and locality-based restrictions. The Shari'ah prohibition of *khamr* (intoxicants) provides the jurisprudential basis. However, a total ban would need to be assessed against constitutional equal protection and due process requirements — the constitutional framework permits regulation but may not permit absolute prohibition of a lawful commercial activity.

**Q. How does BAA 83 (Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission) interact with food regulation?**

A. BAA 83 creates a **policy-making and coordinating body** for food and nutrition — not a food safety regulator. The Nutrition Commission prescribes nutritional policies, coordinates the Bangsamoro Plan of Action for Nutrition (BPAN), and exercises powers ***"in support of health, nutrition, and food security."***[^17] Its interaction with food regulation is upstream: the Commission sets nutritional standards and guidelines that inform food regulation policy, while MOH and FDA handle the regulatory enforcement. The Commission's role is complementary — it addresses the **nutritional quality** of food, while food safety addresses the **safety** of food.
