# (gg) Inland Waters

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(gg)** — ***"Inland waters;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(gg) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national water law. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 grants ***"exclusive powers over inland waters"*** without any "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier referencing national law. However, two limitations apply: (1) Art. V, Sec. 2 chapeau subjects all 55 powers to Art. X, Sec. 15 of the Constitution[^1]; and (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 2 requires co-management where an inland water provides energy to areas outside BARMM, applying the principle under Art. XIII, Sec. 10 and Art. XII, Sec. 34. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title III, Sec. 1-12[^2] |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR); Ministry of Transportation and Communications (MTC); Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources); Chapter 7 (Economic Development — fisheries/aquatic resources); Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development — inland waterways) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | Parliament ***"shall enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection"*** of inland waters; Parliament shall ***"create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 22). Not fulfilled as of BAA 89. |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**

A. Art. V, Sec. 2(gg) enumerates ***"Inland waters"*** as one of the 55 powers of the Bangsamoro Government. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 elaborates this grant with the strongest language in the BOL: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall have exclusive powers over inland waters, including lakes, marshes, rivers, and tributaries within its territorial jurisdiction."*** The word "exclusive" appears in very few BOL power provisions — most powers are granted without this qualifier. It signals that inland waters is intended as a primary area of Bangsamoro authority, not a coordinated or supplementary one.

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Six provisions elaborate this power:

1. **Art. III, Sec. 3** — defines inland waters (***"lakes, rivers, river systems, and streams"***) as part of Bangsamoro territorial jurisdiction itself. This is a territorial provision, not merely a regulatory one.

2. **Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 1** — the central operative provision. Grants authority, mandates legislation, authorizes classification, and mandates institutional creation (see Power Overview).

3. **Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 2** — triggers co-management for inland waters that are energy sources for areas outside BARMM, applying the Art. XIII, Sec. 10 and Art. XII, Sec. 34 framework.

4. **Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 3** — mandates that utilization shall be for the ***"primary benefit of the people in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** with host community revenue sharing.

5. **Art. XIII, Sec. 8** — general natural resources provision that expressly includes ***"inland waters"*** in the enumeration of resources the Bangsamoro Government has ***"the power, authority, and right to explore, develop, and utilize."***

6. **Art. XIII, Sec. 36(b)** — specifically names **Lake Lanao** and the **Agus Hydropower Complex**, requiring cooperation through the **Intergovernmental Energy Board** (Art. VI, Sec. 7).

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 contains two express legislative mandates:

- ***"The Parliament shall enact laws on the regulation, conservation, management, and protection of these resources"*** — a comprehensive legislative mandate covering four functions (regulation, conservation, management, protection).
- ***"It shall create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water that shall exercise management and regulatory powers"*** — an institutional mandate requiring creation of body-of-water-specific authorities.

Additionally, Parliament ***"may classify inland waters in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** — a discretionary power to create a classification system.

**Neither mandate has been fulfilled as of BAA 89.**

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not create a specific body directly. Instead, it directs Parliament to create one: ***"It shall create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water."*** The word "shall" makes this mandatory, not discretionary. Until Parliament acts, MENRE exercises general oversight under its BAA 13 mandate.

The BOL also references the **Intergovernmental Energy Board** (Art. VI, Sec. 7) as the coordination mechanism for Lake Lanao/Agus Hydropower Complex, and the **Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board** (Art. VI, Sec. 8; created by BAA 31) for policy coordination.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Four limitations:

1. **Energy exception** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22 qualifies "exclusive powers" with ***"except those that provide energy to power generating plants."*** Where an inland water provides energy, authority shifts to the co-management regime.

2. **Co-management for extra-regional energy** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 2 applies the co-management principle (Art. XIII, Sec. 10 and Art. XII, Sec. 34) when an inland water is ***"a source of energy for areas outside of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***

3. **PCG coordination for navigation** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 4 requires the Bangsamoro Government to ***"coordinate and cooperate with the Philippine Coast Guard concerning inland waterways for navigation."***

4. **Constitutional constraint** — Art. V, Sec. 2 chapeau subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** (now correctly cited as Art. X, Sec. 15 per the constitutional framework[^1]). Art. XIII, Sec. 8 also subjects natural resources authority to ***"the provisions of the Constitution."***

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**

A. The following constitutional provisions establish the framework within which the Bangsamoro Government exercises its inland waters power:

- **Universal constraint (Art. X, Sec. 15)** — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."***[^1] This applies to all 55 BOL powers.

- **Regalian Doctrine (Art. XII, Sec. 2)** — ***"All lands of the public domain, waters, minerals, coal, petroleum, and other mineral oils, all forces of potential energy, fisheries, forests or timber, wildlife, flora and fauna, and other natural resources are owned by the State."*** This is the foundational constitutional principle: all waters — including inland waters — are State-owned. The BOL's "exclusive powers" grant does not transfer ownership but delegates management and regulatory authority. The Bangsamoro Government exercises authority over inland waters on behalf of the State.

- **Water rights framework (Art. XII, Sec. 2, para. 1)** — ***"In cases of water rights for irrigation, water supply, fisheries, or industrial uses other than the development of water power, beneficial use may be the measure and limit of the grant."*** This establishes the "beneficial use" standard for water rights, which the Bangsamoro Government must respect in its regulatory framework.

- **Small-scale utilization (Art. XII, Sec. 2, para. 3)** — ***"The Congress may, by law, allow small-scale utilization of natural resources by Filipino citizens, as well as cooperative fish farming, with priority to subsistence fishermen and fishworkers in rivers, lakes, bays, and lagoons."*** This provision specifically mentions rivers and lakes — directly relevant to inland waters.

- **Balanced ecology (Art. II, Sec. 16)** — ***"The State shall protect and advance the right of the people to a balanced and healthful ecology in accord with the rhythm and harmony of nature."*** This is a state policy that applies to inland water management — the Bangsamoro Government must manage inland waters in a manner that maintains ecological balance.

- **LGU equitable share (Art. X, Sec. 7)** — ***"Local governments shall be entitled to an equitable share in the proceeds of the utilization and development of the national wealth within their respective areas."*** This establishes that local government units within BARMM are entitled to shares from inland water resource utilization — reinforcing Art. XIII, Sec. 22's "host community" revenue-sharing requirement.

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. Three constitutional guarantees are relevant:

1. **Right to a balanced ecology** — Art. II, Sec. 16 recognizes the people's right to a healthful ecology. Inland water management must ensure that water quality, biodiversity, and ecological functions are maintained.

2. **Beneficial use standard for water rights** — Art. XII, Sec. 2 establishes that beneficial use is the measure and limit of water rights grants. The Bangsamoro Government cannot grant unlimited water extraction — it must ensure water rights are proportional to beneficial use.

3. **Subsistence fishers' priority** — Art. XII, Sec. 2, para. 3 gives subsistence fishermen and fishworkers priority access to rivers, lakes, bays, and lagoons. A Bangsamoro inland waters code must protect subsistence fisherfolk in Lake Lanao, Liguasan Marsh, and other inland fisheries.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 2 mandates that natural resource exploration, development, and utilization be ***"under the full control and supervision of the State."*** This means the Bangsamoro Government, as a subdivision of the State, must exercise its inland waters authority through a regulatory framework — not in an unregulated manner. The absence of a standalone BAA leaves a constitutional gap: the State's duty of control and supervision over inland waters within BARMM remains inadequately discharged.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**

A. The national framework provides a comprehensive water governance system:

- **PD 1067 (Water Code of the Philippines, 1976)** — the foundational national law. Declares all waters as belonging to the State. Establishes a **water permit system** for appropriation and utilization administered by the NWRB. Defines water rights, priorities of use (domestic > municipal > irrigation > power > fisheries > industrial > other), and dispute resolution mechanisms.

- **RA 9275 (Philippine Clean Water Act, 2004)** — establishes the water quality management framework. Creates **Water Quality Management Areas (WQMAs)**, sets water quality standards for all water bodies, requires water quality monitoring, and imposes penalties for water pollution.

- **RA 7586 (NIPAS Act, 1992)** and **RA 11038 (Expanded NIPAS Act, 2018)** — govern protected areas including watershed reservations and aquatic parks that encompass inland water bodies.

- **RA 8550 (Philippine Fisheries Code, 1998)** as amended by **RA 10654 (2014)** — governs fisheries in all waters including inland fisheries.

- **RA 3601 (NIA Charter, 1963)** — governs irrigation systems that depend on inland water sources.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All of the above national laws apply **suppletorily** in BARMM — meaning they govern inland water management until a BAA is enacted to supersede them. Key applications:

- **PD 1067** — water permits for all significant water appropriation in BARMM are still processed through the **NWRB** or its regional counterpart. No BAA transfers this function to MENRE or a Bangsamoro Water Authority.

- **RA 9275** — water quality monitoring and enforcement remain under the national DENR framework, with MENRE's Water Compliance Monitoring Division operating in parallel but without the standalone legislative authority that a Bangsamoro inland waters code would provide.

- **RA 7586/RA 11038** — protected area management is in transition under Art. XIII, Sec. 9 of the BOL, which mandates transfer from DENR to the Bangsamoro Government.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. Three national agencies exercise authority over inland waters in BARMM:

1. **National Water Resources Board (NWRB)** — retains water permit and allocation authority. Issues water rights permits for irrigation, domestic, industrial, and other uses within BARMM.

2. **Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR)** — retains environmental monitoring, watershed management, and protected area management functions. Transfer to BARMM is ongoing under Art. XIII, Sec. 9.

3. **National Irrigation Administration (NIA)** — operates and maintains irrigation systems that draw from BARMM inland water sources. Art. XIII, Sec. 37 provides that nationally-funded irrigation systems remain the responsibility of the National Government.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE)** is the primary responsible ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III establishes MENRE with the mandate (Sec. 2): ***"The Ministry shall be primarily responsible for the exploration, utilization, management, conservation, protection and sustainable development of the region's environment, natural resources and energy sources."*** BAA 85, Chapter XIII allocates MENRE's operational budget.

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **None.** No standalone inland waters code, Bangsamoro Water Authority act, or body-of-water-specific legislation has been enacted as of BAA 89. The Art. XIII, Sec. 22 legislative and institutional mandates remain unfulfilled.

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III, Sec. 1-12 establishes MENRE. The key provisions:

**Declaration of Policy (Sec. 1)** — includes exercising ***"power, authority and right to explore, develop and utilize the natural resources, including surface and sub-surface rights, inland waters, coastal waters, mines and minerals and renewable and non-renewable resources in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***

**Powers and Functions (Sec. 3)** — enumerates 19 functions (a-s) including:
- (a) Recommend enactment of regional laws for natural resources and pollution control
- (b) Formulate, supervise, and implement policies for environment, natural resources, and energy
- (c) Promulgate rules and regulations for exploration, development, conservation
- (d) Exercise supervision over forest lands, agricultural lands, mineral resources, national parks
- (e) Recommend declaration of nature reserves, marine parks, forests, watershed reservations
- (f) Undertake exploration, assessment, classification, and inventory of natural resources
- (h) Undertake geological surveys including territorial waters
- (m) **Promulgate rules and regulations for the control of water, air and land pollution**

**Organizational Structure (Sec. 9)** — MENRE has 11 services/divisions, two directly relevant to inland waters:
- **(9) Environment Management Services** — includes the **Water Compliance Monitoring Division**, Solid Waste Management Division, Environmental Impact Assessment Division, and Air Compliance Monitoring Division
- **(10) Biodiversity Ecosystems Research and Development Services** — includes Protected Area Management Division, relevant for inland water ecosystems within protected areas

**Field Offices (Sec. 10-11)** — Provincial Environment, Natural Resources and Energy Offices (PENREOs) and Community offices (CENREOs) serve as frontline implementors.

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 provides MENRE with general mandate and organizational structure, but it **cannot**:

1. **Issue water permits or allocate water rights** — PD 1067's water permit system remains under the NWRB. A Bangsamoro inland waters code is needed to transfer or establish this function within MENRE or a new Bangsamoro Water Authority.

2. **Classify inland water bodies** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22 grants Parliament the power to classify inland waters. Without legislation, no classification system exists to differentiate water bodies by use, protection status, or management regime.

3. **Create body-of-water-specific authorities** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates Parliament to create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water. MENRE cannot create these through administrative issuance alone.

4. **Establish water quality standards specific to BARMM** — while the Water Compliance Monitoring Division monitors water quality, the standards themselves remain those of the national framework (RA 9275). A standalone BAA could establish BARMM-specific standards.

5. **Impose penalties for water pollution or illegal extraction** — BAA 13, Sec. 6(f) expressly limits MENRE's power: administrative issuances ***"shall not prescribe penalties for their violation, except when expressly authorized by law."*** Without a standalone BAA, MENRE has no penal authority for inland water offenses.

6. **Establish a revenue framework** for water utilization — the host community revenue-sharing provision in Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 3 cannot be implemented without legislation specifying rates, beneficiaries, and distribution mechanisms.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes, three resolutions signal intent:
- **Res. No. 122** — asserts BARMM rights over Lake Lanao
- **Res. No. 251** — urges feasibility study on inland waterway transport
- **Res. No. 509** — monitors the Mindanao River Basin Master Plan

Additionally, three pending PBs (352, 310, 278) directly address inland water management for specific water bodies and river basins.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. MENRE is the correct lead ministry for the **environmental and natural resources dimension** of inland waters. However, the full scope of the (gg) inland waters power exceeds MENRE's BAA 13 mandate:

- **Navigation on inland waterways** — falls under MTC, whose BAA 13 mandate (Book VI, Title XV) includes ***"Manage navigation in inland waterways within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** (Sec. 3(e)). This overlaps with the separate (hh) power.
- **Inland fisheries** — falls under MAFAR, whose BAA 13 mandate includes fisheries management and conservation of municipal waters.
- **Irrigation** — involves coordination with the National Government through NIA (Art. XIII, Sec. 37).
- **Energy (hydropower)** — MENRE's Energy Management and Development Services handles energy, but the Agus Hydropower Complex is specifically governed by the Intergovernmental Energy Board (Art. VI, Sec. 7).

The Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandate to create a **Bangsamoro Authority** for inland waters suggests the BOL envisioned a **dedicated institution** — not merely MENRE exercising this power through its existing structure. The Bangsamoro Authority would be the primary coordinator, with MENRE, MAFAR, MTC, and others as supporting agencies.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Current institutional landscape:

- **Lead ministry**: MENRE (BAA 13, Book VI, Title III) — general environmental and natural resources authority, including water pollution control
- **Supporting agencies**:
  - MAFAR — inland fisheries management
  - MTC — inland waterway navigation
  - BPDA — development planning integration
- **Special body**: Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31) — policy coordination for sustainable development
- **LGU-level**: Provincial Environment, Natural Resources and Energy Offices (PENREOs) and CENREOs under MENRE field offices

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates: ***"It shall create a Bangsamoro Authority and offices for specific inland bodies of water that shall exercise management and regulatory powers over these bodies of water."*** This has not been created. The BOL envisions:

1. A **Bangsamoro Authority** — a central coordinating body for all inland waters
2. **Offices for specific inland bodies of water** — body-of-water-specific management entities (e.g., a Lake Lanao Authority, a Liguasan Marsh Management Council, a Rio Grande de Mindanao River Basin Authority)

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Yes. Beyond the BOL-mandated Bangsamoro Authority, the full exercise of this power likely requires:

1. A **Bangsamoro Water Regulatory Office** — to perform the water permit and allocation functions currently exercised by the NWRB, issuing water rights within BARMM
2. A **Water Quality Management unit** — to establish, monitor, and enforce BARMM-specific water quality standards (currently MENRE's Water Compliance Monitoring Division, but without standalone legislative authority)
3. An **interagency coordination body** — to manage the overlapping jurisdiction between MENRE (environment), MAFAR (fisheries), MTC (navigation), and LGUs for each major water body

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. Three national agencies operate in BARMM for inland waters:

1. **NWRB** — processes water appropriation permits for significant water extraction in BARMM (irrigation, industrial, commercial uses). All water permits within BARMM still go through the national system.

2. **DENR** — manages watersheds, protected areas, and environmental monitoring. The transfer of protected area management to the Bangsamoro Government under Art. XIII, Sec. 9 is ongoing but not yet complete.

3. **NIA** — operates and maintains irrigation systems that draw from BARMM rivers. Art. XIII, Sec. 37 of the BOL preserves the National Government's funding responsibility for irrigation.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. Devolution is **incomplete**:

- **DENR to MENRE** — the transfer of environmental management functions is partially underway under Art. XIII, Sec. 9. MENRE has operational capacity through PENREOs and CENREOs, but the regulatory authority (especially for water permits) has not been formally transferred.
- **NWRB** — no transfer of water permit authority has occurred. This is a critical gap — without water permit authority, the Bangsamoro Government's "exclusive powers" over inland waters are significantly limited in practice.
- **NIA** — irrigation remains a national function per Art. XIII, Sec. 37. This is by BOL design, not a devolution gap.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes, in three key areas:

1. **Water quality monitoring** — both DENR and MENRE's Water Compliance Monitoring Division operate, with unclear boundaries on enforcement authority.
2. **Watershed management** — DENR retains authority over nationally-declared watersheds; MENRE has the general mandate for environmental management. The boundary between national and Bangsamoro watersheds is not legislatively defined.
3. **Fisheries in inland waters** — both the national BFAR and BARMM's MAFAR exercise jurisdiction over fisheries in rivers and lakes, with the BOL's consistency requirement under Art. XIII, Sec. 23 (RA 8550) applying to the (y) fishery power but not directly to the (gg) inland waters power.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. Accomplishments are limited to institutional establishment and initial policy signals:

1. **MENRE operational** — the ministry has been established with field offices (PENREOs and CENREOs) across BARMM provinces
2. **Water Compliance Monitoring Division** — established within MENRE to monitor water quality
3. **Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board** — created by BAA 31, providing a policy coordination mechanism
4. **Parliamentary Resolutions** — Res. Nos. 122, 251, and 509 signal awareness of inland water issues, particularly Lake Lanao rights
5. **Three pending bills** — PBs 352, 310, and 278 demonstrate active legislative interest in body-of-water-specific legislation

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM's strengths are constitutional and contextual rather than operational:

- **BOL "exclusive powers" grant** — no other autonomous region or LGU in the Philippines has a comparable constitutional grant of authority over inland waters. This provides the legal foundation for a comprehensive, locally-responsive framework.
- **Shari'ah integration** — a Bangsamoro inland waters code can integrate Islamic water law principles (communal access, stewardship, prohibition of wasteful use) — a dimension the national framework entirely lacks.
- **Conflict-sensitive approach** — BARMM can design water management policies that account for post-conflict realities — land disputes, IDP communities, and contested water access — which the national one-size-fits-all framework does not address.
- **Lake Lanao and Liguasan Marsh** — BARMM has geographic proximity and community-level knowledge of its most significant water bodies that national agencies lack.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a standalone inland waters code. Without it:

- MENRE cannot issue water permits (NWRB retains this authority)
- No Bangsamoro Authority or body-of-water-specific offices exist
- No classification system for BARMM inland waters exists
- No penalty framework for inland water offenses exists
- Host community revenue sharing under Art. XIII, Sec. 22 cannot be operationalized
- The "exclusive powers" grant in the BOL remains largely theoretical

The removal of this constraint requires enactment of a **Bangsamoro Inland Waters Code** — the single legislative action that would most transform this power from a BOL promise to an operational reality.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national system has decades of accumulated infrastructure:

- **NWRB** — established in 1974 (PD 424, reconstituted by EO 123/1987), with a functioning water permit system covering all water appropriation
- **PD 1067 (Water Code)** — 50 years of implementation, with established IRRs, administrative rulings, and enforcement mechanisms
- **RA 9275 (Clean Water Act)** — water quality standards, WQMA designations, effluent limits, and a comprehensive monitoring system
- **River Basin organizations** — including the Cotabato-Agusan River Basin Program Office (EO 375), demonstrating integrated river basin management
- **NIA** — nationwide irrigation system with engineering capacity, feasibility studies, and maintenance protocols

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM in several critical ways:

1. **No Shari'ah dimension** — the national water framework is entirely secular. It does not incorporate Islamic principles of communal water access, stewardship, or prohibition of wastefulness.
2. **No conflict-sensitive design** — PD 1067 and RA 9275 were not designed for post-conflict contexts where water access has been disrupted by displacement, armed conflict, and contested land claims.
3. **No recognition of "exclusive powers"** — the national framework treats BARMM like any other region, despite the BOL's "exclusive powers" grant. NWRB processes water permits without differentiation.
4. **Understaffed in BARMM** — national agencies (NWRB, DENR, NIA) have historically been underrepresented in BARMM, with fewer staff, less infrastructure, and less data compared to other regions.
5. **Lake Lanao governance gap** — the national system has no mechanism that gives Bangsamoro communities meaningful governance participation over Lake Lanao, despite it being within Bangsamoro territorial jurisdiction.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

- **Water permit system (PD 1067)** — **Adapt**: establish a Bangsamoro water permit system administered by the Bangsamoro Authority (or MENRE), using the national framework as a model but adding Shari'ah-compliant provisions, conflict-sensitive allocation criteria, and host community consultation requirements.
- **Water quality standards (RA 9275)** — **Adapt**: adopt the national standards as a baseline but establish BARMM-specific standards for water bodies with unique characteristics (e.g., Lake Lanao's oligotrophic ecology, Liguasan Marsh's wetland ecosystem).
- **River basin management (EO 510, EO 375)** — **Replicate and localize**: establish a Bangsamoro River Basin Management Council (per PB 278) modeled on national river basin organizations but under Bangsamoro authority.
- **Protected area management (NIPAS)** — **Replace**: once the Art. XIII, Sec. 9 transfer is complete, manage inland water protected areas through a Bangsamoro framework integrated with the inland waters code.
- **Irrigation (NIA)** — **Coordinate, do not replace**: Art. XIII, Sec. 37 preserves the National Government's role in irrigation funding. BARMM should coordinate with NIA rather than establish a parallel system.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Water holds a special status in Islamic law as a communal resource essential to life. The Prophet Muhammad (PBUH) stated that Muslims share in three things: water, pasture, and fire. This makes water governance a subject where Shari'ah principles are directly applicable — not merely aspirational.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not explicitly reference Shari'ah in Art. XIII, Sec. 22 (inland waters). However, the general Shari'ah framework applies:

- Art. X, Sec. 4 empowers Parliament to enact ***"laws on personal, family, and property law jurisdiction"*** — which includes water rights under Islamic jurisprudence as property matters — which includes water rights under Islamic jurisprudence.
- The BOL's "primary benefit" clause in Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 3 aligns with the Islamic principle of water as a communal resource managed for the benefit of the community.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Five principles directly apply:

1. ***Maslahah*** (public welfare) — water regulation serves the common good
2. ***Amanah*** (stewardship) — the government manages water resources as a trust
3. **Communal water rights** (*shirkah*) — water is a shared resource; access to drinking water is a fundamental right
4. **Prohibition of *israf*** (wastefulness) — conservation is an obligation
5. **Prohibition of *darar*** (harm) — pollution and upstream harm to downstream communities is prohibited

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. A **principled** approach is most appropriate: legislation guided by Islamic values of stewardship, communal access, and conservation — without mandating specific compliance mechanisms. Specifically:

- Guarantee **public access** to inland waters for domestic use — no privatization of essential water sources
- Require **community consultation** before granting water extraction permits — reflecting *shura*
- Incorporate **conservation requirements** in all water permits — reflecting the prohibition of *israf*
- Establish **environmental liability** for pollution — grounded in the prohibition of *darar*
- Ensure **host community benefit sharing** — reflecting the communal nature of water resources

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. The inland waters power has a **strong basis for divergence**:

1. The BOL uses the word ***"exclusive"*** — a qualifier not found in most other power grants. This is among the strongest language in the BOL.

2. There is **no national law consistency requirement**. Unlike the (y) fishery power (which must be consistent with RA 8550), or the (i) civil service power (which must accord with national civil service laws), Art. XIII, Sec. 22 imposes no "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier referencing any national water law.

3. The **constitutional floor** established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B) (Regalian Doctrine, beneficial use standard, ecological balance) constrains BARMM — but these are broad principles, not prescriptive standards. BARMM has substantial latitude to design its own inland water governance framework as long as it respects State ownership of waters, applies the beneficial use standard, and maintains ecological balance.

4. The **Shari'ah dimension** from the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) provides an additional unique basis for divergence — Islamic water law principles (communal access, stewardship, prohibition of wastefulness and harm) offer a distinctive jurisprudential foundation that the national framework lacks entirely.

The main limitation on divergence is the **co-management requirement** for energy-producing waters (Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 2) and the **PCG coordination** requirement for navigation (Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 4). For all other inland water functions, BARMM's legislative authority is not subject to express national law consistency requirements.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. Three cross-boundary considerations:

1. **River systems crossing BARMM boundaries** — the Rio Grande de Mindanao (Pulangi River) flows through both BARMM and non-BARMM areas. BARMM's inland water regulations could differ from national standards for the same river system. Upstream-downstream coordination is essential — a Bangsamoro inland waters code should include provisions for **intergovernmental coordination** on shared river systems.

2. **Water rights holders** — entities holding existing NWRB water permits within BARMM would need a transition mechanism. A Bangsamoro inland waters code should provide for recognition of existing permits and a process for re-registration with the Bangsamoro Authority.

3. **Lake Lanao energy co-management** — the co-management regime means that BARMM standards for Lake Lanao must be jointly developed with the National Government through the Intergovernmental Energy Board. BARMM cannot unilaterally change conditions for the Agus Hydropower Complex.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. When a Bangsamoro Inland Waters Code is enacted:

- The BAA governs inland water management within BARMM — PD 1067, RA 9275, and other national water laws cease to apply as primary authority for matters covered by the BAA.
- National laws apply **suppletorily** where the BAA is silent — filling gaps until the BAA is amended to address them.
- **Constitutional provisions always apply** — the Regalian Doctrine, beneficial use standard, and ecological balance requirements are constitutional and cannot be superseded by any legislation.
- The **co-management regime** for energy-producing waters operates independently of the BAA — it is a BOL-level framework that requires intergovernmental agreement.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes, three critical overlaps:

1. **Water permit authority** — the NWRB continues to issue water permits in BARMM because no BAA transfers this function. This is the single most significant jurisdictional gap — the BOL grants "exclusive powers" but the operational authority remains with a national agency.

2. **Watershed management** — DENR and MENRE both claim authority over watersheds within BARMM. The Art. XIII, Sec. 9 transfer process for protected areas (which include key watersheds) is ongoing but unclear on timelines and scope.

3. **Rio Grande de Mindanao** — this river system is partly within BARMM and partly outside. No intergovernmental framework exists to coordinate inland water management for the portions of the river within and outside BARMM.

A Bangsamoro Inland Waters Code would resolve the first two overlaps; an intergovernmental agreement (possibly facilitated through the Congress-Parliament Forum) would resolve the third.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Three pending PBs address specific gaps:

- **PB 352 (Lake Lanao and watershed management)** — would address the most significant single water body. Even if enacted, it does not create the comprehensive framework Art. XIII, Sec. 22 mandates — it is body-of-water-specific, not a general inland waters code.

- **PB 310 (Liguasan Marsh Management Council)** — would create a body-of-water-specific authority for the largest marshland in BARMM. Like PB 352, it is specific rather than comprehensive.

- **PB 278 (Bangsamoro River Basin Management Council)** — would create an interagency coordination body for river systems. This addresses one institutional gap but not the overall regulatory framework.

Even if all three pass, significant gaps remain: no general inland waters code, no water permit system, no classification framework, no penalty provisions, and no revenue-sharing mechanism.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the significance of the BOL's use of "exclusive powers" for inland waters?**

A. The word "exclusive" in Art. XIII, Sec. 22 is legally significant because it is used sparingly in the BOL. Most power grants in Art. V, Sec. 2 are simply enumerated without qualifiers. The use of "exclusive" signals the strongest possible grant of authority short of sovereignty itself. It means the Bangsamoro Government is the **primary and preferred** authority for inland water management — not co-exercising with the National Government (except for the energy exception). When Parliament enacts an inland waters code, the "exclusive" qualifier provides the legal basis for superseding national water law within BARMM rather than merely supplementing it.

**Q. How does the energy exception in Art. XIII, Sec. 22 work in practice?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 22 carves out ***"those that provide energy to power generating plants"*** from the "exclusive powers" grant. Two scenarios:

1. **Inland water provides energy only within BARMM** — the Bangsamoro Government retains authority but the water body is not subject to the "exclusive" qualifier for its energy function. MENRE's Energy Management and Development Services handles the energy dimension.

2. **Inland water provides energy to areas outside BARMM** — Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 2 triggers co-management. The principle under Art. XIII, Sec. 10 (fossil fuels co-management) and Art. XII, Sec. 34 (equal revenue sharing) applies. The primary example is **Lake Lanao**, which feeds the **Agus Hydropower Complex** supplying electricity to Mindanao including areas outside BARMM. For Lake Lanao, the Intergovernmental Energy Board (Art. VI, Sec. 7) is the coordination mechanism, and the Bangsamoro Government has preferential privatization rights under Art. XIII, Sec. 36(b).

The energy exception does **not** remove Lake Lanao from Bangsamoro territorial jurisdiction — it remains part of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region (Art. III, Sec. 3). The exception only shifts the governance regime from "exclusive" to "co-managed" for the energy function. Non-energy functions (fisheries, water supply, environmental management, navigation) remain under Bangsamoro authority.

**Q. What are the major inland water bodies in BARMM and what governance challenges does each present?**

A. Four major categories:

1. **Lake Lanao** (Lanao del Sur) — the Philippines' second-largest lake and the largest in Mindanao [UNVERIFIED — geographic ranking from secondary sources]. Source of the Agus River and the Agus Hydropower Complex (6 plants generating approximately 900 MW). [UNVERIFIED — capacity figure from secondary sources.] Governance challenges: declining water levels, pollution, loss of endemic species, co-management with the National Government, revenue sharing from hydropower, and competing uses (fisheries, water supply, cultural significance to the Meranaw people).

2. **Liguasan Marsh** (Maguindanao del Sur and Norte) — the Philippines' largest freshwater marsh. Governance challenges: seasonal flooding affecting up to 220,000 hectares [UNVERIFIED], habitat loss from drainage and conversion, water quality degradation, and the need for a management authority (PB 310 proposes the LMMC).

3. **Rio Grande de Mindanao (Pulangi River)** system — one of the longest river systems in Mindanao, flowing through Maguindanao and eventually to Cotabato. Governance challenges: crosses BARMM/non-BARMM boundaries requiring intergovernmental coordination, flooding, siltation, and pollution from upstream mining and agriculture.

4. **Other rivers and streams** — numerous smaller rivers, tributaries, and streams across the five BARMM provinces. Governance challenges: unregulated extraction, agricultural runoff, deforestation of riparian zones, and lack of monitoring capacity.

**Q. What is the relationship between (gg) inland waters and (hh) inland waterways for navigation?**

A. Art. V, Sec. 2 lists these as separate enumerated powers: (gg) ***"Inland waters"*** and (hh) ***"Inland waterways for navigation."*** The distinction is between:

- **(gg) Inland waters** — the water bodies themselves: lakes, marshes, rivers, tributaries, streams. Encompasses environmental management, water quality, conservation, resource utilization, and the general governance framework. Lead: MENRE.

- **(hh) Inland waterways for navigation** — the use of inland water bodies as transportation routes. Encompasses vessel regulation, navigation safety, port facilities on inland waters, and waterway maintenance for transport. Lead: MTC, with PCG coordination (Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 4).

The two powers overlap but are managed by different lead agencies. A Bangsamoro inland waters code should address both dimensions while clearly delineating MENRE's environmental authority from MTC's transportation authority.

**Q. What is the role of LGUs in inland water management?**

A. BAA 49 (Local Governance Code) establishes the framework for LGU powers within BARMM. LGUs play a critical role in inland water management:

- **Municipal governments** — frontline management of rivers, streams, and lake shorelines within their jurisdiction. RA 7160 (Local Government Code) assigns environmental management functions to LGUs, and BAA 49 adapts these for BARMM.
- **Provincial governments** — coordination across municipalities for river systems and lake management.
- **Barangay governments** — community-level monitoring and enforcement.

Art. XIII, Sec. 22, para. 3's requirement that ***"host communities"*** receive their share from water utilization revenues implies a significant role for LGUs as revenue beneficiaries and community-level implementors. A Bangsamoro inland waters code must define the LGU role explicitly.
