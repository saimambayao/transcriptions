# (f) Budgeting

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(f)** —***"Budgeting;"***

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. ### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over budgeting?**

A. Article V, Section 2(f) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Budgeting"*** as one of the 55 powers. This power is elaborated across **the entire Article XII** (Fiscal Autonomy —41 sections) plus Art. VII, Sec. 27-29. The grant encompasses **budget preparation**, **revenue mobilization**, **appropriation**, **allocation**, **execution**, and **reporting**. Art. XII, Sec. 1 states: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall enjoy fiscal autonomy with the end in view of attaining economic self-sufficiency and genuine development."*** This is the broadest fiscal authority granted to any autonomous region in Philippine history.[^1][^2]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. The budgeting power is the most elaborately detailed power in the BOL. Key provision clusters:

   (1) **Fiscal autonomy framework** (Art. XII, Sec. 1-5): fiscal autonomy mandate, COA as exclusive auditor, local government finance office, Bangsamoro Treasury Office, national assistance in tax administration.[^2][^3]

   (2) **Revenue sources** (Art. XII, Sec. 6-14): 12 categories of revenue, taxation principles, tax incentives, taxing power limitations, sharing of national taxes, revenue office establishment, corporate tax allocation, LGU shares, and the Revenue Code mandate.[^4]

   (3) **Block grant** (Art. XII, Sec. 15-23): annual block grant formula (5% of net NIRTA + Customs), automatic appropriation, unconditional release, allocation rules (priority to education/health/social services, DBM/DILG rules apply), 45% PS cap, general limitations, block grant deductions after 20 years, formula review every 5 years, development programs.[^5][^6][^7]

   (4) **Budget process** (Art. VII, Sec. 27-29): appropriations law requirement, budget form/content per Parliament-enacted law (consistent with national rules), reenacted budget provision.[^8][^9]

   (5) **Intergovernmental fiscal mechanism** (Art. VI, Sec. 4; Art. XII, Sec. 37-41): IFPB creation, functions, composition, annual reporting, full disclosure.[^10]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. Three express mandates:

   (1) Art. VII, Sec. 28: Parliament shall enact a law prescribing ***"the form, content, and manner of preparation of the Bangsamoro budget."*** **Fulfilled** by BAA 84 (Budget System Act).[^9]

   (2) Art. XII, Sec. 14: ***"The Parliament shall enact a Bangsamoro tax and revenue code."*** This is one of the 7 **priority codes** under Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a). **Not yet fulfilled** as of BAA 89.[^11]

   (3) Art. XII, Sec. 11: Parliament shall establish by law the **Bangsamoro Revenue Office** for tax assessment and collection. **Fulfilled** through BAA 13, Book V, Title IV, Ch. 7.[^12]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL directly creates:

   (1) **Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board (IFPB)** (Art. VI, Sec. 4) —addresses revenue imbalances and fiscal policy coordination between national and Bangsamoro governments.[^10]

   (2) **Bangsamoro Treasury Office** (Art. XII, Sec. 4) —receives and safeguards all revenues; headed by a Regional Treasurer appointed by the Chief Minister, whose term ***"shall be determined by a law to be passed by the Parliament."***[^3]

   (3) **Bangsamoro Regional Office of the Bureau of Local Government Finance** (Art. XII, Sec. 3) —under the Department of Finance, coordinating LGU fiscal operations.[^3]

   BAA 13 further establishes the **Bangsamoro Budget Office**, **Bangsamoro Finance Office**, and **Bangsamoro Revenue Office** within MFBM.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Among the most constrained of all 55 powers. Seven major limitations:

   (1) **National law consistency**: Art. VII, Sec. 28 —budget system ***"consistent with existing laws, rules and regulations of the National Government."***[^9]

   (2) **DBM/DILG rules apply**: Art. XII, Sec. 19 —block grant allocation follows ***"the national laws and the budgeting rules and regulations implemented by the Department of Budget and Management and Department of the Interior and Local Government applicable to local government units."***[^6]

   (3) **COA as exclusive auditor**: Art. XII, Sec. 2 —constitutional constraint.[^3]

   (4) **45% PS cap**: Art. XII, Sec. 20 —personal services cannot exceed 45% of total revenue sources.[^7]

   (5) **Education/health/social services priority**: Art. XII, Sec. 19 —Parliament shall assign ***"the highest budgetary priority to education, health, and social services."***[^6]

   (6) **No firearms from block grant**: Art. XII, Sec. 19 —***"The Parliament shall not include the procurement of firearms, ammunition, armaments, and explosives in its annual appropriations law from the block grant."***[^6]

   (7) **Salary ceiling**: Art. XII, Sec. 20(b) —***"No official or employee shall be entitled to a salary rate higher than the maximum fixed for the position."***[^7]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to budgeting?**

A. Four categories:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: Budgeting operates ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***[^13]

   (2) **Commission on Audit (Art. IX-D)**: COA has constitutional authority as ***"the exclusive auditor"*** of all government entities, including the Bangsamoro Government. This cannot be superseded by any BAA.[^14]

   (3) **Public Financial Management (Art. VI, Sec. 25)**: ***"All money collected on any tax levied for a special purpose shall be treated as a special fund and paid out for such purpose only."*** This constitutional earmarking principle applies to BARMM special purpose funds.[^15]

   (4) **No-Increase Rule (Art. VI, Sec. 25(1))**: Congress may not increase appropriations recommended by the President. While this specifically governs the national GAA process, the principle of executive budget recommendation and legislative appropriation is reflected in the Bangsamoro's own budget process.

**Q. What constitutional mandates for budgeting institutions exist?**

A. **COA** (Art. IX-D) is the constitutionally mandated auditing institution with exclusive authority over all government accounts. No Bangsamoro legislation can diminish COA's authority or create a parallel external audit body.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**

A. The national framework provides: **RA 992** (national budget system); **RA 9184** (procurement reform); **RA 6758** (compensation classification); **RA 7160** (LGU budgeting framework); **RA 3456** (internal auditing). DBM circulars, budget call documents, and COA rules supplement the statutory framework.[^16]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All apply:

   (1) **RA 9184** —directly applicable for procurement.[^16]
   (2) **RA 6758** —directly applicable for compensation.[^16]
   (3) **RA 7160** —directly applicable for LGU fiscal matters.[^16]
   (4) **RA 3456** —directly applicable for internal auditing per Art. XII, Sec. 2.[^3]
   (5) **DBM circulars and budget rules** —directly applicable per Art. XII, Sec. 19.[^6]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. **COA** audits all Bangsamoro Government entities (constitutional authority). **DBM** sets budgeting rules and regulations that apply to the block grant (Art. XII, Sec. 19). **DILG** standards apply to block grant allocation for LGUs. **Bureau of the Treasury** processes block grant releases. **Bureau of Local Government Finance** coordinates LGU fiscal operations through the BARMM regional office (Art. XII, Sec. 3). The **IFPB** serves as the intergovernmental coordination mechanism. These institutions **retain permanent authority** —the BOL does not contemplate their replacement.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management (MFBM)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book V, Title IV (Sec. 1-20) defines its mandate, powers, functions, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter III allocates its operational budget.[^17]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **19 BAAs** —the most of any power: **BAA 84** (Budget System Act); **7 annual GAAs** (BAA 3, 15, 23, 32, 56, 65, 85); **2 supplemental appropriations** (BAA 63, 75); **1 SDF act** (BAA 22); **6 extension acts** (BAA 14, 24, 33, 34, 38, 51, 52); **1 revolving fund** (BAA 36). Additionally, BAA 13 establishes MFBM and its component offices.

**Q. What are MFBM's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book V, Title IV defines MFBM comprehensively:

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): ***"The Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management is primarily mandated to evolve sound, efficient and responsive fiscal management and utilization of resources. It shall be responsible for the formulation, institutionalization and administration of fiscal policies."***[^17]

   **Powers and functions** (Sec. 3) —15 enumerated functions:
   - (a) ***"Formulate medium-term expenditure and annual plans covering the Bangsamoro Government's resources mobilization efforts"***;
   - (b) ***"Undertake the formulation of the annual budget"***;
   - (c) ***"Formulate, institutionalize and administer fiscal and tax policies"***;
   - (d) ***"Supervise, direct, manage, and control the collection of Bangsamoro Government revenues"***;
   - (e) ***"Act as custodian of, and manage all financial resources"***;
   - (f) ***"Manage public debt"***;
   - (g) Review and coordinate policies of financial institutions;
   - (h) Review and coordinate policies of GOCCs;
   - (i) ***"Ensure the implementation of policies and measures on local revenue administration"***;
   - (j) Coordinate on fiscal/monetary policies, credit, international finance, trade;
   - (k) ***"Develop and administer standard accounting system"***;
   - (l) ***"Conduct a continuing study of the bureaucracy and assess as well as make policy recommendation on its role, size, composition, structure and functions"***;
   - (m) ***"Establish rules and procedures for the management of government organization resources"***;
   - (n) ***"Conceptualize and administer the Bangsamoro Government's compensation and position classification consistent with national standards"***;
   - (o) Other functions assigned by the Minister.[^18]

   **Component offices**:
   - **Bangsamoro Treasury Office** (Ch. 4, Sec. 10-14) —receives/safeguards revenues, custodian of financial assets, investment strategy, cash flow analysis;
   - **Bangsamoro Finance Office** (Ch. 5, Sec. 15-16) —fiscal/tax policy formulation, revenue collection supervision, GOCC coordination, expenditure planning;
   - **Bangsamoro Budget Office** (Ch. 6, Sec. 17-18) —annual budget formulation, accounting system, bureaucracy assessment, organization/systems improvement, compensation/position classification;
   - **Bangsamoro Revenue Office** (Ch. 7, Sec. 19-20) —tax assessment/collection, preventive measures for tax evasion, field unit supervision.[^19][^20][^21][^22]

**Q. What can MFBM NOT do without additional legislation?**

A. BAA 13 provides the institutional framework and BAA 84 provides the budget system. What still requires legislation: (a) the **Bangsamoro Revenue Code** (Art. XII, Sec. 14; Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)) —the most critical unfulfilled mandate, which would define taxing powers, rates, exemptions, and enforcement mechanisms; (b) **detailed appropriation procedures** for specific sectors beyond what BAA 84 covers; (c) a comprehensive **public financial management code** integrating accounting, auditing, and fiscal reporting standards specific to BARMM.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **DBM** applies national budgeting rules to the block grant per Art. XII, Sec. 19. **COA** audits all Bangsamoro Government accounts. **Bureau of the Treasury** processes and releases the block grant. **BIR** collects national taxes within BARMM (Art. XII, Sec. 11 —until the Bangsamoro Revenue Office is fully operational). The **IFPB** coordinates fiscal policy between national and Bangsamoro governments.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Budgeting is **substantially devolved** —the Bangsamoro Government prepares its own budget, enacts its own GAA, and manages its own fiscal resources. The constraints are **structural**: COA audit authority (constitutional), DBM budgeting rules (Art. XII, Sec. 19), and the block grant mechanism itself (dependent on national government release). The **Bangsamoro Revenue Office** has been established but revenue collection functions are still transitioning from BIR.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. Extensive accomplishments: (1) **7 annual GAAs enacted** (FY 2020-2026) —unbroken appropriation cycle; (2) **BAA 84** (Budget System Act) enacted —fulfilling Art. VII, Sec. 28; (3) **MFBM fully operationalized** with Treasury, Finance, Budget, and Revenue offices; (4) **2 supplemental appropriations** passed; (5) **SDF mechanism** established (BAA 22) and extended; (6) **Revolving fund** (BAA 36) for nationally-funded salary payments; (7) **Multiple extension acts** demonstrating fiscal flexibility; (8) **Budget transparency** through published GAAs and performance targets.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. Two areas: (1) **Block grant unconditional release** —Art. XII, Sec. 18 provides that the block grant ***"shall not be subject to any lien or holdback"*** —giving the Bangsamoro Government more fiscal predictability than national agencies subject to DBM allotment; (2) **Fiscal autonomy design** —the BOL's comprehensive fiscal framework (12 revenue sources, block grant formula, IFPB mechanism) represents the most advanced fiscal autonomy arrangement for any autonomous region in the Philippines.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. Three constraints: (1) **No Revenue Code** —the most critical gap. Without it, the Bangsamoro Government cannot fully exercise its taxing powers under Art. XII, Sec. 6-9, and the Bangsamoro Revenue Office cannot independently administer regional taxes; (2) **Revenue collection transition** —BIR still collects most taxes in BARMM; the transition to Bangsamoro Revenue Office collection is incomplete; (3) **Absorption capacity** —the ability to spend appropriated funds efficiently and completely is constrained by institutional capacity, especially in infrastructure and capital projects.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has: (1) **BIR** with decades of tax administration infrastructure, IT systems, and trained personnel; (2) **DBM** with comprehensive budget preparation, execution, and monitoring systems (BESF, NEP, UACS); (3) **COA** with established audit methodologies, training programs, and enforcement mechanisms; (4) **PhilGEPS** for electronic procurement; (5) **Government Integrated Financial Management Information System (GIFMIS)** for financial reporting.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. Two areas: (1) **BIR's BARMM presence** has historically been one of the weakest regional offices, with low collection efficiency and limited enforcement capacity —contributing to the revenue gap; (2) **National budgeting rules** designed for the presidential system do not always fit the parliamentary model —some DBM procedures are bureaucratically heavy for a smaller, more agile government.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. **Replicate**: COA audit standards, government accounting rules (NGAFS), and procurement procedures (RA 9184). **Adapt**: DBM budget preparation procedures to fit the parliamentary model; the BIR tax administration framework for the Bangsamoro Revenue Office, incorporating regional context. **Replace**: the national tax collection presence with the Bangsamoro Revenue Office once the Revenue Code is enacted, while maintaining coordination with BIR for national taxes.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. While budgeting is primarily a technical governance function, Islamic public finance principles provide an ethical framework. The principle of *amanah* (trust/stewardship) is foundational —public funds are a sacred trust. The prohibition of *israf* (extravagance/waste) reinforces fiscal discipline. The principle of *maslahah* (public interest) aligns with Art. XII, Sec. 19's priority for education, health, and social services —corresponding to the Islamic hierarchy of needs (*daruriyyat*, *hajiyyat*, *tahsiniyyat*).

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Three principles: (1) ***Amanah*** —budget officials are trustees of public money; (2) **Prohibition of *israf*** —government spending must be proportionate, not extravagant; (3) ***Maslahah*** —budget allocation should prioritize essential services and public welfare. No specific Shari'ah rules on taxation or budgeting procedure override the BOL's fiscal framework, but these principles should guide the exercise of budgetary discretion.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national budgeting rules?**

A. BARMM's divergence authority is **narrower** for budgeting than for most powers. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. VII, Sec. 28 requires budget system consistency with national rules, and Art. XII, Sec. 19 applies DBM/DILG rules to block grant allocation. As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), COA's constitutional audit authority cannot be superseded. Within these constraints, BARMM can: (a) set its own **budget priorities** within the required education/health/social services preference; (b) create its own **special purpose funds** (SDF, Contingent Fund, IMPACT, etc.); (c) enact its own **Revenue Code** defining regional tax rates and exemptions; (d) establish its own **fiscal calendar and submission schedule**; (e) develop **Shari'ah-informed fiscal principles** (e.g., *amanah*-based accountability standards).

**Q. If BARMM enacts different fiscal standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) **Revenue Code differences**: a Bangsamoro Revenue Code can set different tax rates and exemptions within the Art. XII, Sec. 9 limitations (12 categories of taxes the Bangsamoro Government cannot impose). (2) **Budget process differences**: BAA 84 establishes the Bangsamoro budget system, but must remain consistent with national rules per Art. VII, Sec. 28. (3) **Cross-boundary fiscal operations**: corporations operating in both BARMM and non-BARMM areas face dual tax compliance —the IFPB resolves such disputes.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Two primary overlaps: (1) **Tax collection**: BIR and the Bangsamoro Revenue Office both have potential collection authority —Art. XII, Sec. 11 provides for transition, and Sec. 12 addresses corporate tax allocation, but the operational handover is incomplete. The IFPB is the designated dispute resolution mechanism (Art. XII, Sec. 9). (2) **Block grant allocation rules**: Art. XII, Sec. 19 applies DBM/DILG rules, but the specific interaction between Bangsamoro budget priorities and national budgeting procedures can create friction.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Three bills: **PB No. 286** (Bangsamoro Revenue Code —the most critical pending legislation), **PB No. 240** (budget submission schedule), and **PB No. 400** (Bangsamoro Trust Fund for development continuity). Enactment of the Revenue Code would be the most significant legislative achievement for this power after BAA 84.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **All development goals** depend on budgeting —it is the **fiscal infrastructure** for all government action. **BDP Chapter 13** (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) is the dedicated fiscal chapter, targeting revenue mobilization, block grant optimization, and development financing. **Matatag Pillar 1** (Good Governance) depends on transparent, performance-based budgeting. **Pillar 3** (Economic Resilience) depends on adequate budget allocation to economic programs and infrastructure.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **Revenue Code**: the Bangsamoro Government remains dependent on the block grant and BIR-collected taxes, unable to independently mobilize regional revenue —undermining fiscal autonomy. Without full operationalization of the **Bangsamoro Revenue Office**: revenue leakage continues and the government cannot exercise the taxing powers Art. XII, Sec. 6 grants. Without strengthened **absorption capacity**: appropriated funds go unspent, and development projects are delayed.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the block grant formula and how is it calculated?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 16: for the budget year immediately following the BOL's effectivity, the block grant = **5% of net NIRTA of BIR + net collections of Bureau of Customs** from the 3rd fiscal year preceding the current year. "Net" NIRTA = total BIR internal revenue tax collections minus LGU IRA, tax refunds, informers' rewards, and earmarked amounts. The formula is reviewed by the IFPB every 5 years (Art. XII, Sec. 22). After 20 years, certain revenues are deducted from the block grant (Art. XII, Sec. 21).

**Q. What taxes can the Bangsamoro Government NOT impose?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 9 enumerates **12 categories** the Bangsamoro Government's taxing power cannot extend to: (a) income tax (except on banks); (b) customs duties; (c) transit taxes; (d) taxes on marginal farmers'/fisherfolk's products; (e) taxes on BOI-certified pioneer enterprises; (f) excise taxes; (g) VAT; (h) transportation gross receipts taxes; (i) reinsurance premiums; (j) taxes on Philippine exports; (k) taxes on cooperatives under RA 6810/RA 6938; (l) taxes on national government entities.

   However, where **all taxable elements are within BARMM**, Parliament may impose 4 taxes to the exclusion of BIR: Capital Gains Tax, Documentary Stamp Tax, Donor's Tax, and Estate Tax.

**Q. What is the 45% PS cap and what does it include?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 20(a): ***"The total appropriations ... for Personal Services of the Bangsamoro Government for one (1) fiscal year shall not exceed forty-five percent (45%) of the total revenue sources."*** This covers salaries, wages, and allowances of all Bangsamoro Government personnel. **Excluded**: salaries of public utility/economic enterprise employees (charged against their own budgets). The 45% is calculated against **total revenue sources** (Art. XII, Sec. 6), not just the block grant.

**Q. What happens when the block grant formula is reviewed?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 22: the IFPB reviews the formula **5 years after effectivity** and **every 5 years thereafter**, considering fiscal needs, actual revenues generated, and ensuring transparent/performance-based expenditure. If the National Government no longer has representatives in the IFPB, the review is undertaken by the **Intergovernmental Relations Body** (Art. VI, Sec. 2).

**Q. What is the Special Development Fund (SDF)?**

A. Art. XIV, Sec. 2 provides for a **P50 billion** SDF over 10 years for rehabilitation and development of conflict-affected areas. BAA 22 implements this provision, with validity extended by BAA 34, 51. The SDF is separate from the block grant and annual GAA —it is a **peace dividend** for communities most affected by decades of armed conflict.

**Q. How does BAA 84 (Budget System Act) relate to the BOL's fiscal framework?**

A. BAA 84 fulfills the express mandate of Art. VII, Sec. 28 to enact a law prescribing ***"the form, content, and manner of preparation of the Bangsamoro budget."*** It codifies: the budget cycle, preparation procedures, submission requirements, appropriation processes, execution rules, and accountability mechanisms. BAA 84 operates within the constraints of Art. XII (fiscal autonomy framework) and must be ***"consistent with existing laws, rules and regulations of the National Government."***
