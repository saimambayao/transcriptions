# (jj) Labor, Employment, and Occupation

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(jj)** — "Labor, employment, and occupation;"

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|

| **Lead Ministry/Office** | Ministry of Labor and Employment (MOLE) |

| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 9 — Social Services (Education, Health) |

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over labor, employment, and occupation through Article V, Section 2(jj). Article IX, Section 10 is the core elaborating provision. It guarantees the fundamental rights of all workers in the Bangsamoro to self-organization, collective bargaining and negotiations, and peaceful concerted activities including the right to strike, "in accordance with the Constitution and the Labor Code of the Philippines." Workers, whether publicly or privately employed, shall not have their right to form unions, associations, or federations abridged. The provision further mandates that workers shall participate in policy and decision-making processes affecting their rights and benefits, as may be provided by law enacted by Parliament. The rights to security of tenure, humane conditions of work, and a living wage are guaranteed. No trafficking of persons or engagement of minors in hazardous employment shall be tolerated.[^1]

Critically, Article IX, Section 10 contains an expansion clause: "The Parliament may pass labor laws that expand or enhance the rights stated herein." This means the BOL establishes the national Labor Code as a floor, not a ceiling — the Bangsamoro Government may grant workers greater protections and benefits than what national law provides but may not reduce them. This distinction makes the labor power unique among BOL powers with national law consistency requirements: most others simply require consistency, while this one explicitly authorizes the Bangsamoro to go beyond national standards.[^2]

The power encompasses both domestic and overseas labor. The BOL's general grant of authority over "labor, employment, and occupation" covers the regulation of employment relationships, working conditions, wages, dispute resolution, recruitment agencies, and the welfare of Bangsamoro workers abroad. It also intersects with the power over development programs for labor under Article V, Section 2(r).

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

BAA No. 82 (Bangsamoro Labor and Employment Code), enacted in January 2026, is the comprehensive legislation implementing this power. This is one of the most significant standalone codes enacted by the Parliament, providing a complete regional labor framework. BAA 82 codifies worker rights, establishes labor standards, creates dispute resolution mechanisms, and defines the regulatory authority of MOLE within the Bangsamoro. It represents the Parliament's exercise of the Article IX, Section 10 expansion clause, enhancing worker protections beyond the national Labor Code baseline.[^3]

Before BAA 82, the Bangsamoro Government had enacted three targeted labor laws. BAA No. 9 regulates recruitment agencies operating in BARMM, establishing registration requirements and standards for agencies placing Bangsamoro workers domestically and internationally. BAA No. 19 provides specific protections for overseas Bangsamoro workers, addressing the welfare needs of the significant Bangsamoro overseas labor force. BAA No. 72 (Bereavement Leave Act) grants 7-day bereavement leave for all workers in BARMM, both regular and contractual — an example of the Parliament expanding worker benefits beyond what the national Labor Code provides.[^4]

The Ministry of Labor and Employment (MOLE), operating under BAA No. 13 (Administrative Code) with a dedicated chapter in BAA No. 85 (Chapter VI), serves as the lead ministry. MOLE oversees labor standards enforcement, employment facilitation, labor dispute resolution, and labor market information systems within the Bangsamoro.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The labor power carries an explicit national law consistency requirement — one of the clearest in the BOL. Article IX, Section 10 requires worker rights to be guaranteed "in accordance with the Constitution and the Labor Code of the Philippines." This means the national Labor Code (PD 442, as amended) sets the baseline for all labor regulation in the Bangsamoro. The Department of Labor and Employment (DOLE) retains certain enforcement functions in BARMM, particularly for national labor standards, occupational safety and health, and dispute resolution through the National Labor Relations Commission (NLRC).[^5]

However, the expansion clause fundamentally shapes this interaction. BAA 82 exercises the Parliament's explicit authority to "expand or enhance" labor rights beyond national standards. Where BAA 82 provides greater protections than the national Labor Code, BAA 82 prevails. Where BAA 82 is silent, the national Labor Code applies suppletorily. This creates a layered regulatory framework: national standards as the floor, with Bangsamoro enhancements stacked on top. The practical interaction between MOLE's regional mandate and DOLE's national mandate — particularly in areas like minimum wage determination (where the Regional Tripartite Wages and Productivity Board traditionally operates under DOLE) and NLRC dispute resolution — requires ongoing coordination.[^6]

No intergovernmental coordination body exists specifically for labor, though the general Intergovernmental Relations Body under Article VI, Section 2 could address disputes.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

With BAA 82 enacted, the Bangsamoro has one of its most complete legislative frameworks for any single power. However, implementation gaps remain. First, the implementing rules and regulations (IRR) for BAA 82 need to be issued and enforced, defining the practical procedures for the new labor standards, dispute resolution mechanisms, and regulatory processes established by the code. The transition from national Labor Code-only regulation to the BAA 82 framework requires clear guidance for employers, workers, and government agencies.

Second, the jurisdictional demarcation between MOLE and DOLE needs clarification. Specific areas of potential overlap include: minimum wage setting (traditionally a DOLE-supervised Regional Tripartite Wages and Productivity Board function); NLRC jurisdiction versus BAA 82 dispute resolution mechanisms; occupational safety and health standards enforcement; and labor inspection authority. A formal memorandum of agreement or supplementary legislation could establish clear boundaries.

Third, while BAA 9 regulates recruitment agencies and BAA 19 covers overseas workers, a dedicated Bangsamoro Overseas and Migrant Workers Act — as proposed in PB No. 188 (creating a Ministry of Bangsamoro Migrant Workers) — could consolidate and expand protections for the substantial Bangsamoro overseas labor force, including pre-deployment, on-site, and repatriation services.

Fourth, the informal economy, which constitutes a significant portion of employment in the Bangsamoro, may not be fully addressed by BAA 82. PB No. 194 (institutionalizing registration and database for informal workers) signals Parliament's awareness of this gap. Legislation establishing an informal worker registration system and extending basic social protection to informal workers would close a significant coverage gap.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 9 (Social Services), targets improved labor conditions, employment generation, and social protection for workers. BAA 82 provides the legislative foundation for these targets. The BDP's employment generation goals depend on MOLE's regulatory capacity under the new labor code, while the social protection targets require extending coverage to informal and overseas workers. Without clear MOLE-DOLE jurisdictional boundaries and IRR for BAA 82, the BDP's labor and employment targets risk implementation delays.

## Legislative Gaps

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type B**: Partial implementation | BAA 82 is newly enacted (January 2026); implementing rules and regulations needed to operationalize the code's provisions on labor standards, dispute resolution, and regulatory procedures | Issue comprehensive IRR for BAA 82 through MOLE |
| **Type A**: No standalone legislation | Informal workers constitute a large portion of the Bangsamoro workforce but lack registration and social protection coverage. PB No. 194 addresses this gap | Enact informal worker registration and social protection legislation |
| **Type C**: National law interaction | Jurisdictional overlap between MOLE and DOLE — particularly minimum wage setting, NLRC dispute resolution, and labor inspection authority — is not formally delineated | Formalize MOLE-DOLE coordination through intergovernmental agreement or supplementary legislation clarifying jurisdiction |
| **Type B**: Partial implementation | BAA 9 and BAA 19 cover overseas worker regulation, but no consolidated Bangsamoro migrant worker framework with pre-deployment, on-site, and repatriation services exists | Enact a comprehensive Bangsamoro Overseas and Migrant Workers Act, potentially creating a dedicated office as proposed in PB No. 188 |
