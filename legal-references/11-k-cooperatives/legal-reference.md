# (k) Cooperatives and Social Entrepreneurship

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(k)** --- ***"Cooperatives and social entrepreneurship;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(k) --- enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national cooperative law --- Art. XIII, Sec. 24 mandates a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** without any "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier referencing national law. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 1 requires the Bangsamoro economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution"*** and Art. V, Sec. 2 is ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** --- establishing a constitutional floor (see Theme C). |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA)** --- attached to the Office of the Chief Minister (BAA 13, Sec. 57) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT); Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 4 (Development Framework, Strategy 4) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | **Bangsamoro Cooperative Code** (mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 24 --- not yet enacted). Note: the cooperative code is a named legislative mandate but is NOT one of the 7 priority codes listed in Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a). |

## Legal Briefer

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over cooperatives and social entrepreneurship?**

A. Article V, Section 2(k) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"cooperatives and social entrepreneurship"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a broad grant covering the full scope of **cooperative regulation**, **promotion**, **registration**, and **development**, as well as the promotion and regulation of **social enterprises** within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Five provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 24 states: ***"It shall adopt and implement cooperative development policies and programs through the enactment of a Bangsamoro cooperative code."*** This provision places cooperatives within the **trade and industry framework** and expressly names a specific piece of legislation --- the ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** --- that Parliament must enact.[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 27 is the dedicated cooperatives and social entrepreneurship provision. It states: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall recognize, protect, and promote the establishment of cooperative enterprises. It shall likewise encourage and promote social entrepreneurship as a means to engage the private sector to assist in the development, funding, and implementation of solutions to social, cultural, or environmental issues."*** It further mandates: ***"The Parliament shall pass laws to strengthen the growth and development of social enterprises in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region to achieve social justice, social equity, and economic efficiency."***[^3]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 1 establishes the general economic framework: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall establish an economic system based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution."*** The 1987 Constitution, Art. XII, Sec. 15, mandates Congress to create an agency promoting viability and growth of cooperatives --- **CSEA** is the Bangsamoro counterpart.[^4]

   (4) Art. XIII, Sec. 2 provides: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall promote the effective use of economic resources and endeavor to attain economic development that facilitates growth and full employment, human development, and social justice."*** This positions cooperatives as instruments of social justice and full employment.[^5]

   (5) Art. XII, Sec. 9(k) provides **fiscal protection** by excluding from the Bangsamoro Government's taxing power: ***"Taxes, fees, or charges on countryside and barangay business enterprises and cooperatives duly registered under Republic Act No. 6810, otherwise known as the 'Magna Carta for Countryside and Barangay Business Enterprises,' and Republic Act No. 6938, otherwise known as the 'Cooperative Code of the Philippines,' as amended."***[^6]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **two express legislative mandates**:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 24: ***"through the enactment of a Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** --- this names the specific legislation by title, making it a **named legislative mandate**. Note: the cooperative code is NOT one of the 7 priority codes listed in Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) (Administrative Code, Revenue Code, Electoral Code, Local Government Code, Education Code, plus the permissive Civil Service Code and the IP rights law). The cooperative code's mandate comes from Art. XIII, Sec. 24 separately.[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 27: ***"The Parliament shall pass laws to strengthen the growth and development of social enterprises"*** --- a separate mandate for **social enterprise legislation**, distinct from the cooperative code.[^3]

   Neither mandate has been fulfilled as of BAA 89.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not expressly create **CSEA** by name. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 24 and 27, read together with the general power in Art. V, Sec. 2(k), contemplate an **institutional mechanism** for cooperative development. CSEA was established through **BAA 13** (Administrative Code), Book V, Title II, Chapter 3, Section H (Sec. 57-65), and receives budget allocation under **BAA 85**, Chapter XXVI.[^7][^8]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places two types of limitations:

   (1) **Fiscal limitation**: Art. XII, Sec. 9(k) prohibits the Bangsamoro Government from imposing taxes, fees, or charges on cooperatives registered under the national Cooperative Code (**RA 6938**, as amended by **RA 9520**) and the Magna Carta for Countryside and Barangay Business Enterprises (**RA 6810**). This ensures that BARMM taxation does not disadvantage the cooperative sector.[^6]

   (2) **Constitutional floor**: Art. XIII, Sec. 1 requires the Bangsamoro economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution."*** Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** While no express consistency with national cooperative law (RA 9520) is required --- Art. XIII, Sec. 24 and 27 lack any "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier --- the constitutional principles (Art. XII, Sec. 15 cooperative policy; Art. III equal protection and due process) set the floor below which Bangsamoro cooperative legislation cannot fall. See **Theme C** for details.[^4][^12]

### B. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Cooperatives and social entrepreneurship have a **significant Shari'ah dimension**. The cooperative model --- pooling capital, sharing risks and profits, and working collectively for mutual benefit --- is deeply consistent with Islamic economic principles. However, the **conventional cooperative financing model** (interest-bearing loans to members, fixed returns on deposits) conflicts with the **prohibition of *riba*** (interest/usury), which is among the most strictly prohibited transactions in Islamic commercial law. A Bangsamoro Cooperative Code that does not provide Shari'ah-compliant alternatives would exclude observant Muslim members from full participation in their own cooperatives. **EO 316** (1996) already recognized this dimension, noting ***"cooperativism in accordance with Islamic principles and local practices and traditions"*** as a basis for devolving CDA functions to the autonomous region.[^9]

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. XIII, Sec. 24 or 27 (the cooperative provisions). However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact laws on Shari'ah matters, including commercial law: ***"The Parliament has the power to enact laws governing commercial and other civil actions not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083, as amended."***[^10] Since cooperative regulation is commercial in nature --- involving capitalization, lending, profit-sharing, and trade --- and is not provided for under PD 1083 (which covers personal status, marriage, divorce, succession, and property), Parliament has express authority to enact Shari'ah-compliant cooperative legislation under this provision. Additionally, Art. XIII, Sec. 1 requires the Bangsamoro economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution."***

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Six Islamic legal principles directly govern cooperative legislation:

   (1) ***Mudarabah*** (trust financing/profit-sharing) --- applicable where cooperatives pool member savings and provide capital to entrepreneurs. The cooperative acts as *rabb al-mal* (capital provider); the entrepreneur is the *mudarib*. Profits are shared per agreement; losses fall on the capital provider unless the *mudarib* was negligent.

   (2) ***Musharakah*** (joint venture/partnership) --- the most natural Shari'ah-compliant cooperative structure. Members contribute capital, share profits proportionally, and bear losses in proportion to their capital contribution. This is structurally identical to cooperative equity principles.

   (3) ***Murabaha*** (cost-plus financing) --- applicable to cooperative procurement and resale. The cooperative purchases goods (agricultural inputs, equipment) and resells to members at a disclosed markup, paid in installments. No interest is charged; the profit is earned through the sale transaction.

   (4) **Prohibition of *riba*** (interest/usury) --- the central Shari'ah constraint. Conventional cooperative lending charges interest on member loans; this is *riba* regardless of the rate or label. A Bangsamoro Cooperative Code must provide alternatives.

   (5) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) --- cooperative promotion serves the common good, justifying legislation under this principle even where specific structures are not addressed in classical texts.

   (6) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) --- cooperative officers managing member funds are *amin* (trustees). This principle requires transparency, accountability, and prohibition of self-dealing --- strengthening cooperative governance standards.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) governs personal status, marriage, divorce, succession, and property for Muslims but does not specifically address cooperatives or commercial associations. Its relevance to cooperatives is **limited** --- it does not define cooperative structures, financing models, or commercial associations. **BOL Art. X, Sec. 4** authorizes Shari'ah-based commercial legislation but no such law has been enacted for cooperatives. **Shari'ah courts** have jurisdiction over property and commercial disputes among Muslims --- cooperative disputes between Muslim members could potentially fall within this jurisdiction. No known *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address cooperative structures, though Islamic banking and finance opinions on *mudarabah* and *musharakah* are directly analogous.[^11]

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro cooperative legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is an **optional dual-track system**: a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code should provide **both conventional and Shari'ah-compliant cooperative models** as distinct legal categories. Cooperatives would choose their compliance track at registration. The **Shari'ah-compliant track** would require: (a) financing structures limited to *mudarabah*, *musharakah*, *murabaha*, and other permissible contracts; (b) prohibition of *riba*-bearing transactions; (c) a **Shari'ah Advisory Board** or **Shari'ah compliance certification** mechanism, potentially through the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* or a designated Shari'ah review body within CSEA; and (d) governance standards incorporating *amanah* principles. This dual-track approach respects the diversity within BARMM --- not all cooperatives are Muslim-majority, and even Muslim cooperatives may operate conventionally --- while ensuring that observant members have a lawful option. Compliance should be **optional**, not mandatory.

### C. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to cooperatives and social entrepreneurship?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 20)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** This is the **universal constitutional boundary** on all 55 powers.[^12]

   (2) **Cooperative Policy (Art. XII, Sec. 15)**: ***"The Congress shall create an agency to promote the viability and growth of cooperatives as instruments for social justice and economic development."*** This is the **constitutional mandate** that created **CDA**. It establishes the **state policy** favoring cooperatives --- any legislation, whether national or Bangsamoro, must serve this purpose.[^13]

   (3) **Equal Protection and Due Process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, nor shall any person be denied the equal protection of the laws."*** Cooperative members in BARMM are entitled to equal protection. Any regulatory action against a cooperative (suspension, dissolution, penalties) must satisfy procedural due process (notice, hearing, right to appeal) and substantive due process (rational basis). Equal protection does not require identical treatment --- it requires that any differential treatment rests on substantial distinctions germane to the purpose of the law.[^14]

   (4) **Freedom of Association (Art. III, Sec. 8)**: ***"The right of the people, including those employed in the public and private sectors, to form unions, associations, or societies for purposes not contrary to law shall not be abridged."*** Cooperative formation is a constitutionally protected associational right. Any regulatory framework cannot unduly restrict the right to form cooperatives.[^15]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee for cooperatives?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Right to form cooperatives** --- protected under the **freedom of association** (Art. III, Sec. 8). Any Bangsamoro cooperative law cannot impose registration requirements so burdensome as to effectively deny this right.

   (2) **State support and promotion** --- Art. XII, Sec. 15 mandates the State to ***"promote the viability and growth of cooperatives."*** This is an **affirmative obligation** --- not merely a permission. Both the national and Bangsamoro governments must actively support cooperatives.

   (3) **Equal protection** (Art. III, Sec. 1) --- differential treatment must rest on **substantial distinctions** germane to the purpose of the law.

   (4) **Due process** (Art. III, Sec. 1) --- regulatory action must satisfy notice, hearing, and right to appeal.

**Q. What constitutional mandates for cooperative legislation or institutions exist?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 15 mandates Congress to ***"create an agency to promote the viability and growth of cooperatives."*** This mandate was fulfilled nationally by the creation of **CDA** (RA 11364). For BARMM, **CSEA** serves as the counterpart agency --- but CSEA lacks the enabling legislation that CDA has. The constitutional mandate implies that CSEA must be empowered with comparable promotional functions, though the specific regulatory framework may differ.[^13]

### D. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for cooperatives?**

A. The national framework establishes a comprehensive system: **RA 9520** (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) governs cooperative formation, registration, governance, membership, capitalization, financial management, audit, merger/consolidation, dissolution, and tax privileges. **RA 11364** (CDA Charter of 2019) creates the regulatory institution with quasi-judicial powers. **RA 10744** (Credit Surety Fund Cooperatives Act) provides cooperative-organized credit surety mechanisms. **RA 6810** (Magna Carta for Countryside Enterprises) provides rural cooperative incentives. **RA 11535** mandates Cooperative Development Officers in LGUs. Together, these laws provide: a **registration system**, **governance standards**, **financial safeguards**, **tax incentives**, **regulatory oversight**, and an **institutional framework** --- the baseline against which any Bangsamoro cooperative law will be compared.[^16][^17][^18][^19][^20]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national cooperative laws apply in the absence of a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code:

   (1) **RA 9520** --- governs all cooperative operations within BARMM. Applies suppletorily.[^16]
   (2) **RA 11364** --- **CDA** retains full registration and regulatory authority over BARMM cooperatives. Applies directly.[^17]
   (3) **RA 10744** --- applies to BARMM cooperatives for credit surety fund access. Suppletory.[^18]
   (4) **RA 6810** --- referenced in BOL Art. XII, Sec. 9(k) as basis for tax exemption. Applies **directly** (constitutional-level protection).[^19]
   (5) **RA 11535** --- applies to BARMM LGUs mandating Cooperative Development Officers.[^20]
   (6) **RA 10679**, **RA 9178**, **RA 9501**, **RA 10644**, **RA 11293**, **RA 11901** --- suppletory application for youth entrepreneurship, BMBE, MSME, innovation, and agricultural financing frameworks applicable to cooperatives.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for cooperatives?**

A. The **Cooperative Development Authority (CDA)** exercises full authority --- **registration**, **regulation**, **examination**, and **quasi-judicial** functions --- over cooperatives in BARMM. CDA maintains regional and field offices within the region. See **Theme E.2** for details on CDA's operational functions within BARMM. When a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code is enacted, the question is whether **CSEA replaces CDA** within BARMM entirely, or whether CDA retains certain functions (e.g., national registration for cross-territorial cooperatives). **EO 316** (1996) already devolved 10 CDA functions to the ARMM government --- but deferred the registration function to a MOA between CDA and ARMM that was never fully implemented.[^9][^17]

### E. Implementation

#### E.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA)** is the lead agency. See **Theme A** for CSEA's institutional basis in the BOL. BAA 13, Book V, Title II, Chapter 3, Section H (Sec. 57-65) defines CSEA's institutional status, mandate, powers, functions, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter XXVI allocates its operational budget.[^7][^8]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for cooperatives and social entrepreneurship as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through CSEA's **BAA 13 general mandate** and **BAA 85 budget allocation**.

**Q. What are CSEA's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book V, Title II, Chapter 3, Section H (Sec. 57-65) defines CSEA comprehensively:

   **Institutional status** (Sec. 57): CSEA is ***"the primary entity to promote the viability and growth of Bangsamoro cooperatives and social enterprise"*** and ***"the sole agency of the Bangsamoro Government that registers cooperatives and social enterprises."*** It is **attached to the Office of the Chief Minister**.[^7]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 58): CSEA, together with ***"all the branches, sub-divisions, instrumentalities and agencies of the Bangsamoro Government shall ensure the provision of technical guidance, financial assistance and other services to enable the cooperatives and social enterprises to develop into viable and responsive economic enterprises and be platforms for poverty reduction."***[^21]

   **General Concepts** (Sec. 59): A cooperative is defined as ***"a duly registered association of persons, with common bond of interest, who have voluntarily joined together to achieve a lawful common social or economic end, making equitable contributions to the capital required and accepting a fair share of the risks and benefits of the undertaking in accordance with the universally accepted cooperative principles."*** Social enterprise is defined as businesses ***"whose primary purpose is addressing a social, cultural or environmental challenge where creating profit for owners or shareholders is secondary."***[^22]

   **Powers and functions** (Sec. 60) --- headed by an Executive Director with the rank of Director II, with **16 enumerated functions**:

   - (a) ***"Formulate, adopt and implement integrated and comprehensive plans and programs on cooperative and social enterprise development consistent with the existing policy on cooperatives and the overall socio-economic development plans of the Bangsamoro Government;"***
   - (b) ***"Develop and conduct management and training programs upon request of cooperatives and social enterprises;"***
   - (c) ***"Support the voluntary organization and consensual development of activities that promote cooperative movements and provide assistance towards upgrading managerial and technical expertise upon request of the cooperative concerned;"***
   - (d) ***"Ensure that the marginalized, vulnerable and disabled individuals and groups are able to participate in the new socio-economic model for BARMM;"***
   - (e) ***"Coordinate the efforts of the local government units in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region and the private sector in the promotion, organization and development of cooperatives and social enterprise;"***
   - (f) ***"Promote and strengthen cooperatives and social enterprises as pathways to reintegrate former combatants, surrenderees, and disaster-affected communities, among others;"***
   - (g) ***"Support social entrepreneurs on product development, business finance, market and linkage, and legal accreditation;"***
   - (h) ***"Provide social entrepreneurs with capacity development and access to training, mentoring and funding opportunities;"***
   - (i) ***"Promote social enterprise education in schools and universities;"***
   - (j) ***"Convene policy dialogues, conduct research and organize study tours to share knowledge and best practice in creating an enabling environment for social enterprise;"***
   - (k) ***"Require all cooperatives, their federations and unions in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region to submit their annual reports and their financial statements, duly audited by certified public accountants;"***
   - (l) ***"Assist cooperatives in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in accessing financial and other forms of assistance under such terms and conditions as are calculated to strengthen their viability and autonomy;"***
   - (m) ***"Administer all grants and donations coursed through the Bangsamoro Government for cooperative development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region without prejudice to the right of cooperatives to directly receive and administer such grants and donation upon agreement with the grantors and donors thereof;"***
   - (n) ***"Upon request of either or both parties, to mediate and conciliate disputes within a cooperative or between cooperatives in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region;"***
   - (o) ***"Formulate and adopt continuing policy initiatives in consultation with the cooperative and social enterprise sector in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region through public hearings;"***
   - (p) ***"Perform such other functions under existing laws."***[^23]

   **Organizational structure** (Sec. 61): Executive Director (Director II rank) assisted by three Division Chiefs, with support staff. Three divisions:

   (a) **Administrative and Finance Division** (Sec. 62) --- provides administrative support, manages finances, and carries out other administrative functions assigned by the Chief Minister or other competent authority.

   (b) **Cooperative Development Division** (Sec. 63) --- headed by a Division Chief, with the following functions:
   - (a) Recommend policies, strategies, plans and programs on cooperatives and extend necessary support and technical assistance towards upgrading managerial/technical and fiscal capabilities of cooperatives;
   - (b) Process applications for and thereafter issue **Certificates of Registration** and **Certificates of Compliance**;
   - (c) Provide **mediation and arbitration** services to registered cooperatives;
   - (d) Conduct **monitoring and inspection** of cooperatives;
   - (e) Develop and conduct **management and training** for members of cooperatives;
   - (f) Require the submission of annual reports, audited financial statements, and other necessary reports from registered cooperatives;
   - (g) Perform such other functions as may be provided by law.[^24]

   (c) **Social Enterprise Division** (Sec. 64) --- headed by a Division Chief, with the following functions:
   - (a) **Promote and advocate** social enterprise in community development;
   - (b) Assist social enterprises in the **development of their business model**;
   - (c) Encourage **social procurement** within government agencies --- purchasing goods and services from social benefit suppliers and training and employment of socially and economically disadvantaged people;
   - (d) Organize the **Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Coalition**;
   - (e) Provide support and assistance to social enterprises whenever necessary;
   - (f) Perform such other functions as may be provided by law;
   - (g) Support social entrepreneurs on product development, business finance, market and linkage and legal accreditation;
   - (h) Provide social entrepreneurs with capacity development and access to training, mentoring and funding opportunities;
   - (i) Promote social enterprise education in schools and universities;
   - (j) Convene policy dialogues, conduct research and organize study tours to share knowledge and best practice in creating an enabling environment for social enterprise.[^25]

   **Support to Local Government Units** (Sec. 65): ***"In a province, city or municipality that has its own Cooperative Development Office, the Authority shall provide support and assistance whenever necessary. Those local government units without a Cooperative Development Office shall be assisted by a Cooperative Development Specialist in the implementation of their cooperative programs."***[^26]

**Q. What can CSEA NOT do without a standalone Bangsamoro Cooperative Code?**

A. While BAA 13 gives CSEA broad functions --- including registration (Sec. 57, 63(b)) --- it does not provide: a **comprehensive regulatory framework** with detailed governance standards, capitalization requirements, and financial management rules; **penalty provisions** for cooperative violations; a **dissolution and liquidation** process; **Shari'ah-compliant cooperative models** (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*) with legal definitions and requirements; **tax treatment and incentives** specific to BARMM cooperatives; or a **clear jurisdictional boundary** with CDA for cooperatives operating across BARMM and non-BARMM areas. BAA 13 creates the institution but not the **regulatory code** it needs to fully exercise its authority.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. **BTA Resolution No. 220** --- ***"Calling for the Implementation of Section 24 and Section 27, Article XIII of the Bangsamoro Organic Law on the Enactment of a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code and Social Enterprise Code"*** --- explicitly recognizes the **unfulfilled mandate** of Art. XIII, Sec. 24 and Sec. 27.

#### E.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **Cooperative Development Authority (CDA)** is the national government agency exercising authority in BARMM. CDA maintains a **regional extension office** in BARMM that performs the full suite of regulatory functions: **cooperative registration** (issuance of Certificates of Registration), **compliance monitoring** (annual report review), **regulatory examination** (financial audits), **quasi-judicial** functions (resolution of intra-cooperative disputes), and **technical assistance** (training, organizational development). All cooperatives in BARMM are currently registered with CDA, not CSEA, because no Bangsamoro Cooperative Code has been enacted to formally transfer the registration function. **EO 316** (1996) devolved 10 CDA functions to the ARMM government but deferred the registration function to a MOA that was never fully implemented.[^9][^17]

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for cooperatives?**

A. CDA operates several programs within BARMM: (1) **cooperative registration and compliance** --- processing new registrations and annual compliance reviews; (2) **Cooperative Development Program** --- organizational development, governance training, and management improvement for registered cooperatives; (3) **Koop Kapatid Program** --- linking stronger cooperatives to assist weaker ones; (4) **CDA Laboratory Cooperative** concept --- incubation of new cooperative formations; (5) **compliance and examination services** --- financial examination of cooperative operations. Additionally, the **Department of Trade and Industry (DTI)** operates **Negosyo Centers** in BARMM LGUs under RA 10644, and the **Department of Agriculture** implements agricultural cooperative-linked programs under RA 10744 (Credit Surety Fund) and RA 11901 (agricultural financing).

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **incomplete**. **EO 316** (1996) devolved 10 CDA functions to the ARMM government --- including policy formulation, training, technical assistance, financial arrangements, mediation, and annual reporting --- but **deferred the registration function** to a memorandum of agreement between CDA and the ARMM government.[^9] This MOA was never fully implemented. Under the BOL, **BAA 13 Sec. 57** designates CSEA as ***"the sole agency of the Bangsamoro Government that registers cooperatives and social enterprises"*** --- but without a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code establishing the legal framework for registration (standards, forms, requirements, appeals), CSEA cannot operationally exercise this function independently of CDA. The **registration function** is the critical piece that remains with CDA pending enactment of a standalone code.

#### E.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. CSEA has been **institutionally established** under BAA 13 with a defined organizational structure (three divisions), an Executive Director, and an operational budget under BAA 85. It has begun exercising its **programmatic functions** --- conducting cooperative development training, supporting social enterprise formation, promoting cooperative formation as a reintegration pathway for former combatants (Sec. 60(f)), and coordinating with LGUs on cooperative programs. CSEA has also begun processing **cooperative registrations** under the authority of BAA 13 Sec. 57 and Sec. 63(b), though the legal framework for this registration remains thinly specified without a standalone code. No standalone legislation has been enacted, but **PB No. 210** (Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code) has been filed, and **BTA Resolution No. 220** has formally recognized the unfulfilled mandate.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three unique advantages over the national framework:

   (1) **Shari'ah integration** --- CSEA can develop and promote **Shari'ah-compliant cooperative models** (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*, *murabaha*-based structures) that CDA and the national Cooperative Code (RA 9520) do not provide. The national framework is entirely conventional and offers no alternative to interest-bearing cooperative lending.

   (2) **Conflict-sensitive design** --- BAA 13 Sec. 60(f) explicitly mandates CSEA to ***"promote and strengthen cooperatives and social enterprises as pathways to reintegrate former combatants, surrenderees, and disaster-affected communities."*** No comparable mandate exists in the national framework. BARMM's cooperative development is designed for a **post-conflict context** --- addressing IDP livelihoods, ex-combatant economic reintegration, and community rebuilding.

   (3) **Social enterprise dual mandate** --- BAA 13 gives CSEA authority over **both cooperatives and social enterprises** in a single institution, with a dedicated Social Enterprise Division (Sec. 64). Nationally, social enterprise has no comparable institutional home --- CDA handles cooperatives only. CSEA's integrated mandate positions it to build the cooperative-social enterprise ecosystem that the BOL envisions.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a **standalone Bangsamoro Cooperative Code**. Without it:

   - CSEA cannot exercise a **fully independent registration function** --- the regulatory standards, forms, requirements, and appeals process are not codified in Bangsamoro law. BAA 13 Sec. 57 declares CSEA the "sole agency" for registration but provides no implementing framework.
   - **Shari'ah-compliant cooperative models** have no legal definition --- cooperatives cannot be registered under a *musharakah* or *mudarabah* framework because no law defines what these models require.
   - **Penalty provisions** for cooperative violations do not exist in Bangsamoro law --- CSEA cannot enforce compliance.
   - The **jurisdictional boundary with CDA** is unresolved --- cooperatives face dual compliance obligations.
   - **Tax treatment and incentives** specific to BARMM cooperatives cannot be established.

   Enactment of **PB No. 210** (or equivalent) would remove this constraint.

#### E.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a **comprehensive cooperative infrastructure** over three decades:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 9520 (Cooperative Code) provides a complete regulatory framework with 144 sections covering every aspect of cooperative life --- from formation to dissolution. This has been supplemented by RA 11364 (CDA Charter), RA 11535 (mandatory CDOs in LGUs), and multiple supporting laws.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: CDA has been operational since 1990 (RA 6939), now with 16 regional extension offices, field offices in every province, a staff of several hundred, and specialized divisions for registration, compliance, examination, mediation, and training.
   - **Regulatory systems**: CDA maintains a **national cooperative registration database** with over 73,000 registered cooperatives nationwide, an **online registration system**, annual compliance monitoring, financial examination protocols, and a quasi-judicial mechanism for intra-cooperative disputes.
   - **IT and data**: CDA's **Cooperative Information System (CIS)** provides online registration, compliance tracking, and public access to cooperative data. CDA also publishes annual statistical reports on the cooperative sector.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in four critical areas:

   (1) **No Shari'ah-compliant models**: RA 9520 and CDA regulations are entirely conventional. There are no provisions for *mudarabah*-based cooperative financing, *musharakah*-based cooperative equity, or *murabaha*-based cooperative procurement. Muslim cooperative members in BARMM must either participate in interest-bearing structures (violating *riba* prohibition) or forego cooperative lending benefits entirely.

   (2) **No conflict-sensitive provisions**: The national framework does not account for post-conflict realities --- IDP cooperatives, ex-combatant livelihood cooperatives, disaster-recovery cooperatives. CDA's programs are designed for stable communities with established economic infrastructure.

   (3) **Language and cultural barriers**: CDA services are delivered primarily in Filipino and English. Cooperatives in Bangsamoro communities --- particularly in rural Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, Lanao del Sur, Basilan, and Tawi-Tawi --- require services in Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, and Yakan. CDA's materials and training programs do not accommodate these languages. (Note: Sulu is excluded from BARMM territory per *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea*, G.R. Nos. 242255, 243246, 243693 (Sept. 9, 2024; finalized Nov. 26, 2024) and EO 91, s. 2025, which realigned Sulu under Region IX. Tausug and Sama communities remain culturally Bangsamoro regardless of the territorial ruling.)

   (4) **One-size-fits-all framework**: CDA's BARMM regional extension office has historically been one of the least-resourced, with fewer staff, less frequent examinations, and weaker enforcement compared to regions in Luzon and Visayas.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element of the national framework:

   - **Replicate**: **audit standards** (cooperative financial examination protocols), **financial reporting requirements** (annual reports, audited financial statements), and **governance standards** (board composition, member meetings, elections). These are technically sound and do not require regional customization.
   - **Adapt**: **registration system** --- take CDA's registration framework and add a **Shari'ah-compliant cooperative category** with distinct requirements for *musharakah*/*mudarabah*/*murabaha* structures. Adapt the **compliance monitoring** system to include Shari'ah compliance checks. Adapt **cooperative development programs** for conflict-affected communities, local languages, and BARMM's economic context.
   - **Replace**: **cooperative financing models** --- build an entirely distinct Shari'ah-compliant financing track that RA 9520 does not provide. Replace the **institutional framework** --- CSEA replaces CDA within BARMM, with its own regulatory authority, registration database, and compliance system. The **social enterprise framework** is entirely new --- the national system has no equivalent, so BARMM builds from scratch.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national cooperative law?**

A. BARMM has **broad legislative authority** to diverge because: (1) the BOL **expressly mandates** a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** --- this is congressional authorization for a distinct framework; (2) **no consistency requirement** with national law is imposed; (3) the BOL is itself a **congressional act** (RA 11054), providing the strongest basis for differentiation. As established in **Theme A**, the BOL places no national law consistency requirement on cooperatives --- no "consistent with," "in accordance with," or "subject to" qualifier appears in Art. XIII, Sec. 24 or 27. As established in **Theme C**, the constitutional floor is Art. XII, Sec. 15 (cooperative policy) and Art. III (equal protection and due process).

   The **limits of divergence** are defined by the **constitutional floor** established in **Theme C** (Art. XII, Sec. 15 cooperative policy, Art. III equal protection and due process):
   - **Constitutional floor**: Art. XII, Sec. 15's policy of promoting cooperatives as ***"instruments for social justice and economic development"*** cannot be undermined. BARMM can go **above** this floor (more protective) but not **below** it.
   - **Equal protection**: Differential treatment must rest on **substantial distinctions** (BARMM's conflict-affected context, Islamic finance traditions, agricultural patterns). Arbitrary or unreasonable differences could face challenge --- but the BOL's express mandate makes this unlikely to succeed.
   - **Due process**: Registration, regulation, and penalty provisions must provide **notice, hearing, and appeal** mechanisms.
   - **Fiscal limitation**: Art. XII, Sec. 9(k) prohibits taxing nationally-registered cooperatives --- a Bangsamoro code cannot override this for cooperatives registered under RA 9520/RA 6810.[^6]

   In practice, BARMM can: establish a **separate registration system** under CSEA; create **Shari'ah-compliant cooperative models** (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*) that RA 9520 does not provide; set **different capitalization thresholds**; establish **different governance standards**; and create a **Bangsamoro social enterprise framework** with no national equivalent.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For cooperative members within BARMM**: They could gain rights not available under RA 9520 --- particularly **Shari'ah-compliant** financial structures and potentially **lower capitalization** thresholds suited to conflict-affected communities. The risk is if the Bangsamoro code provides **fewer protections** (e.g., weaker audit requirements, less member-protection) --- this could face constitutional challenge under the Art. XII, Sec. 15 policy.

   (2) **For cross-boundary cooperatives**: Cooperatives operating in **both BARMM and non-BARMM areas** face **dual compliance** --- CSEA within BARMM, CDA outside. The Bangsamoro code should include **mutual recognition** or **comity** provisions.

   (3) **For constitutional vulnerability**: A challenge is unlikely to succeed because the BOL --- a congressional act --- **expressly authorizes** the differentiation by mandating a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code."***

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between **CSEA** and **CDA**. Currently, CDA under **RA 11364** is the sole registering and regulatory authority for cooperatives nationwide, including in BARMM. A Bangsamoro Cooperative Code must clarify: (a) whether **CSEA replaces CDA within BARMM** for registration, or whether **dual registration** is required; (b) how cooperatives operating in **both BARMM and non-BARMM areas** comply with both frameworks; and (c) whether **CDA regulatory orders and memorandum circulars** continue to apply within BARMM after the code's effectivity.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB No. 210** (Providing for the Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code) addresses both mandates in a single comprehensive bill. If enacted, it would fulfill **Art. XIII, Sec. 24** (cooperative code) and **Art. XIII, Sec. 27** (social enterprise legislation), and would presumably define **CSEA's regulatory powers**. No other pending PBs on this subject have been identified.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) identifies cooperative development as a **key strategy for inclusive economic growth**, with targets for expanding cooperative membership and capitalization across BARMM. Cooperatives serve **BDP Chapter 4, Strategy 4** (enhanced economic growth and job generation) as community-owned vehicles for productive enterprise. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, depends on cooperative development to strengthen community-based economic resilience and reduce dependence on external aid and remittances. Cooperatives are also critical to **Pillar 1 (Good Governance)** --- as platforms for participatory economic governance and grassroots institutional capacity.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **Bangsamoro Cooperative Code**: CSEA cannot **independently register** cooperatives, meaning all BARMM cooperatives must register with CDA under the national framework --- a process that does not account for BARMM's unique context, including **Shari'ah-compliant cooperative structures**. **Cooperative governance standards** cannot be enforced under Bangsamoro law. The **social enterprise ecosystem** lacks a legal definition, accreditation framework, or incentive structure. The **BDP targets** for cooperative membership expansion depend on institutional infrastructure that **BAA 13's general mandate alone cannot provide**. **Agricultural cooperatives** --- critical for food security in conflict-affected areas --- operate without a region-specific support framework. The **ex-combatant reintegration pathway** through cooperatives (BAA 13 Sec. 60(f)) cannot be fully operationalized without a code that defines cooperative formation standards suited to this population. Without legislation, the two express BOL mandates (Art. XIII, Sec. 24 and 27) remain unfulfilled through the transition period.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why does the BOL name a "cooperative code" by title --- and how does it relate to the priority codes?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 24 is one of the few BOL provisions that **names a specific piece of legislation by title** --- ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code."*** Most BOL provisions mandate legislation in general terms ("the Parliament shall enact laws on..."). By naming the specific code, the BOL signals that **cooperative development is central to the Bangsamoro economic vision**, not a peripheral regulatory matter. However, the cooperative code is **NOT** one of the 7 priority codes listed in **Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)** --- those are the Administrative Code, Revenue Code, Electoral Code, Local Government Code, and Education Code (mandatory), plus the Civil Service Code (permissive) and the IP rights law. The cooperative code's mandate derives from **Art. XIII, Sec. 24** as a named legislative obligation separate from the transition priority list. The absence of the code constitutes an express, identifiable unfulfilled mandate --- unlike powers where the legislative obligation is more general.

**Q. What is the relationship between CSEA and CDA --- will one replace the other within BARMM?**

A. BAA 13 Sec. 57 designates CSEA as ***"the sole agency of the Bangsamoro Government that registers cooperatives and social enterprises."*** CDA under RA 11364 is the sole **national** registering authority. When a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code is enacted, **CSEA should replace CDA within BARMM** for registration, regulation, and development of cooperatives --- completing the devolution that **EO 316** (1996) began. CDA would retain authority only over cooperatives registered nationally that **operate across BARMM and non-BARMM areas** --- requiring a **comity or mutual recognition provision** in the code.[^7][^9]

**Q. How does Art. XII, Sec. 9(k) affect the Bangsamoro Government's fiscal relationship with cooperatives?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 9(k) **excludes from the Bangsamoro Government's taxing power** cooperatives registered under **RA 6810** and **RA 6938** (as amended by RA 9520). This means: (a) cooperatives currently registered with CDA under RA 9520 are **constitutionally protected** from BARMM-imposed taxes; (b) a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code **cannot override this protection** for nationally-registered cooperatives; (c) for cooperatives registered **solely under the Bangsamoro code** (not under RA 9520), the Bangsamoro Government may define its own tax treatment --- but must still comply with Art. XII, Sec. 15's policy of **promoting cooperatives** (i.e., cannot impose punitive taxation). This creates an incentive design question: the code must make Bangsamoro registration **at least as advantageous** as national registration to encourage migration.[^6]

**Q. How does cooperative authority differ from the social enterprise mandate?**

A. The BOL treats cooperatives and social enterprises as **related but distinct** concepts:

   (1) **Cooperatives** (Art. XIII, Sec. 24): the BOL mandates a specific ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** --- naming the legislation by title. Cooperatives are member-owned associations with shared capital and democratic governance.

   (2) **Social enterprises** (Art. XIII, Sec. 27): the BOL mandates Parliament to ***"pass laws to strengthen the growth and development of social enterprises"*** --- broader language, not naming a specific code. Social enterprises are businesses ***"whose primary purpose is addressing a social, cultural or environmental challenge where creating profit for owners or shareholders is secondary"*** (BAA 13 Sec. 59).

   CSEA handles both under a single institutional umbrella, and **PB No. 210** proposes a combined code. But the legal mandates are distinct --- failing to enact either one constitutes a separate unfulfilled BOL obligation.[^2][^3][^22]

**Q. How does this power interact with power (zz) Trade and Industry?**

A. Power (zz) --- ***"trade and industry"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(zz)) --- is the broader economic development power under which cooperatives are situated. Art. XIII, Sec. 24 appears within the ***"Trade and Industry and Tourism"*** subsection of the BOL, and the cooperative code mandate is embedded within a trade and industry provision: ***"It shall adopt and implement cooperative development policies and programs through the enactment of a Bangsamoro cooperative code."*** This means cooperatives are both a **standalone power** under Art. V, Sec. 2(k) and an **instrument of trade and industry** under Art. V, Sec. 2(zz). The lead agency relationship reflects this --- CSEA is attached to the **Office of the Chief Minister** (not MTIT), but the Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT) is a **supporting agency** for cooperative development. A Bangsamoro Cooperative Code should include **coordination mechanisms** between CSEA and MTIT to ensure cooperative development aligns with broader trade and industry policy.

### Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

What laws should the Bangsamoro Parliament pass to fully exercise this power? Note: most powers are already exercised by ministries/offices/agencies through their BAA 13 (Administrative Code) mandates, but standalone legislation codifies standards, creates regulatory frameworks, and establishes institutional structures that a general mandate cannot.

| Gap | What Law Should Be Passed | What It Would Cover | BOL Basis |
|-----|---------------------------|---------------------|-----------|
| No comprehensive cooperative regulatory framework despite express BOL mandate | **Bangsamoro Cooperative Code** | Registration and dissolution procedures; governance standards; capitalization requirements; financial management and audit rules; Shari'ah-compliant cooperative models (*musharakah*, *mudarabah*, *murabaha*); tax treatment and incentives; penalty provisions; dispute resolution; CSEA regulatory and quasi-judicial authority; jurisdictional boundary with CDA | Art. XIII, Sec. 24 --- ***"through the enactment of a Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** |
| No social enterprise legislation despite express BOL mandate | **Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Act** (or title within the Cooperative Code) | Definition and classification of social enterprises; registration and accreditation framework; incentives and support mechanisms; impact measurement standards; social procurement in government; Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Coalition | Art.
