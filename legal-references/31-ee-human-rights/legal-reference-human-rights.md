# (ee) Human Rights

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ee)** — ***"Human rights;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(ee) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national human rights law. Art. IX, Sec. 7 directs the BHRC to ***"cooperate and closely coordinate with the national Commission on Human Rights"*** — a coordination obligation, not a consistency mandate. The general constitutional floor (Art. X, Sec. 15 of the Constitution) applies. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC)** — created directly by Art. IX, Sec. 7 of the BOL; operationalized by BAA 4; institutional provisions in BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Ch. 1, Sec. 1-49 |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC) — coordinates with BHRC on women's human rights per BAA 13, Sec. 7(3)(e) of BWC chapter; Bangsamoro Transitional Justice and Reconciliation Commission (BTJRC) — created by BAA 89; Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office (BAGO) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 5 (Governance and Institutional Development); Chapter 10 (Peace and Security) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | The BOL mandates Parliament to enact a law providing the BHRC's ***"functions, duties, and responsibilities"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 7) — **fulfilled by BAA 4**. Art. IX, Sec. 1 mandates a ***"transitional justice mechanism"*** — **fulfilled by BAA 89**. |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(ee) enumerates ***"Human rights"*** as a power of the Bangsamoro Government, subject to Art. X, Sec. 15 of the Constitution (see the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B)). This two-word grant is among the broadest in scope — it encompasses the full spectrum of civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights, plus the institutional machinery to promote and protect them.

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**
A. The BOL elaborates through four provisions:

1. **Art. IX, Sec. 6** — a government-wide policy declaration: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall fully respect human rights."*** This binds all offices, agencies, and GOCCs, not just the BHRC.

2. **Art. IX, Sec. 7** — directly creates the BHRC with three paragraphs: (a) institutional creation and appointment mechanism (Presidential appointment upon Chief Minister recommendation); (b) legislative mandate to Parliament for the BHRC's functions; (c) salary-setting authority tied to RA 6758.

3. **Art. IX, Sec. 1** — mandates a transitional justice mechanism to address ***"historical injustices, human rights violations, and marginalization through unjust dispossession of territorial and proprietary rights and customary land tenure."***

4. **Art. IX, Sec. 2** — mandates reparation laws for unjust dispossession, including ***"payment of just compensation to and relocation of such people."***

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**
A. Yes — three legislative mandates:

1. Art. IX, Sec. 7 mandates Parliament to enact a law providing the BHRC's ***"functions, duties, and responsibilities"*** — **fulfilled by BAA 4** (Bangsamoro Human Rights Act of 2019).

2. Art. IX, Sec. 1 mandates a transitional justice mechanism — **fulfilled by BAA 89** (Bangsamoro Transitional Justice and Reconciliation Act).

3. Art. IX, Sec. 2 mandates reparation laws — **partially addressed by BAA 89**'s reparation component. A dedicated reparation act has not been enacted.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**
A. Yes — the BOL directly creates the **Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC)** in Art. IX, Sec. 7. This is significant: the BOL does not merely authorize Parliament to create it — it creates the institution itself, delegating only the operational details (functions, duties, responsibilities) to Parliament. The BHRC is one of a small number of BOL-created institutions, alongside the BTA Parliament, the Chief Minister position, and the Wali.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**
A. Five conditions:

1. **General chapeau** — Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all enumerated powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** (see the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B)).

2. **Presidential appointment** — BHRC commissioners are appointed by the President upon the Chief Minister's recommendation (Art. IX, Sec. 7) — the National Government retains the appointing power.

3. **Ethnic composition** — the Commission's composition ***"shall reflect the ethnic distribution of the population"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 7).

4. **CHR coordination** — the BHRC ***"shall cooperate and closely coordinate with the national Commission on Human Rights"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 7, para. 2).

5. **Compensation conformity** — salaries must be ***"in consonance with Republic Act No. 6758, as amended"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 7, para. 3).

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**
A. Human rights has the richest constitutional foundation of any power area:

- **Universal constraint (all 55 powers)**: Art. X, Sec. 15 — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***

- **Bill of Rights (Art. III)**: The entire article applies. Key provisions: due process and equal protection (Sec. 1); freedom from unreasonable searches (Sec. 2); privacy of communications (Sec. 3); freedom of speech/expression/assembly (Sec. 4); free exercise of religion (Sec. 5); liberty of abode/travel (Sec. 6); right to information (Sec. 7); right to association (Sec. 8); right against self-incrimination (Sec. 17); prohibition of detention for political beliefs (Sec. 18); prohibition of cruel/degrading punishment (Sec. 19); right to speedy trial (Sec. 16).

- **Commission on Human Rights (Art. XIII, Sec. 17-19)**: The Constitution creates the national CHR as an independent office. The BHRC operates alongside — not subordinate to — the CHR, with a coordination duty under the BOL.

- **State Policies (Art. II, Sec. 11)**: ***"The State values the dignity of every human person and guarantees full respect for human rights."***

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**
A. The Constitution guarantees the full spectrum of civil and political rights through Art. III (Bill of Rights), economic and social rights through Art. XIII (Social Justice and Human Rights), and creates the institutional machinery for enforcement through Art. XIII, Sec. 17-19 (CHR). The BOL's Art. IV, Sec. 1 expressly incorporates the entire Bill of Rights into the Bangsamoro framework. This means the BHRC must protect all rights at a standard no lower than the national constitutional floor.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 17 creates the CHR as a constitutional body. Art. XIII, Sec. 19 authorizes Congress to expand the CHR's jurisdiction to other cases of human rights violations. The BHRC is not constitutionally created — it is BOL-created — but it exercises functions parallel to the CHR's constitutional mandate within BARMM's territorial jurisdiction. This parallel structure is authorized by the BOL's organic law status under Art. X, Sec. 15.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**
A. The national framework consists of: (1) the **CHR** as the constitutional human rights institution with investigatory and recommendatory powers; (2) a suite of **penal statutes** criminalizing specific human rights violations — RA 9745 (Anti-Torture), RA 10353 (Anti-Enforced Disappearance), RA 9851 (Crimes Against International Humanitarian Law), RA 9262 (Anti-VAWC), RA 7610 (Child Protection), RA 9208 (Anti-Trafficking); (3) **remedial mechanisms** — the Writs of *Amparo*, *Habeas Data*, and *Habeas Corpus*; and (4) a **witness protection program** under RA 6981.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**
A. All national human rights laws apply **directly** in BARMM. These are penal statutes of national application — they are not superseded by any BAA. BAA 4 and BAA 13 create BARMM-specific institutional mechanisms (BHRC investigation, monitoring, preventive measures, witness protection) that **complement** the national framework but do not replace the criminal jurisdiction of national courts over human rights crimes.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**
A. The **Commission on Human Rights (CHR)** retains full constitutional jurisdiction throughout the Philippines, including BARMM. National **courts** (Regional Trial Courts, Court of Appeals, Supreme Court) retain criminal jurisdiction over human rights crimes under RA 9745, RA 10353, RA 9851, and other penal statutes. The **Department of Justice** retains prosecutorial authority. The BHRC's mandate is investigatory and recommendatory — it does not exercise adjudicatory power. BAA 4 and BAA 13 provide for joint investigations between the BHRC and CHR where violations occur outside BARMM involving Bangsamoro people, or within BARMM where the violation is of national complexion.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**
A. The **Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC)** is the lead institution. It is not a ministry — it is an independent commission with guaranteed fiscal autonomy. BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Chapter 1, Sec. 1-49 provides its organizational framework. BAA 85, Chapter XXVIII provides its operational budget.

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**
A. Three standalone BAAs:

1. **BAA 4** (Bangsamoro Human Rights Act of 2019) — operationalizes the BHRC.
2. **BAA 62** (Rights of Internally Displaced Persons Act) — protects IDP human rights.
3. **BAA 89** (Bangsamoro Transitional Justice and Reconciliation Act) — creates the BTJRC for historical human rights violations.

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Chapter 1 details the BHRC's full mandate, organized as follows:

**Mandate** (Sec. 3): ***"As the human rights institution of the Bangsamoro, the BHRC shall promote and protect human rights, and during armed conflict, uphold international humanitarian law."***

**Independence** (Sec. 4): ***"The BHRC's independence is guaranteed. It shall not be subject to interference, directly or indirectly, from any government or private entity."***

**Fiscal Autonomy** (Sec. 5): Guaranteed. Appropriations ***"shall not be reduced from the amount it received in the preceding fiscal year."***

**Composition** (Sec. 6): Chairperson and two (2) Commissioners.

**Organizational Structure** (Sec. 12): Commission Proper; Office of the Chairperson; Office of the Secretariat; Provincial Director's Offices; and such other offices as required.

**Powers and Functions** (Sec. 20, enumerated):
- (a) Promote human rights through education, training, public awareness, and publications
- (b) Protect human rights through investigations, monitoring, fact-finding missions, public inquiries, reporting, and prosecution referral
- (c) Advise the government on human rights and international humanitarian law
- (d) Protect human rights defenders
- (e) Act as the transitional justice mechanism (pending BTJRC operationalization per BAA 89)
- (f) Administrative functions — organizational structure, strategic plans, programs, monitoring, manual of operations
- (g) Approve core services
- (h) Conduct performance audits
- (i) Accept grants, aid, and donations
- (j) Acquire and dispose of property
- (k) Other powers as provided by law

**Special Mandates**:
- **Gender and Development Ombud** (Sec. 21) — promotes and protects women's rights
- **Children Ombud** (Sec. 22) — ensures children's rights are upheld

**Investigation Powers** (Sec. 27-28): Investigate upon complaint or *motu proprio*; administer oaths; issue compulsory subpoena; cite for contempt; petition for Writs of *Amparo*, *Habeas Data*, *Habeas Corpus*, *Mandamus*; issue preventive and mandatory measures; deputize lawyers and medical organizations.

**Preventive and Mandatory Measures** (Sec. 30): Preventive Protection Order, Preventive Transfer Order, Preventive Desistance Order, Mandatory Protection Order, Mandatory Access Order, Mandatory Transfer Order.

**Monitoring** (Sec. 34): Policy monitoring, thematic monitoring, sectoral monitoring, incident monitoring, progressive realization monitoring, and facility monitoring (detention centers, jails, military camps).

**Reporting** (Sec. 37): Annual reports, public inquiry reports, financial reports, special reports. Reports go to the President, Chief Minister, and Parliament.

**International Engagement** (Sec. 38): Active coordination with national CHR for reporting to international human rights mechanisms (Human Rights Council, treaty body committees).

**Witness Protection** (Sec. 33): Manages its own witness protection program — security, shelter, relocation, and financial assistance.

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**
A. BAA 4 and BAA 13 together provide the BHRC with comprehensive powers. However, the BHRC **cannot**: (1) exercise criminal prosecution or adjudicatory power — this remains with courts, as confirmed by jurisprudence (*Simon v. CHR*, *Cariño v. CHR*); (2) compel the national government to implement its recommendations — the "obligation to respond" (BAA 13, Sec. 40) creates a duty to respond, not a duty to comply; (3) operate the BTJRC's functions once that commission is fully operationalized — BHRC served as the interim transitional justice mechanism under BAA 4, Sec. 43, but BAA 89 now creates a separate BTJRC.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**
A. Yes — multiple resolutions as detailed in the Resolutions table above, including Res. No. 56 and 418 urging Congress to create a National TJRC, and numerous condemnation resolutions exercising the Parliament's human rights monitoring function through formal declarations.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**
A. Yes — the BHRC is the correct and only appropriate institution. Unlike other powers where a ministry might handle the function, human rights requires an **independent commission** by constitutional design. The BHRC is modeled on the national CHR (Art. XIII, Sec. 17 of the Constitution) with independence and fiscal autonomy guarantees. No ministry could properly serve as lead for this power because the BHRC must investigate government entities themselves — a function incompatible with ministerial subordination to the Chief Minister.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**
A.

- **Lead institution**: BHRC — BAA 4, BAA 13 Book VI Title V Ch. 1, BAA 85 Ch. XXVIII. Operational.
- **Supporting commission**: BWC — coordinates with BHRC on women's human rights per BAA 13 BWC chapter, Sec. 7(3)(e). Operational.
- **Transitional justice**: BTJRC — created by BAA 89. Operationalization pending.
- **Legal services**: BAGO — provides legal support relevant to human rights cases. Operational.
- **IDP protection**: No dedicated institutional body — BAA 62 mandates IDP protection through existing agencies, with the BHRC playing a monitoring role.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**
A. All BOL-mandated human rights institutions have been created:
- BHRC — created by BAA 4 (per Art. IX, Sec. 7). **Operational.**
- Transitional justice mechanism — created by BAA 89 (per Art. IX, Sec. 1). BTJRC created but **operationalization pending.**

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**
A. Potentially:
- A **Human Rights Defenders Protection Mechanism** — while BAA 4 addresses this through BHRC functions, PB 66 proposes a standalone framework. A dedicated protection mechanism with its own secretariat could strengthen protections.
- A **National-Bangsamoro Joint Human Rights Monitoring Body** — to formalize the BHRC-CHR coordination mandate, particularly for cases involving Bangsamoro people outside BARMM.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**
A. The **CHR** maintains regional presence and investigatory jurisdiction throughout BARMM. The **Department of Justice** handles criminal prosecution for human rights crimes. The **Philippine National Police (PNP)** and **Armed Forces of the Philippines (AFP)** are subject to human rights monitoring by both CHR and BHRC. National courts hear human rights cases. The CHR's constitutional mandate (Art. XIII, Sec. 17-18) is not diminished by the BHRC's creation.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**
A. CHR investigation and monitoring services; DOJ prosecution; the national Witness Protection Program (RA 6981); the Human Rights Victims Claims Board (RA 10368, for Marcos-era claims); and training programs on human rights for law enforcement and military personnel.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**
A. Human rights does not follow the typical devolution model. The CHR retains full constitutional jurisdiction — it is not devolved. Instead, the BHRC operates as a **parallel institution** within BARMM, with a coordination (not subordination) relationship with the CHR per Art. IX, Sec. 7. This dual-institution model is unique among the 55 powers.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**
A. Yes — the BHRC and CHR have overlapping investigatory jurisdiction within BARMM. BAA 13, Sec. 27 addresses this: the BHRC and CHR ***"may agree to conduct joint investigations on human rights violations and abuses that occurred either outside the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region when the subject of the investigation involves Bangsamoro, or within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region when the subject of the investigation is of national complexion."*** This is concurrent-by-design, not a gap.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**
A. Significant legislative achievements: (1) BAA 4 was the **fourth law ever enacted** by the BTA Parliament — demonstrating the priority placed on human rights; (2) BAA 62 provides IDP protection; (3) BAA 89 establishes the transitional justice framework. The BHRC has been operationalized with commissioners appointed, provincial offices being established, and initial investigation and monitoring activities underway. The BHRC serves triple functions as the human rights commission, Gender and Development Ombud, and Children Ombud.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**
A. Three areas of strength:
- **Conflict-sensitive design**: The BHRC is purpose-built for a post-conflict context — its mandate includes international humanitarian law, transitional justice, and IDP protection, which the national CHR addresses but not with the same regional specificity.
- **Integrated protection mandates**: The BHRC's triple role (human rights, GAD Ombud, Children Ombud) consolidates gender and children's rights monitoring under one institution — the national system splits these across CHR, the Philippine Commission on Women, and the Council for the Welfare of Children.
- **Preventive and mandatory measures**: BAA 4/BAA 13 grants the BHRC six types of protective orders (Preventive Protection, Transfer, and Desistance Orders; Mandatory Protection, Access, and Transfer Orders) — a more specific toolkit than the CHR's general investigatory powers.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**
A. The **single biggest constraint** is institutional capacity. The BHRC has the legal framework (BAA 4, BAA 13) but needs: (1) full staffing of provincial offices across five provinces; (2) trained investigation teams for complex human rights cases; (3) operational systems for monitoring, documentation, and reporting; (4) funding to operationalize the witness protection program. The BTJRC created by BAA 89 also requires operationalization — commissioners must be appointed and the commission staffed.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**
A. The **CHR** has operated since 1987 — nearly four decades of institutional development including: national investigation infrastructure, regional offices with trained investigators, established relationships with international human rights mechanisms (UN treaty bodies, special procedures), comprehensive monitoring databases, and a track record of high-profile investigations. The national **criminal justice system** has decades of jurisprudence on human rights crimes. The national **witness protection program** (RA 6981) has established infrastructure and processes.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**
A. Three areas: (1) the national system does not adequately address **conflict-related human rights violations** specific to the Bangsamoro experience — decades of armed conflict, displacement, and historical injustices require a specialized mechanism, which is why BAA 89 was needed; (2) the CHR's presence in BARMM has historically been **limited relative to the severity of violations** in the region; (3) the national framework does not provide **Shari'ah-informed** human rights approaches that integrate Islamic principles of justice and dignity with international human rights standards.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**
A.
- **Replicate**: CHR's investigation methodology, monitoring protocols, and international engagement practices
- **Adapt**: The national witness protection program model — BARMM's version (BAA 13, Sec. 33) should account for the security dynamics of conflict-affected areas
- **Replace**: None — human rights standards are universal. The BHRC should **add** what the national system lacks (transitional justice, IDP-specific monitoring, Shari'ah-informed approaches) rather than replace existing protections.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**
A. Yes — human rights in the Bangsamoro context has a significant Islamic law dimension. Islam provides its own framework of human rights derived from the Qur'an and Sunnah, centering on the *Maqasid al-Shari'ah* (objectives of Islamic law): preservation of life, faith, intellect, lineage, and property. These objectives align with — and in some cases predate — international human rights standards. The Bangsamoro's unique position as an autonomous region with a predominantly Muslim population means its human rights framework should integrate both international and Islamic principles.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**
A. The BOL does not expressly reference Shari'ah in Art. IX, Sec. 6-7 (human rights provisions). However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah laws covering personal, family, property, commercial, and criminal matters — areas that directly intersect with human rights (family rights, property rights, criminal justice). BAA 62 (IDP Act) explicitly references Shari'ah principles, including the ***"preservation of life, intellect, faith, posterity, and property"*** as guiding its IDP protection framework.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**
A.
- ***Maqasid al-Shari'ah*** — the five essential objectives provide the Islamic jurisprudential basis for human rights protection, corresponding to: right to life (*hifz al-nafs*), freedom of thought (*hifz al-'aql*), freedom of religion (*hifz al-din*), family rights (*hifz al-nasl*), and property rights (*hifz al-mal*)
- ***'Adl*** (justice) — the paramount duty to establish justice, including accountability for those who violate rights
- ***Amanah*** (trust) — public office as a trust, creating accountability obligations
- ***Shura*** (consultation) — the requirement for inclusive, participatory governance processes
- ***Sulh*** (reconciliation) and ***Islah*** (reform/restoration) — relevant to the transitional justice framework under BAA 89

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**
A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) governs personal status, marriage, divorce, succession, and property for Muslims — areas where human rights intersections arise (women's rights, children's rights, property rights). Shari'ah courts have jurisdiction over these matters, and the BHRC's GAD Ombud function requires awareness of how personal law intersects with human rights standards. No specific *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* on human rights have been identified. [UNVERIFIED]

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**
A. The integration should be **principled** — human rights legislation should be guided by Islamic values (*maslahah*, *'adl*, *amanah*) without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance for human rights protection mechanisms. Human rights are universal, and the BHRC's mandate applies to all persons within BARMM regardless of religion. The Shari'ah dimension adds depth to the framework — particularly for reconciliation and restoration mechanisms under BAA 89 — but should not create parallel or reduced standards for any group.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**
A. BARMM's divergence capacity in human rights is **broad — upward divergence with no express national law consistency requirement**. Synthesizing from **Themes A-E**:

1. **Constitutional floor** (Section B (Constitutional Considerations)): The Bill of Rights applies fully. The BHRC cannot adopt standards *lower* than the constitutional guarantees.

2. **No express consistency requirement** (Section A (BOL Grant and Scope)): Unlike civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39) or labor (Art. IX, Sec. 10), the BOL does not require human rights legislation to be "consistent with" or "in accordance with" national law. The BHRC has a coordination duty with the CHR, not a subordination relationship.

3. **Upward divergence**: BARMM can adopt **higher** human rights standards than the national framework. BAA 4 does this in several ways: (a) the BHRC has express mandate over international humanitarian law, which the CHR's constitutional mandate does not specifically include; (b) the BHRC has six types of protective orders not available to the CHR; (c) the BHRC has express human rights defender protection functions.

4. **Shari'ah dimension** (Section E (Shari'ah Considerations)): The integration of *Maqasid al-Shari'ah* principles — particularly through BAA 62's explicit reference — creates a distinctively Bangsamoro human rights approach that enriches rather than diminishes the universal framework.

5. **Transitional justice divergence**: BAA 89 creates a transitional justice framework that has no national counterpart — the BTA Parliament has repeatedly called for Congress to create a national TJRC (Res. No. 56, 418). BARMM leads the national government in this area.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**
A. Rights holders **gain** additional protections under the dual-institution model — both the BHRC and CHR have jurisdiction. The joint investigation mechanism (BAA 13, Sec. 27) addresses cross-boundary cases involving Bangsamoro people outside BARMM. The practical implication is that a person within BARMM can seek redress from either the BHRC or CHR — the institutions cooperate rather than compete.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**
A. National human rights penal statutes (RA 9745, RA 10353, RA 9851) continue to apply directly — they define crimes and penalties under national criminal jurisdiction. BAA 4 and BAA 13 create complementary investigatory and monitoring mechanisms, not competing criminal law frameworks. The BHRC's mandate is **additive**: it adds a BARMM-specific institution without displacing the national framework.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**
A. The BHRC-CHR relationship is the primary jurisdictional question. BAA 13, Sec. 27 addresses this through the joint investigation mechanism, but the operational parameters need formalization: (1) when does a violation within BARMM become "of national complexion" requiring CHR involvement? (2) who leads investigations when both institutions are involved? (3) how are investigation findings and recommendations reconciled if they differ? An **interagency MOA** between BHRC and CHR, or a formal protocol adopted by both bodies, would resolve these ambiguities without requiring legislation.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**
A. Two pending bills address identified gaps:

1. **PB 66** (Human Rights Defenders Protection Act) — would create a standalone framework for defending human rights defenders, going beyond BAA 4's general provisions. If enacted, it would give BARMM one of the most comprehensive human rights defender protection frameworks in the Philippines.

2. **PB 141** (Resettlement and Rehabilitation Framework for IDPs) — would create a Resettlement and Rehabilitation Development Council, providing operational mechanisms beyond BAA 62's rights-based framework. This addresses the implementation gap between rights declaration and practical resettlement.

Even if both pass, a gap remains: there is no **standalone anti-discrimination act** for BARMM that would comprehensively address discrimination based on ethnicity, religion, gender, sexual orientation, disability, or other grounds.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**
A. **BDP Chapter 5** (Governance and Institutional Development) targets rule of law and human rights protection as foundational governance indicators. **BDP Chapter 10** (Peace and Security) centers human rights, transitional justice, and IDP protection as mechanisms for sustainable peace. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** Pillar 1 (Matatag na Kapayapaan/Enduring Peace) depends on the BTJRC's operationalization and the BHRC's capacity to monitor and protect rights in the transition to regular governance.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**
A. Two outcomes are at risk: (1) **BTJRC operationalization delay** — BAA 89 creates the legal framework, but without operational funding and commissioner appointments, the transitional justice mechanism cannot fulfill its mandate before the 2028 transition to elected government. Historical injustices remain unaddressed, undermining peace; (2) **Human rights defender safety** — without PB 66 or an equivalent framework, human rights defenders in BARMM operate with BHRC-mediated protection but no standalone legal protections. In a post-conflict environment with ongoing security challenges, this gap creates real risk.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the relationship between the BHRC and the national CHR?**
A. The relationship is **cooperative and coordinate**, not hierarchical. The BOL uses the language ***"cooperate and closely coordinate"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 7) — not "subject to," "under the supervision of," or "in accordance with." Key parameters:

1. The CHR retains full constitutional jurisdiction within BARMM (Art. XIII, Sec. 17-18 of the Constitution).
2. The BHRC exercises BOL-granted jurisdiction within BARMM.
3. Joint investigations are authorized for: (a) violations outside BARMM involving Bangsamoro people; (b) violations within BARMM of national complexion (BAA 13, Sec. 27).
4. The BHRC coordinates with the CHR for international human rights reporting (BAA 13, Sec. 38).
5. Neither institution is subordinate to the other.

**Q. Why is the BHRC's direct creation by the BOL significant?**
A. Most BOL powers are exercised through ministries and offices created by Parliament through legislation. The BHRC is one of a handful of institutions the BOL itself creates, alongside the BTA Parliament, the Chief Minister, and the Wali. This means: (1) the BHRC's existence does not depend on parliamentary action — it exists by force of the organic law; (2) Parliament cannot abolish the BHRC — it can only modify its functions; (3) the institution has **organic-law-level** protection, placing it above ordinary legislation in the legal hierarchy. BAA 4 implements the BHRC, but the institution's existence is anchored in RA 11054 itself.

**Q. What are the BHRC's preventive and mandatory measures, and how do they compare to the CHR's powers?**
A. BAA 13, Sec. 30 grants the BHRC six types of orders, all incident to investigation:

**Preventive measures**: (1) Preventive Protection Order — directing respondent to refrain from acts causing irreparable harm; (2) Preventive Transfer Order — directing government to desist from hiding, transferring, torturing, or abusing victims; (3) Preventive Desistance Order — prohibiting respondent from entering the vicinity of affected areas.

**Mandatory measures**: (4) Mandatory Protection Order — directing government to provide specific protection to victims and witnesses; (5) Mandatory Access Order — directing government to allow access to persons deprived of liberty by counsel, physicians, spiritual advisers, and relatives; (6) Mandatory Transfer Order — directing government to transfer detained persons in danger of reprisal.

The national CHR has no comparable express statutory authority to issue these specific types of orders. This represents an area where BARMM's human rights framework **exceeds** the national standard.

**Q. How does the BHRC's transitional justice role interact with BAA 89's BTJRC?**
A. BAA 4, Sec. 43 assigned the BHRC as the **interim transitional justice mechanism** — ***"Until such time that a law establishing the transitional justice mechanism for the Bangsamoro is enacted, the Commission shall perform the human rights component of the transitional justice mechanism."*** BAA 89 now creates the BTJRC as the permanent mechanism. Under BAA 4, Sec. 43, the BHRC must ***"make its terminal report and transfer the documents and other data on transitional justice"*** to the BTJRC once it is operationalized. The BHRC retains its general human rights mandate but hands over the transitional justice function to the BTJRC.

**Q. What makes human rights unique among the 55 enumerated powers?**
A. Human rights is unique in four ways: (1) it is the only power where the BOL directly creates the implementing institution (BHRC) rather than delegating creation to Parliament; (2) it involves **Presidential appointment** of commissioners — one of the few BOL provisions where the National Government retains direct institutional control within BARMM; (3) it creates a **dual-institution model** with both BHRC and CHR having jurisdiction — rather than the typical devolution pattern of national-to-regional transfer; and (4) it has the richest **enacted legislation** of any power — three standalone BAAs (BAA 4, BAA 62, BAA 89), the Administrative Code chapter (BAA 13), and budget allocation (BAA 85).
