> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ee)** — "Human rights;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over human rights through Article V, Section 2(ee). Article IX, Section 6 declares that "The Bangsamoro Government shall fully respect human rights." Article IX, Section 7 creates the Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC), specifying that the chairperson and two commissioners shall be appointed by the President upon recommendation of the Chief Minister. The composition must reflect the ethnic distribution of the Bangsamoro population. The chairperson must be a member of the Philippine Bar and a resident of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The BOL mandates the Parliament to define the BHRC's functions, duties, and responsibilities by law, and requires the BHRC to "cooperate and closely coordinate with the national Commission on Human Rights."[^1]

This power also extends into transitional justice. Article IX, Section 1 mandates the Bangsamoro Parliament, "taking into account the report of the Transitional Justice and Reconciliation Commission," to enact a transitional justice mechanism addressing "the legitimate grievances of the Bangsamoro people and the indigenous peoples, such as historical injustices, human rights violations, and marginalization through unjust dispossession of territorial and proprietary rights and customary land tenure." Article IX, Section 2 further requires Parliament to enact laws providing for "adequate reparation" to those affected by unjust dispossession, including just compensation and relocation. The human rights power thus encompasses both ongoing rights monitoring and enforcement and the redress of historical human rights violations through transitional justice.[^2]

The scope of this power also intersects with the protection of internally displaced persons, women's rights, children's rights, and indigenous peoples' rights, each of which has additional BOL provisions. The BOL's human rights framework is not merely about creating an institution — it encompasses a comprehensive mandate to address both contemporary human rights concerns and the legacy of decades of armed conflict and marginalization.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

BAA No. 4 created the Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC), fulfilling the Article IX, Section 7 mandate. The BHRC has its own chapter in BAA No. 85 (Chapter XXVIII) with dedicated appropriations and staffing. It operates as the primary human rights monitoring and advocacy body within the Bangsamoro, coordinating with the national Commission on Human Rights. BAA 4 defines the BHRC's powers, functions, and duties, including investigation of human rights violations, policy advocacy, and public education on human rights.[^3]

BAA No. 62 (Rights of Internally Displaced Persons) implements a significant dimension of the human rights power by establishing legal protections for IDPs in BARMM, grounded in international humanitarian law and the BOL's humanitarian services mandate. BAA No. 89 (Transitional Justice and Reconciliation), enacted in 2025, creates the Bangsamoro Transitional Justice and Reconciliation Commission (BTJRC), fulfilling the Article IX, Section 1 mandate. The BTJRC addresses historical injustices, human rights violations, and marginalization through a comprehensive transitional justice mechanism that considers both formal and traditional approaches to justice and reconciliation.[^4]

Together, BAAs 4, 62, and 89 form a three-part human rights legislative framework: the BHRC monitors ongoing rights conditions, BAA 62 protects displaced populations, and BAA 89 addresses historical violations. The BAGO (BAA 5) provides legal services that support human rights enforcement.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not require this power to be exercised "consistent with" national law. However, it establishes a cooperative relationship: the BHRC must "cooperate and closely coordinate with the national Commission on Human Rights" (Art. IX, Sec. 7). This is a coordination mandate, not a subordination requirement — the BHRC operates as a Bangsamoro institution with its own mandate, not as a regional office of the national CHR. The salaries of the BHRC chairperson and commissioners are fixed by the Parliament "in consonance with Republic Act No. 6758, as amended" (the compensation standardization law), which is the only national law consistency requirement specific to the BHRC.[^5]

The Philippines is a party to major international human rights instruments — the ICCPR, ICESCR, CEDAW, CRC, CAT, and CRPD — and these commitments apply throughout the national territory including BARMM. The national CHR continues to operate in BARMM alongside the BHRC. No intergovernmental coordination body exists specifically for human rights, though the general Intergovernmental Relations Body under Article VI, Section 2 could address overlapping mandates.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

While the institutional framework is relatively well-developed (BHRC, IDP protections, and transitional justice commission all exist through enacted BAAs), several gaps remain. First, no standalone Bangsamoro Human Rights Code exists that consolidates all human rights protections — civil, political, economic, social, and cultural — into a single comprehensive framework. Such a code could establish: a regional bill of rights with enforcement mechanisms specific to the Bangsamoro context; standards for human rights impact assessment of all proposed legislation; mandatory human rights training for all Bangsamoro government personnel; and community-level human rights monitoring systems.[^6]

Second, the interaction between the BHRC and the national CHR lacks a formal memorandum of agreement or legislative framework defining their respective jurisdictions, referral mechanisms, and shared responsibilities. While the BOL mandates "cooperation and close coordination," the actual mechanism for this coordination is not codified, creating potential gaps in coverage or duplication of effort.

Third, while BAA 89 establishes the BTJRC, the reparation mechanism mandated by Article IX, Section 2 — specifically laws providing for "adequate reparation" including "just compensation" and "relocation" — may require additional implementing legislation once the BTJRC completes its work and makes recommendations. The reparation framework involves complex questions of eligibility, funding, and administration that standalone legislation would need to address.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 5 (Governance and Institutional Development) includes human rights as a core governance pillar, and Chapter 10 (Peace and Security) targets transitional justice and reconciliation as prerequisites for lasting peace. The BHRC's monitoring function (BAA 4), IDP protections (BAA 62), and transitional justice mechanism (BAA 89) directly serve these BDP goals. However, the absence of a comprehensive human rights code and formalized BHRC-CHR coordination mechanism means that human rights mainstreaming across all government programs remains dependent on institutional goodwill rather than legislative mandate.

## Legislative Gaps

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type B**: Partial implementation | BAA 4 creates the BHRC but no comprehensive Bangsamoro Human Rights Code consolidates all civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights protections with enforcement mechanisms | Enact a Bangsamoro Human Rights Code establishing a regional bill of rights, human rights impact assessment standards, and community-level monitoring |
| **Type B**: Partial implementation | Art. IX, Sec. 2 mandates reparation legislation for unjust dispossession. BAA 89 creates the BTJRC but reparation implementation requires additional legislation once BTJRC recommendations are issued | Draft implementing legislation for the BTJRC reparation framework upon receipt of commission recommendations |
| **Type C**: National law interaction | BHRC-CHR coordination mechanism is mandated by BOL but not formalized through legislation or binding agreement; potential gaps in jurisdiction | Formalize BHRC-CHR coordination through a joint memorandum or legislative framework defining referral procedures and shared responsibilities |
