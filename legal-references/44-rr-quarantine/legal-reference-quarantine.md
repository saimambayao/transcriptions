# (rr) Quarantine Regulations

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(rr)** — ***"Quarantine regulations;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(rr) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3) mandates ***"assist and cooperate with the National Government in the provision of quarantine services."*** This is an intergovernmental cooperation mandate, not an independent exercise of authority. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Health (MOH)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Sec. 3(d): ***"Administer all laws, rules and regulations in the field of health, including quarantine laws and food and drug safety laws."*** MOH organizational structure includes a dedicated **Quarantine Division** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Sec. 9(6)). |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bureau of Quarantine (national — DOH); Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR — animal/plant quarantine); Bureau of Customs (port clearance); Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG — LGU quarantine measures) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 9 (Social Development — Health subsector); Chapter 7 (Economy — agri-fishery quarantine, Strategy 4) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None — Art. IX, Sec. 22 mandates "appropriate legislation" for health generally but does not name a standalone quarantine code. Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3) establishes the cooperation mandate. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant -> constitutional floor -> national law -> implementation -> Shari'ah dimension -> divergence -> gaps -> development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in the area of quarantine regulations?**

A. Art. V, Sec. 2(rr) enumerates ***"Quarantine regulations"*** as one of the 55 powers of the Bangsamoro Government. However, unlike most other enumerated powers, this power is immediately qualified by Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3), which provides: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall assist and cooperate with the National Government in the provision of quarantine services for the prevention and control of epidemic and other communicable diseases."*** The operative language is **"assist and cooperate"** — positioning the Bangsamoro Government as a **supporting partner** to the National Government, not an independent quarantine authority. This is one of the most explicitly cooperative powers in the BOL.[^1][^2]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Art. IX, Sec. 22 is the primary elaborating provision. Its three paragraphs address the full health mandate, of which quarantine is one component:

- **Paragraph 1** establishes the overarching health policy directive and mandates a **general hospital system** established ***"by law"*** — this provides the institutional infrastructure (hospitals, isolation facilities) required for quarantine response.[^2]
- **Paragraph 2** mandates the Parliament to ***"enact appropriate legislation"*** for health — including the regulation of food, drugs, and tobacco ***"in accordance with the policies, rules and regulations of the concerned national government agencies."*** This establishes the pattern of **national conformity** for health regulation that extends to quarantine.[^2]
- **Paragraph 3** is the quarantine-specific provision establishing the ***"assist and cooperate"*** framework.[^2]

Additionally, Art. IX, Sec. 8 obligates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"provide, maintain, and ensure the delivery of basic and responsive health programs"*** — quarantine-related services fall within this obligation. Art. XII, Sec. 19 mandates the ***"highest budgetary priority"*** for health, which includes quarantine operations. Art. VI, Sec. 2 creates the **Intergovernmental Relations Body (IGRB)** as the institutional platform for the cooperative quarantine relationship.[^3][^4][^5]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for quarantine?**

A. No express mandate names a quarantine act or code. Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 2) mandates ***"appropriate legislation"*** for health generally, which encompasses quarantine. But unlike the express mandate to ***"establish by law a general hospital system"*** in paragraph 1, there is no directive stating "the Parliament shall enact a quarantine code." The legislative authority to enact quarantine legislation derives from the general health legislative mandate and the enumerated power under Art. V, Sec. 2(rr).[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for quarantine?**

A. No. The BOL does not create a quarantine bureau, office, or division. The institutional arrangement is left to implementing legislation and the Administrative Code. BAA 13 subsequently created the **Quarantine Division** within MOH (Book VI, Title V, Sec. 9(6)) and assigned quarantine law administration to the MOH's general functions (Sec. 3(d)).[^6]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The primary qualification is Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3): ***"shall assist and cooperate with the National Government."*** This is not merely a coordination requirement — it positions the National Government as the **lead authority** for quarantine services. The Bangsamoro Government's role is assistive and cooperative, not primary. This is distinct from powers where the BOL says "consistent with national laws" (which implies BARMM leads but must conform) or where the BOL is silent (which implies BARMM has broad authority). The "assist and cooperate" formulation is the **strongest form of cooperative limitation** in the BOL, short of the "shall prevail" supremacy clause found in civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39).[^2]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to quarantine regulations?**

A. The following constitutional provisions apply:

- **Art. X, Sec. 15** (universal constraint) — Autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This is the overarching limit on all Bangsamoro legislation, including quarantine.[^7]
- **Art. II, Sec. 15** (state policy) — ***"The State shall protect and promote the right to health of the people and instill health consciousness among them."*** Quarantine is a direct instrument for fulfilling this constitutional mandate.[^7]
- **Art. III, Sec. 1** (due process) — Quarantine measures — detention, isolation, movement restriction — engage **liberty interests**. Due process requires that quarantine orders be based on reasonable grounds, proportional to the threat, and subject to judicial review.[^7]
- **Art. III, Sec. 6** (liberty of abode and travel) — The right to travel and choose one's residence may be ***"impaired... in the interest of national security, public safety, or public health, as may be provided by law."*** Quarantine legislation satisfies the "public health" exception — but only when **provided by law** (not by executive order alone).[^7]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees: (1) the **right to health** (Art. II, Sec. 15); (2) **due process** before liberty is restricted (Art. III, Sec. 1) — quarantine detention must follow lawful procedures; (3) the **right to travel** subject to the public health exception (Art. III, Sec. 6); (4) **equal protection** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — quarantine measures cannot discriminate by ethnicity, religion, or political status. All quarantine legislation — whether national or Bangsamoro — must satisfy these guarantees.[^7]

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. II, Sec. 15 mandates the State to protect public health. This constitutional directive is implemented through national quarantine legislation (RA 9271, RA 11332) and carries constitutional weight — meaning these national laws implement a constitutional mandate, not merely a statutory one. When the BOL directs the Bangsamoro Government to ***"assist and cooperate"*** with the National Government in quarantine, it is channeling the Bangsamoro Government's participation in fulfilling this constitutional health mandate.[^7][^2]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for quarantine?**

A. The national framework is anchored in **RA 9271 (Quarantine Act of 2004)**, which establishes the **Bureau of Quarantine (BOQ)** under the DOH with a ***"nationwide scope of function and international commitment in accord with the International Health Regulations (IHR) of the World Health Organization (WHO)."*** The BOQ exercises: examination of ports of entry/exit; surveillance over vessels, aircraft, cargoes, passengers, and crews; issuance of quarantine clearances (Pratique); authority to promulgate rules on disease prevention; power to order detention, isolation, and surveillance; prohibition of hazardous cargo entry; and management of quarantine stations. Penalties range from P10,000 to P500,000.[^8]

**RA 11332 (2019)** complements the quarantine framework by establishing the **national disease surveillance and response system** — mandatory reporting of notifiable diseases, epidemics, and health events of public health concern. It defines "public health authorities" to include the DOH Bureau of Quarantine, Epidemiology Bureau, and regional health offices.[^9]

The **Local Government Code (RA 7160)** grants LGUs the power to ***"approve measures and adopt quarantine regulations to prevent the introduction and spread of diseases within its territorial jurisdiction"*** — providing a local-level quarantine authority that operates alongside the national framework.[^10]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. **RA 9271** applies **directly** — the Bureau of Quarantine exercises nationwide jurisdiction including at BARMM ports of entry. **RA 11332** applies **directly** as a law of general application implementing a constitutional mandate — BARMM health facilities must comply with mandatory disease reporting. **RA 7160** applies **suppletorily** for LGU-level quarantine measures. During the COVID-19 pandemic, **RA 11469** (Bayanihan to Heal as One Act) and **RA 11525** (COVID-19 Vaccination Program Act) applied directly in BARMM, with the BTA explicitly invoking both the BOL cooperation mandate and the Bayanihan Act as legal bases for its pandemic response.[^8][^9][^10][^11]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for quarantine?**

A. The **Bureau of Quarantine (BOQ)** under the DOH exercises quarantine authority at BARMM ports. The BOQ operates quarantine stations at seaports and airports — including Cotabato Airport and the seaports of Polloc, Jolo, and Zamboanga (which serves as the gateway for much BARMM-bound traffic). The **DOH Epidemiology Bureau** manages the national disease surveillance system that BARMM health units feed into. The **DOH Regional Office** (formerly DOH-ARMM) coordinates national health programs within the region. Unlike other health functions that have been devolved to BARMM, **international quarantine remains a national function** because it involves foreign relations and treaty obligations (WHO IHR) that fall outside autonomous region authority.[^8]

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Health (MOH)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Sec. 2 defines the MOH mandate: ***"The Ministry shall be primarily responsible for the formulation, planning, implementation and coordination of policies and programs for health."*** Sec. 3(d) expressly assigns quarantine: ***"Administer all laws, rules and regulations in the field of health, including quarantine laws and food and drug safety laws."***[^6]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for quarantine?**

A. None. No standalone quarantine BAA has been enacted. The quarantine function is exercised through MOH's general mandate under BAA 13.[^6]

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title V, Sec. 3 enumerates 12 powers and functions of the MOH. Those directly relevant to quarantine include:

- Sec. 3(a): ***"Define the health policy and formulate and implement a Bangsamoro health plan within the framework of the Bangsamoro Government's general policies and plans"***
- Sec. 3(c): ***"Coordinate or collaborate with, and assist local communities, agencies and interested groups including international organizations in activities related to health"***
- Sec. 3(d): ***"Administer all laws, rules and regulations in the field of health, including quarantine laws and food and drug safety laws"***
- Sec. 3(e): ***"Collect, analyze and disseminate statistical and other relevant information on the Bangsamoro health situation, and require the reporting of such information from appropriate sources"***
- Sec. 3(i): ***"Issue orders and regulations concerning the implementation of established health policies"***
- Sec. 3(j): ***"Formulate policies, guidelines, regulations as well as operating procedures congruent to Islamic teachings on health and sanitation"***
- Sec. 3(k): ***"Exercise control and supervision over Provincial Health Offices within the region, except in local government units where health services have been previously devolved"***

The MOH organizational structure (Sec. 9) includes a dedicated **Quarantine Division** (Sec. 9(6)) headed by a Division Chief, a **Health Emergency Management Division** (Sec. 9(5)), and an **Epidemiology Division** under Technical Services (Sec. 9(8)).[^6]

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 creates the MOH and assigns general functions, including quarantine law administration. But without a standalone quarantine BAA, the MOH cannot:

1. **Establish a Bureau of Quarantine** with statutory authority — BAA 13 creates a Quarantine Division, but a Division Chief has limited regulatory authority compared to a Bureau Director with independent statutory mandate.
2. **Promulgate enforceable quarantine rules with penalties** — BAA 13, Sec. 3(i) authorizes "orders and regulations" but Sec. 6(f) specifically limits the Minister: ***"These issuances shall not prescribe penalties for their violation, except when expressly authorized by law."*** Without a standalone BAA authorizing penalties, the MOH cannot impose fines or detention for quarantine violations.
3. **Issue quarantine clearances** for domestic ports as an independent function — currently, this authority is exercised by the national BOQ.
4. **Establish and manage quarantine stations** with statutory authority over their boundaries, operations, and closure.
5. **Order community quarantine or lockdown measures** with clear legal basis — PB 421 would expressly grant this authority.
6. **Operate a disease surveillance information system** with statutory data-sharing mandates and privacy protections.

The gap between what BAA 13 authorizes and what a standalone BAA would codify is the difference between a **ministry that administers quarantine laws** (current) and a **ministry that makes and enforces quarantine law** (proposed).[^6]

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. **Res. No. 76** (adopted June 25, 2020) invoked the quarantine cooperation mandate during the COVID-19 pandemic and authorized BTA members to use MOOEs for pandemic response. Additionally, **PR No. 28** (Rules of the Parliament) assigns quarantine to the **Committee on Health**, which has jurisdiction over ***"all matters relating to public health and hygiene, medical, allied healthcare, hospital and quarantine services and facilities."*** **PR No. 320** urged the NCMF and DOH-Bureau of Quarantine to adopt mechanisms minimizing the vaccination burden on Hajj pilgrims from island provinces — demonstrating the intersection of quarantine, health, and Hajj services in BARMM.[^12]

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. The **Ministry of Health (MOH)** is the correct lead. Quarantine is fundamentally a public health function — disease prevention and epidemic control. BAA 13 expressly assigns quarantine law administration to the MOH (Sec. 3(d)) and creates a Quarantine Division within the MOH structure (Sec. 9(6)). Both pending Parliament Bills (PB 229 and PB 421) propose placing the quarantine bureau under MOH. There is no alternative MOA claimant for this function.

The only complexity is **agri-fishery quarantine** (animal and plant health), which falls under the **Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR)** rather than MOH. BDP Chapter 7 Strategy 4 references ***"regulatory and quarantine"*** in the agri-fishery context. Human health quarantine and agri-fishery quarantine are distinct regulatory domains with different lead ministries.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. The following institutions currently exercise quarantine-related functions:

- **Lead ministry**: Ministry of Health (MOH) — BAA 13, Book VI, Title V. Operational.
- **MOH Quarantine Division** — BAA 13, Sec. 9(6). Established in the organizational structure; operationalization is limited by the absence of a standalone BAA.
- **MOH Health Emergency Management Division** — BAA 13, Sec. 9(5). Handles health emergency response, including epidemic preparedness.
- **MOH Epidemiology Division** — BAA 13, Sec. 9(8) under Technical Services. Handles disease surveillance and data analysis.
- **Provincial Health Offices** — under MOH supervision (BAA 13, Sec. 3(k)), except where previously devolved.
- **LGU health offices** — exercise local quarantine authority under RA 7160.

No standalone Bureau of Quarantine for BARMM currently exists. The national Bureau of Quarantine (DOH-BOQ) exercises quarantine authority at BARMM ports.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not mandate a specific quarantine institution. Art. IX, Sec. 22 requires the ***"provision of quarantine services"*** but leaves institutional arrangements to implementing legislation. PB 229 and PB 421 both propose creating a Bureau of Quarantine under MOH — but this is a legislative choice, not a BOL mandate.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Yes. Fully exercising this power requires:

1. **A Bangsamoro Bureau of Quarantine** (proposed in PB 229 and PB 421) — to exercise domestic quarantine functions at BARMM ports, manage quarantine stations, issue domestic quarantine clearances, and coordinate with the national BOQ for international quarantine.
2. **A Quarantine Management Information System (QMIS)** (proposed in PB 421) — a digital disease surveillance and quarantine clearance platform.
3. **An interagency coordination body** for quarantine — linking MOH, MAFAR (agri-fishery quarantine), MILG (LGU quarantine), and the national BOQ. The IGRB (Art. VI, Sec. 2) provides the national-level platform, but a regional coordination mechanism is also needed.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise quarantine authority in BARMM?**

A. The **Bureau of Quarantine (BOQ)** under the DOH exercises quarantine functions at BARMM ports of entry and exit. The BOQ examines incoming and outgoing vessels and aircraft, conducts surveillance over sanitary conditions, issues quarantine clearances (Pratique), and enforces the International Health Regulations. The DOH Epidemiology Bureau manages the national disease surveillance network that includes BARMM reporting units. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the national government implemented community quarantine, vaccination programs, and contact tracing across BARMM through the national Inter-Agency Task Force (IATF) framework.[^8]

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for quarantine?**

A. The BOQ maintains quarantine stations at airports and seaports serving BARMM — the primary function is international quarantine (foreign vessels and aircraft). The DOH Regional Office coordinates disease surveillance and epidemic response. The national vaccination program (RA 11525) operates in BARMM through MOH as the regional implementing partner. The Epidemiology Bureau maintains the Philippine Integrated Disease Surveillance and Response (PIDSR) system, which BARMM health units report into.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. **International quarantine has NOT been devolved** — the BOQ retains nationwide jurisdiction because quarantine at ports of entry involves international treaty obligations (WHO IHR) and foreign relations functions that fall outside autonomous region authority. **Domestic quarantine functions** (surveillance, disease prevention, local quarantine measures) have been partially devolved to the MOH through the general health mandate in BAA 13. The gap is the intermediate tier — a regional quarantine bureau with statutory authority over domestic port health, quarantine clearances for domestic travel, and disease surveillance — which neither the national BOQ nor the MOH Quarantine Division currently fills with full legal authority.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes. **Disease surveillance** is a concurrent function — the MOH Epidemiology Division collects data that feeds into the national PIDSR system. **Health emergency response** (e.g., COVID-19) involves both national IATF coordination and BARMM MOH implementation. **Hajj pilgrim vaccination** involves the national BOQ (which issues vaccine passports) and the MOH (which coordinates local delivery). These overlaps are by design — Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3) establishes cooperation, not separation. The absence of a standalone BAA means the boundary between national and BARMM quarantine functions remains undefined by Bangsamoro legislation.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. Without a standalone quarantine BAA, BARMM's accomplishments are exercised through the MOH's general health mandate: (1) the **Quarantine Division** exists in the MOH organizational structure under BAA 13; (2) during the **COVID-19 pandemic**, the MOH coordinated regional quarantine response, vaccination rollout, and disease surveillance — demonstrating operational capacity even without standalone legislation; (3) BTA Res. No. 76 authorized emergency pandemic funding; (4) **two Parliament Bills** (PB 229 and PB 421) have been filed, indicating sustained legislative intent to formalize quarantine authority; (5) the MOH Epidemiology Division participates in national disease surveillance.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM's primary advantage is **cultural and religious integration**. BAA 13, Sec. 3(j) mandates health policies ***"congruent to Islamic teachings on health and sanitation"*** — the national quarantine framework does not offer this. PB 229 specifically proposes **halal-based health services integration** in the quarantine bureau. Additionally, BARMM has **local knowledge** of disease patterns, population movement, and community structures (particularly in conflict-affected and island communities) that the national BOQ — designed primarily for international port health — does not possess. The BARMM can design quarantine messaging, community engagement, and isolation protocols that resonate with the Bangsamoro population's cultural and linguistic context.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint is the absence of a standalone quarantine BAA**. Without it, the MOH Quarantine Division operates without independent regulatory authority, cannot impose penalties for quarantine violations, cannot issue domestic quarantine clearances with statutory force, and cannot establish quarantine stations with legally defined boundaries. The two pending bills (PB 229 and PB 421) would address this. The secondary constraint is **devolution** — the national BOQ retains jurisdiction at BARMM ports, and no formal agreement defines the boundary between national and BARMM quarantine functions.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for quarantine that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national framework provides: (1) the **Bureau of Quarantine** — a first-class line bureau with nationwide infrastructure, quarantine stations at all ports of entry, and decades of operational experience; (2) **RA 9271** — a comprehensive quarantine statute with regulatory authority, enforcement powers, and penalty provisions; (3) the **Philippine Integrated Disease Surveillance and Response (PIDSR)** system — a nationwide electronic disease surveillance network; (4) **WHO IHR compliance infrastructure** — international quarantine documentation, certification, and coordination with foreign health authorities; (5) the **RA 11332 framework** — mandatory disease reporting with defined public health authorities and response protocols.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national system does not account for: (1) **island geography** — BARMM includes Sulu and Tawi-Tawi, archipelagic provinces with numerous points of entry that are difficult for the Manila-based BOQ to monitor; (2) **cross-border movement** — significant unmonitored maritime traffic between BARMM and neighboring countries (Malaysia, Indonesia) through traditional trade routes, which the BOQ's formal port-based system cannot cover; (3) **cultural and linguistic barriers** — quarantine communications and procedures are designed for a general Filipino audience, not the Bangsamoro population's Moro languages and Islamic cultural context; (4) **conflict-affected populations** — IDPs and communities in areas with armed conflict require conflict-sensitive quarantine approaches that the national system is not designed to deliver; (5) **halal compliance** — no halal-certified quarantine procedures, food services in quarantine facilities, or Islamic prayer accommodations in the national BOQ framework.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. **Replicate**: WHO IHR compliance protocols, quarantine clearance documentation standards, and penalty structures (PB 421 already mirrors RA 9271's penalty ranges). **Adapt**: disease surveillance systems (integrate with PIDSR but add BARMM-specific disease monitoring for local epidemiological patterns), quarantine station operations (add halal food services, prayer facilities, culturally appropriate communications), and port health procedures (extend to domestic maritime traffic between islands). **Replace**: the delivery model — shift from a centralized Manila-based structure to a regional bureau with authority over BARMM's domestic ports, leaving international quarantine to the national BOQ while BARMM handles domestic quarantine functions.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Islamic law addresses disease prevention and quarantine directly. The preservation of life (*hifz al-nafs*) is one of the five essential objectives (*maqasid al-Shari'ah*), and quarantine is an instrument for protecting life at the community level. The principle of preventing harm (*la darar wa la dirar*) supports compulsory isolation and movement restrictions where necessary to protect public health. Significantly, Islamic tradition contains one of the **earliest recorded quarantine principles** — the Prophet Muhammad's (peace be upon him) instruction regarding plague: *"If you hear of a plague in a land, do not enter it; and if it breaks out in a land where you are, do not leave it"* (Sahih al-Bukhari; Sahih Muslim). This predates European quarantine practices and provides BARMM quarantine legislation with deep cultural and religious grounding.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to quarantine?**

A. The BOL does not specifically reference Shari'ah in relation to quarantine. However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes the Parliament to ***"enact laws governing personal, family, and property relations of Muslims"*** — and health-related personal restrictions (isolation, movement control) touch on personal liberty governed by Islamic principles. BAA 13, Sec. 3(j) expressly mandates the MOH to ***"Formulate policies, guidelines, regulations as well as operating procedures congruent to Islamic teachings on health and sanitation"*** — this is the statutory basis for integrating Shari'ah into quarantine operations.[^6]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to quarantine legislation?**

A. Four principles are directly relevant:

1. ***Hifz al-nafs*** (preservation of life) — quarantine protects life, fulfilling this highest-priority objective.
2. ***La darar wa la dirar*** (no harm and no reciprocal harm) — justifies compulsory measures (isolation, travel restriction) to prevent disease transmission, even where they restrict individual liberty.
3. ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — quarantine serves the collective good, a recognized basis for legislation in Islamic jurisprudence.
4. ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — public officials exercising quarantine authority bear a trust obligation to use their powers proportionally and for the purpose of health protection, not political control.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro quarantine legislation?**

A. Integration should be **principled** — quarantine legislation guided by Islamic values without mandating specific religious compliance. Practical integration includes: (1) quarantine facility design accommodating Islamic prayer, halal food, and gender-segregated spaces where religiously required; (2) quarantine communications in local Moro languages with Islamic framing (citing Prophetic precedent for compliance); (3) MOH Quarantine Division staff trained in culturally and religiously sensitive health communication; (4) the **Shari'ah Legislative Review Board (SLRB)** reviewing quarantine legislation for consistency with Islamic principles. PB 229 already proposes **halal-based health services integration** in the quarantine bureau.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in quarantine?**

A. Quarantine is among the **most constrained** of the 55 enumerated powers in terms of divergence. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3) mandates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"assist and cooperate with the National Government"*** — the only enumerated power with this specific cooperative formulation for its elaborating provision. As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), Art. III, Sec. 6 permits travel restrictions only when ***"provided by law"*** — any BARMM quarantine legislation restricting movement must satisfy this constitutional requirement.

Using the 5-layer constraint framework from the constitutional analysis:

- **Layer 2** (BOL "shall prevail" provisions): No express supremacy clause applies to quarantine, but the "assist and cooperate" language in Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3) effectively operates as a subordination clause — BARMM assists the National Government, not the reverse.
- **Layer 4** (national institutional jurisdiction retention): The Bureau of Quarantine retains nationwide jurisdiction under RA 9271. International quarantine — involving WHO IHR obligations — is a national function that cannot be displaced.
- **Constitutional floor**: Due process and the right to travel require that quarantine measures be lawful, proportional, and subject to review.

BARMM can diverge in **domestic quarantine operations** — surveillance, local disease reporting, community quarantine measures, domestic port health, and quarantine facility management. BARMM **cannot** diverge from the national framework for **international quarantine** at ports of entry, compliance with WHO IHR, or quarantine clearance for international vessels and aircraft. The pending PB 421 correctly recognizes this by referencing coordination with the national BOQ and alignment with WHO IHR.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different quarantine standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. Persons traveling between BARMM and other Philippine regions could face different quarantine requirements — community quarantine protocols, domestic port health clearances, and disease reporting obligations. Cross-boundary implications require: (1) **mutual recognition** of quarantine clearances between BARMM and national authorities; (2) **data interoperability** between the proposed QMIS (PB 421) and the national PIDSR; (3) **consistent penalty structures** — PB 421 mirrors RA 9271's penalty ranges, which avoids creating an enforcement disparity. The cooperative framework under Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3) inherently limits the divergence risk — BARMM assists the National Government rather than operating independently.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on quarantine?**

A. Unlike most other enumerated powers, a quarantine BAA would not displace national law. RA 9271 would continue to apply for **international quarantine** (the BOQ retains nationwide jurisdiction). RA 11332 would continue to apply for **mandatory disease reporting** (implements a constitutional mandate). The BAA would fill the **domestic quarantine gap** — establishing a regional bureau with statutory authority over domestic port health, local quarantine stations, community quarantine measures, and BARMM-specific disease surveillance. National law applies **concurrently** — not suppletorily — because the BOL establishes cooperation, not substitution.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national quarantine authority?**

A. Yes. The following overlaps exist:

1. **Domestic port health**: The national BOQ has authority over all ports under RA 9271, but its primary focus is international quarantine. Domestic vessels traveling between BARMM ports (Jolo to Zamboanga, Tawi-Tawi to Cotabato) fall in a jurisdictional gray area — does the national BOQ or the MOH Quarantine Division have primary authority?
2. **Disease surveillance data**: The MOH Epidemiology Division collects data that feeds into the national PIDSR. Without a standalone BAA defining reporting obligations, the MOH's disease reporting relies on national law (RA 11332) rather than Bangsamoro legislation.
3. **Community quarantine authority**: During COVID-19, both national (IATF) and BARMM authorities issued quarantine directives. No legislation defines the boundary between national and BARMM community quarantine powers.
4. **Hajj vaccination**: The national BOQ issues vaccine passports for pilgrims. BARMM's unique interest in Hajj (Art. V, Sec. 2(aa)) means quarantine-related vaccination for pilgrims involves both the BOQ and MOH — PR No. 320 highlighted this overlap.

A standalone BAA and an intergovernmental MOA would resolve these overlaps by defining the scope of BARMM domestic quarantine authority vis-a-vis the national BOQ's international quarantine jurisdiction.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Two bills address the gaps:

**PB 229** (First Regular Session, Second Parliament) — the "Bangsamoro Quarantine Health Services Act of 2023" — proposes a Bureau of Health Quarantine under MOH with four units (Administrative, Local Health Surveillance, Special Services, Domestic Ports/Airports Health Services). Led by a Director I (SG 25). Focuses on domestic quarantine functions and halal-based health services integration.

**PB 421** (Fourth Regular Session, Second Parliament) — the "Bangsamoro Quarantine Act of 2025" — is a more comprehensive proposal. It creates a Bangsamoro Bureau of Quarantine under MOH headed by a Director III. Key additions beyond PB 229: mandatory disease reporting, contact tracing authority, community quarantine and lockdown powers, Quarantine Management Information System (QMIS), express intergovernmental coordination through the IGRB (Art. VI, Sec. 2), penalty provisions (P10,000-P50,000 fines and up to 1 year imprisonment), initial appropriation of P15 million, and data privacy compliance (RA 10173).

If PB 421 passes, the primary remaining gap would be an **intergovernmental MOA with the national DOH-BOQ** defining the operational boundary between international and domestic quarantine functions — something legislation alone cannot fully accomplish.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 9** (Social Development — Health subsector) — quarantine capacity is integral to achieving health service delivery targets for disease prevention and epidemic preparedness. BARMM's inadequate health facility access (noted in BDP Chapter 11) means quarantine infrastructure — isolation facilities, surveillance systems, health worker capacity — is a critical gap for both routine disease prevention and emergency response.

**BDP Chapter 7** (Economy) — Strategy 4 calls to ***"Strengthen agri-fishery data management planning, regulatory and quarantine, resource programming, implementation processes, and M&E."*** This references agri-fishery quarantine under MAFAR — a related but distinct quarantine domain.

**Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028), Pillar C** (Mas Matatag na Pamayanan) — this pillar covers health, social justice, and infrastructure. Quarantine capacity directly serves the health component. The pandemic experience demonstrated that inadequate quarantine infrastructure — both institutional and legislative — undermines community resilience.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a standalone quarantine BAA: (1) the MOH cannot enforce quarantine regulations with penalties, making compliance voluntary rather than mandatory; (2) BARMM's domestic ports — serving significant inter-island traffic — lack formal quarantine protocols, creating a disease surveillance blind spot; (3) the next pandemic or epidemic response will rely on the same ad hoc framework used during COVID-19, without statutory authority for community quarantine, contact tracing, or mandatory reporting under Bangsamoro law; (4) Hajj pilgrims from BARMM will continue to depend entirely on the national BOQ for vaccination documentation, without a regional service point; (5) the MOH Quarantine Division will remain a Division-level unit without the statutory authority, staffing, or funding that a Bureau-level institution would command; (6) BARMM cannot operationalize disease surveillance data systems with legal force — the proposed QMIS (PB 421) requires an enacted BAA to mandate data sharing and enforce privacy protections.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the relationship between power (rr) Quarantine regulations and power (bb) Health?**

A. Quarantine regulations (rr) are a **subset** of the broader health power (bb). Art. IX, Sec. 22 — the elaborating provision for health — addresses quarantine in paragraph 3, embedded within the health article. The health reference (28-bb) identifies quarantine as one of the component functions of the MOH. The two powers share: the same lead ministry (MOH), the same BAA 13 mandate provisions, the same BDP chapter (Chapter 9), and the same constitutional and Shari'ah framework. The distinguishing feature is that quarantine (rr) has an express **cooperative limitation** under Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3) that the broader health power (bb) does not — health service delivery is an independent BARMM function, while quarantine is cooperative.

**Q. What is the relationship between human health quarantine and agri-fishery quarantine?**

A. These are **distinct regulatory domains** with different lead ministries. Human health quarantine — the subject of this reference — falls under the **MOH** and addresses epidemic disease prevention, port health, and communicable disease control. Agri-fishery quarantine — preventing the introduction and spread of animal and plant diseases — falls under **MAFAR** and is referenced in BDP Chapter 7, Strategy 4 (***"regulatory and quarantine"***). National law assigns agri-fishery quarantine to the Bureau of Animal Industry and Bureau of Plant Industry under the Department of Agriculture, not the DOH. A comprehensive quarantine framework for BARMM should address both domains, but through separate legislation — PB 229 and PB 421 address only human health quarantine.

**Q. How does the cooperative nature of quarantine affect the Parliament's legislative authority?**

A. The ***"assist and cooperate"*** language in Art. IX, Sec. 22 (para. 3) does **not** extinguish the Parliament's legislative authority under Art. V, Sec. 2(rr). The Parliament can still enact quarantine legislation — both PB 229 and PB 421 are exercises of this authority. What the cooperative limitation means is that Bangsamoro quarantine legislation must: (1) **complement**, not contradict, the national quarantine framework (RA 9271, RA 11332); (2) preserve the national BOQ's jurisdiction over international quarantine; (3) focus on the **domestic quarantine gap** — the functions the national system does not adequately cover within BARMM; (4) include **intergovernmental coordination mechanisms** (PB 421 expressly references the IGRB). The Parliament's role is to legislate the BARMM component of the cooperative framework, not to assert independent quarantine sovereignty.

**Q. Why is quarantine one of only a few cooperative powers in the BOL?**

A. Quarantine is cooperative because of its inherent **cross-boundary and international dimensions**. Epidemic diseases do not respect regional boundaries — effective quarantine requires a unified national response coordinated with international obligations (WHO IHR). The BOL recognizes this by mandating cooperation rather than separation. Other powers with cooperative or constrained language include: food/drug regulation (Art. IX, Sec. 22, para. 2 — "in accordance with" national agencies); civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39 — "national civil service laws... shall prevail"); and municipal creation (Art. VI, Sec. 10 — "criteria laid down in RA 7160... shall be satisfied"). Quarantine occupies a middle position — more constrained than most enumerated powers but less constrained than civil service. BARMM retains legislative authority but exercises it within the cooperative framework.

**Q. What role does the Bureau of Quarantine play in Hajj pilgrim health services?**

A. The national BOQ has the mandate to vaccinate international travelers, including Hajj pilgrims, and to issue **vaccine passports** — certificates of vaccination required for entry into Saudi Arabia. For BARMM constituents, this creates a practical burden documented in **PR No. 320**: pilgrims from island provinces (Sulu, Tawi-Tawi, Basilan) must travel to the nearest BOQ-serviced vaccination point, which may be in Zamboanga or Cotabato. The resolution urged the NCMF and DOH-BOQ to ***"adopt and institute mechanisms that will minimize the burden on pilgrims from the island provinces in complying with the Hajj vaccination requirement."*** A BARMM Bureau of Quarantine could assume responsibility for Hajj-related vaccination and documentation as a domestic quarantine function, reducing the burden on island-based pilgrims while coordinating with the national BOQ for international certification.
