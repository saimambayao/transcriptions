# (dd) Humanitarian Services and Institutions

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(dd)** — ***"Humanitarian services and institutions;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(dd) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national humanitarian or social welfare law. However, Art. IV, Sec. 8 requires the Bangsamoro Government to ***"respect and adhere to all international treaties and agreements binding upon the National Government"*** — which includes the Geneva Conventions, the UN Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement, and other international humanitarian law instruments. Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — establishing a constitutional floor (see Section B (Constitutional Considerations)). |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII, Sec. 1-22; BAA 85, Chapter IV |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC) — BAA 13, Book VII, Title II; Office of the Bangsamoro Mujahideen Under Special Circumstance (OBMUSC) — BAA 57; Bangsamoro Internal Displacement Coordinating Council (BIDCC) — BAA 62; Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG); Ministry of Public Order and Safety (MPOS); Bangsamoro Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council — Art. XIII, Sec. 7 |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 9: Social Development; Chapter 12: Peace, Security, and Justice |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None. The BOL does not name a specific code or act for humanitarian services. However, Art. XIV mandates a ***"program for rehabilitation and development"*** and Art. IX, Sec. 9 mandates a ***"housing and human settlements agency."*** |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over humanitarian services and institutions?**

A. Article V, Section 2(dd) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"humanitarian services and institutions"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** The chapeau of Section 2 states these powers are exercised ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law"*** and ***"without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The grant covers two dimensions: (1) **humanitarian services** — the delivery of relief, rehabilitation, and development assistance to conflict-affected and vulnerable populations; and (2) **humanitarian institutions** — the creation, regulation, and operation of entities that deliver such services.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Seven provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) **Art. XIV, Sec. 1** (Rehabilitation and Development): The BOL's most specific humanitarian provision. It directs the Bangsamoro Government to ***"intensify development efforts for the rehabilitation, reconstruction, and development of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region as part of the normalization process"*** and to ***"formulate and implement a program for rehabilitation and development"*** addressing the needs of **five named populations**: MILF/BIAF members and their decommissioned women auxiliary force, MNLF/BAF members, IDPs, widows and orphans, and poverty-stricken communities.[^2]

   (2) **Art. XIV, Sec. 2** (Special Development Fund): Establishes the fiscal mechanism — ***"Fifty billion pesos (P50,000,000,000.00), at Five billion pesos (P5,000,000,000.00) per year, for a period of ten (10) years"*** — for rebuilding, rehabilitation, and development of conflict-affected communities. Utilization must follow the Bangsamoro Development Plan.[^3]

   (3) **Art. IX, Sec. 8** (Right of Access to Basic Services): Mandates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"maintain appropriate disaster-preparedness units for immediate and effective relief services to victims of natural and man-made calamities"*** and to ***"ensure the rehabilitation of calamity-affected areas and victims of calamities."***[^4]

   (4) **Art. IV, Sec. 7** (Social Justice): ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall ensure every Filipino citizen in its territorial jurisdiction the provision of the basic necessities and equal opportunities in life."***[^5]

   (5) **Art. IV, Sec. 8** (International Treaties): Binds the Bangsamoro Government to ***"respect and adhere to all international treaties and agreements binding upon the National Government"*** — including the Geneva Conventions and UN Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement.[^6]

   (6) **Art. VI, Sec. 12** (Assistance to Other Bangsamoro Communities): Extends the humanitarian mandate beyond BARMM borders — the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall provide assistance to enhance their economic, social, and cultural development"*** to Bangsamoro communities outside the region, in coordination with LGUs and national agencies.[^7]

   (7) **Art. VI, Sec. 13** (National Programs and Projects): Names specific humanitarian/social programs that continue under national funding — the ***"Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program"***, ***"social pension for senior citizens"***, and ***"Task Force Bangon Marawi"*** — while preserving the Bangsamoro Government's power to provide ***"supplemental funding."***[^8]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. The BOL does not name a specific "Bangsamoro Humanitarian Code" or equivalent legislation. However, two related mandates create legislative obligations:

   (1) **Art. IX, Sec. 9** mandates Parliament to ***"pass a law creating a housing and human settlements agency to address the lack of shelters, settlements, and livelihood for the disadvantaged and homeless, especially those victims of conflicts and atrocities."*** This agency has **not yet been created** as of BAA 89.[^9]

   (2) **Art. XIII, Sec. 7** mandates a law defining the powers and functions of the **Bangsamoro DRRM Council** — the ***"law providing for disaster preparedness and response."*** This law has **not yet been enacted** as of BAA 89.[^10]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL creates one institution directly: the **Bangsamoro Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council**, ***"headed by the Chief Minister"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 7). It also mandates the creation of a **housing and human settlements agency** (Art. IX, Sec. 9) and a **special agency for persons with special needs** (Art. IX, Sec. 23). The primary implementing ministry — **MSSD** — was established through BAA 13, not expressly created by the BOL itself.[^10][^9][^11]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three types of limitations:

   (1) **Constitutional floor**: Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — the autonomous region must operate within the constitutional framework. See the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B) for details.[^1]

   (2) **International law obligation**: Art. IV, Sec. 8 requires adherence to ***"all international treaties and agreements"*** — humanitarian services must meet international humanitarian law standards. BAA 62 expressly incorporates UN Guiding Principles on Internal Displacement.[^6]

   (3) **SDF utilization constraint**: Art. XIV, Sec. 2 requires SDF utilization to follow the ***"Bangsamoro Development Plan."*** This prevents discretionary spending — humanitarian rehabilitation funding must align with planned development priorities.[^3]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to humanitarian services and institutions?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** This is the **universal constitutional boundary** on all 55 powers.[^12]

   (2) **Social Justice and Human Rights (Art. XIII, Sec. 1)**: ***"The Congress shall give highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity, reduce social, economic, and political inequalities, and remove cultural inequities..."*** Humanitarian services are an instrument of the constitutional mandate for **social justice**.[^13]

   (3) **Right to Life and Social Services (Art. XIII, Sec. 11)**: ***"The State shall adopt an integrated and comprehensive approach to health development which shall endeavor to make essential goods, health and other social services available to all the people at affordable cost."*** This constitutional duty applies to BARMM — humanitarian services must include health, essential goods, and social services.[^14]

   (4) **State Policy on Social Order (Art. II, Sec. 9)**: ***"The State shall promote a just and dynamic social order that will ensure the prosperity and independence of the nation and free the people from poverty through policies that provide adequate social services, promote full employment, a rising standard of living, and an improved quality of life for all."*** This is the constitutional policy foundation for humanitarian interventions.[^15]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in the humanitarian services area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Right to life** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — no person shall be deprived of life without due process of law. Humanitarian services preserve life.

   (2) **Equal protection** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — humanitarian services must be delivered without discrimination. All inhabitants — Moro, non-Moro indigenous peoples, settlers — are entitled to equal humanitarian protection.

   (3) **Right to adequate social services** (Art. XIII, Sec. 11) — the State shall make essential goods, health, and social services available at affordable cost, with priority for the underprivileged, sick, elderly, disabled, women, and children.

   (4) **Right to shelter** (Art. XIII, Sec. 9-10) — the State shall provide housing for underprivileged and homeless citizens and urban and rural poor dwellers.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for humanitarian-related legislation or institutions?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 11 mandates the State to adopt an ***"integrated and comprehensive approach to health development."*** Art. II, Sec. 9 mandates policies providing ***"adequate social services."*** These constitutional mandates carry weight even without a specific BOL consistency requirement — national humanitarian standards implement constitutional obligations that apply to all government units, including BARMM.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for humanitarian services?**

A. The national framework has three pillars: (1) **RA 10121** (Philippine DRRM Act) — establishes the national DRRM system with the NDRRMC, mandates DRRM Councils and Funds at all levels, and provides the framework for disaster response, relief, and rehabilitation; (2) **RA 5416** and subsequent DSWD legislation — creates the national social welfare apparatus that delivers humanitarian services (cash transfers, livelihood support, welfare facilities); (3) **RA 9851** (International Humanitarian Law Act) — penalizes violations of humanitarian law and protects civilians during armed conflict.[^16][^17][^18]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. The following apply:

   (1) **RA 10121** — applies **directly** to BARMM LGUs pending a standalone Bangsamoro DRRM law. LGUs must maintain Local DRRM Councils and allocate DRRM Funds.[^16]
   (2) **RA 9851** — applies **directly**. International humanitarian law obligations are not affected by autonomy.[^17]
   (3) **RA 11310** — applies **directly** per BOL Art. VI, Sec. 13. The 4Ps program operates within BARMM under DSWD administration.[^19]
   (4) **RA 95** (PNRC Charter) — applies **directly**. The Philippine Red Cross operates in BARMM under its national charter.[^20]
   (5) **RA 10174** (People's Survival Fund) — applies **directly**. BARMM LGUs can access climate adaptation funding.[^21]
   (6) **RA 7875/RA 11223** (PhilHealth/UHC) — applies **directly** per BOL Art. VI, Sec. 13.[^22]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for humanitarian services?**

A. Three national institutions operate within BARMM: (1) **DSWD** — administers the 4Ps, social pension for senior citizens, supplementary feeding programs, and disaster response through its BARMM field offices. DSWD has not devolved its functions to MSSD. (2) **NDRRMC** — coordinates national-level disaster response operations within BARMM, including military and logistical support during major calamities. (3) **Task Force Bangon Marawi** — expressly named in BOL Art. VI, Sec. 13 as a continuing nationally-funded program for Marawi rehabilitation.[^8]

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD)** is the lead agency. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII, Sec. 1-22 defines its institutional status, mandate, powers, functions, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter IV allocates its operational budget.[^23][^24]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. Two standalone BAAs directly implement this power:

   (1) **BAA 57** (Mujahideen Under Special Circumstance Act) — creates OBMUSC and provides P7,000/month financial assistance to senior and permanently disabled MILF/MNLF mujahideen who participated in the Bangsamoro struggle between 1969 and 2014 for at least 20 years.[^25]

   (2) **BAA 62** (Rights of Internally Displaced Persons Act) — comprehensive IDP protection framework that creates the BIDCC, defines IDP rights, establishes durable solutions (return, local integration, relocation), and integrates Shari'ah principles.[^26]

   Additionally, **BAA 22** (as extended by BAA 34 and BAA 51) appropriates the Special Development Fund for conflict-affected community rehabilitation.[^27]

**Q. What are MSSD's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII provides:

   **Declaration of Policy** (Sec. 1): ***"The Bangsamoro Government is committed to the care, protection, and rehabilitation of individuals, families, and communities in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, especially those who have less in life and those who need social welfare interventions to restore their normal functioning and enable them to participate in community affairs."***[^23]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): MSSD shall provide ***"a balanced and responsive approach to social welfare whereby the rights, needs, and interests of all citizens within its jurisdiction, with special focus on the marginalized and vulnerable sectors, including women, children, senior citizens, differently abled persons, indigenous cultural communities, internally displaced persons, and those similarly situated by analogy, are protected and addressed at all times, especially during the existence of a crisis, whether natural or human-induced."***[^23]

   **Powers and Functions** (Sec. 3) — 18 enumerated functions including:

   - (a) Formulate, develop and implement plans, programs and projects in the field of social welfare and development;
   - (b) Adopt policies for effective implementation of public and private social welfare services;
   - (c) Promote, support and coordinate non-governmental social welfare facilities;
   - (d) Establish, operate, maintain and support institutional facilities for constituents;
   - (e) Promote and strengthen people's organizations for grassroots welfare;
   - (f) Promote networks for delivery of interventions to welfare constituents;
   - (g) Accredit institutions and organizations engaged in social welfare activities;
   - (l) Deputize law enforcement agencies for protection of exploited, abused, and disadvantaged;
   - (m) Regulate fund drives, public solicitations and donations for charitable or welfare purposes;
   - (n) Set standards, accredit and monitor all social welfare activities in public and private sectors;
   - (o) Exercise functional and technical supervision over social workers in other government settings;
   - (p) ***"Deputize local government units and other agencies of government as are necessary in providing disaster relief;"***
   - (q) Coordinate programs for marginalized sectors including IDPs.[^23]

   **Organizational Structure** (Sec. 4-9): The Ministry comprises: (1) Ministry Proper (Minister, Deputy Minister, Bangsamoro Director-General); (2) Administrative and Finance Services; (3) **Programs and Operations Services** — with three divisions: (a) Protective Services and Welfare Division, (b) **Disaster Response and Management Division**, and (c) Specialized Programs Division; (4) **Provincial and City Operations Office** — with provincial social welfare offices in Sulu, Tawi-Tawi, Maguindanao, Lanao del Sur, and the 63-barangay Special Geographic Area.[^23]

   **Welfare Facilities** (Sec. 12): MSSD is authorized to establish and operate: (a) Vocational Rehabilitation and Special Learning Centers for Differently-Abled Persons; (b) Senior Citizens Centers; (c) Centers for Street Children; (d) Crisis Intervention Facilities; (e) Facilities for Survivors of VAWC; (f) Rehabilitation Centers for Children in Conflict with the Law; (g) Reception and Study Centers for Children; (h) Orphanage Institutions; (i) other necessary facilities.[^23]

   **Fund Drives** (Sec. 15-18): MSSD regulates solicitation permits for charitable and welfare purposes. In times of ***"disaster and pandemic that result in a huge humanitarian demand, the Ministry will exert reasonable efforts to simplify the implementation of the registration requirement for solicitation."***[^23]

**Q. What can MSSD NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 provides MSSD with a general institutional mandate and organizational structure, but does **not** provide:

   (1) A **comprehensive humanitarian services code** that defines standards for humanitarian response, beneficiary targeting, accountability mechanisms, and coordination protocols;
   (2) A **dedicated humanitarian fund** separate from the general budget and the SDF — BAA 13 does not create a permanent humanitarian response fund;
   (3) **Regulatory authority over international humanitarian organizations** operating in BARMM — standards for humanitarian agencies beyond the general solicitation permit;
   (4) A **Bangsamoro DRRM law** defining the DRRM Council's powers and functions as mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 7;
   (5) The **housing and human settlements agency** mandated by Art. IX, Sec. 9.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. Resolution No. 43 calls for creation of a bureau for orphans and widows of war. Resolution No. 179 supports IDPs' return to Marawi properties (now partially addressed by BAA 62). Resolution No. 476 urges extension of leases for Marawi IDP temporary shelters, indicating ongoing humanitarian concerns.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is MSSD the correct lead MOA for this power?**

A. **Partially.** MSSD's BAA 13 mandate covers the **social welfare dimension** of humanitarian services comprehensively — IDPs, vulnerable populations, disaster relief, welfare facilities. However, the **full scope** of "humanitarian services and institutions" extends beyond MSSD's social welfare mandate to include:

   - **Disaster response** — BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 7 assigns this to the Bangsamoro DRRM Council (headed by the Chief Minister), not MSSD;
   - **Rehabilitation and development** — Art. XIV, Sec. 1 is a cross-cutting mandate that involves MSSD, BPDA, MILG, and line ministries;
   - **International humanitarian law compliance** — BHRC's mandate includes upholding international humanitarian law.

   MSSD is the correct **primary coordinator** for humanitarian services, but this power requires **multi-agency coordination** — MSSD for social welfare, the DRRM Council for disaster response, BHRC for human rights/IHL compliance, and BPDA for SDF utilization and development planning.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Six institutions currently exercise functions:

   - **MSSD** (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII) — lead social welfare ministry with disaster response division; operational
   - **BHRC** (BAA 13, Book VII, Title II) — human rights commission mandated to uphold international humanitarian law; operational
   - **OBMUSC** (BAA 57) — office providing financial assistance to senior/disabled mujahideen; operational
   - **BIDCC** (BAA 62) — Bangsamoro Internal Displacement Coordinating Council for IDP protection; operational
   - **Bangsamoro DRRM Council** (Art. XIII, Sec. 7) — created by BOL but **enabling law not yet enacted**; operating on executive authority
   - **Provincial/City Social Welfare Offices** (BAA 13, Sec. 10-14) — MSSD field offices in each province/city; operational

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Two BOL-mandated institutions remain unestablished:

   (1) **Housing and human settlements agency** — Art. IX, Sec. 9 mandates Parliament to ***"pass a law creating a housing and human settlements agency to address the lack of shelters, settlements, and livelihood for the disadvantaged and homeless, especially those victims of conflicts and atrocities."*** Not created as of BAA 89.[^9]

   (2) **Special agency for persons with special needs** — Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"establish a special agency, support facilities and livelihood or skills training for persons with special needs."*** Not created as separate agency as of BAA 89.[^11]

   Additionally, the **Bangsamoro DRRM Council** exists by BOL mandate (Art. XIII, Sec. 7) but its powers and functions have not been defined by the ***"law providing for disaster preparedness and response"*** that the BOL requires.[^10]

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Yes — fully exercising this power would benefit from:

   (1) A **Bangsamoro Humanitarian Coordination Center** — a permanent coordinating body for humanitarian operations during emergencies, distinct from the DRRM Council (which focuses on disaster preparedness) and MSSD (which focuses on social welfare). This would coordinate between BARMM agencies, national government, international organizations, and NGOs during humanitarian crises.

   (2) A **Bangsamoro Zakat and Sadaqah Board** — to institutionalize Islamic charitable giving as a supplementary funding mechanism for humanitarian services. See the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E).

DSWD's **Disaster Response and Management Group** deploys to BARMM during calamities.

   (2) **Task Force Bangon Marawi (TFBM)** — the dedicated national humanitarian response mechanism for the Marawi siege rehabilitation. Expressly named in BOL Art. VI, Sec. 13. Manages the rehabilitation of Marawi's Most Affected Area (MAA), permanent housing construction, and livelihood restoration for displaced families.    (3) **NDRRMC** coordinates national disaster response assets (military, logistics, communications) when BARMM is affected by major calamities. The Office of Civil Defense (OCD) maintains a regional office.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. Devolution is **incomplete**. DSWD has not formally devolved its BARMM operations to MSSD — the two agencies operate in parallel. DSWD continues to directly administer nationally-funded programs (4Ps, social pension) while MSSD manages BARMM-funded social welfare programs. This creates a dual-track system where beneficiaries interact with both agencies. No formal MOA or transition agreement has been published governing the eventual transfer of DSWD functions to MSSD. **Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes, significantly:

   (1) **Disaster relief** — both DSWD's Disaster Response Group and MSSD's Disaster Response and Management Division respond to calamities in BARMM, sometimes in the same areas without clear delineation;
   (2) **IDP assistance** — DSWD has historically managed Marawi IDP assistance while BAA 62 now creates the BIDCC under BARMM authority;
   (3) **Social welfare facilities** — DSWD operates residential care facilities alongside MSSD's authorized welfare facilities;
   (4) **Fund drive regulation** — MSSD regulates solicitation permits within BARMM, but nationally-chartered organizations (PNRC, DSWD-accredited NGOs) operate under their national authority.

   This concurrency is partly **by design** (BOL Art. VI, Sec. 13 preserves national programs) and partly **by default** (no BAA has defined the boundary between DSWD and MSSD jurisdictions).

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. Three concrete legislative achievements:

   (1) **BAA 62** (IDP Rights Act) — the most comprehensive IDP protection law enacted by any subnational government in the Philippines. It creates the BIDCC, defines IDP rights aligned with the UN Guiding Principles, integrates Shari'ah principles, and establishes durable solutions for displacement.

   (2) **BAA 57** (Mujahideen Under Special Circumstance Act) — provides monthly financial assistance to senior and disabled veterans of the Bangsamoro struggle, directly implementing the Art. XIV, Sec. 1 rehabilitation mandate.

   (3) **SDF appropriation and utilization** — BAA 22 and its extensions (BAA 34, BAA 51) have channeled SDF resources toward conflict-affected community rehabilitation.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. Two areas of distinctiveness:

   (1) **Shari'ah integration** — BAA 62 is the only Philippine legislation that expressly integrates *maqasid al-Shari'ah* into humanitarian protection. The national framework (RA 10121, DSWD programs) has no Islamic law dimension.

   (2) **Conflict-sensitive design** — BAA 57 and BAA 62 are specifically designed for the Bangsamoro post-conflict context — addressing mujahideen rehabilitation, protracted displacement from decades of armed conflict, and property restitution. National programs are generic disaster-response frameworks not calibrated for conflict-affected populations.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the **absence of a Bangsamoro DRRM law** defining the Bangsamoro DRRM Council's powers and functions as mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 7. Without this law: the DRRM Council operates without a clear legal framework; humanitarian disaster response lacks a dedicated Bangsamoro fund; coordination protocols between the BARMM DRRM Council and the NDRRMC are ad hoc; and BARMM LGUs default to RA 10121 for their DRRM obligations. The second binding constraint is the **absence of the housing and human settlements agency** mandated by Art. IX, Sec. 9 — without it, shelter provision for IDPs and disaster victims lacks a dedicated institutional lead.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has:

   (1) **NDRRMC institutional infrastructure** — a fully operational national DRRM council with regional offices, established protocols, a National DRRM Fund, and coordination mechanisms with military, police, and civil defense assets. The BARMM DRRM Council lacks its own enabling legislation.

   (2) **DSWD operational depth** — decades of program implementation, beneficiary databases (Listahanan), accreditation systems for social welfare agencies, and a trained social worker workforce. MSSD is building equivalent capacity.

   (3) **Legislative depth** — RA 10121 has implementing rules, regional DRRM plans, established funding streams (NDRRMF and Local DRRM Funds), and jurisprudence on disaster response accountability. BARMM has no equivalent DRRM statute.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. Three critical shortcomings:

   (1) **No conflict-sensitivity** — the national DRRM framework (RA 10121) addresses natural disasters but is not designed for **armed conflict displacement**, which is BARMM's predominant humanitarian challenge. It does not account for protracted displacement (Marawi IDPs displaced since 2017 [UNVERIFIED — exact displacement timeline and current IDP count]), clan conflict (*rido*) displacement, or military operations displacement.

   (2) **No Shari'ah dimension** — national humanitarian programs do not provide for Islamic charitable mechanisms (*zakat*, *sadaqah*), Shari'ah-guided service delivery, or culturally appropriate humanitarian protocols for Muslim populations.

   (3) **Underservice** — BARMM consistently ranks among the most disaster-vulnerable and conflict-affected regions in the Philippines, yet national agency presence (DSWD staffing, NDRRMC assets) is not proportional to the region's humanitarian burden. **Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. (1) **Replicate**: the national DRRM Council structure (RA 10121) — multi-level councils from regional to barangay with dedicated funds and plans. This proven structure should be adopted for the Bangsamoro DRRM law.

   (2) **Adapt**: DSWD's Listahanan beneficiary targeting system — adapt for BARMM context with additional criteria for conflict-affected populations, IDP status, and mujahideen veteran status. Add *zakat*-eligible categories.

   (3) **Replace**: the generic disaster response framework with a **conflict-and-disaster humanitarian response framework** that treats armed conflict displacement, natural disaster, and pandemic response as integrated components — something the national system separates into different agencies and statutes.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes — **significantly**. Humanitarian services in the Bangsamoro context intersect with Islamic charitable obligations (*zakat*, *sadaqah*), the *maqasid al-Shari'ah* (objectives of preserving life, property, progeny, intellect, and faith), and culturally appropriate service delivery for Muslim populations. BAA 62 has already set the legal precedent by expressly incorporating Shari'ah principles into IDP protection.[^26]

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not expressly reference Shari'ah for humanitarian services specifically. However, Art. X broadly empowers Parliament to enact Shari'ah laws on personal, family, property, commercial, and criminal matters — and BAA 62 has established the precedent that humanitarian legislation can be ***"guided by the principles and objectives of Shari'ah."*** Art. IV, Sec. 8's adherence to international treaties is complementary, not contradictory, to Shari'ah integration — international humanitarian law and Islamic humanitarian principles share common ground on protection of civilians, prohibition of targeting non-combatants, and care for the wounded and displaced.[^26]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four principles are directly applicable:

   (1) ***Maqasid al-Shari'ah*** — the five essential objectives (preservation of life, religion, intellect, progeny, property) are the framework that BAA 62 already uses. All humanitarian legislation should preserve these objectives.

   (2) ***Zakat*** — the obligatory alms whose eligible recipients include the poor (*fuqara*), the needy (*masakin*), debtors (*gharimin*), and wayfarers (*ibn al-sabil*). IDPs, conflict-affected communities, and disaster victims qualify. Institutionalized *zakat* collection and distribution could be a major supplementary funding mechanism for humanitarian services.

   (3) ***Sadaqah*** — voluntary charity. Fund drive regulation under BAA 13 (Sec. 15-18) could be expanded to create a Shari'ah-compliant charitable giving framework that channels *sadaqah* toward humanitarian priorities.

   (4) ***Amanah*** — trust and stewardship. Managers of humanitarian funds (SDF, DRRM funds, charitable donations) are trustees (*amin*) — *amanah* principles demand transparency, accountability, and prohibition of misuse.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro humanitarian legislation?**

A. **Principled integration** — humanitarian services should be guided by Shari'ah values (as BAA 62 already establishes) without mandating compliance for non-Muslim beneficiaries or secular humanitarian organizations. The approach should be: (1) Shari'ah principles guide **policy design** (prioritizing *maqasid*-aligned outcomes); (2) *zakat* and *sadaqah* mechanisms are available as **supplementary funding** streams alongside government appropriations; (3) humanitarian service delivery respects **Islamic cultural practices** (gender-appropriate shelter, *halal* food in relief distributions, prayer facilities in evacuation centers) while remaining inclusive of non-Muslim populations.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM has **strong legal basis for divergence** in humanitarian services:

   (1) **No express consistency requirement** — the BOL does not require humanitarian services to be exercised "consistent with" any national law. This contrasts with powers like civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39: ***"in accordance with"*** national law) or DRRM (Art. XIII, Sec. 7: ***"complement"*** the national DRRM Plan).

   (2) **Art. XIV provides BARMM-specific mandates** — the rehabilitation program for five named populations and the SDF are unique to BARMM. No national law governs these.

   (3) **Shari'ah integration** (as established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E)) provides an additional unique basis for divergence — BAA 62's incorporation of *maqasid al-Shari'ah* creates a distinctly Bangsamoro humanitarian framework.

   The **limits on divergence** are: the constitutional floor established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B) (equal protection, right to life, right to social services); adherence to international humanitarian law and treaties per Art. IV, Sec. 8 (established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A)); and BOL Art. VI, Sec. 13's preservation of national programs, which means BARMM cannot block or replace nationally-funded programs (4Ps, PhilHealth, TFBM) — it can only supplement them.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different humanitarian standards, what are the implications?**

A. Minimal adverse implications for rights holders — BARMM humanitarian legislation (BAA 57, BAA 62) already provides **additional protections** above the national baseline, not fewer. Cross-boundary implications arise only for: (1) **IDPs who move between BARMM and non-BARMM areas** — BAA 62 protections apply within BARMM, while national law governs outside; (2) **humanitarian organizations** operating both within and outside BARMM — they must comply with MSSD accreditation within BARMM and DSWD accreditation nationally.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. The BAA governs within BARMM; national law applies suppletorily where the BAA is silent. For humanitarian services: RA 10121 (DRRM) will apply suppletorily until a Bangsamoro DRRM law is enacted, then the BAA will govern within BARMM with RA 10121 filling gaps. Nationally-funded programs named in Art. VI, Sec. 13 continue regardless of BAAs.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes — three areas of jurisdictional ambiguity:

   (1) **DSWD-MSSD boundary**: No legislation or formal agreement defines which social welfare functions belong to DSWD and which to MSSD within BARMM. Both agencies deliver disaster relief, welfare services, and IDP assistance.

   (2) **NDRRMC-BARMM DRRM Council coordination**: Without the enabling law mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 7, the coordination protocol between the Bangsamoro DRRM Council and the NDRRMC is undefined. During calamities, the question of who leads response operations is resolved ad hoc.

   (3) **International humanitarian organizations**: Organizations like UNHCR, ICRC, and international NGOs operate in BARMM under national government coordination (DSWD, OCD). BAA 62 creates the BIDCC for IDP coordination, but the relationship between BIDCC and these international actors is not fully defined.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. As noted in the Pending Bills table: PB 205 and PB 394 address assistance to Bangsamoro communities outside BARMM (Art. VI, Sec. 12). PB 326 and PB 392 extend mujahideen benefits. PB 382 creates women's welfare centers. However, **no pending bill addresses** the two most critical gaps: the **Bangsamoro DRRM law** (Art. XIII, Sec. 7 mandate) and the **housing and human settlements agency** (Art. IX, Sec. 9 mandate). These remain unfulfilled BOL mandates as established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A).

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 9** (Social Development) depends on humanitarian services for its social protection targets — reducing poverty, addressing vulnerability, and building community resilience. **BDP Chapter 12** (Peace, Security, and Justice) depends on this power for the normalization process — SDF utilization, mujahideen rehabilitation, and IDP reintegration are prerequisites for sustainable peace.

   Under the **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda**: **Pillar 1** (Responsive and Accountable Governance) — effective humanitarian service delivery demonstrates BARMM's governance capacity, particularly in reaching marginalized communities. **Pillar 4** (Sustainable Peace and Security) — humanitarian rehabilitation programs address the root conditions of conflict and support the normalization track of the peace process.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **Bangsamoro DRRM law**: (1) the DRRM Council cannot exercise its full BOL mandate; (2) BARMM LGUs default to the national framework which is not conflict-sensitive; (3) humanitarian response during disasters remains ad hoc rather than systematic. Without the **housing and human settlements agency**: protracted displacement continues for conflict victims (Marawi IDPs and others [UNVERIFIED — specific numbers of protracted IDPs]) as no dedicated institution leads shelter provision. Without a **comprehensive humanitarian services framework**: MSSD lacks regulatory tools to coordinate the many actors (BARMM agencies, national agencies, international organizations, NGOs, faith-based organizations) operating in the humanitarian space.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does humanitarian services (dd) relate to disaster risk reduction and management (s)?**

A. The two powers are **complementary but distinct**. Power (s) — DRRM — focuses on **preparedness, mitigation, prevention, and response** to natural and man-made disasters. Power (dd) — humanitarian services — is **broader**: it encompasses the full spectrum of humanitarian assistance including post-conflict rehabilitation, IDP protection, social welfare for conflict-affected populations, and institutional humanitarian capacity. DRRM is one component of the humanitarian services landscape. The BOL's Art. IX, Sec. 8 bridges both — mandating disaster-preparedness units (DRRM) and rehabilitation of victims (humanitarian services). In practice, MSSD's **Disaster Response and Management Division** operationalizes the overlap.

**Q. What is the legal status and fiscal mechanism of the Special Development Fund?**

A. The SDF is established by Art. XIV, Sec. 2 — P50 billion over 10 years (P5B/year) from the National Government. It is the **single largest dedicated funding mechanism** for humanitarian rehabilitation in BARMM. Key fiscal parameters: (1) utilization must follow the Bangsamoro Development Plan (Art. XIV, Sec. 2); (2) the fund is specifically for ***"rebuilding, rehabilitation, and development of its conflict-affected communities"*** — not general budgetary purposes; (3) BAA 22 was the first appropriation, extended by BAA 34 and BAA 51; (4) BAA 85 allocates the SDF under Chapter XXXII. The 10-year period runs from BOL ratification (January 2019), placing the final tranche around 2029. [UNVERIFIED — total SDF disbursement to date and utilization rate]

**Q. How does the Marawi rehabilitation intersect with this power?**

A. The Marawi siege (2017) created what is arguably BARMM's largest single humanitarian crisis. The BOL expressly addresses it through Art. VI, Sec. 13's naming of ***"Task Force Bangon Marawi"*** as a continuing nationally-funded program. BAA 62 provides the Bangsamoro legislative framework for IDP protection that applies to Marawi IDPs. Multiple parliamentary resolutions (Res. 179, 356, 476) address ongoing Marawi humanitarian concerns. The jurisdictional complexity is acute: TFBM operates under national government authority while the displaced Maranao population falls within BARMM's humanitarian services mandate. BAA 62's creation of the BIDCC provides a potential coordination mechanism, but the boundary between TFBM's national mandate and BIDCC's BARMM mandate requires intergovernmental clarification. **Q. Can the Bangsamoro Government regulate international humanitarian organizations operating within BARMM?**

A. BAA 13, Sec. 15-18 gives MSSD authority to regulate fund drives and solicitation for charitable or welfare purposes — including requiring permits for entities soliciting contributions within BARMM. However, international humanitarian organizations (ICRC, UNHCR, WFP, international NGOs) typically operate under agreements with the National Government, not subnational entities. BARMM's regulatory authority over these organizations is **limited to**: (1) coordination through the BIDCC (BAA 62); (2) solicitation permit requirements under BAA 13; (3) accreditation of social welfare agencies (BAA 13, Sec. 20). Full regulatory authority would require a standalone BAA establishing a humanitarian coordination framework that defines registration, reporting, and accountability requirements for all humanitarian actors within BARMM.

**Q. What role does Islamic charity (*zakat* and *sadaqah*) play in the humanitarian framework?**

A. *Zakat* and *sadaqah* are potentially the **largest untapped humanitarian funding source** in BARMM. As noted in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), IDPs, the poor, the needy, debtors, and wayfarers are all eligible *zakat* recipients under Islamic law. Currently, *zakat* collection and distribution in BARMM is decentralized and mosque-based — there is no institutional mechanism to channel it toward systematic humanitarian services. A **Bangsamoro Zakat and Sadaqah Board** (or empowering MSSD with a *zakat* coordination function) would: (1) institutionalize *zakat* as a supplementary humanitarian fund; (2) ensure transparent and accountable distribution aligned with *Shari'ah* categories; (3) coordinate with MSSD's social welfare targeting to avoid duplication. The BOL's Islamic governance framework (Art. X) and BAA 62's Shari'ah integration precedent provide the legal basis for such legislation.
