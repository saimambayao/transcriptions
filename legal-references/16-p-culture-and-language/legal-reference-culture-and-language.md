# (p) Culture and Language

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(p)** — "Culture and language;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over culture and language as an enumerated power under Article V, Section 2(p).[^1] This power is elaborated across several provisions. Article IX, Sections 24 through 26 establish the institutional architecture for cultural preservation. Section 24 directs the Bangsamoro Government to "preserve the history, culture, arts, traditions, and the rich cultural heritage of the Bangsamoro people and their Sultanates, such as those of Sulu, Maguindanao, Kabuntalan, Buayan, the Royal Houses of Ranao and of the Iranun, and the non-Moro indigenous peoples."[^2] For this purpose, the BOL creates the Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH) in coordination with appropriate national government agencies.

Section 25 defines the BCPCH's primary responsibility: to write the history of the Bangsamoro people and to "establish and sustain the cultural institutions, programs, and projects in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."[^3] The Commission is directed to coordinate with the National Commission for Culture and the Arts, the National Historical Commission of the Philippines, the National Museum of the Philippines, and other cultural agencies. It must establish libraries and museums, declare and restore historical shrines and cultural sites, and ensure the inclusion of Philippine history, culture, and heritage in the establishment of museums and similar institutions. Section 26 mandates the National Government to transfer management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites currently under national jurisdiction to the BCPCH through intergovernmental relations mechanisms.[^4]

Additionally, Article II, Section 2 empowers the Parliament to adopt the official flag, emblem, and hymn of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, establishing the cultural symbols of Bangsamoro identity.[^5] The BOL places no explicit national law consistency requirement on this power — the relationship with national cultural agencies is one of coordination rather than subordination.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH) is the primary institution exercising this power, funded through BAA 85, Chapter XXII.[^6] The Parliament has enacted four BAAs touching culture and language. BAA 20 mandates the dual calendar system (Hijri and Gregorian), embedding Islamic temporal culture into official government operations.[^7] BAA 39 establishes Bangsamoro regional and special holidays, codifying culturally and religiously significant dates.[^8] BAA 60 declares the burial site of Hashim and Mimbantas as a memorial site, preserving revolutionary heritage.[^9] BAA 61 establishes the Camp Abubakar Memorial Marker and Eco-Park as a memorial for Bangsamoro shuhada (martyrs).[^10]

The earlier identity BAAs — BAA 1 (Official Flag), BAA 2 (Official Emblem), BAA 7 (Official Hymn), and BAA 16 (Official Parliament Seal) — implement the cultural identity dimension of this power under Article II, Section 2, though they are classified as general/administrative rather than culture-specific.[^11]

Parliamentary resolutions demonstrate extensive engagement with cultural heritage. Resolution 62 requires annual commemorations on March 18 (Jabidah Massacre anniversary). Resolution 66 and Resolution 320 call for display of the BARMM flag in all public buildings. Resolution 281 encourages the Assalamu Alaykum greeting as official policy. Resolution 311 urges airplane and airport announcements in local languages. Resolution 521 encourages observance of Bangsamoro Foundation Day. Multiple resolutions recognize cultural achievers — Resolution 219 mourned National Living Treasure Ambalang Ausalin, and Resolution 424 commended Sakinur-Ain Delasas as Manlilikha ng Bayan for the Sama Igal dance.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not impose a national law consistency requirement on culture and language. The relationship is coordination-based rather than hierarchical. Section 25 directs the BCPCH to "coordinate and work closely" with the NCCA, NHCP, National Museum, and other cultural agencies.[^12] Section 26 provides that the National Government "shall transfer the management" of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites to the BCPCH.[^13] This transfer mechanism operates through intergovernmental relations, meaning the process requires bilateral cooperation but not national government approval of Bangsamoro cultural policy.

National cultural laws such as RA 10066 (National Cultural Heritage Act of 2009) apply suppletorily in the absence of a dedicated Bangsamoro cultural heritage code. RA 7356 (creating the NCCA) and RA 10086 (strengthening the National Historical Commission) define the national cultural governance framework. Where no BAA has been enacted on a specific cultural matter, these national laws govern. However, once the Parliament enacts a comprehensive cultural heritage code, Bangsamoro cultural policy would be governed primarily by that code, with national law continuing to apply only on matters of national significance or where both national and Bangsamoro interests converge.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The most significant gap is the absence of a comprehensive Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code. While BAA 60 and BAA 61 protect two specific memorial sites, no omnibus legislation exists to establish a systematic framework for identifying, classifying, conserving, and managing the full range of Bangsamoro cultural heritage — tangible (historic buildings, artifacts, archaeological sites) and intangible (languages, oral traditions, performing arts, traditional crafts). Bill 204, which provides for the "protection, preservation, conservation, and promotion of cultural heritage," addresses this gap but remains pending.

Language policy is entirely unlegislated beyond BAA 20's dual calendar mandate. Bill 176, proposing English-Arabic as an official language for signages, and the broader question of Bangsamoro language preservation and promotion — for Meranao, Maguindanaon, Tausug, Sama, Yakan, and indigenous languages — have no legislative framework. A Bangsamoro Languages Act could codify language rights, preservation programs, and the use of local languages in education and government.

The transfer of management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites from national agencies to the BCPCH, mandated by Article IX, Section 26, appears to remain incomplete. No BAA codifies the transfer process, the sites to be transferred, or the management standards to be applied post-transfer. Similarly, Bill 299 (a museum exhibiting Bangsamoro history toward autonomy) and Bill 358 (establishing the Shariff Kabunsuan Shrine) point to unmet demand for institutional cultural infrastructure that has not yet been legislated.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

Culture and heritage are central to the BDP 2023-2028, addressed in Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage), which aims to preserve Bangsamoro identity, protect cultural heritage sites, and promote intercultural understanding.[^14] Without a comprehensive cultural heritage code, the BDP's cultural preservation targets lack the legal architecture for systematic implementation. The BDP's emphasis on cultural diversity as a foundation for social cohesion also depends on language preservation legislation that does not yet exist.

## Legislative Gaps

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type A**: No comprehensive cultural heritage code | No standalone Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code; BCPCH exercises its mandate through BAA 13 without a systematic framework for heritage classification, conservation, and management | Enact Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code (Bill 204 pending) covering tangible and intangible heritage |
| **Type A**: No language policy legislation | No BAA codifies language rights, preservation programs, or official language use beyond the dual calendar | Enact Bangsamoro Languages Act covering language preservation, promotion, and use in education and government |
| **Type B**: Incomplete site transfer | Art. IX, Sec. 26 mandates transfer of Bangsamoro historical/cultural sites from national agencies to BCPCH — process remains uncodified | Enact legislation codifying the transfer process, inventory of sites, and post-transfer management standards |
| **Type A**: No cultural institutions framework | No standalone BAA for museums, cultural centers, or cultural infrastructure beyond the two memorial sites | Enact Bangsamoro Museums and Cultural Institutions Act (Bills 203, 299 pending) |
