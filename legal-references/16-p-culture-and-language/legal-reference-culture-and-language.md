# (p) Culture and Language

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(p)** — ***"Culture and language;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(p) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No — the BOL does not impose a national law consistency requirement on culture and language. Art. IX, Sec. 25 directs the BCPCH to ***"coordinate and work closely with"*** national cultural agencies — a coordination relationship, not subordination. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH)** — attached to the Office of the Chief Minister (BAA 13, Title XVI, Ch. 4, Sec. 46) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE — cultural education); Office of the Wali of the Bangsamoro (OTWB — ceremonial/cultural functions); Bangsamoro Library and Archives (OCM — BAA 13, Book II, Sec. (9)) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage) |
| **Priority Code** | — (no named legislative mandate, but BOL creates BCPCH by name) |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over culture and language?**

A. Article V, Section 2(p) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"culture and language"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a **broad grant** covering cultural heritage preservation, promotion, and development, as well as language policy within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. Art. IX, Sections 24 through 26 elaborate this power extensively — creating the BCPCH by name, defining its mandate, and requiring the transfer of historical and cultural site management from national agencies.[^1][^2]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Seven provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. IX, Sec. 24 — **creates the BCPCH** and defines the scope of cultural preservation: ***"the history, culture, arts, traditions, and the rich cultural heritage of the Bangsamoro people and their Sultanates."*** The provision names specific heritage streams — Sultanates of Sulu and Maguindanao, Kabuntalan, Buayan, Royal Houses of Ranao and Iranun, and ***"the non-Moro indigenous peoples."***[^2]

   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 25 — defines BCPCH's **five mandates**: write Bangsamoro history, establish cultural institutions, coordinate with NCCA/NHCP/National Museum, establish libraries and museums, and include Philippine national history in Bangsamoro institutions.[^3]

   (3) Art. IX, Sec. 26 — mandates **transfer of site management**: ***"The National Government shall transfer the management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites."***[^4]

   (4) Art. II, Sec. 2 — **cultural symbols**: ***"The Parliament shall adopt the official flag, emblem, and hymn."***[^5]

   (5) Art. IX, Sec. 4 — **customary cultural protection**: ***"The customs, beliefs, and traditions of the Bangsamoro people shall be recognized, protected, and guaranteed."***[^6]

   (6) Art. IX, Sec. 3(6) — **IP cultural rights**: the right to ***"preservation and development of their cultures, traditions, and institutions."***[^7]

   (7) Art. V, Sec. 2(kk) — the related power ***"Libraries and museums, and historical, cultural, and archaeological sites"*** — directly overlaps with BCPCH's institutional mandate.[^8]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No — the BOL does not name a specific cultural heritage code or language act. However, Art. IX, Sec. 24 mandates the **creation of the BCPCH** (***"it shall create"***), which BAA 13 has fulfilled. The implied legislative mandate is for comprehensive enabling legislation that provides the BCPCH with the regulatory framework, heritage classification system, and enforcement tools it needs.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL **expressly creates the BCPCH** in Art. IX, Sec. 24: ***"it shall create the Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage."*** This is one of the few institutions the BOL creates by name — alongside the Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (Art. IX, Sec. 7), the Bangsamoro Sports Commission (Art. IX, Sec. 20), and others.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Two operational qualifications:

   (1) **Coordination requirement**: Art. IX, Sec. 25 — BCPCH must ***"coordinate and work closely with"*** the NCCA, NHCP, National Museum, and other cultural agencies. This is a **coordination** obligation, not a subordination or consistency requirement.[^3]

   (2) **National unity mandate**: Art. IX, Sec. 25 — BCPCH must ***"ensure the inclusion of Philippine history, culture, and heritage in the establishment of museums and similar institutions, programs and projects to foster unity among Filipinos."*** Bangsamoro cultural institutions must not be exclusively parochial — they must include the national narrative.[^3]

   No national law consistency requirement is imposed — unlike powers such as civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39) or education (Art. IX, Sec. 16).

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to culture and language?**

A. Five categories apply:

   (1) **State policy on culture (Art. II, Sec. 17)**: ***"The State shall give priority to education, science and technology, arts, culture, and sports to foster patriotism and nationalism."***[^9]

   (2) **National culture (Art. XIV, Sec. 14)**: ***"The State shall foster the preservation, enrichment, and dynamic evolution of a Filipino national culture based on the principle of unity in diversity in a climate of free artistic and intellectual expression."***[^10]

   (3) **Arts and letters (Art. XIV, Sec. 15)**: ***"Arts and letters shall enjoy the patronage of the State. The State shall conserve, promote, and popularize the nation's historical and cultural heritage and resources."***[^11]

   (4) **Cultural treasure (Art. XIV, Sec. 16)**: ***"All the country's artistic and historic wealth constitutes the cultural treasure of the nation and shall be under the protection of the State."***[^12]

   (5) **IP cultural rights (Art. XIV, Sec. 17)**: ***"The State shall recognize, respect, and protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities to preserve and develop their cultures, traditions, and institutions."***[^13]

   (6) **Autonomous region powers (Art. X, Sec. 20(8))**: ***"Preservation and development of the cultural heritage"*** is expressly listed as an autonomous region legislative power.[^14]

   (7) **Language (Art. XIV, Sec. 6-9)**: Filipino is the national language; Filipino and English are the official languages; regional languages are auxiliary.[^15]

**Q. What constitutional mandates for cultural legislation or institutions exist?**

A. Art. XIV, Sec. 14-18 establishes the State's affirmative obligations for cultural preservation, arts patronage, and cultural access. Art. X, Sec. 20(8) expressly authorizes autonomous regions to legislate on ***"preservation and development of the cultural heritage."*** These constitutional mandates provide the strongest basis for comprehensive Bangsamoro cultural heritage legislation.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**

A. The national framework centers on: **RA 10066** (National Cultural Heritage Act, as amended by RA 11961) — comprehensive heritage protection, conservation, and management; **RA 7356** (NCCA) — national cultural coordination; **RA 10086** (NHCP) — national historical commission; **RA 11333** (National Museum) — national museum system. Together, these laws provide: a **heritage classification system**, **regulatory framework** for cultural property, **institutional infrastructure**, and **enforcement mechanisms**.[^16][^17][^18][^19]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. (1) **RA 10066 / RA 11961** — **suppletory**: governs cultural heritage protection in BARMM absent a comprehensive Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code.[^16]
   (2) **RA 7356** — **coordination**: BCPCH coordinates with NCCA per Art. IX, Sec. 25.[^17]
   (3) **RA 10086** — **transfer mandate**: NHCP sites in BARMM to be transferred to BCPCH per Art. IX, Sec. 26.[^18]
   (4) **RA 11333** — **transfer mandate**: National Museum sites in BARMM to be transferred to BCPCH.[^19]
   (5) **RA 10573** — **directly applicable**: Sheikh Karimul Makhdum Mosque in Tawi-Tawi declared national historical landmark.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. **NCCA** exercises national cultural coordination. **NHCP** manages historical sites and markers. **National Museum** manages museum collections and cultural properties. All three retain operational presence in BARMM pending full implementation of the Art. IX, Sec. 26 transfer mandate.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **BCPCH** is the lead institution. BAA 13, Title XVI, Chapter 4, Sec. 46-60 defines its institutional status, mandate, powers, functions, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter XXII allocates its operational budget.[^20][^21]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. Five BAAs directly implement this power: **BAA 20** (dual calendar), **BAA 39** (Bangsamoro holidays), **BAA 60** (Hashim/Mimbantas memorial), **BAA 61** (Camp Abubakar memorial). Additionally, **BAA 1** (flag), **BAA 2** (emblem), **BAA 7** (hymn), and **BAA 16** (Parliament seal) implement the cultural identity dimension.

**Q. What are BCPCH's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Title XVI, Chapter 4 (Sec. 46-60) defines BCPCH comprehensively:

   **Institutional status** (Sec. 46): BCPCH is composed of a **Chairperson and six (6) Commissioners**, with ***"the same status as the other agencies or offices of the Bangsamoro Government attached to the Office of the Chief Minister."***[^20]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 47): Repeats the BOL Art. IX, Sec. 25 mandate verbatim — write Bangsamoro history, establish and sustain cultural institutions, coordinate with NCCA/NHCP/National Museum, establish libraries and museums, declare and restore historical shrines and cultural sites.[^22]

   **Site transfer** (Sec. 48): Repeats the BOL Art. IX, Sec. 26 mandate — ***"The National Government shall transfer the management of Bangsamoro historical and cultural sites."***[^23]

   **Powers** (Sec. 49) — six enumerated powers:
   - (a) ***"Identify, declare, validate, and monitor historical shrines and sites, including the institution or markers and landmarks"***
   - (b) ***"Recommend to the Bangsamoro Government regional policies and legislation on culture, arts, heritage promotion and development"***
   - (c) ***"Establish consultative mechanisms...for continuing dialogue between the Bangsamoro Government and the cultural groups"***
   - (d) ***"Accredit, register, and oversee cultural mapping initiatives and registry of cultural property"***
   - (e) ***"Solicit and accept donations, gifts, requests, and grants...subject to the approval of the Cabinet"***
   - (f) ***"Perform any and all other acts incident to or required by virtue of its creation"***[^24]

   **Functions** (Sec. 50) — eight enumerated functions:
   - (a) Support retrieval and conservation of Bangsamoro cultural artifacts
   - (b) Encourage preservation of ***"endangered human cultural resources such as weavers, chanters, dancers, and other craftsmen"***
   - (c) Support preservation of cultural and historical monuments, markers, sites
   - (d) Encourage establishment of ***"museums, libraries, archives"***
   - (e) Encourage private sector to establish museums and libraries
   - (f) Encourage scholarly research into Bangsamoro cultural traditions
   - (g) Encourage ***"writing of history from the Bangsamoro perspective"***
   - (h) Ensure standards of excellence through symposia, workshops, and publications[^25]

   **Composition** (Sec. 51): Seven Commissioners — five representing the major political subdivisions (Maguindanao, Lanao del Sur, Sulu, Basilan, Tawi-Tawi), plus two *ex officio* from non-Moro indigenous peoples and settler communities.[^26]

   **Provincial offices** (Sec. 56-57): Each Commissioner heads a provincial office executing cultural development programs at the provincial level.[^27]

   **Executive Director** (Sec. 58): Appointed by the Chief Minister, responsible for day-to-day operations, four-year fixed term.[^28]

**Q. What can BCPCH NOT do without a standalone cultural heritage code?**

A. BCPCH can identify, declare, validate, and monitor heritage sites under BAA 13. However, without standalone legislation, it cannot: (a) establish a **systematic heritage classification system** (tangible/intangible, movable/immovable, grades of significance); (b) impose **conservation standards and penalties** for heritage destruction; (c) create a **comprehensive cultural property registry** with legal effect; (d) regulate **excavation and artifact trade**; (e) establish a **language preservation and promotion framework**; (f) define **heritage buffer zones** and development controls around cultural sites; (g) codify the **site transfer process** from national agencies (Art. IX, Sec. 26).

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. The Parliament has expressed extensive engagement through **17 resolutions** touching culture and heritage (see Resolutions table above). These demonstrate strong legislative interest but are non-binding expressions — the legislative gap is in enacted law, not intent.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL **expressly creates the BCPCH** for this power (Art. IX, Sec. 24). The assignment is constitutionally and legislatively unambiguous. BCPCH covers the full scope of cultural heritage preservation. The **language dimension** is not fully covered by BCPCH — language preservation and promotion may require a separate institutional mechanism or an expansion of BCPCH's mandate.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. - **Lead agency**: BCPCH (BAA 13, Title XVI, Ch. 4; BAA 85, Ch. XXII)
   - **Supporting**: MBHTE — cultural education in schools (Art. IX, Sec. 14-19)
   - **Supporting**: OTWB — ceremonial and cultural functions of the Wali
   - **Supporting**: Bangsamoro Library and Archives (BAA 13, Book II, Sec. (9)) — institutional repository
   - **Cultural identity dimension**: Parliament has enacted BAAs 1, 2, 7, 16 (cultural symbols)

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **NCCA** provides cultural coordination and grant funding for cultural programs. **NHCP** manages historical markers and sites including those in BARMM (pending transfer). **National Museum** manages cultural properties and collections (pending transfer). These agencies maintain their operations in BARMM, though the BOL mandates management transfer for Bangsamoro-specific sites.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **partially complete**. BCPCH has been created and funded, but the **Art. IX, Sec. 26 transfer of site management** from NHCP, National Museum, and other agencies appears to remain incomplete. No inventory of sites to be transferred has been enacted, and no BAA codifies the transfer process, timeline, or post-transfer management standards.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. The Bangsamoro Government has: (a) **created BCPCH** as a fully constituted commission with 7 Commissioners and provincial offices; (b) enacted **4 culture-specific BAAs** (BAA 20, 39, 60, 61) and **4 cultural identity BAAs** (BAA 1, 2, 7, 16); (c) adopted **17 parliamentary resolutions** on cultural matters; (d) established the **Bangsamoro Library and Archives**; (e) begun cultural mapping and heritage identification at the provincial level. This is among the most actively legislated power areas, though the legislation is **piecemeal** rather than comprehensive.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three unique advantages:
   (1) **Islamic cultural heritage expertise** — BCPCH understands Bangsamoro Islamic cultural heritage (Sultanate records, mosque architecture, *okir* traditions, Arabic manuscripts) in ways that national cultural agencies do not.
   (2) **Ethno-linguistic depth** — BCPCH can operate in Meranao, Maguindanaon, Tausug, Sama, and Yakan — reaching communities that national agencies serve primarily in Filipino and English.
   (3) **Living cultural resource protection** — BAA 13 Sec. 50(b) mandates preservation of ***"endangered human cultural resources such as weavers, chanters, dancers, and other craftsmen"*** — a mandate the national system addresses less directly.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **primary constraint** is the absence of a **comprehensive Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code** — without it, BCPCH operates on the general mandate of BAA 13 without systematic heritage classification, conservation standards, penalty provisions, or regulatory tools. The **secondary constraint** is the **incomplete transfer** of site management from national agencies (Art. IX, Sec. 26).

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes — culture and language have a **significant Shari'ah dimension** for the Bangsamoro people. Bangsamoro cultural heritage is inseparable from Islamic heritage — the Sultanates, mosques, madaris, Islamic scholarship traditions, Arabic-script manuscripts, and Islamic artistic traditions (okir, geometric patterns, calligraphy) are simultaneously cultural and religious heritage. Three Islamic principles apply:

   (1) ***Hifz al-Din*** (preservation of faith) — preserving Islamic cultural heritage, including mosques, religious manuscripts, and Sultanate records, is an exercise of this *maqasid* objective [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

   (2) ***Hifz al-Nasl*** (preservation of lineage/identity) — preserving Sultanate genealogies, clan structures (*tarsila*), and ethno-linguistic identity serves this objective [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

   (3) **Arabic as liturgical language** — Arabic language preservation supports Qur'anic literacy, Islamic education, and religious practice [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not specifically reference Shari'ah in Art. IX, Sec. 24-26. However, Art. IX, Sec. 24's reference to ***"the rich cultural heritage of the Bangsamoro people and their Sultanates"*** necessarily encompasses Islamic cultural heritage. The **dual calendar** (BAA 20) and **Bangsamoro holidays** (BAA 39) reflect the Islamic dimension of cultural policy.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM has **broad authority to diverge** because: (1) as established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL imposes no national law consistency requirement — only a coordination obligation; (2) as established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), Art. X, Sec. 20(8) expressly lists ***"preservation and development of the cultural heritage"*** as an autonomous region legislative power; (3) the BOL creates BCPCH by name as the Bangsamoro cultural institution, signaling congressional intent for a distinct cultural governance framework.

   BARMM can: (a) establish a **distinct heritage classification system** recognizing Islamic, Sultanate, and indigenous heritage categories that RA 10066 does not specifically address; (b) create **Bangsamoro-specific heritage categories** (e.g., Sultanate heritage, liberation movement heritage, Islamic architectural heritage); (c) enact **language preservation legislation** that the national framework does not provide; (d) develop **culturally appropriate conservation standards** that account for Islamic requirements (mosque preservation, *halal* considerations in heritage sites).

   The **constitutional floor** is Art. XIV, Sec. 14 (***"unity in diversity"***) and Art. IX, Sec. 25's own requirement to ***"ensure the inclusion of Philippine history, culture, and heritage."***

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. The principal overlap is between **BCPCH** and the three national cultural agencies (NCCA, NHCP, National Museum). Art. IX, Sec. 26 mandates transfer of site management — but until a comprehensive code codifies the transfer, both BCPCH and national agencies may exercise parallel functions over the same sites. Additionally, the **cultural property registry** function (BCPCH Sec. 49(d)) overlaps with NCCA's cultural mapping functions under RA 11961.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB 204** (comprehensive cultural heritage code) is the most significant — if enacted, it would address heritage classification, conservation, management, and enforcement. **PB 176** (English-Arabic signage) addresses language policy. **PB 203** (Peace and Culture Centers) and **PB 299** (Bangsamoro history museum) address cultural infrastructure. Even if all pending bills pass, a **language preservation and promotion framework** and a **site transfer codification** remain unaddressed.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 6** (Cultural Diversity and Heritage) is entirely dependent on this power — its goals of preserving Bangsamoro identity, protecting heritage sites, and promoting intercultural understanding require comprehensive cultural heritage legislation. **BDP Chapter 10** (Peace and Security) depends on cultural preservation as a foundation for social cohesion. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda**, **Pillar 5 (Social Cohesion)**, requires cultural diversity and language preservation as foundations for an inclusive Bangsamoro identity that embraces all constituent peoples.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a comprehensive cultural heritage code: (a) **heritage sites** continue to deteriorate without systematic conservation standards; (b) the **Marawi cultural recovery** (PB 181) lacks a legal framework for recovering and protecting cultural properties lost during the siege; (c) **intangible cultural heritage** — languages, oral traditions, performing arts, traditional crafts — continue to erode without preservation programs; (d) the **Art. IX, Sec. 26 site transfer** remains incomplete, with Bangsamoro heritage sites managed by national agencies unfamiliar with their cultural significance; (e) **language diversity** declines as Bangsamoro languages lack official support in education and governance.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does this power relate to power (kk) Libraries, museums, and historical, cultural, and archaeological sites?**

A. Powers (p) and (kk) **overlap significantly**. Art. V, Sec. 2(kk) — ***"Libraries and museums, and historical, cultural, and archaeological sites"*** — is the institutional dimension of cultural heritage. Art. V, Sec. 2(p) — ***"Culture and language"*** — is the broader policy dimension. BCPCH exercises both powers under a single institutional mandate. Art. IX, Sec. 25 explicitly assigns BCPCH the function of establishing ***"libraries and museums"*** and declaring and restoring ***"historical shrines and cultural sites."*** A comprehensive cultural heritage code should address both powers together.

**Q. What languages are spoken in BARMM and how does the BOL address them?**

A. BARMM's major languages include **Maguindanaon** (Maguindanao provinces), **Meranao** (Lanao del Sur), **Tausug** (Sulu), **Sama** (Tawi-Tawi, Basilan), **Yakan** (Basilan), plus **Iranun**, **Teduray**, **Lambangian**, and other indigenous languages. Arabic serves as a liturgical and scholarly language. The BOL is **largely silent** on specific language policy — Art. V, Sec. 2(p) grants the power but no provision specifies which languages should be preserved, promoted, or used in governance. BAA 20's dual calendar (Hijri/Gregorian) is the closest to language-adjacent legislation. A **Bangsamoro Languages Act** is needed to codify language rights, preservation programs, and official language use in regional governance and education.

**Q. What is the significance of the "history writing" mandate?**

A. Art. IX, Sec. 25 assigns BCPCH the ***"primary responsibility to write the history of the Bangsamoro people."*** This is an extraordinary mandate — it authorizes a government institution to produce an **official historical narrative**. The significance is that Bangsamoro history has historically been written from external perspectives (colonial, national). The BOL empowers the Bangsamoro people to write their own history — including the narrative of the Sultanates, colonialism, the Moro liberation struggle, and the peace process. BAA 13 Sec. 50(g) reinforces this: ***"encourage and support the writing of history from the Bangsamoro perspective."*** This mandate has both cultural and political dimensions — it shapes collective identity and informs the Bangsamoro people's understanding of their place in Philippine and world history.
