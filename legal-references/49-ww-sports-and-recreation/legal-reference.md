# (ww) Sports and Recreation

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ww)** — "Sports and recreation;"

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over sports and recreation as an enumerated power under Article V, Section 2(ww).[^1] This power is elaborated in Article IX, Section 20, which mandates the Bangsamoro educational system to "develop and maintain an integrated and comprehensive physical education program" that "shall develop healthy, disciplined, innovative, and productive individuals, and promote sportsmanship, cooperation, and teamwork."[^2] The provision further requires the Bangsamoro educational system to "encourage and support sports programs, league competitions, indigenous games, martial arts, and amateur sports including training for regional, national, and international competitions."

Notably, Section 20 explicitly directs the Parliament to "pass a law creating a Bangsamoro sports commission."[^3] This is one of the BOL's direct legislative mandates — the Parliament is required, not merely authorized, to create the sports commission through legislation. The sports and recreation power is thus linked to the education power (v) through physical education but has its own institutional identity through the BSC.

The scope encompasses physical education within the formal education system, competitive sports at all levels (regional, national, international), indigenous games and martial arts, amateur sports development, and recreational programs. The BOL does not impose a national law consistency requirement on this power.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Bangsamoro Sports Commission (BSC) is the lead institution, created by BAA 12 as mandated by the BOL.[^4] BAA 12 fulfills the BOL's explicit directive in Article IX, Section 20 for the Parliament to create a sports commission. The BSC is funded through BAA 85, Chapter XXI, and is responsible for sports development, athlete support, and coordination of sports programs across the region.[^5]

BAA 12 is the only standalone BAA implementing this power. The BSC exercises a broad mandate under this law, including supporting athletes, organizing regional competitions such as the BARMMAA (Bangsamoro Regional Athletic Association) Meet, and coordinating participation in national and international competitions. MBHTE supports the power through physical education programs within the formal education system, as directed by Article IX, Section 20.

Parliamentary resolutions demonstrate extraordinary engagement with sports. The Parliament has adopted dozens of resolutions recognizing Bangsamoro athletes: Resolution 110 commended chess player Al-Basher Buto; Resolution 144 commended Hidilyn Diaz for her Olympic gold; Resolution 217 paid tribute to three-time Olympian Amman Jalmaani; Resolution 328 congratulated Fatima Ayesha Amirul for a SEA Games gold in soft tennis; Resolution 368 commended Samsodin Abdulrahman for a weightlifting gold; Resolution 486 congratulated Carlos Yulo for 2024 Paris Olympics gold medals. Multiple resolutions celebrate BARMMAA Meet results and Palarong Pambansa achievements. This volume of resolutions signals strong parliamentary support for sports as a vehicle for Bangsamoro identity and pride.

Pending legislation includes Bill 377 (Bangsamoro Sports Institute), Bill 198/200/201/202/207/208 (various incentive bills for athletes and topnotchers), and Bill 413 (Bangsamoro Grand Athletic and Sports Complexes). These indicate demand for expanded institutional and infrastructure support beyond the BSC's current mandate.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not require this power to be exercised in accordance with national law. No Article VI intergovernmental body exists for sports. The BSC operates autonomously within BARMM, though it coordinates with the Philippine Sports Commission (PSC) for national competition participation, athlete development pipelines, and access to national training programs.

Republic Act No. 6847 (Philippine Sports Commission Act) and Republic Act No. 9850 (Philippine National Games Act) apply suppletorily where no BAA covers a specific sports matter.[^6] RA 10699 (National Athletes, Coaches, and Trainers Benefits and Incentives Act) provides national-level incentives for athletes, which Bangsamoro athletes can access in addition to any regional incentives established by the Parliament. Once the Parliament enacts a comprehensive sports development code, Bangsamoro policy would govern regional sports matters, with national law applying for national-level competitions and PSC coordination.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

While BAA 12 creates the institutional foundation through the BSC, the power lacks the comprehensive legislative framework needed for full implementation. No Bangsamoro Sports Development Code exists to codify: sports facility standards for provinces and municipalities, athlete development pathways from grassroots to elite, coach and trainer certification and incentive frameworks, indigenous games preservation and promotion programs, recreational sports programs for non-competitive participation, and sports tourism development.

The athlete incentive framework is particularly fragmented. Multiple bills (198, 200, 201, 202, 207, 208) propose various forms of cash incentives for athletes and topnotchers, but none has been enacted. A unified Bangsamoro Athletes and Coaches Incentives Act would consolidate these proposals, providing a clear and sustainable incentive structure that complements RA 10699 at the national level.

Sports infrastructure legislation is also absent. Bill 413 proposes grand athletic and sports complexes, and Bill 377 envisions a Bangsamoro Sports Institute for elite athlete development, but neither has been enacted. A dedicated sports infrastructure act would provide the legal basis for systematic investment in sports facilities across the region, rather than relying on individual appropriation items in the annual GAA.

The indigenous games dimension — explicitly mentioned in Article IX, Section 20 — remains entirely unlegislated. A provisions within a sports development code could establish programs for preserving and promoting traditional Bangsamoro and indigenous sports, including martial arts traditions.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

Sports and recreation are addressed in the BDP 2023-2028 Chapter 9 (Social Services), which recognizes physical fitness and sports development as components of social development.[^7] The BDP's targets for youth development and social cohesion benefit from robust sports programs. Without a comprehensive sports development code, the BSC operates with organizational authority but lacks the codified standards and infrastructure mandates needed to systematically implement the BDP's sports and recreation goals across all provinces and municipalities.

## Legislative Gaps

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type A**: No comprehensive sports development code | BSC created by BAA 12 but operates without codified standards for facility development, athlete pathways, coaching certification, or indigenous games preservation | Enact Bangsamoro Sports Development Code covering facility standards, athlete development, coach certification, indigenous games, and recreation programs |
| **Type A**: No athlete incentive legislation | Multiple bills (198, 200, 201, 202, 207, 208) propose incentives for athletes but none enacted; no unified framework | Enact Bangsamoro Athletes and Coaches Incentives Act consolidating pending bills |
| **Type A**: No sports infrastructure legislation | No BAA provides for systematic sports facility investment beyond annual GAA appropriations | Enact Bangsamoro Sports Infrastructure Act (Bills 377 and 413 pending) for sports institute and athletic complexes |
