# (ww) Sports and Recreation

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ww)** — ***"Sports and recreation;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(ww) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement — Art. IX, Sec. 20 mandates creation of a Bangsamoro sports commission without any "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier referencing national law. ## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**

A. Art. V, Sec. 2(ww) grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over ***"Sports and recreation."*** This is a broadly stated enumerated power that encompasses the full range of sports governance — policy-making, promotion, regulation, development, competition, facilities, athlete welfare, and recreational programs. The grant carries no express qualification requiring national law consistency, making it one of the less constrained powers in the Art. V, Sec. 2 enumeration.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. The primary elaborating provision is **Art. IX, Sec. 20**, which situates sports within the education framework and imposes three specific obligations:

1. **Physical education integration** — ***"The Bangsamoro educational system shall develop and maintain an integrated and comprehensive physical education program."***[^2] This positions physical education as a core component of the Bangsamoro educational system, not merely an extracurricular activity.

2. **Sports program support** — ***"The Bangsamoro educational system shall encourage and support sports programs, league competitions, indigenous games, martial arts, and amateur sports including training for regional, national, and international competitions."***[^3] This names five specific activity categories: (a) sports programs, (b) league competitions, (c) indigenous games, (d) martial arts, and (e) amateur sports including international competition training.

3. **Legislative mandate** — ***"The Parliament shall pass a law creating a Bangsamoro sports commission."***[^4] This is a direct legislative mandate — not discretionary. The Parliament is obligated to create the commission by law.

Additionally, **Art. IX, Sec. 13** connects sports to youth development, stating that the Bangsamoro Government shall ***"promote mental and physical fitness through sports"*** as part of its obligations toward the youth.[^5]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. IX, Sec. 20 expressly mandates: ***"The Parliament shall pass a law creating a Bangsamoro sports commission."***[^4] This mandate has been **fulfilled** — the Parliament enacted BAA No. 12 (The Bangsamoro Sports Commission Act of 2020), creating the BSC.[^6] No other specific legislative mandate for this power area remains unfulfilled. However, the BOL's broad grant under Art. V, Sec. 2(ww) authorizes the Parliament to enact additional legislation — such as a comprehensive sports development code, athlete incentives act, or sports infrastructure program — under the general welfare clause of Art. V, Sec. 3.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL directs the Parliament to create ***"a Bangsamoro sports commission"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 20).[^4] The Parliament fulfilled this mandate by enacting BAA No. 12, which created the **Bangsamoro Sports Commission (BSC)**.[^6] The BOL does not name any other institution for this power. However, BAA 13 additionally creates the **Bureau for Physical Education and Sports Development (BPESD)** under MBHTE, handling school-based sports and physical education distinct from BSC's broader mandate.[^8]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places **no subject-specific limitations** on the sports and recreation power. The only constraints are the universal ones that apply to all 55 enumerated powers:

- Art. V, Sec. 2 (chapeau): ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law"***[^1] — the constitutional framework applies.
- Art. VI, Sec. 1: Presidential general supervision to ensure laws are faithfully executed.[^9]
- Art. XII, Sec. 26: foreign grants require Presidential clearance — relevant to BSC's grant-acceptance function.[^10]

The absence of any "consistent with national law" or "in accordance with" qualifier makes this power one of the **least constrained** enumerated powers, contrasting with powers like civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39 — "in accordance with existing national civil service laws") or education (Art. IX, Sec. 16 — "conform to the minimum standards set by the National Government").

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**

A. The constitutional framework for sports and recreation operates at two levels:

- **Universal constraint (all 55 powers)**: Art. X, Sec. 15 — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."***
- **State policy**: Art. II, Sec. 17 — ***"The State shall give priority to education, science and technology, arts, culture, and sports to foster patriotism and nationalism, accelerate social progress, and promote total human liberation and development."***[^11] This elevates sports to a national priority alongside education, establishing a constitutional imperative that both national and regional governments must uphold.
- **Sports-specific provision**: Art. XIV, Sec. 19(1) — ***"The State shall promote physical education and encourage sports programs, league competitions, and amateur sports, including training for international competitions, to foster self-discipline, teamwork, and excellence for the development of a healthy and alert citizenry."***[^12]
- **Educational institutions mandate**: Art. XIV, Sec. 19(2) — ***"All educational institutions shall undertake regular sports activities throughout the country in cooperation with athletic clubs and other sectors."***[^13]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution does not create an individual **right to sports** in the same way it creates rights to education (Art. XIV, Sec. 1) or health (Art. XIII, Sec. 11). Rather, it imposes a **state duty** to promote sports — the obligation runs from the State to the people, not as a justiciable individual right. The constitutional provisions establish a **floor**: no government — national or regional — may fail to promote physical education and sports programs. Both the national government (through PSC) and the Bangsamoro Government (through BSC) must meet this floor.

The **equal protection clause** (Art. III, Sec. 1) applies to athlete incentives and awards — BARMM may create additional regional incentives but may not discriminate against athletes based on religion, ethnicity, or province of origin within the region.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. XIV, Sec. 19 is a directive provision — it mandates state promotion of sports but does not require Congress to create specific legislation or institutions by name. The creation of PSC (RA 6847) and NAS (RA 11470) are congressional implementations of this directive. The BOL's Art. IX, Sec. 20 mirrors Art. XIV, Sec. 19(1) almost verbatim, then goes further by expressly mandating the creation of a Bangsamoro sports commission.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**

A. The national sports framework consists of:

1. **Institutional framework**: RA 6847 creates the **Philippine Sports Commission (PSC)** as the sole national policy-making and coordinating body for amateur sports, attached to the Office of the President. PSC formulates national sports policy, coordinates with national sports associations, and manages national sports facilities.

2. **School sports**: RA 10588 institutionalizes the **Palarong Pambansa** (national school games) — the annual nationwide school sports competition. BARMM participates through its regional qualifying event, the BARMMAA Meet.

3. **Athlete incentives**: RA 10699 provides mandatory cash rewards and non-monetary benefits for national athletes winning medals in Olympic Games, Asian Games, SEA Games, and world championships. RA 7549 exempts sports prizes and awards from income tax.

4. **Student-athlete protection**: RA 10676 regulates residency requirements and prohibits commercialization of student-athletes.

5. **Specialized institutions**: RA 11470 creates the National Academy of Sports; RA 11214 establishes the Philippine Sports Training Center. Multiple laws create regional sports academies and training centers.

6. **National martial art**: RA 9850 declares Arnis as the national martial art and sport.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. National sports laws apply **suppletorily** — they govern where no BAA provides otherwise. Specifically:

- **RA 6847** (PSC Act): PSC retains national jurisdiction; BSC is the regional counterpart coordinating with PSC. Both agencies co-exist — PSC governs national-level programs, BSC governs regional-level programs.
- **RA 10699** (Athlete Incentives): applies **directly** to Bangsamoro athletes competing as Philippine national team members. BSC may provide **additional** regional incentives.
- **RA 10588** (Palarong Pambansa): applies **directly** — BARMM participates in the national school games.
- **RA 10676** (Student-Athlete Protection): applies **suppletorily** to BARMM educational institutions.
- **RA 7549** (Tax Exemption): applies **directly** as a national tax provision.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. The **Philippine Sports Commission (PSC)** retains national jurisdiction over amateur sports development. PSC does not have a BARMM regional office — instead, BSC serves as the regional policy-making body, coordinating with PSC for national-level programs. The **Department of Education (DepEd)** manages the Palarong Pambansa nationally, with MBHTE serving as the regional counterpart for school sports in BARMM.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Bangsamoro Sports Commission (BSC)** is the primary body, established by BAA No. 12 and codified in BAA 13, Book VI, Title I, Chapter 3, Sections 27–45.[^6] [^14] BSC is attached to the Office of the Chief Minister and exercises corporate powers. The **Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE)** has a supporting role — its Bureau for Physical Education and Sports Development (BPESD) handles school-based sports and physical education programs (BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Sec. 3(a)(1) and Sec. 13(d)).[^8] BAA 85, Chapter XXI provides BSC's operational budget for FY 2026.[^15]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **BAA No. 12** (The Bangsamoro Sports Commission Act of 2020) is the sole standalone legislation.[^6] It fulfills the Art. IX, Sec. 20 mandate to create a Bangsamoro sports commission. Key features:

- **Mandate**: formulate policy, promote, regulate, coordinate, and implement programs for sports; make sports a tool for peace and transitional justice (Sec. 6)
- **Powers and functions**: organized into three domains — (a) plan and policy-making (7 functions), (b) coordination and sports promotion (10 functions), (c) regulation (5 functions), plus corporate and grant-acceptance powers (Sec. 11)
- **Composition**: Chairperson + 4 regular Commissioners (one per province) + 2 *ex officio* Commissioners representing IP and settler communities (Sec. 7)
- **Term**: 7 years, with staggered initial terms — Chairperson (7 years), 2 commissioners (5 years), 2 commissioners (3 years) (Secs. 8, 26)
- **Organizational structure**: Commission Proper, Office of the Chairperson, Office of the Secretariat with three divisions — Policy Research and Development; Regulatory Promotion and Coordination; Financial and Administrative (Secs. 12, 19–23)
- **Attachment**: attached agency of the Office of the Chief Minister (Sec. 4)

**Q. What are the lead agency's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title I, Chapter 3 (Sections 27–45) codifies the BSC's institutional framework within the Administrative Code:[^14]

- **Declaration of Policy** (Sec. 27): ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall promote physical education and encourage sports programs, league competitions, and amateur sports including training for regional, national, and international competitions, to foster self-discipline, teamwork, and excellence for the development of a healthy and alert citizenry."*** Also: develop innovative and productive individuals, promote sportsmanship, cooperation, encourage and support indigenous games, martial arts, and amateur sports, and ***"recognize the diverse cultures of the region and consider gender, culture and religious sensitivity."***
- **Mandate** (Sec. 28): ***"The Bangsamoro Sports Commission (BSC) is mandated to formulate policy, promote, regulate, coordinate, and implement programs for sports. It shall also provide for system, support and assistance in the development of sports. It shall, as a policy, make sure that sports is a tool for peace and transitional justice."***
- **Nature** (Sec. 29): corporate powers; policy-making and coordinating body of all amateur sports development programs in the Bangsamoro. School sports development is formulated and implemented by MBHTE with BSC's assistance. **Attached agency of the Office of the Chief Minister.**
- **Powers and functions** (Sec. 30): identical to BAA 12, Sec. 11 — three functional domains with 22 enumerated functions plus 4 corporate powers.
- **Organizational divisions** (Secs. 41–43): (1) Policy, Research, and Development Division — research, policy formulation, knowledge and skills development; (2) Regulatory, Promotion, and Coordination Division — rules and regulations, compliance, monitoring, sports education and training, public information, coordination with sports associations; (3) Financial and Administrative Division — budget, finance, planning, management, IT, personnel, records, supplies.
- **Assistance provision** (Sec. 44): BSC may call upon any Bangsamoro Government entity for assistance; all heads of MOAs are enjoined to render full cooperation.
- **Direct budget access** (Sec. 45): BSC budget is directly transferred to the Commission for operational efficiency.

Additionally, MBHTE's mandate under BAA 13, Book IV, Title II includes:
- Sec. 3(a)(1): powers over ***"physical education and sports"*** as part of basic education
- Sec. 3(h): ***"Develop and maintain an integrated and comprehensive physical education program"***
- Sec. 13(d): creates the **Bureau for Physical Education and Sports Development (BPESD)** under the supervision of the Director-General for Basic Education

**Q. What can the lead agency NOT do without standalone legislation beyond BAA 12 and BAA 13?**

A. BAA 12 already provides a robust institutional framework for BSC. However, it does not cover:

- **Comprehensive sports code**: BAA 12 creates the institution but does not establish a comprehensive regulatory code with detailed standards for sports facilities, athlete welfare, doping control, dispute resolution mechanisms, or sports association governance standards.
- **Athlete incentive framework**: BSC can provide incentives under BAA 12, Sec. 11(b)(9) but there is no codified rate schedule, eligibility criteria, or funding mechanism — pending bills (PBs 198, 200, 201, 202, 207, 208) would address this.
- **Sports infrastructure program**: BSC can establish facilities under BAA 12, Sec. 11(b)(10) "as far as practicable" but there is no capital investment plan or institutional framework for constructing and managing sports complexes — PB No. 413 would address this.
- **Sports academy**: no legislation establishes a specialized sports educational institution — PB No. 377 (Bangsamoro Sports Institute) would address this.
- **Penalty provisions**: BSC can impose sanctions on sports associations and athletes (BAA 12, Sec. 11(c)(1)) but the law lacks a detailed penalty schedule, due process procedures, or appellate mechanism.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No resolution specifically calls for new sports legislation. However, the 19 commendatory resolutions listed in the Parliamentary Resolutions table demonstrate sustained parliamentary attention to sports achievement, signaling political interest in the sector. Resolution No. 342 commending MBHTE for the BARMMAA Meet signals recognition of the school sports system's importance.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. The **BSC** is the correct lead for power (ww). The BOL expressly mandates the creation of a ***"Bangsamoro sports commission"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 20), and BAA No. 12 fulfills this mandate. BSC's mandate covers the full scope of power (ww) — sports policy, promotion, regulation, coordination, implementation, and recreation. The assignment is validated by BAA 85, Chapter XXI (dedicated budget chapter) and BAA 13, Book VI, Title I, Chapter 3 (Administrative Code codification). The one qualification is that **school-based sports and physical education** fall under MBHTE through the BPESD (BAA 13, Sec. 29 — school sports development is formulated and implemented by MBHTE with BSC's assistance). This division is appropriate — MBHTE handles the educational system's sports component while BSC handles the broader sports development mandate.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. The institutional landscape consists of:

- **Lead agency**: Bangsamoro Sports Commission (BSC) — created by BAA 12, codified in BAA 13 Sec. 27–45, budgeted in BAA 85 Ch. XXI. **Operational.**
- **Supporting ministry**: Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE) — through the Bureau for Physical Education and Sports Development (BPESD), BAA 13, Sec. 13(d). Handles school sports and physical education programs. **Operational.**
- **Supporting commission**: Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC) — coordinates with BSC on youth sports engagement (BAA 12, Sec. 11(b)(3); BAA 13, Sec. 30(b)(3)). **Operational.**
- **Supporting ministry**: Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD) — coordinates with BSC on sports for marginalized children, persons with disability, and vulnerable sectors (BAA 13, Sec. 30(b)(3)). **Operational.**

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The only BOL-mandated institution for this power — the Bangsamoro sports commission — has been created (BAA 12). No unfulfilled institutional mandates remain for power (ww).

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Pending legislation suggests two new institutions:

1. **Bangsamoro Sports Institute (BSI)** — PB No. 377 proposes a specialized sports educational institution integrating academic and sports curricula. This would be a new entity not named in the BOL but authorized under the general welfare clause (Art. V, Sec. 3) and the education power (Art. V, Sec. 2(v)).

2. **Bangsamoro Grand Athletic and Sports Complexes** — PB No. 413 proposes establishing sports complexes. These would be infrastructure assets managed by BSC or a dedicated entity, not named in the BOL but authorized by Art. V, Sec. 2(ww) and the general welfare clause.

Neither is BOL-mandated, but both would strengthen BSC's capacity to fulfill its mandate.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **Philippine Sports Commission (PSC)** exercises national-level sports authority but does not have a dedicated regional office in BARMM. PSC's presence is primarily through:

- National-level program coordination (e.g., national sports development plan)
- Funding and support for national athletes from BARMM
- Oversight of national sports associations operating within BARMM
- Coordination with BSC for national and international competition participation

The **Department of Education (DepEd)** administers the Palarong Pambansa nationally, with MBHTE serving as the regional implementing partner for BARMM's participation.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. Key national programs operating in BARMM:

- **Palarong Pambansa** — annual national school games; BARMM participates through the BARMMAA Meet qualifying process (BSC-MBHTE coordination, BAA 13, Sec. 30(b)(2))
- **PSC grassroots sports programs** — national amateur sports development at the community level
- **National athlete support** — Bangsamoro athletes who make national teams receive incentives under RA 10699

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. Sports governance is **substantially devolved**. The creation of BSC by BAA 12 established a fully functional regional sports commission that mirrors PSC's national mandate at the regional level. BSC operates as a parallel body — not subordinate to PSC — with its own policy-making, regulatory, and coordination authority within BARMM. There is no pending devolution of PSC functions to BSC because PSC never operated through a regional office structure that required transfer. The relationship is **coordinating**, not hierarchical.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes — in two areas:

1. **National and international competition participation**: BSC coordinates with PSC, the Philippine Olympic Committee, and national sports associations for BARMM athletes participating in events beyond the region. Both agencies have jurisdiction — BSC at the regional level (selection, training, support) and PSC at the national level (team composition, international representation).

2. **School sports**: MBHTE administers school sports within BARMM, but participation in the Palarong Pambansa requires coordination with DepEd's national framework. The BARMMAA Meet operates under MBHTE-BSC coordination, while the Palarong Pambansa operates under DepEd's national rules.

These overlaps are **by design** — the BOL envisions coordination, not replacement.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. Key accomplishments:

- **Institutional establishment**: BSC created by BAA 12 (2020), codified in BAA 13, funded through successive appropriations acts including BAA 85 (FY 2026). The Commission is operational with a Chairperson and Commissioners.
- **BARMMAA Meet**: the Bangsamoro Athletic Association Meet is conducted as the regional school athletic competition, serving as the qualifying event for the Palarong Pambansa.
- **Athlete recognition**: Parliament has adopted 19 resolutions commending Bangsamoro athletes across multiple sports — chess, weightlifting, taekwondo, karate, jiu jitsu, badminton, beach volleyball, soft tennis, and volleyball — demonstrating growing regional sports culture.
- **BDP integration**: sports development is included in BDP 2023–2028, Chapter 9, with three specific programs: Athlete Profiling and Data Banking, Bangsamoro Talent Identification, and Laro't Saya sa Komunidad.
- **Legislative pipeline**: 8 pending bills address gaps in athlete incentives (PBs 198, 200, 201, 202, 207, 208), sports education (PB 377), and sports infrastructure (PB 413).

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has a distinctive advantage in three areas:

- **Gender and religious sensitivity**: BAA 12's declaration of policy requires consideration of ***"gender, culture and religious sensitivity"*** — a feature absent from the national PSC Act (RA 6847). This allows BSC to design sports programs that accommodate Islamic values, including women-only events, religiously appropriate sportswear, and scheduling around prayer times.
- **Sports as a peace tool**: BAA 12 mandates that ***"sports is a tool for peace and transitional justice"*** (Sec. 6) — a unique mandate absent from RA 6847 that positions BSC as a peacebuilding instrument in a post-conflict region.
- **Indigenous games**: Art. IX, Sec. 20 and BAA 12 specifically mandate support for ***"indigenous games"*** and ***"martial arts"*** — BAA 12, Sec. 11(a)(3) mandates research on the "promotion and preservation of indigenous sports," providing a cultural preservation dimension absent from the national framework.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint is sports infrastructure** — the lack of fully equipped sports facilities and complexes. BAA 12, Sec. 11(b)(10) authorizes BSC to ***"establish, supervise, manage and maintain fully-equipped sports facilities and centers in strategic places"*** but qualifies this with ***"as far as practicable."*** The absence of a capital investment program (PB No. 413 would address this) and limited budget allocation restricts BSC's ability to build the physical infrastructure required for serious athlete development and competition hosting. Secondary constraints include the lack of a codified athlete incentive framework (addressed by multiple pending bills) and the absence of a sports academy (PB No. 377).

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national system has four advantages BARMM has not yet replicated:

- **Legislative depth**: multiple laws covering sports from different angles — PSC creation (RA 6847), Palarong Pambansa institutionalization (RA 10588), athlete incentives (RA 10699), student-athlete protection (RA 10676), tax exemption for prizes (RA 7549), National Academy of Sports (RA 11470), Philippine Sports Training Center (RA 11214), Philippine Sports Hall of Fame (RA 8757). BARMM has BAA 12 as its sole sports legislation.
- **Infrastructure**: the Philippine Sports Training Center (RA 11214), National Academy of Sports (RA 11470), and multiple regional sports academies established by law. BARMM has no comparable facility established by legislation.
- **Codified incentives**: RA 10699 provides mandatory cash rewards at specific rates for Olympic, Asian Games, SEA Games, and world championship medals. BSC's incentive function under BAA 12 is discretionary, not codified.
- **Athlete protection**: RA 10676 provides a legal framework for student-athlete protection — residency requirements, anti-commercialization provisions. BARMM has no equivalent legislation.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework does not accommodate:

- **Religious sensitivity**: PSC programs do not account for Islamic values in sports programming — mixed-gender events, non-*halal* food at competition venues, scheduling conflicts with Islamic prayer times and religious observances.
- **Indigenous Bangsamoro sports**: the national system recognizes Arnis (RA 9850) but does not specifically promote or preserve Bangsamoro indigenous games and martial arts such as *sipa*, *suntukan*, or traditional Maranao and Tausug combat sports.
- **Post-conflict context**: PSC programs are not designed for conflict-affected communities. BSC's unique mandate to use sports as a tool for peace and transitional justice addresses a need the national system does not.
- **Geographic access**: PSC's physical infrastructure and national programs are concentrated in urban centers, with limited reach in island provinces (Sulu, Tawi-Tawi, Basilan) and remote mainland areas of the Bangsamoro.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

- **Replicate**: RA 10699's codified incentive framework — establish specific cash reward rates for Bangsamoro athletes at different competition levels (regional, national, international). The pending bills (PBs 198, 200, 201, 202, 207, 208) move in this direction.
- **Adapt**: RA 11470's National Academy of Sports model — create a Bangsamoro Sports Institute (PB No. 377) adapted for Islamic values, indigenous sports, and Bangsamoro cultural context. Similarly, adapt RA 10676's student-athlete protection framework for BARMM's educational system.
- **Replace**: none — the national framework does not need to be entirely replaced, only supplemented and contextualized for BARMM's cultural, religious, and post-conflict realities.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Sports and recreation has an **indirect but meaningful** Shari'ah dimension. Islamic law does not regulate sports per se, but several principles inform how sports programs should be designed and conducted in a Muslim-majority region. BAA 12 explicitly recognizes this by requiring consideration of ***"gender, culture and religious sensitivity"*** (Sec. 2).[^16]

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not reference Shari'ah directly in Art. IX, Sec. 20 (the sports provision). However, BSC operates within the Bangsamoro Government's general framework, which includes Art. X, Sec. 4 — the Parliament's power to enact Shari'ah laws on ***"personal, family, property, commercial, and criminal matters"*** — and Art. IX, Sec. 4 — recognition and protection of ***"customs, beliefs, traditions."*** These general provisions provide a legal basis for Shari'ah-informed sports programming without requiring specific Shari'ah legislation for sports.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four principles are directly relevant:

- ***Hifz al-nafs*** (preservation of life and health) — physical fitness is encouraged as stewardship of the body, which is a trust from God. This provides an Islamic imperative for sports development.
- ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — sports as a vehicle for community welfare, preventing social harms (drugs, criminality, violent extremism). BAA 12 explicitly adopts this principle in Sec. 11(a)(5).
- ***Haya'*** (modesty) — informs gender-sensitive sports programming. Women-only facilities, appropriate sportswear, and separate competition spaces align with Islamic modesty principles.
- ***Shura*** (consultation) — the BSC's stakeholder engagement and coordination functions (BAA 12, Sec. 11(b)) reflect consultative governance consistent with Islamic principles.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. The approach should be **principled, not mandatory** — legislation guided by Islamic values without mandating specific compliance mechanisms. BAA 12 already takes this approach: it does not mandate Shari'ah compliance for sports programs but requires ***"gender, culture and religious sensitivity."*** Future legislation — particularly PB No. 377 (Sports Institute) — should incorporate Shari'ah-informed facility design (prayer rooms, *halal* food services, gender-appropriate spaces) as standard features rather than optional add-ons.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM has **broad latitude to diverge** from the national sports framework. The BOL does not require consistency with national sports law — no "in accordance with" or "consistent with" qualifier applies to Art. V, Sec. 2(ww) or Art. IX, Sec. 20. The only constraints are the constitutional floor established in the Constitutional Considerations section (Art. XIV, Sec. 19 — the State must promote sports) and the universal limitations identified in the BOL Grant and Scope section (constitutional framework, Presidential general supervision, foreign grant clearance).

BARMM has already exercised this divergence authority:

- BSC's mandate includes ***"sports as a tool for peace and transitional justice"*** — absent from RA 6847.
- BSC explicitly requires ***"gender, culture and religious sensitivity"*** — absent from the national framework.
- BAA 12 mandates promotion of ***"indigenous games"*** and ***"martial arts"*** as cultural preservation — a distinct emphasis.

Additional divergence opportunities include:
- Establishing a **regional athlete incentive framework** with different rates and eligibility criteria from RA 10699
- Creating **gender-separated sports facilities and programs** as standard policy
- Developing a **Bangsamoro Sports Institute** with integrated Islamic education — going beyond RA 11470's secular model
- Establishing **sports-for-peace programs** targeting ex-combatants and conflict-affected youth

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. Bangsamoro athletes competing at the national level remain subject to PSC rules and national sports association regulations. BARMM's regional standards (e.g., gender-sensitive policies, indigenous games promotion) apply within the region. For cross-boundary operations — such as athletes transferring between BARMM and non-BARMM regions — BSC's coordination with PSC and national sports associations ensures recognition of athlete credentials and competition results. Student-athlete transfer rules would need to align with both RA 10676 and any future BARMM student-athlete protection legislation.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. BAA No. 12 has already been enacted and governs sports governance within BARMM. National sports laws (RA 6847, RA 10588, RA 10699, etc.) apply **suppletorily** — they fill gaps where BAA 12 is silent (e.g., specific incentive rates, student-athlete protection standards, tax treatment of prizes). If the Parliament enacts additional legislation (e.g., a comprehensive sports development code or athlete incentives act), it would further narrow the suppletory role of national law within BARMM while maintaining coordination with the national system for national and international competitions.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. The BSC-PSC relationship is **coordinating, not hierarchical**, which creates two areas of potential overlap:

1. **Athlete selection for national competitions**: when BARMM athletes qualify for national teams, BSC and PSC share responsibility — BSC for regional selection and support, PSC for national team composition. No formal protocol governs disagreements over athlete selection or support allocation.

2. **Sports association accreditation**: BSC accredits regional sports organizations (BAA 12, Sec. 11(c)(2)) while PSC coordinates with national sports associations. A regional sports association seeking to participate in national-level competitions must satisfy both BSC's regional accreditation and the relevant national sports association's requirements.

Neither overlap creates a critical jurisdictional conflict — the relationship is cooperative. A **BSC-PSC coordination agreement** (memorandum of understanding) would formalize protocols for athlete selection, competition coordination, and mutual recognition of accreditation.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Eight pending bills address identified gaps:

- **Athlete incentives** (PBs 198, 200, 201, 202, 207, 208) — six bills propose various formulations of a Bangsamoro athlete incentive framework. If enacted, these would codify incentive rates and eligibility criteria currently left to BSC's discretion. However, consolidation into a single comprehensive bill would be more effective than six overlapping proposals.
- **Sports education** (PB 377) — proposes the Bangsamoro Sports Institute, addressing the gap in specialized sports education. If enacted, the remaining gap would be an institutional governance framework connecting BSI to BSC and MBHTE.
- **Sports infrastructure** (PB 413) — proposes Bangsamoro Grand Athletic and Sports Complexes, addressing the infrastructure deficit. If enacted, the remaining gap would be a long-term capital investment and maintenance plan.

Even if all 8 pending bills are enacted, one significant gap remains: a **comprehensive Bangsamoro Sports Development Code** that consolidates athlete welfare standards, anti-doping provisions, dispute resolution mechanisms, sports association governance standards, facility standards, and recreational program guidelines into a single regulatory framework.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023–2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023–2028, Chapter 9 (Social Services)** includes the **Bangsamoro Sports Development** program with three projects: (1) Athlete Profiling and Data Banking, (2) Bangsamoro Talent Identification, and (3) Laro't Saya sa Komunidad. These programs aim to ***"create a safe environment for athletes, coaches, sports coordinators, and enthusiasts in the provinces, municipalities and cities under the region."***[^17]

**BDP Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage)** includes Table 6.1 on Bangsamoro Traditional Sports and Music, documenting indigenous sports as cultural heritage to be preserved — connecting power (ww) to power (p) (culture and language) and power (kk) (cultural heritage preservation).[^18]

The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026–2028), Pillar 4 (Social Transformation)** positions sports as a component of human capital formation. Sports development serves multiple Matatag objectives — youth empowerment, peace and social cohesion, and physical well-being.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Three development outcomes are directly at risk:

1. **Athlete retention and development**: without codified incentives (the pending PBs would address this), Bangsamoro athletes lack predictable financial support — risking talent loss to regions with established incentive programs. The BDP's Talent Identification program depends on a credible support pathway from identification through development to competition.

2. **Sports infrastructure**: without a capital investment framework (PB No. 413), the BDP's goal of creating safe sports environments remains aspirational. BSC's "as far as practicable" facility mandate (BAA 12, Sec. 11(b)(10)) is insufficient without dedicated funding and institutional support.

3. **Cultural preservation**: indigenous Bangsamoro games and martial arts identified in BDP Chapter 6 require active promotion and competitive platforms. Without programs specifically designed for indigenous sports — which a comprehensive sports development code would establish — these traditions face gradual disappearance.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does BSC relate to MBHTE on school-based sports?**

A. BAA 13, Sec. 29 draws a clear institutional boundary: ***"in the case of the school sports development program, the same shall be formulated and implemented by the Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE) with the assistance of the Commission within the framework of the regional and national sports development program."***[^14] This means:

- **MBHTE leads** on school-based sports and physical education through the Bureau for Physical Education and Sports Development (BPESD)
- **BSC assists** MBHTE on school sports but does not formulate or implement school sports programs independently
- **BSC leads** on all non-school sports — community sports, regional competitions, athlete development, indigenous games, sports regulation, and national/international competition coordination
- The **BARMMAA Meet** (regional school athletic competition) is conducted by MBHTE in coordination with BSC (BAA 13, Sec. 30(b)(2))

**Q. What makes BSC's mandate unique compared to the Philippine Sports Commission?**

A. Three features distinguish BSC from its national counterpart PSC (RA 6847):

1. **Peace and transitional justice mandate**: BAA 12, Sec. 6 states that BSC shall ***"as a policy, make sure that sports is a tool for peace and transitional justice."***[^6] BAA 12, Sec. 11(a)(5) mandates research on ***"making sports as instrument to fight against criminalities, drugs, and violent extremism; and as a vehicle for peace."*** RA 6847 contains no analogous provision.

2. **Cultural and religious sensitivity**: BAA 12, Sec. 2 requires BSC to ***"recognize the diverse cultures of the region and consider gender, culture and religious sensitivity."***[^16] RA 6847 does not address religious sensitivity in sports programming.

3. **Indigenous games preservation**: BAA 12 and BAA 13 mandate the promotion and preservation of ***"indigenous games"*** and ***"martial arts"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 20; BAA 12, Sec. 2; BAA 12, Sec. 11(a)(3)).[^3] While RA 9850 recognizes Arnis nationally, no national law mandates preservation of the full range of indigenous Filipino sports.

**Q. What is the "sports as a tool for peace" mandate and how does it operate?**

A. BAA 12 embeds peacebuilding as a **core institutional function**, not merely an incidental benefit. Specifically:

- **Policy level**: Sec. 6 — BSC's mandate includes making ***"sports a tool for peace and transitional justice"***
- **Research level**: Sec. 11(a)(5) — BSC must undertake research on ***"making sports as instrument to fight against criminalities, drugs, and violent extremism; and as a vehicle for peace"***
- **Program level**: Sec. 11(a)(7) — ***"Make sports as a tool for peace and transitional justice"*** is listed as an independent plan and policy-making function
- **Youth engagement**: Sec. 11(b)(3) — coordination with BYC and MSSD to engage out-of-school youth, marginalized children, and vulnerable sectors through sports

This mandate is operationally significant in a region with an ongoing normalization process, decommissioned combatants, and communities affected by decades of armed conflict. Sports programs targeting ex-combatant communities and conflict-affected youth serve both development and peacebuilding objectives simultaneously.

**Q. How does BARMM participate in the national Palarong Pambansa system?**

A. The participation pathway is:

1. **School-level competitions** — conducted within BARMM educational institutions
2. **BARMMAA Meet** — the Bangsamoro Athletic Association Meet, the regional school athletic competition conducted by MBHTE in coordination with BSC (BAA 13, Sec. 30(b)(2))
3. **Palarong Pambansa** — the national school games institutionalized by RA 10588. BARMM athletes who excel at the BARMMAA Meet represent the region at the national competition.

The BARMMAA Meet is BARMM's flagship school sports event — Parliament has adopted resolutions commending both its conduct (Res. No. 342) and its champions (Res. Nos. 348–349).
