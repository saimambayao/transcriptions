# (yy) Tourism Development

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(yy)** — ***"Tourism development;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(yy) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XIII, Sec. 35 requires tourism enterprise zone designation ***"in accordance with Republic Act No. 9593"*** (The Tourism Act of 2009). Additionally, BOL Art. XVIII, Sec. 4(r) modifies Sections 59, 60, 61, and 62 of RA 9593 as they apply to BARMM — establishing a hybrid framework where BARMM recommends TEZs to TIEZA under the national law, but the BOL modifies the TEZ provisions to account for Bangsamoro autonomy. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV; **Bureau of Tourism** (BAA 13, Sec. 15-16) is the operational unit within MTIT |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH); Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE); Ministry of Public Works (MOPW); Ministry of Transportation and Communications (MTC); Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage); Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None. Art. XIII, Sec. 35 does not name a specific code or act for tourism. The BOL provides a general promotional mandate and a specific TEZ recommendation power, but no named "Bangsamoro Tourism Code" or equivalent. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant --> constitutional floor --> national law --> implementation --> Shari'ah dimension --> divergence --> gaps --> development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over tourism development?**

A. Article V, Section 2(yy) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"tourism development"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a broad grant covering **tourism promotion**, **tourism regulation**, **tourism infrastructure development**, **tourism enterprise zone recommendation**, and **tourism product development** within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Two provisions directly elaborate, and four cross-reference provisions support this power:

   (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 35** is the dedicated tourism provision. It states: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall promote tourism within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It may recommend the designation of tourism enterprise zones to the Tourism Infrastructure and Enterprise Zone Authority, in accordance with Republic Act No. 9593, otherwise known as 'The Tourism Act of 2009.'"*** This establishes two distinct authorities: a **mandatory promotional function** ("shall promote") and a **discretionary recommendation power** ("may recommend") for TEZ designation to TIEZA.[^2]

   (2) **Art. XVIII, Sec. 4(r)** provides that ***"Sections 59, 60, 61, and 62 of Republic Act No. 9593, otherwise known as 'The Tourism Act of 2009'"*** are among the national laws modified by the BOL. These are the core TEZ provisions — Section 59 (TEZ characteristics), Section 60 (TEZ designation), Section 61 (development planning), and Section 62 (TEZ operation). The modification means the **national TEZ framework does not apply in full** within BARMM; instead, the BOL's Art. XIII, Sec. 35 establishes the BARMM-specific process where the Bangsamoro Government recommends and TIEZA designates.[^3]

   (3) **Art. XIII, Sec. 30** (Halal Program) gives BARMM the power to ***"accredit halal-certifying bodies"*** and promote a ***"halal campaign program"*** — directly supporting **halal tourism** as a unique Bangsamoro tourism product.[^4]

   (4) **Art. XIII, Sec. 26** (Barter Trade and Countertrade) — the BIMP-EAGA framework supports **cross-border tourism cooperation** with Malaysia, Indonesia, and Brunei.[^5]

   (5) **Art. XVI, Sec. 8(c)** lists ***"Trade, Investments, and Tourism"*** as one of the 15 primary interim ministries, establishing the institutional home for tourism.[^6]

   (6) **Art. XIII, Sec. 1** establishes the general economic framework: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall establish an economic system based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution."***[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. Unlike cooperatives (Art. XIII, Sec. 24, which names a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"***), tourism has **no named legislative mandate**. Art. XIII, Sec. 35 imposes a promotional obligation and a TEZ recommendation power but does not mandate Parliament to enact a "Bangsamoro Tourism Code" or equivalent. The absence of a named mandate means Parliament has **discretion** over whether and when to enact comprehensive tourism legislation — but the promotional obligation ("shall promote") implies that adequate institutional and legal mechanisms must exist.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not create a separate tourism body. **Art. XVI, Sec. 8(c)** establishes the Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism as one of 15 primary ministries. Tourism is embedded within MTIT rather than receiving a standalone commission or authority. The **Bureau of Tourism** within MTIT was established through **BAA 13**, Book VI, Title XIV, Sec. 15-16.[^8]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places two specific limitations:

   (1) **TEZ designation requires national coordination**: Art. XIII, Sec. 35 limits the TEZ power to **recommendation** to TIEZA, ***"in accordance with Republic Act No. 9593."*** BARMM cannot unilaterally designate tourism enterprise zones — TIEZA retains designation authority. This is the most significant operational limitation.[^2]

   (2) **Constitutional and general supervision**: Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** and ***"the general supervision of the President."*** Tourism falls within Art. X, Sec. 20(6) — ***"Economic, social, and tourism development"*** — giving constitutional grounding but also imposing the constitutional framework as a boundary.[^1][^9]

   For the **general promotional function**, the BOL imposes no express national law consistency requirement. The phrase ***"in accordance with Republic Act No. 9593"*** applies specifically to TEZ designation, not to all tourism activities.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to tourism development?**

A. Three categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."***[^9]

   (2) **Express Tourism Power (Art. X, Sec. 20(6))**: The Constitution explicitly lists ***"Economic, social, and tourism development"*** as one of nine subject areas over which ***"the organic act of autonomous regions shall provide for legislative powers."*** Tourism is one of the few powers with **express constitutional enumeration** — unlike many Art. V, Sec. 2 powers that derive authority from the general clause of Art. X, Sec. 20(9) (***"Such other matters as may be authorized by law"***). This express enumeration strengthens the Bangsamoro Government's legislative authority over tourism.[^10]

   (3) **National Economy and Patrimony (Art. XII)**: Art. XII, Sec. 1 provides: ***"The goals of the national economy are a more equitable distribution of opportunities, income, and wealth."*** Art. XII, Sec. 2 states: ***"The State shall protect the nation's marine wealth in its archipelagic waters, territorial sea, and exclusive economic zone."*** Art. XII, Sec. 10 requires that no private lands shall be transferred except to Filipino citizens — relevant to foreign investment in tourism estates. Tourism operates within this economic framework.[^11]

**Q. What are the constitutional boundaries for Bangsamoro tourism legislation?**

A. The boundaries are:

   (1) **Constitutional floor**: Art. X, Sec. 20(6) empowers the organic act to provide legislative powers over tourism, but does not create an independent right to override national tourism policy. Tourism legislation must operate ***"subject to the provisions of this Constitution and national laws"*** (Art. X, Sec. 20 chapeau).

   (2) **Due process**: Tourism regulations — accreditation, licensing, closure of establishments — must satisfy procedural due process (notice, hearing, right to appeal). This is particularly relevant for MTIT's power to ***"administratively adjudicate and impose reasonable fines and penalties"*** under BAA 13, Sec. 3(y).[^12]

   (3) **Equal protection**: Tourism regulations cannot arbitrarily discriminate between similarly situated tourism enterprises.

   (4) **National patrimony**: Restrictions on foreign ownership of land (Art. XII, Sec. 7) affect tourism real estate development — foreign investors cannot own land for resort or hotel development, only lease.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for tourism?**

A. The national framework establishes a comprehensive system: **RA 9593** (The Tourism Act of 2009) is the primary law — creating the DOT mandate, TIEZA (tourism infrastructure and enterprise zone designation), TPB (tourism promotions), accreditation standards, tourism enterprise definitions, and TEZ incentive structures. **RA 10816** (Farm Tourism Development Act, 2016) promotes integration of agriculture and tourism for rural development. **EO 111** (1999) establishes national ecotourism guidelines. **EO 120** (1987) reorganized the Department of Tourism. Together, these laws provide: a **national tourism policy**, an **institutional structure** (DOT, TIEZA, TPB), **accreditation standards**, a **TEZ incentive framework**, and **specialized programs** (ecotourism, farm tourism).[^13][^14][^15][^16]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. National tourism laws apply with one critical modification:

   (1) **RA 9593** — applies in BARMM with Sections 59-62 **modified** by BOL Art. XVIII, Sec. 4(r). This means: TEZ designation procedures are adjusted (BARMM recommends, TIEZA designates per Art. XIII, Sec. 35); other RA 9593 provisions (DOT accreditation, tourism enterprise regulation, tourism promotions) apply suppletorily.[^3][^13]

   (2) **RA 10816** (Farm Tourism) — applies suppletorily. Particularly relevant to BARMM's agricultural base in Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, and Lanao del Sur.[^14]

   (3) **EO 111** (Ecotourism Guidelines) — applies suppletorily. Relevant to BARMM's ecological assets.[^15]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for tourism?**

A. Two national agencies exercise authority: (1) The **Department of Tourism (DOT)** maintains a regional office that handles tourism accreditation, tourism enterprise registration, tourism statistics, and coordination of national tourism programs. DOT's accreditation function covers hotels, resorts, restaurants, travel agencies, and tour operators within BARMM. (2) The **Tourism Infrastructure and Enterprise Zone Authority (TIEZA)** retains TEZ designation authority — BARMM's role under Art. XIII, Sec. 35 is limited to recommendation. TIEZA designates, regulates, and supervises TEZs.[^13]

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT)** is the lead agency. Within MTIT, the **Bureau of Tourism** (BAA 13, Sec. 15-16) is the operational unit responsible for tourism development. MTIT is established under BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV. BAA 85, Chapter V allocates its operational budget.[^8][^17]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for tourism development as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through MTIT's **BAA 13 general mandate** and **BAA 85 budget allocation**.

##### D.1.A BAA 13 Mandate and Functions

**Q. What are MTIT's tourism-specific functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Sec. 1 declares the policy that the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall promote tourism as a major socio-economic activity respecting, upholding, and maintaining diverse cultural heritage and the moral and spiritual values of the people in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^18]

   MTIT's tourism-specific powers and functions under Sec. 3 include:

   - (e) ***"Design an integrated marketing program to attract and encourage local and foreign investors and tourists to invest and visit the region;"***
   - (f) ***"Formulate standards for the operation of tourism-oriented establishments within the minimum level acceptable to local and international norms;"***
   - (g) ***"Promulgate rules and regulations governing the operations and activities of all persons, firms, entities and establishments that cater to tourists;"***
   - (h) ***"Provide standards for accreditation of hotels, resorts and tourist-oriented facilities for classification purposes consistent with national laws;"***
   - (i) ***"Develop programs to encourage private sector investment and participation in tourism activities and projects;"***
   - (j) ***"Promote the protection, maintenance and preservation of historical, cultural and natural assets;"***
   - (k) ***"Assist in the enforcement of all laws and regulations for the protection of tourists and other transients;"***
   - (l) ***"Coordinate with appropriate government agencies the development of infrastructure requirements supporting a tourist zone such as, but not limited to, access roads to the zone, electric power brought to the proper line of the zone, airports, harbors, and other support facilities;"***
   - (m) ***"Coordinate with concerned government agencies the provision of social infrastructure requirements supporting a tourist zone as educational facilities, health centers, social and recreational outlets and other necessary amenities for the social upliftment of the populace and preservation of ecological balance;"***[^19]

**Q. What are the Bureau of Tourism's functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Sec. 15 establishes the Bureau of Tourism under a Director II, with **9 enumerated functions**:

   - (a) ***"Assist the Minister of Trade, Investments, and Tourism in the formulation and implementation of the Ministry's policies, plans and programs on Tourism;"***
   - (b) ***"Coordinate the implementation of operational, field-level plans/programs of the Bureau;"***
   - (c) ***"Oversee the administration of the Tourism Marketing, and Tourism Product Development Service Offices of the Ministry, consistent with the mandate of the Ministry;"***
   - (d) ***"Coordinate with concerned agencies and institutions, both government and private, the development of the regional tourism plans and policies;"***
   - (e) ***"Enlist the assistance and support of local government units and other regional/provincial line agencies in the implementation of the programs and projects of the bureau;"***
   - (f) ***"Provide support to all tourism-related activities of private sectors needing government assistance;"***
   - (g) ***"Organize tourism partners and inter-island advocacy enhancement activities;"***
   - (h) ***"Strengthening safety coordination network, putting in-place necessary tourism services, and development of institutional linkages and social assets;"***
   - (i) ***"Perform such other functions as may be provided by law."***[^20]

   Sec. 16 organizes the Bureau into two divisions: **(a) Product Development and Standards and Accreditation Division**; and **(b) Tourism Promotions and Marketing Division**.[^21]

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **Department of Tourism (DOT)** is the national government agency exercising authority in BARMM. DOT operates through regional office structures that perform: **accreditation** of tourism enterprises (hotels, resorts, restaurants, travel agencies, tour operators); **monitoring and enforcement** of tourism standards; **tourism statistics** collection and reporting; **coordination** of national tourism marketing programs (It's More Fun in the Philippines and successor campaigns); and **technical assistance** to LGUs on local tourism development plans. **TIEZA** exercises TEZ designation authority, though no TEZs have been designated within BARMM to date.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for tourism?**

A. DOT programs include: (1) **accreditation and standards** — processing primary and secondary tourism enterprise accreditations; (2) **destination marketing** — inclusion of BARMM destinations in national and international tourism campaigns; (3) **tourism infrastructure** coordination with DPWH for tourism access roads; (4) **training programs** under RA 9593 Sec. 13 (Office of Industry Manpower Development) for tourism workers; (5) **tourism councils** — DOT facilitates regional tourism councils per RA 9593 Sec. 44 for tourism site classification and evaluation.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MTIT's Bureau of Tourism has been **institutionally established** under BAA 13 with a defined organizational structure (2 divisions), a Director II, and operational budget under BAA 85. It has begun exercising its **programmatic functions** — tourism marketing, coordination with LGUs, standards formulation for tourism establishments, and inter-island tourism advocacy. MTIT field offices operate in 7 provincial areas including conflict-affected zones. The **Bangsamoro Halal Board** (BAA 13, Sec. 33-36), attached to MTIT, provides the institutional basis for halal tourism certification. No standalone tourism legislation has been enacted, but **6 Parliament Bills** (PB 217, 218, 261, 280, 324, 346) have been filed addressing various tourism aspects. **BTA Resolution No. 84** (Marawi MAA protection) and **Resolution No. 472** (tourist-police assistance) signal parliamentary attention to tourism-related concerns.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has four unique advantages over the national framework:

   (1) **Halal tourism** — BARMM's halal accreditation power (Art. XIII, Sec. 30) and the Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13, Sec. 33-36) create a regulatory framework for halal-compliant tourism enterprises that the national DOT does not provide. Halal tourism is a rapidly growing global market, estimated at $200 billion and projected to reach $300 billion by 2026 [UNVERIFIED]. BARMM is uniquely positioned to serve this market.

   (2) **Conflict-sensitive tourism** — BARMM's post-conflict context requires tourism development models that account for IDP communities, war-damaged heritage sites (Marawi), and peace-building objectives. BTA Resolution No. 84's protection of the Marawi Most Affected Area demonstrates a conflict-sensitive approach that national tourism policy does not address.

   (3) **Islamic heritage tourism** — BARMM possesses Islamic cultural heritage sites, mosques, madaris, and cultural practices that no other Philippine region can offer. This includes historical sites linked to the Sultanates of Maguindanao and Sulu, the arrival of Islam in the Philippines, and Bangsamoro cultural traditions.

   (4) **BIMP-EAGA cross-border tourism** — BARMM's geographic and cultural proximity to BIMP-EAGA member states (Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei) creates natural cross-border tourism corridors that the national framework does not optimize for.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. Five constraints limit full implementation:

   (1) **Absence of standalone tourism legislation** — no Bangsamoro Tourism Code or equivalent defines the regulatory framework, accreditation standards, incentive structures, or institutional authorities specific to BARMM. All tourism regulation operates under MTIT's general BAA 13 mandate supplemented by national law.

   (2) **TEZ designation remains with TIEZA** — BARMM can only recommend, not designate, tourism enterprise zones. No BARMM TEZs have been designated to date, suggesting the recommendation mechanism is either unused or ineffective.

   (3) **Security environment** — many BARMM tourism assets are in areas with unresolved security concerns. BTA Resolution No. 472's request for tourist-police assistance desks signals this unmet need.

   (4) **Infrastructure gaps** — BARMM's tourism destinations lack adequate transportation infrastructure (roads, airports, seaport facilities), utilities, and accommodation facilities. Access to island destinations in Tawi-Tawi and Basilan is particularly constrained.

   (5) **Marawi reconstruction** — the 2017 siege destroyed significant tourism infrastructure and heritage sites. Reconstruction and the development of Marawi as a heritage tourism destination remain incomplete.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a comprehensive tourism infrastructure:

   - **Institutional depth**: DOT has 17 regional offices, each with tourism regulation, accreditation, planning, and promotion functions. TIEZA manages TEZ designations nationwide. TPB conducts international and domestic tourism marketing with multi-billion peso budgets.
   - **Regulatory systems**: DOT maintains a national accreditation system for all primary and secondary tourism enterprises — with established standards, inspection protocols, and enforcement mechanisms.
   - **Tourism enterprise zones**: TIEZA has designated TEZs across the Philippines with incentive packages, development plans, and regulatory oversight.
   - **Tourism data**: DOT collects and publishes comprehensive tourism statistics — arrivals, receipts, occupancy rates, and enterprise data.
   - **Training infrastructure**: DOT's Office of Industry Manpower Development conducts training programs for tourism workers, guides, and enterprise operators.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in four areas:

   (1) **No halal tourism standards**: DOT's accreditation system does not include halal compliance criteria. Hotels, restaurants, and tour operators in BARMM cannot be accredited as halal-compliant under the national system.

   (2) **No conflict-sensitive tourism policy**: National tourism promotion does not account for post-conflict contexts — IDP communities, war-damaged heritage, and peace-building through tourism are not addressed in RA 9593 or DOT programs.

   (3) **No Islamic heritage tourism category**: DOT's product development framework does not recognize Islamic heritage as a distinct tourism product category — mosques, madaris, sultanate heritage sites, and Islamic cultural practices are not integrated into the national tourism development plan as a category.

   (4) **TEZ designation gap**: No TEZs have been designated within BARMM, despite the region's tourism assets. The national TEZ process may be inaccessible to BARMM communities due to the complexity and cost of application requirements under RA 9593, Sec. 59-62.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element of the national framework:

   - **Replicate**: **accreditation standards** (tourism enterprise quality and safety standards), **tourism data collection** (arrivals, receipts, enterprise statistics), and **tourism council mechanisms** (regional/provincial tourism councils for destination planning). These are technically sound and provide comparability with national benchmarks.
   - **Adapt**: **TEZ framework** — create a BARMM-specific tourism zone designation process that complements the TIEZA recommendation mechanism; add **halal tourism accreditation** as a layer on top of standard accreditation; adapt **tourism worker training** for local languages and cultural contexts; adapt **destination marketing** for Islamic heritage and halal tourism products.
   - **Replace**: **tourism product development** — build an entirely distinct product development framework centered on BARMM's unique assets: Islamic heritage, halal tourism, peace-building tourism (Marawi), marine ecotourism (Turtle Islands, island communities), and cultural tourism (Bangsamoro cultural festivals, traditional crafts). The national product development framework does not serve these priorities.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Tourism has a **significant Shari'ah dimension** in BARMM because: (1) the **halal tourism** market requires Shari'ah-compliant hospitality standards (halal food, prayer facilities, gender-appropriate services); (2) **cultural and religious site management** must respect Islamic principles of sacred space stewardship; (3) tourism activities must comply with Islamic norms regarding **modesty** (*haya'*), **prohibition of alcohol** in certain contexts, and **protection of community dignity** (*hifz al-'ird*); and (4) tourism revenue generation must be through **lawful** (*halal*) means, avoiding exploitation, gambling, or other prohibited activities.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Five Islamic legal principles directly govern tourism legislation:

   (1) **Halal standards** — Islamic dietary and hospitality law requires tourism establishments serving Muslim travelers to provide halal food, prayer facilities, and Shari'ah-compliant services. BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 30 provides the legal basis through halal accreditation authority.[^4]

   (2) ***Hifz al-'ird*** (protection of dignity) — tourism must respect local cultural norms and religious sensibilities. Legislation should include provisions against cultural exploitation, insensitive commercialization of sacred sites, and activities that degrade community dignity.

   (3) ***Hifz al-mal*** (protection of wealth) — tourism as economic development serves this objective by generating lawful income. Legislation should ensure tourism revenue benefits local communities through benefit-sharing mechanisms.

   (4) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — tourism operators managing cultural and natural heritage are stewards (*amin*) of community assets. Sustainable management, transparency, and community accountability are required.

   (5) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — tourism promotion serves the common good through employment, infrastructure development, and cultural exchange. Tourism that harms communities or environment contradicts *maslahah* and may be restricted.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro tourism legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is a **voluntary halal tourism certification** system: tourism enterprises (hotels, restaurants, tour operators, transport services) may apply for **halal tourism certification** through the Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13, Sec. 33-36) in addition to standard MTIT accreditation. Certification would require: halal-certified food service; prayer room facilities; availability of qibla direction indicators; gender-appropriate services where requested; no gambling facilities; and compliance with local modesty norms. The system should be **voluntary** — not all tourism enterprises in BARMM serve exclusively Muslim markets, and mandatory halal compliance would restrict non-Muslim tourism operators. The halal certification would serve as a **market differentiator** attracting the global halal tourism market.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national tourism law?**

A. BARMM's divergence authority is **constrained but significant**:

   **Constrained**: Art. XIII, Sec. 35 requires TEZ designation ***"in accordance with Republic Act No. 9593"*** — this express consistency requirement limits BARMM's ability to create an independent TEZ framework. BARMM cannot unilaterally designate TEZs outside the TIEZA process. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL modifies (but does not eliminate) RA 9593 Sections 59-62 within BARMM.

   **Significant**: Beyond TEZ designation, the BOL imposes **no other consistency requirement** on tourism. The general promotional mandate (***"shall promote tourism"***) contains no "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier. Combined with the **express constitutional enumeration** of tourism in Art. X, Sec. 20(6) (established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B)), BARMM has broad legislative authority to:

   - Enact a **Bangsamoro Tourism Code** establishing tourism governance, accreditation, and regulatory standards that go beyond (not below) national standards
   - Create a **Bangsamoro tourism zone** framework that operates independently of TEZs (i.e., a regional tourism development zone system that does not require TIEZA designation)
   - Establish **halal tourism certification** standards not available under RA 9593
   - Develop **conflict-sensitive tourism** policies and programs
   - Create **Islamic heritage tourism** categories and protection standards
   - Establish a **Bangsamoro Tourism Promotion Board** (as proposed in PB No. 261) with independent marketing authority

   The **limits of divergence** are:
   - **Constitutional floor**: Art. X, Sec. 20 chapeau requires tourism legislation to be ***"subject to the provisions of this Constitution and national laws."*** BARMM tourism standards must meet or exceed national minimums.
   - **TEZ consistency**: Art. XIII, Sec. 35's express consistency with RA 9593 for TEZ designation cannot be circumvented.
   - **General supervision**: Presidential general supervision ensures faithful execution of laws, including national tourism law.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders?**

A. (1) **For tourism enterprises within BARMM**: They could gain benefits not available nationally — particularly halal tourism certification and access to the global halal tourism market. Higher BARMM-specific standards (e.g., cultural sensitivity requirements, heritage site management standards) would impose additional compliance obligations but could enhance destination quality and marketability.

   (2) **For tourists visiting BARMM**: Different standards could create confusion if BARMM accreditation is not recognized nationally or internationally. A **mutual recognition** mechanism with DOT would ensure BARMM-accredited enterprises are recognized in the national system.

   (3) **For constitutional vulnerability**: A challenge is unlikely to succeed because Art. X, Sec. 20(6) expressly lists ***"tourism development"*** as an autonomous region legislative power, and the BOL is itself a congressional act authorizing differentiation.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Three jurisdictional overlaps exist:

   (1) **Accreditation**: MTIT has the power to ***"provide standards for accreditation of hotels, resorts and tourist-oriented facilities"*** (BAA 13, Sec. 3(h)) ***"consistent with national laws."*** DOT under RA 9593, Sec. 6(l) has the power to formulate ***"a national standard for licensing, accreditation and classification of tourism enterprises."*** Currently, DOT accredits and MTIT accredits — tourism enterprises may face **dual accreditation requirements**. A standalone Bangsamoro Tourism Code should clarify whether MTIT accreditation **replaces** DOT accreditation within BARMM or operates alongside it.

   (2) **TEZ designation vs. regional tourism zones**: Art. XIII, Sec. 35 limits TEZ designation to recommendation to TIEZA. But nothing prohibits BARMM from creating a **separate "Bangsamoro Tourism Zone"** category outside the TEZ framework — a regional tourism development area with BARMM-defined incentives and standards. The jurisdictional boundary between national TEZs and potential Bangsamoro tourism zones needs legislative clarification.

   (3) **Tourism enterprise regulation**: MTIT regulates tourism enterprises under BAA 13, Sec. 3(g), while DOT regulates under RA 9593, Sec. 6. Tourism enterprises within BARMM may be subject to both regulatory frameworks simultaneously.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Six PBs address various gaps: **PB No. 261** (comprehensive tourism development with Bangsamoro Tourism Promotion Board) and **PB No. 280** (comprehensive tourism framework) address the overall legislative gap. **PB Nos. 217 and 218** (ecotourism site designations) address specific site protection. **PB No. 324** (ecotourism villages in all LGUs) addresses community-based ecotourism. **PB No. 346** (trade and tourism centers) addresses physical infrastructure. None of these PBs has been enacted as of BAA 89. The most comprehensive are PB 261 and PB 280, which would address the institutional, regulatory, and promotional framework gaps.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) identifies tourism as a **key strategy for economic diversification** — reducing dependence on agriculture and government employment [UNVERIFIED]. Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage) connects tourism to **cultural preservation** — heritage tourism incentivizes maintenance and restoration of cultural sites. Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development) addresses tourism-supporting infrastructure. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, depends on tourism development as a revenue diversification strategy. Tourism also supports **Pillar 5 (Peace, Security, and Justice)** [UNVERIFIED] through peace-building tourism in post-conflict areas like Marawi.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without standalone tourism legislation: MTIT cannot establish a **comprehensive accreditation system** independent of DOT — BARMM tourism enterprises remain subject to national accreditation that does not account for halal compliance or conflict-sensitive standards. The **halal tourism market** — estimated to be the fastest-growing tourism segment globally [UNVERIFIED] — cannot be fully captured because no Bangsamoro-specific halal tourism certification framework exists in law. **Tourism enterprise zones** cannot be designated because the recommendation mechanism to TIEZA appears unutilized. **Ecotourism** in BARMM's ecological assets (Liguasan Marsh, Turtle Islands, Lake Lanao) lacks a legal framework for sustainable development and community benefit-sharing. The **BDP targets** for tourism development depend on institutional infrastructure that BAA 13's general mandate alone cannot fully provide. **Marawi's reconstruction as a heritage tourism destination** requires legal protections (BTA Resolution No. 84 signals the concern but is non-binding). **BIMP-EAGA cross-border tourism cooperation** cannot be fully operationalized without a legal framework for cross-border tourism facilitation.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why does the BOL limit BARMM's TEZ authority to recommendation rather than designation?**

A. The BOL's design reflects the **hybrid nature of tourism governance**. Tourism enterprise zones under RA 9593 carry significant **fiscal incentives** (tax holidays, duty-free importation, special economic zone benefits per RA 9593, Sec. 59-62) that affect national revenue. TIEZA designation triggers these national fiscal incentives — allowing BARMM to unilaterally designate TEZs would effectively grant BARMM the power to create national tax expenditures without national government consent. The **recommendation mechanism** preserves BARMM's initiative while maintaining national fiscal control. This is structurally similar to other BOL coordination mechanisms — BEZA (Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority) and PEZA operate under similar coordination frameworks per Art. XIII, Sec. 28.

   However, the modification of RA 9593 Sections 59-62 by Art. XVIII, Sec. 4(r) opens the possibility that BARMM's recommendation carries **heightened weight** — the modified provisions may require TIEZA to give preferential consideration to BARMM recommendations, though the extent of the modification requires implementing rules.

**Q. How does this power interact with power (zz) Trade and Industry?**

A. Power (yy) — ***"tourism development"*** — is closely related to but distinct from power (zz) — ***"trade and industry."*** Both are housed within MTIT. The BOL's Art. XIII places tourism (Sec. 35) within the same ***"Trade and Industry and Tourism"*** subsection as trade and industry (Sec. 24). MTIT's BAA 13 mandate covers all three — trade, investments, and tourism — in an integrated framework. The distinction matters because: (1) tourism has a **specific national law consistency requirement** (RA 9593) that trade and industry do not; (2) tourism's **TEZ recommendation** mechanism has no equivalent in trade or industry; (3) tourism involves **cultural heritage and environmental** dimensions that trade and industry do not. Tourism legislation should include **coordination mechanisms** between the Bureau of Tourism, the Bureau of Trade and Industry, the Bureau of Investments, and BEZA to ensure integrated economic development.

**Q. What is the significance of halal tourism for BARMM's tourism strategy?**

A. Halal tourism represents BARMM's **most significant competitive advantage** in Philippine tourism. No other Philippine region has: (a) the **halal accreditation authority** provided by Art. XIII, Sec. 30; (b) the **Bangsamoro Halal Board** (BAA 13, Sec. 33-36) as an institutional mechanism; (c) a **Muslim-majority population** with authentic Islamic cultural heritage; and (d) **proximity to major Muslim-majority tourism source markets** (Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei, Middle East). The global halal tourism market is projected to be among the fastest-growing tourism segments [UNVERIFIED]. A Bangsamoro Tourism Code that integrates halal tourism certification into the accreditation framework would position BARMM as the **Philippines' premier halal tourism destination** — a unique market position that no other region can replicate. As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), the recommended approach is voluntary halal tourism certification through the Bangsamoro Halal Board.

**Q. What is the significance of Marawi's reconstruction for tourism development?**

A. Marawi City, the seat of the Maranao people and historically known as the ***"City of Marawi"*** (Islamic City of Marawi), was severely damaged during the 2017 siege. Its reconstruction presents a **dual tourism opportunity**: (1) **heritage tourism** — Marawi's pre-siege Islamic heritage (the Torogan royal houses, mosques, Lake Lanao shoreline, Maranao cultural traditions) can be restored and protected as a cultural tourism destination; (2) **peace-building tourism** — the siege site itself, like other post-conflict memorial sites worldwide, can serve as a site of remembrance and peace education. BTA Resolution No. 84 already signals the Parliament's concern about **insensitive treatment** of the Most Affected Area. A Bangsamoro Tourism Code should include: (a) **heritage protection provisions** for Marawi's cultural assets; (b) **community consent requirements** for tourism development in the Most Affected Area; and (c) **revenue-sharing mechanisms** ensuring that Marawi residents benefit from tourism in their city.

**Q. How can BIMP-EAGA cooperation support BARMM tourism?**

A. The BIMP-EAGA framework (established under Art. XIII, Sec. 26 for barter trade) provides a platform for **cross-border tourism cooperation** — particularly with Sabah (Malaysia), North Kalimantan (Indonesia), and Brunei. BARMM's island communities in Tawi-Tawi and Basilan have historical, cultural, and economic ties with these neighboring territories. Cross-border tourism products could include: (a) **inter-island cultural heritage trails** linking Bangsamoro, Sabahan, and Indonesian Malay heritage sites; (b) **marine ecotourism circuits** connecting Turtle Islands (shared Philippines-Malaysia heritage), the Sulu Archipelago, and Coral Triangle dive sites; and (c) **halal tourism packages** that integrate BARMM destinations into broader Southeast Asian Muslim tourism circuits. Legislation should provide frameworks for: immigration facilitation (tourist visa waivers for BIMP-EAGA nationals), customs coordination, and joint tourism marketing.
