## B. Constitutional Dimensions

Shari'ah governance in the Bangsamoro operates within a constitutional framework that simultaneously authorizes and constrains it.

### B.1 Authorization Provisions

**Q. What constitutional provisions authorize Shari'ah governance?**

A. The 1987 Constitution provides five bases for Shari'ah governance:

1. **Art. X, Sec. 1** — ***"There shall be autonomous regions in Muslim Mindanao and the Cordilleras as hereinafter provided."*** The Constitution names "Muslim Mindanao" as a basis for autonomy — the only reference to a specific religious community in the autonomy provisions.

2. **Art. X, Sec. 15** — ***"There shall be created autonomous regions in Muslim Mindanao and in the Cordilleras consisting of provinces, cities, municipalities, and geographical areas sharing common and distinctive historical and cultural heritage, economic and social structures, and other relevant characteristics within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This is the "within the framework" clause — the constitutional ceiling under which all Bangsamoro governance, including Shari'ah governance, must operate.

3. **1987 Const., Art. X, Sec. 18** — ***"...The organic acts shall likewise provide for special courts with personal, family, and property law jurisdiction consistent with the provisions of this Constitution and national laws."*** The Constitution expressly mandates special courts (i.e., Shari'ah courts) with jurisdiction over personal, family, and property law — a direct constitutional authorization for the Shari'ah court system.

4. **1987 Const., Art. X, Sec. 20(4)** — Autonomous regions have legislative power over ***"Personal, family, and property relations."*** This is the constitutional basis for Parliament's Shari'ah legislative power under BOL Art. X, Sec. 4.

5. **Art. XIV, Sec. 3(3)** — ***"At the option expressed in writing by the parents or guardians, religion shall be allowed to be taught to their children or wards in public elementary and high schools..."*** This authorizes Islamic and Arabic studies in BARMM public schools, supporting BOL Art. IX, Sec. 18's madaris integration mandate.

### B.2 Religious Freedom Framework

**Q. How does the constitutional religious freedom framework interact with Shari'ah governance?**

A. Two provisions create the framework:

1. **Art. III, Sec. 5** — ***"No law shall be made respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof. The free exercise and enjoyment of religious profession and worship, without discrimination or preference, shall forever be allowed. No religious test shall be required for the exercise of civil or political rights."*** The non-establishment clause creates a tension point: Shari'ah governance must avoid becoming an establishment of religion. The resolution is that Shari'ah applies only to Muslims (and non-Muslims who voluntarily submit) — maintaining the voluntary character required by the non-establishment clause.

2. **Art. VI, Sec. 29(2)** — ***"No public money or property shall be appropriated, applied, paid, or employed, directly or indirectly, for the use, benefit, or support of any sect, church, denomination, sectarian institution, or system of religion..."*** This non-appropriation clause creates a tension with state funding of Islamic institutions (madaris, Darul Ifta', BPA). The constitutional resolution lies in treating these as governance institutions serving the autonomous region's mandate rather than religious institutions per se.

### B.3 Fiscal Protection

**Q. What constitutional fiscal provision specifically mentions mosques?**

A. **Art. VI, Sec. 28(3)** — ***"Charitable institutions, churches and parsonages or convents appurtenant thereto, mosques, non-profit cemeteries, and all lands, buildings, and improvements, actually, directly, and exclusively used for religious, charitable, or educational purposes shall be exempt from taxation."*** Mosques are constitutionally exempt from taxation — one of only a few constitutional provisions that specifically names an Islamic institution.

### B.4 Constraint Provisions

**Q. What constitutional provisions constrain Shari'ah governance?**

A. Two constraint provisions apply:

1. **Art. X, Sec. 16** — ***"The President shall exercise general supervision over autonomous regions to ensure that the laws are faithfully executed."*** Presidential supervision applies to all Bangsamoro governance, including Shari'ah-related institutions and legislation.

2. **1987 Const., Art. X, Sec. 17** — ***"All powers, functions, and responsibilities not granted by this Constitution or by law to the autonomous regions shall be vested in the National Government."*** This residual powers clause means that any Shari'ah authority not granted by the Constitution or the BOL remains with the national government. Parliament cannot expand Shari'ah jurisdiction beyond what the BOL authorizes.

## C. National Shari'ah Law

### C.1 PD 1083 — Code of Muslim Personal Laws (1977)

PD 1083 is the foundational national Shari'ah legislation in the Philippines, issued under Martial Law on February 4, 1977. It remains in force as amended by the BOL.

**Structure (4 Books):**

**Book One — General Provisions** (Arts. 1-7)
- Art. 3: PD 1083 prevails over laws of general application in case of conflict
- Art. 6: Four Sunni schools (Hanafi, Hanbali, Maliki, Shafi'i) recognized as authoritative
- Art. 7(h): Muslim Law (Shari'a) defined as ***"all ordinances and regulations governing Muslims as found principally in the Qur'an and the Hadith"***

**Book Two — Persons and Family Relations** (Arts. 8-88)
- Civil personality, marriage (Nikah) with Islamic requirements (ijab/qabul, wali, mahr), prohibited marriages (consanguinity, affinity, fosterage), subsequent marriages, batil/fasid marriages
- Seven forms of divorce (Talaq): talaq, ila, zihar, li'an, khul', tafwid, faskh; 'idda waiting period
- Paternity and filiation, adoption prohibition (no status transfer under Islamic law)
- Support (Nafaqa) during marriage and 'idda
- Parental authority (father's decision prevails), custody rules (mother below 7, child's choice after 7)
- Civil registry: Shari'ah court clerks serve as District/Circuit Registrars for Muslim marriages, divorces, revocations, and conversions

**Book Three — Succession** (Arts. 89-128+)
- Islamic inheritance system: sharers (ashab-ul-furud), residuaries (ashab-ul-mirath), distant kindred (dhaw-ul-arham)
- Testamentary succession: will (wasiya) limited to 1/3 of estate if heirs exist; testamentary waqf (wagf-bill-wasiya)
- Legal succession with detailed share tables per Islamic inheritance rules

**Book Four — Administration and Adjudication**
- Shari'ah courts jurisdiction and organization
- Agama Arbitration Councils

**Key PD 1083 Concepts:**

| Concept | PD 1083 Articles | Description |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|
| Marriage (Nikah) | Arts. 14-22 | Islamic marriage contract with ijab/qabul, wali consent, mahr |
| Prohibited marriages | Arts. 23-26 | Consanguinity, affinity, fosterage |
| Polygyny | Art. 27 | Permitted with conditions of just treatment |
| Divorce (Talaq) | Arts. 45-55 | Seven forms including judicial (faskh) |
| 'Idda | Arts. 56-57 | Waiting period after divorce or death |
| Custody | Art. 78 | Mother below 7; child's choice above 7; pubescent daughter with father |
| Succession | Arts. 89-128+ | Full Islamic inheritance (furud system) |
| Waqf | Art. 104 | Testamentary endowment for Islamic purposes |
| Four Madhahib | Art. 6 | Hanafi, Hanbali, Maliki, Shafi'i recognized |

**BOL Amendments to PD 1083:** The BOL expressly amends Articles 140, 143, 152, 153, 154, 164, 165, 166, 167, and 168 of PD 1083 — all relating to Shari'ah court jurisdiction and organization (Art. XVIII, Sec. 4(d)).

# PART II: SHARI'AH INSTITUTIONS

## E. The Shari'ah Court System

### E.1 Three-Tier Structure

**Q. What is the structure of the Shari'ah court system?**

A. The Shari'ah court system consists of three tiers, all forming part of the Philippine judicial system under Supreme Court supervision (BOL Art. X, Sec. 2):

| Court Level | BOL Provision | Jurisdiction | Rank Equivalent |
|------------|---------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Shari'ah Circuit Court** | Art. X, Sec. 5 | First-instance: personal status (marriage, divorce, mahr, maintenance), communal property, ta'zir offenses, real property up to P400,000, civil actions up to P200,000 | Municipal Trial Court Judge |
| **Shari'ah District Court** | Art. X, Sec. 6 | First-instance: custody, guardianship, succession, contracts between Muslims, extraordinary writs, real property above P400,000, civil actions above P200,000. Appellate: over Circuit Courts | Regional Trial Court Judge |
| **Shari'ah High Court** | Art. X, Sec. 7 | First-instance: extraordinary writs, annulment of District Court judgments. Appellate: over all District Courts within or outside BARMM. Decisions final except on questions of law (appealable to Supreme Court) | Court of Appeals Justice |

The Shari'ah High Court is a **BOL creation** — it did not exist before RA 11054. It consists of five Justices including the Presiding Justice (Art. X, Sec. 9). Pending its organization, Shari'ah District Court decisions are appealable to the Court of Appeals.

### E.2 Jurisdiction by Subject and Amount

**Q. How is jurisdiction divided between Circuit and District Courts?**

A. Jurisdiction divides by **subject matter** and **amount in controversy**:

**Subject-based division:**
- **Circuit Courts:** Marriage, divorce, betrothal, mahr, property on divorce, maintenance, restitution of marital rights (Art. X, Sec. 5(b)); offenses under PD 1083 (Sec. 5(a)); ta'zir offenses under Parliament-enacted Shari'ah law (Sec. 5(d)); communal property disputes (Sec. 5(c))
- **District Courts:** Custody, guardianship, legitimacy, paternity/filiation (Art. X, Sec. 6(a)); estate settlement, probate, administration (Sec. 6(b)); absence/death declarations, Muslim Registry corrections (Sec. 6(c)); Shari'ah-compliant contracts (Sec. 6(d)); extraordinary writs (Sec. 6(e)); family home, name change, insanity commitment (Sec. 6(f)); special civil actions (Sec. 6(h))

**Amount-based division:**
- Real property actions under Shari'ah law: up to P400,000 in Circuit Court (Sec. 5(e)), above P400,000 in District Court (Sec. 6(i))
- Civil actions where governing law not specified: up to P200,000 in Circuit Court (Sec. 5(f)), above P200,000 in District Court (Sec. 6(j))

### E.3 Judge Qualifications

**Q. What are the qualifications for Shari'ah judges?**

A. All Shari'ah judges must be **Muslim citizens** and **regular members of the Philippine Bar**. Additional requirements by level:

| Court Level | Minimum Age | Bar Membership | Years of Practice | Shari'ah/Islamic Jurisprudence |
|------------|-------------|----------------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Circuit Court | 30 | Regular member | 5+ years | 2+ years |
| District Court | 35 | Regular member | 10+ years | 2+ years |
| High Court | 40 | Natural-born citizen, regular member | 15+ years | 2+ years |

The Supreme Court may grant incumbent judges not in the Bar a reasonable period to qualify (Art. X, Sec. 8). The **dual qualification** — Philippine Bar plus Shari'ah expertise — is unique in Philippine legal practice.

### E.4 Shari'ah Bar

**Q. How does the Shari'ah Bar function?**

A. Three provisions govern the Shari'ah Bar:

1. **Special Bar Examinations** (Art. X, Sec. 12): The Supreme Court administers Shari'ah Special Bar Examinations. Successful examinees are admitted to the Philippine Bar as ***"special members thereof to practice law in the Shari'ah courts"*** — earning the title "counselor-at-law."

2. **Practice pathways** (Art. X, Sec. 13): Two pathways exist: (a) Shari'ah counselor-at-law (via the special bar exam); (b) regular Philippine Bar member. Both may appear before Shari'ah courts.

3. **Integrated Shari'ah Bar** (Art. X, Sec. 15): The Supreme Court ***"may adopt the rules for the integration of the Shari'ah Bar"*** — authorizing a separate integrated bar for Shari'ah practitioners within the Philippine Bar system.

### E.5 Sources of Shari'ah (Art. X, Sec. 3)

**Q. What are the recognized sources of Shari'ah in Philippine law?**

A. The BOL formally codifies the four sources of Islamic jurisprudence:

**Principal Sources:**
- (a) ***Al-Qur'an*** or the Koran
- (b) ***Al-Sunnah*** or traditions of Prophet Muhammad SAW

**Secondary Sources:**
- (a) ***Al-Ijma*** or Consensus
- (b) ***Al-Qiyas*** or Analogy

PD 1083 supplements this by recognizing four Sunni schools of jurisprudence (madhahib): **Hanafi, Hanbali, Maliki, and Shafi'i** (Art. 6). This is significant because the Bangsamoro population is predominantly Shafi'i, but the recognition of all four schools provides flexibility in Shari'ah adjudication and legislation.

### E.6 Supreme Court Supervision

**Q. What is the relationship between Shari'ah courts and the Supreme Court?**

A. The Supreme Court exercises five specific authorities over Shari'ah courts:

1. **Supervision** of all Shari'ah courts as part of the Philippine judicial system (Art. X, Sec. 2)
2. **Appointment and discipline** of all Shari'ah court personnel (Art. X, Sec. 14)
3. **Administration** of Shari'ah Special Bar Examinations (Art. X, Sec. 12)
4. **Promulgation** of Shari'ah Rules of Court, replacing existing special rules (Art. X, Sec. 11)
5. **Integration** of the Shari'ah Bar, adopting rules for its organization (Art. X, Sec. 15)

The Bangsamoro Parliament cannot unilaterally modify Shari'ah court structure or procedures — these powers reside with the Supreme Court and Congress.

### E.7 Shari'ah Principles Applicable to Bangsamoro Governance

**Q. What Islamic legal principles guide Bangsamoro governance and legislation?**

A. Beyond the four formal sources of Shari'ah (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) codified in Art. X, Sec. 3, Islamic jurisprudence recognizes principles (maqasid al-Shari'ah and subsidiary principles) that should guide legislation across all 55 powers:

**Maqasid al-Shari'ah (Objectives of Shari'ah):**
The five objectives of Islamic law, as systematized by classical scholars, provide the framework for evaluating whether legislation serves Islamic purposes:

1. ***Hifz al-Din*** (Preservation of Faith) — Legislation should protect religious practice and Islamic identity. Relevant to: madaris education, Darul Ifta' authority, Hajj/Umrah administration, religious freedom guarantees.

2. ***Hifz al-Nafs*** (Preservation of Life) — Legislation should protect human life and dignity. Relevant to: health governance (Islamic health policies), humanitarian services, human rights, disaster risk reduction.

3. ***Hifz al-'Aql*** (Preservation of Intellect) — Legislation should promote knowledge and protect intellectual capacity. Relevant to: education (madaris system, Islamic higher education), science and technology, libraries and museums.

4. ***Hifz al-Nasl*** (Preservation of Lineage/Family) — Legislation should protect family structure and reproduction. Relevant to: Shari'ah court jurisdiction over marriage, divorce, custody, filiation; civil registration of Muslim births, marriages, and deaths.

5. ***Hifz al-Mal*** (Preservation of Wealth) — Legislation should protect property and promote economic justice. Relevant to: Islamic banking and finance, cooperatives (prohibition of riba), trade and industry (halal), waqf, zakat, budgeting, loans and credits.

**Subsidiary Principles:**

| Principle | Arabic | Description | Application in Bangsamoro Governance |
|-----------|--------|-------------|-------------------------------------|
| Public Interest | *Maslahah* | Legislation serving the common good is permissible even where no specific textual authority exists, provided it does not contradict established Shari'ah rules | Justifies BARMM legislation on modern subjects (technology, environment, urban planning) through Islamic values without requiring specific Qur'anic or Hadith authority |
| Justice | *'Adl* | All state action must be just and equitable | Applies to all 55 powers — every BAA, every regulation, every government decision must meet the standard of justice |
| Trust/Stewardship | *Amanah* | Public officials are trustees of the people's resources and authority — accountability and transparency are religious obligations | Governs civil service conduct, budget management, public procurement, and all administrative functions |
| Consultation | *Shura* | Governance must include consultation with stakeholders before decisions | Parallels the consultation mechanisms power (Art. V, Sec. 2(mm)); applies to all legislative and executive processes |
| Prohibition of Harm | *La darar wa la dirar* | No harm shall be inflicted or reciprocated | Governs environmental regulation, public health, consumer protection, and dispute resolution |
| Facilitation | *Taysir* | Where hardship exists, facilitation and ease should be provided | Justifies accommodations for vulnerable populations, flexible service delivery, and simplified procedures |

**Economic Principles:**

| Principle | Arabic | Prohibition/Requirement | Application |
|-----------|--------|------------------------|-------------|
| Prohibition of Interest | *Riba* | All forms of predetermined return on money lent — regardless of label — are prohibited | Shari'ah-compliant alternatives needed for cooperatives, housing finance, loans and credits, banking |
| Prohibition of Uncertainty | *Gharar* | Excessive uncertainty in contracts is prohibited; contracts must be clear in terms | Applies to trade contracts, insurance, investment, public procurement |
| Prohibition of Gambling | *Maysir* | Speculative transactions with no real economic activity are prohibited | Applies to financial regulation, investment instruments, revenue generation |
| Wealth Circulation | *Tadawul* | Wealth should circulate broadly, not concentrate in few hands | Justifies cooperative development, social enterprise promotion, zakat distribution, anti-monopoly regulation |

These principles do not require specific legislation to be binding on BARMM governance — they operate as interpretive guides for all legislation and executive action. However, a centralized Shari'ah Legislative Review Board (as discussed in Section J.4) would institutionalize the application of these principles to the legislative process.

## F. Shari'ah Governance Institutions

### F.1 Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta'

**Q. What is the Darul-Ifta' and what are its powers?**

A. The Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' is the highest Islamic advisory body in Bangsamoro governance. Created under BAA 13, Secs. 52-56, it is attached to the Office of the Chief Minister.

**Composition:** The Bangsamoro Mufti and six recognized ulama — one representing each of the five provinces (Lanao del Sur including Marawi City, Maguindanao including Cotabato City, Sulu, Basilan including Lamitan City, Tawi-Tawi) and one from the Special Geographic Area. Provincial representatives are selected through ***mashuwarah*** (consultative process).

**Mandate:** BAA 13, Sec. 53: ***"The Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' is mandated to strengthen Islamic preaching and guidance, and utilize knowledge and social capital of the Ulama for the common good."***

**Powers and Functions** (Sec. 54):
- (a) Promulgate ***fatwa*** (religious edicts) pertaining to the whole breadth of Islamic jurisprudence
- (b) Spearhead intrareligious and interreligious dialogues
- (c) Serve as consultant to the Office of the Chief Minister on religious affairs
- (d) Collaborate on Ramadan moon-sighting locally and internationally
- (e) Other functions assigned by law or the Chief Minister

**Bangsamoro Mufti Powers** (Sec. 56):
- Call sessions with Islamic Jurists for promulgating fatawa
- Preside over Darul-Ifta' sessions
- Exercise overall management and supervision
- Represent in interfaith dialogue
- **Declare the start and end of Ramadan, Eid'l Fitr, and Eid'l Adha**
- Provide reference to the Chief Minister on religious affairs and Islamic events

**Organization** (Sec. 55): Headed by the Bangsamoro Mufti (appointed by Chief Minister, preferably from resident Ulama), with a Secretariat headed by an Executive Director. Divisions: Fatwa Division, Research Division, Information and Communications Division, Administration Section.

**Cross-institutional role:** The Mufti sits on the Bangsamoro Halal Board (BAA 13, Sec. 35(g)). BAA 13, Art. VI, Sec. 25 grants the Bangsamoro Mufti general authority to administer oaths — placing this authority on par with the Chief Minister, the Wali, and members of the Judiciary.

### F.2 BAGO Legal Research and Opinion Division — Shari'ah Function

**Q. What Shari'ah role does the Bangsamoro Attorney-General play?**

A. The Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office (BAGO) has a specific Shari'ah compliance function through its Legal Research and Opinion Division (LROD). BAA 13, Sec. 17(3)(d) mandates the LROD to: ***"Formulate and review policies on Shari'ah matters to ensure compliance with Shari'ah rules and principles, and provide written Shari'ah opinions to the Chief Minister and the Cabinet."***

This makes BAGO the institutional bridge between the Darul Ifta' (which issues religious fatwas) and the executive branch (which needs legal opinions on Shari'ah compliance for governance decisions).

### F.3 Shari'ah Public Assistance Office

**Q. What is the Shari'ah Public Assistance Office?**

A. Created by BAA 13, Secs. 13-14, implementing BOL Art. X, Sec. 16. Headed by a director assisted by two counselors who must be members of the Philippine Shari'ah Bar.

**Functions** (Sec. 14):
- (a) Provide free legal assistance to indigent party litigants with cases before Shari'ah Courts
- (b) Prepare and file petitions, complaints, and responsive pleadings; represent indigent defendants in civil actions
- (c) Prepare responsive pleadings and represent indigents in special civil actions and proceedings
- (d) Prepare affidavits and represent indigents in criminal proceedings before Shari'ah Courts

Established in each Shari'ah judicial district. Interim attachment to the Office of the Chief Minister pending assignment to an appropriate ministry. Compensation: Director III equivalent for the director; trial lawyer equivalent for counselors.

### F.4 Shari'ah Special Prosecution Service

**Q. What is the Shari'ah Special Prosecution Service?**

A. Created by BAA 13, Secs. 15-16, implementing BOL Art. X, Sec. 16. Attached to the National Prosecutorial Service of the National Government — not to the Bangsamoro Government directly. The Bangsamoro Government recommends qualified applicants to the Secretary of Justice.

**Functions** (Sec. 16):
- (a) Assist in the administration of the Shari'ah justice system in the Bangsamoro
- (b) Provide legal assistance and advice to residents seeking to file criminal cases in Shari'ah courts
- (c) Conduct preliminary investigation and prosecution of criminal cases where both parties are Muslims and fall under Shari'ah criminal jurisdiction
- (d) Monitor all criminal cases filed with Shari'ah Courts, maintain case records, and adopt systems to expedite disposition

### F.5 Unfulfilled BOL-Mandated Institutions

**Q. What BOL-authorized Shari'ah institutions have not yet been created?**

A. Of the four institutions authorized by BOL Art. X, Sec. 16, two remain unfulfilled:

1. **Shari'ah Academy** — A judicial training institution for Shari'ah practitioners. No BAA has been enacted. Pending: PB No. 363 (Bangsamoro Shari'ah Judicial and Legal Academy).

2. **Office of the Jurisconsult of Islamic Law** — An authoritative interpretation office for Islamic law. No BAA has been enacted and no pending bill addresses it directly.

Additionally, the **Shari'ah High Court** authorized by BOL Art. X, Sec. 7 has not yet been organized. Parliament Resolution No. 458 requests the Supreme Court to organize the Shari'ah High Court in BARMM.

## G. Islamic Economic Institutions

### G.1 Bangsamoro Halal Board

**Q. What is the Halal Board's structure and function?**

A. Created by BAA 13, Secs. 33-36, implementing BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 30. Attached to the Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT). Note: BAA 13, Sec. 33 uses ***"Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Industry"*** for the Halal Board attachment, while the ministry's own chapter (Title XIV) uses "Tourism" — an internal BAA 13 inconsistency.

**Mandate** (Sec. 33): The Bangsamoro Halal Board is the ***"policy-making body of the Bangsamoro Government on Halal Industry Development"*** and sets the ***"overall direction for the implementation of the Bangsamoro Halal Industry Development and Promotion Program."***

**Powers and Functions** (Sec. 34):
- (a) Grant or deny applications for issuance of certificates of accreditation of halal standards
- (b) Formulate, advocate, coordinate, oversee, and assess the implementation of the Bangsamoro Halal Development and Promotion Program
- (c) Coordinate with existing government regulatory agencies on halal standard setting
- (d) Promulgate policies and guidelines
- (e) Create technical panels, working groups, or task forces
- (f) Investigate complaints, controversies, or disputes on halal standards enforcement
- (g) Request assistance from any ministry or agency in implementation
- (h) Other powers prescribed by law

**Composition** (Sec. 35): Chaired by the MTIT Minister; Vice-Chaired by the MAFAR Minister. Members include the Ministers of Science and Technology, Health, Labor and Employment, Transportation and Communications; the ***Mufti of the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta'***; an MSU System representative; and two Bangsamoro professionals with halal industry experience (appointed by the Chief Minister from nominees by civil society). The Mufti's membership ensures Shari'ah compliance in halal governance.

**Secretariat** (Sec. 36): Established by the MTIT Minister as an inter-agency secretariat. MAFAR and MOST assign technical staff.

### G.2 Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority

**Q. What is the BPA's structure and function?**

A. Created by BAA 13, Secs. 43-46, implementing BOL Art. VII, Sec. 42.

**Mandate** (Sec. 44): Administration of the annual Muslim pilgrimage to Mecca (Hajj) and Umrah.

**Functions** (Sec. 45): Travel document processing, sheikh accreditation, Hajj timetable management, ensuring free choice of travel agencies for pilgrims.

**Organization** (Sec. 46): Headed by an Executive Director (Director II rank), with a Deputy Director (Director I). Three divisions: Pilgrimage Operations Division, Endowment Administration Division, and Administration/Finance. The **Endowment Administration Division** is notable — it suggests a waqf (Islamic endowment) management function within the BPA.

### G.3 Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank

**Q. What is the Bangsamoro Government's relationship with Al-Amanah?**

A. The Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank of the Philippines was created by RA 6848 (1989) — the first and only government-chartered Islamic bank in the Philippines. BOL Art. XII, Sec. 33 provides that the ***"Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board shall determine the participation of the Bangsamoro Government in the Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank."*** This means Bangsamoro Government participation is subject to intergovernmental negotiation, not unilateral Bangsamoro action.

### G.4 Islamic Banking Framework Under BSP

**Q. How does Islamic banking regulation interact with Bangsamoro authority?**

A. RA 11439 (2019) establishes the national framework for Islamic banking regulation under the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas. BOL Art. XIII, Secs. 31-34 create a parallel Bangsamoro mandate:

- **Islamic windows** in conventional banks and offshore Islamic banking units are authorized within BARMM (Art. XIII, Sec. 31)
- A **joint development obligation** binds the Bangsamoro Government, BSP, DOF, and NCMF (Art. XIII, Sec. 32)
- An **Islamic banking unit within BSP** is mandated (Art. XIII, Sec. 33)
- A **Shari'ah Supervisory Board** with fatwa-issuing authority monitors compliance (Art. XIII, Sec. 34)

BSP retains regulatory authority over all banks (including Islamic banks) operating in BARMM. The Bangsamoro Government's role is promotional and developmental — not regulatory in the banking supervision sense.

## H. Islamic Education and Culture

### H.1 Madaris Education System (MBHTE)

**Q. How is Islamic education institutionalized in BARMM?**

A. BAA 13, Secs. 3-28 (MBHTE chapter) establish a comprehensive Islamic education system within the Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education:

**Scope** (Sec. 3): MBHTE covers ***"higher education, including Islamic higher education"*** and is mandated to ***"establish, maintain, and supervise Madaris education in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** and ***"ensure the integration on the elementary and high school education curricula the teaching of Islamic and Arabic studies for Muslim pupils and students in public schools."***

**Organizational Structure:**
- **Directorate-General for Madaris Education** (Sec. 5(d)) — a dedicated organizational unit
- **Bureau of Madaris Education** (Sec. 28) — under a Director-General

**Madaris Education Functions** (Secs. 27-28): Eight specific powers:
1. Formulate general Madaris education objectives
2. Ensure standards for public and private Madaris
3. Develop curricula, textbooks, and teaching materials for Madaris
4. Formulate annual budget for Madaris education
5. Allocate and disburse funds for Madaris education
6. Approve and support establishment of Madaris and learning centers
7. Recruit, appoint, and promote Madaris personnel
8. Promulgate rules for Madaris education

**Islamic Higher Education** (Secs. 17-18): The Ministry covers Islamic Higher Education and transnational institutions. The Minister formulates programs on ***"higher education and research, including higher Islamic education."***

### H.2 Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies (BAA 50)

**Q. What is the BKIS?**

A. BAA 50 creates the Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies — a dedicated institution for Islamic higher education. This fulfills part of the BOL Art. IX, Sec. 18 mandate. The term "Kulliyyah" (Arabic for college/faculty of knowledge) itself reflects the Islamic educational tradition.

### H.3 Cultural Heritage — Islamic Dimension

**Q. How does cultural heritage preservation connect to Shari'ah governance?**

A. The Bangsamoro Commission on the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (established under BAA 13) has an Islamic heritage dimension — preserving mosques, Islamic manuscripts, traditional Islamic architectural sites, and intangible Islamic cultural heritage. The Bangsamoro Youth Commission is mandated to organize programs with ***"particular emphasis on Bangsamoro culture and Islamic values"*** (BAA 13, line 4272). The Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission recognizes the imam as a spiritual adviser with access to persons deprived of liberty (BAA 13, line 4723).

### H.4 Islamic Health Policies

**Q. How does Islamic teaching affect health governance?**

A. The Ministry of Health is mandated under BAA 13, Book VI, Title V (MOH), Sec. 3(j) to ***"Formulate policies, guidelines, regulations as well as operating procedures congruent to Islamic teachings on health and sanitation."*** This creates a unique intersection between Shari'ah principles and public health — accommodating Islamic practices related to dietary restrictions, modesty norms in healthcare, and end-of-life care in accordance with Islamic rites.

## J. Legislative Gaps

### J.1 Unfulfilled Shari'ah Mandates

**Q. What Shari'ah mandates in the BOL remain unfulfilled?**

A. Six categories of unfulfilled mandates span the 55 powers:

1. **Parliament's Shari'ah Legislative Power** (Art. X, Sec. 4) — Parliament ***"shall have the power to enact laws on personal, family, and property law jurisdiction"*** and ***"laws governing commercial and other civil actions not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083...and criminal jurisdiction on minor offenses punishable by arresto menor or ta'zir."*** **No standalone Shari'ah code has been enacted.** PD 1083 remains the operative Shari'ah legislation within BARMM.

2. **Shari'ah Academy** (Art. X, Sec. 16) — Parliament is authorized to create a Shari'ah academy for judicial training. **Not yet created.** PB No. 363 (Bangsamoro Shari'ah Judicial and Legal Academy) is pending.

3. **Office of the Jurisconsult of Islamic Law** (Art. X, Sec. 16) — Parliament is authorized to create an Office of the Jurisconsult. **Not yet created and no pending bill addresses it.**

4. **Halal Development Act** (Art. XIII, Sec. 30) — Parliament ***"shall enact laws to further strengthen its policy and programs on halal development."*** **No standalone halal act has been enacted.** The Halal Board operates under BAA 13 general mandate only. PB No. 268 (Halal Consumers Act) is pending.

5. **Waqf Act** — No BOL provision expressly mandates a Waqf Act, but the Islamic endowment (waqf) framework is necessary for full implementation of Islamic social finance. PB No. 337 (Bangsamoro Waqf Commission) is pending.

6. **Islamic Banking Framework BAA** — The BOL mandates a comprehensive Islamic banking and finance system (Art. XIII, Secs. 31-34), including a Shari'ah Supervisory Board. **No BAA implements this mandate.** While RA 11439 provides the national framework, a Bangsamoro BAA would operationalize the specific BARMM role in promoting Islamic finance institutions within the region.

| **(q) Customary laws** | No dedicated BAA | A Bangsamoro Customary Law Recognition Act would establish: procedures for recognizing Islamic customs ('ada), relationship between customary law and enacted BAAs, customary dispute resolution mechanisms, documentation and codification of Islamic customary practices |

### J.4 Institutional Gaps Across the Shari'ah Governance Landscape

**Q. What institutional gaps exist in the Shari'ah governance landscape?**

A. Beyond the specific unfulfilled BOL mandates identified in Section J.1, five structural institutional gaps affect the full exercise of Shari'ah governance across the 55 powers:

1. **No centralized Shari'ah Legislative Review Board (SLRB)** — Parliament has no institutional mechanism for Shari'ah compliance review of all proposed legislation. The BAGO LROD Shari'ah function (BAA 13, Sec. 17(3)(d)) provides executive-branch Shari'ah opinions, and the Darul Ifta' issues religious fatwas, but neither is formally embedded in the legislative process. An SLRB — composed of Shari'ah scholars and legal practitioners — would review bills for Shari'ah compliance before enactment.

2. **No Shari'ah mediation/arbitration framework** — BOL Art. X, Sec. 20 authorizes Shari'ah ADR, but no BAA establishes the institutional framework for Shari'ah-based mediation and arbitration. The Agama Arbitration Councils under PD 1083 provide a precedent but are limited to personal status matters.

3. **No Shari'ah-compliant public procurement framework** — BARMM procurement follows the national Government Procurement Reform Act (RA 9184). Where procurement involves halal certification, Islamic education materials, or religious items, no Shari'ah-specific procurement guidelines exist.

4. **No integrated Shari'ah training pipeline** — The pathway from Islamic studies education (madaris/BKIS) to Shari'ah legal practice (Shari'ah Bar) to judicial appointment lacks institutional integration. A Shari'ah Academy (PB No. 363) would partially address this, but a comprehensive pipeline connecting BKIS, law school Islamic law programs, the Shari'ah Bar, and judicial continuing education does not yet exist.

5. **No Shari'ah court automation** — The Shari'ah court system lacks the case management and e-filing infrastructure that regular courts have through the Judiciary's eCourt system. Digital infrastructure for Muslim civil registry records, Shari'ah court case management, and Shari'ah Bar membership records is needed.

# PART IV: DIVERGENCE AND DEVELOPMENT

## K. Shari'ah as Basis for Divergence

### K.1 The 5-Layer Constraint Framework Applied to Shari'ah

**Q. How does Shari'ah interact with the constraints on Bangsamoro lawmaking?**

A. All Bangsamoro legislation operates under a 5-layer constraint framework. Shari'ah has a unique position within this framework:

**Layer 1: Constitution** — Art. X, Sec. 15 requires all autonomous region governance to operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."*** Shari'ah governance must comply with constitutional rights (equal protection, due process, religious freedom). Shari'ah cannot be applied compulsorily to non-Muslims — the voluntary submission rule in BOL Art. X, Sec. 1 operationalizes this constitutional constraint.

**Layer 2: BOL (RA 11054)** — The organic law both authorizes and limits Shari'ah governance. Art. X provides the detailed Shari'ah framework; Art. V, Sec. 2 enumerates Shari'ah-related powers. Parliament's Shari'ah legislative authority is bounded by Art. X, Sec. 4 — it cannot exceed the subject matters specified (personal, family, property, commercial, ta'zir criminal).

**Layer 3: National Laws** — PD 1083, RA 11439, and other national Shari'ah laws continue to apply within BARMM. A BAA can supplement but not contradict these national laws where the BOL requires consistency. Where the BOL is silent on consistency, the Bangsamoro Government has broader discretion.

**Layer 4: BAAs** — Enacted Bangsamoro legislation implements the BOL's Shari'ah mandates. BAA 13 creates the institutional infrastructure; dedicated BAAs (BAA 20, 39, 50) implement specific Shari'ah governance functions.

**Layer 5: IRRs and Executive Action** — Chief Minister executive orders, ministry orders, and implementing rules operationalize the BAAs within the Shari'ah framework.

### K.2 Where Shari'ah Provides Unique BARMM Justification

**Q. Where does Shari'ah provide the strongest basis for BARMM divergence from national standards?**

A. Shari'ah provides the strongest basis for divergence in five areas:

1. **Cooperative and economic models** — The prohibition of riba (interest) justifies Shari'ah-compliant cooperative structures (mudarabah, musharakah, murabaha) that differ fundamentally from interest-based models in the national Cooperative Code (RA 9520). A Bangsamoro Cooperative Code could create a dual-track system: conventional and Shari'ah-compliant.

2. **Family law and personal status** — PD 1083 already provides a different family law regime for Muslims. Parliament's authority under Art. X, Sec. 4 to legislate on personal, family, and property law allows BARMM to expand this regime beyond PD 1083's scope.

3. **Criminal jurisdiction (ta'zir)** — Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to legislate on ***"criminal jurisdiction on minor offenses punishable by arresto menor or ta'zir."*** This is a unique criminal law power that no other local government or autonomous region possesses — allowing Islamic criminal penalties for minor offenses involving Muslims.

4. **Social finance** — Zakat (Islamic obligatory charity), waqf (Islamic endowments), and Shari'ah-compliant investment create an alternative social protection framework that the national system does not provide. PB No. 160 (Zakat-Income Tax integration) would create a precedent-setting divergence from national tax law.

5. **Islamic education** — The comprehensive madaris system and Islamic higher education (BKIS) represent a distinct educational track that the national DepEd/CHED framework does not provide. BOL Art. IX, Sec. 18 is the basis for this divergence.

### K.3 Shari'ah as Institutional Differentiator

**Q. How does Shari'ah make BARMM governance structurally different from other Philippine LGUs and regions?**

A. Shari'ah creates seven structural differences that no other Philippine local government or region possesses:

1. **Parallel court system** — BARMM is the only jurisdiction with a three-tier court system (Circuit, District, High Court) operating alongside the regular judiciary. No LGU has its own court system.

2. **Fatwa-issuing governance body** — The Darul Ifta' has authority to promulgate fatwas (religious edicts) — a government-mandated religious advisory function that does not exist elsewhere in Philippine governance.

3. **Dual calendar system** — BAA 20 mandates the Hijri calendar alongside the Gregorian — the only jurisdiction in the Philippines operating under two official calendar systems.

4. **Religious holiday authority** — The Bangsamoro Mufti declares the start and end of Ramadan, Eid'l Fitr, and Eid'l Adha — decisions that affect work schedules, government operations, and public life across the region.

5. **Halal regulatory authority** — The Bangsamoro Halal Board exercises halal certification and standard-setting authority — a regulatory function derived from Islamic dietary law that has no parallel in other Philippine jurisdictions.

6. **Islamic endowment administration** — The BPA's Endowment Administration Division manages waqf-related functions — an Islamic institutional form that the Philippine legal system does not otherwise accommodate.

7. **Shari'ah compliance function in the Attorney-General** — The BAGO LROD's mandate to review policies for Shari'ah compliance is unique — no other Attorney-General's office in the Philippines has a religious compliance function.

These are not merely cultural accommodations — they are governance structures mandated by the BOL and operationalized through BAA 13. They constitute the structural case for why BARMM legislation must diverge from national norms to fulfill its organic law mandate.

### K.4 Equal Protection Applied to Shari'ah Legislation

**Q. How does equal protection apply to Shari'ah-based legislation?**

A. The constitutional equal protection guarantee (Art. III, Sec. 1) requires that similarly situated persons be treated alike. Shari'ah legislation raises three equal protection questions:

1. **Muslim/non-Muslim distinction** — Shari'ah applies only to Muslims (BOL Art. X, Sec. 1). This is constitutionally permissible because 1987 Const., Art. X, Sec. 18 expressly authorizes ***"special courts with personal, family, and property law jurisdiction"*** and 1987 Const., Art. X, Sec. 20(4) grants legislative power over ***"personal, family, and property relations."*** The classification is based on religious identity (a substantial distinction) and serves a legitimate purpose (respecting the right to be governed by one's own personal law system).

2. **Shari'ah-compliant alternatives** — When BARMM legislation creates Shari'ah-compliant alternatives (e.g., in cooperatives, housing finance, banking), equal protection requires that conventional alternatives remain available for non-Muslims and Muslims who choose them. A mandatory Shari'ah-only system would raise equal protection concerns.

3. **Women's rights** — Certain PD 1083 provisions (polygyny, custody rules, inheritance shares) create different outcomes for women. The constitutionality of these provisions rests on the specific constitutional authorization for personal law systems in autonomous regions (Art. X, Sec. 18). However, Parliament's expansion of Shari'ah law under Art. X, Sec. 4 must be balanced against the constitutional mandate for gender equality (Art. II, Sec. 14).

## L. Development Connection

### L.1 BDP 2023-2028 Goals

**Q. How does Shari'ah governance connect to BARMM's development goals?**

A. The Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP) 2023-2028 connects Shari'ah governance to development outcomes across multiple chapters:

- **Chapter 7 (Economic Development)** — Islamic banking and finance, halal industry development, Shari'ah-compliant cooperatives, and waqf as instruments of economic growth
- **Chapter 9 (Social Development)** — Madaris education, Islamic higher education, and Ibadah-friendly health services
- **Chapter 4 (Development Framework)** — The BDP's development strategy recognizes moral governance and Islamic values as part of the Bangsamoro development identity

### L.2 Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028)

**Q. How does the Matatag Agenda relate to Shari'ah governance?**

A. The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda's five pillars intersect with Shari'ah governance:

- **Pillar 1 (Moral Governance)** — The Darul Ifta's role in policy consultation, the Bangsamoro Mufti's authority, and the BAGO's Shari'ah compliance function directly support moral governance
- **Pillar 2 (Security and Justice)** — The Shari'ah court system, SPAO, and SSPS are the justice infrastructure for Bangsamoro Muslims
- **Pillar 3 (Economic Empowerment)** — Islamic banking, halal industry, waqf, zakat, and Shari'ah-compliant cooperatives are economic empowerment instruments
- **Pillar 4 (Social Development)** — Madaris education, Islamic higher education, and Ibadah-friendly health services serve social development

### L.3 Islamic Development Finance

**Q. What international Islamic development cooperation is available to BARMM?**

A. BARMM can access Islamic development finance through multiple channels:

- **Islamic Development Bank (IsDB)** — the multilateral development bank of the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC), which has historically funded projects in Mindanao. IsDB provides concessional financing through Shari'ah-compliant instruments (istisna'a for infrastructure, murabaha for procurement, technical assistance grants). BARMM's status as an autonomous region with Islamic governance structures makes it an eligible sub-sovereign borrower.

- **OIC development cooperation** — bilateral and multilateral programs among OIC member states, including technical cooperation in Islamic education, halal industry development, and governance capacity-building.

- **Zakat as social protection** — PB No. 160 (Zakat-Income Tax integration) would formalize zakat as a domestic social protection instrument, potentially unlocking significant charitable resources from Muslim taxpayers in BARMM. Zakat's eight prescribed categories of beneficiaries (asnaf) — including the poor (fuqara'), the needy (masakin), zakat administrators, those in bondage, debtors, those in the way of Allah, and wayfarers — map directly onto social protection objectives.

- **Waqf as development capital** — Islamic endowments (waqf), if properly institutionalized through PB No. 337 (Bangsamoro Waqf Commission), can provide perpetual funding for mosques, schools, hospitals, and community infrastructure. The BPA's Endowment Administration Division already suggests a waqf management function; a dedicated Waqf Act would create the legal framework for productive waqf investment.

- **Sukuk (Islamic bonds)** — If BARMM develops its Islamic finance infrastructure, it could potentially issue sukuk for infrastructure financing — Shari'ah-compliant bonds backed by tangible assets. This would require enabling legislation and a functioning Shari'ah Supervisory Board.

### L.4 Shari'ah Governance as Development Enabler

**Q. How does Shari'ah governance enable development outcomes that secular governance cannot?**

A. Shari'ah governance enables five development outcomes unique to BARMM:

1. **Legitimacy and compliance** — For a predominantly Muslim population, Shari'ah-compliant governance carries religious legitimacy that secular governance alone cannot achieve. This translates into higher compliance with government programs, greater trust in public institutions, and stronger social cohesion. The Darul Ifta's fatwa authority, for example, can mobilize community compliance with public health campaigns, tax collection, and environmental protection — areas where secular government authority alone has historically been weak in BARMM.

2. **Financial inclusion** — A significant portion of the Bangsamoro Muslim population avoids conventional banking due to riba (interest) prohibition. Islamic banking and Shari'ah-compliant financial products expand financial inclusion for this population — connecting them to the formal financial system through instruments that comply with their religious obligations.

3. **Social protection through religious obligation** — Zakat is not merely a charitable donation — it is a religious obligation (one of the Five Pillars of Islam). Formalizing zakat through legislation transforms a voluntary/informal practice into a systematic social protection mechanism with institutional collection, distribution, and accountability.

4. **Dispute resolution through religious authority** — Shari'ah courts resolve personal status disputes (marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody) for Muslims through a system they recognize as authoritative. Without Shari'ah courts, these disputes either go unresolved or are resolved through informal channels without legal protections. A functioning Shari'ah court system — with the SPAO providing free legal assistance — ensures access to justice for Muslim communities.

5. **International Islamic cooperation** — Shari'ah governance structures (halal certification, Islamic education institutions, Islamic banking) create entry points for cooperation with OIC member states and Islamic development institutions. These are channels that secular governance structures cannot access with the same credibility.

### L.5 Risks of Incomplete Shari'ah Governance Implementation

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if Shari'ah governance gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Five categories of development risk arise from incomplete implementation:

1. **Justice deficit** — Without the Shari'ah High Court, Shari'ah Academy, and Office of the Jurisconsult, the Shari'ah justice system operates without an apex court, without a training institution, and without an authoritative interpretation body. Shari'ah District Court decisions go to the secular Court of Appeals instead of a specialized Shari'ah appellate body — potentially producing inconsistent Shari'ah jurisprudence.

2. **Financial exclusion** — Without a Bangsamoro Islamic Banking and Finance Development Act, the BOL's mandate for Islamic finance development (Art. XIII, Secs. 31-34) remains aspirational. Muslim entrepreneurs, cooperatives, and households who need Shari'ah-compliant financing have limited options within the formal system.

3. **Halal industry underdevelopment** — Without a standalone Halal Development Act, the Bangsamoro Halal Board operates under BAA 13's general mandate without comprehensive regulatory teeth — no enforcement penalties, no detailed standards, no systematic accreditation framework. BARMM's potential as a halal production hub remains unrealized.

4. **Social protection gap** — Without Zakat and Waqf Acts, two of the most powerful Islamic social finance instruments remain outside the formal governance framework. Zakat collections happen informally; waqf properties lack legal protection and professional management.

5. **Human capital gap** — Without the Shari'ah Academy, the pipeline of qualified Shari'ah judges, prosecutors, and legal practitioners is constrained. The dual qualification requirement (Philippine Bar + Shari'ah expertise) already limits the pool of eligible candidates; without a dedicated training institution, this pool cannot expand to meet the needs of an expanding court system.

### L.6 Implementation Priority Sequencing

**Q. In what order should the unfulfilled Shari'ah mandates be addressed?**

A. Based on development impact, institutional readiness, and BOL urgency, the recommended sequencing is:

**Immediate Priority (current legislative term):**

1. **Shari'ah High Court Organization** — Requires Supreme Court action, not Parliament legislation. Parliament has already passed Res. No. 458 requesting SC action. The absence of the SHC means the apex Shari'ah court created by the BOL (Art. X, Sec. 7) — the signature judicial innovation of the organic law — does not exist. Every year of delay undermines the legitimacy and coherence of the Shari'ah justice system.

2. **Bangsamoro Shari'ah Code** — The Parliament's unused authority under Art. X, Sec. 4 is the single most significant legislative gap. Enacting Shari'ah legislation on personal, family, property, commercial, and ta'zir criminal matters would modernize PD 1083 (a 1977 martial-law-era decree) for the Bangsamoro context. This is the legislative foundation upon which all other Shari'ah institutions operate.

3. **Bangsamoro Halal Development and Promotion Act** — The Halal Board exists (BAA 13, Secs. 33-36) but operates without comprehensive regulatory authority. A standalone act would provide: halal certification standards with enforcement penalties, producer/processor compliance requirements, halal audit protocols, halal export development program, and coordination with the national halal framework (RA 10817).

**Medium-Term Priority (within 2 years):**

4. **Bangsamoro Waqf Act** — PB No. 337 (Bangsamoro Waqf Commission) is pending. Waqf has the potential to become a significant development resource if properly institutionalized — providing perpetual funding for Islamic education, healthcare, mosques, and community infrastructure.

5. **Bangsamoro Shari'ah Judicial and Legal Academy Act** — PB No. 363 is pending. The academy would create the training infrastructure needed to expand the pool of qualified Shari'ah judges, prosecutors, and practitioners — addressing the human capital bottleneck in the Shari'ah justice system.

6. **Bangsamoro Zakat Act** — PB No. 160 (Zakat-Income Tax integration) is pending. This would formalize zakat as a systematic social protection instrument, with institutional collection, distribution based on the eight asnaf categories, and accountability mechanisms.

**Longer-Term Priority:**

7. **Office of the Jurisconsult of Islamic Law** — No pending bill. This institution would provide authoritative Islamic law interpretation for Parliament, courts, and the executive — a missing institutional link between the Darul Ifta' (which issues religious fatwas) and the formal legal system (which needs binding legal interpretations of Shari'ah).

8. **Bangsamoro Islamic Banking and Finance Development Act** — Would operationalize Art. XIII, Secs. 31-34 by establishing the Bangsamoro Government's role in Islamic finance promotion, creating enabling conditions for Shari'ah-compliant financial institutions, and establishing the Shari'ah Supervisory Board.

9. **Shari'ah-Compliant provisions in the Bangsamoro Cooperative Code** — The cooperative code is an independent legislative mandate (Art. XIII, Sec. 24) that should include a Shari'ah-compliant track (mudarabah, musharakah, murabaha cooperative models) as an alternative to interest-based cooperative finance.
