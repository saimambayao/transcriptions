# (ii) Islamic Banking and Finance

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(ii)** — ***"Islamic banking and finance;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(ii) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XIII, Sec. 32 subjects Islamic bank operations to ***"the power of supervision of the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas."*** Art. XIII, Sec. 32 also mandates **joint promotion** with BSP, DOF, and NCMF. This is one of the most intergovernmentally constrained powers among the 55. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Finance, Budget and Management (MFBM)** — BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. IV (Sec. 1-20); BAA 85, Ch. III. MFBM's mandate includes coordination of ***"fiscal and monetary policies, credit, economic development, international finance, trade and investment"*** (BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. IV, Sec. 3(j)). |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP — national, constitutionally mandated supervisor); Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT); Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI); Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' (Shari'ah compliance advisory); Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank of the Philippines (AAIIBP) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 4 (Development Framework) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | ***"The Parliament shall enact laws that promote the growth of Islamic finance such as those that promote tax incentives and ensure tax neutrality of Islamic finance transactions"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 32) — not yet enacted. Additionally, ***"crafting of the Bangsamoro Islamic banking and finance framework by the Parliament"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 34) — not yet enacted. |

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over Islamic banking and finance?**

A. Article V, Section 2(ii) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Islamic banking and finance"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This grant covers the promotion, development, and regulatory participation in Islamic banking and Shari'ah-compliant financial services within BARMM. Critically, this is among the BOL's **most detailed powers** — Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34 devote four full sections to elaborating scope, institutional requirements, intergovernmental relationships, and legislative mandates.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Seven provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 31 imposes a **promotional mandate** — the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall encourage the establishment of"*** banks, financial institutions (including ***"an Islamic window in domestic and foreign conventional banks"***), and ***"offshore banking units of foreign banks within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, and in accordance with the principles of the Islamic banking system."***[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 32 (para. 1) establishes an **intergovernmental joint mandate** — four institutions (Bangsamoro Government, BSP, DOF, NCMF) must ***"jointly promote the development of an Islamic banking and finance system."*** BSP determines the organizational structure. This is the most intergovernmentally constrained economic power in the BOL.[^3]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 32 (para. 2) mandates **market development** — joint review of market environment policies, measures to enhance competitiveness of Islamic finance products, and promotion of investor awareness.[^3]

   (4) Art. XIII, Sec. 32 (para. 3) confirms **BSP supervisory supremacy** — all Islamic banks and Shari'ah-compliant financial institutions ***"shall be subject to the power of supervision of the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas."***[^3]

   (5) Art. XIII, Sec. 33 directs BSP to establish an **Islamic banking unit** within its structure.[^4]

   (6) Art. XIII, Sec. 34 defines the **Shari'ah Supervisory Board** — monitoring Shari'ah compliance in banking transactions, issuing *fatwas* on banking products, and requiring knowledge of both Islamic jurisprudence and conventional banking.[^5]

   (7) Art. XII, Sec. 33 mandates the IFPB to determine the **Bangsamoro Government's participation in AAIIBP** within six months of BTA establishment.[^6]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **two express legislative mandates**:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 32 (para. 4): ***"The Parliament shall enact laws that promote the growth of Islamic finance such as those that promote tax incentives and ensure tax neutrality of Islamic finance transactions in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** This mandates both **tax incentives** and **tax neutrality** legislation.[^3]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 34 (opening clause): ***"Without prejudice to the crafting of the Bangsamoro Islamic banking and finance framework by the Parliament"*** — authorizing Parliament to craft the comprehensive Bangsamoro Islamic banking framework.[^5]

   Neither mandate has been fulfilled as of BAA 89.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL envisions but does not directly create two bodies: (1) a **Shari'ah Supervisory Board** for monitoring banking Shari'ah compliance (Art. XIII, Sec. 34 defines its functions and qualifications but Art. XIII, Sec. 32 assigns BSP the authority to determine its organizational structure); and (2) an **Islamic banking unit** within BSP (Art. XIII, Sec. 33). Neither is a Bangsamoro Government body — both operate under BSP authority. The BOL also references AAIIBP (Art. XII, Sec. 33) as an existing national institution in which the Bangsamoro Government has a mandated participatory interest.[^3][^4][^5][^6]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places three types of constraints:

   (1) **BSP supervisory supremacy**: Art. XIII, Sec. 32 (para. 3) — all Islamic banks ***"shall be subject to the power of supervision of the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas."*** This is the single most significant constraint. The Bangsamoro Government cannot independently license, regulate, or supervise banks.[^3]

   (2) **Intergovernmental joint action**: Art. XIII, Sec. 32 (para. 1) — the Islamic banking system must be ***"jointly"*** promoted by four institutions. The Bangsamoro Government cannot unilaterally develop the system.[^3]

   (3) **BSP organizational authority**: Art. XIII, Sec. 32 (para. 1) — ***"The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas shall determine the type of organizational structure to be created and its composition"*** for the Shari'ah Supervisory Board. Even the Shari'ah compliance mechanism is subject to BSP's structural determination.[^3]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to Islamic banking and finance?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."***[^7]

   (2) **Central Monetary Authority (Art. XII, Sec. 20)**: ***"The Congress shall establish an independent central monetary authority, the members of whose governing board must be natural-born Filipino citizens, of known probity, integrity, and patriotism, the majority of whom shall come from the private sector."*** This is the **constitutional mandate** that created BSP. BSP's independence is constitutionally protected — the Bangsamoro Government cannot create a parallel banking regulator, override BSP's supervisory authority, or direct BSP's regulatory decisions within BARMM.[^8]

   (3) **Banks and Banking Institutions (Art. XII, Sec. 20, last paragraph)**: ***"The Congress shall prescribe the qualifications and disqualifications of the members of the governing board of the central monetary authority. The Congress shall provide for an industry regulatory body composed of the heads of the central monetary authority, the insurance commission, and the securities exchange commission."*** This confirms that banking regulation is a **congressional prerogative** delegated to BSP — not a power that autonomous regions can independently exercise.[^8]

   (4) **Equal Protection and Due Process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: Any Islamic banking framework must provide equal protection — persons in BARMM cannot be subjected to more restrictive banking regulations than those elsewhere, and Shari'ah-compliant products cannot be taxed more heavily than conventional equivalents (this is the constitutional basis for tax neutrality).[^9]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee relevant to Islamic banking?**

A. The Constitution guarantees: (1) **right to equal protection** — Islamic finance products must not be subjected to discriminatory taxation (the tax neutrality mandate); (2) **due process** — any regulatory action against Islamic financial institutions must satisfy notice, hearing, and appeal; (3) **freedom of religion (Art. III, Sec. 5)** — the right to practice religion includes the right to conduct economic activity in accordance with religious principles, supporting the availability of Shari'ah-compliant financial options.[^9][^10]

**Q. What constitutional mandates for banking legislation or institutions exist?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 20 mandates Congress to establish an **independent central monetary authority** (BSP). This constitutional mandate means BSP's supervisory authority over all banks — including Islamic banks in BARMM — carries constitutional weight. The BOL's recognition of BSP supervision (Art. XIII, Sec. 32) is not merely a policy choice but a constitutional necessity.[^8]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for Islamic banking?**

A. The national framework establishes a comprehensive Islamic banking system: **RA 11439** (Islamic Banking Act, 2019) — authorizes Islamic bank establishment, Islamic banking units within conventional banks, foreign Islamic bank entry; defines Islamic banking powers (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*, *murabaha*, *ijarah*, *istisnaa*, *sukuk*); requires Shari'ah advisory councils; grants BSP full regulatory authority. **RA 6848** (1990) — creates AAIIBP as the only government-owned Islamic bank with Shari'ah Advisory Council. **RA 7653**, as amended by **RA 11211** — BSP charter establishing full supervisory authority. Together, these provide: a **licensing system**, **prudential standards**, **Shari'ah governance mechanisms**, **product definitions**, and an **existing Islamic bank** (AAIIBP).[^11][^12][^13]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national banking laws apply **directly** in BARMM — not suppletorily:

   (1) **RA 11439** — governs all Islamic banking operations within BARMM. Applies **directly** — BSP supervisory authority is constitutionally mandated.[^11]
   (2) **RA 6848** — AAIIBP operates branches within BARMM. Applies **directly**.[^12]
   (3) **RA 7653 / RA 11211** — BSP exercises full regulatory authority over all banks in BARMM. Applies **directly**.[^13]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for Islamic banking?**

A. **BSP** exercises full supervisory authority — licensing, prudential regulation, examination, and enforcement over all banks and banking units (conventional and Islamic) operating in BARMM. **AAIIBP** operates as the primary Islamic banking institution accessible within BARMM, with its head office in Zamboanga City and branches in Muslim Mindanao. Unlike other powers where national agency authority transfers to BARMM upon enactment of a BAA, BSP's authority **cannot transfer** — it is constitutionally mandated. See **Section D (Implementation).2** for operational details.[^3][^13]

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Finance, Budget and Management (MFBM)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. IV, Sec. 2 defines MFBM's mandate as ***"primarily mandated to evolve sound, efficient and responsive fiscal management and utilization of resources."*** Sec. 3(j) specifically assigns MFBM to ***"coordinate with other Bangsamoro Ministries and Offices on matters concerning fiscal and monetary policies, credit, economic development, international finance, trade and investment."*** BAA 85, Ch. III allocates its operational budget.[^14][^15]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for Islamic banking and finance as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through MFBM's BAA 13 general mandate, the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta''s Shari'ah advisory function (BAA 13, Bk. V, Tit. II, Ch. 3, Sec. 52-55), and BBOI's investment promotion function (BAA 13, Bk. V, Tit. II, Ch. 2, Sec. 21-29).

**Q. What are MFBM's mandate and functions under BAA 13 relevant to Islamic banking?**

A. BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. IV defines MFBM's structure comprehensively. The functions most relevant to Islamic banking are:

   - Sec. 3(g): ***"Review and coordinate the policies, plans and programs of Bangsamoro Government financial institutions and, thereafter, recommend to them courses of action consistent with the Bangsamoro government fiscal policies, plans and programs"***
   - Sec. 3(h): ***"Review and coordinate policies, plans and programs of GOCCs in the BARMM, and, thereafter, recommend to them courses of action consistent with the Bangsamoro Government's fiscal policies, plans and programs"*** — relevant to Bangsamoro participation in AAIIBP
   - Sec. 3(j): ***"Coordinate with other Bangsamoro Ministries and Offices on matters concerning fiscal and monetary policies, credit, economic development, international finance, trade and investment"*** — the broadest basis for Islamic finance coordination

   The **Bangsamoro Finance Office** (Sec. 15-16) includes: Fiscal Policy Development Division, Revenue Policy Development Division, Corporate Affairs Division, International and Other Finance Division, and Strategy, Economics, and Results Division — all relevant to Islamic finance framework development.

   The **Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta'** (BAA 13, Bk. V, Tit. II, Ch. 3, Sec. 52-55) — mandated to promulgate ***"fatwa (religious edicts) pertaining to the whole breadth of Islamic jurisprudence"*** (Sec. 54(a)) — serves as the Shari'ah compliance advisory body for Islamic finance policy.[^14][^16]

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone Bangsamoro Islamic Banking and Finance Act?**

A. While BAA 13 gives MFBM broad coordination functions, it does not provide: a **Bangsamoro Islamic banking and finance framework** (the second legislative mandate in Art. XIII, Sec. 34); **tax incentives** specific to Islamic finance transactions; **tax neutrality provisions** ensuring Shari'ah-compliant structures are not taxed more heavily than conventional equivalents; a **Bangsamoro Shari'ah Supervisory Board** with legally defined functions and qualifications; an **institutional mechanism** for the Bangsamoro Government's participation in AAIIBP (per Art. XII, Sec. 33); a regulatory framework for **Shari'ah-compliant financial institutions** that fall outside BSP's banking supervision (e.g., Islamic microfinance, *takaful* mutual insurance, Shari'ah-compliant cooperative financing). BAA 13 creates the coordinating ministry but not the **Islamic finance regulatory ecosystem** the BOL envisions.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. **BTA Resolution No. 24** commended the passage of RA 11439 (Islamic Banking Act), signaling Parliament's interest in Islamic banking development. **Resolution No. 470** congratulated the new CEO of AAIIBP, demonstrating engagement with the bank's leadership. **Resolution No. 200** encouraged adoption of the Bangsamoro Halal Ecosystem Program, indicating Parliament's commitment to the broader Shari'ah-compliant economic framework.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. MFBM is the **correct lead** for Islamic banking and finance — its BAA 13 mandate covers fiscal and monetary policy coordination, financial institution policy review, and GOCC coordination (including AAIIBP). However, the power's full scope requires **multiple institutions**: the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' for Shari'ah compliance; BBOI for investment promotion; and the yet-to-be-established Bangsamoro Shari'ah Supervisory Board for banking product certification. MFBM is the policy coordinator, not the sole implementer.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Three institutions play roles:
   - **MFBM** (BAA 13, Bk. VI, Tit. IV; BAA 85, Ch. III) — lead ministry for fiscal and monetary policy coordination. Operational.
   - **Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta'** (BAA 13, Bk. V, Tit. II, Ch. 3, Sec. 52-55) — Shari'ah advisory body with *fatwa*-issuing authority. Operational. Attached to the Office of the Chief Minister.
   - **Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI)** (BAA 13, Bk. V, Tit. II, Ch. 2, Sec. 21-29) — promotes investment in BARMM. The Board of Governors includes the Minister of Finance, Budget and Management as *ex officio* member (Sec. 28). Operational.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Two BOL-envisioned institutions do not yet exist in Bangsamoro law:
   (1) **Bangsamoro Shari'ah Supervisory Board** — Art. XIII, Sec. 34 defines its functions and qualifications, but Art. XIII, Sec. 32 assigns BSP the authority to determine its organizational structure. This creates ambiguity: is this a BSP body or a Bangsamoro body? The BOL text suggests it operates under BSP's structural authority but serves the Bangsamoro Islamic banking system. No BAA has established it.
   (2) **AAIIBP Participation Mechanism** — Art. XII, Sec. 33 mandates the IFPB to determine the Bangsamoro Government's participation in AAIIBP within six months of BTA establishment (2019). The timeline has lapsed without a formalized mechanism. [UNVERIFIED: whether the IFPB has formally made this determination.]

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Yes. Full exercise of this power would benefit from: (1) a **Bangsamoro Islamic Finance Authority or Unit** within MFBM — dedicated to Islamic finance policy development, market analysis, and coordination with BSP; (2) an **Islamic Microfinance Regulatory Framework** — microfinance institutions and cooperatives offering Shari'ah-compliant products may fall outside BSP's banking supervision, requiring a Bangsamoro-level regulatory body; (3) a ***Takaful*** **(Islamic Insurance) Framework** — mutual insurance based on Shari'ah principles, which is not banking and therefore falls outside RA 11439's scope.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **BSP** exercises full supervisory authority over all banking operations in BARMM — licensing, prudential regulation, examination, and enforcement. BSP's Mindanao-based regional offices cover BARMM. **AAIIBP** operates as the only government-owned Islamic bank, headquartered in Zamboanga City with branches serving Muslim Mindanao. The **Department of Finance** coordinates national fiscal policy affecting Islamic finance. The **NCMF** (National Commission on Muslim Filipinos) has a BOL-mandated role in jointly promoting Islamic banking (Art. XIII, Sec. 32).[^3]

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for Islamic banking?**

A. BSP has issued implementing rules and regulations for RA 11439, enabling conventional banks to establish Islamic banking units. AAIIBP offers Islamic banking products (savings accounts, investment accounts, trade financing) through its Mindanao branch network. BSP has promoted financial inclusion programs that include Islamic banking awareness. However, [UNVERIFIED: the current number and location of AAIIBP branches actively serving BARMM communities, and the number of conventional banks that have established Islamic banking units within BARMM].

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. **Devolution is structurally limited.** Banking regulation **cannot be devolved** — BSP's supervisory authority is constitutionally mandated (Art. XII, Sec. 20 of the 1987 Constitution) and confirmed by the BOL (Art. XIII, Sec. 32). What can be developed at the Bangsamoro level is: (1) **tax policy** — Parliament can enact tax incentives and tax neutrality for Islamic finance; (2) **market development** — the Bangsamoro Government can promote Islamic finance products; (3) **Shari'ah governance** — the Bangsamoro Government can operationalize the Shari'ah Supervisory Board's Bangsamoro-side functions; (4) **AAIIBP participation** — through the IFPB determination mandated by Art. XII, Sec. 33.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. BARMM has accomplished: (1) **institutional establishment** — MFBM, Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta', and BBOI are all operational under BAA 13; (2) **legislative engagement** — Parliament passed Resolution No. 24 commending RA 11439 and Resolution No. 470 engaging with AAIIBP leadership; (3) **pending legislation** — PB No. 383 (Bangsamoro Social Equity Investment Fund with Shari'ah-compliant governance) and PB No. 337 (Bangsamoro Waqf Act) signal legislative intent to build the Islamic finance ecosystem. However, **no standalone Islamic banking legislation** has been enacted, and the two express BOL legislative mandates (Art. XIII, Sec. 32 and Art. XIII, Sec. 34) remain unfulfilled.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has two potential advantages:

   (1) **Shari'ah governance infrastructure** — the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' provides an institutional mechanism for Shari'ah compliance advisory that BSP does not have nationally. The Darul-Ifta' can issue *fatwas* on financial products and practices, providing authoritative Islamic jurisprudential guidance that a national-level Shari'ah advisory council may lack.

   (2) **Population demand** — BARMM has the highest concentration of Muslim population in the Philippines, creating the **strongest market demand** for Islamic banking products. This demographic reality means BARMM is the natural center for Islamic finance development in the country.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of both BOL-mandated legislative acts:

   - Without **tax incentives and tax neutrality legislation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 32), Islamic finance transactions in BARMM are taxed under conventional frameworks — potentially subjecting *murabaha* sale transactions to double taxation (VAT on the purchase and VAT on the resale), or treating *ijarah* lease payments differently from conventional loan interest deductions. This makes Islamic finance **structurally more expensive** than conventional finance within BARMM.
   - Without a **Bangsamoro Islamic banking and finance framework** (Art. XIII, Sec. 34), there is no Bangsamoro-level institutional architecture for Shari'ah governance, market development, or coordination with BSP.
   - The **IFPB determination on AAIIBP participation** (Art. XII, Sec. 33) has not been formalized, leaving the Bangsamoro Government without a clear role in the only government-owned Islamic bank.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 11439 provides a comprehensive Islamic banking framework with product definitions, licensing standards, capital requirements, and Shari'ah advisory council requirements. This law is only from 2019 — relatively new, but it provides the essential structure.
   - **Institutional framework**: BSP has issued implementing rules and regulations for RA 11439. BSP's regulatory framework covers Islamic bank licensing, capital adequacy, risk management, and examination.
   - **Existing Islamic bank**: AAIIBP has operated since 1990 (RA 6848), providing over three decades of Islamic banking experience — though its operations have been constrained by limited capitalization and branch network.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework falls short in three areas:

   (1) **No tax neutrality**: RA 11439 does not address the tax treatment of Islamic finance transactions. *Murabaha* transactions involve a **sale and resale** structure that can trigger double taxation (documentary stamp tax on each sale, VAT on the markup) compared to a simple loan that is exempt from VAT. *Ijarah* leases may not qualify for the same tax deductions as conventional mortgage interest. Without tax neutrality legislation — which the BOL assigns to the Bangsamoro Parliament — Islamic finance remains structurally disadvantaged.

   (2) **Limited branch presence**: AAIIBP's branch network does not adequately cover BARMM's geographically dispersed, often conflict-affected communities. Conventional banks with Islamic windows are concentrated in urban areas.

   (3) **Insufficient Shari'ah governance**: RA 11439 requires each Islamic bank to constitute its own Shari'ah advisory council (Sec. 5) but does not mandate a **centralized Shari'ah Supervisory Board** — it merely states that ***"Nothing contained herein precludes the establishment of a centralized Shariah Supervisory Board."*** The BOL (Art. XIII, Sec. 34) envisions a centralized board that has not been established.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: BSP prudential standards (capital adequacy, risk management, examination) — these are technically sound and constitutionally mandated. Islamic banking product definitions from RA 11439 (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*, *murabaha*, *ijarah*, *istisnaa*, *sukuk*) — adopt these directly into Bangsamoro legislation.
   - **Adapt**: Shari'ah advisory council requirements from RA 11439 Sec. 5 — adapt into a **centralized Bangsamoro Shari'ah Supervisory Board** with authority over all Islamic financial products and services within BARMM, potentially housed under the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' or MFBM. Adapt AAIIBP's structure to ensure Bangsamoro participation per Art. XII, Sec. 33.
   - **Replace**: The **tax framework** — build an entirely distinct tax incentive and tax neutrality regime for Islamic finance transactions within BARMM, since the national framework provides none. Build a **non-bank Islamic finance framework** (Islamic microfinance, *takaful*, *waqf* management) that RA 11439 does not cover.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes — this power has the **most intensive Shari'ah dimension** of all 55 powers. Islamic banking IS Shari'ah-based finance. The entire subject is grounded in Islamic jurisprudence: the prohibition of *riba* (interest), prohibition of *gharar* (excessive uncertainty), prohibition of *maysir* (gambling/speculation), and the requirement that all financial transactions be linked to real economic activity through permissible contracts (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*, *murabaha*, *ijarah*, *istisnaa*, *sukuk*). Unlike other powers where Shari'ah is an overlay or optional dimension, for Islamic banking Shari'ah compliance is the **definitional characteristic** — without it, there is no Islamic banking.[^11]

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL directly integrates Shari'ah throughout Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34: Sec. 31 requires financial institutions to operate ***"in accordance with the principles of the Islamic banking system"***; Sec. 32 mandates establishment of ***"Shari'ah-compliant financial institutions"*** and a ***"Shari'ah Supervisory Board"***; Sec. 34 specifies the Board's function as ***"monitoring the compliance of Shari'ah rules in banking and finance transactions and issuance of Shari'ah products"*** and its authority to ***"issue fatwas regarding the products and practices employed by banks and other institutions."***[^2][^3][^5]

   Additionally, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact laws on Shari'ah matters including commercial law — ***"The Parliament has the power to enact laws governing commercial and other civil actions not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083, as amended."*** Since Islamic banking is commercial in nature and PD 1083 does not cover it, Parliament has express authority for Shari'ah-based banking legislation.[^17]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Seven Islamic legal principles directly govern Islamic banking:

   (1) **Prohibition of *riba*** — the foundational principle. All predetermined returns on money lent are prohibited.
   (2) **Prohibition of *gharar*** — excessive uncertainty in contract terms is prohibited, driving higher transparency and disclosure standards.
   (3) **Prohibition of *maysir*** — speculation and gambling are prohibited, requiring financial products to be asset-backed.
   (4) **Permissible contracts** — *mudarabah*, *musharakah*, *murabaha*, *ijarah*, *istisnaa*, *sukuk* — these are the building blocks of Islamic finance.
   (5) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — financial institutions managing depositor and investor funds are *amin* (trustees), subject to the highest fiduciary standards.
   (6) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — Islamic banking serves financial inclusion and economic development of the Muslim population.
   (7) ***Zakat*** (obligatory alms) — RA 6848, Sec. 3 provides that AAIIBP shall ***"appropriate said zakat fund and to disburse it in legitimate channels."*** Islamic banks have an obligation to manage *zakat* on behalf of shareholders and depositors.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **RA 11439** (Islamic Banking Act) is itself Shari'ah-based legislation — it defines Islamic banking as ***"banking business with objectives and operations that do not involve interest (riba) as prohibited by the Shariah and which conducts its business transactions in accordance with Shariah principles"*** (Sec. 2(a)(4)). **RA 6848** requires AAIIBP to operate under ***"the basic principles and rulings of Islamic Shari'a"*** (Sec. 3) and creates a Shari'ah Advisory Council (Sec. 4-5). **PD 1083** does not cover banking but establishes the legal recognition of Shari'ah in Philippine law. **BOL Art. X, Sec. 4** provides the parliamentary authority for further Shari'ah-based commercial legislation.[^11][^12][^17]

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro Islamic banking legislation?**

A. Shari'ah compliance should be **mandatory for all institutions operating as Islamic banks or offering Islamic financial products within BARMM** — this is inherent in the definition of Islamic banking. The recommended institutional mechanism is a **centralized Bangsamoro Shari'ah Supervisory Board** (as envisioned by Art. XIII, Sec. 34) that: (a) certifies Islamic financial products as Shari'ah-compliant before they are offered in BARMM; (b) conducts periodic Shari'ah audits of Islamic banks and Islamic banking units; (c) issues *fatwas* on novel financial products and practices; (d) coordinates with BSP's national-level regulatory framework. This Board should draw on the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta''s *fatwa*-issuing infrastructure while maintaining the specialized banking expertise required by Art. XIII, Sec. 34(b).

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM's divergence authority is **more constrained** for Islamic banking than for most other powers, because of the unique combination of constraints established in **Themes A** and **B**:

   - **BSP supervisory supremacy** (established in Section A (BOL Grant and Scope)) — the Bangsamoro Government cannot create a parallel banking regulator, independently license banks, or override BSP prudential standards. This is constitutionally grounded (Section B (Constitutional Considerations), Art. XII, Sec. 20).
   - **Intergovernmental joint mandate** (established in Section A (BOL Grant and Scope)) — Islamic banking development requires collaboration with BSP, DOF, and NCMF.

   However, within these constraints, BARMM has **significant legislative space**:
   - **Tax policy**: the BOL expressly mandates Parliament to enact tax incentives and tax neutrality — this is an area where BARMM can create a framework **more favorable to Islamic finance** than the national system provides.
   - **Non-bank Islamic finance**: *takaful* (Islamic insurance), Islamic microfinance, *waqf* management, and Shari'ah-compliant cooperative financing fall **outside BSP's banking supervision** and are within BARMM's regulatory reach.
   - **Shari'ah governance**: BARMM can establish a Bangsamoro Shari'ah Supervisory Board with functions that go beyond what RA 11439 requires of individual bank advisory councils.
   - **Market development**: BARMM can promote Islamic finance products, conduct awareness campaigns, support capacity building, and create enabling conditions — all within its promotional mandate under Art. XIII, Sec. 31 and 32.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For banking customers within BARMM**: They would gain access to tax-neutral Islamic finance products and a stronger Shari'ah governance framework — making Islamic banking more accessible and cost-competitive with conventional banking.

   (2) **For Islamic banks operating in BARMM**: BSP standards apply uniformly nationwide — BARMM legislation cannot relax or override these. BARMM legislation can only **add** to the national framework (tax incentives, Shari'ah governance, non-bank Islamic finance) without contradicting it.

   (3) **For cross-boundary operations**: Islamic banks operating in both BARMM and non-BARMM areas would benefit from BARMM tax incentives only for transactions within BARMM. BSP prudential standards apply regardless of location.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. National banking law (**RA 11439**, **RA 7653**) continues to apply **directly** — these laws are constitutionally mandated and cannot be superseded by a BAA. A Bangsamoro Islamic Banking and Finance Act would operate **alongside** the national framework — adding tax incentives, Shari'ah governance, non-bank Islamic finance regulation, and market development programs that the national framework does not provide. The BAA fills gaps in the national framework rather than replacing it.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Three jurisdictional ambiguities exist:

   (1) **Shari'ah Supervisory Board**: Art. XIII, Sec. 32 assigns BSP authority over its organizational structure, while Art. XIII, Sec. 34 defines its functions in the context of the ***"Bangsamoro Islamic banking and finance framework."*** It is unclear whether the Board is a BSP body, a Bangsamoro body, or a joint body. Legislation must clarify.

   (2) **Non-bank Islamic financial institutions**: RA 11439 covers banks and banking units. Shari'ah-compliant microfinance institutions, *takaful* companies, *waqf* management entities, and Islamic cooperative financing programs may fall outside BSP's jurisdiction. The question is whether these are regulated by BSP, the Insurance Commission, the Cooperative Development Authority, or the Bangsamoro Government. A Bangsamoro Islamic Finance Act should delineate jurisdiction over non-bank Islamic financial services.

   (3) **AAIIBP participation**: Art. XII, Sec. 33 mandates IFPB determination of Bangsamoro Government participation. The modalities — board seat, equity interest, policy influence, revenue share — remain unspecified.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB No. 383** (Bangsamoro Social Equity Investment Fund) partially addresses the gap by creating a Shari'ah-compliant investment vehicle. **PB No. 337** (Bangsamoro Waqf Act) addresses the adjacent *waqf* management gap. **Neither bill** addresses the core legislative mandates: tax incentives, tax neutrality, or the comprehensive Bangsamoro Islamic banking and finance framework. These remain **entirely unaddressed** by pending legislation.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) identifies financial inclusion as essential for inclusive economic growth — Islamic banking directly addresses the financial exclusion of Muslim populations who avoid conventional interest-based banking. BDP Chapter 4 (Development Framework) positions financial access as a cross-cutting enabler for all six development goals. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, depends on accessible financial services — including Islamic banking — to expand the economic base and reduce dependence on informal lending and remittances.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without Islamic banking and finance legislation: **tax neutrality** cannot be achieved, meaning Shari'ah-compliant products remain structurally more expensive than conventional equivalents — deterring banks from offering Islamic windows in BARMM and deterring customers from using Islamic products. **Financial inclusion** of observant Muslims remains incomplete — those who refuse interest-based banking are excluded from formal financial services. **Infrastructure financing** through *sukuk* (Islamic bonds) cannot be developed at the Bangsamoro level without a tax framework that treats *sukuk* returns equivalently to conventional bond interest. The **AAIIBP relationship** remains undefined, preventing the Bangsamoro Government from leveraging the only government-owned Islamic bank. **Non-bank Islamic finance** (*takaful*, Islamic microfinance, *waqf* management) operates without a regulatory framework, increasing risk for both providers and consumers. The two express BOL legislative mandates (Art. XIII, Sec. 32 and 34) remain unfulfilled through the transition period.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What makes this power unique among the 55 powers — and why is it the BOL's most detailed economic framework?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 31-34 devotes **four consecutive sections** to Islamic banking and finance — more than any other single economic power. This reflects three realities: (1) Islamic banking requires a **complex institutional architecture** (Shari'ah Supervisory Board, BSP coordination, AAIIBP participation, legislative framework) that cannot be addressed in a single provision; (2) **BSP's constitutionally mandated supervisory role** creates intergovernmental complexity that must be explicitly addressed; (3) the **Bangsamoro peace process** recognized financial inclusion through Shari'ah-compliant banking as a core commitment — the BOL's detailed treatment reflects the political importance of Islamic finance to the Bangsamoro self-governance agenda.

**Q. How does BSP's supervisory supremacy limit the Bangsamoro Government's authority — and where are the boundaries?**

A. BSP's authority covers: **licensing** (approving establishment of Islamic banks and Islamic banking units); **prudential regulation** (capital adequacy, risk management, governance standards); **examination** (periodic inspection of bank operations); and **enforcement** (sanctions, suspension, receivership). The Bangsamoro Government **cannot** independently license a bank, set capital requirements, or examine a bank's operations.

   The Bangsamoro Government **can**: (1) enact **tax policy** affecting Islamic finance (incentives, neutrality); (2) promote **market development** (awareness, capacity building, investor attraction); (3) regulate **non-bank Islamic financial services** (*takaful*, Islamic microfinance, *waqf*); (4) operationalize the **Shari'ah Supervisory Board** for Bangsamoro-side Shari'ah governance (in coordination with BSP per Art. XIII, Sec. 32); (5) exercise its **AAIIBP participation rights** per Art. XII, Sec. 33.

**Q. What is tax neutrality and why does the BOL specifically mandate it?**

A. **Tax neutrality** means that Islamic finance transactions should not be taxed more heavily than economically equivalent conventional transactions. Because Islamic finance uses **sale**, **lease**, and **partnership** structures instead of **loan** structures, the tax consequences can differ. For example: a conventional home loan involves a bank lending money at interest — the interest is tax-deductible for the borrower and subject to income tax for the bank. An Islamic *murabaha* home financing involves the bank buying the property and reselling it at a markup — this can trigger **documentary stamp tax on both transfers** (bank purchase and resale to customer), **VAT on the markup**, and loss of the interest deduction benefit. Without tax neutrality, the *murabaha* structure costs more in taxes than the conventional loan — making Islamic banking uncompetitive.

   The BOL mandates Parliament to enact laws ensuring ***"tax neutrality of Islamic finance transactions"*** (Art. XIII, Sec. 32) precisely because this structural disadvantage would undermine the entire Islamic banking system in BARMM.

**Q. What is the relationship between the Bangsamoro Government and AAIIBP?**

A. The relationship operates at three levels: (1) **BOL mandate** — Art. XII, Sec. 33 requires the IFPB to determine the Bangsamoro Government's participation in AAIIBP within six months of BTA establishment. Art. XII, Sec. 37(e) lists this as an IFPB fiscal function. (2) **National law** — AAIIBP was created by RA 6848 as a government-owned Islamic bank, headquartered in Zamboanga City, with a Shari'ah Advisory Council. (3) **Parliamentary engagement** — Resolution No. 470 formally engaged with AAIIBP's CEO appointment. The relationship is currently **undefined in practice** — the IFPB determination timeline (six months from 2019) has lapsed, and no formal mechanism for Bangsamoro Government participation (board representation, equity interest, policy input) has been established. [UNVERIFIED: whether the IFPB has informally or partially addressed this determination.]

**Q. How does this power interact with the halal ecosystem and waqf management?**

A. Islamic banking is part of a broader **Shari'ah-compliant economic ecosystem** that includes: (1) ***halal*** **accreditation and regulation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 30) — *halal*-certified businesses are natural customers for Islamic banking products; (2) ***waqf*** **management** — endowment properties and their income can be managed through Islamic financial instruments; PB No. 337 would create the Bangsamoro Waqf Commission; (3) **cooperative financing** — power (k) cooperatives can operate through Shari'ah-compliant financing models (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*) as detailed in the cooperatives legal reference. A comprehensive Bangsamoro Islamic banking and finance framework should integrate with, or at minimum coordinate with, legislation on *halal*, *waqf*, and Shari'ah-compliant cooperatives.
