# (j) Classification of Public Lands

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(j)** — ***"Classification of public lands;"***

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over classification of public lands?**

A. Article V, Section 2(j) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"classification of public lands"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** However, this power is among the most heavily qualified in the BOL. Art. XIII, Sec. 17 (para. 1) limits the Bangsamoro Government's role to **recommendation**: ***"The classification of public lands in the Bangsamoro into alienable and disposable lands shall be recommended by the Bangsamoro Government to the President for the timely implementation of Bangsamoro development plans and targets."*** The President retains **final approval authority**.[^1][^2]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Four provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 17 (para. 1) — the **core implementing provision** establishing the recommendatory framework for land classification, quoted above.[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 17 (para. 2) — grants the related **cadastral survey** power: ***"The Bangsamoro Government may, upon authority of the President, conduct cadastral surveys, lot surveys, and isolated and special surveys in the Bangsamoro."*** Surveys inform classification decisions by generating the physical data needed to determine land characteristics.[^3]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 8 — the broader **natural resources** power: ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution, the Bangsamoro Government shall have the power, authority, and right to explore, develop, and utilize the natural resources."*** Land classification determines which lands are available for exploration and development.[^4]

   (4) Art. XIII, Sec. 8 (para. 3) — the **nature reserves** power: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall also have the power to declare nature reserves and aquatic parks, forests, watershed reservations, and other protected areas in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** Reserved lands are withdrawn from classification as alienable and disposable.[^5]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. Unlike Art. XIII, Sec. 24 (which names a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"***), the BOL does not mandate the Parliament to enact a specific public land classification act. The power is exercised through the recommendatory mechanism in Art. XIII, Sec. 17. However, nothing prevents the Parliament from enacting legislation to systematize the classification process, establish criteria, and define MENRE's role in preparing recommendations.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not create a dedicated land classification body. The function falls within MENRE's general mandate under BAA 13. The **Land Management Services** (BAA 13, Sec. 9(7)) — with its **Surveys Division** and **Land Utilization Development Division** — is the organizational unit most directly responsible.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three layers of limitations apply:

   (1) **Presidential approval**: Art. XIII, Sec. 17 — classification ***"shall be recommended"*** to the President. The Bangsamoro Government cannot unilaterally classify public lands.[^2]

   (2) **Constitutional subjection**: Art. XIII, Sec. 8 — natural resources powers are ***"subject to the provisions of the Constitution."*** Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."***[^4][^6]

   (3) **National coordination**: Art. XIII, Sec. 17 (para. 2) — survey results must be furnished to ***"relevant national government agencies to effect inclusion into the National Cadastral Survey."***[^3]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to classification of public lands?**

A. Five categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Regalian Doctrine (Art. XII, Sec. 2)**: ***"All lands of the public domain, waters, minerals, coal, petroleum, and other mineral oils, all forces of potential energy, fisheries, forests or timber, wildlife, flora and fauna, and other natural resources are owned by the State."*** This is the foundational principle — all unclassified lands belong to the State.[^7]

   (2) **Four-Category Classification (Art. XII, Sec. 3)**: ***"Lands of the public domain are classified into agricultural, forest or timber, mineral lands, and national parks."*** The Bangsamoro Government **cannot create new categories** — all classification recommendations must fall within these four constitutionally prescribed categories. Only ***"agricultural lands"*** may be alienated.[^8]

   (3) **Forest Land Protection (Art. XII, Sec. 4)**: ***"The Congress shall, as soon as possible, determine by law the specific limits of forest lands and national parks, marking clearly their boundaries on the ground. Thereafter, such forest lands and national parks shall be conserved and may not be increased nor diminished, except by law."*** Forest and national park boundaries require **Congressional action** to change.[^9]

   (4) **Indigenous Peoples' Rights (Art. XII, Sec. 5)**: ***"The State...shall protect the rights of indigenous cultural communities to their ancestral lands."*** Ancestral domains recognized under RA 8371 are removed from the public domain — they cannot be classified or disposed of as public lands.[^10]

   (5) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***[^6]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees: (a) **state ownership** of all public domain lands (Regalian doctrine); (b) **inalienability** of forest, mineral, and national park lands — only agricultural lands may be alienated; (c) **protection of ancestral domains** of indigenous cultural communities; (d) **due process** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — reclassification affecting private rights must satisfy procedural and substantive due process.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for public land classification?**

A. The national framework centers on **Commonwealth Act No. 141** (Public Land Act), as amended, which establishes: (a) the classification system (agricultural, forest, mineral, national parks); (b) disposition modes for alienable lands (homestead, sale, lease, free patent); (c) the President's authority to classify and reclassify; and (d) survey requirements. **PD 705** (Revised Forestry Code) governs forest land classification and prohibits alienation of forest lands. **RA 7586** and **RA 11038** (NIPAS and Expanded NIPAS) establish protected area classifications that withdraw lands from disposition. **RA 7942** (Mining Act) governs mineral land classification. **RA 8371** (IPRA) removes ancestral domains from the public domain entirely.[^11][^12][^13][^14][^15]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national land classification laws apply **directly**:
   (1) **CA 141** — the Public Land Act governs all public land classification and disposition in BARMM.[^11]
   (2) **PD 705** — the Forestry Code prohibits alienation of forest lands in BARMM.[^12]
   (3) **RA 7586 / RA 11038** — NIPAS Act governs protected areas within BARMM.[^13]
   (4) **RA 7942** — the Mining Act governs mineral land classification.[^14]
   (5) **RA 8371** — IPRA protects ancestral domains including those in BARMM.[^15]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. **DENR** retains primary authority over land classification within BARMM through its regional, provincial (PENRO), and community (CENRO) offices. DENR processes land classification applications, conducts verifications, and prepares recommendations for Presidential approval. The **Land Management Bureau** under DENR handles survey and title functions. **NAMRIA** maintains the national mapping and resource information system used in land classification.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Title III, Chapter 1 defines MENRE's mandate; BAA 85, Chapter XIII allocates its operational budget.[^16][^17]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for classification of public lands. MENRE exercises related functions through its BAA 13 general mandate.

**Q. What are MENRE's mandate and functions under BAA 13 relevant to land classification?**

A. BAA 13, Title III, Chapter 1 defines MENRE comprehensively:

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): ***"The Ministry shall be primarily responsible for the exploration, utilization, management, conservation, protection and sustainable development of the region's environment, natural resources and energy sources."***[^16]

   **Powers and functions** (Sec. 3) — seven functions directly relevant to land classification:

   - (d) ***"Exercise supervision and control over forest lands, agricultural lands, mineral resources, and national parks within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region;"***
   - (f) ***"Undertake exploration, assessment, classification and inventory of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region's environment, natural resources, and energy;"***
   - (j)(1) Promulgate rules to ***"accelerate cadastral surveys, land use planning and public land titling;"***
   - (j)(4) ***"Expedite mineral resources surveys, promote the production of metallic and non-metallic minerals and encourage mineral marketing;"***
   - (k) ***"Regulate the development, disposition, exploration and utilization of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region's forests, land, mineral and energy resources, as defined by subsequent laws to be enacted by Parliament;"***
   - (l) ***"Exercise administration and supervision of all lands of the public domain within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region;"***
   - (i)(1) Establish programs for ***"accelerated inventory, surveys and classification of the region's environment, natural resources and energy."***[^18]

   **Organizational structure** (Sec. 9): MENRE's **Land Management Services** (headed by Director II) includes: (a) **Surveys Division**; and (b) **Land Utilization Development Division** — these are the units directly responsible for land classification surveys and recommendations.[^19]

**Q. What can MENRE NOT do without a standalone BAA?**

A. MENRE can: conduct surveys, classify and inventory lands, prepare recommendations to the President. MENRE **cannot**: (a) **unilaterally classify lands** — Presidential approval is constitutionally and BOL-mandated; (b) establish a **systematic regional classification framework** with criteria, procedures, and timelines specific to Bangsamoro development needs; (c) create a **streamlined approval process** with the Office of the President — this requires an intergovernmental mechanism; (d) define **regional land use categories** more granular than the four constitutional categories.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No enacted resolution directly addresses land classification legislation. **Res. 399** (directing MENRE to intensify reforestation) relates indirectly to forest land classification.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. MENRE's BAA 13 mandate explicitly covers ***"classification and inventory"*** (Sec. 3(f)), ***"supervision and control over forest lands, agricultural lands, mineral resources, and national parks"*** (Sec. 3(d)), and ***"administration and supervision of all lands of the public domain"*** (Sec. 3(l)). The Land Management Services with its Surveys Division and Land Utilization Development Division provide the organizational capacity.[^18][^19]

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. - **Lead ministry**: MENRE (BAA 13, Title III; BAA 85, Chapter XIII)
   - **Organizational unit**: Land Management Services — Surveys Division + Land Utilization Development Division (BAA 13, Sec. 9(7))
   - **Supporting body**: Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31) — policy guidance on natural resource management
   - **Field offices**: Provincial Environment, Natural Resources and Energy Offices (PENREOs) and Community Environment, Natural Resources and Energy Offices (CENREOs) (BAA 13, Sec. 10)

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not mandate creation of a specific land classification institution. The existing MENRE structure under BAA 13 satisfies the institutional requirement.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **DENR** retains the primary operational role through its BARMM regional office, PENROs, and CENROs. DENR: (a) processes applications for land classification and reclassification; (b) conducts ground verification and surveys; (c) prepares recommendations for the Secretary of Environment and Natural Resources, who recommends to the President; (d) issues certifications of alienable and disposable status. The **President** retains final approval authority — land classification is a Presidential prerogative delegated to DENR for processing but not for final decision.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **incomplete**. While MENRE has been established with a land management mandate, the **Presidential approval requirement** in Art. XIII, Sec. 17 means this power can never be fully devolved to the Bangsamoro Government — it is structurally a **joint exercise** between the Bangsamoro Government (recommendation) and the National Government (Presidential approval). What can be devolved is the **technical and preparatory functions** — surveys, inventories, criteria application — which BAA 13 already assigns to MENRE.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MENRE has been **institutionally established** with a Land Management Services unit. It exercises supervision over public lands within the region and conducts surveys. However, the **classification recommendation process** to the President has not been systematized through a standing coordination mechanism. No known batch of land classification recommendations has been formally transmitted to the President under the BOL framework.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has a **contextual advantage**: MENRE understands the region's unique land tenure challenges — including conflict-displaced communities, ancestral domain claims overlapping with public lands, and development needs of formerly conflict-affected areas. MENRE can prepare classification recommendations calibrated to Bangsamoro development plans (the stated purpose in Art. XIII, Sec. 17) rather than applying generic national criteria.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **primary constraint** is the **Presidential approval requirement** — this is a structural constitutional feature, not a gap that legislation can fill. The **secondary constraint** is the absence of a **standing coordination mechanism** with DENR and the Office of the President for processing classification recommendations expeditiously.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. DENR has: (a) decades of **land classification data** and **cadastral survey records** for BARMM territories; (b) established **procedures and criteria** for classification and reclassification; (c) a **trained workforce** of land management officers, surveyors, and geodetic engineers; (d) **digital mapping systems** and integration with NAMRIA's national geodetic network.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national system: (a) does not **prioritize BARMM lands** for classification — historically, land classification in BARMM has been slow compared to other regions; (b) does not account for **conflict-related land tenure disruptions** — populations displaced by armed conflict may have lost documentation; (c) has a **cumbersome approval process** — recommendations must travel from CENRO to PENRO to DENR Regional Office to DENR Central Office to the Office of the President.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. The Shari'ah dimension is **indirect but principled**. Land classification is fundamentally a technical and administrative function. However, three Islamic legal principles inform how the Bangsamoro Government should exercise this power:

   (1) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — public lands are held in trust for the community. Classification decisions that irreversibly convert forest lands to alienable status must be made with inter-generational stewardship in mind [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

   (2) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — reclassification that enables agricultural settlement and livelihood for conflict-affected communities serves the public interest. Classification decisions should balance development needs against environmental conservation [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

   (3) ***Hima*** (protected zone/conservation) — the classical Islamic concept of the leader reserving public lands for communal use supports maintaining forest and protected area classifications [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law].

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL is **silent** on Shari'ah in relation to land classification. Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah laws on property matters, but land classification is a **public domain regulatory function** distinct from private property rights governed by Shari'ah personal law.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. The recommended approach is **principled** rather than mandatory. Shari'ah values of stewardship (*amanah*), public interest (*maslahah*), and conservation (*hima*) should inform the criteria used in land classification recommendations — but compliance with specific Shari'ah rules is not required since this is a public regulatory function rather than a private commercial or personal transaction.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM's ability to diverge is **severely limited** by two factors established in Themes A and C: (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 17 restricts the Bangsamoro Government to **recommendation** — the President decides; and (2) Art. XII, Sec. 3 of the Constitution prescribes the **four immutable categories** of public lands. BARMM cannot: create new land categories, alienate forest lands, or classify lands without Presidential approval. BARMM **can**: (a) establish its own **criteria and procedures** for preparing classification recommendations; (b) set **regional development-informed priorities** for which lands to recommend for classification; (c) develop a **streamlined coordination mechanism** with the national government.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. A Bangsamoro Public Land Classification Act would govern the **recommendation process** — the criteria, procedures, institutional mechanisms, and priorities for preparing recommendations to the President. National law (CA 141, PD 705, RA 7586) would continue to apply for the **Presidential approval** process and the **disposition** of classified lands. The BAA would supplement, not replace, the national framework.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between **MENRE** and **DENR**. BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 17 gives the Bangsamoro Government the power to recommend classification, but DENR continues to exercise operational classification functions through its BARMM offices. No intergovernmental mechanism defines: (a) whether MENRE or DENR prepares the initial classification recommendation; (b) how MENRE recommendations reach the President — through DENR channels or directly; (c) the division of survey responsibilities between MENRE's Land Management Services and DENR's land management offices.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **No pending Parliament Bill** directly addresses land classification. Related bills — PB 310 (Liguasan Marsh conservation), PB 352 (Lake Lanao management), PB 362 (greening program), PB 385 (water sustainability) — address specific natural resource issues but do not establish a comprehensive land classification framework.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 12** (Environment and Natural Resources) targets land administration reform, tenure security, and sustainable resource management — all dependent on systematic land classification. **BDP Chapter 3** (Spatial Development Strategy) establishes a land use framework whose implementation requires knowing which public lands are alienable and disposable. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda**, Pillar 4 (Environmental Sustainability) depends on proper classification to balance development with conservation.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a systematic classification framework: (a) **agricultural development** is constrained — communities cannot obtain title to lands that remain unclassified as alienable; (b) the **spatial development strategy** lacks its foundational data layer; (c) **settlement and housing programs** cannot proceed on unclassified public lands; (d) **conflict-affected communities** seeking land tenure security face bureaucratic barriers without a streamlined classification process; (e) **investment attraction** is impeded — investors require certainty about land status.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why is this power structured as a recommendation rather than a direct grant?**

A. The **Regalian doctrine** — constitutionally embedded in Art. XII, Sec. 2 — vests ownership of all public domain lands in the State. Classification of public lands is a **sovereign function** that determines which State-owned lands can be alienated. The 1987 Constitution places this power within the executive and legislative branches of the national government (Art. XII, Sec. 3-4). The BOL, as a congressional act, cannot transfer full classification authority to the Bangsamoro Government without a constitutional amendment. Art. XIII, Sec. 17 represents the **maximum delegation** possible under the constitutional framework — granting the Bangsamoro Government the power to initiate and recommend, while the President retains final approval.

**Q. How does the interplay between powers (j), (h), and (bbb) work?**

A. Three Art. V, Sec. 2 powers are addressed in Art. XIII, Sec. 17:
   - **(j) Classification of public lands** — the subject of this legal reference
   - **(h) Cadastral land survey** — the data-gathering function that informs classification
   - **(bbb) Urban land reform and land use** — the planning function that depends on classification outcomes

   The sequence is: **survey (h)** produces physical data → **classification (j)** determines which lands are alienable and disposable → **land use planning (bbb)** determines what uses those lands may be put to. All three require Presidential authorization (surveys require Presidential authority, classification requires Presidential approval, urban land reform is subject to the Constitution).

**Q. What is the relationship between land classification and ancestral domain recognition?**

A. Ancestral domains recognized under **RA 8371** (IPRA) and **BAA 64** (Indigenous Peoples' Act) are **removed from the public domain**. Once a Certificate of Ancestral Domain Title (CADT) or Certificate of Ancestral Land Title (CALT) is issued, the land is no longer classifiable as public land — it is recognized as **private land** held under native title (*fusaka inged*, per BOL Art. IX, Sec. 3). The land classification framework applies only to **remaining public domain lands** not covered by ancestral domain claims. The overlap between unresolved ancestral domain claims and public land classification is a significant practical challenge in BARMM.
