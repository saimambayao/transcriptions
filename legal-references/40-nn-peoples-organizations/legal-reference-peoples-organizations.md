## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over people's organizations through Article V, Section 2(nn). The primary elaborating provision is Article IV, Section 3, which establishes a "democratic political system" with "free participation in political processes." While the BOL's provisions on people's organizations are relatively sparse compared to other powers, the grant of authority encompasses the registration, regulation, support, and empowerment of civil society organizations, community-based organizations, non-governmental organizations, and other forms of organized civic participation within the Bangsamoro.[^1]

The power connects to several other BOL provisions that envision people's participation in governance. Article XII, Section 40 mandates a "full disclosure policy" for budget and finances, including "the participation of representatives from civil society in the budget process." Article XIII, Section 2 commits the Bangsamoro Government to "strengthen governance systems to ensure people's participation." Article XIII, Section 5 establishes consultation mechanisms with women and local communities — a function that requires organized civil society partners to be effective. The Council of Leaders under Article VI, Section 9 includes "representatives of other sectors," providing a formal channel for people's organizations to participate in governance advice to the Chief Minister.[^2]

This power also intersects with the Bangsamoro's commitment to democratic governance and transparency. People's organizations serve as intermediaries between government and citizens, performing functions including service delivery, community mobilization, policy advocacy, budget monitoring, and accountability oversight. The BOL's overall vision of participatory governance depends on a healthy, organized civil society — making this power a foundational enabler for democratic governance in the Bangsamoro.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

No standalone BAA exists for people's organizations. The power is exercised through the general mandates of various government bodies under BAA No. 13 (Administrative Code). The Office of the Chief Minister coordinates civil society engagement through executive initiatives and policy directives. MILG interfaces with people's organizations at the LGU level through BAA No. 49 (Local Governance Code), which provides for citizen participation mechanisms in local governance including local development councils and local special bodies where civil society organizations participate.[^3]

Several proposed bills signal Parliament's intent to legislate in this area. PB No. 319 would establish a legal framework for CSO registration and regulation within the Bangsamoro. PB No. 399 would institutionalize CSO engagement and establish a Bangsamoro CSO Coordinating Office. PB No. 320 would institutionalize public petitions in the Bangsamoro Parliament, creating a direct mechanism for people's organizations to engage with the legislative process. PB No. 165 would provide for a system of initiative in the Bangsamoro, enabling organized citizen groups to directly propose legislation. None of these has been enacted.[^4]

The BTA Parliament's Rules of Procedure provide for some forms of civil society engagement through committee hearings and public consultations, but these are procedural rather than legislative mandates. The practical exercise of this power remains largely informal and executive-driven rather than legislatively structured.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not require this power to be exercised consistent with national law. At the national level, people's organizations and NGOs operate under a patchwork of laws: the Corporation Code of the Philippines (RA 11232) governs non-stock non-profit corporations, the Cooperative Code (RA 9520) governs cooperatives, and various sectoral laws regulate specific types of organizations. The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) handles national registration of non-profit corporations.

In the absence of a Bangsamoro CSO registration framework, people's organizations in BARMM continue to register through the SEC under national law. A Bangsamoro CSO registration act would create a parallel regional registration system — similar to how CSEA (BAA 13) handles cooperative registration within BARMM. The national framework applies suppletorily in its entirety because no BAA has been enacted in this area. RA 7160 (Local Government Code), through its provisions on accreditation of people's organizations and NGOs for participation in local governance, also applies in BARMM LGUs.[^5]

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

This is one of the most under-legislated powers in the BOL. No standalone BAA exists, and the entire area is governed by national law applied suppletorily and executive-level initiatives. The legislative gaps are substantial.

First and most critically, no Bangsamoro People's Organizations Act or CSO Framework Law exists. Such legislation could establish: a regional CSO registration system (either through MILG, a dedicated office, or the Bangsamoro CSO Coordinating Office proposed in PB No. 399); standards for CSO accreditation to participate in government consultations, budget processes, and local governance bodies; tax incentives or fiscal support mechanisms for CSOs operating in the Bangsamoro; legal protections for civic space, including protections for CSO workers and volunteers; and reporting and transparency requirements for CSOs receiving government or donor funds.[^6]

Second, the full disclosure policy mandated by Article XII, Section 40 — which specifically requires "participation of representatives from civil society in the budget process" — lacks implementing legislation that defines who qualifies, how they participate, and what legal weight their input carries. A Bangsamoro Budget Transparency and Civil Society Participation Act could operationalize this constitutional mandate.

Third, the mechanisms for people's initiative and public petition contemplated in PB No. 165 and PB No. 320 would give people's organizations direct tools for democratic participation beyond consultation. These represent advanced democratic governance mechanisms that the BOL's framework of participatory democracy supports but which require legislation to activate.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 5 (Governance and Institutional Development), identifies people's participation as a pillar of good governance and institutional strengthening. The BDP's governance reform agenda — transparency, accountability, and responsive institutions — depends on an active, organized civil society that can serve as both a partner and a watchdog. Without a CSO framework law, the BDP's participatory governance goals lack the institutional infrastructure needed for systematic implementation. The civil society participation mandate in budget processes (Art. XII, Sec. 40) directly connects to the BDP's plan financing strategy in Chapter 13.
