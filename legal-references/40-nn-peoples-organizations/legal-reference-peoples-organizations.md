# (nn) People's Organizations

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(nn)** — ***"People's organizations;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(nn) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement — Art. V, Sec. 2(nn) contains no "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier. However, Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — establishing a constitutional floor (see Section B (Constitutional Considerations)). Art. XII, Sec. 40 of the BOL requires ***"participation of representatives from civil society in the budget process"*** — signaling the centrality of people's organizations to governance, but without a national law conformity requirement. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG)** — BAA 13, Book IV, Title V (Sec. 1-27); BAA 85 budget allocation. MILG's field operations include providing ***"technical assistance to civil society organizations including non-governmental organizations, people's organizations, and the indigenous people's organizations in promoting a culture of good governance through people's participation"*** (BAA 13, Sec. 10(f)). |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD) — accreditation of social welfare agencies (BAA 13, Sec. 20); Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA) — local development planning coordination; Bangsamoro Economic and Development Council (BEDC) — 5 private sector and CSO representatives in membership (BAA 13); Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC); Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 1 (Extended Transition — inclusive and participatory governance); Chapter 10 (Peace and Security — civil society engagement); Chapter 13 (Plan Financing — CSOs as development partners) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None — the BOL does not name a specific "people's organizations code" or comparable legislation by title. However, BOL Art. XII, Sec. 40 mandates civil society participation in the budget process, and multiple BOL provisions contemplate legislation on consultation mechanisms (Art. V, Sec. 2(mm)) and sectoral representation. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over people's organizations?**

A. Article V, Section 2(nn) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"People's organizations"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** The formulation is terse — a two-word grant — but its scope is broad. It encompasses the **regulation**, **promotion**, **accreditation**, **capacity-building**, and **facilitation of participation** of people's organizations within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. Read together with the companion power in Art. V, Sec. 2(mm) — ***"Mechanisms for consultations for women and marginalized sectors"*** — power (nn) covers the **organizational dimension** (the POs themselves) while power (mm) covers the **procedural dimension** (how POs participate in governance).[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Five provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XII, Sec. 40 (Full Disclosure Policy) mandates: ***"The Bangsamoro Government adopts a policy of full disclosure of its budget, finances, bids and public offerings, and shall provide protocols for the guidance of local authorities in the implementation of said policy which shall include, among others, the posting of the Summary of Income and Expenditures, and the participation of representatives from civil society in the budget process."*** This is the most operationally specific BOL provision on PO engagement — it requires CSO participation in a particular government function (budgeting), not merely in general governance.[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 5 guarantees ***"full and direct participation of women in governance and development process"*** — women's organizations are a key subset of POs, and this provision supports their direct engagement in governance and development.[^3]

   (3) Art. VII, Sec. 5(c) guarantees that ***"Reserved seats and sectoral representatives shall constitute at least ten percent (10%) of the members of the Parliament"*** — POs serve as the institutional base through which sectoral nominees emerge for women, youth, traditional leaders, Ulama, IP, and settler community seats.[^4]

   (4) Art. IX, Sec. 11 mandates Parliament to enact a law ***"ensuring the representation of women in other decision-making and policy-determining bodies of the Bangsamoro Government"*** — women's POs are the primary vehicle for this representation.[^5]

   (5) Art. IX, Sec. 3(f) guarantees ***"Political participation in the Bangsamoro Government including reserved seats for the non-Moro indigenous peoples in the Parliament"*** — IP POs are the vehicles for exercising this right.[^6]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not name a specific "People's Organizations Act" or comparable legislation by title. Unlike power (k) (cooperatives — Art. XIII, Sec. 24 mandates a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"***), there is **no named legislative mandate** for people's organizations. However, the budget participation mandate in Art. XII, Sec. 40 and the women's participation guarantee in Art. XIII, Sec. 5 **contemplate implementing legislation** to give effect to these obligations. The absence of a named mandate means the legislative gap is less acute than for powers with express mandates — but it also means there is no automatic political urgency to legislate.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not expressly create a dedicated body for people's organizations. However, it mandates several **institutional mechanisms** through which POs participate: (1) the **Council of Leaders** (Art. VII) includes sectoral representatives and CSO nominees; (2) the **Bangsamoro Economic and Development Council (BEDC)** includes 5 private sector and CSO representatives; (3) **local development councils** (detailed in BAA 49, based on BOL Art. VIII framework) include NGO representatives. No standalone **"Bangsamoro CSO Coordinating Office"** or **"PO Accreditation Authority"** is mandated by the BOL — **PB No. 399** proposes creating such an office through legislation.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places two types of limitations:

   (1) **Constitutional floor**: Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** The constitutional provisions on people's organizations (Art. XIII, Sec. 15-16 — see the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B)) set the floor below which Bangsamoro legislation cannot fall. Any PO regulatory framework must respect the **freedom of association** (Art. III, Sec. 8) and the **right to participation** (Art. XIII, Sec. 16).[^7]

   (2) **Policy framework**: Art. XII, Sec. 40 establishes the full disclosure policy and civil society budget participation as affirmative obligations — Parliament may define the modalities of PO participation through implementing legislation but cannot extinguish the participation mandate.

   No express national law consistency requirement is imposed — there is no "consistent with," "in accordance with," or "subject to" national law qualifier for this power.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to people's organizations?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** This is the **universal constitutional boundary** on all 55 powers.[^7]

   (2) **People's Organizations (Art. XIII, Sec. 15)**: ***"The State shall respect the role of independent people's organizations to enable the people to pursue and protect, within the democratic framework, their legitimate and collective interests and aspirations through peaceful and lawful means."*** This is the **defining constitutional provision** — it establishes three requirements: (a) the State must **respect** PO independence; (b) POs must operate **within the democratic framework**; and (c) their activities must be **peaceful and lawful**. Crucially, the second sentence provides the constitutional definition: ***"People's organizations are bona fide associations of citizens with demonstrated capacity to promote the public interest and with identifiable leadership, membership, and structure."***[^8]

   (3) **Right to Participation (Art. XIII, Sec. 16)**: ***"The right of the people and their organizations to effective and reasonable participation at all levels of social, political, and economic decision-making shall not be abridged. The State shall, by law, facilitate the establishment of adequate consultation mechanisms."*** This is an **affirmative obligation** with two components: (a) a **right** that shall not be abridged — participation at all levels of decision-making; and (b) a **duty** — the State must establish consultation mechanisms "by law." Any Bangsamoro PO framework must give effect to both.[^9]

   (4) **Freedom of Association (Art. III, Sec. 8)**: ***"The right of the people, including those employed in the public and private sectors, to form unions, associations, or societies for purposes not contrary to law shall not be abridged."*** PO formation is constitutionally protected. A regulatory framework for accreditation cannot be so burdensome as to effectively deny the right to organize.[^10]

   (5) **State Policy (Art. II, Sec. 23)**: ***"The State shall encourage non-governmental, community-based, or sectoral organizations that promote the welfare of the nation."*** This mirrors the BOL's commitment to civil society participation (Art. XII, Sec. 40) — establishing a constitutional policy favoring PO promotion.[^11]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee for people's organizations?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Independence of POs** — Art. XIII, Sec. 15 requires the State to ***"respect the role of independent people's organizations."*** A regulatory framework cannot convert POs into government-controlled entities or subject them to political control.

   (2) **Right to participation** — Art. XIII, Sec. 16 guarantees ***"effective and reasonable participation at all levels of social, political, and economic decision-making."*** This is not merely advisory — it is a right that ***"shall not be abridged."***

   (3) **Freedom of association** — Art. III, Sec. 8 protects the right to form associations for lawful purposes.

   (4) **Equal protection** — Art. III, Sec. 1 applies to PO regulation: differential treatment of POs (e.g., favoring certain organizations over others in accreditation) must rest on **substantial distinctions** germane to the law's purpose.

   (5) **Due process** — Art. III, Sec. 1 requires that regulatory actions affecting POs (accreditation denial, revocation, suspension) satisfy notice, hearing, and right to appeal.

**Q. What constitutional mandates for legislation or institutions exist in this area?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 16 mandates that the State shall ***"by law, facilitate the establishment of adequate consultation mechanisms."*** This is a **direct constitutional directive** for legislation — both national and regional governments must establish consultation mechanisms through law, not merely through executive policy. Art. X, Sec. 9 mandates that ***"Legislative bodies of local governments shall have sectoral representation as may be prescribed by law"*** — requiring legislation for PO-based sectoral representation at the local level.[^12]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for people's organizations?**

A. The national framework establishes a multi-layered system: **RA 7160** (Local Government Code) provides the primary operational framework — Sec. 34 mandates LGUs to promote the establishment of POs; Sec. 35 defines POs as ***"bona fide associations of citizens with demonstrated capacity to promote the public interest and with identifiable leadership, membership, and structure"***; Sec. 36 provides for **accreditation** by local sanggunian; and Title V requires NGO representation in local development councils (not less than one-fourth of membership). **RA 8425** (Social Reform and Poverty Alleviation Act) provides for **sectoral representation** in anti-poverty governance through 14 basic sectors. **RA 7941** (Party-List System Act) enables marginalized sectors to gain congressional representation through party-list organizations. **RA 6713** and **RA 9485** (as amended by RA 11032) establish transparency, access, and service standards that support PO engagement. Together, these laws provide: a **definition**, an **accreditation system**, **participation rights** in development planning, **sectoral representation** mechanisms, and **transparency obligations** — the baseline against which any Bangsamoro PO legislation will be compared.[^13][^14][^15]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. The following national laws apply:

   (1) **RA 7160** — the PO-related provisions (Sec. 34-36, Title V) are **partially superseded** by BAA 49, which replicates and adapts the NGO representation requirement for BARMM local development councils (Sec. 119-120). Where BAA 49 is silent, RA 7160 applies suppletorily — including the PO definition in Sec. 35.[^13]

   (2) **RA 8425** — applies suppletorily for NAPC's sectoral framework and PO engagement in anti-poverty programs.[^14]

   (3) **RA 7941** — applies directly for national party-list representation; does not govern BTA Parliament sectoral seats (governed by BAA 35).[^15]

   (4) **RA 6713**, **RA 9485/RA 11032** — apply directly as laws of general application to all BARMM public officials and government offices.

   (5) **RA 11292** — applies suppletorily for governance quality assessment standards.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. The **Department of the Interior and Local Government (DILG)** is the primary national agency for PO engagement at the local government level — it issues guidelines for LGU accreditation of POs and oversight of local development council NGO representation. **NAPC** (National Anti-Poverty Commission) coordinates sectoral representation in anti-poverty governance. In BARMM, **MILG** has assumed the DILG function for PO engagement — but the accreditation framework (guidelines, standards, procedures) remains thinly specified without standalone legislation. The **Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)** handles national-level registration of non-stock, non-profit corporations (including NGOs), and **DOLE** accredits labor organizations — both retain national jurisdiction within BARMM for their respective registration functions.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG)** is the primary ministry. BAA 13, Book IV, Title V defines MILG's mandate — with Sec. 10(f) explicitly assigning the function of providing ***"technical assistance to civil society organizations including non-governmental organizations, people's organizations, and the indigenous people's organizations in promoting a culture of good governance through people's participation."*** BAA 85 allocates MILG's operational budget. The **Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD)** has a complementary role: BAA 13, Book V, Title I, Sec. 3(e) mandates MSSD to ***"promote, build and strengthen people's organizations for a self-directing welfare system at the grassroots level"*** and Sec. 20 assigns MSSD the **accreditation** of social welfare agencies — a subset of POs/CSOs operating in the welfare space.[^16][^17]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted specifically for people's organizations as of BAA 89. This power is exercised through multiple intersecting mandates: (1) **BAA 13** (Administrative Code) — assigns PO engagement functions to MILG and MSSD; (2) **BAA 49** (Local Governance Code) — establishes the local development council framework with mandatory NGO representation and accreditation; (3) **BAA 35** (Electoral Code) — governs sectoral representation in Parliament. None of these is a dedicated PO framework law.

**Q. What are MILG's mandate and functions under BAA 13 relevant to people's organizations?**

A. BAA 13, Book IV, Title V assigns MILG broad local governance functions. For people's organizations specifically:

   **MILG Provincial/City Office Functions** (Sec. 10) — headed by a Local Government Operations Officer VII (province) or similar rank:
   - (f) ***"Provide technical assistance to civil society organizations including non-governmental organizations, people's organizations, and the indigenous people's organizations in promoting a culture of good governance through people's participation."***[^16]

   This is MILG's express PO-related function. Additionally, MILG exercises general supervision over LGUs, and BAA 49 Sec. 120 assigns MILG the responsibility to issue **guidelines for NGO accreditation** by local sanggunians — a critical function that determines which POs can participate in local development councils.

   **MSSD Functions** (BAA 13, Book V, Title I, Sec. 3):
   - (e) ***"Promote, build and strengthen people's organizations for a self-directing welfare system at the grassroots level;"***
   - (g) ***"Accredit institutions and organizations engaged in social welfare activities and provide consultative and information services to them;"***[^17]

**Q. What can MILG NOT do without a standalone PO legislation?**

A. While BAA 13 and BAA 49 provide institutional functions, they do not establish: a **comprehensive PO accreditation framework** with detailed criteria, standards, and procedures beyond the thin provisions in BAA 49 Sec. 120; a **unified PO registration system** — currently POs may need SEC registration nationally and sanggunian accreditation locally, with no Bangsamoro-specific registration; **rights and protections** for POs against government interference — BAA 13 assigns a promotional function but does not codify PO independence or enumerated rights; **standards for PO participation** in government processes beyond local development councils — the budget participation mandate (Art. XII, Sec. 40) lacks implementing legislation; **penalty provisions** for government officials who obstruct PO participation; or **coordination mechanisms** across MILG, MSSD, BPDA, and other agencies that engage with POs. BAA 49 Sec. 120 requires MILG guidelines for accreditation — but guidelines are not legislation, and the absence of a standalone law means the accreditation framework can be changed by executive action without Parliamentary approval.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No adopted resolutions specifically address people's organizations or CSO engagement frameworks. However, **Res. No. 238** (directing implementation of Citizen's Charter and Public Assistance Compliance Desk) and **Res. No. 322** (encouraging QMS certification for all ministries and LGUs) indirectly support the participatory governance infrastructure within which POs operate.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. MILG is the **correct but incomplete** lead. BAA 13 Sec. 10(f) explicitly assigns the PO engagement function to MILG — making it the primary ministry for promoting people's participation and providing technical assistance to CSOs. However, the **full scope** of power (nn) extends beyond MILG's mandate: MSSD handles PO capacity-building in the welfare sector and accreditation of social welfare agencies; BPDA coordinates PO participation in development planning; the Parliament Secretariat manages PO engagement in the legislative process (petitions, committee consultations); and the Council of Leaders includes CSO representatives. No single ministry covers the complete scope. MILG is the **primary coordinator** for local government-level PO engagement — but a standalone PO framework law would need to assign responsibilities across multiple agencies.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Existing institutions:
   - **Lead ministry**: MILG — BAA 13, Book IV, Title V; BAA 85 budget
   - **Supporting ministry**: MSSD — BAA 13, Book V, Title I, Sec. 3(e) and Sec. 20 (PO capacity-building and accreditation of social welfare agencies)
   - **Development planning**: BPDA — coordinates local development council system (BAA 49 Sec. 119-125) where NGOs participate
   - **Regional economic body**: BEDC — BAA 13, includes 5 private sector and CSO representatives in membership
   - **Commissions**: BWC (women's POs), BYC (youth POs) — created by BAAs 8 and 10 respectively; serve as institutional links between sector-specific POs and government
   - **Local development councils**: provincial, city/municipal, and barangay — created by BAA 49, each with mandatory NGO representation (not less than 1/4 membership)
   - **Council of Leaders**: BAA 13, Book II — includes sectoral and community representatives

   All are operational. No dedicated **CSO Coordinating Office** exists — PB No. 399 proposes creating one.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not mandate a specific institution for people's organizations by name. However, Art. XII, Sec. 40's mandate for ***"participation of representatives from civil society in the budget process"*** implies an institutional mechanism for selecting, accrediting, and enabling CSO representatives to participate in budgeting — no such formal mechanism has been established through legislation. The budget participation requirement operates through informal or executive-level arrangements rather than a legislated framework.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Full exercise of this power would benefit from:
   - A **Bangsamoro CSO Coordinating Office** — as proposed in PB No. 399 — to serve as the institutional hub for PO/CSO engagement across all ministries and agencies, centralizing accreditation, capacity-building, and coordination functions currently scattered across MILG, MSSD, and other bodies.
   - A **PO Accreditation Board** or equivalent — to establish unified criteria, process, and standards for PO accreditation at both regional and local levels, replacing the ad hoc guidelines-based system under BAA 49 Sec. 120.
   - A **CSO Participation Mechanism for Budgeting** — to give effect to Art. XII, Sec. 40's mandate for civil society participation in the budget process.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **DILG** is the national counterpart to MILG for PO engagement. In BARMM, DILG's functions have been substantially devolved to MILG through BAA 13 and BAA 49. However, the **SEC** retains jurisdiction over **registration of non-stock, non-profit corporations** (the legal personality under which most NGOs and POs are organized) — POs in BARMM must register with SEC to acquire juridical personality, even if they also seek local accreditation through the sanggunian. **NAPC** coordinates national anti-poverty sectoral representation — its framework operates alongside BARMM's own participatory governance mechanisms.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. DILG's **CSO capacity-building programs** and **governance quality assessment** programs (Seal of Good Local Governance under RA 11292) operate in BARMM LGUs. NAPC's **Basic Sector Representation** programs engage POs in anti-poverty governance. The **Philippines-Australia Human Resource and Organisational Development Facility (PAHRODF)** and similar international development programs support CSO capacity-building in BARMM — though these are donor-funded, not national government programs.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **substantially complete** at the operational level. MILG has assumed DILG's local governance and PO engagement functions within BARMM through BAA 13. BAA 49 has replaced RA 7160's local development council provisions with a BARMM-specific framework. The critical **remaining national function** is **SEC registration** — the legal personality of POs in BARMM is still acquired through national registration, not through a Bangsamoro mechanism. If Parliament enacts a standalone PO/CSO framework law, it could establish a **regional registration or accreditation system** that complements or replaces the SEC requirement for certain categories of organizations operating exclusively within BARMM.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes. Two areas of overlap exist: (1) **Registration**: SEC registers POs nationally (juridical personality); sanggunians accredit POs locally per BAA 49 Sec. 120. These are distinct functions but create dual requirements. (2) **Anti-poverty governance**: NAPC's sectoral framework and BARMM's own participatory governance mechanisms may overlap in how POs engage in poverty alleviation — though in practice, BARMM's own mechanisms dominate within the region.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. BARMM has established the **institutional infrastructure** for PO participation through enacted legislation: BAA 49 mandates NGO representation (at least 1/4) in all local development councils, with accreditation by sanggunians per MILG guidelines; BAA 13 assigns PO engagement functions to MILG and MSSD; the BEDC includes 5 private sector and CSO representatives; and the Council of Leaders includes sectoral and community representatives. The **BDP 2023-2028** was developed through consultations with **49 CSOs**, demonstrating operational commitment to participatory planning. Women's and youth POs engage through BWC and BYC respectively. **No standalone PO legislation** has been enacted, but **two PBs** (319 and 399) have been filed addressing CSO registration/regulation and CSO engagement institutionalization.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has two distinctive advantages:

   (1) **Constitutional and Islamic dual legitimacy**: BARMM can ground PO participation in both the **constitutional framework** (Art. XIII, Sec. 15-16) and **Islamic governance principles** (*shura*, *hisbah*, *amanah*). This dual basis gives PO participation deeper cultural resonance in the Bangsamoro context than the purely secular national framework.

   (2) **Post-conflict participatory imperative**: BARMM's peace process embedded civil society engagement as a governance norm — the BDP formulation process, the transition planning, and the BOL ratification campaign all involved structured CSO participation. The national framework treats PO participation as a regulatory compliance issue; BARMM treats it as a **peace-building and governance legitimacy** mechanism.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of **standalone PO/CSO legislation**. Without it:

   - The **accreditation framework** relies on MILG guidelines under BAA 49 Sec. 120 — not legislation. Guidelines can be changed by executive action without Parliamentary oversight, creating uncertainty for POs.
   - The **budget participation mandate** (Art. XII, Sec. 40) has no implementing law — CSO participation in budgeting depends on discretionary invitation rather than legislated entitlement.
   - **PO rights and protections** against government interference are not codified in Bangsamoro law — the constitutional guarantee (Art. XIII, Sec. 15) is the only protection.
   - The **coordination** of PO engagement across MILG, MSSD, BPDA, BWC, BYC, and other agencies is ad hoc — no legislative framework assigns lead responsibility or defines interagency coordination.

   Enactment of **PB No. 319** (CSO registration framework) and/or **PB No. 399** (CSO engagement institutionalization) would address these gaps.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a **mature PO engagement infrastructure** over three decades:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 7160 provides detailed PO provisions (Sec. 34-36, Title V); RA 8425 provides sectoral representation in anti-poverty governance; RA 7941 provides party-list representation. Together, these provide a comprehensive legal framework.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: DILG maintains PO engagement units; NAPC coordinates 14 basic sectors; SEC provides registration. LGU accreditation systems have been operational since 1992.
   - **Regulatory systems**: DILG has issued detailed guidelines for NGO accreditation (MC 2019-121 and predecessors), including criteria for accreditation, de-accreditation, and dispute resolution.
   - **Track record**: Over three decades of local development council NGO participation, with refined processes for selection, accreditation, and participation protocols.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in three critical areas:

   (1) **No Islamic governance dimension**: The national PO framework is entirely secular. It does not recognize *shura*-based consultation models, *waqf*-based POs, or Islamic charitable foundations (*bayt al-mal* community funds) as distinct categories of people's organizations with their own governance traditions.

   (2) **No post-conflict sensitivity**: The national framework assumes stable communities with established institutional capacity. BARMM's PO landscape includes **conflict-affected community organizations**, **ex-combatant livelihood associations**, **IDP community groups**, and **peace-building CSOs** — none of which fit neatly into the RA 7160 accreditation model designed for stable LGU environments.

   (3) **Language and cultural accessibility**: PO accreditation processes, guidelines, and forms are issued in Filipino/English. Community-based organizations in rural BARMM — particularly in Lanao del Sur, Basilan, and Tawi-Tawi — require processes accessible in **Maranao**, **Tausug**, **Sama**, and **Yakan**.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: the **one-fourth (1/4) NGO membership requirement** in local development councils — BAA 49 has already done this. Also replicate the basic **PO definition** from RA 7160 Sec. 35 as a starting point.
   - **Adapt**: the **accreditation framework** — take the DILG guidelines model and add categories for Islamic community organizations, post-conflict community groups, and organizations operating in local languages. Adapt the **criteria for accreditation** to account for BARMM's organizational landscape — which includes *masjid*-based community groups, tribal organizations, and informal associations that may not meet conventional NGO criteria but serve genuine community functions.
   - **Replace**: the **SEC registration requirement** for POs operating exclusively within BARMM — a Bangsamoro CSO registration or accreditation system would reduce the dual compliance burden. Replace the **purely secular participation model** with one that recognizes both secular and *shura*-based consultation as legitimate governance mechanisms.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. People's organizations have a **significant Shari'ah dimension** because the concept of organized community participation in governance is deeply rooted in Islamic principles. The *shura* (consultation) principle — elevated to a Quranic mandate in Surah Al-Shura (42:38) — establishes that collective affairs must be conducted through mutual consultation. POs are the **modern institutional expression** of *shura*. Beyond *shura*, Islamic charitable and community organizations — *waqf* foundations, *zakat* collection bodies, *masjid*-based community councils, and *jama'ah* (congregational) organizations — represent a rich tradition of organized community engagement that predates and parallels Western NGO models. A Bangsamoro PO framework that ignores these traditions would be culturally incomplete.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in relation to people's organizations. However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah-based legislation on commercial and civil matters — which could extend to the organizational governance of Shari'ah-compliant POs. More directly, Art. V, Sec. 2(nn) grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over ***"People's organizations"*** — a term broad enough to encompass *waqf* foundations, *masjid* councils, and other Islamic organizational forms without requiring separate mention.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four Islamic legal principles directly inform PO legislation:

   (1) ***Shura*** (consultation) — the foundational principle. Governance decisions affecting the community must involve consultation with those affected. POs are the institutional mechanism for *shura* in modern governance.

   (2) ***Hisbah*** (commanding right, forbidding wrong) — the collective duty to promote good and prevent harm. POs exercising social monitoring, anti-corruption advocacy, and public accountability perform a *hisbah* function.

   (3) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — both government officials and PO leaders are *amin* (trustees). This principle supports governance accountability standards and PO internal governance requirements.

   (4) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — legislation promoting PO formation and participation serves the common good by strengthening community voice in governance.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) does not address people's organizations directly. Its relevance is limited to the extent that *waqf*-based POs may have property governance issues falling under PD 1083's property provisions. No Shari'ah court rulings or *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address PO formation or governance. The relevance of Shari'ah to this power is **principled** (*shura*, *hisbah*, *amanah*, *maslahah*) rather than **textual** (no existing legislation or jurisprudence).

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. The recommended approach is **principled integration** — embedding Islamic governance values into the PO framework as foundational principles rather than requiring formal Shari'ah compliance certification. Specifically: (a) the preamble and declaration of principles should reference *shura* as a governance foundation; (b) the accreditation framework should recognize **Islamic organizational forms** (*waqf* foundations, *masjid*-based community councils, *jama'ah* organizations) as legitimate PO categories alongside conventional NGO models; (c) PO governance standards should incorporate *amanah* principles — transparency, accountability, and prohibition of self-dealing; and (d) the framework should be **inclusive**, not exclusive — both Islamic and secular organizational models should be recognized without requiring one to conform to the other.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM has **broad legislative authority** to diverge. Applying the **5-layer constraint framework** from the constitutional analysis:

   - **Layer 1 (Constitutional rights — absolute)**: Art. XIII, Sec. 15-16 (people's organizations and participation) and Art. III, Sec. 8 (freedom of association) are the binding constitutional floor. Any Bangsamoro PO framework must respect PO independence, protect the right to participation, and preserve freedom of association. These constrain **purposes and rights**, not specific procedures — BARMM may design different accreditation processes, representation mechanisms, and engagement frameworks as long as they serve the constitutional purposes.

   - **Layers 2-3 (BOL supremacy/minimum standards)**: No "shall prevail" supremacy clause or minimum standard requirement applies to this power. Art. V, Sec. 2(nn) has no "consistent with" qualifier. This places people's organizations in the **unconstrained** category — no BOL provision requires conformity with national PO law.

   - **Layer 4 (Institutional jurisdiction retention)**: **SEC** retains national jurisdiction over registration of non-stock, non-profit corporations. A Bangsamoro PO framework could establish a **regional accreditation system** that complements SEC registration — but cannot prevent POs from registering with SEC if they choose to do so. SEC jurisdiction is institutional, not substantive.

   - **Layer 5 (Shall not diminish)**: No "shall not diminish" provision applies specifically to PO rights. However, Art. VI, Sec. 10's general provision that LGU privileges under RA 7160 shall not be diminished implies that the **one-fourth NGO representation** in local development councils (already replicated in BAA 49 Sec. 119) cannot be reduced.

   Using the **validated divergence spectrum** from the constitutional analysis, people's organizations falls in **Tier 4 — Broad (constitutional floor only)**: no national law consistency requirement, no BOL minimum standard, constitutional floor is the only binding constraint. The judicial test from *Ali v. BTA Parliament* (GR-02219, 2025) applies: BARMM's legislative authority ***"must always operate within the bounds of the Constitution."***

   In practice, BARMM can: establish its **own PO accreditation system** distinct from DILG guidelines; create **additional categories of POs** (Islamic organizational forms, post-conflict community groups) not recognized in RA 7160; mandate **stronger participation rights** than the national framework provides (e.g., expanding PO participation beyond development councils to budgeting, legislative, and oversight functions as contemplated by Art. XII, Sec. 40); and design **culturally appropriate engagement mechanisms** including *shura*-based consultation models. As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), *shura* provides a unique Bangsamoro basis for divergence — the Islamic governance tradition of consultation supports a more robust PO participation framework than the national model.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For PO members within BARMM**: They would gain **stronger participation rights** if a Bangsamoro framework provides more robust engagement mechanisms than the national model — particularly in budgeting (Art. XII, Sec. 40), legislative processes (PB No. 320 on public petitions), and policy-making. Islamic organizational forms would gain formal recognition.

   (2) **For POs operating across BARMM and non-BARMM areas**: Organizations registered with SEC and accredited by BARMM would face **dual compliance** — but this is already the case for local development council accreditation. A Bangsamoro framework should include **mutual recognition** provisions for nationally-registered POs operating within BARMM.

   (3) **For constitutional vulnerability**: A challenge is unlikely because the BOL expressly enumerates ***"People's organizations"*** as a Bangsamoro power, and the Constitution (Art. XIII, Sec. 15-16) establishes minimum rights that any framework would need to **meet or exceed** — not a specific framework that BARMM must replicate.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. The BAA would govern within BARMM for matters within its scope. RA 7160's PO provisions (Sec. 34-36) — already partially superseded by BAA 49 — would be further superseded for BARMM-specific accreditation, participation, and engagement. National laws of general application (RA 6713, RA 9485/RA 11032) would continue to apply. SEC registration for juridical personality would continue as a national function unless the BAA establishes an alternative registration mechanism for POs operating exclusively within BARMM.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Two jurisdictional overlaps exist:

   (1) **PO registration/juridical personality**: SEC registers POs nationally (as non-stock, non-profit corporations). BAA 49 Sec. 120 provides for local accreditation by sanggunians. The relationship between national registration (SEC) and local accreditation (sanggunian) is unclear — particularly whether a PO must have SEC registration to be accredited, or whether BAA 49 accreditation is sufficient for participation in BARMM governance. A standalone PO framework must clarify this boundary.

   (2) **Accreditation standards**: DILG issues national guidelines for NGO accreditation in local development councils. MILG is supposed to issue BARMM-specific guidelines under BAA 49 Sec. 120. The relationship between DILG guidelines and MILG guidelines is not defined — LGUs may be uncertain which to follow. A legislative framework would supersede both guideline sets.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Three pending PBs address the gaps:

   (1) **PB No. 319** (CSO Registration and Regulation) — would provide a **comprehensive regulatory framework** for CSO/PO registration and operations in BARMM. This is the most directly relevant pending bill — it would create the standalone legislation that currently does not exist.

   (2) **PB No. 399** (CSO Engagement Institutionalization) — would create a **dedicated CSO Coordinating Office** and formalize engagement mechanisms. This addresses the institutional coordination gap.

   (3) **PB No. 320** (Public Petitions) — would create a **legislative participation channel** for POs beyond development councils. This addresses the gap between the BOL's participatory governance vision and the limited engagement mechanisms currently in law.

   **Remaining gaps even if all three pass**: (a) implementing legislation for Art. XII, Sec. 40's budget participation mandate; (b) recognition of Islamic organizational forms as PO categories; (c) conflict-sensitive accreditation standards for post-conflict community groups; (d) interagency coordination framework for PO engagement across MILG, MSSD, BPDA, and commissions.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028** — Chapter 1 (Extended Transition) identifies inclusive and participatory governance as a core planning principle, with the BDP itself developed through consultations with **49 CSOs**. Chapter 10 (Peace and Security) depends on civil society engagement in peace-building and conflict resolution. Chapter 13 (Plan Financing) identifies CSOs as **development partners** in BDP implementation and financing. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 1 (Good Governance)**, depends on strengthening participatory governance institutions — active PO engagement is essential for transparency, accountability, and citizen trust. **Pillar 5 (Moral Governance)** connects PO participation to the anti-corruption and integrity framework.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without standalone PO legislation:

   - The **budget participation mandate** (Art. XII, Sec. 40) remains unimplemented through legislation — CSO engagement in budgeting is discretionary rather than mandatory, undermining transparency and accountability.
   - **PO accreditation** depends on executive guidelines rather than legislation — creating instability and reducing PO confidence in the system.
   - **Islamic community organizations** (*waqf* foundations, *masjid* councils) lack formal recognition as PO categories — excluding a significant segment of BARMM's organized community life from formal governance participation.
   - **Post-conflict community organizations** face accreditation barriers designed for stable LGU environments — undermining reintegration and peace-building through organized community participation.
   - The **BDP participatory governance vision** cannot be fully operationalized — each BDP cycle depends on structured CSO engagement that currently lacks a legislative foundation.
   - **BARMM's governance legitimacy** — particularly important during the extended transition — is weakened when civil society participation is ad hoc rather than institutionalized.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does power (nn) relate to power (mm) — mechanisms for consultations for women and marginalized sectors?**

A. Powers (nn) and (mm) form a **complementary pair**: (nn) covers the **organizational dimension** — the people's organizations themselves (formation, accreditation, capacity-building, rights); (mm) covers the **procedural dimension** — the consultation mechanisms through which these organizations participate in governance (development councils, budget processes, legislative consultations, policy dialogues). A comprehensive participatory governance framework must address both powers — legislation on one without the other is incomplete. **PB No. 399** (CSO engagement institutionalization) appears to address both dimensions; **PB No. 319** (CSO registration and regulation) focuses primarily on (nn).

**Q. What is the relationship between POs and the sectoral representation system in the BTA Parliament?**

A. POs serve as the **nominating and electoral infrastructure** for sectoral representation in Parliament. Art. VII, Sec. 5(c) reserves at least 8 seats for non-Moro IPs, settler communities, women, youth, traditional leaders, and Ulama. Each sector's representatives emerge from organized constituencies — the women's movement, youth organizations, IP tribal governance structures, *ulama* bodies, and settler community associations. BAA 35 (Electoral Code) governs the election process. The strength and legitimacy of sectoral representation depends directly on the strength of the underlying POs. Without a robust PO framework, sectoral representation risks becoming a formality — nominees may lack genuine organizational backing.

**Q. How does the one-fourth (1/4) NGO representation requirement in local development councils work in practice?**

A. BAA 49 Sec. 119 requires that NGO representatives constitute ***"not less than one fourth (1/4) of the members of the fully organized council"*** at all levels — barangay, municipal/city, and provincial. Sec. 120 provides that NGOs ***"shall choose from among themselves their representatives"*** within 60 days of council organization, and the ***"local sanggunian concerned shall accredit non-governmental organizations subject to the guidelines that shall be issued by the MILG."*** This creates a three-step process: (1) MILG issues accreditation guidelines; (2) local sanggunian accredits NGOs based on those guidelines; (3) accredited NGOs choose their own representatives. The practical challenge in BARMM is that many communities have few formally registered NGOs — particularly in rural and conflict-affected areas. If the accreditation criteria are drawn from conventional NGO models (SEC registration, audited financial statements, formal organizational structure), many genuine community organizations — *masjid*-based groups, tribal councils, informal livelihood associations — may be excluded. Legislation could address this by creating **flexible accreditation categories** suited to BARMM's organizational landscape.

**Q. How does the BOL's full disclosure policy interact with PO participation?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 40 creates a **direct linkage** between fiscal transparency and civil society participation: ***"the participation of representatives from civil society in the budget process."*** This is not general transparency — it mandates CSO participation in a specific government function. This creates a legislative space for a **Bangsamoro Budget Participation Act** or equivalent — legislation that would define how CSOs participate in budget formulation, review, and monitoring. **BAA 84** (Budget System Act) governs the budget process but does not include detailed provisions for CSO participation. Implementing Art. XII, Sec. 40 through legislation would be the most impactful near-term action for people's organizations — it would give POs a formal, legislated role in government resource allocation.

**Q. What categories of POs operate in BARMM that may not be recognized under the national framework?**

A. At least four categories of BARMM-specific POs exist outside conventional NGO models:

   (1) **Islamic community organizations** — *waqf*-based foundations, *masjid*-based community development councils, *zakat* collection and distribution committees, *madrasah* parent-teacher associations, and *jama'ah* (congregational) organizations. These have deep roots in Bangsamoro communities but may not meet SEC registration requirements designed for corporate-style NGOs.

   (2) **Post-conflict community organizations** — ex-combatant livelihood associations (particularly MILF and MNLF reintegration groups), IDP community councils, and peace-building organizations formed during or after the conflict. These emerged from the peace process and serve critical governance functions but may be informally organized.

   (3) **Traditional/tribal organizations** — *sultanate* councils, *datu* networks, and indigenous community governance structures for non-Moro IPs (Teduray, Lambangian, B'laan, Higaonon). These exercise genuine community governance functions but do not conform to Western organizational models.

   (4) **Island and maritime community organizations** — fishing community associations and island barangay development groups in Tawi-Tawi, Basilan, and Sulu-adjacent areas that face unique geographic barriers to formal organization.

   A Bangsamoro PO framework should create **accreditation categories** that recognize these diverse organizational forms.
