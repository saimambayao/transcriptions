# (g) Business Name Registration

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(g)** — ***"Business name registration;"***

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis in the style of Philippine bar review materials (Bernas, Golden Notes). Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy: BOL > Shari'ah > Constitution > National Law > Implementation > Divergence > Gaps > Development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over business name registration?**

A. Article V, Section 2(g) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"business name registration"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a **specific, discrete regulatory function** — the authority to register, maintain records of, and regulate business names used by enterprises within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. One provision elaborates on this power:

   Art. XIII, Sec. 25 states: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall have the power to register business names which shall be listed in the Philippine Business Registry."*** This provision: (1) **restates** the registration authority in more specific terms; and (2) **creates an interoperability requirement** — Bangsamoro-registered business names must be listed in the **Philippine Business Registry (PBR)**, the integrated national database established by DTI under EO 587. This is not a "consistent with national law" requirement — it is a **data-sharing mandate** ensuring that businesses registered in BARMM are visible in the national commercial database.[^2]

   Additionally, Art. XIII, Sec. 1 and Sec. 2 provide the general economic framework within which business registration operates — requiring the Bangsamoro economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution"*** and to promote ***"economic development that facilitates growth and full employment."***[^3][^4]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not expressly mandate the Parliament to enact a business registration law by name. Art. XIII, Sec. 25 grants the power but does not use the mandatory language "the Parliament shall enact" or name a specific piece of legislation (unlike Art. XIII, Sec. 24 for cooperatives or Art. XII, Sec. 14 for the Revenue Code). The power is therefore exercised through the **general legislative authority** of Parliament and the **institutional mandate** of MTIT under BAA 13.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not create a specific registration office for business names. The function is institutionally assigned to **MTIT** through BAA 13, specifically the **Bureau of Trade and Industry** (Sec. 11(c)), which is tasked with the ***"Implementation of the Fair Trade Laws, Price Act and related policies on consumer protection and business name registration."***[^5]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. One condition:

   **PBR listing requirement**: Art. XIII, Sec. 25 requires that BARMM-registered business names ***"shall be listed in the Philippine Business Registry."*** This is a **mandatory interoperability condition** — not a subordination of BARMM registration to DTI authority, but a requirement that the BARMM registration system feed data into the national database. This protects against duplicate business name registrations across jurisdictions and ensures that BARMM-registered businesses are discoverable nationally.[^2]

   No "consistent with national law" or "in accordance with" qualifier is attached to this power. The constitutional floor (Art. X, Sec. 20) applies as to all 55 powers.

### B. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. **Minimal direct Shari'ah dimension**. Business name registration is a regulatory-administrative function. However, two indirect connections exist: (1) the ***halal*** certification system — enterprises using *halal* designations in their business names should be verified for compliance, creating an intersection between business name registration and the Bangsamoro *Halal* Board's accreditation mandate under BAA 13; and (2) the general Shari'ah principles of ***maslahah*** (public interest) and ***amanah*** (trust/stewardship) support orderly business registration as a mechanism for consumer protection and commercial transparency.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. XIII, Sec. 25 (business name registration). The *halal* connection arises from BAA 13, which attaches the **Bangsamoro *Halal* Board** to MTIT (Sec. 5) and assigns MTIT the function of promoting, developing, and regulating ***"Halal Industry development, and accredit Halal certifying bodies and Halal auditors"*** (Sec. 3(n)).[^6]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. No specific Shari'ah principles beyond the general values of *maslahah* (public interest), *amanah* (trust), and *'adl* (justice) directly govern business name registration. A Bangsamoro business registration law should, however, include provisions for verifying *halal* claims in business names — preventing fraudulent use of *halal* designations, which would violate consumer trust and Islamic commercial ethics.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) does not address business name registration. No Shari'ah court jurisdiction over business name disputes exists under current law. Commercial disputes arising from business name conflicts between Muslim parties could potentially fall within Shari'ah District Court jurisdiction under BOL Art. X, Sec. 6(d) — ***"All actions arising from customary and Shari'ah compliant contracts in which the parties are Muslims."***[^7]

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. The recommended approach is **principled** — legislation guided by Islamic values without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance for business name registration itself. However, a Bangsamoro business registration law should include: (a) a **prohibition on fraudulent *halal* designations** in business names, enforceable through the *Halal* Board; and (b) a **dispute resolution** option through Shari'ah courts for business name disputes between Muslim parties.

### C. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to business name registration?**

A. Three categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 20)**: The universal constitutional boundary — all autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***[^8]

   (2) **National Economy and Patrimony (Art. XII)**: Art. XII, Sec. 1 mandates the State to promote ***"a more equitable distribution of opportunities, income, and wealth"*** and to promote ***"industrialization and full employment."*** Business name registration facilitates enterprise formation in furtherance of this mandate.

   (3) **Equal Protection and Due Process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law, nor shall any person be denied the equal protection of the laws."*** Business registration decisions (approval, denial, cancellation) must satisfy procedural due process. Business owners in BARMM must not face more burdensome registration requirements than those outside BARMM without rational basis.[^9]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. (1) **Right to engage in trade and livelihood** — Art. XII provisions promote private enterprise and the right to engage in lawful business. Business name registration cannot be used as a barrier to this right. (2) **Equal protection** — differential treatment of BARMM-registered businesses must rest on substantial distinctions. (3) **Due process** — denial or cancellation of business name registration must provide notice, hearing, and appeal.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. No specific constitutional mandate for business name registration exists. The general mandate to promote trade and enterprise (Art. XII) applies.

### D. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for business name registration?**

A. The national framework provides a **simple, low-cost registration system** administered by the **Department of Trade and Industry (DTI)**. **Act No. 3883** (Business Name Law, 1931) requires persons using business names, trade names, or trade styles to register with DTI. **EO 587** established the **Philippine Business Registry (PBR)** — an integrated online database linking DTI business name registrations with SEC corporate registrations and other government databases. **RA 11032** (Ease of Doing Business Act) mandates processing time standards for registration. **RA 9501** (Magna Carta for MSMEs) and **RA 9178** (BMBEs Act) provide simplified registration for micro and small enterprises.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national business registration laws apply in the absence of a Bangsamoro business registration law:

   (1) **Act No. 3883** — DTI registers all business names in BARMM. Applies suppletorily.[^10]
   (2) **EO 587** — PBR operates in BARMM; BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 25 expressly requires interoperability.[^11]
   (3) **RA 11032** — ARTA standards apply to all government agencies including BARMM. Applies directly.
   (4) **RA 11232** — SEC retains corporate registration jurisdiction. Applies directly.
   (5) **RA 9501**, **RA 9178**, **RA 10644** — suppletory application for MSME, BMBE, and Negosyo Center frameworks.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. The **Department of Trade and Industry (DTI)** exercises full business name registration authority in BARMM through its regional and provincial offices. DTI processes all business name registrations, issues Certificates of Registration, and maintains the PBR database entries for BARMM businesses. When a Bangsamoro business registration law is enacted, the question is whether **MTIT replaces DTI** within BARMM for business name registration or whether **DTI retains a coordination role** for PBR interoperability.[^11]

### E. Implementation

#### E.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT)** is the lead ministry, specifically the **Bureau of Trade and Industry**. BAA 13, Book VII, Title V, Chapter 4, Sec. 11(c) assigns business name registration to this bureau. BAA 85, Chapter V (MTIT) allocates its operational budget.[^5][^12]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for business name registration as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through MTIT's **BAA 13 general mandate**.

**Q. What are MTIT's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VII, Title V defines MTIT comprehensively:

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): MTIT is ***"the primary government agency mandated to implement laws, programs and projects on trade, investments, and tourism"*** through: ***(a) Comprehensive industrial growth strategy; (b) Progressive and socially responsible liberalization program; and (c) Policies designed for the expansion and diversification of trade.***[^13]

   **Powers and functions** (Sec. 3) — **26 enumerated functions** including:
   - (a) ***"Formulate policies, plans, programs and projects for the development of the trade, investments, and tourism in the region;"***
   - (n) ***"Promote, develop, and regulate Halal Industry development, and accredit Halal certifying bodies and Halal auditors;"***
   - (x) ***"Create, in coordination with related agencies of the Bangsamoro Government, a one-stop-shop Business Development Support Center to promote the growth of micro, small and medium-scale businesses and enterprises;"***
   - (y) ***"Administratively adjudicate and impose reasonable fines and penalties for violation of existing trade, investments, tourism, and cooperative development laws;"***[^14]

   **Bureau of Trade and Industry** (Sec. 11) — headed by a Director II, with functions including:
   - (c) ***"Assist the Minister in the Implementation of the Fair Trade Laws, Price Act and related policies on consumer protection and business name registration;"***[^5]

   **Divisions under the Bureau of Trade and Industry** (Sec. 12): (a) Trade Regulation and Consumer Protection Division; (b) Enterprise and Trade Development Division; (c) Industry Development and Investment Promotion Division.

   **Attached agencies** (Sec. 5): (1) Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA); (2) Bangsamoro *Halal* Board.

**Q. What can MTIT NOT do without a standalone business registration act?**

A. While BAA 13 assigns business name registration to the Bureau of Trade and Industry, it does not provide: a **comprehensive registration framework** with procedures, requirements, forms, and timelines; **penalty provisions** for operating under an unregistered business name; a **dispute resolution mechanism** for business name conflicts; **fee schedules** for registration; a **digital registration system** with PBR interoperability (as required by Art. XIII, Sec. 25); or a **clear jurisdictional boundary** with DTI for the transition of registration authority. BAA 13 assigns the function but does not create the **regulatory framework** for its full exercise.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No parliamentary resolutions specifically addressing business name registration have been identified.

#### E.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes. **MTIT** is the correct lead ministry. BAA 13 Sec. 11(c) expressly assigns business name registration to the Bureau of Trade and Industry within MTIT. The national counterpart — **DTI** — performs the same function nationally. There is no mismatch between the power's scope and the MOA's mandate.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. - **Lead ministry**: MTIT (BAA 13, Book VII, Title V; BAA 85, Chapter V)
   - **Bureau of Trade and Industry**: Division responsible for business name registration (BAA 13, Sec. 11(c))
   - **Bangsamoro *Halal* Board**: Attached to MTIT (BAA 13, Sec. 5) — relevant for verifying *halal* designations in business names
   - **BEZA**: Attached to MTIT (BAA 13, Sec. 5; Sec. 22-23) — economic zone enterprises have separate registration
   - **MTIT Provincial Offices**: 7 field offices (BAA 13, Sec. 19) — potential registration access points
   - **Negosyo Centers**: Operating in BARMM LGUs under RA 10644 (national program)

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not mandate a specific institution for business name registration beyond MTIT. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 25's requirement that registrations be ***"listed in the Philippine Business Registry"*** implies a **technical integration mechanism** — either a BARMM registration database linked to the PBR, or a data-sharing agreement with DTI — that has not been formally established.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. No new institutions are required. A **Bangsamoro Business Registration Unit** within the Bureau of Trade and Industry could be formalized by legislation to handle the function, with a digital registration system interoperable with the PBR. This would be an organizational unit, not a new institution.

#### E.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **DTI** registers all business names within BARMM through its regional and provincial offices. DTI issues **Certificates of Business Name Registration**, maintains records in the PBR, and processes renewals (business name registrations are valid for 5 years). All sole proprietorships and partnerships using trade names in BARMM must register with DTI.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. (1) **DTI business name registration** — routine processing of new registrations and renewals; (2) **Negosyo Centers** — one-stop shops in BARMM LGUs for business registration, permits, and enterprise development (RA 10644); (3) **Philippine Business Registry (PBR)** — online database listing all registered businesses including those in BARMM; (4) **ARTA compliance monitoring** — processing time standards for government transactions including business registration.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution has **not commenced**. DTI retains full business name registration authority within BARMM. MTIT's Bureau of Trade and Industry has an assigned function under BAA 13 Sec. 11(c) but has not operationalized an independent registration system. No memorandum of agreement, executive order, or transition plan governs the transfer of the registration function from DTI to MTIT.

#### E.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MTIT has been **institutionally established** under BAA 13 with the Bureau of Trade and Industry, provincial offices, and attached agencies. It has begun exercising its **programmatic functions** — trade promotion, investment facilitation, *halal* industry development, and coordination with LGUs on economic development. However, the **business name registration function** itself has not been independently operationalized — registrations continue to be processed through DTI.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has two potential advantages, though neither has been fully realized:

   (1) ***Halal* integration** — MTIT, through the *Halal* Board, can integrate *halal* certification verification into the business registration process. DTI does not have this capability. Enterprises using *halal* designations could be simultaneously registered and *halal*-verified.

   (2) **Conflict-sensitive accessibility** — MTIT's 7 provincial offices and the one-stop-shop Business Development Support Center (BAA 13 Sec. 3(x)) can be designed for BARMM's context — delivering services in local languages, accommodating post-conflict enterprise formation, and integrating with community-based economic development programs.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a **standalone business registration act**. Without it: MTIT cannot independently register business names — the regulatory framework (standards, forms, fees, timelines, penalties, appeals) does not exist in Bangsamoro law; the **PBR interoperability** required by Art. XIII, Sec. 25 has no implementing mechanism; and the **jurisdictional boundary** with DTI remains undefined.

#### E.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. - **Legislative depth**: Act No. 3883 has been operational since 1931, supplemented by RA 11032, RA 9501, and multiple DTI administrative orders.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: DTI has 17 regional offices, provincial offices in every province, and the Bureau of Trade Regulation and Consumer Protection at the national level.
   - **Digital systems**: The **Philippine Business Registry (PBR)** is an integrated online platform for business name searches, new registrations, and renewals. DTI's **eBNRS (electronic Business Name Registration System)** enables online processing.
   - **Negosyo Centers**: 960+ centers in LGUs nationwide, serving as one-stop shops for business registration.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. (1) **No *halal* integration**: DTI registration does not verify *halal* claims in business names — a significant gap in a region where *halal* certification is central to consumer trust. (2) **Language barriers**: DTI services are primarily in Filipino and English; registration assistance in Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, and Sama is limited. (3) **No conflict-sensitive enterprise support**: The national system does not account for enterprise formation in post-conflict, IDP, or ex-combatant livelihood contexts. (4) **Accessibility**: DTI provincial offices in island provinces (Basilan, Tawi-Tawi) are underresourced.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. - **Replicate**: **registration standards** (business name search, uniqueness verification, validity periods), **digital registration** (online system), and **processing timelines** (ARTA-compliant).
   - **Adapt**: **one-stop-shop model** — integrate business name registration with LGU business permits, *halal* certification, BIR/BRO tax registration, and MTIT trade facilitation services. Adapt for **local languages** and **conflict-affected enterprise** formation.
   - **Replace**: **institutional framework** — MTIT (Bureau of Trade and Industry) replaces DTI for business name registration within BARMM. The Bangsamoro system must maintain **PBR interoperability** per Art. XIII, Sec. 25 but operates independently.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national business name registration law?**

A. BARMM has **moderate legislative authority** to diverge. The BOL **expressly grants** the power to register business names (Art. V, Sec. 2(g); Art. XIII, Sec. 25) — this is congressional authorization for a distinct regional registration system. **No consistency requirement** with national business name law is imposed. The sole constraint is the **PBR listing requirement** (Art. XIII, Sec. 25), which mandates interoperability but not conformity with DTI procedures.

   As established in **Theme A**, the limits are: (1) the **PBR interoperability** mandate — BARMM cannot operate a closed system; (2) the **constitutional floor** established in **Theme C** — equal protection and due process in registration decisions. Within these limits, BARMM can establish its own **registration procedures**, **fee schedules**, **validity periods**, **penalty provisions**, and **dispute resolution mechanisms** — and integrate *halal* verification as a distinct feature.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For business owners within BARMM**: They would register with MTIT instead of DTI — potentially gaining faster processing through a local one-stop-shop and *halal* integration. The PBR listing ensures their registration is nationally visible.

   (2) **For businesses operating across BARMM and non-BARMM areas**: The PBR listing requirement prevents confusion. However, a **mutual recognition provision** may be needed — should a DTI-registered business operating in BARMM also register with MTIT? A Bangsamoro business registration law should address cross-jurisdictional recognition.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. The BAA governs business name registration within BARMM. DTI ceases to exercise registration authority within BARMM (unless the BAA provides otherwise for transition). National law (Act No. 3883, RA 11032) applies suppletorily where the BAA is silent. Constitutional provisions (equal protection, due process) always apply.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between **MTIT** (Bureau of Trade and Industry) and **DTI**. Currently, DTI registers all business names in BARMM. MTIT has an assigned function under BAA 13 but no operational registration system. The jurisdictional boundary is entirely undefined — no MOA, EO, or transition plan governs the transfer. A Bangsamoro business registration act must clarify: (a) when MTIT assumes registration authority; (b) what happens to existing DTI-registered businesses in BARMM; (c) how PBR interoperability is achieved; and (d) whether DTI retains any coordination role.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. No pending Parliament Bills specifically addressing business name registration have been identified. This is a **legislative vacuum** — no bills filed, no resolutions adopted, and no legislative intent signaled for this power area.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) depends on an efficient business registration system to achieve private sector growth targets. Enterprise formation — the starting point for job creation and economic activity — begins with business registration. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, requires streamlined business registration to reduce barriers to enterprise creation in BARMM's post-conflict economic context.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **Bangsamoro business registration act**: MTIT cannot independently register business names — all registrations flow through DTI's national system. The **one-stop-shop** vision (BAA 13 Sec. 3(x)) cannot fully integrate business name registration with LGU permits and MTIT trade services. The ***halal* verification** integration cannot be formalized. The **PBR interoperability** required by Art. XIII, Sec. 25 lacks an implementing mechanism on the BARMM side. Enterprise formation in **conflict-affected areas** continues through a national system not designed for BARMM's context.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the significance of the PBR listing requirement in Art. XIII, Sec. 25?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 25's requirement that BARMM-registered business names ***"shall be listed in the Philippine Business Registry"*** serves three purposes: (1) **prevents duplicate registration** — a business name registered in BARMM cannot conflict with a name already registered elsewhere in the Philippines, and vice versa; (2) **ensures national commercial visibility** — BARMM-registered businesses are discoverable by potential customers, partners, and regulators nationwide; (3) **maintains commercial order** — a single national database prevents the fragmentation that could occur if BARMM maintained a completely separate system. This is a **data interoperability** requirement, not a subordination of BARMM registration authority to DTI. MTIT can register business names under its own procedures and standards, but must feed the data into the PBR.[^2]

**Q. How does this power differ from corporate registration (SEC) and business permits (LGUs)?**

A. Three distinct registration systems exist:

   (1) **Business name registration** (Art. V, Sec. 2(g)) — the registration of sole proprietorships and partnerships operating under trade names. This is the power granted to BARMM. Nationally administered by DTI.

   (2) **Corporate registration** — the registration of corporations and partnerships with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) under RA 11232. This is **not** part of Art. V, Sec. 2(g) — SEC retains jurisdiction over corporate entities in BARMM.

   (3) **Business permits** — the licensing of businesses to operate in specific LGUs, administered by municipal or city governments under the Local Government Code (RA 7160) and BAA 49. This is a **local government function**, not a regional government function.

   A Bangsamoro business registration law should integrate with all three — ensuring that MTIT business name registration, SEC corporate registration, and LGU business permits form a coherent process.

**Q. How does this power interact with power (zz) Trade and Industry?**

A. Power (zz) — ***"trade and industry"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(zz)) — is the broader economic regulatory power. Business name registration (power (g)) is a **specific component** of the trade and industry framework. Art. XIII, Sec. 25 appears within the ***"Trade and Industry and Tourism"*** subsection of the BOL. MTIT is the lead for both powers. A comprehensive **Bangsamoro Trade and Industry Code** could address business name registration as one chapter within a broader framework — or it could be enacted as a standalone **Bangsamoro Business Registration Act**. Either approach fulfills the BOL mandate.
