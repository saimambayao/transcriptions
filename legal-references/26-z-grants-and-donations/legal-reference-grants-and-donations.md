# (z) Grants and Donations

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(z)** — ***"Grants and donations;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(z) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Not for domestic grants. For foreign government and multilateral grants: ***"subject to the prior clearance and approval by the President or his authorized representative"*** (Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2). For ODA: ***"upon review and approval of the National Government in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182"*** (Art. XII, Sec. 25). IFPB rulemaking authority: Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 3. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management (MFBM)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title IV, Ch. 1-7 (Sec. 1-20); Bangsamoro Finance Office **International and Other Finance Division** (Sec. 15). |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA) — project identification and monitoring for foreign-assisted projects; Office of the Chief Minister (OCM) — coordinates acceptance of foreign grants; Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board (IFPB) — promulgates implementing rules for grants and donations (Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 3) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 3 (Governance); Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 13 (Financing the Plan) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None. The BOL does not mandate a specific "Bangsamoro Grants and Donations Code." However, BAA 84 (Budget System Act) defines grants and foreign-assisted projects within the budget framework. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over grants and donations?**

A. Article V, Section 2(z) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Grants and donations"*** as one of the 55 powers over which the Bangsamoro Government exercises authority. The chapeau states: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This power encompasses the **acceptance**, **administration**, and **utilization** of grants, donations, contributions, bequests, gifts, endowments, foreign assistance, and other forms of aid — both domestic and foreign.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Seven provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 1 — the **primary implementing provision** — authorizes ***"Departments, bureaus, offices, and state universities and colleges of the Bangsamoro Government"*** to ***"accept donations, contributions, grants, bequests, or gifts, in cash or in kind, from domestic or foreign sources for purposes relevant to their functions."*** This is a **broad, decentralized authority** — each MOA may accept grants directly without routing through a central body, provided the grant is relevant to its functions.[^2]

   (2) Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2 — establishes the **foreign government clearance regime**: ***"In case of such grants or donations from governments of foreign countries, their agencies and instrumentalities or multilateral institutions or organizations, acceptance thereof shall be subject to the prior clearance and approval by the President or his authorized representative."*** This creates a **two-tier system**: domestic grants require no central clearance; foreign government and multilateral grants require Presidential approval.[^2]

   (3) Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 3 — assigns **rulemaking authority** to the IFPB: ***"The Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board shall promulgate rules for the implementation of this section."***[^2]

   (4) Art. XII, Sec. 6(g) and (h) — list grants as **sources of revenue**: ***"Grants from economic agreements or conventions"*** and ***"Grants, endowments, donations, foreign assistance, and other forms of aid."***[^3]

   (5) Art. XII, Sec. 25 — governs **ODA** separately: ***"The Bangsamoro Government may avail of official development assistance, upon review and approval of the National Government in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182."*** ODA is distinct from grants under Sec. 26 because it follows the RA 8182 approval process (NEDA Board, congressional oversight), not the simpler Presidential clearance under Sec. 26.[^4]

   (6) Art. XII, Sec. 24(c) — **excludes grants from the debt servicing ceiling**: ***"excluding those coming from grants, endowments, donations, loans, foreign assistance and official development assistance."*** This ensures that grants fund development, not debt repayment.[^5]

   (7) Art. XII, Sec. 28-29 — grants flowing from **economic and technical cooperation agreements** and from **international conventions** to which the National Government is party.[^6]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. Unlike Art. XIII, Sec. 24 (which mandates a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"*** by name), the BOL does not mandate specific grants and donations legislation. The BOL instead assigns rulemaking to the **IFPB** (Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 3), not to Parliament. However, Parliament may enact legislation under its general legislative power (Art. VII, Sec. 5), and has done so incidentally through **BAA 84** (Budget System Act), which defines grants and foreign-assisted projects within the budget framework.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not create a dedicated grants management body. It assigns three institutional roles:

   (1) **IFPB** (Art. VI, Sec. 4; Art. XII, Sec. 37-38) — promulgates implementing rules for grants and donations.

   (2) **The President or authorized representative** (Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2) — provides prior clearance for foreign government and multilateral grants.

   (3) **Each Bangsamoro MOA** (Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 1) — exercises decentralized authority to accept grants for purposes relevant to its functions.[^2]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places four conditions:

   (1) **Purposive limitation**: Grants must be ***"for purposes relevant to their functions"*** — a MOA cannot accept a grant outside its mandate (Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 1).[^2]

   (2) **Presidential clearance for foreign government/multilateral grants**: ***"subject to the prior clearance and approval by the President or his authorized representative"*** (Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2). This applies to: governments of foreign countries, their agencies and instrumentalities, and multilateral institutions or organizations.[^2]

   (3) **RA 8182 compliance for ODA**: ***"upon review and approval of the National Government in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182"*** (Art. XII, Sec. 25). This is the strictest approval regime — involving NEDA/DEPDEV Board review, counterpart fund appropriation, and congressional oversight.[^4]

   (4) **IFPB rulemaking**: ***"The Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board shall promulgate rules for the implementation of this section"*** — meaning operational procedures are not unilaterally set by BARMM but jointly with the national government through the IFPB.[^2]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to grants and donations?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"Within its territorial jurisdiction and subject to the provisions of this Constitution and national laws."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** This is the **universal constitutional boundary** on all 55 powers.[^8]

   (2) **General Supervision by the President (Art. X, Sec. 16)**: ***"The President shall exercise general supervision over autonomous regions to ensure that the laws are faithfully executed."*** The Presidential clearance requirement in Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2 is a specific manifestation of this general supervisory power applied to foreign government grant acceptance.[^9]

   (3) **COA Authority (Art. IX-D, Sec. 2)**: The Commission on Audit has constitutional authority to ***"examine, audit, and settle all accounts pertaining to the revenue and receipts of, and expenditures or uses of funds and property"*** of all government entities. This applies to all grants received by BARMM agencies — COA's constitutional mandate cannot be diminished by Bangsamoro legislation.[^10]

   (4) **Treaty and Foreign Affairs Power (Art. VII, Sec. 21; Art. XVIII, Sec. 25)**: The President's power over foreign affairs and the Senate's concurrence requirement for treaties are relevant when grants arise from international agreements. The Presidential clearance requirement in Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2 reflects the national government's constitutional primacy over foreign relations.[^11]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution does not guarantee a **right to receive grants**. However, two constitutional guarantees are relevant:

   (1) **Equal protection (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: If grants are distributed among BARMM agencies, the distribution criteria must have a **rational basis**. Arbitrary favoritism in grant allocation could face challenge.

   (2) **Accountability of public officers (Art. XI, Sec. 1)**: Public officers managing grants are accountable to the people and must serve with ***"utmost responsibility, integrity, loyalty, and efficiency."*** Misuse of grant funds triggers constitutional accountability mechanisms.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. The Constitution does not mandate specific grants legislation. The relevant constitutional institution is the **Commission on Audit**, which has plenary authority to audit grant utilization. The NEDA (now DEPDEV) was created by executive order, not constitutional mandate, but exercises the ODA coordination function referenced in Art. XII, Sec. 25 of the BOL.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for grants and donations?**

A. The national framework establishes three regulatory layers:

   (1) **ODA management**: RA 8182, as amended by RA 8555, defines ODA criteria (grant element of at least 25%, diplomatic relations, development objective), establishes NEDA/DEPDEV as the lead coordinating body, requires counterpart fund appropriation in the annual budget, mandates congressional oversight, and prohibits ODA utilization for purposes better served by the private sector.[^12]

   (2) **Government auditing**: PD 1445 (Government Auditing Code) requires all government agencies — including BARMM MOAs — to account for grants received and expended, subject to COA audit. COA Circulars provide specific rules on: recording of grants in government books, utilization reports, liquidation of grant funds, and treatment of unexpended grant balances.[^10]

   (3) **LGU grants**: RA 7160 (Local Government Code), Sec. 22(d), authorizes LGUs to accept grants and donations. Sec. 294-295 establishes that grant proceeds held by LGUs are treated as **special trust funds** subject to the specific terms of the grant.[^13]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. Three national laws apply directly:

   (1) **RA 8182** (as amended by RA 8555) — governs BARMM ODA availment. Applies **directly** per express BOL reference (Art. XII, Sec. 25).[^12]

   (2) **PD 1445** — COA auditing authority over all BARMM grant receipts and utilization. Applies **directly** per constitutional mandate (Art. IX-D).[^10]

   (3) **RA 7160** — applies **suppletorily** to BARMM LGU grant acceptance pending BAA 49 implementation.[^13]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. Two national institutions exercise direct authority:

   (1) **NEDA/DEPDEV** (reorganized by RA 12145) — reviews and approves ODA availment by BARMM; coordinates project identification, feasibility studies, and monitoring of foreign-assisted projects. BPDA is the Bangsamoro counterpart that interfaces with DEPDEV for development planning and ODA coordination.[^14]

   (2) **Commission on Audit** — exercises constitutional audit authority over all grants received by BARMM agencies, regardless of source (domestic/foreign, government/private). COA's regional office in BARMM audits grant utilization and compliance with grant conditions.[^10]

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management (MFBM)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title IV, Sec. 2 states the ministry's mandate: ***"The Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management is primarily mandated to evolve sound, efficient and responsive fiscal management and utilization of resources."*** Sec. 3(j) authorizes MFBM to ***"Coordinate with other Bangsamoro Ministries and Offices on matters concerning fiscal and monetary policies, credit, economic development, international finance, trade and investment."*** The Bangsamoro Finance Office's **International and Other Finance Division** (Sec. 15) is the organizational unit most directly relevant to foreign grant coordination.[^15]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. No standalone Bangsamoro Grants and Donations Act has been enacted. The power is exercised through:

   (1) **BAA 13** (Administrative Code) — individual MOA authorization provisions and MFBM's general fiscal management mandate.

   (2) **BAA 84** (Budget System Act) — defines grants, foreign-assisted projects, and their treatment in the budget process. Sec. 4(ab) defines grants; Sec. 4(x) defines foreign-assisted projects; BPDA's monitoring role is established for grant-funded projects.[^7]

**Q. What are MFBM's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title IV, Ch. 1 (Sec. 1-3) defines MFBM:

   **Declaration of Policy** (Sec. 1): ***"It is the policy of the Bangsamoro Government to promote, adopt, and implement sound and efficient use of government resources for regional development and for the attainment of economic self-sufficiency and genuine development."***[^15]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): ***"The Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management is primarily mandated to evolve sound, efficient and responsive fiscal management and utilization of resources. It shall be responsible for the formulation, institutionalization and administration of fiscal policies in coordination with other concerned ministries, agencies and instrumentalities of the Bangsamoro Government."***[^15]

   **Powers and Functions** (Sec. 3) — 15 enumerated functions, of which the following are directly relevant to grants:

   - (a) ***"Formulate medium-term expenditure and annual plans covering the Bangsamoro Government's resources mobilization efforts"***
   - (e) ***"Act as custodian of, and manage all financial resources of Bangsamoro Government"***
   - (f) ***"Manage public debt"*** (relevant to the distinction between grants and loans)
   - (j) ***"Coordinate with other Bangsamoro Ministries and Offices on matters concerning fiscal and monetary policies, credit, economic development, international finance, trade and investment"***
   - (k) ***"Develop and administer standard accounting system essential to fiscal management and control"***[^15]

   **Bangsamoro Finance Office** (Sec. 15-16) — includes the **International and Other Finance Division** among five divisions. Sec. 16 enumerates 8 functions including: (f) ***"Coordinate with other Ministries and Offices on matters concerning fiscal and monetary policies, credit, economic development, international finance, trade investment"*** and (g) ***"Formulate the overall resource application strategy to match the Bangsamoro Government's macro-economic policy."***[^16]

**Q. What can MFBM NOT do without a standalone grants and donations BAA?**

A. BAA 13 provides MFBM with general fiscal management authority, but does not provide:

   - A **specific grants registration and tracking system** — no centralized registry of all grants received across all BARMM MOAs.
   - **Standardized grant acceptance procedures** — each MOA follows its own process; no uniform requirements for due diligence, conflict-of-interest screening, or conditionality review.
   - **Grant utilization reporting standards** specific to BARMM — MOAs rely on COA rules but lack BARMM-specific guidelines for development impact assessment.
   - A **grants coordination mechanism** to prevent duplication, ensure strategic alignment with the BDP, and aggregate donor information across MOAs.
   - **Clear procedures for Presidential clearance** of foreign government grants — the process between BARMM and the Office of the President is not codified in Bangsamoro law.
   - A **Shari'ah compliance review mechanism** for grant conditions.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No adopted resolutions specifically addressing a standalone grants and donations framework have been identified.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. MFBM is the **correct lead** for the fiscal management dimension — accepting, recording, and accounting for grants. However, grants and donations is a **cross-cutting power** that involves multiple institutional roles:

   - **MFBM** — fiscal management, accounting, revenue recording, debt/grant distinction.
   - **BPDA** — development planning alignment, project identification for foreign-assisted projects, monitoring and evaluation of grant-funded programs (BAA 84 assigns BPDA standards-setting for this).
   - **OCM** — political coordination for foreign grant acceptance, liaison with the President's office for clearance.
   - **Individual MOAs** — direct acceptance authority under Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 1.

   No single MOA has a monopoly over this power. MFBM is the appropriate institutional anchor for fiscal management, but **BPDA** plays an equally important role in ensuring grants align with development priorities.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. The following institutions exercise functions related to grants and donations:

   - **Lead ministry**: MFBM — BAA 13, Book VI, Title IV; BAA 85, Chapter III. Operational. Fiscal management of all revenue including grants.
   - **Planning authority**: BPDA — BAA 13 (Book V) and BAA 84. Operational. Sets monitoring/evaluation standards for grant-funded projects.
   - **IFPB** — Art. VI, Sec. 4 and Art. XII, Sec. 37-38 of the BOL. Operational (meets at least every 6 months). Mandated to promulgate rules for Art. XII, Sec. 26.
   - **Individual MOAs with specific grant authorization**: BWC (BAA 13, Sec. 5 of BWC chapter — ***"Solicit and accept grants, aid, donations and gift"***); BSC (BAA 13, Sec. (f) of BSC chapter — ***"solicit and accept grants, aid, donations and gifts, in cash or in kind, or enter into agreements with any legitimate entity, local or foreign, consistent with its mandate subject to the limitations as set for under Section 26, Article 12 of the Bangsamoro Organic Law"***); BHRC; BCPCH; MIAP.[^17]

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not mandate creation of a specific grants management institution. The IFPB already exists and is mandated to promulgate implementing rules for Art. XII, Sec. 26 — but it is unclear whether the IFPB has actually promulgated these specific rules. [UNVERIFIED — the operational status of IFPB rules specifically for grants and donations under Art. XII, Sec. 26 could not be confirmed from available local sources.]

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Full exercise of this power would benefit from:

   (1) A **Bangsamoro Grants Coordination Unit** (or equivalent) — a body within MFBM or BPDA that maintains a centralized registry of all grants, coordinates donor engagement, prevents duplication, and ensures alignment with BDP priorities. Currently, each MOA accepts grants independently with no centralized visibility.

   (2) A **Shari'ah compliance review mechanism** for grant conditions — a review process (possibly through the Darul Ifta' or an advisory panel within MFBM) to assess whether grant conditions are consistent with Islamic principles before acceptance.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The national government exercises two forms of authority:

   (1) **ODA coordination**: NEDA/DEPDEV reviews and approves all ODA proposals from BARMM, coordinates project identification and feasibility studies, and monitors project implementation. The DEPDEV Board approves specific foreign-assisted projects.

   (2) **Audit**: COA audits all grants received by BARMM agencies — reviewing utilization reports, compliance with grant conditions, and proper accounting treatment.

   The **Presidential clearance** function for foreign government grants (Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2) is exercised through the Office of the President, though the operational mechanism for processing BARMM clearance requests is not documented in available sources.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. Several national programs channel grants and foreign assistance into BARMM:

   (1) **NEDA/DEPDEV ODA portfolio** — manages the pipeline of foreign-assisted projects in BARMM, including projects from the World Bank, Asian Development Bank (ADB), Japan International Cooperation Agency (JICA), and other bilateral/multilateral partners.

   (2) **Bangsamoro Normalization Trust Fund** — established under the Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro, involving both national government and international development partner funding for decommissioning, transitional justice, and community development.

   (3) **National government agency programs** — various national agencies implement grant-funded programs within BARMM (e.g., DSWD Kalahi-CIDSS community-driven development, DOH health facility grants, DepEd school building programs).

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Grant acceptance authority under Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 1 is already **fully devolved** — BARMM MOAs may accept domestic and foreign private grants directly without national government intermediation. For **foreign government and multilateral grants**, devolution is **structurally limited** by the Presidential clearance requirement — this is a permanent feature of the BOL, not a transitional arrangement. For **ODA**, national government control is permanent under RA 8182.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate in parallel?**

A. Yes. **ODA coordination** is an area of overlapping authority — BPDA identifies development priorities and proposes projects, but DEPDEV approves them. National agencies may also implement their own grant-funded programs within BARMM (e.g., DSWD community-driven development) alongside BARMM MOA programs. Without a grants coordination mechanism, there is a risk of **duplication** — the same donor community being approached by both national agencies and BARMM MOAs for similar purposes in the same geographic area.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. BARMM exercises this power through several institutional actions:

   (1) **Budget framework**: BAA 84 (Budget System Act) codified grants and foreign-assisted projects as recognized budget categories with defined treatment in the appropriation and expenditure process.

   (2) **Individual MOA grant acceptance**: Multiple BARMM agencies actively receive and administer grants — from international development organizations, foreign private foundations, domestic NGOs, and private sector donors. BAA 13 provisions for BWC, BSC, BHRC, BCPCH, and MIAP specifically authorize grant solicitation and acceptance.

   (3) **Development planning alignment**: BPDA's role in monitoring grant-funded projects (established in BAA 84) provides a degree of strategic coordination.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM's potential advantage lies in **proximity to the development context**:

   (1) **Conflict-sensitive design** — BARMM agencies understand post-conflict realities (IDP communities, decommissioned combatants, contested territories) and can design grant-funded programs tailored to these contexts. National agencies apply standardized program designs that may not account for conflict dynamics.

   (2) **Cultural and linguistic fit** — BARMM agencies can engage donors and implement grant-funded programs in local languages and through culturally appropriate mechanisms.

   (3) **Shari'ah-sensitive engagement** — BARMM can engage with Islamic development institutions (Islamic Development Bank, Muslim Aid, international Islamic charities) with greater cultural competence than national agencies.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a **centralized grants coordination and management framework**. Without it:

   - There is no **consolidated view** of all grants received across BARMM MOAs — no single database or registry.
   - There is no **strategic alignment mechanism** — grants may flow to donor-preferred sectors rather than BDP priorities.
   - The **Presidential clearance process** for foreign government grants lacks a codified procedure from the BARMM side — there is no published guide for how a BARMM MOA initiates and tracks a clearance request.
   - **ODA absorption capacity** remains constrained — BPDA's project preparation and MFBM's counterpart fund management capabilities are still developing.

   Promulgation of **IFPB rules** under Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 3, combined with an internal BARMM grants management protocol, would address the most critical gaps without requiring new legislation.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built:

   - **ODA management infrastructure**: NEDA/DEPDEV has decades of experience coordinating ODA from hundreds of bilateral and multilateral partners, with established project identification, approval, implementation, and monitoring processes. The ODA portfolio is tracked through the NEDA Project Monitoring Staff.
   - **COA audit framework**: COA has detailed circulars and issuances governing the recording, utilization, and reporting of grants received by government agencies — providing standardized procedures that BARMM MOAs follow by default.
   - **Donor coordination mechanisms**: The national government has established donor coordination groups, sector working groups, and regular consultative meetings with development partners — structured forums for aligning aid with national priorities.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national system falls short in three areas:

   (1) **Bangsamoro-specific priorities**: National ODA programming reflects national priorities (Manila-centric development planning). BARMM's post-conflict, poverty-intensive, infrastructure-deficit context requires a different ODA strategy than what national planning produces.

   (2) **Islamic development finance**: The national government has limited engagement with **Islamic Development Bank (IsDB)** modalities, Islamic charitable funding streams (*zakat*, *waqf*, *sadaqah*-funded development), and Organization of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) development mechanisms. BARMM is better positioned to access these resources but needs institutional capacity.

   (3) **Speed and flexibility**: National ODA processes (NEDA Board approval, counterpart fund appropriation) can take years from project concept to implementation. For a region needing rapid post-conflict development, this timeline is a constraint.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: COA audit standards for grant utilization — these are technically sound, constitutionally mandated, and non-negotiable.
   - **Adapt**: The NEDA/DEPDEV ODA coordination model — BPDA should develop a comparable project pipeline management system tailored to BARMM's smaller scale and conflict-sensitive context. Adapt the national grants recording system to include BARMM-specific categories (Islamic development finance, peace process-related grants, Shari'ah-compliant grants).
   - **Replace**: The one-size-fits-all approach to donor coordination — BARMM should develop its own donor coordination mechanism that engages Islamic development institutions, OIC members, and Bangsamoro diaspora communities as distinct donor categories not addressed by the national framework.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes, though less central than for powers like Islamic banking or cooperatives. Grants and donations have a Shari'ah dimension in three respects: (1) **Islamic charitable giving traditions** (*sadaqah*, *waqf*, *zakat*) are established mechanisms for channeling resources to public purposes; (2) **Islamic development finance institutions** (Islamic Development Bank, Islamic Solidarity Fund, OIC development programs) represent a significant potential donor pool for BARMM; (3) **grant conditions** may raise Shari'ah compliance questions when they require interest-bearing co-financing or impose conditions inconsistent with Islamic principles.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. XII, Sec. 26 (the grants and donations provision). However, BOL Art. XII, Sec. 6(h) lists ***"endowments"*** as a revenue source — which encompasses *waqf* properties and income. Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah-based commercial legislation, which could include grant management provisions for Islamic development finance instruments.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four principles are relevant:

   (1) ***Sadaqah*** (voluntary charity) — encourages charitable giving for public purposes. A Bangsamoro grants framework should facilitate and recognize *sadaqah*-based donations to government programs.

   (2) ***Waqf*** (endowment) — as established in the Shari'ah Sources table above, *waqf* properties and income could serve as a perpetual funding source for BARMM development. PD 1083, Art. 104-112 provides the existing legal framework.

   (3) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — government officers receiving grants are trustees (*amin*) of the donor's intention. This reinforces legal accountability requirements.

   (4) **Prohibition of *riba*-laden conditions** — grant packages that include interest-bearing loan components or require interest-based co-financing arrangements should be reviewed for Shari'ah compliance before acceptance.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977), Art. 104-112 governs *waqf* — including creation, administration, and judicial supervision of endowments. Shari'ah courts have jurisdiction over *waqf* disputes (BOL Art. X, Sec. 5-6 — property matters). No known *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address government grant acceptance, though general *fatawa* on *sadaqah* and *waqf* administration are analogous.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro grants management?**

A. The recommended approach is a **principled review mechanism** — not mandatory compliance for all grants, but a review process for grant conditions:

   (1) **Pure grants** (non-repayable, no interest-bearing conditions) — no Shari'ah concern. Accept per standard procedures.
   (2) **Grants with tied conditions** — review whether conditions require the recipient to enter into *riba*-bearing financial arrangements (e.g., co-financing through interest-bearing loans, deposit of grant funds in interest-bearing accounts).
   (3) **Islamic development finance instruments** — grants from IsDB, Islamic Solidarity Fund, and OIC programs may come with Shari'ah-compliant conditions that BARMM should be prepared to implement.

   A **Shari'ah advisory review** — either through the *Darul Ifta'* or an advisory panel within MFBM — should be available for grant conditions that raise Islamic finance questions. This need not delay grant processing for routine grants.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM's divergence is **structurally limited** by the BOL itself:

   (1) **No divergence possible** for ODA: Art. XII, Sec. 25 expressly requires compliance ***"in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182."*** This is a permanent national law consistency requirement — BARMM cannot create an alternative ODA approval process.

   (2) **No divergence possible** for COA audit: The constitutional mandate of the Commission on Audit (Art. IX-D) applies to BARMM grants regardless of any Bangsamoro legislation.

   (3) **Limited divergence** for foreign government/multilateral grants: Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2 requires Presidential clearance — BARMM cannot waive or substitute an alternative approval mechanism.

   (4) **Broad divergence** for domestic grants and private foreign grants: As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 1 grants decentralized acceptance authority to each BARMM MOA. BARMM may establish its own grant management framework — registration, due diligence, conditionality review, utilization reporting, Shari'ah compliance screening — for grants that do not require Presidential clearance.

   The **unique basis for divergence** is BARMM's access to **Islamic development finance** (see the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E)) — Islamic charitable funding streams (*sadaqah*, *waqf*), Islamic development institutions (IsDB), and OIC mechanisms. These are distinct donor categories with distinct legal and operational requirements that the national framework does not address.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different grant management standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) **For BARMM agencies**: Improved grant management would attract more donor funding by demonstrating institutional maturity and strategic alignment. A Shari'ah-compliant grant framework would open access to Islamic development finance streams currently untapped.

   (2) **For donors**: Standardized BARMM procedures would reduce transaction costs. Donors could engage one coordinating body (rather than negotiating with individual MOAs) for strategic alignment.

   (3) **For cross-boundary operations**: National agencies implementing programs in BARMM (DSWD, DOH, DepEd) would need to coordinate with any BARMM grants coordination mechanism to avoid duplication. This requires an intergovernmental coordination protocol.

**Q. What happens to national law when Parliament enacts a grants and donations BAA?**

A. National law (RA 8182 for ODA, PD 1445 for COA audit) continues to apply directly — the BOL expressly requires this. A Bangsamoro grants law would add a layer of **Bangsamoro-specific standards** (registration, coordination, Shari'ah review, reporting) on top of, not in replacement of, national requirements. For domestic and private foreign grants where no national law prescribes specific procedures, a Bangsamoro law would fill the regulatory space entirely.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Three jurisdictional overlaps exist:

   (1) **ODA project identification**: Both BPDA and DEPDEV identify priority projects. When BARMM-identified priorities differ from national ODA programming, there is no established resolution mechanism beyond the NEDA Board (where BARMM has limited representation).

   (2) **National agency programs in BARMM**: National agencies (DSWD, DOH, DepEd) implement grant-funded programs in BARMM alongside BARMM MOAs. There is no consolidated coordination mechanism to prevent duplication or ensure alignment with BDP priorities.

   (3) **IFPB rulemaking gap**: Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 3 assigns the IFPB to promulgate implementing rules, but it is unclear whether these rules have been issued. Without them, the operational procedures for the two-tier grant acceptance system (domestic vs. foreign government) remain undefined.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. No pending Parliament Bills specifically address grants and donations management. The gap is currently filled incidentally by **BAA 84** (Budget System Act) and individual BAA 13 MOA mandates. A dedicated Bangsamoro Grants Management Act or IFPB issuance would be needed to establish: a grants registry, coordination protocol, Shari'ah review mechanism, Presidential clearance procedures from the BARMM side, and donor coordination framework.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. Grants and donations are a **cross-cutting fiscal enabler** for virtually all BDP goals:

   (1) **BDP Chapter 13 (Financing the Plan)**: The BDP identifies grants, donations, and ODA as critical revenue sources — beyond the annual block grant — for financing the gap between available revenues and development needs. Infrastructure, health, education, and social protection programs depend heavily on foreign-assisted projects and development partner grants.

   (2) **BDP Chapter 7 (Economic Development)**: Private sector development, MSME financing, agricultural extension, and cooperative development programs receive significant donor support.

   (3) **BDP Chapter 3 (Governance)**: Institutional capacity building in BARMM agencies is largely funded through development partner grants (World Bank, ADB, JICA technical assistance programs).

   (4) **Mas Matatag Pillar 1 (Good Governance)**: Transparent and accountable grant management is a governance indicator that builds donor confidence and attracts continued assistance.

   (5) **Mas Matatag Pillar 5 (Inclusive Development)**: Post-conflict development in BARMM — normalization, decommissioning support, IDP livelihood programs — depends on continued grant and ODA flows.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a grants coordination and management framework:

   (1) **ODA absorption capacity** remains limited — BARMM cannot fully utilize available foreign assistance due to weak project preparation, counterpart fund management, and procurement capacity.

   (2) **Donor fragmentation** persists — multiple donors engage with multiple BARMM agencies without strategic coordination, leading to duplication in some sectors and neglect in others.

   (3) **Islamic development finance** remains untapped — without Shari'ah-compliant grant management procedures, BARMM cannot fully engage IsDB, OIC programs, and international Islamic charitable institutions.

   (4) **Accountability risks** increase — decentralized grant acceptance without centralized reporting creates gaps in transparency and exposes BARMM to audit findings.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the distinction between Art. XII, Sec. 26 (grants/donations) and Art. XII, Sec. 25 (ODA)?**

A. The distinction is critical:

   **Art. XII, Sec. 25 (ODA)**: Requires ***"review and approval of the National Government in accordance with Republic Act No. 8182."*** ODA is a loan, or a loan-and-grant package, that meets RA 8182 criteria: (a) promotes sustainable development, (b) contracted with governments or multilateral institutions, (c) no comparable commercial instruments available, (d) grant element of at least 25%. ODA follows the NEDA/DEPDEV Board approval process, requires counterpart fund appropriation, and triggers congressional oversight.

   **Art. XII, Sec. 26 (Grants and Donations)**: Covers **pure grants** (non-repayable transfers) — donations, contributions, bequests, gifts. For domestic and private foreign sources: no central clearance required. For foreign government and multilateral sources: Presidential clearance required but **not** the full RA 8182/NEDA Board process.

   A **grant from the World Bank or ADB** could fall under either provision depending on the instrument: if it is a pure grant (non-repayable), it falls under Art. XII, Sec. 26 (Presidential clearance). If it is an ODA package with both loan and grant components, it falls under Art. XII, Sec. 25 (RA 8182 compliance).

**Q. Who qualifies as "the President or his authorized representative" for foreign grant clearance?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2 uses the phrase ***"the President or his authorized representative."*** The "authorized representative" is not defined in the BOL. In national government practice, the President may delegate this function to a Cabinet member (typically the Secretary of Finance or the NEDA Director-General) or to the head of the Presidential Management Staff. The IFPB rules contemplated by Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 3 should clarify who serves as the authorized representative and the procedure for obtaining clearance.

**Q. Can private foreign organizations donate to BARMM agencies without Presidential clearance?**

A. Yes. Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 2 requires Presidential clearance only for ***"grants or donations from governments of foreign countries, their agencies and instrumentalities or multilateral institutions or organizations."*** Grants from **foreign private organizations** — foreign NGOs, foreign private foundations, foreign corporations, foreign philanthropic trusts, foreign individuals — fall under Art. XII, Sec. 26, par. 1 as "foreign sources" and require **no Presidential clearance**. This is a significant distinction: a donation from the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation (private) does not require Presidential clearance, while a grant from USAID (U.S. government agency) does.

**Q. What reporting obligations apply to grants received by BARMM agencies?**

A. Three layers of reporting apply:

   (1) **COA audit** — all grants are subject to constitutional audit authority. Agencies must record grants in government books per COA circulars, submit utilization reports, and account for unexpended balances.

   (2) **BAA 84 budget reporting** — grant-funded expenditures are included in the annual budget execution reports. Foreign-assisted project expenditures follow the reporting standards set by BPDA.

   (3) **Donor-specific reporting** — most grants carry their own reporting conditions specified in the grant agreement, memorandum of understanding, or cooperation framework. These vary by donor and instrument.

   A **Bangsamoro grants management framework** would add a fourth layer: centralized BARMM reporting that aggregates all grant receipts, utilization, and development impact across all MOAs.

**Q. How does the *waqf* institution relate to grants and donations?**

A. BOL Art. XII, Sec. 6(h) lists ***"endowments"*** as a Bangsamoro Government revenue source. In Islamic law, *waqf* is the primary form of endowment — an irrevocable dedication of property for a charitable or public purpose. PD 1083, Art. 104-112 provides the existing legal framework for *waqf* creation and administration. A Bangsamoro government entity could serve as a *waqf* beneficiary or *mutawalli* (administrator), receiving income from *waqf* properties dedicated to public purposes (education, health, infrastructure). This represents a **perpetual, self-sustaining** source of grants-equivalent funding that does not depend on donor cycles. Developing a *waqf* management capacity within BARMM is a distinct institutional opportunity that connects to but extends beyond the conventional grants framework.
