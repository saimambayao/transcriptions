# (z) Grants and Donations

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(z)** — "Grants and donations;"

## Power Overview

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The Bangsamoro Organic Law grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over grants and donations as one of 55 enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2(z). This power authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to accept and manage financial and in-kind contributions from domestic and foreign sources.

Article XII, Section 26 is the sole elaboration. It provides that "departments, bureaus, offices, and state universities and colleges of the Bangsamoro Government may accept donations, contributions, grants, bequests, or gifts, in cash or in kind, from domestic or foreign sources for purposes relevant to their functions." This provision is notable for its breadth — it authorizes every unit of the Bangsamoro Government, including state universities and colleges, to accept grants directly. However, the provision contains a critical limitation for foreign grants: "In case of such grants or donations from governments of foreign countries, their agencies and instrumentalities or multilateral institutions or organizations, acceptance thereof shall be subject to the prior clearance and approval by the President or his authorized representative." The Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board is tasked with promulgating rules for implementation.

This power is distinct from the loans power (Art. V, Sec. 2(ll)) in that grants and donations do not create repayment obligations. They include bilateral development assistance from foreign governments, contributions from multilateral organizations (World Bank, ADB, JICA, USAID), private philanthropic donations, and domestic corporate or individual giving.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

MFBM handles fiscal management including grant accounting, while BPDA coordinates development assistance and donor relations. The Office of the Chief Minister engages with international development partners at the executive level. There is no standalone BAA governing grants and donations; the power is exercised under BAA 13 (Administrative Code) general mandate and BAA 84 (Budget System Act) fiscal management provisions.

In practice, BARMM receives significant grants and development assistance from international organizations. JICA, USAID, World Bank, ADB, and various bilateral donors provide technical and financial assistance for BARMM governance, infrastructure, and development programs. Resolution 301 commended JICA's contributions to BARMM. These grants flow through Presidential clearance as required by the BOL, coordinated through the National Economic and Development Authority (NEDA) and the Department of Finance (DOF) for ODA-related grants.

Domestic grants — from Philippine corporations, civil society organizations, and private individuals — can be accepted directly by Bangsamoro Government units without Presidential approval. The BAA 85 (General Appropriations Act) budget structure accounts for grant-funded programs alongside block grant and locally-generated revenues.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The Presidential clearance requirement for foreign grants is one of the BOL's most explicit national law consistency provisions. Foreign government grants, multilateral organization grants, and international institutional donations all require "prior clearance and approval by the President or his authorized representative" (Art. XII, Sec. 26). This requirement ensures that the Bangsamoro Government's acceptance of foreign assistance aligns with Philippine foreign policy and existing international commitments.

RA 8182 (Official Development Assistance Act of 1996) governs ODA in the Philippines, including grant-based ODA. While Section 26 does not explicitly reference RA 8182 (that is reserved for loans in Section 25), the ODA framework applies to foreign grants that qualify as official development assistance. The NEDA Investment Coordination Committee and DOF serve as the national pipeline for ODA-related grants. The Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board, created by the BOL (Art. VI, Sec. 4; Art. XII, Sec. 37-38), is tasked with promulgating rules for the implementation of the grants provision — though whether such rules have been promulgated is not confirmed by enacted legislation.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The grants and donations power is exercised without standalone legislation. While this may suffice for routine grant acceptance, the absence of a dedicated law creates gaps in: transparency and accountability standards for grant management, a systematic donor coordination framework that aligns grant-funded programs with BDP priorities, reporting requirements for all Bangsamoro Government units that accept grants, a mechanism for ensuring that grant-funded programs are sustainable beyond the grant period, and standards for in-kind donations (equipment, supplies, technical assistance).

A Bangsamoro Grant Management and Donor Coordination Act would codify: grant acceptance procedures for domestic and foreign grants, accountability and transparency standards (financial reporting, audit requirements), donor coordination mechanisms to prevent duplication and align with BDP priorities, sustainability planning for grant-funded programs, and the role of BPDA and MFBM in grant coordination. This legislation is less urgent than unfulfilled BOL mandates (where the BOL says "Parliament shall enact") but would strengthen the institutional framework for managing what is a significant revenue source for BARMM.

### Development and Policy Connection

BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 13 (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) identifies grants, ODA, and development partner contributions as critical sources of financing for the Bangsamoro Development Plan. The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028) relies on continued international development assistance for its governance, infrastructure, and economic resilience pillars. Without a grant management framework, BARMM risks fragmented donor coordination, duplicated programs, and grant-funded projects that do not align with regional development priorities.
