# (mm) Mechanisms for Consultations for Women and Marginalized Sectors

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(mm)** — "Mechanisms for consultations for women and marginalized sectors;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over consultation mechanisms for women and marginalized sectors through Article V, Section 2(mm). The core elaborating provision is Article XIII, Section 5, which mandates the Bangsamoro Government to "establish a mechanism for consultation with women and local communities to further ensure the allocation and proper utilization of development funds." This provision links consultation directly to fiscal governance — the consultation mechanism is not merely advisory but is designed to ensure that the mandatory 5% gender and development (GAD) budget and development funds are allocated and utilized properly. The same section requires that at least 5% of each ministry and LGU's total budget appropriation be set aside for gender-responsive programs, and 5%-30% of ODA be allocated for GAD.[^1]

Article IX, Sections 11-12 further elaborate this power. Section 11 mandates the creation of a commission on women by law and requires at least one woman in the Bangsamoro Cabinet. Section 12 directs the Bangsamoro Government to "uphold and protect the fundamental rights of women, including the right to engage in lawful employment, and to be protected from exploitation, abuse, or discrimination, as embodied in the Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination Against Women." The Parliament must enact laws to implement women's protection. These provisions establish the institutional and legislative framework within which consultation mechanisms operate — they are not standalone processes but part of a broader gender mainstreaming architecture.[^2]

The power extends beyond women to "marginalized sectors" broadly. While the BOL specifies women's consultation mechanisms in Article XIII, Section 5, the authority over consultation mechanisms for marginalized sectors encompasses the elderly, persons with disabilities, indigenous peoples, youth, and other vulnerable populations. Article IX, Section 23 (special agency for persons with special needs, senior citizens, and disadvantaged groups), Article IX, Section 13 (youth commission), and Article IX, Section 3 (indigenous peoples' ministry) all create institutional points through which consultation with marginalized sectors could be formalized.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

BAA No. 8 (Women Commission) is the primary implementing legislation for this power. It creates the Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC) as the policy-making and coordinating body for gender and development. The BWC serves as the institutional mechanism through which women's consultation is conducted — it coordinates the implementation of the 5% GAD budget across all ministries and LGUs, and establishes processes for consulting women and local communities on development fund allocation. Under BAA No. 85 (Chapter XX), BWC has dedicated appropriations and staffing for its operations.[^3]

The Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BAA 10) provides a parallel consultation mechanism for youth as a marginalized sector. The BYC channels youth perspectives into policy-making, serving as the institutional bridge between the youth population and government decision-making. For indigenous peoples, MIAP (under BAA 13 and funded through BAA 85, Chapter IX) provides consultation mechanisms through its community engagement programs, and BAA No. 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act) establishes FPIC requirements that function as formalized consultation mechanisms for IP communities.[^4]

However, no standalone legislation creates a comprehensive consultation framework for all marginalized sectors. The existing mechanisms are sector-specific (women through BWC, youth through BYC, IPs through MIAP/BAA 64) rather than integrated. There is no overarching Bangsamoro Consultation Act that establishes standards, procedures, and legal requirements for government consultation with marginalized populations across all policy and budgetary decisions.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not require this power to be exercised consistent with national law. The consultation mechanisms mandated by Article XIII, Section 5 are Bangsamoro-specific institutional requirements with no direct national law counterpart that constrains them. At the national level, the Philippine Commission on Women (PCW) implements the national GAD framework under RA 9710 (Magna Carta of Women), which includes consultation requirements. The PCW's national framework and the BWC's Bangsamoro framework operate in parallel, with no formal coordination mechanism required by the BOL.[^5]

RA 9710 (Magna Carta of Women) applies suppletorily in areas where the Bangsamoro has not enacted standalone legislation. Since no comprehensive Bangsamoro Women's Code exists beyond the BWC's creation through BAA 8, many women's rights provisions in RA 9710 continue to govern in BARMM. Similarly, RA 7160 (Local Government Code) contains provisions on citizen participation and consultation at the LGU level, which apply through BAA 49 (Local Governance Code) and its interface with the national LGC framework.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The primary gap is the absence of a comprehensive Bangsamoro Consultation Framework Act. While individual commissions (BWC, BYC) and sectoral legislation (BAA 64 for IPs) create consultation mechanisms, no overarching legislation establishes: mandatory consultation standards for all government policy and budget decisions affecting marginalized sectors; minimum procedural requirements (notice periods, documentation, feedback mechanisms, incorporation of recommendations); legal effect of consultation outcomes — whether advisory or binding; coverage of all marginalized sectors in a unified framework (women, youth, elderly, PWD, IPs, settler communities); and community-level consultation mechanisms that connect grassroots voices to Parliament and ministry decision-making.[^6]

A second gap involves the enforcement of the 5% GAD budget consultation requirement. Article XIII, Section 5 mandates that consultation mechanisms ensure the "allocation and proper utilization of development funds," but no BAA establishes monitoring, reporting, and enforcement procedures for this fiscal consultation mandate. Without teeth, the consultation requirement risks becoming a formality rather than a substantive check on development spending.

A third gap concerns marginalized sectors beyond women and youth. The elderly, persons with disabilities, and settler communities lack dedicated consultation bodies or legislated consultation processes. Article IX, Section 15 mandates the creation of an office for settler communities, and Article IX, Section 23 mandates a special agency for persons with special needs and senior citizens — both of which would serve as consultation points for their respective populations. Neither has been established through standalone legislation.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 9 (Social Services), envisions participatory social development that includes the voices of marginalized populations in planning and implementation. Effective consultation mechanisms are the prerequisite for this participatory approach. Without a comprehensive consultation framework, the BDP's participatory development goals depend on ad hoc arrangements rather than systematized processes. The 5% GAD budget's impact on development outcomes is also directly dependent on the quality of consultation that informs its allocation.
