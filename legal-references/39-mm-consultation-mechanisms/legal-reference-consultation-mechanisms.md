# (mm) Mechanisms and Processes of Consultation

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(mm)** — ***"Mechanisms for consultations for women and marginalized sectors;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(mm) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national consultation or gender law. Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — establishing a constitutional floor. Art. XIII, Sec. 5 (Gender and Development) imposes substantive consultation obligations without referencing national law. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC)** — attached to the Office of the Chief Minister (BAA 8; BAA 13, Book VII, Title XVI, Ch. 1) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC); Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC); Office for Settler Communities; Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 3 (Governance); Chapter 11 (Social Development); Chapter 14 (Gender and Development) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | Parliament to enact a law recognizing the role of women in nation-building and ensuring representation in decision-making bodies (Art. IX, Sec. 11). Parliament to create by law a commission on women (Art. IX, Sec. 11, para. 2) — **fulfilled by BAA 8**. Parliament to enact laws for the protection of women (Art. IX, Sec. 12). |

## Legal Briefer

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over consultation mechanisms?**

A. Article V, Section 2(mm) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"mechanisms for consultations for women and marginalized sectors"*** as one of the 55 powers over which the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall exercise its authority."*** The chapeau of Art. V, Sec. 2 provides: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This is a broad grant covering the design, establishment, and operation of **institutional mechanisms and processes** for consulting with women and marginalized sectors — including but not limited to indigenous peoples, youth, elderly, differently-abled, labor, and settler communities.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Eight provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 5, para. 1 mandates: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall recognize the role of women in governance and ensure their fundamental equality before the law. It shall guarantee full and direct participation of women in governance and development process."*** This establishes the **substantive policy** behind the consultation power — full participation, not merely token input.[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 5, para. 2 imposes a **fiscal obligation**: ***"at least five percent (5%) of the total budget appropriation of each ministry, office, and constituent local government unit"*** for GAD, plus ***"five percent (5%) to thirty percent (30%) of the official development assistance"*** for gender and development. These funds must be informed by consultation.[^3]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 5, para. 3 is the **core directive**: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall establish a mechanism for consultation with women and local communities to further ensure the allocation and proper utilization of development funds."*** This names both the institutional requirement (a mechanism) and its purpose (ensuring fund allocation and utilization).[^4]

   (4) Art. IX, Sec. 11 mandates women's representation: ***"The Parliament shall enact a law that gives recognition to the important role of women in nation-building and regional development, ensuring the representation of women in other decision-making and policy-determining bodies of the Bangsamoro Government."***[^5]

   (5) Art. IX, Sec. 11, para. 2 mandates the BWC: ***"The Parliament shall create by law a commission on women and shall define its powers, functions, and composition."*** — fulfilled by **BAA 8**.[^6]

   (6) Art. IX, Sec. 12 embeds **CEDAW**: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall uphold and protect the fundamental rights of women ... as embodied in the Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination Against Women."***[^7]

   (7) Art. IV, Sec. 3 establishes the democratic foundation: ***"The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall have a democratic political system that allows its people to freely participate in the political processes within its territorial jurisdiction."***[^8]

   (8) Art. XIII, Sec. 2 mandates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"strengthen governance systems to ensure people's participation."***[^9]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **three express legislative mandates**:

   (1) Art. IX, Sec. 11: ***"The Parliament shall enact a law that gives recognition to the important role of women in nation-building and regional development"*** — a general women's rights law. **Partially fulfilled** by BAA 8, which focuses on the BWC's institutional mandate but does not comprehensively address all women's rights and participation mechanisms.

   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 11, para. 2: ***"The Parliament shall create by law a commission on women"*** — **fulfilled by BAA 8**.

   (3) Art. IX, Sec. 12: ***"The Parliament shall enact the necessary laws for the implementation of this section"*** — requiring legislation on women's protection. **Not yet fulfilled** with a standalone women's protection code specific to BARMM.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Art. IX, Sec. 11, para. 2 directs Parliament to ***"create by law a commission on women."*** This was fulfilled by **BAA 8**, which establishes the **Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC)** as the primary institutional mechanism for this power. The BWC is attached to the Office of the Chief Minister (BAA 8 Sec. 3; BAA 13 Sec. 2). Additionally, Art. VII, Sec. 7(c) establishes **reserved seats** for women, youth, traditional leaders, *ulama*, non-Moro indigenous peoples, and settler communities in Parliament — a structural consultation mechanism embedded in legislative governance.[^10]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places two types of conditions:

   (1) **Constitutional framework**: Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — all consultation mechanisms must operate within the constitutional framework. See the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B).

   (2) **Purpose limitation**: Art. XIII, Sec. 5 links the consultation mechanism to a specific purpose — ***"to further ensure the allocation and proper utilization of development funds."*** While this does not restrict consultation to fiscal matters alone, it identifies **budget accountability** as the primary function the mechanism must serve.

   No national law consistency requirement, presidential approval requirement, or intergovernmental coordination requirement is imposed on this power.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to consultation mechanisms for women and marginalized sectors?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: Autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This is the **universal constitutional boundary** on all 55 powers.[^11]

   (2) **Right to information (Art. III, Sec. 7)**: ***"The right of the people to information on matters of public concern shall be recognized. Access to official records, and to documents, and papers pertaining to official acts, transactions, or decisions, as well as to government research data used as basis for policy development, shall be afforded the citizen."*** Meaningful consultation requires access to information — this is the constitutional prerequisite for informed participation.[^12]

   (3) **Women's role in nation-building (Art. II, Sec. 14)**: ***"The State recognizes the role of women in nation-building, and shall ensure the fundamental equality before the law of women and men."*** This is a **state policy** that both national and Bangsamoro legislation must serve.[^13]

   (4) **Sectoral representation (Art. X, Sec. 9)**: ***"Legislative bodies of local governments shall have sectoral representation as may be prescribed by law."*** Mandates that local legislative bodies include representatives of marginalized sectors — a constitutional foundation for the consultation mechanisms in Art. V, Sec. 2(mm).[^14]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Right to participate in democratic processes** — Art. III, Sec. 1 (due process and equal protection) ensures that women and marginalized sectors cannot be arbitrarily excluded from consultation processes.

   (2) **Freedom of association** (Art. III, Sec. 8) — ***"The right of the people, including those employed in the public and private sectors, to form unions, associations, or societies for purposes not contrary to law shall not be abridged."*** Women's organizations, CSOs, and people's organizations derive their right to participate in consultation from this guarantee.

   (3) **Right to information** (Art. III, Sec. 7) — consultation without information is meaningless. Government must provide access to policy data and decisions.

   (4) **Gender equality** (Art. II, Sec. 14) — any consultation mechanism that structurally excludes or marginalizes women violates this constitutional policy.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. II, Sec. 14 mandates the State to ***"ensure the fundamental equality before the law of women and men"*** — an affirmative obligation that consultation mechanisms must implement. Art. XIII, Sec. 14 of the Constitution mandates: ***"The State shall protect working women by providing safe and healthful working conditions, taking into account their maternal functions, and such facilities and opportunities that will enhance their welfare and enable them to realize their full potential in the service of the nation."*** The Local Government Code's mandatory sectoral representation implements Art. X, Sec. 9.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for consultation with women and marginalized sectors?**

A. The national framework provides a comprehensive but fragmented system. **RA 9710** (Magna Carta of Women, 2009) is the primary national law — it mandates GAD mainstreaming, women's participation in all levels of planning and policy-making, sex-disaggregated data, and a minimum 5% GAD budget. **RA 7192** (Women in Development Act, 1992) requires women's integration as full partners in development. **RA 7160** (Local Government Code, 1991) mandates sectoral representation in local development councils and provides for initiative and referendum. **RA 8371** (IPRA, 1997) establishes FPIC for indigenous peoples. **RA 10121** (DRRM Act, 2010) requires gender-responsive disaster planning. Together, these laws provide **multiple consultation channels** — GAD focal points in every agency, sectoral seats in local legislative bodies, FPIC for IP communities, and participatory planning in DRRM.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national consultation-related laws apply suppletorily or directly:

   (1) **RA 9710** — applies suppletorily; BWC performs functions nationally assigned to PCW.
   (2) **RA 7192** — applies suppletorily; substantially superseded by BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 5 and BAA 8.
   (3) **RA 7160** — applies suppletorily to BARMM LGUs; BAA 49 provides the BARMM-specific framework.
   (4) **RA 8371** — applies alongside BAA 64; FPIC obligations remain.
   (5) **RA 9262** — applies directly (criminal law).
   (6) **RA 11313** — applies directly (criminal law).
   (7) **RA 10121** — applies suppletorily for DRRM consultation.
   (8) **RA 11032** — applies suppletorily for citizen feedback mechanisms.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. Three national institutions have consultation-related functions in BARMM: (1) the **Philippine Commission on Women (PCW)** — the national gender policy-making body, which coordinates GAD compliance nationwide; its functions within BARMM are performed by **BWC**. (2) The **National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP)** — exercises FPIC authority within BARMM alongside BAA 64 mechanisms. (3) The **Department of the Interior and Local Government (DILG)** — oversees local development council compliance with sectoral representation requirements.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC)** is the lead agency. BAA 8 (Sec. 4) defines its mandate: ***"The Commission shall promote, protect and uphold women's rights as human rights, work for the elimination of all forms of discrimination against women, ensure that legal measures are taken to promote gender justice, women's rights and welfare, and promote gender and development including the meaningful participation of women in all levels of governance, policy and decision-making."*** BAA 13, Book VII, Title XVI, Ch. 1 (Sec. 1-12) incorporates the BWC into the Administrative Code. BAA 85, Chapter XX allocates its operational budget.[^15][^16]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **BAA 8** (Bangsamoro Women Commission Act, 2020) is the primary standalone legislation. It creates the BWC as a collegial body (Chairperson + 4 regular Commissioners + 2 ex-officio Commissioners representing non-Moro indigenous peoples and settler communities) with defined powers, functions, and organizational structure. Additionally, **BAA 64** (Indigenous Peoples' Act) provides FPIC mechanisms — the strongest form of consultation — and **BAA 49** (Local Governance Code) establishes sectoral representation at the LGU level.

**Q. What are BWC's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VII, Title XVI, Ch. 1 (Sec. 1-12) defines BWC comprehensively:

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): BWC is ***"the primary policy-making, coordinating, and monitoring body of women, gender and development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^17]

   **Functions** (Sec. 7) — three clusters:

   **(1) Policy Formulation and Technical Assistance**:
   - (a) Issue guidelines for GAD plans of the Bangsamoro Government and LGUs;
   - (b) Prepare the Bangsamoro GAD Plan with relevant ministries, agencies, and stakeholders;
   - (c) Conduct research to support development of policies for women;
   - (d) Pilot special programs for women;
   - (e) Recommend policies and legislations to Parliament relating to women;
   - (f) Provide technical assistance and capacity building to agencies, LGUs, and stakeholders.

   **(2) Ensure and Monitor Compliance**:
   - (a) Monitor, assess, and ensure compliance with GAD guidelines and plans — may require all MOAs to submit GAD plans and budgets;
   - (b) Recommend measures for successful GAD plan implementation;
   - (c) Monitor compliance with **CEDAW**, **UNSCR 1325**, and all international instruments;
   - (d) Participate in reporting to international monitoring bodies.

   **(3) Coordination with Bangsamoro Government, LGUs and Stakeholders**:
   - (a) Coordinate with BPDA to generate sex-disaggregated data;
   - (b) Refer cases of women needing assistance to appropriate agencies;
   - (c) Work with law enforcement, Shari'ah and regular courts, and stakeholders to address gender-based violence;
   - (d) **Convene an advisory council** composed of: Bangsamoro ministry representatives, LGU representatives, and one woman representative each from **youth, civil society organizations, traditional leaders, Bangsamoro communities outside BARMM, and religious leaders** — this is the BWC's principal consultation mechanism;
   - (e) Coordinate with BHRC on human rights monitoring.[^18]

   **Organizational structure** (Sec. 9): Office of the Chairperson; Office of the Commissioners; Executive Director's Office (head Secretariat); Administrative and Finance Services Division; and Technical Services Division (with Policy and Program Development Unit, Monitoring and Evaluation Unit, and **Coordinating and Public Engagement Unit**).[^19]

**Q. What can BWC NOT do without standalone legislation beyond BAA 8 and BAA 13?**

A. While BAA 8 and BAA 13 give BWC a robust institutional mandate, they do not provide: a **comprehensive Gender and Development Code** with detailed rights, obligations, and enforcement mechanisms; **mandatory GAD focal points** in every BARMM ministry with defined functions and accountability; **penalty provisions** for MOAs that fail to comply with GAD budget requirements or consultation obligations; a **formal consultation framework** with defined timelines, processes, documentation requirements, and feedback mechanisms; **gender-responsive budgeting standards** with detailed methodology; or a **Bangsamoro Women's Magna Carta** that adapts RA 9710's provisions to BARMM's specific context (including Shari'ah-sensitive approaches to women's rights).

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. **Res. No. 202** calls for an **annual forum on sexual harassment and related laws** — signaling intent to institutionalize recurring consultation on women's safety. **Res. No. 238** directs implementation of Citizen's Charter and PACD — expanding citizen consultation beyond gender to all government services. **Res. No. 394** encourages participation in values transformation trainings — promoting participatory governance culture. These resolutions collectively demonstrate Parliament's recognition of consultation as a governance priority.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. **Yes** — BWC is the correct lead agency. Its BAA 8/BAA 13 mandate explicitly covers ***"the meaningful participation of women in all levels of governance, policy and decision-making"*** and the advisory council mechanism directly implements Art. XIII, Sec. 5's consultation directive. However, BWC's mandate covers only **women's consultation** (and marginalized sectors through its advisory council). For non-gender-specific marginalized sector consultation (IP communities, youth, elderly, differently-abled, labor), **additional agencies** share responsibility: the **BYC** for youth (BAA 13, Sec. 14(b) creates a youth consultative mechanism), the **Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs** for IP consultation (BAA 64), and the **BHRC** for human rights monitoring that includes marginalized sector input.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Multiple institutions:
   - **Lead agency**: Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC) — BAA 8; BAA 13, Bk. VII, Tit. XVI, Ch. 1; BAA 85, Ch. XX. **Operational**.
   - **Youth consultation**: Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC) — BAA 10; BAA 13, Bk. VII, Tit. XVI, Ch. 2. BAA 13 Sec. 14(b) mandates a ***"consultative mechanism which shall provide a forum for continuing dialogue between the Bangsamoro Government and the youth sector."*** **Operational**.
   - **IP consultation**: Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs — BAA 13; BAA 64 (FPIC mechanisms). **Operational**.
   - **Human rights monitoring**: Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC) — BAA 4; BAA 13. **Operational**.
   - **Settler communities**: Office for Settler Communities — BAA 13. Provides representation on BWC as ex-officio commissioner (BAA 8 Sec. 5). **Operational**.
   - **Parliamentary representation**: Reserved seats for women, youth, traditional leaders, *ulama*, non-Moro IPs, settler communities — Art. VII, Sec. 7(c). **Operational**.
   - **LGU-level**: Local development councils with sectoral representation — BAA 49. **Operational**.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes Parliament to create offices for youth, women, settler communities, DRRM, and planning — BWC and BYC have been created; the Office for Settler Communities exists. No major BOL-mandated institution specific to this power remains uncreated. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 5's directive to ***"establish a mechanism for consultation with women and local communities"*** envisions a **formal consultation mechanism** broader than the BWC advisory council alone — potentially a participatory planning framework that has not been legislated.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Two institutional innovations would strengthen this power:

   (1) A **Bangsamoro CSO Coordinating Office** — as proposed in **PB 399** — to systematize consultation with civil society organizations across all BARMM agencies. Currently, CSO engagement is ad hoc and agency-specific.

   (2) A **Marginalized Sectors Consultation Council** — a multi-sectoral body that convenes representatives of all marginalized sectors (women, youth, IP, elderly, differently-abled, labor, settler communities) for cross-cutting policy consultations. The BWC advisory council covers women; the BYC has a youth consultative mechanism; but no unified consultation body exists for all marginalized sectors together.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The **Philippine Commission on Women (PCW)** coordinates GAD compliance nationwide, including in BARMM — reviewing GAD plans, issuing guidelines, and monitoring RA 9710 implementation. The **NCIP** exercises FPIC authority for IP communities within BARMM. The **DILG** oversees local development council compliance with sectoral representation requirements. These national agencies operate within BARMM primarily through their field offices and coordinate with BARMM counterparts (BWC, Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs, LGUs).

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. PCW's **GAD mainstreaming program** requires all government agencies — including those operating in BARMM — to maintain GAD focal points and submit GAD plans. NCIP's **FPIC processes** are conducted within BARMM for development projects affecting ancestral domains. DILG's **local governance programs** include training on sectoral representation and participatory planning in BARMM LGUs. The **Commission on Human Rights (CHR)** operates a regional office in BARMM that monitors women's and marginalized sectors' rights.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **substantially complete** for women's consultation: **BWC** has assumed the PCW's coordination and monitoring functions within BARMM. BAA 8 and BAA 13 provide the institutional framework. For IP consultation, **BAA 64** provides the BARMM framework, though NCIP retains FPIC authority under RA 8371. For LGU-level consultation, **BAA 49** provides the BARMM local governance framework. The remaining area where national authority persists is **NCIP's FPIC jurisdiction** — where BAA 64 and RA 8371 create parallel frameworks.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate in parallel?**

A. Yes — **FPIC jurisdiction** is the primary overlap. Both NCIP (under RA 8371) and the Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (under BAA 64) exercise authority over consultation with indigenous peoples within BARMM. Additionally, **PCW** continues to monitor GAD compliance nationwide, including within BARMM, while **BWC** performs the same function regionally — creating a monitoring overlap. These overlaps are **by design** (national agencies retain general oversight) rather than by gap, but clarity on the boundary would reduce duplication.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. The BARMM has achieved **significant institutional infrastructure** for consultation:

   - **BWC operationalized**: fully operational with a Chairperson, Commissioners, Executive Director, and staff; budget under BAA 85 Ch. XX.
   - **Advisory council convened**: BWC's multi-sectoral advisory council (BAA 8 Sec. 9(3)(d)) provides a formal consultation platform for women and representative stakeholders.
   - **GAD mainstreaming**: BWC monitors all BARMM MOA compliance with the 5% GAD budget requirement.
   - **CEDAW and UNSCR 1325 monitoring**: BWC tracks BARMM compliance with international commitments.
   - **Youth consultative mechanism**: BYC operates a consultative forum for continuing dialogue with youth sector (BAA 13, Sec. 14(b)).
   - **IP consultation through BAA 64**: FPIC processes established for indigenous communities.
   - **Sectoral parliamentary representation**: Women, youth, IP, settler, traditional leader, and *ulama* seats in Parliament operational.
   - **Local development councils**: BAA 49 establishes participatory local planning with sectoral representation.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three unique advantages:

   (1) **Post-conflict consultation design**: BARMM's consultation framework is designed for a society emerging from decades of armed conflict — where women were disproportionately affected and where marginalized communities (IDPs, ex-combatant families, conflict-orphaned youth) require targeted consultation. BAA 8 explicitly commits BWC to peace-building (Sec. 2), and its advisory council includes representatives from ***"Bangsamoro communities outside BARMM"*** — recognizing diaspora communities that the national framework does not reach.

   (2) **Islamic governance legitimacy**: The *shura* principle (see the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E)) gives consultation mechanisms a dual legitimacy — constitutional and Islamic — that strengthens community acceptance, particularly in religiously observant communities where government consultation may otherwise be viewed with skepticism.

   (3) **Integrated multi-sectoral representation**: The BWC advisory council's composition (youth, CSOs, traditional leaders, diaspora, religious leaders) combined with BYC's youth consultative mechanism and BAA 64's IP consultation creates a **more comprehensive consultation ecosystem** than the national framework's fragmented approach (separate PCW, NCIP, NYC, each with their own consultation processes).

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a **comprehensive Gender and Development Code** that transforms the consultation mandate from institutional (BWC exists) to **systemic** (every agency must consult, with defined processes, timelines, and accountability). Without it:

   - The 5% GAD budget requirement has **no detailed methodology** for gender-responsive budgeting.
   - Consultation is **voluntary** for most agencies beyond the BWC's monitoring authority.
   - CSO participation lacks a **legal framework** — accreditation, rights, and obligations are undefined.
   - The advisory council mechanism is **BWC-specific** — no equivalent exists for other marginalized sectors across all BARMM agencies.
   - **Gender impact assessment** of legislation is not mandated — bills can pass without consulting affected women and communities.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 9710 (Magna Carta of Women) provides 47 sections covering every aspect of women's rights and participation — from political representation to social protection to gender mainstreaming. This has been supplemented by RA 7192, RA 9262, RA 11313, and multiple IRRs.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: PCW has been operational since 1975 (originally as NCRFW), with decades of institutional knowledge, a comprehensive GAD planning toolkit, and established coordination with all national agencies. Every national agency has a **GAD focal point** with defined functions.
   - **Regulatory systems**: Harmonized Gender and Development Guidelines (HGDG) provide a detailed methodology for gender analysis in plans, programs, and projects. The GAD Budget Policy (DBM-NEDA-PCW Joint Circular) mandates 5% GAD budget with detailed compliance procedures.
   - **Data systems**: PCW maintains the **Gender Statistics** database and the **GAD Results Database**, enabling evidence-based policy-making.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in four areas:

   (1) **No conflict-sensitive consultation**: PCW's GAD guidelines are designed for stable, peacetime governance. They do not account for consulting with **IDP women**, **ex-combatant spouses**, **conflict-orphaned youth**, or communities recovering from armed conflict. BARMM needs consultation methods that work in **security-compromised** and **trust-deficit** environments.

   (2) **No Shari'ah-sensitive framework**: National GAD mainstreaming assumes secular governance. In BARMM, where Islamic principles inform governance, consultation mechanisms must be designed to work **within** Islamic cultural norms — including gender-appropriate consultation settings, engagement with women religious leaders, and alignment with *shura* principles. The national framework does not address this.

   (3) **Language barriers**: PCW materials are in Filipino and English. Effective consultation in BARMM requires engagement in **Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, Yakan**, and other local languages.

   (4) **Diaspora consultation**: BARMM has significant Bangsamoro communities **outside the region** (Metro Manila, other Mindanao cities, overseas). BAA 8's advisory council includes diaspora representation; the national framework has no equivalent.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: GAD budget compliance methodology (Harmonized GDG); sex-disaggregated data systems; gender impact assessment tools.
   - **Adapt**: PCW's GAD planning toolkit — modify for conflict-sensitive and Shari'ah-sensitive contexts; adapt the GAD focal point system to work within BARMM's ministry structure; adapt citizen feedback mechanisms for local languages.
   - **Replace**: The institutional framework — BWC replaces PCW within BARMM; the advisory council replaces PCW's national consultation mechanisms; BARMM-specific consultation protocols replace the national one-size-fits-all approach.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Consultation mechanisms have a **direct and central Shari'ah dimension**. ***Shura*** (consultation) is one of the foundational principles of Islamic governance — referenced in Surah Ash-Shura (42:38) and Surah Al-Imran (3:159). Unlike many other powers where the Shari'ah dimension is indirect, consultation is **explicitly mandated by the Quran** as a governance methodology. This gives BARMM's consultation mechanisms a dual mandate: the BOL's constitutional-legal requirement AND the Islamic governance principle of *shura*. This dual legitimacy is a **unique strength** for BARMM — consultation is not merely a legal compliance exercise but a fulfillment of Islamic governance ideals.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. XIII, Sec. 5 or Art. V, Sec. 2(mm). However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact laws on Shari'ah matters, including ***"commercial and other civil actions not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083."*** Governance consultation processes — institutional design, advisory bodies, participatory mechanisms — are civil in nature and could be grounded in Shari'ah principles. Additionally, Art. IV, Sec. 3's democratic political system and Art. XIII, Sec. 2's people's participation mandate are **consistent with** *shura* without explicitly invoking it.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Four Islamic principles directly govern this power:

   (1) ***Shura*** — the primary principle. Governance decisions should be made through consultation with the people. This applies to all sectors, including women — contrary to cultural practices that exclude women, the Islamic mandate for *shura* is **universal** and does not exclude any segment of the community.

   (2) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — inclusive consultation serves the common good by ensuring policies reflect actual community needs rather than elite preferences.

   (3) ***'Adl*** (justice/equity) — consultation mechanisms must provide **equitable access** to all sectors. Excluding marginalized voices from governance produces injustice.

   (4) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — government officials who fail to consult their constituents violate the trust placed in them.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) does not specifically address governance consultation mechanisms. **BOL Art. X, Sec. 4** provides the legislative basis for Shari'ah-informed governance. No known *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address consultation mechanisms, though the principle of *shura* is well-established in Islamic scholarship. **Shari'ah courts** do not have jurisdiction over governance consultation disputes — these fall within the parliamentary and administrative domains.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro consultation legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is **principled integration** — legislation guided by Islamic values without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance procedures. Specifically: (a) the preamble or policy section of any Gender and Development Code should reference ***shura*** as a governance foundation alongside the constitutional and BOL mandates; (b) consultation settings should accommodate **gender-appropriate** participation methods consistent with Islamic cultural norms (e.g., separate consultation sessions where culturally preferred, though not mandated); (c) engagement with **women religious scholars** and ***alimat*** should be institutionalized as part of the consultation mechanism; and (d) consultation methodologies should respect the role of ***ulama*** and traditional leaders while ensuring they do not override women's direct participation rights under the BOL.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM has **broad authority to diverge** because: (1) the BOL **expressly grants** this as an enumerated power with no national law consistency requirement; (2) the BOL's Art. XIII, Sec. 5 mandates a **BARMM-specific** consultation mechanism — not adoption of the national framework; (3) the Shari'ah dimension (as established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E)) provides an **additional basis for distinctiveness** that the national framework does not accommodate.

   The **limits of divergence** are defined by the **constitutional floor** established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B): Art. II, Sec. 14 (gender equality), Art. III, Sec. 1 (equal protection and due process), Art. III, Sec. 7 (right to information), and Art. X, Sec. 9 (sectoral representation). BARMM can go **above** these floors (more inclusive, more robust consultation) but not **below** them. Any consultation mechanism must provide: (a) **equal access** for women and marginalized sectors (not merely formal, but substantive); (b) **transparency** — access to the information needed for meaningful participation; (c) **due process** — consultation processes must provide notice, opportunity to participate, and a feedback mechanism.

   In practice, BARMM can: design consultation mechanisms that incorporate ***shura*** principles; create **conflict-sensitive** consultation processes suited to post-conflict communities; establish **language-accessible** consultation in local languages; require **Shari'ah-sensitive** consultation settings; mandate **gender impact assessment** of all legislation; and create a **CSO engagement framework** unique to BARMM's civil society landscape.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For women and marginalized sectors within BARMM**: They gain stronger, more context-appropriate consultation rights — conflict-sensitive, culturally adapted, and Shari'ah-informed. The risk is minimal because the divergence is **additive** (more consultation, not less).

   (2) **For CSOs operating across BARMM and non-BARMM areas**: They may face different accreditation and participation requirements. A CSO registered nationally that operates in BARMM may need to comply with BARMM-specific consultation protocols.

   (3) **For national agencies**: PCW, NCIP, and DILG retain general oversight authority but must coordinate with BWC, MIPA, and BARMM LGUs respectively. Clear coordination protocols would prevent duplication.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. BAA governs within BARMM; national law applies suppletorily where BAA is silent. RA 9710 (Magna Carta of Women) would continue to apply as a **minimum standard** — BARMM legislation can exceed but cannot fall below its protections. Criminal laws (RA 9262, RA 11313) apply directly regardless of BAA enactment.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Two overlaps exist: (1) **PCW-BWC monitoring overlap** — both agencies monitor GAD compliance within BARMM. PCW's national mandate and BWC's regional mandate create parallel monitoring streams with no formal coordination protocol. (2) **NCIP-MIPA FPIC overlap** — both agencies exercise authority over IP consultation within BARMM. BAA 64 provides the BARMM framework, but RA 8371 has not been superseded. A legislative or intergovernmental clarification of boundaries is needed.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Six pending PBs partially address consultation gaps:

   - **PB 165** (System of Initiative) — would create direct democratic participation.
   - **PB 319** (CSO Registration) — would formalize CSO engagement.
   - **PB 320** (Public Petitions) — would create a parliamentary petition mechanism.
   - **PB 399** (CSO Coordinating Office) — would institutionalize CSO engagement across all agencies.
   - **PB 301** and **PB 382** — would create women's service delivery facilities.

   Even if all passed, **two critical gaps remain**: (a) no **comprehensive Gender and Development Code** that transforms GAD from a budget allocation requirement to a systemic governance framework; and (b) no **unified Consultation and Participation Act** that defines the legal right to consultation, establishes mandatory processes, and creates accountability for non-compliance across all BARMM agencies and for all marginalized sectors.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**: Chapter 3 (Governance) depends on consultation mechanisms to deliver **participatory governance** — one of the core governance reforms targeted by the plan. Chapter 14 (Gender and Development) requires gender-responsive consultation in all planning and budgeting processes. Chapter 11 (Social Development) depends on marginalized sector consultation to ensure social services reach the most vulnerable. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026): **Pillar 1 (Good Governance)** — participatory governance and citizen engagement are core to the Matatag agenda, and consultation mechanisms are the delivery system. **Pillar 4 (Social Well-Being)** — women and marginalized sector consultation directly informs social well-being programs and resource allocation.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **comprehensive Gender and Development Code** and a **Consultation and Participation Act**: the 5% GAD budget requirement remains **procedurally unregulated** — agencies comply in form but may not in substance because no detailed methodology exists. **Gender impact assessment** of legislation cannot be mandated — bills affecting women's lives pass without systematic consultation. **CSO participation** remains at the discretion of individual agencies — no legal right to consultation exists. The **advisory council** mechanism under BAA 8 operates within BWC's authority only — other agencies have no obligation to establish equivalent consultation channels. **Marginalized sectors beyond women** (differently-abled, elderly, labor) have no dedicated consultation mechanism beyond the LGU-level development councils. The **BDP Chapter 14 targets** for gender-responsive governance depend on systemic GAD mainstreaming that BAA 8's institutional mandate alone cannot deliver.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does power (mm) "consultation mechanisms" differ from power (r) "development programs and laws for women"?**

A. Power (r) — ***"Development programs and laws for women, labor, the youth, the elderly, the differently-abled, and indigenous peoples"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(r)) — is the **substantive** power to create programs and laws benefiting these sectors. Power (mm) is the **procedural** power to establish how these sectors are consulted in governance decisions. They are complementary: power (r) determines **what** the government does for women and marginalized sectors; power (mm) determines **how** those sectors participate in deciding what gets done. BWC straddles both powers — its advisory council implements (mm), while its policy formulation and program functions implement (r).

**Q. What is the relationship between consultation mechanisms and parliamentary reserved seats?**

A. Art. VII, Sec. 7(c) provides reserved seats for women, youth, traditional leaders, *ulama*, non-Moro IPs, and settler communities. These are **structural representation mechanisms** — they ensure marginalized voices are present in the **legislative** process. The consultation mechanisms under Art. V, Sec. 2(mm) and Art. XIII, Sec. 5 are broader — they require engagement in **executive** decision-making (planning, budgeting, program design, fund utilization). Parliamentary representation provides a voice in law-making; consultation mechanisms provide a voice in governance. Both are necessary because legislative representation alone does not ensure executive consultation.

**Q. How does FPIC for indigenous peoples relate to consultation mechanisms for marginalized sectors?**

A. **FPIC** (free, prior and informed consent) under Art. IX, Sec. 3 and BAA 64 is the **strongest form** of consultation — it requires **consent**, not merely input. For non-IP marginalized sectors, the BOL mandates consultation (***"establish a mechanism for consultation"***) but does not require consent. This creates a **hierarchy of consultation rights**: IP communities have a right to say "no" (through FPIC); women and other marginalized sectors have a right to be heard (through consultation) but not to veto. Any comprehensive consultation legislation should clarify this distinction and consider whether certain decisions — such as displacement, relocation, or elimination of social services — should require higher-than-standard consultation for all affected marginalized communities, not just IPs.

**Q. Why does the BOL specifically link consultation mechanisms to "allocation and proper utilization of development funds" in Art. XIII, Sec. 5?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 5's directive to ***"establish a mechanism for consultation with women and local communities to further ensure the allocation and proper utilization of development funds"*** ties consultation directly to **fiscal accountability**. This is deliberate — it reflects the BOL's understanding that women and marginalized communities are most vulnerable to resource diversion, misallocation, and exclusion from development spending. The 5% GAD budget mandate in the same section creates a **fiscal obligation** that the consultation mechanism is designed to **monitor and enforce**. Without the consultation mechanism, the GAD budget becomes a paper compliance exercise. This fiscal linkage also provides **standing** for consultation — communities are not merely expressing opinions; they are exercising oversight over public funds allocated for their benefit.

**Q. How should the Bangsamoro Government operationalize *shura* within the consultation framework?**

A. As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), *shura* provides Islamic legitimacy for consultation. Operationalizing it means: (a) **naming *shura* in policy documents** as a governance principle alongside the BOL mandate — this increases acceptance in religiously observant communities; (b) **engaging *ulama* and *alimat* (women religious scholars)** in consultation processes, not as replacements for women's direct participation but as additional participants who can bridge religious and governance discourse; (c) **using existing community structures** — *masjid* committees, *madrasa* networks, tribal councils — as consultation channels in addition to formal government mechanisms; (d) **ensuring women's direct access** — *shura* historically included women (the Prophet Muhammad consulted Umm Salamah at Hudaybiyyah), and this historical precedent should be cited against cultural arguments for excluding women from consultation.
