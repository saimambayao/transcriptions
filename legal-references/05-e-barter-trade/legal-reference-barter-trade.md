## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis in the style of Philippine . Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over barter trade and countertrade?**

A. Article V, Section 2(e) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"barter trade and countertrade"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a **unique power** — it grants the Bangsamoro Government regulatory authority over two distinct forms of cross-border trade: **traditional barter trade** with BIMP-EAGA states and **countertrade** with ASEAN states. No other Philippine LGU or autonomous region has been granted this authority.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power?**

A. Four provisions elaborate:

   (1) **Art. XIII, Sec. 26** — the primary implementing provision. Defines scope (barter with BIMP-EAGA, countertrade with ASEAN), imposes the customs duty constraint, and requires ASEAN compliance for countertrade.[^2]

   (2) **Art. XIII, Sec. 28** — customs cooperation between the Bangsamoro Government and National Government for economic zones; relevant by analogy to barter trade customs enforcement, though not a direct barter trade mandate.[^3]

   (3) **Art. XII, Sec. 28** — international economic linkages with countries having diplomatic relations, subject to National Government review.[^4]

   (4) **Art. XIII, Sec. 24** — general trade and industry framework positioning the private sector as the mover of trade.[^5]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation?**

A. No — unlike other powers (e.g., cooperative code, mining policy), the BOL does **not expressly name** a specific piece of legislation for barter trade. Art. XIII, Sec. 26 uses ***"shall regulate"*** — granting regulatory authority but not mandating a specific code by name. However, effective exercise of this power requires legislation to establish registration systems, permitted goods lists, trading post designations, and enforcement mechanisms.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three express limitations:

   (1) **Geographic scope** — barter trade is limited to ***"BIMP-EAGA Member States"*** (Brunei, Indonesia, Malaysia, Philippines); countertrade is limited to ***"ASEAN Member States."*** The Bangsamoro Government cannot regulate barter or countertrade with non-BIMP-EAGA or non-ASEAN countries under this provision.[^2]

   (2) **Customs duty constraint** — goods traded ***"shall not be sold elsewhere in the country without the payment of appropriate customs or import duties in accordance with existing national laws."*** This means barter-traded goods entering the domestic Philippine market must clear customs — the Bangsamoro Government cannot waive national customs duties.[^2]

   (3) **ASEAN compliance** — countertrade ***"shall be in accordance with the spirit, intent, goals, processes, modalities, and arrangements in the ASEAN economic integration agreements."*** BARMM countertrade must conform to the Philippines' international trade commitments.[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not create a specific barter trade authority. MTIT is the institutional home by virtue of its general trade mandate under BAA 13. A standalone Barter Trade Act could create a dedicated Barter Trade Authority or division within MTIT.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply?**

A. Three categories:

   (1) **Foreign affairs prerogative (Art. VII, Sec. 21)**: Treaty-making is a national prerogative. Barter trade arrangements touching international commitments (BIMP-EAGA framework, ASEAN agreements) must operate within the foreign affairs power of the National Government.

   (2) **Customs authority (Art. VI, Sec. 28(2))**: Congress has the power to set tariff rates and customs duties. The BOL's customs duty constraint (Art. XIII, Sec. 26) reflects this constitutional distribution — BARMM regulates the trade but cannot waive nationally-set customs duties.

   (3) **Autonomous region framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: The organic act may provide legislative powers over ***"Economic, social, and tourism development"*** — barter trade regulation falls within this constitutional authorization.

**Q. What is the constitutional floor?**

A. The constitutional floor is: (a) **national customs authority** — cannot be overridden by BARMM legislation; (b) **foreign affairs prerogative** — barter trade regulation cannot create independent foreign trade relationships outside the Philippines' diplomatic framework; (c) **equal protection** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — barter trade regulation must not discriminate unreasonably against traders.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist?**

A. No specific constitutional mandate exists for barter trade. The authority derives from the organic act provision for economic development powers (Art. X, Sec. 20) as implemented through the BOL (Art. V, Sec. 2(e); Art. XIII, Sec. 26).

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the national framework provide?**

A. The national framework has an unusual history: **RA 3524** (1963) **prohibited** barter in international trade. This prohibition was effectively lifted for Mindanao through executive orders — **EO 427** (1990) placed barter trade supervision under ARMM for Jolo; **EO 313** (1996) legalized and regulated barter trade across Mindanao; **EO 64** (2011) revived and modernized the framework. The BOL now grants BARMM express authority that supersedes this executive order framework.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. Three national instruments apply:
   - **RA 10863 (CMTA)** — applies directly for customs duty enforcement on barter-traded goods entering the domestic market.
   - **EO 313 (1996) and EO 64 (2011)** — apply suppletorily as the baseline regulatory framework pending BARMM legislation.
   - **RA 12022 (2024)** — anti-smuggling enforcement for illegal diversion of barter-traded goods.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority?**

A. The **Bureau of Customs** exercises customs duty collection and anti-smuggling enforcement at designated barter trading posts. The **DTI** provides general trade policy coordination. The **Mindanao Barter Trade Council** (EO 64) provides an inter-agency coordination mechanism. Upon enactment of a Bangsamoro Barter Trade Act, MTIT would assume regulatory functions, but customs enforcement would remain a joint responsibility (by analogy with Art. XIII, Sec. 28's economic zones customs cooperation framework).

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry is primarily responsible?**

A. **MTIT** is the lead ministry by virtue of its general trade mandate. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Sec. 3 enumerates 26 powers and functions covering trade, investments, and tourism — but **barter trade is not specifically listed** among these functions. MTIT's authority over barter trade derives from the general trade mandate and the BOL's Art. XIII, Sec. 26 grant.[^6]

**Q. What are MTIT's relevant functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Sec. 3 provides general trade functions relevant to barter trade:
   - (a) ***"Formulate policies, plans, programs and projects for the development of the trade, investments, and tourism in the region"***
   - (v) ***"Promote domestic trade, marketing and distributions to ensure the rational, economical and steady flow of commodities from producing and/or marketing centers to areas where there is a shortage of supply"***
   - (z) ***"Exercise such other powers and functions as necessary, proper, or incidental to the attainment of its mandate"***[^6]

No specific barter trade regulatory functions — registration, permitted goods lists, trading post designation, or enforcement — are enumerated.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Does MTIT have a dedicated division for barter trade?**

A. No. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIV, Sec. 10 lists MTIT's organizational structure but does not include a dedicated barter trade division. A Bangsamoro Barter Trade Act should create either a **Barter Trade Division** within MTIT or a dedicated **Bangsamoro Barter Trade Authority** attached to MTIT.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority?**

A. The national government exercises authority through: (a) **Bureau of Customs** — collects customs duties on barter-traded goods and enforces anti-smuggling laws at designated barter trading posts; (b) **Mindanao Barter Trade Council** (EO 64) — inter-agency coordination body; (c) **Philippine Coast Guard and Philippine National Police Maritime Group** — enforcement against illegal barter trade and smuggling. The barter trading posts historically designated in Sulu (Jolo) are no longer within BARMM territory; BARMM's barter trade infrastructure must be developed in its remaining island provinces (Tawi-Tawi, Basilan) and mainland coastal areas.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has BARMM accomplished?**

A. BARMM has **institutionally established** MTIT under BAA 13 but has **not enacted any standalone legislation** for barter trade. No Bangsamoro Barter Trade Act, no designated barter trading posts, no registration system, and no permitted goods list have been established under Bangsamoro law. The power remains **entirely unexercised** through dedicated legislation.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back?**

A. Three factors: (1) **No enabling legislation** — Art. XIII, Sec. 26 grants the power but MTIT has no specific regulatory authority codified in Bangsamoro law; (2) **Sulu's exclusion** — the traditional center of barter trade (Jolo) is no longer in BARMM, reducing the immediate commercial pressure to legislate; (3) **Customs coordination complexity** — effective barter trade regulation requires a formal agreement with the Bureau of Customs under Art. XIII, Sec. 28, which has not been established.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government through EO 313 and EO 64 established: designated barter trading posts; a registration system for barter traders; a list of permitted goods; the Mindanao Barter Trade Council for inter-agency coordination; and customs duty processing protocols. BARMM has **none of these** under its own authority.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. (1) The existing framework is designed for Mindanao-wide barter trade centered on Zamboanga and Jolo — not on BARMM's specific trading communities. (2) The framework does not account for the **Islamic commercial traditions** (*bay' al-muqayadah*) that underpin Bangsamoro barter practice. (3) The institutional structure (Mindanao Barter Trade Council) does not give BARMM governance representation proportional to its role in traditional barter trade.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Traditional barter trade in the Bangsamoro homeland — particularly in the Sulu Archipelago, Tawi-Tawi, and Basilan — has been practiced for centuries by Muslim traders as *bay' al-muqayadah* (goods-for-goods exchange). This is a **Shari'ah-compliant form of commerce** with deep historical and cultural roots in the Bangsamoro identity. The Tausug, Sama, and Yakan peoples have maintained barter relationships with Sabah (Malaysia), Kalimantan (Indonesia), and Brunei long before modern national boundaries existed.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant?**

A. Three *fiqh* principles are relevant:

   (1) ***Bay' al-muqayadah*** (barter sale) — permissible provided goods are specified, exchange is completed, and parties are not coerced.

   (2) **Prohibition of *gharar*** (excessive uncertainty) — barter contracts must have clear terms regarding goods, quantities, and quality.

   (3) **Prohibition of *riba*** — relevant to countertrade financing instruments that should avoid interest-based mechanisms.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated?**

A. A Bangsamoro Barter Trade Act should: (a) recognize the **historical legitimacy** of *bay' al-muqayadah* in the preamble; (b) ensure that registration requirements do not criminalize traditional Islamic commerce; (c) provide **Shari'ah-compliant countertrade financing** options (e.g., *murabaha* trade credit) for BARMM traders engaging in countertrade with ASEAN states.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can BARMM diverge from national barter trade regulation?**

A. BARMM has **moderate** authority to diverge. The BOL grants express regulatory authority (***"shall regulate"***), but imposes two hard constraints: (a) **customs duties** per ***"existing national laws"*** cannot be waived — this is a **non-negotiable national law consistency requirement**; (b) **ASEAN compliance** for countertrade — BARMM cannot create countertrade arrangements inconsistent with the Philippines' ASEAN commitments.

Within these constraints, BARMM can: establish its **own barter trading posts**; create a distinct **registration and permit system**; define its own **permitted goods list**; impose **additional quality and safety standards**; and establish **Shari'ah-compliant barter trade frameworks** — all without needing to mirror the EO 313/EO 64 framework.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) For **traders**: a BARMM barter trade system could offer simpler registration, culturally appropriate processes, and proximity to traditional trading communities — potentially encouraging formalization of currently informal trade. (2) For **customs enforcement**: dual compliance is inevitable — BARMM registration for trade regulation, national customs clearance for goods entering the domestic market. A well-designed system would integrate both into a single process at designated barter trading posts.

**Q. What cross-references from other themes constrain divergence?**

A. The customs constraint from the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A) and the constitutional foreign affairs prerogative from the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) are the binding limits. BARMM cannot create an independent international trade regime — only regulate traditional barter and countertrade within the BIMP-EAGA/ASEAN framework specified by the BOL.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between the **Bureau of Customs** (national customs enforcement) and **MTIT** (trade regulation). The BOL's Art. XIII, Sec. 28 establishes customs cooperation for economic zones (not directly for barter trade), but no barter-trade-specific customs protocol has been established. Additionally, the **Mindanao Barter Trade Council** (EO 64) operates region-wide, potentially overlapping with MTIT's regulatory authority within BARMM.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **No pending Parliament Bill** addresses barter trade and countertrade. This is one of the powers with the **least legislative attention** — no filed PB, no resolution, no legislative committee action identified. The power has been completely dormant legislatively.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 7** (Economic Development) identifies cross-border trade facilitation within the BIMP-EAGA framework as a strategy for economic growth. **BDP Chapter 13** (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) identifies international economic cooperation, including trade linkages, as a source of revenue and livelihood. **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)** of the Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda depends on formalizing traditional trade channels to build economic resilience.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without legislation: (1) **Informal barter trade continues unregulated** — traditional Bangsamoro traders operate outside the formal economy, missing revenue capture opportunities and creating smuggling risks. (2) **BIMP-EAGA integration stalls** — BARMM cannot formally participate in BIMP-EAGA trade facilitation programs without a regulatory framework. (3) **Countertrade potential unrealized** — ASEAN countertrade opportunities (e.g., exchange of Bangsamoro agricultural products for manufactured goods) cannot be structured without a legal framework. (4) **Revenue leakage** — without a formal barter trade registration system, the Bangsamoro Government cannot capture fees, permits, or data on cross-border trade volumes.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What makes this power unique among the 55 BOL powers?**

A. Barter trade and countertrade is **the only power** that grants the Bangsamoro Government direct regulatory authority over a form of **international trade**. While the power is constrained by customs and ASEAN compliance requirements, it effectively gives BARMM a role in cross-border commerce that no other Philippine LGU or autonomous region possesses. This reflects the **historical reality** of Bangsamoro maritime trade networks — the Sulu Sultanate and Maranao traders maintained commerce with Southeast Asian neighbors for centuries before the Philippines existed as a nation-state.

**Q. How does the loss of Sulu affect this power?**

A. Sulu's exclusion from BARMM territory (per *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea*, G.R. Nos. 242255, 243246, 243693 (Sept. 9, 2024; finalized Nov. 26, 2024); EO 91, s. 2025, realigning Sulu to Region IX) significantly reduces the traditional barter trade infrastructure available to BARMM. Jolo was the **primary barter trading center** under EO 313 and EO 427. BARMM must now develop barter trade infrastructure in **Tawi-Tawi** (closest to Sabah), **Basilan** (closest to Zamboanga trade routes), and potentially **coastal Maguindanao** and **Cotabato City** for BIMP-EAGA land-based trade.

**Q. What is the relationship between barter trade and the customs cooperation requirement?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 26 grants BARMM the power to ***"regulate"*** barter trade, while Art. XIII, Sec. 28 requires ***"cooperation on customs, immigration, and quarantine services."*** This creates a **bifurcated authority model**: BARMM regulates who can trade, what can be traded, where trading posts are located, and how trade is conducted; the **National Government** (through the Bureau of Customs) enforces customs duties on goods entering the domestic market. A Bangsamoro Barter Trade Act should establish a **joint customs processing mechanism** at designated barter trading posts where BARMM trade permits and national customs clearance are processed simultaneously.

**Q. How does this power interact with power (u) Economic zones and free ports?**

A. Power (u) — ***"economic zones, industrial centers, and free ports"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(u)) — and power (e) are complementary. Art. XIII, Sec. 28 establishes customs cooperation for economic zones — a similar framework would need to be established for barter trade through standalone legislation. A **barter trade zone** could be established within a Bangsamoro economic zone, combining the barter trade regulatory authority of power (e) with the fiscal incentives of power (u).
