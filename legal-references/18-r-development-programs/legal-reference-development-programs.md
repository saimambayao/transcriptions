# (r) Development Programs and Laws for Women, Labor, the Youth, the Elderly, the Differently-Abled, and Indigenous Peoples

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(r)** — ***"Development programs and laws for women, labor, the youth, the elderly, the differently-abled, and indigenous peoples;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(r) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Partial — labor programs must be ***"in accordance with the Constitution and the Labor Code of the Philippines"*** (Art. IX, Sec. 10); IP programs must not diminish RA 8371 (IPRA) rights (Art. IX, Sec. 3, last para.). No express consistency requirement for women, youth, elderly, or disability programs. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD)** — overall social protection; **Ministry of Labor and Employment (MOLE)** — labor development programs |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC); Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC); Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIAP); Ministry of Health (MOH) — health-related programs for vulnerable groups |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 9 (Social Services); Chapter 5 (Governance and Institutional Development) |
| **Priority Code** | None named — but the BOL mandates creation of commissions for women and youth, a ministry for indigenous peoples, and a special agency for persons with special needs, senior citizens, and disadvantaged groups |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over development programs for vulnerable populations?**

A. Article V, Section 2(r) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"development programs and laws for women, labor, the youth, the elderly, the differently-abled, and indigenous peoples"*** as one of the 55 powers over which the Bangsamoro Government exercises authority. This is an **umbrella power** that encompasses both **programmatic authority** (development programs) and **legislative authority** (laws) for six specifically named population groups. The power is unusually broad — it cuts across multiple sectoral ministries and carries some of the heaviest legislative mandates in the BOL.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Ten provisions elaborate on this power across four BOL articles:

   (1) **Women** — Art. IX, Sec. 11 mandates at least one woman in the Cabinet, Parliament to enact a law recognizing women's role, and Parliament to create by law a ***"commission on women."*** Art. IX, Sec. 12 mandates protection of women's rights consistent with the ***"Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination Against Women"*** (CEDAW). Art. XIII, Sec. 5 establishes the ***"five percent (5%)"*** GAD budget requirement and mandates consultation mechanisms with women.[^2][^3][^4]

   (2) **Youth** — Art. IX, Sec. 13 mandates Parliament to ***"create by law a commission on youth and shall define its powers, functions, and composition."***[^5]

   (3) **Children** — Art. IX, Sec. 14 mandates the Bangsamoro Government to ***"respect, protect, and promote the rights of children, especially orphans of tender age"*** and requires policies taking into ***"utmost consideration the best interest of children."***[^6]

   (4) **Elderly and PWD** — Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandates the establishment of ***"a special agency, support facilities and livelihood or skills training for persons with special needs, and other disadvantaged persons for their rehabilitation and productive integration into mainstream society."***[^7]

   (5) **Indigenous peoples** — Art. IX, Sec. 3 enumerates eight protected rights and mandates creation of ***"a ministry for indigenous peoples."***[^8]

   (6) **Labor** — Art. IX, Sec. 10 guarantees fundamental rights of workers ***"in accordance with the Constitution and the Labor Code of the Philippines"*** but permits Parliament to ***"pass labor laws that expand or enhance the rights stated herein."***[^9]

   (7) **Settler communities** — Art. IX, Sec. 15 mandates Parliament to ***"create an office for settler communities."***[^10]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL contains **at least six express legislative mandates**:

   (1) Art. IX, Sec. 11: Parliament shall ***"enact a law that gives recognition to the important role of women"*** and ***"create by law a commission on women."*** Partially fulfilled — BWC created by BAA 8 but no comprehensive women's development code.[^2]

   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 12: Parliament shall ***"enact the necessary laws"*** for women's protection. Not fulfilled through standalone legislation.[^3]

   (3) Art. IX, Sec. 13: Parliament shall ***"create by law a commission on youth."*** Fulfilled by BAA 10.[^5]

   (4) Art. IX, Sec. 23: Parliament shall establish ***"a special agency"*** for persons with special needs. **Not fulfilled as of BAA 89.**[^7]

   (5) Art. IX, Sec. 15: Parliament shall ***"create an office for settler communities."*** **Not fulfilled as of BAA 89.**[^10]

   (6) Art. IX, Sec. 10: Parliament ***"may pass labor laws that expand or enhance"*** worker rights. Fulfilled by BAA 82.[^9]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL directly creates or mandates creation of: (a) a **commission on women** (Art. IX, Sec. 11 — created by BAA 8 as BWC); (b) a **commission on youth** (Art. IX, Sec. 13 — created by BAA 10 as BYC); (c) a **ministry for indigenous peoples** (Art. IX, Sec. 3 — MIAP under BAA 13); (d) a **special agency** for persons with special needs (Art. IX, Sec. 23 — **not yet created**); (e) an **office for settler communities** (Art. IX, Sec. 15 — **not yet created**). Art. VII, Sec. 42 confirms Parliament's authority to create offices ***"for the youth, women, settler communities."***[^11]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Two specific limitations apply:

   (1) **Labor**: Art. IX, Sec. 10 requires labor programs to be ***"in accordance with the Constitution and the Labor Code of the Philippines."*** However, Parliament may ***"pass labor laws that expand or enhance"*** these rights — a one-way ratchet. BAA 82 exercises this authority.[^9]

   (2) **Indigenous peoples**: Art. IX, Sec. 3 (last para.) provides that ***"This Organic Law shall not in any manner diminish the rights and benefits of the non-Moro indigenous peoples... under the Constitution, national laws, particularly Republic Act No. 8371."*** IPRA serves as an irreducible floor.[^8]

   No express consistency requirement applies to women, youth, elderly, or disability programs. The general chapeau limitation — ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — applies to all 55 powers.[^12]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to development programs for vulnerable populations?**

A. Five categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: the universal constitutional boundary on all 55 powers.[^12]

   (2) **Social Justice and Human Rights (Art. XIII)**: Sec. 1 mandates Congress to ***"give highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity."*** Sec. 11 protects women: ***"The State shall provide women workers with opportunities that will enhance their welfare and enable them to realize their full potential in the service of the nation."*** Sec. 14 establishes the role of women in nation-building.[^13]

   (3) **Education, Science and Technology, Arts, Culture, and Sports (Art. XIV)**: Sec. 2 — the State shall ***"protect and promote the right of all citizens to quality education at all levels."*** Relevant to youth and children's development programs.

   (4) **Bill of Rights (Art. III)**: Sec. 1 (equal protection and due process) — all development programs must treat similarly situated persons equally. Sec. 4 (freedom of expression) and Sec. 8 (freedom of association) protect the organizational rights of women's groups, labor unions, youth organizations, and IP communities.

   (5) **State Policies (Art. II)**: Sec. 9 — ***"The State shall promote a just and dynamic social order."*** Sec. 13 — the State recognizes the ***"vital role of the youth in nation-building."*** Sec. 14 — the State recognizes the ***"role of women in nation-building."***

**Q. What constitutional mandates for legislation or institutions exist?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 13 mandates Congress to establish an independent office for ***"the protection and enforcement of rights and quality standards for persons with disabilities."*** This is the constitutional basis for RA 7277. Art. XIII, Sec. 14 mandates protection of the ***"rights of women."*** These constitutional mandates carry weight even without specific BOL consistency requirements.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for vulnerable populations?**

A. The national framework is extensive:

   - **Women**: RA 9710 (Magna Carta of Women), RA 9262 (Anti-VAWC), RA 7192 (Women in Development), RA 10354 (RH Act)
   - **Labor**: PD 442 (Labor Code), supplemented by BAA 82 within BARMM
   - **Youth**: RA 8044 (Youth in Nation-Building Act), RA 10917 (Filipino Youth Entrepreneurship Act)
   - **Children**: RA 7610 (Anti-Child Abuse), RA 9231 (Anti-Child Labor), RA 9344 (Juvenile Justice), RA 10821 (Children's Emergency Relief), RA 11596 (Anti-Child Marriage)
   - **Elderly**: RA 9994 (Expanded Senior Citizens Act), RA 7876 (Senior Citizens Centers)
   - **PWD**: RA 7277 (Magna Carta for Disabled Persons, as amended by RA 9442 and RA 10524)
   - **Indigenous peoples**: RA 8371 (IPRA) — expressly protected by the BOL

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national laws apply suppletorily except where superseded by a BAA:

   - **Superseded**: The national Labor Code is supplemented and expanded by **BAA 82** for matters within BARMM. RA 8371 (IPRA) is supplemented by **BAA 64** (IP Act).
   - **Directly applicable**: Criminal laws (RA 9262, RA 7610, RA 9231, RA 11596) apply directly.
   - **Suppletory**: RA 7277 (PWD), RA 9994 (elderly), RA 9710 (women), RA 9344 (juvenile justice), RA 10354 (RH) — apply in BARMM pending Bangsamoro-specific legislation.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for these populations?**

A. The **Philippine Commission on Women (PCW)**, **Council for the Welfare of Children (CWC)**, **National Youth Commission (NYC)**, **National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP)**, **National Council on Disability Affairs (NCDA)**, and **Office of Senior Citizens Affairs (OSCA)** continue to operate in BARMM alongside their Bangsamoro counterparts. The **Department of Labor and Employment (DOLE)** retains certain functions pending complete devolution to MOLE.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. This power is distributed across multiple institutions:
   - **MSSD** — lead for elderly, PWD, children, and disadvantaged groups (BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII, Ch. 1, Sec. 1-18; BAA 85, Ch. IV)
   - **MOLE** — lead for labor development programs (BAA 13, Title IX, Ch. 1, Sec. 1-13; BAA 85, Ch. VI)
   - **BWC** — lead for women's programs (BAA 8; BAA 85, Ch. XX)
   - **BYC** — lead for youth programs (BAA 10; BAA 85, Ch. XIX)
   - **MIAP** — lead for indigenous peoples (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VII; BAA 64; BAA 85, Ch. IX)

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. Eight standalone BAAs: **BAA 8** (BWC), **BAA 10** (BYC), **BAA 64** (IP Act), **BAA 82** (Labor Code), **BAA 9** (Recruitment Agency), **BAA 19** (Overseas Workers), **BAA 72** (Bereavement Leave), and **BAA 57** (Mujahideen Financial Assistance). See the BAAs table above for details.

**Q. What are MSSD's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII, Chapter 1, Section 2 defines MSSD's mandate: ***"The Ministry of Social Services and Development shall provide a balanced and responsive approach to social welfare whereby the rights, needs, and interests of all citizens within its jurisdiction, with special focus on the marginalized and vulnerable sectors, including women, children, senior citizens, differently abled persons, indigenous cultural communities, internally displaced persons, and those similarly situated by analogy, are protected and addressed at all times."***

   Section 3 enumerates **18 powers and functions** including: (a) formulate and implement plans in social welfare and development; (b) adopt policies for public and private social welfare services; (c) promote NGO social welfare facilities; (d) establish and maintain institutional facilities; (e) promote people's organizations; (f) promote networks for welfare delivery; (g) accredit social welfare institutions; (h) undertake research; (i) maintain bilateral/multilateral linkages; (j) provide advisory and training services; (k) disseminate information; (l) deputize law enforcement; (m) regulate fund drives and donations; (n) set standards and monitor performance; (o) exercise supervision over social workers; (p) deputize LGUs for disaster relief; (q) coordinate all programs for marginalized sectors; (r) other functions as provided by law.[^14]

**Q. What can MSSD NOT do without standalone legislation for the elderly and PWD?**

A. While BAA 13 gives MSSD a broad mandate covering all marginalized sectors, it does not provide: a **dedicated institutional structure** for PWD and elderly welfare (the Art. IX, Sec. 23 "special agency"); **enforceable standards** for accessible public facilities; a **registration and ID system** for PWD and senior citizens specific to BARMM; **pension or financial assistance frameworks** beyond national law; or **penalty provisions** for disability discrimination within Bangsamoro law. BAA 13 assigns the general mandate; standalone legislation creates the regulatory framework.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Yes. **Res. 118** calls for monitoring of PWD program implementation under RA 7277. **Res. 404** calls for compliance with the Magna Carta for PWD. **Res. 48** supports the Bangsamoro Children's Declaration aligned with the UN CRC. **Res. 500** encourages child-minding centers in government workplaces. These collectively signal recognition of gaps in elderly and PWD legislation.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. This power has **no single lead MOA** — it is an umbrella power distributed across five institutions (MSSD, MOLE, BWC, BYC, MIAP). MSSD is the closest to a primary coordinator for the power's "social protection" dimension, but MOLE, BWC, BYC, and MIAP each own distinct sub-populations. The **coordination challenge** is that no single body integrates programming across all six population groups.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Existing institutions:
   - **MSSD** — lead for elderly, PWD, disadvantaged groups (BAA 13; BAA 85, Ch. IV) — operational
   - **BWC** — lead for women (BAA 8; BAA 85, Ch. XX) — operational
   - **BYC** — lead for youth (BAA 10; BAA 85, Ch. XIX) — operational
   - **MIAP** — lead for indigenous peoples (BAA 13; BAA 64; BAA 85, Ch. IX) — operational
   - **MOLE** — lead for labor (BAA 13; BAA 82; BAA 85, Ch. VI) — operational

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. Two mandated institutions remain unestablished:
   (1) Art. IX, Sec. 23: ***"special agency"*** for persons with special needs, senior citizens, and disadvantaged groups — **not established** by standalone BAA. PB No. 250 (Bangsamoro Disability Affairs Office) addresses part of this mandate.
   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 15: ***"office for settler communities"*** — **not established** by standalone BAA.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. National agencies maintain presence in BARMM: **DSWD** operates Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program (4Ps) and social welfare programs; **DOLE** manages employment facilitation and labor standards enforcement; **NCIP** exercises authority over IP ancestral domain recognition and certification; **PCW** coordinates GAD programming; **CWC** coordinates children's programs; **NCDA** coordinates disability programs. Many of these functions have been partially devolved to BARMM counterparts, but **4Ps and NCIP certification** remain substantially national-government-operated.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **advanced for some sub-populations and incomplete for others**. BWC and BYC are fully operational Bangsamoro bodies. MIAP and BAA 64 provide a comprehensive Bangsamoro IP framework, though NCIP retains ancestral domain certification functions. MOLE and BAA 82 provide a comprehensive labor framework, with DOLE retaining some enforcement functions. For elderly and PWD, devolution is **essentially non-existent** — no Bangsamoro institutional counterpart to NCDA or OSCA exists.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. Significant accomplishments include: BWC operational with GAD budget monitoring across ministries; BYC operational with youth development programming; BAA 64 enacted as one of the most comprehensive IP rights laws in the Philippines; BAA 82 enacted as a regional labor code expanding worker rights beyond the national Labor Code (e.g., BAA 72 bereavement leave has no national counterpart); BAA 57 providing financial assistance to senior/disabled mujahideen. **Eight pending PBs** signal active legislative intent for remaining gaps.

**Q. Where is BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three unique advantages: (1) **post-conflict programming** — BAA 82 and BAA 57 address former combatant reintegration and mujahideen welfare; PB 292 and PB 406 propose programming for former women combatants and children in armed conflict — needs the national framework does not address; (2) **Islamic cultural integration** — development programs delivered through culturally appropriate channels, including Islamic counseling and gender-responsive approaches aligned with both CEDAW and Islamic values; (3) **comprehensive regional labor code** — BAA 82 provides the first sub-national labor code in the Philippines, expanding rights beyond the national Labor Code.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of standalone legislation for **the elderly and persons with disabilities**. The Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandate for a "special agency" remains unfulfilled after six years. Without it, MSSD's general mandate cannot be translated into enforceable standards, a dedicated institutional structure, or a registration/ID system for these populations. The secondary constraint is the absence of a **comprehensive Bangsamoro Women's Development Code** consolidating CEDAW obligations, GAD enforcement, anti-discrimination measures, and economic empowerment.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has decades of legislative and institutional infrastructure: RA 7277 (since 1992) with implementing rules, accessibility standards, employment quotas, and NCDA enforcement; RA 9994 (since 2010) with senior citizen ID systems, discount mechanisms, and OSCA in every LGU; RA 9710 (since 2009) with comprehensive women's rights protections and PCW institutional capacity; RA 9344 (since 2006) with juvenile justice diversion programs and JJWC coordination. BARMM lacks dedicated equivalents for the elderly, PWD, children, and women's comprehensive code.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework does not account for: (1) **post-conflict populations** — IDPs, former combatants, children affected by armed conflict, widows of conflict; (2) **Islamic family law context** — women's development programs that must navigate both CEDAW and PD 1083 personal status provisions; (3) **cultural and linguistic diversity** — services must be delivered in Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, and Yakan; (4) **limited national agency presence** — NCDA and OSCA have minimal operational capacity in BARMM's remote island and conflict-affected areas.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes, particularly for **women's** and **children's** development programs. Islamic personal status law (PD 1083) governs marriage, divorce, child custody, and succession for Muslims — all of which directly intersect with women's rights and children's welfare programming. The relationship between **CEDAW obligations** (Art. IX, Sec. 12) and **Shari'ah personal status provisions** (PD 1083) creates a legal tension that development programming must navigate.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Three Islamic legal principles directly govern this power:

   (1) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — development programs for vulnerable populations serve the common good. This principle justifies legislation for elderly welfare, disability rights, and children's protection even where specific classical textual authority is limited.

   (2) ***'Adl*** (justice/equity) — the Quranic mandate to establish justice requires equitable treatment across all six population groups. The principle strengthens the case for standalone legislation for populations currently without dedicated Bangsamoro laws.

   (3) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — public officials administering development programs are trustees (*amin*) obligated to protect vulnerable populations. This reinforces the accountability framework for program implementation.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) governs personal status matters that intersect with women's and children's programs — marriage age (Sec. 16), divorce rights (Secs. 27-36), child custody, and succession. Where women's development programs address gender-based violence, economic empowerment, and access to justice, they must account for PD 1083's personal status framework. No *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address development programming for vulnerable populations.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. The recommended approach is **principled integration** rather than mandatory compliance — legislation should be guided by Islamic values of *maslahah*, *'adl*, and *amanah* without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance structures. Development programs should be **culturally responsive** to Islamic norms and practices (e.g., gender-segregated service delivery where appropriate, Islamic counseling alongside social work services) while ensuring that women's rights under CEDAW are not compromised by invocation of cultural or religious norms.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. The divergence space varies by sub-population:

   - **Labor**: Limited divergence — Art. IX, Sec. 10 requires consistency with the Labor Code, but Parliament may **expand or enhance** rights. BAA 82 has already exercised this authority. The direction is one-way: BARMM can provide **more rights**, not fewer.
   - **Indigenous peoples**: Limited divergence — Art. IX, Sec. 3 establishes IPRA as a floor. BAA 64 can provide **additional rights** but cannot diminish IPRA protections.
   - **Women, youth, elderly, PWD**: Broad divergence — no express consistency requirement. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL places no "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier on these programs. BARMM can create entirely distinct frameworks, subject only to the constitutional floor established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E) (equal protection, due process, social justice).

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) **For rights holders within BARMM**: They could gain rights not available under national law — particularly post-conflict welfare programs, Islamic counseling services, and culturally responsive programming. (2) **For cross-boundary operations**: National agency programs (4Ps, NCIP certification) continue to operate — coordination mechanisms must prevent duplication. (3) **Constitutional vulnerability**: Low — the BOL expressly grants this power, and BARMM's divergence expands rather than restricts rights.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlaps are: (a) **MSSD vs. DSWD** — both deliver social welfare services, with DSWD continuing to operate 4Ps in BARMM; (b) **MIAP vs. NCIP** — NCIP retains ancestral domain certification and free, prior and informed consent functions; (c) **MOLE vs. DOLE** — BAA 82 provides a regional labor code, but DOLE retains some enforcement functions. These overlaps require **intergovernmental coordination mechanisms** or **formal devolution agreements**.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Eight pending PBs address specific gaps: PB 250 (Disability Affairs Office), PB 301 (women's haven), PB 292 (former women combatants), PB 382 (women's welfare centers), PB 406 (children in armed conflict), PB 293 (senior citizen medication aid), PB 375 (senior citizens health program), PB 422 (women deprived of liberty). These address **fragments** of the gaps but no single comprehensive bill addresses the elderly/PWD special agency mandate or the women's development code.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 9 (Social Services) targets comprehensive social protection covering all vulnerable populations. Chapter 5 (Governance) targets institutional strengthening for commissions and agencies. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 4 (Social Protection and Human Capital)**, depends on the legislative framework that this power enables — particularly for elderly, PWD, and children's welfare.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without standalone legislation for the elderly and PWD: the Art. IX, Sec. 23 special agency cannot be established, leaving MSSD's general mandate as the only legal basis; **BDP targets for inclusive social protection** remain aspirational for these populations; accessibility standards for public infrastructure cannot be enforced under Bangsamoro law; and pension or financial assistance programs for the elderly cannot be established beyond the suppletory national framework. Without a **Bangsamoro Women's Development Code**: the 5% GAD budget lacks a comprehensive enforcement framework; women's economic empowerment programs lack legislative teeth; and the CEDAW mandate in Art. IX, Sec. 12 remains implemented through BWC's general mandate rather than dedicated legislation.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why does this power name six specific population groups rather than using a general "vulnerable populations" formulation?**

A. The BOL's enumeration of ***"women, labor, the youth, the elderly, the differently-abled, and indigenous peoples"*** is deliberate — it mirrors the **constitutional framework** (Art. II, Secs. 13-14; Art. XIII, Secs. 11-14) and signals that each group requires **distinct** legislative and programmatic responses. The enumeration also creates **identifiable unfulfilled mandates** — failure to legislate for a named group (e.g., the elderly, PWD) is traceable to a specific BOL provision, unlike a generic welfare mandate.

**Q. How does this power interact with other BOL enumerated powers?**

A. Power (r) is the **most cross-cutting** of all 55 powers. It intersects with: (bb) Health — for maternal health, PWD rehabilitation, elderly healthcare; (v) Education — for youth and children's education access; (jj) Labor — BAA 82 directly exercises the labor component of this power; (ff) Indigenous peoples' rights — BAA 64 exercises the IP component; (vv) Social services — MSSD's general welfare mandate; (mm) Consultation mechanisms for women — BWC's consultation function. A comprehensive Bangsamoro Social Protection Code could consolidate the unfilled mandates under a single legislative framework.

**Q. What makes the Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandate distinctive?**

A. Art. IX, Sec. 23 is the **only BOL provision** that mandates a ***"special agency"*** for a specific group using that exact term — creating an identifiable institutional obligation that has remained unfulfilled. Unlike the women and youth commissions (which were created relatively early through BAA 8 and BAA 10), the Art. IX, Sec. 23 agency has no institutional counterpart despite six years of the BTA's transition period. PB No. 250 (Bangsamoro Disability Affairs Office) addresses only part of this mandate — Art. IX, Sec. 23 covers not just PWD but also ***"other disadvantaged persons"*** including senior citizens.

**Q. How does the child marriage prohibition interact with Shari'ah personal status law?**

A. RA 11596 (Prohibition of Child Marriage Act, 2022) criminalizes child marriage nationwide. PD 1083, Sec. 16 permits marriage for Muslim males at 15 and females at puberty with court approval. RA 11596 **supersedes** PD 1083 on this point — it explicitly applies ***"notwithstanding any law, decree, executive order, proclamation, custom, or tradition to the contrary."*** Development programs for women and children in BARMM must account for this legal framework — promoting awareness that child marriage is now criminalized while respecting Islamic family counseling approaches.
