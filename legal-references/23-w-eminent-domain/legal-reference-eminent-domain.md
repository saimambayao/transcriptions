> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(w)** — "Eminent domain;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The Bangsamoro Organic Law grants the Bangsamoro Government the power of eminent domain — the inherent sovereign authority to take private property for public use upon payment of just compensation. This is enumerated as one of 55 powers under Article V, Section 2(w). Eminent domain is one of the three inherent powers of the state (alongside taxation and police power), and its grant to the Bangsamoro Government reflects a high degree of autonomy.

The BOL does not elaborate on eminent domain in a dedicated article or section. Instead, the general framework is established through two provisions. Article IV, Section 7 (General Principles and Policies) states: "The Bangsamoro Government shall ensure every Filipino citizen in its territorial jurisdiction the provision of the basic necessities and equal opportunities in life. Social justice shall be promoted in all aspects of life and phases of development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region." While this does not mention eminent domain explicitly, the cross-reference tables identify this as the contextual provision for the eminent domain power — social justice as the governing principle for property acquisition. Article VII, Section 5(f) authorizes Parliament to "enact a law within the competencies of the Bangsamoro Government to regulate the grant of regional franchises and concessions, and empower the Chief Minister to grant leases, permits, and licenses over agricultural lands and for forest management," which relates to the land management aspect that eminent domain supports.

The exercise of eminent domain is fundamentally constrained by the Philippine Constitution, Article III, Section 9: "Private property shall not be taken for public use without just compensation." The BOL's general limitation (Art. V, Sec. 2 chapeau) subjects all 55 powers to "Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law" and "the general supervision of the President."

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

No standalone BAA governs eminent domain. The power exists as an enumerated authority under the BOL, exercised through BAA 13 (Administrative Code) general mandate. In practice, land acquisition for Bangsamoro Government projects follows the general procedures established by national law — specifically, RA 10752 (Right-of-Way Act) for infrastructure projects and Rule 67 of the Rules of Court for judicial expropriation proceedings.

MOPW handles land acquisition for infrastructure projects, while MHSD deals with land for housing and human settlements. MAFAR may exercise eminent domain-related authority for agrarian reform purposes. BAGO provides legal services for expropriation cases. The absence of a Bangsamoro expropriation code means the region has no codified procedures for: determining what constitutes "public use" in the Bangsamoro context, valuation standards for just compensation (including culturally sensitive valuation of ancestral lands), negotiated purchase protocols before resorting to judicial expropriation, or grievance mechanisms for affected landowners.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

Eminent domain is governed by constitutional principles that apply uniformly across the Philippines. The Philippine Constitution (Art. III, Sec. 9) requires just compensation for any taking of private property for public use. This constitutional requirement is non-derogable — the Bangsamoro Government cannot exercise eminent domain without it. RA 10752 (Right-of-Way Act) provides the primary statutory framework for land acquisition for national government infrastructure projects. While it applies directly to DPWH and other national agencies, it serves as the default framework for Bangsamoro infrastructure land acquisition absent a regional law.

Rule 67 of the Rules of Court governs the judicial procedure for expropriation. The regular courts (not Shari'ah courts) have jurisdiction over expropriation cases, as this involves constitutional rights adjudicated under the regular court system. RA 6657 (Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Law) governs the exercise of eminent domain for agrarian reform — the taking of agricultural land for redistribution to landless farmers. This law applies suppletorily within BARMM for agrarian reform acquisitions.

A significant challenge in BARMM is the overlap between eminent domain and ancestral domain rights under RA 8371 (Indigenous Peoples Rights Act). Expropriating land covered by a Certificate of Ancestral Domain Title (CADT) raises free, prior, and informed consent requirements that national expropriation law does not fully address.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The absence of a Bangsamoro Expropriation Code is a significant gap given the region's massive infrastructure and development needs. BARMM's unique land tenure landscape — where ancestral domain, Islamic property law (waqf, family property under PD 1083), Torrens title, and tax declaration-based claims overlap — makes a generic application of national expropriation law inadequate.

A Bangsamoro Expropriation Code would codify: the definition of "public use" in the Bangsamoro context (including infrastructure, social housing, economic development, and environmental protection), just compensation standards that account for the region's unique property valuation challenges, mandatory negotiated purchase procedures before judicial expropriation, protections for ancestral domain holders (FPIC requirements, enhanced compensation), protections for properties under Islamic law (waqf endowments, family property), resettlement and livelihood restoration programs for displaced communities, expedited procedures for infrastructure land acquisition, and a grievance mechanism. Without this law, land acquisition for BARMM's infrastructure modernization program faces legal uncertainty, delays, and potential human rights issues — particularly in conflict-affected areas where land tenure records are incomplete or contested.

### Development and Policy Connection

BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 3 (Spatial Development Strategy) requires systematic land use planning that depends on the ability to acquire land for public purposes. Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development) targets expansion of roads, bridges, ports, and airports — all requiring land acquisition. The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028) prioritizes infrastructure development under its connectivity pillar, which will require exercising eminent domain at scale. Without a codified expropriation framework, the Bangsamoro Government cannot efficiently acquire the land needed for its development programs, creating bottlenecks that delay infrastructure projects and increase costs.
