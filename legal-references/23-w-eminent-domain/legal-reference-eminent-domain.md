# (w) Eminent Domain

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(w)** — ***"Eminent domain;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(w) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national expropriation law. However, Art. III, Sec. 9 of the Constitution (just compensation) and Art. X, Sec. 20 (autonomous regions operate within the constitutional framework) set the constitutional floor. National procedural requirements (Rule 67, Rules of Court; RA 10752) apply suppletorily where no BAA establishes a distinct Bangsamoro expropriation procedure. |

| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development); Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 10 (Peace, Security, and Rule of Law) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None. The BOL does not name a specific code for eminent domain. BAA 13 provides the general institutional framework. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over eminent domain?**

A. Article V, Section 2(w) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Eminent domain"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a grant of the **sovereign power to take private property for public use upon payment of just compensation** — one of the three inherent powers of the State (alongside police power and taxation). The BOL's enumeration is notable for its brevity: unlike many other powers that include qualifying phrases, Art. V, Sec. 2(w) grants eminent domain without any subject-matter qualifier, suggesting the **full scope** of this sovereign power is delegated to the Bangsamoro Government for exercise within its territorial jurisdiction.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Unlike cooperatives or education, the BOL does not dedicate an entire section to eminent domain. However, two provisions provide context:

   (1) Art. IV, Sec. 7 establishes: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall ensure every Filipino citizen in its territorial jurisdiction the provision of the basic necessities and equal opportunities in life. Social justice shall be promoted in all aspects of life and phases of development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** This frames the **purpose** for which eminent domain should be exercised — social justice, basic necessities, and equal opportunities.[^2]

   (2) Art. IX, Sec. 2 provides: ***"The Parliament shall enact laws providing for adequate reparation to the Bangsamoro people affected by unjust dispossession of territorial and proprietary rights or customary land tenure, which may include payment of just compensation to and relocation of such people."*** This provision intersects with eminent domain in the context of **reparative land acquisition** — the government may need to expropriate private lands to provide reparation to dispossessed Bangsamoro communities.[^3]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not name a specific "eminent domain code" or "right-of-way act" by title. Unlike Art. XIII, Sec. 24 (which mandates a ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"***), the eminent domain power is granted without an express legislative mandate. However, the comprehensive exercise of this power — including valuation standards, procedural rules, funding mechanisms, and institutional coordination — requires legislative action beyond what BAA 13's general provision offers.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not create a dedicated eminent domain body. The power is vested in the **Chief Minister** as the executive authority, with the **Bangsamoro Attorney General's Office** serving as the litigation arm. This institutional assignment was formalized not by the BOL itself but by **BAA 13**, Bk. I, Tit. I, Ch. 5, Sec. 10.[^4]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places the following limitations:

   (1) **Constitutional framework**: Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — meaning eminent domain must operate within the constitutional framework, including the just compensation guarantee of Art. III, Sec. 9.[^5]

   (2) **Torrens title protection**: Art. IX, Sec. 2 provides: ***"No land title issued by the National Government under the Torrens System shall be invalidated."*** While this addresses reparation rather than eminent domain directly, it confirms that registered private land titles are constitutionally protected — the government must compensate, not invalidate.[^3]

   (3) **Social justice purpose**: Art. IV, Sec. 7 directs that all government authority — including eminent domain — must serve social justice and the provision of basic necessities.[^2]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to eminent domain?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Universal constraint (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."*** The BOL chapeau reiterates this. For eminent domain, the constitutional framework is especially constraining because the just compensation guarantee is a **Bill of Rights provision** that cannot be overridden by any statute.[^5]

   (2) **Just compensation guarantee (Art. III, Sec. 9)**: ***"Private property shall not be taken for public use without just compensation."*** This is the **foundational limitation** on eminent domain. Three elements must be satisfied: (a) **taking** — the government must acquire, destroy, or substantially impair ownership rights; (b) **public use** — the taking must serve a legitimate public purpose; (c) **just compensation** — the property owner must receive the full and fair equivalent of the loss.[^6]

   (3) **Due process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law."*** Expropriation proceedings must satisfy procedural due process — notice to the owner, opportunity to be heard, judicial determination of compensation, and right to appeal.[^7]

   (4) **National economy provisions (Art. XII, Sec. 18)**: ***"The State may, in the interest of national welfare or defense, establish and operate vital industries and, upon payment of just compensation, transfer to public ownership utilities and other private enterprises to be operated by the Government."*** This constitutional provision authorizes the broadest form of eminent domain — transferring entire private enterprises to public ownership — and establishes "national welfare" as a valid public purpose.[^8]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee for property owners facing expropriation?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Just compensation** — the full and fair equivalent of the property at the time of taking. This is a **judicial determination** — no executive or legislative body can unilaterally fix the amount.

   (2) **Public use requirement** — the taking must serve a genuine public purpose. The Supreme Court has interpreted "public use" broadly to include indirect public benefit (not only direct government use).

   (3) **Due process** — notice, hearing, and the right to contest the necessity of the taking and the amount of compensation.

   (4) **Non-impairment of contracts** (Art. III, Sec. 10) — expropriation of property subject to existing contracts must account for contractual rights in the just compensation determination.

**Q. What constitutional mandates for legislation or institutions exist in this area?**

A. The Constitution does not mandate specific eminent domain legislation — the power is inherent in sovereignty. However, Art. III, Sec. 9 effectively mandates a **compensation framework** by requiring "just compensation." Congress has implemented this through procedural rules (Rule 67) and statutory frameworks (RA 10752). The Bangsamoro Government, exercising this delegated sovereign power, must ensure its own framework meets the constitutional standard.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for eminent domain?**

A. The national framework establishes a comprehensive system: **Rule 67 of the Rules of Court** provides the procedural rules for expropriation proceedings before the courts — including filing requirements, deposit, writ of possession, appointment of commissioners, and determination of just compensation. **RA 10752** (The Right-of-Way Act, 2016) provides the substantive standards for national government infrastructure right-of-way acquisition — including valuation methodology (replacement cost for improvements; fair market value or zonal value for land, whichever is higher), modes of acquisition (donation, negotiated sale, expropriation), and expedited procedures. **RA 12289** (2025) amends RA 10752 to extend coverage to public service providers. **RA 6657** (CARL) authorizes agrarian reform expropriation. **RA 7279** (UDHA) authorizes socialized housing expropriation. Together, these laws provide **procedural rules**, **valuation standards**, **deposit requirements**, **institutional frameworks**, and **sector-specific expropriation authority**.[^9][^10][^11]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national eminent domain laws apply in BARMM in the absence of a standalone Bangsamoro right-of-way act:

   (1) **Rule 67, Rules of Court** — governs all expropriation proceedings. Applies **directly** — the Bangsamoro Government files expropriation cases before the Regional Trial Courts under this rule.[^9]

   (2) **RA 10752** — applies to **national government infrastructure projects** within BARMM (DPWH-funded roads, national projects). For Bangsamoro Government-funded projects, applicability is **uncertain** because RA 10752 defines "implementing agency" as national government instrumentalities.[^10]

   (3) **RA 12289** — amends RA 10752. Same applicability issue as RA 10752 for Bangsamoro Government projects.[^11]

   (4) **RA 6657** (CARL) — applies **directly** for agrarian reform acquisition within BARMM.

   (5) **RA 7279** (UDHA) — applies **suppletorily** for housing and resettlement expropriation, supplemented by MHSD's BAA 13 mandate.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for eminent domain?**

A. (1) **Department of Public Works and Highways (DPWH)** — exercises right-of-way acquisition for nationally-funded infrastructure projects within BARMM. DPWH retains authority over national road networks and nationally-funded projects even within the autonomous region.

   (2) **Department of Agrarian Reform (DAR)** — exercises compulsory acquisition of private agricultural lands for agrarian reform distribution under RA 6657.

   (3) **Regional Trial Courts** — the **judiciary** determines just compensation in all expropriation cases, whether initiated by the national government, the Bangsamoro Government, or LGUs. This function is constitutionally vested and cannot be transferred.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. Eminent domain is not assigned to a single ministry. It is a **cross-cutting power** exercised by multiple entities:

   (1) **Office of the Chief Minister** — the Chief Minister determines when eminent domain should be exercised on behalf of the Bangsamoro Government (BAA 13, Bk. I, Tit. I, Ch. 5, Sec. 10).[^4]

   (2) **Bangsamoro Attorney General's Office (BAGO)** — institutes expropriation proceedings in the proper court upon direction of the Chief Minister (BAA 13, Sec. 10).[^4]

   (3) **Ministry of Public Works (MOPW)** — the primary implementing agency for infrastructure projects requiring right-of-way acquisition. BAA 85, Chapter XI allocates P5.77 billion to MOPW, of which P4.79 billion is capital outlays for infrastructure.[^12][^13]

   (4) **Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (MHSD)** — authorized to ***"identify and acquire government lands and private lands through donation, expropriation, and payment of just compensation or any other form of alienation for housing and rural development"*** (BAA 13, Sec. 3(r)).[^14]

   (5) **Bangsamoro Ports Management Authority (BPMA)** — the Governing Board may prescribe rules governing ***"the exercise of eminent domain"*** within port districts (BAA 13, Sec. 31(a)).[^15]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for eminent domain as of BAA 89. The power is exercised through: (a) BAA 13's general grant to the Chief Minister (Sec. 10); (b) BAA 49's LGU eminent domain provision (Sec. 22); and (c) sector-specific mandates in BAA 13 for MHSD and BPMA. No comprehensive right-of-way code or expropriation act exists.

**Q. What are the relevant mandates and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 addresses eminent domain in three separate locations:

   **Chief Minister's power** (Bk. I, Tit. I, Ch. 5, Sec. 10): ***"The Chief Minister shall determine when it is necessary or advantageous to exercise the power of eminent domain in behalf of the Bangsamoro Government, and direct the Bangsamoro Attorney General, whenever such action is deemed appropriate, to institute expropriation proceedings in the proper court."*** This vests the **decision** to expropriate in the Chief Minister and the **litigation function** in BAGO.[^4]

   **MHSD's expropriation authority** (Bk. VI, Tit. VII, Sec. 3(r)): MHSD may ***"identify and acquire government lands and private lands through donation, expropriation, and payment of just compensation or any other form of alienation for housing and rural development in coordination with the Ministry of Interior and Local Government (MILG), constituent local government units, Land Registration Authority (LRA), and other relevant agencies."***[^14]

   **BPMA Governing Board** (Bk. VI, Tit. IX, Sec. 31(a)): The Board shall ***"prescribe guidelines, rules, and regulations governing the establishment and management of ports and the use of lands and properties within Port Districts, as well as the exercise of eminent domain."***[^15]

   **MOPW mandate** (Bk. VI, Tit. XI, Sec. 2-3): While MOPW does not have express eminent domain authority in BAA 13, it is mandated to undertake ***"the planning, design and construction of infrastructures, such as, but not limited to roads and bridges, flood control, water supply projects and other public works facilities"*** — the very projects for which right-of-way acquisition is required. The absence of explicit eminent domain authority for MOPW creates a **procedural gap**: MOPW must request the Chief Minister to authorize expropriation through BAGO for every infrastructure project requiring right-of-way.[^16]

**Q. What can the Bangsamoro Government NOT do without standalone eminent domain legislation?**

A. BAA 13 provides the basic institutional framework — the Chief Minister decides, BAGO litigates — but does not provide:

   - **Valuation standards** specific to BARMM (how to determine fair market value for land in conflict-affected areas, ancestral domains, or *waqf* property)
   - **Expedited procedures** for infrastructure right-of-way acquisition (the current system requires filing in court under Rule 67 for every parcel)
   - **Funding mechanisms** mandating that right-of-way costs be built into every infrastructure project's Program of Work
   - **Negotiated sale procedures** with defined standards for when MOPW can acquire by agreement before resorting to expropriation
   - **Special rules for *waqf* property** and ancestral domains
   - **Institutional delegation** — currently, every expropriation requires Chief Minister authorization, which creates bottlenecks for routine right-of-way acquisition

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. No enacted resolutions directly address eminent domain. However, **PB No. 364** (Bangsamoro Right-of-Way Indemnity Act of 2025) demonstrates clear legislative intent to address the right-of-way acquisition gap.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Eminent domain is unusual among the 55 powers because it is **not primarily a ministerial function** — it is an **inherent sovereign power** vested in the Chief Minister (the executive) and exercised through the courts (the judiciary). The assignment to the Office of the Chief Minister is correct for the **decision to expropriate**. However, the **operational implementation** — identifying parcels, negotiating with owners, processing acquisitions — is primarily performed by **MOPW** (for infrastructure), **MHSD** (for housing), and **BPMA** (for ports). None of these agencies has a self-contained eminent domain procedure. The gap is one of **institutional coordination** — no single entity manages the end-to-end process from identification of needed property to final payment of just compensation.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. - **Office of the Chief Minister**: authorizes eminent domain exercise (BAA 13, Sec. 10)
   - **Bangsamoro Attorney General's Office (BAGO)**: institutes and litigates expropriation proceedings — operational
   - **Ministry of Public Works (MOPW)**: primary user of eminent domain for infrastructure right-of-way — operational, BAA 85 Ch. XI budget
   - **Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (MHSD)**: exercises expropriation for housing — operational, BAA 13 Sec. 3(r)
   - **Bangsamoro Ports Management Authority (BPMA)**: exercises eminent domain within port districts — operational, BAA 13 Sec. 31(a)
   - **Constituent LGUs**: exercise eminent domain under BAA 49, Sec. 22 — operational, through chief executives and sanggunians

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not mandate the creation of a specific eminent domain institution. However, if **PB No. 342** (Bangsamoro Economic Zone Act) is enacted, the **Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA)** — mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 28 — would gain eminent domain authority within economic zones. BEZA has not yet been created.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. A **Right-of-Way Management Unit** within MOPW or within the OCM would streamline the acquisition process. Currently, every expropriation requires ad hoc coordination between MOPW (identifying the parcel), OCM (authorizing), and BAGO (litigating). A dedicated unit would manage negotiations, valuations, documentation, and coordination with courts — similar to the DPWH Right-of-Way Section at the national level.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **DPWH** exercises eminent domain within BARMM for nationally-funded projects — particularly national roads, bridges, and flood control. DPWH maintains a **Right-of-Way Section** that handles identification, negotiation, and court proceedings for land acquisition. **DAR** exercises compulsory acquisition for agrarian reform lands. Both agencies operate under national law (RA 10752 for DPWH; RA 6657 for DAR) and file proceedings in **Regional Trial Courts** within BARMM.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. (1) **DPWH Right-of-Way Acquisition Program** — routine acquisition for national road and infrastructure projects within BARMM provinces, funded through the national General Appropriations Act.
   (2) **DAR Compulsory Acquisition** — identification and acquisition of private agricultural lands for agrarian reform distribution.
   (3) **NHA Resettlement Program** — National Housing Authority exercises expropriation for socialized housing sites, though limited presence in BARMM.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **inherent in the BOL grant**: Art. V, Sec. 2(w) vests eminent domain authority in the Bangsamoro Government, and BAA 13 operationalizes this through the Chief Minister. There is no "transfer" required from a national agency because eminent domain is a sovereign power that the BOL directly delegates. However, for **nationally-funded projects** within BARMM, DPWH retains the authority to expropriate under RA 10752 because it is the implementing agency. The boundary question is whether Bangsamoro Government-funded projects can benefit from RA 10752's expedited procedures and valuation standards, or whether the absence of a Bangsamoro-specific act means these projects must rely solely on Rule 67's general procedures.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes. For **road and bridge construction**, both DPWH (nationally-funded projects) and MOPW (Bangsamoro-funded projects) acquire right-of-way within the same territory. Both file expropriation proceedings in the same Regional Trial Courts. The difference is that DPWH operates under **RA 10752** (with defined valuation standards and expedited procedures), while MOPW operates under **Rule 67 alone** (with less specific valuation standards). This creates an **asymmetry** — property owners whose land is expropriated for national projects receive compensation under RA 10752's standards, while those whose land is expropriated for Bangsamoro projects may receive different treatment. PB 364 aims to eliminate this asymmetry.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. The Bangsamoro Government has exercised eminent domain primarily through: (1) MOPW infrastructure projects requiring right-of-way acquisition for roads and bridges — BAA 85 allocates P5.77 billion to MOPW, with P3.14 billion for road and bridge development; (2) BAA 49's operationalization of LGU eminent domain power — BARMM LGUs exercise expropriation through sanggunian ordinances; (3) the filing of PB 364 — the first legislative attempt to establish a systematic right-of-way acquisition framework specifically for Bangsamoro Government projects. The absence of comprehensive data on the number of expropriation cases filed by BAGO on behalf of the Bangsamoro Government is itself a gap, suggesting the need for the annual reporting mechanism proposed in PB 364, Sec. 9. [UNVERIFIED — specific number of expropriation cases filed]

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM does not currently hold a clear advantage over the national system in eminent domain. The national framework (RA 10752, Rule 67) is mature, well-tested, and backed by extensive jurisprudence. BARMM's potential advantage lies in designing a framework **sensitive to its unique context**:

   - **Conflict-affected land valuation**: standard market value approaches may undervalue land in BARMM where formal markets are thin, titles are contested, and conflict has depressed values. A Bangsamoro framework could develop alternative valuation methods.
   - **Shari'ah-sensitive treatment of *waqf* property**: the national framework does not distinguish between private property and religiously endowed property. A Bangsamoro framework could provide special protections for *waqf*.
   - **Ancestral domain sensitivity**: expropriation of land within ancestral domains or areas covered by native titles (*fusaka inged*) requires heightened protections that BAA 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act) may provide but no eminent domain code currently integrates.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a **standalone right-of-way act**. Without it:

   - MOPW cannot acquire right-of-way through negotiated sale under defined standards — it must either negotiate informally (with no legal framework for valuation) or immediately resort to court expropriation
   - Every expropriation requires **Chief Minister authorization** and BAGO litigation — creating bottlenecks for routine right-of-way for small projects
   - Right-of-way costs are not systematically included in infrastructure project budgets
   - There are no **standard valuation rules** for BARMM-specific contexts (conflict-affected areas, *waqf* property, ancestral domains)

   Enactment of **PB No. 364** (or an expanded version) would remove the primary constraint.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a **mature right-of-way acquisition system** over decades:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 10752 (2016), now amended by RA 12289 (2025), provides comprehensive valuation standards, expedited procedures, and defined institutional roles. This law has been refined through multiple amendments (from RA 8974 in 2000 to RA 10752 to RA 12289).
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: DPWH maintains a dedicated **Right-of-Way Section** in each regional and district office, with specialized staff for negotiation, valuation, documentation, and court proceedings.
   - **Jurisprudence**: Decades of Supreme Court decisions define "public use," "just compensation," valuation methodology, deposit requirements, and writ of possession procedures. This jurisprudential framework provides legal certainty.
   - **IT and data**: DPWH maintains right-of-way management databases for tracking project-level acquisitions, payments, and pending cases.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in three critical areas:

   (1) **RA 10752 applicability gap**: The law defines its coverage as ***"national government infrastructure projects"*** — Bangsamoro Government-funded projects may not qualify, leaving MOPW without the benefit of RA 10752's valuation standards and expedited procedures.

   (2) **No *waqf* property provisions**: The national framework treats all private property identically. In BARMM, *waqf* property — land endowed for religious or charitable purposes — is prevalent and carries religious significance. Expropriation of *waqf* without adequate substitute provisions causes community harm beyond the monetary value.

   (3) **No conflict-sensitive valuation**: Standard "fair market value" approaches assume functioning property markets with willing buyers and sellers. In BARMM's conflict-affected areas — particularly in Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, Lanao del Sur, and Basilan — formal property markets are thin, many parcels are untitled or covered by customary tenure, and property values have been depressed by decades of conflict. Standard valuation may systematically undercompensate BARMM property owners.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element of the national framework:

   - **Replicate**: **judicial determination of just compensation** (constitutionally required — cannot be changed); **modes of acquisition** (donation, negotiated sale, expropriation — RA 10752's framework is sound); **deposit and writ of possession procedures** (Rule 67 procedures work and are judicially established).
   - **Adapt**: **valuation standards** — take RA 10752's replacement cost and fair market value approach and add provisions for conflict-affected areas, untitled lands under customary tenure, and *waqf* property. **Negotiated sale procedures** — adapt for BARMM's cultural context (involving *sultan*, *datu*, or community leaders in negotiations). **Budgeting requirements** — adopt PB 364's approach of mandating right-of-way costs in every Program of Work.
   - **Replace**: **institutional coordination** — build a dedicated right-of-way management function within MOPW (or a cross-cutting unit under OCM) that the national system has but BARMM lacks. **Delegation framework** — allow MOPW to negotiate and acquire right-of-way up to a defined threshold without requiring Chief Minister authorization for every parcel.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes, though the dimension is narrower than for economic powers like cooperatives or banking. The Shari'ah dimension of eminent domain arises primarily from: (1) the treatment of **property rights** under Islamic law, which generally recognizes private property as sacrosanct except where the public interest (*maslahah*) genuinely requires taking; (2) the treatment of ***waqf*** **property**, which creates a direct conflict between the State's power to take and the Islamic principle that endowed property is inalienable; and (3) the requirement of ***'adl*** **(justice)** in compensation — Islamic law demands prompt, full, and fair payment, making delayed or inadequate compensation not merely a legal violation but a moral transgression.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL is silent on Shari'ah specifically in connection with eminent domain. However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah-based laws on property matters: ***"The Parliament shall have the power to enact laws on personal, family, and property law jurisdiction."*** This includes authority to legislate Shari'ah-compliant rules for expropriation of Muslim-owned and *waqf* property. Additionally, Art. IV, Sec. 7's social justice mandate must be read alongside Islamic principles of *'adl* and *maslahah*.[^17]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Three principles directly govern eminent domain:

   (1) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — the primary justification for the State's power to take private property. Classical Islamic jurisprudence recognizes that the ruler may take property for genuine public benefit (*masalih 'ammah*), provided just compensation is paid. The taking must serve a real, demonstrable public purpose — not speculative benefit or private advantage.

   (2) ***'Adl*** (justice/equity) — compensation must be **full, fair, and prompt**. Delay in payment is a separate injustice. The *'adl* principle strengthens the constitutional just compensation guarantee by adding a moral imperative for timely payment.

   (3) ***Waqf*** (religious endowment) — *waqf* property is dedicated in perpetuity for religious or charitable purposes and is considered inalienable under classical Islamic law. Expropriation of *waqf* creates a direct tension between State power and religious obligation. The predominant scholarly position permits taking of *waqf* only where: (a) the public purpose is **compelling and cannot be achieved through alternative means**; (b) **replacement *waqf*** of equivalent value and similar purpose is provided; and (c) the proceeds are held in trust for the original *waqf* purpose.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) governs property relations for Muslims, including succession and marital property, but does not specifically address eminent domain or *waqf* administration. Shari'ah courts have jurisdiction over property disputes among Muslims and could hear cases involving the valuation or ownership of expropriated property governed by Islamic law. No *fatawa* from the Bangsamoro *Darul Ifta'* specifically address government expropriation of *waqf* property.[^18]

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro eminent domain legislation?**

A. A Bangsamoro right-of-way act should include a **dedicated chapter on *waqf* property** providing: (a) **heightened necessity standard** — *waqf* property may be expropriated only when no reasonable alternative exists; (b) **replacement *waqf* requirement** — the government must dedicate replacement property of equivalent value and similar purpose before taking the *waqf*; (c) **Shari'ah advisory process** — the *Darul Ifta'* or a designated Shari'ah authority should be consulted before any expropriation of *waqf*; (d) **proceeds protection** — if cash compensation is paid instead of replacement property, proceeds must be held in trust and used only for the original *waqf* purpose. Compliance should be **mandatory** for *waqf* property, not optional — unlike the dual-track approach for cooperatives.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national eminent domain law?**

A. BARMM has **moderate legislative authority** to diverge, constrained by constitutional requirements that are unusually rigid for this power:

   As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL grants eminent domain without a national law consistency requirement. As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), the constitutional floor is **Art. III, Sec. 9** (just compensation) and **Art. III, Sec. 1** (due process) — both Bill of Rights provisions that no legislation can diminish. The **judicial determination** of just compensation is constitutionally vested in the courts and cannot be transferred to an administrative body.

   Within these constraints, BARMM can diverge in the following areas:

   (1) **Valuation methodology** — BARMM can establish its own standards for determining fair market value, including special rules for conflict-affected areas, customary land tenure, and *waqf* property. However, the final determination remains judicial.

   (2) **Procedural framework** — BARMM can establish negotiated sale procedures, threshold amounts for delegation of authority, and budgeting requirements (as PB 364 proposes) without conflicting with Rule 67 (which governs only court proceedings).

   (3) **Institutional delegation** — BARMM can delegate acquisition authority to MOPW or other agencies up to defined thresholds, reducing the bottleneck at the Chief Minister level.

   (4) ***Waqf* protections** — as established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), BARMM can establish Shari'ah-sensitive protections for *waqf* property that the national framework lacks. This is a **unique basis for divergence** rooted in the Bangsamoro identity and religious tradition.

   (5) **Ancestral domain protections** — BARMM can integrate heightened protections for expropriation within ancestral domains, consistent with BAA 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act) and BOL Art. IX, Sec. 3.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For property owners**: they could gain stronger protections — particularly *waqf* owners and IP communities — if the Bangsamoro framework provides higher compensation standards or heightened necessity tests. The risk is if the framework provides **lower** compensation than RA 10752 — this would face constitutional challenge under Art. III, Sec. 9.

   (2) **For infrastructure projects**: a Bangsamoro right-of-way act would provide legal clarity for MOPW-funded projects that currently fall in the **applicability gap** between RA 10752 (national projects) and Rule 67 (general procedures). Property owners would have clearer expectations.

   (3) **For dual jurisdiction**: nationally-funded projects within BARMM would continue under RA 10752/RA 12289, while Bangsamoro-funded projects would operate under the Bangsamoro act. The two systems should be **harmonized** to avoid situations where neighboring parcels expropriated for the same road project receive different compensation because one is a national project and the other is regional.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. The BAA would govern Bangsamoro Government-initiated expropriation within BARMM. Rule 67 would continue to apply as the procedural framework for court proceedings unless the Supreme Court promulgates specific rules for Bangsamoro expropriation. RA 10752 would continue to govern DPWH and other national agency projects within BARMM. Constitutional provisions (Art. III, Sec. 9) would apply regardless.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. Two jurisdictional overlaps require resolution:

   (1) **MOPW vs. DPWH right-of-way acquisition**: both agencies build infrastructure within BARMM, but DPWH operates under RA 10752's defined framework while MOPW has no comparable statutory authority. When both agencies work on the same road network — as happens in BARMM where national and regional roads intersect — property owners face different acquisition regimes depending on which agency is building. A Bangsamoro right-of-way act should establish comparable standards and provide for **coordination protocols** between DPWH and MOPW.

   (2) **LGU eminent domain under BAA 49 vs. Bangsamoro Government eminent domain under BAA 13**: BAA 49, Sec. 22 authorizes LGUs to expropriate "for public use, or purpose, or welfare for the benefit of the poor and the landless" while BAA 13, Sec. 10 vests the Chief Minister with the broader eminent domain power. When a Bangsamoro Government project requires land within an LGU's jurisdiction, the relationship between these two authorities is unclear. A standalone act should define which entity takes the lead and how coordination works.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB No. 364** (Bangsamoro Right-of-Way Indemnity Act of 2025) is the most directly relevant pending bill. It addresses the critical gap of **mandatory budgeting** for right-of-way costs and establishes **valuation rules** for negotiated sales. However, PB 364 is narrower than a comprehensive eminent domain act — it focuses on the **indemnification** aspect without addressing *waqf* property, ancestral domain protections, institutional delegation, or the MOPW-DPWH coordination framework.

   **PB No. 92** (Housing Act), **PB No. 141** (Resettlement Act), **PB No. 342** (Economic Zone Act), and **PB No. 243** (PPP Act) each contain sector-specific eminent domain provisions. If all are enacted, eminent domain would be scattered across multiple BAAs without a unifying framework — reinforcing the need for a comprehensive act.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 11 (Infrastructure Development) sets targets for road construction, bridge building, water system development, and flood control — all of which require right-of-way acquisition as a **prerequisite**. The BDP's infrastructure targets cannot be met if every road project faces delays from ad hoc land acquisition with no defined framework.

   The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 2 (Accelerated Infrastructure and Connectivity)**, explicitly prioritizes rapid infrastructure development — making efficient right-of-way acquisition a **binding constraint** on pillar achievement. **Pillar 4 (Social Protection and Human Capital)** depends on housing and resettlement programs that may require expropriation under PB 92 and PB 141.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a Bangsamoro right-of-way act:

   - **Infrastructure project delays**: every road, bridge, and water system project requiring land acquisition faces uncertainty — no defined valuation standards, no mandatory budgeting for right-of-way costs, no delegation of authority for routine acquisitions. The P5.77 billion allocated to MOPW in BAA 85 cannot be efficiently deployed.
   - **Cost overruns**: without systematic right-of-way planning, projects underestimate land acquisition costs. Mid-project realizations that right-of-way was not budgeted cause delays, redesigns, or project abandonment.
   - **Injustice to property owners**: informal acquisition — where MOPW constructs through private land without formal proceedings — exposes the government to **inverse condemnation** claims and undermines public trust.
   - **Housing and resettlement gaps**: IDP resettlement (PB 141) and socialized housing (PB 92) cannot proceed without a clear legal framework for acquiring private land at scale.
   - **Economic zone development**: BEZA (Art. XIII, Sec. 28) cannot be operationalized without expropriation authority — PB 342 provides this, but a general framework would be more efficient than sector-specific provisions.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the relationship between eminent domain and the Bangsamoro post-conflict context?**

A. BARMM's post-conflict context creates unique eminent domain challenges that no national law addresses:

   (1) **Conflict-displaced land**: decades of armed conflict have displaced Bangsamoro communities from their lands. Some lands are now occupied by settlers or have been titled to third parties. Art. IX, Sec. 2 mandates reparation through ***"payment of just compensation"*** — the government may need to expropriate currently-titled lands to return them to displaced communities. This is a form of **reparative expropriation** with no national precedent.

   (2) **Contested ownership**: in conflict-affected areas, multiple parties may claim the same land — original owners, settlers, IP communities with native titles, and government proclamation holders. Expropriation proceedings require clear identification of the "owner" to be compensated, which is difficult in these contexts.

   (3) **Depressed land values**: fair market value in conflict-affected areas may be artificially low because of security risks, infrastructure deficits, and displacement. Compensating at depressed fair market value penalizes owners for the consequences of conflict they did not cause.

   A Bangsamoro expropriation framework should include **conflict-sensitive valuation rules** that address these realities — potentially using pre-conflict value benchmarks, replacement cost standards, or area-specific valuation committees with local knowledge.

**Q. How does eminent domain interact with the power over ancestral domains (Art. V, Sec. 2(d))?**

A. The interaction is critical. **BAA 64** (Indigenous Peoples' Act) recognizes and protects ancestral domains, including native titles (*fusaka inged*). When the government seeks to expropriate land within an ancestral domain, it must comply with:

   (1) **Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC)** — BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 12 requires FPIC for development initiatives within ancestral domains covered by Certificate of Ancestral Domain Title.

   (2) **Heightened scrutiny** — expropriation of ancestral domain land should meet a **higher threshold** of public necessity than ordinary private property, given the constitutional and statutory protections for indigenous peoples' rights.

   (3) **Cultural compensation** — just compensation for ancestral domain land may need to go beyond monetary payment to include **replacement land, cultural site preservation, and community benefit agreements**.

   A comprehensive eminent domain framework should integrate these protections, ensuring that BAA 64's IP rights are not undermined by the exercise of eminent domain.

**Q. What is the significance of PB 364's valuation formula — and does it meet the just compensation standard?**

A. PB 364, Sec. 7 proposes that for negotiated sales, the implementing agency may offer: (a) for agricultural land — ***"the current market value of crops and trees thereon"***; (b) for new right-of-way sites — ***"the current market value or the zonal value of the land, whichever is lower."*** The "whichever is lower" standard raises a **constitutional concern**: the Supreme Court in *Republic v. Gingoyon* (G.R. No. 166429) held that the standard should favor the property owner's interest. Using the **lower** of market value or zonal value may not satisfy the "full and fair equivalent" standard for just compensation. PB 364 mitigates this concern through Sec. 8 (Non-Diminution of Rights): ***"Nothing in this Act shall be construed to diminish or impair the right of property owners to just compensation under the Bangsamoro Organic Law, the Philippine Constitution, or existing national laws."*** This savings clause preserves the owner's right to contest the valuation in court. The "whichever is lower" standard therefore applies only to the **negotiated sale offer** — not the final compensation, which remains subject to judicial determination.

**Q. Can the Bangsamoro Government exercise eminent domain over national government property within BARMM?**

A. No. Eminent domain is exercised by a sovereign over **private property**. The Bangsamoro Government cannot expropriate property owned by the National Government — this would constitute one government entity taking from another, which is not within the scope of the delegated eminent domain power under the BOL. The proper mechanism for transferring national government property to the Bangsamoro Government is through intergovernmental agreement, executive order, or legislation — as contemplated in the transition provisions (Art. XVI).

**Q. Who determines "public use" when the Bangsamoro Government exercises eminent domain?**

A. The determination of **public use** is initially made by the **expropriating authority** — the Chief Minister (for the Bangsamoro Government) or the LGU chief executive (for LGUs under BAA 49). However, the determination is **subject to judicial review**. The property owner may challenge the existence of "public use" before the court in the expropriation proceeding. The Supreme Court has interpreted "public use" broadly to include not only direct government use but also indirect public benefit — housing, infrastructure, economic development, and even aesthetic improvement. This broad interpretation benefits the Bangsamoro Government, which often exercises eminent domain for purposes like infrastructure in conflict-affected communities, IDP resettlement, and economic zone development.
