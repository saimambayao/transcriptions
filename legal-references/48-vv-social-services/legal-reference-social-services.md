# (vv) Social Services, Social Welfare, and Charities

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(vv)** — ***"Social services, social welfare, and charities;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(vv) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national social welfare law. Art. V, Sec. 2 is ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — establishing a constitutional floor. Art. IX, Sec. 8 mandates delivery of ***"basic and responsive health programs, quality education, appropriate services, livelihood opportunities"*** but does not require consistency with any national statute. Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandates a ***"special agency"*** for persons with special needs and disadvantaged groups — again without a national law consistency qualifier. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII, Sec. 1-22 |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC, BAA 8); Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC, BAA 10); Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC, BAA 4); Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission (BAA 83); Provincial/City/Municipal Social Welfare Offices (BAA 13, Title XIII, Ch. 4) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 9 (Social Services — Education, Health, Social Welfare); Chapter 2 (Regional Trends — social protection targets) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | No named code. Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandates creation of a ***"special agency"*** for persons with special needs — not yet established as a standalone body. Art. IX, Sec. 14 mandates protection of children's rights. |

  <td>The constitutional floor: all enumerated powers operate within the framework of the Constitution. The President retains general supervision but not control over how the Bangsamoro Government exercises this power.</td>

</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 12</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall uphold and protect the fundamental rights of women, including the right to engage in lawful employment, and to be protected from exploitation, abuse, or discrimination, as embodied in the Convention on the Elimination of all Forms of Discrimination Against Women. The Parliament shall enact the necessary laws for the implementation of this section."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Mandates <strong>protection of women's rights</strong> and requires Parliament to enact implementing legislation. Directly intersects with MSSD's protective services mandate for women victims of gender-based violence.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 13</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall recognize the vital role of the youth in nation-building, promote and protect their physical, moral, spiritual, intellectual, and social well-being. It shall inculcate patriotism and nationalism, encourage involvement in public and civic affairs, and promote mental and physical fitness through sports. The Parliament shall create by law a commission on youth and shall define its powers, functions, and composition."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Mandates <strong>youth protection and development</strong>. The BYC (BAA 10) fulfills the commission mandate. MSSD's welfare programs for street children, children in conflict with the law, and orphans complement the BYC's broader youth development mandate.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 14</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall respect, protect, and promote the rights of children, especially orphans of tender age. They shall be protected from exploitation, abuse, or discrimination. Their education and development, both physical and mental, shall be fully addressed."</strong></em></td>
  <td><strong>Child protection mandate</strong> — specifically names orphans. Provides the BOL basis for MSSD's orphanage institutions, centers for street children, and rehabilitation centers for children in conflict with the law (BAA 13, Title XIII, Sec. 12).</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. IX, Sec. 23</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall establish a special agency, support facilities and livelihood or skills training for persons with special needs, and other disadvantaged persons for their rehabilitation and productive integration into mainstream society. The Bangsamoro Government shall coordinate all existing governmental and nongovernmental agencies concerned with the rehabilitation and education of these persons. The Bangsamoro Government shall ensure the welfare of senior citizens by upholding their rights and providing the benefits and privileges they presently enjoy."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Three mandates in one provision: (1) create a <strong>special agency</strong> for persons with special needs and disadvantaged persons; (2) <strong>coordinate</strong> all governmental and non-governmental rehabilitation agencies; (3) <strong>ensure senior citizens' welfare</strong> — with the floor set by benefits and privileges they "presently enjoy" (i.e., those under RA 9994 and RA 7432). This is the strongest BOL mandate for social welfare institutional development.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XII, Sec. 19</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Parliament shall assign the highest budgetary priority to education, health, and social services as may be provided in its appropriations law."</strong></em></td>
  <td><strong>Fiscal priority mandate</strong> — social services is one of three sectors that must receive the "highest budgetary priority" in the annual appropriations law. This constitutionalizes the funding floor for MSSD and related agencies.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. VII, Sec. 38</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall have the power to create offices and organize its own bureaucracy. It may establish other offices and institutions necessary for the exercise of its functions and delivery of social services in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."</strong></em></td>
  <td>General organizational authority — explicitly mentions <strong>"delivery of social services"</strong> as a basis for creating offices and institutions. Provides the legal basis for MSSD's organizational structure and any new social welfare bodies.</td>
</tr>

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(vv) grants authority over ***"Social services, social welfare, and charities."*** This three-part enumeration encompasses: (1) **social services** — direct delivery of welfare programs, interventions, and support facilities to individuals, families, and communities; (2) **social welfare** — the policy, regulatory, and institutional framework governing welfare programs; and (3) **charities** — the regulation of charitable solicitation, fund drives, and donations for welfare purposes. The power is broad and does not contain an internal limitation or qualifier — it is subject only to the Art. V, Sec. 2 chapeau's constitutional floor.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**
A. Seven provisions elaborate on this power:

- **Art. IX, Sec. 8** (Right of Access to Basic Services) — imposes a positive obligation to provide ***"basic and responsive health programs, quality education, appropriate services, livelihood opportunities"*** and mandates disaster-preparedness and calamity rehabilitation.[^2] The phrase "appropriate services" encompasses social welfare interventions.
- **Art. IX, Sec. 12** (Protection of Women) — mandates protection from ***"exploitation, abuse, or discrimination"*** and requires implementing legislation.[^3]
- **Art. IX, Sec. 13** (Rights of the Youth) — mandates protection of youth ***"physical, moral, spiritual, intellectual, and social well-being"*** and creation of a youth commission (fulfilled by BAA 10).[^4]
- **Art. IX, Sec. 14** (Rights of Children) — mandates protection of children, ***"especially orphans of tender age,"*** from exploitation, abuse, or discrimination.[^5]
- **Art. IX, Sec. 23** (Support for Persons with Special Needs, Senior Citizens, and Disadvantaged Groups) — mandates creation of a ***"special agency, support facilities and livelihood or skills training"*** for persons with special needs; coordination of all rehabilitation agencies; and preservation of senior citizens' existing benefits and privileges.[^6]
- **Art. XII, Sec. 19** (Allocation of the Block Grant) — mandates ***"highest budgetary priority to education, health, and social services."***[^7]
- **Art. VII, Sec. 38** (Organization of the Bureaucracy) — authorizes creation of offices ***"necessary for the exercise of its functions and delivery of social services."***[^8]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**
A. The BOL does not name a specific code (unlike the "Bangsamoro cooperative code" in Art. XIII, Sec. 24). However, Art. IX, Sec. 12 mandates: ***"The Parliament shall enact the necessary laws for the implementation of this section"*** — referring to women's protection. Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandates the establishment of a ***"special agency"*** for persons with special needs — an institutional mandate that requires legislation. These are implicit legislative mandates, not named codes.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**
A. The BOL does not directly create MSSD — that was accomplished by BAA 13. But Art. IX, Sec. 23 directs the Bangsamoro Government to ***"establish a special agency"*** for persons with special needs. Art. IX, Sec. 13 directs Parliament to ***"create by law a commission on youth"*** (fulfilled by BAA 10/BYC). Art. IX, Sec. 7 directs creation of a human rights commission (fulfilled by BAA 4/BHRC). Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes Parliament to create offices for women, youth, settler communities, DRRM, and planning.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**
A. Three qualifications:
1. **Constitutional floor** (Art. V, Sec. 2 chapeau) — ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** — all social welfare legislation must operate within the constitutional framework.[^1]
2. **Senior citizens' benefits floor** (Art. IX, Sec. 23) — ***"the benefits and privileges they presently enjoy"*** — existing national benefits under RA 9994 and related laws cannot be diminished.[^6]
3. **Budgetary priority** (Art. XII, Sec. 19) — social services must receive ***"highest budgetary priority"*** — the Parliament cannot deprioritize social services funding.[^7]

No express national law consistency requirement applies to this power.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**
A. Social services and welfare engages some of the Constitution's strongest and most numerous mandates:

- **Universal constraint**: Art. X, Sec. 15 — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***
- **Art. II, Sec. 9** — the State shall ***"promote a just and dynamic social order"*** and ***"provide adequate social services."***
- **Art. II, Sec. 10** — the State shall ***"promote social justice in all phases of national development."***
- **Art. II, Sec. 12** — the State ***"recognizes the sanctity of family life"*** and shall ***"protect and strengthen the family as a basic autonomous social institution."***
- **Art. II, Sec. 13** — the State ***"recognizes the vital role of the youth in nation-building"*** and shall ***"promote and protect their physical, moral, spiritual, intellectual, and social well-being."***
- **Art. II, Sec. 14** — the State ***"recognizes the role of women in nation-building"*** and shall ***"ensure the fundamental equality before the law of women and men."***
- **Art. XIII, Sec. 1** — Congress shall give ***"highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity."***
- **Art. XIII, Sec. 11** — the State shall ***"make essential goods, health and other social services available to all the people at affordable cost"*** with ***"priority for the needs of the underprivileged sick, elderly, disabled, women, and children."***
- **Art. XIII, Sec. 13** — the State shall ***"establish a special agency for disabled persons for rehabilitation, self-development and self-reliance, and their integration into the mainstream of society."***
- **Art. XIII, Sec. 14** — the State shall ***"protect working women"*** and provide facilities to ***"enhance their welfare."***

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**
A. The Constitution guarantees: (1) **equal protection** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — social welfare programs must not discriminate on unconstitutional grounds; (2) **due process** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — removal of benefits requires procedural safeguards; (3) **right to social services** (Art. II, Sec. 9; Art. XIII, Sec. 11) — state obligation to provide affordable social services; (4) **children's welfare** (Art. II, Sec. 13; Art. XV, Sec. 3(2)) — the State shall defend the right of children to assistance; (5) **women's equality** (Art. II, Sec. 14) — fundamental equality before the law; (6) **PWD integration** (Art. XIII, Sec. 13) — rehabilitation and mainstreaming.

These constitutional rights set the **floor** — BARMM cannot reduce protections below what the Constitution mandates, but can exceed them.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 13 mandates a ***"special agency for disabled persons"*** — the national counterpart is the National Council on Disability Affairs (NCDA). Art. IX, Sec. 23 of the BOL mirrors this mandate for the Bangsamoro level. Congress enacted RA 7277 (Magna Carta for Disabled Persons) and RA 10070 (PWD Affairs Offices in LGUs) to implement this constitutional mandate — these laws carry constitutional weight even without a BOL consistency requirement.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**
A. The national social welfare framework is extensive, comprising over a dozen major statutes covering:
- **Child protection**: RA 7610 (child abuse), RA 9344/10630 (juvenile justice), RA 6972 (day care centers), RA 10821 (children's emergency relief), RA 11596 (anti-child marriage)
- **Women's protection**: RA 9262 (VAWC), RA 9710 (Magna Carta of Women), RA 9208/10364 (anti-trafficking)
- **PWD welfare**: RA 7277/9442/10524/10754 (Magna Carta for PWDs), RA 10070 (PWD offices in LGUs), RA 11228 (PhilHealth for PWDs)
- **Senior citizen welfare**: RA 9994 (Expanded Senior Citizens Act), RA 10645 (PhilHealth for seniors)
- **Social protection**: RA 11310 (4Ps/Pantawid), RA 8972 (solo parents)
- **Adoption**: RA 8552 (domestic adoption), RA 8043 (inter-country adoption)

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**
A. All listed national laws apply in BARMM — most **directly** because their criminal law and benefits provisions are residual national powers. Key distinctions:
- **Criminal law provisions** (RA 7610, RA 9262, RA 11596, RA 9208, RA 9775) — apply **directly**; penalties beyond *arresto menor* exceed BARMM jurisdiction
- **Benefits and privileges** (RA 9994, RA 10754, RA 11228) — apply **directly**; Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandates these be preserved
- **Social protection programs** (RA 11310/4Ps) — apply **directly**; DSWD administers nationally
- **Institutional mandates** (RA 10070, RA 6972) — apply **suppletorily** where BAA 49 or a future Bangsamoro social welfare code addresses the same function

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**
A. Three national agencies exercise significant authority:
1. **DSWD** — administers the 4Ps (RA 11310), adoption proceedings (RA 8552), VAWC shelters, and supplementary feeding programs. Retains BARMM regional presence through DSWD-BARMM field office.
2. **NCDA** — coordinates national PWD policy; issues PWD IDs in coordination with LGUs.
3. **Council for the Welfare of Children (CWC)** — coordinates national child welfare policy; monitors RA 7610 compliance.

DSWD's operational presence in BARMM is the most significant overlap — the 4Ps alone serves hundreds of thousands of BARMM beneficiaries and its transfer to MSSD would require legislation, a social registry, and institutional capacity that MSSD does not yet possess.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**
A. The **Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD)** — established by BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII, Sec. 1-22. BAA 85 (FY 2026 GAA) allocates its operational budget.[^9][^10]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**
A. No standalone social welfare code exists. However, five BAAs directly implement aspects of this power: **BAA 57** (mujahideen welfare), **BAA 62** (IDP rights), **BAA 83** (nutrition), **BAA 8** (BWC/women), and **BAA 10** (BYC/youth). These are sector-specific rather than comprehensive.

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title XIII, Sec. 2 states the MSSD mandate: ***"The Ministry of Social Services and Development shall provide a balanced and responsive approach to social welfare whereby the rights, needs, and interests of all citizens within its jurisdiction, with special focus on the marginalized and vulnerable sectors, including women, children, senior citizens, differently abled persons, indigenous cultural communities, internally displaced persons, and those similarly situated by analogy, are protected and addressed at all times, especially during the existence of a crisis, whether natural or human-induced, that affects their well-being and their participation in community affairs."***[^9]

Sec. 3 enumerates **18 powers and functions**:
(a) Formulate, develop and implement plans/programs in social welfare; (b) adopt policies for effective implementation; (c) promote/support/coordinate non-governmental social welfare facilities; (d) establish/operate/maintain institutional facilities; (e) promote/build people's organizations for self-directing welfare; (f) promote/coordinate networks for welfare delivery; (g) accredit social welfare institutions and organizations; (h) undertake researches and studies; (i) initiate bilateral/multilateral linkages; (j) provide advisory services and training programs; (k) disseminate information; (l) deputize law enforcement for protection of exploited/abused/disadvantaged; (m) regulate fund drives, public solicitations, and charitable donations; (n) set standards, accredit, and monitor social welfare activities; (o) exercise functional/technical supervision over social workers in other government settings; (p) deputize LGUs for disaster relief; (q) coordinate programs for marginalized sectors (women, children, senior citizens, PWDs, ICCs, IDPs); and (r) perform other functions as provided by law.[^9]

Sec. 12 authorizes MSSD to establish and operate **9 types of welfare facilities**: (a) Vocational Rehabilitation and Special Learning Centers for PWDs; (b) Senior Citizens Centers; (c) Centers for Street Children; (d) Crisis Intervention Facilities; (e) VAWC Survivor Facilities; (f) Rehabilitation Centers for Children in Conflict with the Law; (g) Reception and Study Centers for Children; (h) Orphanage Institutions; and (i) other facilities as necessary.[^9]

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13 provides the institutional framework but cannot deliver:
- **Comprehensive regulatory standards** — licensing requirements for social welfare agencies, service delivery standards, quality assurance mechanisms
- **Detailed benefits framework** — a Bangsamoro-specific social protection floor defining benefits, eligibility, and delivery mechanisms
- **Penalty provisions** — sanctions for violations of social welfare standards (child abuse, exploitation, non-compliance with facility standards)
- **Shari'ah-compliant models** — *kafala* guardianship system, zakat-based social protection, Islamic orphan care standards
- **Social registry** — a Bangsamoro targeting database for social welfare programs (called for in Res. No. 117)
- **Jurisdictional boundaries** — formal delineation of MSSD vs. DSWD functions, especially for the 4Ps and adoption proceedings

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**
A. Yes — eight resolutions signal legislative intent: Res. No. 43 (orphans/widows bureau), Res. No. 115 (livelihood guidelines), Res. No. 116 (4Ps monitoring), Res. No. 117 (indigent database), Res. No. 147 (drug rehabilitation centers), Res. No. 179 (Marawi IDP return), and Res. No. 450 (MSSD worker safety). These collectively signal demand for a more comprehensive social welfare framework than BAA 13's general mandate provides.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**
A. Yes — MSSD's BAA 13 mandate closely matches the full scope of Art. V, Sec. 2(vv). Its 18 enumerated functions cover social services delivery, welfare regulation, and charities. However, the power's scope extends to populations also served by **BWC** (women), **BYC** (youth), **BHRC** (human rights dimensions), and the **Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission** (BAA 83). MSSD is the correct lead but must coordinate with at least four supporting agencies and the LGU-level social welfare offices.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**
A. The institutional landscape:
- **Lead ministry**: MSSD (BAA 13, Title XIII; BAA 85)
- **Supporting commissions**: BWC (BAA 8) — women's welfare; BYC (BAA 10) — youth welfare; BHRC (BAA 4) — human rights monitoring
- **Specialized body**: Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission (BAA 83) — nutrition programs
- **Sector-specific legislation**: BAA 57 — mujahideen welfare; BAA 62 — IDP rights
- **LGU-level**: Provincial/City Social Welfare Offices under MSSD (BAA 13, Sec. 10-11); Municipal Social Welfare Offices (BAA 13, Sec. 14)
- **Not yet operational**: Bangsamoro Disability Affairs Office (PB 250 — filed, not enacted)

All listed institutions are operational except the proposed Disability Affairs Office.

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**
A. Art. IX, Sec. 23 mandates ***"a special agency"*** for persons with special needs and disadvantaged groups. This has **not** been created as a standalone body. PB 250 (Bangsamoro Disability Affairs Office) would fulfill this mandate but has not been enacted. Currently, MSSD handles PWD services through its general mandate, but the BOL contemplates a dedicated agency.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**
A. Yes — full exercise of this power requires:
1. **Social registry/targeting system** — a database of Bangsamoro indigents and welfare beneficiaries (called for in Res. No. 117) to enable means-tested social protection
2. **Social welfare regulatory body or division** — to license and monitor private social welfare agencies, which MSSD currently does through its general accreditation function (Sec. 3(g)/(n)) but without a regulatory code
3. **Shari'ah social welfare advisory mechanism** — to ensure welfare programs comply with Islamic principles, particularly for orphan care (*kafala*), zakat distribution, and family welfare

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**
A. **DSWD** is the dominant national agency operating in BARMM for social services. It administers: the 4Ps conditional cash transfer program (RA 11310) — the largest social protection program in the country with significant BARMM coverage; the Supplementary Feeding Program; VAWC assistance; adoption proceedings; and disaster response through its Quick Response Fund. DSWD maintains a BARMM regional field office that operates independently of MSSD.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**
A. Key programs: (1) **Pantawid Pamilyang Pilipino Program (4Ps)** — conditional cash transfers to poor households; (2) **Supplementary Feeding Program** — nutrition assistance for underweight children; (3) **Assistance to Individuals in Crisis Situation (AICS)** — emergency financial assistance; (4) **Sustainable Livelihood Program (SLP)** — livelihood development; (5) **KALAHI-CIDSS** — community-driven development; (6) **Listahanan** — the National Household Targeting System for poverty reduction, which is the social registry BARMM lacks.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**
A. Devolution is **partial and incomplete**. MSSD was established and operational social welfare employees were absorbed and transferred to the Bangsamoro Government. However, DSWD retains operational authority over the 4Ps, adoption proceedings, and the Listahanan social registry. The devolution of the 4Ps alone — which requires the social registry infrastructure, payment systems, and compliance verification mechanisms — represents the single largest unresolved devolution issue for this power.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**
A. Yes — significant overlap exists in: (1) **disaster response** — both DSWD and MSSD deploy to disaster-affected areas, sometimes with unclear coordination protocols; (2) **VAWC services** — both DSWD and MSSD operate shelters and crisis intervention facilities; (3) **child welfare** — DSWD retains adoption authority while MSSD operates children's facilities; (4) **livelihood programs** — both run livelihood assistance with different targeting systems. This concurrency is by default (devolution incomplete), not by BOL design.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**
A. Key accomplishments: (1) **Institutional establishment** — MSSD fully operational with provincial/city offices across all five provinces and the Special Geographic Area; (2) **Welfare facilities** — crisis intervention facilities, children's centers, and senior citizen facilities established in multiple locations; (3) **Mujahideen welfare** — BAA 57 implemented, providing financial assistance to senior/disabled ex-combatants; (4) **IDP protection** — BAA 62 enacted, establishing rights framework for displaced persons; (5) **Nutrition commission** — BAA 83 created the Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission; (6) **Fund drive regulation** — MSSD exercises solicitation permit authority under BAA 13, Sec. 15-18.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**
A. BARMM's unique advantages:
- **Conflict-sensitive design** — BAA 57 (mujahideen welfare) and BAA 62 (IDP rights) address populations and situations unique to the Bangsamoro context that the national framework does not specifically serve
- **Cultural and linguistic fit** — MSSD delivers services in local languages through staff who understand Bangsamoro cultural norms
- **Shari'ah integration potential** — the ability to integrate Islamic welfare principles (*kafala*, zakat-based targeting) into social welfare design, which the national system cannot accommodate
- **Institutional proximity** — MSSD's provincial/city offices have closer community access than DSWD's regional structure

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**
A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a standalone social welfare code that would: (a) establish a Bangsamoro social registry to enable means-tested targeting; (b) define jurisdictional boundaries with DSWD, particularly for the 4Ps; (c) create Shari'ah-compliant welfare models; and (d) set regulatory standards for private social welfare agencies. Without this legislation, MSSD operates on BAA 13's general mandate, which provides institutional existence but not the regulatory framework to fully exercise the (vv) power. The secondary constraint is incomplete devolution — DSWD continues to administer major social protection programs within BARMM.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**
A. The national system provides:
- **Legislative depth** — over a dozen major social welfare statutes with decades of IRRs, judicial interpretation, and amendment cycles
- **Listahanan (NHTS-PR)** — a national social registry covering approximately 15 million poor households, enabling means-tested targeting for all social protection programs. BARMM has no equivalent.
- **4Ps payment and compliance system** — automated cash transfer disbursement, school attendance verification, health checkup monitoring — a mature operational system
- **DSWD institutional infrastructure** — 17 regional offices, 81 provincial offices, specialized bureaus (Standards, Protective Services, Community Development), and a network of government-run residential care facilities
- **Adoption and foster care system** — centralized database, accreditation of child-placing agencies, inter-country adoption protocols

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**
A. The national framework fails BARMM in several critical areas:
- **No Shari'ah-compliant alternatives** — RA 8552's adoption framework does not accommodate *kafala* (Islamic guardianship), which many Bangsamoro Muslim families prefer to formal adoption
- **Conflict-insensitive targeting** — Listahanan's poverty assessment does not account for conflict-affected populations, IDPs, or ex-combatants as distinct vulnerability categories
- **Language barriers** — national social welfare forms, communications, and training materials are in Filipino/English, not in Maguindanaon, Maranao, or Tausug
- **Underserved presence** — DSWD's operational presence in BARMM is proportionally lower than in other regions, particularly in island provinces (Sulu, Tawi-Tawi, Basilan)
- **No recognition of Islamic welfare institutions** — the national framework does not integrate mosque-based welfare, zakat distribution, or *ulama*-based counseling into the formal social protection system

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**
A.
- **Replicate**: Financial reporting and audit standards for social welfare programs; social welfare agency licensing requirements (adapt to include Shari'ah-based organizations)
- **Adapt**: Listahanan model — build a Bangsamoro social registry using DSWD's methodology but incorporating conflict-sensitivity indicators, IDP status, and ex-combatant categories; 4Ps compliance system — adapt for local languages and cultural contexts
- **Replace**: RA 8552's adoption framework — enact a *kafala*-based alternative for Muslim families while maintaining RA 8552 for non-Muslim residents; orphan care institutional model — replace residential institutionalization with community-based *kafala* fostering as the primary model

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**
A. Yes — social welfare has a **strong** Shari'ah dimension. Islam places extensive obligations on both individuals and the state to care for the vulnerable. The five *maqasid al-Shari'ah* (objectives of Islamic law) — preservation of religion (*din*), life (*nafs*), intellect (*'aql*), progeny (*nasl*), and wealth (*mal*) — collectively require a social safety net. Orphan care, widow support, and poverty alleviation are among the most emphasized obligations in the Qur'an and *hadith*.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**
A. The BOL does not expressly require Shari'ah compliance for social welfare programs. However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact ***"laws on personal, family, and property law jurisdiction"*** — all of which intersect with social welfare (guardianship, inheritance, orphan care) — all of which intersect with social welfare (guardianship, inheritance, orphan care). PD 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws), Art. 70-72, governs guardianship (*walaya*) of Muslim minors through Shari'ah courts.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**
A. Key principles:
- ***Hifz al-Nafs*** (preservation of life) — the state must protect life, which encompasses disaster response, health-related social services, and support for the destitute
- ***Hifz al-Nasl*** (preservation of progeny) — child protection, orphan care, and family welfare are direct applications
- ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — justifies government social welfare programs and expenditures
- ***Kafala*** (Islamic fostering/guardianship) — the Shari'ah-preferred model for orphan care, distinct from Western-style adoption that severs biological lineage
- ***Zakat*** — the third pillar of Islam; its eight recipient categories (*asnaf*) overlap substantially with social welfare beneficiary categories (the poor, the needy, wayfarers, those in debt)
- ***Waqf*** (Islamic endowment) — a potential institutional mechanism for sustained social welfare funding through endowed assets

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**
A. PD 1083, Art. 70-72 governs guardianship of Muslim minors — Shari'ah courts exercise jurisdiction over custody and guardianship disputes. There is no enacted Bangsamoro Shari'ah legislation on social welfare as of BAA 89. The Shari'ah Legislative Review Board (SLRB) would review any proposed social welfare code for Islamic compliance.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro social welfare legislation?**
A. A **dual-track** approach is appropriate: (1) **mandatory Shari'ah compliance** for programs serving exclusively Muslim beneficiaries (orphan care, guardianship, family welfare) — *kafala* rather than adoption, halal food in welfare facilities, gender-appropriate service delivery; (2) **optional Shari'ah-compliant track** for programs serving mixed populations (livelihood assistance, disaster response) — conventional and Islamic models available side by side; (3) **principled** — all social welfare legislation guided by *maslahah*, *'adl* (justice), and *amanah* (accountability) without mandating specific compliance. The SLRB should review proposed social welfare legislation and the *Darul Ifta'* should be consulted on specific Islamic welfare models.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**
A. Social services and welfare falls in **Tier 4 — Broad** on the divergence spectrum (constitutional floor only). No BOL provision requires consistency with national social welfare laws. The divergence space is as follows:

1. **No consistency requirement** — the BOL does not contain "consistent with" or "in accordance with" language referencing national social welfare statutes for this power. BARMM has broad legislative authority.
2. **Constitutional floor applies** — as established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), Art. XIII, Sec. 11 and Art. XIII, Sec. 13 mandate social services availability and a special agency for PWDs. Any BAA must serve these constitutional purposes.
3. **"Presently enjoy" floor for senior citizens** — as established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. IX, Sec. 23's language creates a one-way ratchet: BARMM can go above RA 9994's benefits but cannot reduce them.
4. **Criminal law stays national** — as established in the **National Law Considerations** section (Section C), criminal provisions in RA 7610, RA 9262, RA 11596, RA 9208, and RA 9344 are residual national powers. BARMM cannot modify criminal penalties for child abuse, VAWC, trafficking, or related offenses.
5. **Shari'ah as unique basis for divergence** — as established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), *kafala*, zakat, and Islamic welfare principles provide a uniquely Bangsamoro justification for different social welfare models. This is the strongest basis for substantive divergence: BARMM can establish a *kafala*-based orphan care system, zakat-integrated social protection, and Islamic-informed welfare standards that the national system cannot provide.

Per *Ali v. BTA Parliament* (GR-02219, 2025), BARMM legislative authority ***"must always operate within the bounds of the Constitution and national statutes."*** For social welfare, this means: divergence is broad for service delivery models, institutional design, and welfare standards; it is limited for criminal protections and existing benefits.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**
A. A Bangsamoro social welfare code that establishes *kafala* instead of adoption would create a dual-track system: Muslim families within BARMM use *kafala* for orphan placement; non-Muslim families and cross-boundary cases revert to RA 8552. Mutual recognition provisions would be needed for: (1) social welfare agency accreditation recognized across BARMM/non-BARMM boundaries; (2) protection order enforcement (RA 9262 protection orders issued by BARMM courts must be recognized nationally, and vice versa); (3) senior citizen and PWD benefit portability — benefits earned under RA 9994/RA 10754 must be honored within BARMM and vice versa.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**
A. The BAA governs within BARMM for matters within the enumerated power. National criminal law provisions (RA 7610, RA 9262, RA 11596, RA 9208, RA 9344) continue to apply as residual national powers. RA 11310 (4Ps) continues to apply until a formal devolution agreement transfers the program to MSSD. National laws on benefits and privileges (RA 9994, RA 10754) continue to apply as the floor — the BAA can add but not subtract.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**
A. The most significant jurisdictional overlap is between **MSSD and DSWD** in BARMM. Specific overlaps: (1) **4Ps administration** — DSWD administers nationally, MSSD has no formal role despite being the Bangsamoro social welfare ministry; (2) **adoption proceedings** — DSWD retains central authority under RA 8552, while MSSD handles local child welfare; (3) **disaster response** — both deploy teams with sometimes unclear coordination; (4) **social welfare agency accreditation** — MSSD accredits under BAA 13, Sec. 3(g), while DSWD also accredits social welfare agencies under its national mandate. Resolution requires either a formal MOA delineating functions or legislation that clearly assigns BARMM-level authority to MSSD.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**
A. Eleven pending PBs address various aspects (listed in the Parliament Bills table above). However, they are **sector-specific** — addressing individual populations (ASD children, women combatants, senior citizens, *ulama*, mujahideen families) rather than providing a comprehensive framework. Even if all were enacted, BARMM would still lack: (a) a unified social welfare code with regulatory standards; (b) a social registry; (c) *kafala* legislation; and (d) jurisdictional delineation with DSWD. PB 250 (Disability Affairs Office) is the most structurally significant because it fulfills the Art. IX, Sec. 23 institutional mandate.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**
A. **BDP Chapter 9** (Social Services) sets targets for poverty reduction, social protection coverage, and improved welfare service delivery. **BDP Chapter 2** (Regional Trends) identifies that ***"social protection will be efficiently and adequately delivered to internally displaced and marginalized communities"*** as a desired outcome. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028), Pillar 3** (Resilient Communities) depends directly on functional social services delivery — disaster preparedness, protection of vulnerable populations, and community-level welfare.

BARMM has the **highest poverty incidence** among Philippine regions. Social services are not just a welfare function but the primary mechanism for poverty reduction, post-conflict recovery, and human development in the region.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**
A. Without a comprehensive social welfare code: (1) **poverty targeting remains blind** — no Bangsamoro social registry means welfare programs cannot effectively identify and reach the poorest households; (2) **4Ps transition stalls** — MSSD cannot assume the region's largest social protection program without legislative authority and institutional infrastructure; (3) **orphan care remains institutional** — without *kafala* legislation, orphans are placed in institutional facilities rather than family-based care, contrary to both Islamic values and international best practice; (4) **PWD services remain fragmented** — the Art. IX, Sec. 23 special agency mandate remains unfulfilled, and PWD services lack a dedicated institutional home; (5) **post-conflict welfare gaps persist** — Marawi IDPs, ex-combatants, and conflict widows/orphans lack a comprehensive framework beyond sector-specific BAAs.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does the (vv) social services power relate to (r) development programs for women, children, youth, and labor?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(r) covers ***"development programs for women, children, youth, and labor."*** The (vv) social services power covers the **welfare, protective, and rehabilitative** dimension for these same populations, while (r) covers the **development, empowerment, and participatory** dimension. In practice: BWC (BAA 8) and BYC (BAA 10) implement the (r) power; MSSD implements the (vv) power. The overlap is at the population level (both serve women, children, youth) but distinct at the intervention level (development programs vs. welfare services). MSSD's coordination function (BAA 13, Sec. 3(q)) is the mechanism for harmonizing (vv) and (r) programs.

**Q. What is the legal basis for MSSD's regulation of charities and fund drives?**
A. BAA 13, Title XIII, Chapter 5 (Sec. 15-18) provides the regulatory framework. Sec. 15 requires any entity ***"desiring to solicit or receive contributions for charitable or public welfare purposes"*** to secure a permit from MSSD. The Minister may issue permanent or temporary permits or disapprove applications. Three categories are exempt: (1) entities with express legislative authority to solicit; (2) religious solicitations within mosques/churches; and (3) personal solicitations among relatives and friends (Sec. 16). Sec. 17 requires reporting on collections and expenditures. Sec. 18 authorizes fee collection.[^9] This is one of the few areas where BAA 13 already provides a relatively complete regulatory framework.

**Q. Can BARMM establish a zakat-based social protection system?**
A. Yes — there is a strong legal basis. Art. V, Sec. 2(vv) grants authority over social services and charities; Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah laws on property matters (zakat is a property-related obligation). A Bangsamoro Zakat Act could: (a) establish a Bangsamoro Zakat Board to collect and distribute zakat; (b) define the eight *asnaf* categories as the targeting framework; (c) integrate zakat collections with MSSD's social welfare programs; (d) provide for voluntary zakat remittance through formal channels. This would be a uniquely Bangsamoro social protection instrument with no national counterpart — and one of the clearest expressions of the divergence authority available under this power.

**Q. How should MSSD handle the *kafala* vs. adoption question?**
A. The tension: RA 8552 (Domestic Adoption Act) establishes a national adoption system administered by DSWD. Classical Islamic jurisprudence does not recognize adoption (*tabanni*) that severs biological lineage but endorses *kafala* (fostering/sponsorship that preserves the child's lineage). A Bangsamoro social welfare code should: (1) establish *kafala* as the primary orphan care model for Muslim children in BARMM; (2) maintain RA 8552 as the suppletory framework for non-Muslim families and inter-country cases; (3) ensure Shari'ah court jurisdiction over *kafala* proceedings (Art. 70-72, PD 1083, already provides guardianship jurisdiction); (4) create a *kafala* registry administered by MSSD. This dual-track approach respects both Islamic law and the national framework.

**Q. What unique populations does BARMM's social welfare system serve that the national system does not specifically address?**
A. Five populations unique to the Bangsamoro context:
1. **Mujahideen** — senior and disabled MILF-MNLF ex-combatants (BAA 57)
2. **Conflict orphans and widows** — children and spouses of those killed in the Bangsamoro armed conflict (Res. No. 43)
3. **Marawi IDPs** — populations displaced by the 2017 Marawi siege who remain in transitional shelters (Res. No. 179)
4. ***Ulama*** and faith-based counselors — religious leaders who provide community welfare services but lack formal social protection (PB 381)
5. **Former women combatants** — female ex-fighters requiring economic empowerment and social reintegration (PB 292)

These populations require specialized social welfare programs that the national DSWD framework does not provide.
