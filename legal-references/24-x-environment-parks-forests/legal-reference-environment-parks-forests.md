# (x) Environment, Parks, Forest Management, Wildlife, and Nature Reserves Conservation

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(x)** — ***"Environment, parks, forest management, wildlife, and nature reserves conservation;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(x) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national environmental law for protected areas — Art. XIII, Sec. 8 subjects the power to ***"the provisions of the Constitution"*** (Regalian Doctrine, Art. XII, Sec. 2). However, Art. XIII, Sec. 8 (para. 5) provides that ***"pending the enactment of such law, the declaration and management of protected areas shall be governed by existing laws, rules and regulations"*** — establishing national law as the interim framework until Parliament legislates. For climate change, Art. XIII, Sec. 7 requires consistency with **RA 9729** by name. Art. XIII, Sec. 10 requires **joint exercise** with the National Government over fossil fuels and uranium. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title III (Sec. 1-12) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31); Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA); Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR); Ministry of Public Works (MOPW) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources); Chapter 4 (Development Framework) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | **Protected areas law** (mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 8 — not yet enacted). Note: the protected areas law is not one of the 7 priority codes listed in Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a). |

  <td>The central provision for this power. It grants: (1) <strong>plenary authority</strong> over natural resources (subject to the Constitution); (2) a <strong>priority mandate</strong> for conservation of forests, coastal, and marine resources; (3) <strong>declaratory power</strong> over nature reserves, parks, forests, watershed reservations, and protected areas; (4) a <strong>named legislative mandate</strong> for a protected areas law; (5) an <strong>FPIC requirement</strong> for protected areas within ancestral domains; and (6) a <strong>transitional provision</strong> applying existing national laws until Parliament legislates. The phrase "subject to the provisions of the Constitution" invokes the <strong>Regalian Doctrine</strong> (Art. XII, Sec. 2).</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XIII, Sec. 9</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The management and protection of nature reserves and aquatic parks, forests, watershed reservations, and other protected areas in the territorial jurisdiction of the Bangsamoro that have already been defined by and under the authority of the National Government shall be transferred to the Bangsamoro Government."</strong></em><br/><br/><em><strong>"Within three (3) months from the establishment of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority, the Bangsamoro Government and the Department of Environment and Natural Resources, as well as other relevant government agencies, shall start the process of transferring the management of these areas to the Bangsamoro Government, including the conduct of surveys of all affected areas and the planning and transition for each and every protected area. The transfer shall be completed within a period not exceeding two (2) years."</strong></em></td>
  <td><strong>Management transfer mandate</strong> with a specific timeline — the process must <strong>start within 3 months</strong> of BTA establishment and be <strong>completed within 2 years</strong>. This is one of the most time-bound provisions in the BOL. The BTA was established in 2019, making the 2-year deadline 2021. <strong>The status of this transfer is a critical implementation question.</strong></td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XIII, Sec. 3</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall develop a comprehensive framework for sustainable development through the proper conservation, utilization, and development of natural resources, which shall guide it in adopting program and policy mechanisms that focus on the environment dimensions of social and economic interventions. It shall include measures for environmental justice and governance, as well as for the reduction of the vulnerability of women and marginalized groups to climate change and variability."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Mandates a <strong>comprehensive framework for sustainable development</strong> with three specific requirements: (1) focus on <strong>environmental dimensions</strong> of all interventions; (2) <strong>environmental justice and governance</strong>; and (3) <strong>climate change vulnerability reduction</strong> for women and marginalized groups. This provision positions the environment not as a standalone sector but as a cross-cutting dimension of all Bangsamoro policy.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XIII, Sec. 29</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government shall regulate, restrict, or prohibit the use, importation, transit, transport, deposit, disposal, and dumping of toxic or hazardous substances within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It shall, in the same manner, regulate activities that may adversely impact the environment and may be harmful to the health, safety, and welfare of the Bangsamoro people."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Environmental protection provision — grants <strong>regulatory power over toxic and hazardous substances</strong> and authority to <strong>regulate environmentally harmful activities</strong>. Broader than pollution control: covers the full chain from importation through disposal.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. VI, Sec. 8</td>
  <td><em><strong>"The Parliament shall create a Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board composed of representatives from the National Government and the Bangsamoro Government.   <td>Mandates an <strong>intergovernmental body</strong> for sustainable development — the <strong>Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BSDB)</strong>. Fulfilled by <strong>BAA 31</strong>. The BSDB's mandate is to <strong>harmonize environmental considerations</strong> with economic and social policy — positioning it as a cross-cutting coordination body, not a regulatory one.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XIII, Sec. 2</td>
  <td><em><strong>"In order to protect and improve the quality of life of its inhabitants, the development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall be carefully planned taking into consideration the ecological balance and the natural resources that are available for its use and for the use of future generations."</strong></em></td>
  <td>Establishes <strong>intergenerational equity</strong> as a guiding principle — development must consider resources <strong>for future generations</strong>. This is an environmental safeguard embedded in the economic development framework.</td>
</tr>
<tr>

  <td><em><strong>"The Bangsamoro Government, consistent with Republic Act No. 9729, otherwise known as the 'Climate Change Act of 2009,' shall ensure that its constituent local government units formulate their respective local climate change action plans and for the proper and effective implementation thereof."</strong></em></td>

Climate change action plans must be <strong>consistent with RA 9729</strong>. Also creates the <strong>Bangsamoro DRRM Council</strong> headed by the Chief Minister.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td>Art. XIII, Sec. 10</td>
  <td><em><strong>"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution and national laws, the Bangsamoro Government and the National Government shall jointly exercise the power to grant rights, privileges, and concessions over the exploration, development, and utilization of uranium and fossil fuels such as petroleum, natural gas, and coal in the territorial jurisdiction of the Bangsamoro."</strong></em></td>
  <td><strong>Joint exercise</strong> requirement — fossil fuels and uranium are the only natural resources where the Bangsamoro Government must act <strong>jointly with the National Government</strong>. All other natural resources fall under the Bangsamoro Government's plenary authority (subject to the Constitution). This creates a two-tier system: (1) non-fossil natural resources — Bangsamoro authority; (2) fossil fuels/uranium — joint authority.</td>

  <td><em>Khalifah</em> (Stewardship / Vicegerency)</td>
  <td>Quran (2:30): Humanity is appointed as <em>khalifah</em> (steward/vicegerent) on earth — entrusted with the care and management of the natural world. The earth is an <em>amanah</em> (trust) from Allah.</td>
  <td>The foundational Islamic environmental principle. Environmental management is not merely a regulatory function but a <strong>sacred trust</strong>. Legislation that degrades the environment violates the <em>khalifah</em> obligation. This principle strengthens the BOL's priority mandate for conservation.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td><em>La Darar wa la Dirar</em> (No Harm, No Reciprocal Harm)</td>
  <td>Prophetic hadith (Ibn Majah, Muwatta): <em>"There should be neither harm nor reciprocal harm."</em> One of the five maxims of Islamic jurisprudence.</td>
  <td>Directly applicable to <strong>pollution control, toxic substance regulation, and environmental impact assessment</strong>. Activities that harm the environment — deforestation, water pollution, hazardous waste dumping — violate this principle. Provides Shari'ah basis for the regulatory powers in BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 29.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td><em>Hima</em> (Protected Reserve)</td>
  <td>Classical Islamic institution: a designated area where resource extraction (grazing, logging, hunting) is restricted or prohibited for the common good. Established by the Prophet Muhammad and the early caliphs for communal pasture and watershed protection.</td>
  <td>Direct <strong>Shari'ah precedent for protected areas</strong>. The <em>hima</em> system predates modern protected area management by 14 centuries and provides a culturally resonant framework for BARMM protected areas legislation. A Bangsamoro Protected Areas Act could formally recognize <em>hima</em> as a category of protected area alongside NIPAS categories.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td><em>Harim</em> (Inviolable Zone)</td>
  <td>Buffer zone around wells, rivers, and water sources where no development or resource extraction is permitted. Purpose: protect water quality and ensure equitable access.</td>
  <td>Applicable to <strong>watershed protection and buffer zones</strong> around water bodies in BARMM. Provides Shari'ah basis for watershed reservation declarations under BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 8.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td><em>Ihya al-Mawat</em> (Reviving Dead Land)</td>
  <td>Prophetic hadith (Bukhari, Muslim): <em>"Whoever revives dead land, it becomes his."</em> Islamic land reclamation doctrine — encourages productive use of barren or abandoned land.</td>
  <td>Applicable to <strong>reforestation and land rehabilitation</strong> programs. Provides Shari'ah basis for community-based forest management and reforestation initiatives on degraded land. However, must be balanced with protected area restrictions — <em>ihya</em> cannot override <em>hima</em> designations.</td>
</tr>
<tr>
  <td><em>Maslahah</em> (Public Interest / Welfare)</td>
  <td>Islamic jurisprudential principle permitting legislation that serves the common good, even where no specific textual authority exists, provided it does not contradict established Shari'ah rules.</td>
  <td>Environmental regulation serves <em>maslahah</em> by protecting public health, preserving resources for future generations, and maintaining ecological balance. Legislation restricting resource extraction or mandating environmental protection is justified under this principle.</td>
</tr>

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over environment, parks, forests, wildlife, and nature reserves?**

A. Article V, Section 2(x) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"environment, parks, forest management, wildlife, and nature reserves conservation"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is among the broadest single-power grants in the BOL — covering five distinct subject-matter areas (**environmental protection**, **park management**, **forest management**, **wildlife conservation**, and **nature reserves conservation**) in a single enumeration. The scope extends to regulatory, management, conservation, and development functions across the entire environmental sector within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Seven provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 8 is the central provision. It grants ***"the power, authority, and right to explore, develop, and utilize the natural resources"*** (subject to the Constitution); mandates that ***"the protection, conservation, rehabilitation, and development of forests, coastal, and marine resources... to ensure the maintenance of ecological balance and biodiversity shall be given priority"***; grants the power to ***"declare nature reserves and aquatic parks, forests, watershed reservations, and other protected areas"***; and mandates Parliament to ***"pass a law to establish protected areas, the procedure for the declaration and the management thereof."***[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandates the ***"transfer"*** of management of existing protected areas from national agencies to the Bangsamoro Government, with a timeline: ***"within three (3) months"*** to start, ***"completed within a period not exceeding two (2) years."***[^3]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 3 mandates a ***"comprehensive framework for sustainable development through the proper conservation, utilization, and development of natural resources"*** — including measures for ***"environmental justice and governance"*** and ***"reduction of the vulnerability of women and marginalized groups to climate change."***[^4]

   (4) Art. XIII, Sec. 29 grants regulatory power over ***"toxic or hazardous substances"*** and authority to ***"regulate activities that may adversely impact the environment."***[^5]

   (5) Art. VI, Sec. 8 mandates the creation of a ***"Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board"*** to ***"ensure the integration and harmonization of economic, social, and environmental considerations."***[^6]

   (6) Art. XIII, Sec. 2 establishes intergenerational equity: development must take into account ***"the ecological balance and the natural resources that are available for its use and for the use of future generations."***[^7]

   (7) Art. XIII, Sec. 7 addresses climate change and DRRM, requiring consistency with RA 9729 and mandating LGU climate change action plans.[^8]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. Yes. Art. XIII, Sec. 8 mandates: ***"The Parliament shall pass a law to establish protected areas, the procedure for the declaration and the management thereof, and the role of the Bangsamoro Government and other stakeholders in the process."*** This is a **named legislative mandate** — the BOL specifies what the law must cover (protected areas establishment, procedure, management, stakeholder roles). This mandate has **not been fulfilled** as of BAA 89.[^2]

   Additionally, Art. XIII, Sec. 16 mandates that ***"policies on mining and other extractive industries shall be established by the Parliament in accordance with a comprehensive sustainable development plan"*** — relevant to the environmental protection dimension of mining regulation.[^9]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. Yes. The BOL creates two bodies:

   (1) The **Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board** — Art. VI, Sec. 8 mandates its creation by Parliament. Fulfilled by **BAA 31**.[^6]

   (2) The **Bangsamoro DRRM Council** — Art. XIII, Sec. 7 creates this body, headed by the Chief Minister. The environmental component (climate change adaptation) intersects with this power.[^8]

   MENRE itself is not expressly created by the BOL but is established through **BAA 13**, Book VI, Title III.[^10]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places five types of limitations:

   (1) **Constitutional subjection**: Art. XIII, Sec. 8 states ***"subject to the provisions of the Constitution"*** — invoking the Regalian Doctrine (all natural resources are owned by the State) and Art. X, Sec. 20 (autonomous region framework). See the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B) for details.[^2]

   (2) **FPIC requirement**: Protected areas within ancestral domains require ***"free, prior and informed consent of the non-Moro indigenous peoples."***[^2]

   (3) **Transitional provision**: ***"Pending the enactment of such law, the declaration and management of protected areas shall be governed by existing laws, rules and regulations."*** National law applies until Parliament legislates.[^2]

   (4) **Joint exercise for fossil fuels**: Art. XIII, Sec. 10 requires the Bangsamoro Government and National Government to ***"jointly exercise"*** authority over fossil fuels and uranium — the only natural resources with this constraint.[^11]

   (5) **Named national law consistency**: Art. XIII, Sec. 7 requires consistency with ***"Republic Act No. 9729"*** (Climate Change Act) for climate change action plans.[^8]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to environment, parks, forests, wildlife, and nature reserves?**

A. Five categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15 and 20)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law."*** This is the universal constitutional boundary on all 55 powers.[^12]

   (2) **Regalian Doctrine (Art. XII, Sec. 2)**: ***"All lands of the public domain, waters, minerals, coal, petroleum, and other mineral oils, all forces of potential energy, fisheries, forests or timber, wildlife, flora and fauna, and other natural resources are owned by the State."*** This is the foundational constitutional limitation on this power. The Bangsamoro Government exercises **management and regulatory authority** over natural resources but does not acquire **ownership** — that remains with the State.[^13]

   (3) **Classification of Public Lands (Art. XII, Sec. 3)**: ***"Lands of the public domain are classified into agricultural, forest or timber, mineral lands, and national parks."*** Only Congress has the power to **reclassify** lands of the public domain. The Bangsamoro Government may recommend classification (BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 17) but cannot unilaterally reclassify forest land or national parks.[^14]

   (4) **Right to a Balanced and Healthful Ecology (Art. II, Sec. 16)**: ***"The State shall protect and advance the right of the people to a balanced and healthful ecology in accord with the rhythm and harmony of nature."*** Recognized as a **self-executory right** by the Supreme Court in *Oposa v. Factoran* (1993). The Bangsamoro Government has an affirmative obligation to protect this right.[^15]

   (5) **Equal Protection and Due Process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: Regulatory actions — permit denials, closures, penalties — must satisfy procedural due process (notice, hearing, right to appeal) and substantive due process (rational basis).[^16]

**Q. How does the Regalian Doctrine interact with the BOL's grant of environmental authority?**

A. The Regalian Doctrine means that the Bangsamoro Government exercises **delegated authority** over natural resources — not ownership. The State retains ownership of forests, minerals, wildlife, and all natural resources. What the BOL grants is the **power to manage, regulate, conserve, and develop** these resources within the BARMM territory. This has three practical implications:

   (1) The Bangsamoro Government **cannot alienate** forest lands, mineral lands, or national parks — Art. XII, Sec. 3 limits alienation to agricultural lands.

   (2) Permits, licenses, and agreements for resource utilization are exercises of **management authority**, not dispositions of State property.

   (3) Any environmental legislation enacted by Parliament operates **within** the Regalian framework — it cannot claim Bangsamoro ownership of natural resources separate from the Philippine State.

**Q. What constitutional mandates for environmental legislation or institutions exist?**

A. Art. II, Sec. 16 establishes the **state policy** on ecology. Art. XII, Sec. 2 mandates that natural resource utilization must be ***"under the full control and supervision of the State"*** — the Bangsamoro Government acts as the State's delegated agent within BARMM. Congress created the DENR (via EO 192) to implement this mandate nationally; MENRE is the BARMM counterpart.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for environment, parks, forests, wildlife, and nature reserves?**

A. The national framework is a layered system of laws accumulated over five decades:

   **Protected areas**: RA 7586 (NIPAS Act, 1992) as expanded by RA 11038 (ENIPAS Act, 2018) — establishes categories (strict nature reserve, natural park, wildlife sanctuary, protected landscape/seascape, resource reserve, natural biotic area, marine reserve), management structure (Protected Area Management Board), buffer zones, prohibited acts, and management plans.

   **Forests**: PD 705 (Revised Forestry Code, 1975) — forest classification, timber licensing, reforestation, penalties for illegal logging. Supplemented by EO 263 (1995, community-based forest management) and EO 318 (2004, sustainable forest management).

   **Wildlife**: RA 9147 (Wildlife Resources Conservation and Protection Act, 2001) — protected species, collection permits, trade regulation, wildlife management areas.

   **Air quality**: RA 8749 (Clean Air Act, 1999) — emission standards, permits to operate, ambient air quality standards.

   **Water quality**: RA 9275 (Clean Water Act, 2004) — effluent standards, discharge permits, water quality management areas.

   **Solid waste**: RA 9003 (Ecological Solid Waste Management Act, 2000) — LGU-level waste management.

   **Climate change**: RA 9729 (Climate Change Act, 2009) — Climate Change Commission, national framework strategy.

   **Mining**: RA 7942 (Philippine Mining Act, 1995) — mining agreements, environmental protection requirements, mine rehabilitation.

   **Environmental impact**: EO 291 (1987, EIS system) — environmental impact assessment for development projects.

   Together, these laws provide: a **protected areas system**, **forest management framework**, **wildlife conservation regime**, **pollution control standards**, **environmental impact assessment**, and **institutional infrastructure** — the baseline against which any Bangsamoro environmental law will be compared.[^17][^18][^19][^20][^21][^22][^23][^24]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national environmental laws apply — either directly or suppletorily:

   (1) **RA 7586/RA 11038** (NIPAS/ENIPAS) — **directly applicable** pending Parliament's protected areas law per BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 8, para. 5.[^17]
   (2) **RA 9729** (Climate Change Act) — **directly applicable** by express BOL reference (Art. XIII, Sec. 7).[^23]
   (3) **PD 705** (Revised Forestry Code) — **suppletory**: no Bangsamoro forestry code enacted.[^18]
   (4) **RA 9147** (Wildlife Act) — **suppletory**: no Bangsamoro wildlife law enacted.[^19]
   (5) **RA 8749** (Clean Air Act) — **suppletory**: MENRE implements within BARMM.[^20]
   (6) **RA 9275** (Clean Water Act) — **suppletory**: MENRE implements within BARMM.[^21]
   (7) **RA 9003** (Solid Waste Management) — **suppletory**: applies to BARMM LGUs.[^22]
   (8) **RA 7942** (Mining Act) — applicable as baseline; BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 13 adds Bangsamoro authority.[^24]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this power?**

A. The **Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR)** is the primary national agency. DENR maintains regional and field offices within BARMM territory. BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandates the **transfer of management** of existing protected areas from DENR to the Bangsamoro Government — but this transfer is an ongoing process. Other national agencies with environmental jurisdiction in BARMM include: the **Climate Change Commission** (RA 9729), the **National Commission on Indigenous Peoples (NCIP)** for FPIC on protected areas within ancestral domains, and the **Mines and Geosciences Bureau (MGB)** for mining-related environmental compliance.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III (Sec. 1-12) defines MENRE's institutional status, mandate, powers, functions, organizational structure, and field offices. BAA 85, Chapter XIII allocates its operational budget.[^10][^25]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. One dedicated BAA: **BAA 31** (Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board Act of 2022) creates the BSDB per BOL Art. VI, Sec. 8. No standalone environmental code, protected areas law, forestry code, wildlife act, or pollution control law has been enacted as of BAA 89. This power is exercised primarily through **MENRE's BAA 13 general mandate** and **BAA 85 budget allocation**.

**Q. What are MENRE's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III (Sec. 1-12) defines MENRE comprehensively:

   **Declaration of Policies** (Sec. 1): Three policy objectives — (a) exercise power over natural resources including ***"surface and sub-surface rights, inland waters, coastal waters, mines and minerals and renewable and non-renewable resources"***; (b) ensure ***"continuous, adequate and economic supply of energy"*** and promote renewable energy; (c) give ***"priority to the protection, conservation, rehabilitation and development of forests, coastal and marine resources"*** for ***"ecological balance and biodiversity."***[^10]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 2): MENRE shall be ***"primarily responsible for the exploration, utilization, management, conservation, protection and sustainable development of the region's environment, natural resources and energy sources."***[^26]

   **Powers and functions** (Sec. 3) — **19 enumerated functions**:

   - (a) ***"Recommend the enactment of regional laws relative to the development, utilization, regulation and conservation of the natural resources and energy and the control of pollution;"***
   - (b) ***"Formulate, supervise, and implement policies, plans and programs pertaining to the management, conservation, protection, development and utilization of the region's environment, natural resources, and energy;"***
   - (c) Promulgate rules and regulations governing exploration, development, conservation, and activities tending to cause ***"depletion and degradation of our environment, natural resources, and energy;"***
   - (d) ***"Exercise supervision and control over forest lands, agricultural lands, mineral resources, and national parks"***
   - (e) ***"Recommend the declaration of nature reserves and marine parks, forests, watershed reservations and other protected areas"***
   - (f) Undertake exploration, assessment, classification and inventory of the region's environment, natural resources, and energy
   - (g) Promote consultation with the private sector on natural resources development
   - (h) Undertake geological surveys including territorial waters
   - (i) Establish programs for: (1) accelerated inventory; (2) equitable distribution of natural resources; (3) promotion of natural resources-based industries; (4) ***"preservation of cultural and natural heritage through wildlife conservation and segregation of national parks and other protected areas"***; (5) ***"maintenance of a wholesome natural environment by enforcing environmental protection laws"***; (6) greater people participation
   - (j) Promulgate rules for: (1) accelerated cadastral surveys; (2) sustainable forest utilization; (3) forest-based industry support; (4) mineral resource surveys
   - (k) Regulate the development, disposition, exploration and utilization of forests, land, mineral and energy resources
   - (l) Exercise administration and supervision of all public domain lands
   - (m) Promulgate rules for ***"control of water, air and land pollution"***
   - (n) Recommend policies for fossil fuel and uranium concessions
   - (o) Supervise electric power industry restructuring (subject to Parliament law)
   - (p) Build and supervise energy-related projects
   - (q) Promulgate policies for ***"conservation of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region's genetic resources and biological diversity, and endangered habitats"***
   - (r) Formulate an ***"integrated, multi-sectoral and multi-disciplinary regional conservation strategy"***
   - (s) Perform such other acts as necessary[^27]

   **Organizational structure** (Sec. 4, 9): MENRE consists of the Ministry Proper, Services, and Field Offices. Eleven services and bureaus:

   (1) Legal and Legislative Liaison Division
   (2) Internal Audit Division
   (3) Information and Communications Division
   (4) Planning Division
   (5) **Administrative and Finance Services** (Director II)
   (6) **Forest Management Services** (Director II) — with Community-Based Forest Management Division, Forest Protection and Law Enforcement Division, and Forest Resources Management Division
   (7) **Land Management Services** (Director II) — with Surveys Division and Land Utilization Development Division
   (8) **Mines and Geosciences Services** (Director II) — with Geosciences Division, Mines and Technology Management Division, and Mines Safety Environment and Social Development Division
   (9) **Environment Management Services** (Director II) — with Water Compliance Monitoring Division, Solid Waste Management Division, Environmental Impact Assessment Division, and Air Compliance Monitoring Division
   (10) **Biodiversity Ecosystems Research and Development Services** (Director II) — with Biodiversity Resource Management Division, Protected Area Management Division, Research and Development Information Division, and Technology Transfer Division
   (11) **Energy Management and Development Services** (Director II) — with Energy Resource Development Division and Energy Utilization Management Division[^28]

   **Ministry leadership** (Sec. 5-8): The Minister heads MENRE, assisted by a Deputy Minister. Two **Bangsamoro Directors-General** — the highest career officials — oversee (a) Environment and Natural Resources and (b) Energy, respectively. They supervise the Service Directors.[^29]

   **Field Offices** (Sec. 10-12): **Provincial Environment, Natural Resources and Energy Offices (PENREOs)** and two **Community Environment, Natural Resources and Energy Offices (CENREOs)** in every province. Field offices deliver frontline services, coordinate with LGUs, and implement ministry programs.[^30]

#### D.1.A What the Lead Ministry Cannot Do Without Standalone Legislation

**Q. What can MENRE NOT do without standalone environmental legislation?**

A. While BAA 13 gives MENRE broad functions, it does not provide:

   (1) A **protected areas law** with specific categories, declaration procedures, management plans, Protected Area Management Boards, buffer zone regulations, and penalties — the BOL expressly mandates this (Art. XIII, Sec. 8).

   (2) A **Bangsamoro forestry code** with forest classification, timber licensing standards, community forestry tenure instruments, and penalty provisions specific to BARMM conditions.

   (3) An **environmental code** integrating EIA requirements, pollution standards, and environmental enforcement mechanisms into a single BARMM regulatory framework.

   (4) **Penalty provisions** for environmental violations under Bangsamoro law — MENRE must rely on national law penalties.

   (5) A **clear jurisdictional boundary** with DENR for the management transfer of existing protected areas (Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandates transfer but no implementing legislation defines the process, timelines, or resource allocation).

   (6) **Revenue mechanisms** from environmental permits and resource utilization — without a legislative basis, MENRE's revenue-generating capacity is limited.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **DENR** maintains regional and field offices within BARMM territory and continues to exercise significant authority — particularly over **protected areas management**, **Environmental Compliance Certificates (ECCs)**, **forest management permits**, and **mining environmental compliance**. The BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandated that management transfer to the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall be completed within a period not exceeding two (2) years"*** from BTA establishment (2019). The 2-year deadline passed in 2021. The transfer process has been initiated but **not completed** for all protected areas and natural resource management functions.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. DENR operates several programs: (1) **Protected Area Management** — through the Biodiversity Management Bureau and regional PAMB offices for NIPAS/ENIPAS-declared areas; (2) **Community-Based Forest Management Program (CBFM)** — tenure instruments and community forest agreements; (3) **National Greening Program** — reforestation and agroforestry; (4) **Environmental Impact Assessment** — processing ECCs for development projects; (5) **Forest Protection** — anti-illegal logging task forces; (6) **Air and Water Quality Monitoring** — through Environmental Management Bureau field offices. The **Mines and Geosciences Bureau (MGB)** monitors mining environmental compliance. The **Climate Change Commission** coordinates climate change adaptation. **NCIP** processes FPIC requirements for projects in ancestral domains.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MENRE has been **institutionally established** under BAA 13 with the full organizational structure (11 services/bureaus), two Directors-General, PENREOs in each province, and CENREOs. It has an operational budget under BAA 85, Chapter XIII. The **Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board** has been created through BAA 31. MENRE processes **environmental permits** (ECCs and Certificates of Non-Coverage), conducts **air and water compliance monitoring**, implements **reforestation programs** (as urged by Resolution 399), and manages **biodiversity research and development** through its dedicated services. Parliament has passed **three resolutions** signaling environmental priorities (reforestation, tree planting, single-use plastics). **Seven PBs** have been filed addressing environmental protection. However, **no standalone environmental legislation** has been enacted — the protected areas law mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 8 remains unfulfilled.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three unique advantages:

   (1) **Shari'ah-based environmental stewardship** — the *khalifah* (vicegerency) and *hima* (protected reserve) concepts provide culturally resonant frameworks for environmental protection that the national system does not offer. A Bangsamoro Protected Areas Act could formally recognize *hima* as a protected area category — giving environmental protection a **religious and cultural mandate** alongside the legal one (see the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E)).

   (2) **Conflict-sensitive environmental management** — BARMM's environmental programs must account for post-conflict realities: displaced communities occupying forest lands, degraded landscapes from decades of conflict, and the need to balance livelihood recovery with conservation. MENRE's integrated mandate allows it to coordinate environmental rehabilitation with peace and development programs in ways DENR's standard frameworks cannot.

   (3) **Intergovernmental coordination body** — the BSDB (BAA 31) provides a dedicated intergovernmental mechanism for harmonizing environmental, economic, and social considerations. No comparable body exists in non-autonomous regions.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. Three primary constraints:

   (1) **Absence of standalone environmental legislation** — the protected areas law mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 8 is the most critical gap. Without it, MENRE cannot establish a BARMM-specific protected areas management framework, create region-specific categories (including *hima*), or define penalties under Bangsamoro law.

   (2) **Incomplete management transfer** — the 2-year deadline for transferring protected area management from DENR (Art. XIII, Sec. 9) has passed. The transfer is ongoing but incomplete, creating **dual management** situations where both DENR and MENRE have overlapping claims.

   (3) **Technical capacity** — environmental management requires specialized expertise (EIA review, biodiversity assessment, pollution monitoring, GIS mapping) that MENRE is still building.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built a **comprehensive environmental governance system** over five decades:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 7586/11038 (NIPAS/ENIPAS) provides a complete protected areas framework with 94+ declared areas, management boards, zoning regulations, and penalty provisions. PD 705 (Forestry Code) provides comprehensive forest management. RA 9147 (Wildlife Act), RA 8749 (Clean Air Act), RA 9275 (Clean Water Act), and RA 9003 (Solid Waste Act) cover the full spectrum of environmental protection.
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: DENR has been operational since 1987 (EO 192), with regional offices in every region, provincial (PENROs) and community (CENROs) offices, and specialized bureaus — Forest Management Bureau, Biodiversity Management Bureau, Environmental Management Bureau, Mines and Geosciences Bureau, and Land Management Bureau.
   - **Regulatory systems**: Environmental Impact Statement system, Protected Area Management Boards, NIPAS management plans, forest tenure instruments (CBFM agreements, community-based forest management plans), pollution monitoring networks.
   - **IT and data**: National NIPAS database, GIS mapping of forest cover, air and water quality monitoring networks.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. Four critical gaps:

   (1) **No recognition of Islamic environmental principles**: The national framework has no concept of *hima*, *harim*, or *khalifah*-based stewardship. These culturally resonant frameworks could dramatically improve community participation in environmental protection within BARMM — but the national law does not accommodate them.

   (2) **No conflict-sensitive environmental governance**: NIPAS management assumes stable communities with functioning LGUs. In BARMM's post-conflict context — where communities have been displaced, forests degraded by decades of armed conflict, and LGU capacity is still being rebuilt — the standard DENR approach does not fit.

   (3) **Underresourcing**: DENR's BARMM regional offices have historically been among the least-resourced nationally — fewer staff, less equipment, and weaker enforcement capacity compared to Luzon and Visayas regions.

   (4) **Management transfer delays**: The BOL mandated transfer within 2 years (by 2021); the process is still incomplete. This creates confusion about jurisdictional authority and accountability.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Environmental stewardship is among the **most extensively addressed** subjects in Islamic law and theology. The Quran describes humanity's role as *khalifah* (vicegerent/steward) on earth — entrusted with the care and management of the natural world as an *amanah* (trust) from Allah. Environmental degradation is not merely an administrative or regulatory failure but a **violation of sacred trust**. This gives environmental legislation in BARMM a dual foundation — both statutory (BOL, national law) and religious (Shari'ah principles). The *hima* system of protected reserves predates modern protected area management by 14 centuries and provides a culturally resonant model for community-based conservation.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. XIII, Sec. 8 or 9 (the environmental provisions). However, Art. XIII, Sec. 3 mandates a ***"comprehensive framework for sustainable development"*** — which, in the BARMM context, necessarily integrates Islamic principles. Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact laws on Shari'ah matters that extend beyond PD 1083, including commercial law that could encompass natural resource concessions and environmental regulations. The BOL's broader framework — particularly Art. II (identity provisions) and Art. IX (rights provisions) — recognizes Islam as foundational to Bangsamoro identity.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Six Islamic legal principles directly apply:

   (1) ***Khalifah*** (stewardship/vicegerency) — humanity is entrusted with caring for the earth. Environmental management is a sacred duty, not merely a regulatory function.

   (2) ***La darar wa la dirar*** (no harm, no reciprocal harm) — one of the five maxims of Islamic jurisprudence. Pollution, deforestation, and environmental degradation constitute *darar* (harm) that Islamic law prohibits.

   (3) ***Hima*** (protected reserve) — the Prophet Muhammad and early caliphs established *hima* areas where grazing, logging, and hunting were restricted for the common good. This is the oldest documented system of protected area management and provides a direct Shari'ah basis for BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 8.

   (4) ***Harim*** (inviolable zone) — buffer zones around water sources where no development is permitted. Directly applicable to watershed protection.

   (5) ***Ihya al-mawat*** (reviving dead land) — encourages rehabilitation of degraded land. Applicable to reforestation but must be balanced with protected area restrictions.

   (6) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — environmental protection serves the common good, justifying legislation that restricts individual resource exploitation.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro environmental legislation?**

A. The recommended approach is **integration, not a separate track**. Unlike cooperatives (where conventional and Shari'ah-compliant models need distinct legal categories), environmental protection is universally required under both secular and Islamic law. A Bangsamoro Protected Areas Act should: (a) formally recognize ***hima*** as a category of protected area alongside NIPAS categories — giving communities a culturally resonant framework for conservation; (b) incorporate ***harim*** principles into watershed and buffer zone regulations; (c) include ***khalifah*** stewardship language in the preamble and policy declaration — strengthening the moral and cultural authority of environmental protection; and (d) allow **community-managed protected areas** modeled on the classical *hima* institution, where traditional leaders and ulama participate in management alongside MENRE.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national environmental law?**

A. BARMM has **significant but structured** legislative authority to diverge. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), the BOL expressly mandates Parliament to pass a protected areas law — congressional authorization for a distinct framework. However, divergence capacity varies by subject area:

   (1) **Protected areas** — broadest divergence. Art. XIII, Sec. 8 mandates Parliament to legislate, and the transitional provision (existing laws apply "pending the enactment of such law") makes clear that a Bangsamoro law will **replace** NIPAS within BARMM. BARMM can create new categories (e.g., *hima*), different management structures, and region-specific procedures.

   (2) **Forest management** — moderate divergence. PD 705 (Revised Forestry Code) applies suppletorily but no BOL provision expressly mandates a Bangsamoro forestry code. Parliament can legislate (under the general Art. V, Sec. 2(x) power), but divergence should build on — not contradict — constitutional provisions on forest land classification.

   (3) **Pollution control** — moderate divergence. BARMM can set **stricter** standards than national law (RA 8749, RA 9275, RA 9003) but cannot set **lower** standards — the constitutional right to a balanced and healthful ecology (Art. II, Sec. 16, as established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B)) sets the floor.

   (4) **Climate change** — limited divergence. Art. XIII, Sec. 7 **names RA 9729 expressly** and requires consistency — this is one of the few areas where national law consistency is mandated by the BOL.

   (5) **Mining environmental standards** — limited divergence. Art. XIII, Sec. 13 subjects mining authority to ***"the provisions of the Constitution and national laws."***

   The constitutional floor is the **Regalian Doctrine** (Art. XII, Sec. 2, as established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B)) and the **right to a balanced ecology** (Art. II, Sec. 16). BARMM can go **above** this floor (more protective) but not **below** it. As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), Shari'ah principles — particularly *hima* and *khalifah* — provide additional bases for environmental legislation that go beyond the national framework without conflicting with it.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For residents within BARMM**: They would gain stronger environmental protection if the Bangsamoro framework incorporates *hima* reserves, stricter pollution standards, and community-based management models. The risk is if standards are **lower** — this could face constitutional challenge under Art. II, Sec. 16.

   (2) **For cross-boundary operations**: Companies operating in both BARMM and non-BARMM areas (particularly mining and forestry operations) would face **dual compliance** — MENRE within BARMM, DENR outside. A Bangsamoro environmental code should include **mutual recognition** provisions for ECCs and other environmental permits.

   (3) **For existing protected areas**: Protected areas that straddle BARMM and non-BARMM territory (or that were declared under NIPAS/ENIPAS) need a **transition provision** defining how they are governed during and after the management transfer under Art. XIII, Sec. 9.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between **MENRE** and **DENR**. Three specific jurisdictional uncertainties:

   (1) **Protected areas management transfer** — Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandates transfer, but no implementing legislation defines: which protected areas have been transferred, the resource allocation (staff, equipment, budget) accompanying transfer, or how PAMB composition changes when management shifts from DENR to MENRE.

   (2) **Environmental Compliance Certificates (ECCs)** — both DENR (under the national EIS system) and MENRE (under BAA 13 Sec. 3(c)) claim authority to process environmental permits. Without a Bangsamoro EIA law, the jurisdictional boundary is unclear for projects within BARMM.

   (3) **Mining environmental compliance** — BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 13 gives BARMM jurisdiction subject to national laws, while MGB continues to monitor mining environmental compliance under RA 7942. The overlap creates compliance uncertainty for mining operations.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Seven PBs address various aspects of this power, but **none addresses the core gap** — the protected areas law mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 8. The filed PBs (362, 278, 248, 389, 378, 385, 238) address specific environmental issues (reforestation, river basin management, plastics, solid waste, water sustainability, rainwater harvesting) but are sectoral rather than comprehensive. A **Bangsamoro Environmental Code** or at minimum a **Bangsamoro Protected Areas Act** is the critical unfiled legislation.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) is the primary connection — encompassing forest management, protected areas, biodiversity conservation, climate change adaptation, and pollution control. Chapter 4 (Development Framework) establishes sustainable development as a foundational principle, positioning environment as a cross-cutting dimension of all development strategies. Environmental protection also supports Chapter 7 (Economic Development) through sustainable natural resource utilization and nature-based tourism, and Chapter 10 (Governance) through environmental justice and governance capacity. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028) addresses environmental stewardship as part of its sustainable development priorities [UNVERIFIED — pillar numbering not confirmed against OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026].

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **Bangsamoro Protected Areas Act**: existing protected areas in BARMM continue to be governed under NIPAS — a framework that does not account for BARMM's unique conditions (post-conflict communities, ancestral domain overlaps, Islamic environmental principles). The management transfer mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 9 cannot be fully operationalized without implementing legislation. **Deforestation** — BARMM provinces have among the highest deforestation rates in the Philippines; without a Bangsamoro forestry framework, MENRE relies on PD 705 (a 1975 law designed for a different context). **Biodiversity loss** — BARMM's ecosystems (Liguasan Marsh, Tawi-Tawi coral reefs, Lake Lanao watershed) require region-specific conservation strategies that the national framework does not provide. **Climate change vulnerability** — BARMM's coastal and riverine communities are among the most vulnerable to climate change; the comprehensive sustainable development framework mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 3 cannot be fully realized without environmental legislation. **Revenue loss** — environmental permits and resource utilization fees cannot be maximized without a legislative basis for MENRE's revenue-generating authority. The **BDP targets** for environmental protection depend on institutional and legislative infrastructure that BAA 13's general mandate alone cannot provide.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the status of the management transfer mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 9?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandated that the process of transferring management of existing protected areas from DENR to the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall start"*** within ***"three (3) months from the establishment of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority"*** and ***"shall be completed within a period not exceeding two (2) years."*** The BTA was established in March 2019. The 3-month deadline for starting the process was June 2019; the 2-year completion deadline was March 2021. This is one of the most time-bound provisions in the BOL. The transfer process has been initiated — MENRE and DENR have coordinated on protected area inventories and transition planning — but **full completion of the transfer for all protected areas has not been confirmed** as of this writing. This is a critical implementation gap: without completed transfer, MENRE cannot exercise full management authority over existing protected areas, and the dual jurisdiction (MENRE under BOL, DENR under NIPAS) creates governance uncertainty. [UNVERIFIED — exact status of the transfer as of 2026 requires confirmation from MENRE or the Office of the Chief Minister.]

**Q. How does this power interact with power (d) Ancestral domain and natural resources?**

A. Power (d) — ***"ancestral domain and natural resources"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(d)) — intersects with power (x) in two critical areas:

   (1) **Protected areas in ancestral domains**: Art. XIII, Sec. 8 requires FPIC for protected areas ***"within the ancestral domains"*** — creating a procedural gateway that makes protected area declaration dependent on the consent of indigenous peoples. BAA 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act) provides the BARMM framework for IP rights, including FPIC protocols.

   (2) **Natural resource management**: Both powers address natural resources — power (d) from the perspective of ancestral domain rights and resource revenue sharing (Art. XII, Sec. 34-36), power (x) from the perspective of conservation, environmental protection, and sustainable development. MENRE and the Ministry for Indigenous Peoples Affairs must coordinate to ensure that environmental protection and IP rights are harmonized.

**Q. How does this power interact with power (t) Ecological solid waste management and pollution control?**

A. Power (t) — ***"ecological solid waste management and pollution control"*** (Art. V, Sec. 2(t)) — is a **narrower subset** of the broader environmental authority under power (x). Art. XIII, Sec. 29 (toxic/hazardous substances regulation) could be implemented under either power. In practice: (1) **solid waste management** falls primarily under power (t) and RA 9003, with LGUs as the primary implementing units; (2) **air and water pollution** falls under power (x) and MENRE's Environmental Management Services; (3) **hazardous waste** straddles both powers. A comprehensive Bangsamoro Environmental Code should address both powers in a unified framework.

**Q. What are the specific protected areas within BARMM territory?**

A. Several NIPAS/ENIPAS-declared protected areas are within or partially within BARMM territory, including but not limited to: **Liguasan Marsh** (one of the largest wetland areas in Southeast Asia, straddling Maguindanao del Norte and Maguindanao del Sur), **Turtle Islands Wildlife Sanctuary** (Tawi-Tawi), and various forest reserves and watershed reservations across the five BARMM provinces and Cotabato City. The complete inventory of protected areas subject to Art. XIII, Sec. 9 transfer requires confirmation from MENRE and DENR. [UNVERIFIED — complete list requires official MENRE/DENR inventory.]

**Q. What role does the Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board play in environmental governance?**

A. The BSDB (BAA 31) is an **intergovernmental coordination body**, not a regulatory agency. It does not exercise the environmental regulatory, management, or enforcement functions that MENRE holds. Its mandate is to ***"ensure the integration and harmonization of economic, social, and environmental considerations"*** — making it a **policy coordination platform** where the National Government and Bangsamoro Government align on sustainable development. The BSDB's environmental role is: (a) ensuring that economic development projects incorporate environmental safeguards; (b) harmonizing national and Bangsamoro environmental policies; (c) coordinating the management transfer under Art. XIII, Sec. 9; and (d) resolving intergovernmental disputes on environmental matters. The BSDB is chaired by the **Senior Minister** and co-chaired by an appropriate National Government official.[^31]

**Q. Why does the BOL treat fossil fuels and uranium differently from other natural resources?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 10 requires ***"joint exercise"*** with the National Government only for ***"uranium and fossil fuels such as petroleum, natural gas, and coal."*** All other natural resources — forests, wildlife, minerals, water — fall under the Bangsamoro Government's authority (subject to the Constitution). This two-tier system reflects: (1) the **strategic national significance** of fossil fuels and uranium — they affect national energy security and national defense; (2) **existing national concession agreements** — petroleum exploration and production in the Philippines is governed by national-level service contracts under DOE supervision; (3) the **constitutional mandate** (Art. XII, Sec. 2) for State control over natural resources, which is most strictly applied to energy resources. The joint exercise requirement means that neither the Bangsamoro Government nor the National Government can unilaterally grant fossil fuel or uranium concessions within BARMM — both must participate.
