# (q) Customary Laws

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(q)** — "Customary laws;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over customary laws through Article V, Section 2(q). This is a broad power that encompasses the recognition, protection, codification, and application of the customs, beliefs, and traditions of the Bangsamoro people and the non-Moro indigenous peoples within the autonomous region. Article IX, Section 4 elaborates that "the customs, beliefs, and traditions of the Bangsamoro people are hereby recognized, protected, and guaranteed," and mandates the Parliament to "adopt measures to ensure mutual respect and protection of the distinct beliefs, customs, and traditions of the Bangsamoro people and the other inhabitants in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."[^1]

The power over customary laws intersects deeply with the Bangsamoro justice system. Article X, Section 1 establishes that the dispensation of justice in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall be "in consonance with the Constitution, Shari'ah, traditional or tribal laws, and other relevant laws." Article X, Sections 17 and 18 specifically address traditional and tribal justice systems, mandating the creation of an Office for Tribal Justice System to protect and promote the use of indigenous dispute resolution mechanisms. For non-Moro indigenous peoples, Article IX, Section 3 recognizes eight enumerated rights, including "indigenous customs and traditions" and "justice systems and indigenous political structures." Customary law therefore operates in two distinct spheres: Bangsamoro Islamic customary practices (adat) and the indigenous customs and traditions of the Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, B'laan, Higaonon, and other non-Moro peoples.[^2]

The BOL also connects customary law to political participation. Article VII, Section 8 provides that reserved seats for non-Moro indigenous peoples in Parliament shall adhere to "their customary laws and indigenous processes," establishing customary law as the basis for indigenous political representation. This makes customary law not merely a matter of dispute resolution or cultural preservation but a foundational element of the Bangsamoro political system itself.[^3]

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIAP), created under BAA No. 13 (Administrative Code), has primary responsibility for formulating and implementing policies related to indigenous peoples, including the documentation and promotion of customary laws and practices. Under BAA No. 85, MIAP receives annual appropriations for programs that include customary law documentation and indigenous justice system support. However, MIAP's mandate under BAA 13 is general — it does not have a standalone legislative framework specifically dedicated to customary laws codification or the institutionalization of customary dispute resolution.[^4]

BAA No. 64 (Indigenous Peoples' Act) is the most significant legislation touching this power. Enacted in 2024, it provides a comprehensive framework for the recognition and protection of indigenous peoples' rights, including customary law systems, within the Bangsamoro. It addresses the relationship between customary law and formal legal systems, native titles (fusaka inged), and the requirement for free, prior, and informed consent. BAA No. 89 (Transitional Justice and Reconciliation) also intersects with customary law by creating the Bangsamoro Transitional Justice and Reconciliation Commission, which considers customary and traditional approaches to justice for historical grievances. The Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH), established under Article IX, Section 24 of the BOL, supports customary law indirectly through its cultural preservation mandate.[^5]

No standalone Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code exists. The Parliament has not enacted legislation specifically dedicated to codifying, systematizing, or establishing mechanisms for the formal application of customary laws in governance, dispute resolution, or community affairs beyond what BAA 64 covers for indigenous peoples.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not explicitly require customary laws to be exercised "consistent with" national law. However, Article IX, Section 3 contains an important constraint: "This Organic Law shall not in any manner diminish the rights and benefits of the non-Moro indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region under the Constitution, national laws, particularly Republic Act No. 8371, otherwise known as the 'Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act of 1997.'" This means that while the Bangsamoro Government has broad authority over customary laws, any measures affecting non-Moro indigenous peoples' customary law systems must not reduce protections already provided by IPRA. Republic Act No. 8371 establishes indigenous peoples' rights to their customary laws and justice systems at the national level, and the BOL explicitly preserves these as a floor, not a ceiling.[^6]

National law also intersects through Presidential Decree No. 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws), which codifies certain Islamic customary practices for Muslims nationwide. The BOL's grant of authority over customary laws allows the Bangsamoro Parliament to legislate in areas beyond PD 1083's scope — particularly adat (local customary practices) that are not strictly Shari'ah-based but reflect Bangsamoro community traditions. No intergovernmental coordination body exists specifically for customary law matters, though the Intergovernmental Relations Body under Article VI, Section 2 could address disputes if they arise.[^7]

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The most significant gap is the absence of a comprehensive Bangsamoro Customary Laws Code. While BAA 64 addresses customary law in the context of indigenous peoples' rights, there is no legislation that systematically recognizes, codifies, and establishes application mechanisms for Bangsamoro adat (Moro customary law) across the autonomous region. A dedicated code could establish: a registry of recognized customary law practices by community and area; procedures for customary dispute resolution as a formal alternative or complement to the Shari'ah and regular court systems; qualifications and authority of traditional leaders acting as customary law adjudicators; and the relationship between customary law, Shari'ah, and the formal legal system.[^8]

The BOL's mandate in Article X, Sections 17-18 for traditional and tribal justice systems, including the Office for Tribal Justice System, has not been fully implemented through standalone legislation. While BAA 64 covers indigenous justice systems, the broader traditional justice framework contemplated by the BOL — which includes Bangsamoro Muslim adat practices like rido (clan conflict) resolution — lacks a legislative foundation. A Bangsamoro Traditional and Tribal Justice Systems Act could codify the procedures, jurisdiction, and legal effect of decisions rendered through traditional justice mechanisms.

A third gap concerns the documentation and preservation of customary laws themselves. Customary laws are, by definition, often unwritten and transmitted orally. Without systematic documentation, they risk being lost as communities modernize. Legislation mandating a Bangsamoro Customary Laws Documentation Program — coordinating MIAP, BCPCH, and local communities — would ensure that customary practices are recorded, verified by community elders, and made accessible for reference in legal proceedings and governance.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage) and Chapter 10 (Peace and Security), both depend on the effective exercise of the customary laws power. Chapter 6 targets the preservation of cultural heritage, which includes customary law traditions as living cultural practices. Chapter 10 envisions community-level peace and security systems that integrate traditional dispute resolution — an outcome impossible without legislative frameworks that give legal effect to customary adjudication. Failure to enact a customary laws code and traditional justice systems act would leave these BDP goals dependent on informal, ad hoc arrangements rather than institutionalized mechanisms with legal standing.
