## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over ecological solid waste management and pollution control?**

A. Article V, Section 2(t) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"ecological solid waste management and pollution control"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This is a compound power covering two distinct but related domains: (1) **ecological solid waste management** — the collection, segregation, recycling, treatment, and disposal of solid waste in an ecologically sound manner; and (2) **pollution control** — the regulation, restriction, and prevention of air, water, and land pollution within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Three provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 29 is the **dedicated environmental regulatory provision**. It states: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall regulate, restrict, or prohibit the use, importation, transit, transport, deposit, disposal, and dumping of toxic or hazardous substances within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It shall, in the same manner, regulate activities that may adversely impact the environment and may be harmful to the health, safety, and welfare of the Bangsamoro people."*** This is the broadest environmental regulatory grant in the BOL — it authorizes not only regulation but **restriction and outright prohibition** of harmful activities. The second sentence extends authority beyond toxic substances to **all activities adversely impacting the environment**.[^2]

   (2) Art. XIII, Sec. 3 mandates: ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall develop a comprehensive framework for sustainable development through the proper conservation, utilization, and development of natural resources."*** This framework must include ***"measures for environmental justice and governance."*** Solid waste management and pollution control must be integrated into this comprehensive framework — they are not standalone concerns but instruments of **sustainable development** and **environmental justice**.[^3]

   (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 2 establishes that development must be ***"carefully planned taking into consideration the ecological balance"*** and natural resources available ***"for its use and for the use of future generations."*** This embeds **intergenerational equity** as a governing principle.[^4]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. Unlike powers such as cooperatives (Art. XIII, Sec. 24 — named "cooperative code"), the BOL does not name a specific piece of legislation for solid waste management or pollution control. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 29's regulatory mandate and Art. XIII, Sec. 3's sustainable development framework mandate **imply** that comprehensive legislation is necessary to operationalize these obligations. The mandate to ***"regulate, restrict, or prohibit"*** in Art. XIII, Sec. 29 requires a legislative framework — MENRE cannot effectively exercise these powers through administrative mandate alone.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not expressly create a body for solid waste management or pollution control. However, Art. VI, Sec. 8 mandates the creation of a **Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board**, fulfilled through **BAA 31**. MENRE is the lead ministry, established through **BAA 13** (Administrative Code) with a dedicated **Solid Waste Management Division** under the Environment Management Services (BAA 13, Sec. 9(9)).[^5]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places **no express national law consistency requirement** on solid waste management and pollution control — Art. XIII, Sec. 29 contains no "consistent with," "in accordance with," or "subject to national law" qualifier. The limitations are:

   (1) **Constitutional floor**: Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects all 55 powers to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."*** Art. XIII, Sec. 1 requires the Bangsamoro economic system to be ***"based on the principles and state policies declared in the Constitution."*** The constitutional right to a balanced and healthful ecology (Art. II, Sec. 16) and the environmental protection obligation (Art. XII, Sec. 2) set the floor. See the **National Law Considerations** section (Section C).[^6]

   (2) **General supervision**: Art. VI, Sec. 1 provides that ***"The President shall exercise general supervision over the Bangsamoro Government to ensure that laws are faithfully executed."***

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to ecological solid waste management and pollution control?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Right to a Balanced and Healthful Ecology (Art. II, Sec. 16)**: ***"The State shall protect and advance the right of the people to a balanced and healthful ecology in accord with the rhythm and harmony of nature."*** The Supreme Court in *Oposa v. Factoran* (G.R. No. 101083, July 30, 1993) recognized this as a **self-executory constitutional right**. This is the **constitutional foundation** for all environmental legislation in BARMM.[^8]

   (2) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate within the constitutional framework. The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution."***[^6]

   (3) **Environmental Protection (Art. XII, Sec. 2)**: The State ***"shall protect its marine wealth in its archipelagic waters, territorial sea, and exclusive economic zone, and reserve its use and enjoyment exclusively to Filipino citizens."*** This establishes national environmental protection as a constitutional principle.

   (4) **Due Process (Art. III, Sec. 1)**: ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law."*** Environmental regulatory actions — penalties for pollution, closure orders, waste disposal restrictions — must satisfy procedural due process (notice, hearing, right to appeal).[^9]

**Q. What rights does the Constitution guarantee in relation to this power?**

A. Citizens in BARMM are constitutionally entitled to a **balanced and healthful ecology** — a right that BARMM legislation must protect and cannot diminish. Any regulatory framework must also satisfy **equal protection** (no arbitrary enforcement) and **due process** (fair procedures for penalties and restrictions).

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for solid waste management and pollution control?**

A. The national framework is comprehensive: **RA 9003** (Ecological Solid Waste Management Act, 2000) provides the complete solid waste management framework — the NSWMC, 10-year plans, LGU solid waste management boards, materials recovery facilities (MRFs), prohibited acts (open dumping, open burning, littering), and penalties. **RA 11898** (2022) amends RA 9003 to institutionalize extended producer responsibility (EPR) for plastic packaging. **RA 8749** (Clean Air Act, 1999) provides comprehensive air pollution control. **RA 9275** (Clean Water Act, 2004) provides water quality management. **RA 6969** (Toxic Substances and Hazardous Wastes Control Act, 1990) governs toxic and hazardous waste. Together these laws create the national environmental regulatory baseline.[^10][^11][^12][^13][^14]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. All national environmental laws apply in the absence of dedicated Bangsamoro environmental legislation:

   (1) **RA 9003** — governs all solid waste management within BARMM. LGU obligations (segregation, collection, MRFs, disposal) apply directly. Suppletory.[^10]
   (2) **RA 11898** — EPR obligations apply to enterprises in BARMM. Suppletory.[^11]
   (3) **RA 8749** — air quality standards and emission limits apply. The incineration ban affects solid waste disposal options. Suppletory.[^12]
   (4) **RA 9275** — water quality standards apply. Leachate management from waste facilities must comply. Suppletory.[^13]
   (5) **RA 6969** — governs toxic and hazardous waste handling in BARMM. Directly relevant to Art. XIII, Sec. 29 authority.[^14]
   (6) **RA 7160** — LGU solid waste functions as modified by BAA 49 (Local Governance Code).

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for solid waste and pollution control?**

A. The **Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR)** exercises environmental regulatory authority nationally. The **Environmental Management Bureau (EMB)** under DENR handles pollution control, environmental impact assessment, and solid waste compliance monitoring. The **NSWMC** sets national solid waste policy. DENR maintains a regional office that coordinates with MENRE on environmental matters. See **Section E (Shari'ah Considerations).2** for details on DENR's operational functions within BARMM.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE)** is the lead ministry. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III establishes MENRE's mandate, powers, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter XIII allocates its operational budget.[^5][^15]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for ecological solid waste management or pollution control as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through MENRE's **BAA 13 general mandate** and **BAA 85 budget allocation**, supplemented by **BAA 31** (Sustainable Development Board).

**Q. What are MENRE's mandate and functions under BAA 13 relevant to this power?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title III defines MENRE's environmental regulatory role:

   **Pollution control functions** (Sec. 3):
   - (a) ***"Recommend the enactment of regional laws relative to the development, utilization, regulation and conservation of the natural resources and energy and the control of pollution;"***
   - (m) ***"Promulgate rules and regulations for the control of water, air and land pollution within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region;"***

   **Organizational structure** (Sec. 9(9)): The **Environment Management Services** is headed by a Director II and includes four specialized divisions:
   - (a) **Water Compliance Monitoring Division**
   - (b) **Solid Waste Management Division**
   - (c) **Environmental Impact Assessment Division**
   - (d) **Air Compliance Monitoring Division**

   Each division is headed by a Division Chief.[^16]

**Q. What can MENRE NOT do without standalone solid waste/pollution legislation?**

A. While BAA 13 gives MENRE powers to promulgate rules for pollution control (Sec. 3(m)), it does not provide: a **comprehensive solid waste management framework** with disposal standards, segregation requirements, and LGU compliance obligations; **penalty provisions** for environmental violations specific to BARMM law; a **solid waste management fund** or financing mechanism; **extended producer responsibility** requirements adapted to BARMM's waste profile; **environmental standards** (air quality, water quality, waste facility siting) specific to BARMM conditions; or a **clear jurisdictional framework** between MENRE, DENR-EMB, and LGUs.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **DENR** through its **Environmental Management Bureau (EMB)** exercises environmental regulatory authority in BARMM. EMB handles: **environmental compliance certificates** (ECCs) for development projects; **pollution monitoring** (air, water, waste); **enforcement** of national environmental laws; **technical assistance** to LGUs on solid waste management. The **NSWMC** sets national solid waste policy that applies to BARMM LGUs. BARMM LGUs are required to comply with RA 9003 obligations — establishing solid waste management boards, maintaining MRFs, submitting 10-year solid waste management plans, and phasing out open dumpsites.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in three critical areas:

   (1) **Limited enforcement capacity**: DENR-EMB's BARMM presence has historically been under-resourced — fewer monitoring officers, less frequent compliance inspections, and weaker enforcement compared to Luzon and Visayas regions.

   (2) **No conflict-sensitive design**: RA 9003 assumes stable LGU infrastructure for solid waste management. Many BARMM LGUs — particularly in conflict-affected municipalities of Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, Lanao del Sur, Basilan, and Tawi-Tawi — lack the institutional capacity, infrastructure, and financial resources to comply with national solid waste requirements (MRFs, engineered sanitary landfills, segregation-at-source programs).

   (3) **Island province challenges**: Basilan and Tawi-Tawi face unique solid waste challenges as island provinces — limited land for disposal facilities, ocean-based waste pollution, and high costs of waste transport. RA 9003's mainland-oriented framework does not adequately address these conditions.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. MENRE has been **institutionally established** under BAA 13 with a dedicated **Solid Waste Management Division** within its Environment Management Services. It has begun exercising its general **pollution control mandate** through administrative issuances. The executive branch has issued directives on single-use plastics (Memo Order 285, enforced through Res. 367). BAA 31 created the Sustainable Development Board for cross-cutting environmental coordination. Four pending bills (PB Nos. 378, 248, 389, 305) signal Parliament's legislative interest in dedicated solid waste legislation.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has two emerging advantages:

   (1) **Broader regulatory authority**: Art. XIII, Sec. 29 grants authority to ***"regulate, restrict, or prohibit"*** toxic substances and environmentally harmful activities — **including outright prohibition**, which is a stronger grant than some national environmental provisions that focus on regulation and management rather than prohibition.

   (2) **Integration with Islamic values**: The *khalifah* stewardship principle and prohibition of *fasad* provide a normative framework for environmental compliance that resonates with BARMM communities. A Bangsamoro environmental law grounded in Islamic environmental ethics may achieve higher community compliance than a purely regulatory approach.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has built comprehensive environmental infrastructure:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 9003 provides 74 sections covering every aspect of solid waste — from waste characterization to disposal facility standards. Supplemented by RA 11898 (EPR), RA 8749 (air), RA 9275 (water), and RA 6969 (hazardous waste).
   - **Institutional infrastructure**: DENR-EMB has regional offices, provincial offices, and a staff of environmental management specialists. The NSWMC coordinates national policy.
   - **Regulatory systems**: Environmental compliance monitoring, emissions standards, water quality standards, waste facility permitting, and environmental impact assessment.
   - **IT and data**: DENR maintains environmental databases including air quality monitoring, water quality data, and solid waste compliance tracking.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: **environmental quality standards** (air quality, water quality) — technically sound and internationally benchmarked. **Environmental impact assessment** protocols. **Prohibited acts** (open dumping, open burning) from RA 9003.
   - **Adapt**: **solid waste management planning** — adapt the 10-year plan cycle to BARMM LGU capacity constraints, with phased compliance timelines for conflict-affected municipalities. **MRF requirements** — scale to BARMM community sizes and economic conditions. **Extended producer responsibility** — adapt to BARMM's waste profile (which may differ from the national average).
   - **Replace**: **institutional framework** — MENRE replaces DENR-EMB within BARMM for environmental regulation. **Compliance monitoring** — build BARMM-specific monitoring capacity rather than relying on under-resourced DENR field offices. **Island waste management** — develop a dedicated island solid waste framework for Basilan and Tawi-Tawi that RA 9003 does not provide.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes. Environmental stewardship is a **core Islamic obligation**. The concept of ***khalifah*** (stewardship/trusteeship of the earth) places humans as custodians, not absolute owners, of the natural environment. The Quran explicitly prohibits ***fasad*** (corruption/destruction) on earth — Quran 7:56: ***"Do not cause corruption on earth after it has been set in order."*** Pollution and improper waste disposal are forms of ***fasad*** that Islamic law prohibits. The hadith principle ***la darar wa la dirar*** (no harm, no reciprocal harm) — one of the five universal maxims of Islamic jurisprudence — prohibits activities that cause environmental harm to others, directly supporting pollution control legislation.[^7]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Five Islamic legal principles directly support solid waste and pollution control legislation:

   (1) ***Khalifah*** (stewardship) — humans are trustees of the earth, obligated to preserve its ecological balance. Waste management is a duty of stewardship.

   (2) ***Prohibition of fasad*** (corruption/destruction) — environmental pollution and improper waste disposal constitute *fasad*. Legislation prohibiting open dumping, uncontrolled emissions, and water pollution implements this Quranic prohibition.

   (3) ***La darar wa la dirar*** (no harm, no reciprocal harm) — pollution that harms community health constitutes *darar*. This principle supports both preventive regulation (environmental standards) and remedial action (cleanup obligations, penalties).

   (4) ***Hisbah*** (public enforcement) — the Islamic governance institution for enforcing public standards, historically including market sanitation and waste management. MENRE's regulatory and enforcement functions parallel *hisbah* responsibilities.

   (5) ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — solid waste management legislation serves the common good even where classical texts do not specifically address modern waste challenges.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro environmental legislation?**

A. Unlike cooperatives or banking (where Shari'ah creates distinct financial structures), solid waste management and pollution control do not conflict with Shari'ah principles — they **implement** them. A Bangsamoro solid waste act should include a **declaration of policy** recognizing the Islamic duty of environmental stewardship (*khalifah*) and the prohibition against environmental destruction (*fasad*) as foundational principles. This strengthens the normative basis for environmental regulation in a region where Islamic values are a primary source of community compliance and moral authority.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national environmental law?**

A. BARMM has **significant legislative authority** to diverge because: (1) the BOL grants authority over solid waste and pollution control as an **enumerated power** — Art. V, Sec. 2(t); (2) **no consistency requirement** with national environmental law is imposed — Art. XIII, Sec. 29 contains no "consistent with" or "in accordance with" qualifier; (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 29's authority to ***"prohibit"*** environmentally harmful activities implies authority to set **stricter standards** than national law.

   The **limits of divergence** are:

   (1) **Constitutional floor**: As established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E), the constitutional right to a balanced and healthful ecology (Art. II, Sec. 16) cannot be diminished. BARMM can go **above** this floor (stricter environmental standards) but not **below** it. The *Oposa v. Factoran* doctrine makes this right enforceable.

   (2) **Equal protection and due process**: Environmental penalties, closure orders, and regulatory restrictions must satisfy constitutional due process.

   In practice, BARMM can: enact **stricter** solid waste management standards than RA 9003; **ban single-use plastics** completely (going beyond national regulation); establish **BARMM-specific disposal facility standards** adapted to island and conflict-affected conditions; create a **Bangsamoro Environmental Quality Fund** separate from national environmental funds; and develop **Islamic environmental principles** as a normative basis for environmental law.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications?**

A. (1) **For businesses within BARMM**: Enterprises may face **different** (potentially stricter) environmental compliance requirements than those in non-BARMM areas. This creates a **regulatory advantage** if BARMM standards attract environmentally conscious investment, or a **compliance burden** if standards are perceived as more restrictive.

   (2) **For cross-boundary pollution**: Pollution does not respect administrative boundaries. Solid waste management and water pollution in BARMM may affect non-BARMM areas and vice versa. A Bangsamoro environmental law should include **intergovernmental coordination** provisions for cross-boundary environmental issues.

   (3) **For constitutional vulnerability**: A challenge would be unlikely to succeed if BARMM standards are **stricter** than national standards — the constitutional right to a balanced ecology supports higher protection. A challenge could arise only if BARMM standards fall **below** constitutional minimums.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between **MENRE** and **DENR-EMB**. Currently, DENR-EMB under national law exercises environmental regulatory authority within BARMM — issuing ECCs, monitoring pollution compliance, and enforcing RA 9003 requirements. A Bangsamoro solid waste management act must clarify: (a) whether **MENRE replaces DENR-EMB** within BARMM for solid waste and pollution control; (b) how **cross-boundary environmental issues** are coordinated between MENRE and DENR; (c) whether **national environmental standards** (air quality, water quality) continue to apply as minimums within BARMM after Bangsamoro standards are enacted.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. Four pending bills address solid waste management:

   (1) **PB No. 378** (Creating institutional mechanisms for solid waste management) — the most directly relevant, creating the institutional framework for BARMM solid waste management.
   (2) **PB No. 248** (Regulating single-use plastics) — addresses plastic waste reduction.
   (3) **PB No. 389** (Prohibiting single-use plastics) — stronger prohibition approach.
   (4) **PB No. 305** (Waste Workers' Health and Welfare Program) — addresses occupational health dimension.

   No comprehensive **Bangsamoro Ecological Solid Waste Management Act** or **Bangsamoro Pollution Control Code** has been filed.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 12 (Environment and Natural Resources) identifies solid waste management and pollution control as core components of environmental sustainability. Targets include improved waste management infrastructure, increased LGU compliance with waste management plans, and reduction of environmental health risks. Chapter 9 (Social Services) links environmental quality to health outcomes — pollution-related disease burden in BARMM is among the highest in the country, driven by inadequate solid waste management and water pollution. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 4 (Resilient Communities)**, depends on environmental protection to strengthen community resilience.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a **Bangsamoro Ecological Solid Waste Management Act**: MENRE cannot enforce **BARMM-specific solid waste standards** — LGUs operate under national requirements they often cannot meet. **Open dumping** continues in conflict-affected municipalities lacking engineered disposal facilities. **Plastic pollution** in coastal waters of Basilan and Tawi-Tawi degrades marine resources critical for fisherfolk livelihoods. The **health burden** from waste-related pollution (waterborne diseases, respiratory illness from waste burning) remains unaddressed by legislation. The **BDP Chapter 12 targets** for environmental sustainability require the institutional and legal infrastructure that only dedicated legislation can provide.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does this power relate to power (x) Environment, parks, forest management, wildlife, and nature reserves conservation?**

A. Power (t) — ***"ecological solid waste management and pollution control"*** — is the **regulatory dimension** of environmental protection: controlling human-generated waste and pollution. Power (x) — ***"environment, parks, forest management, wildlife, and nature reserves conservation"*** — is the **conservation dimension**: protecting natural ecosystems, forests, wildlife, and protected areas. Both powers are exercised by **MENRE** but through different institutional mechanisms. The Environment Management Services (Solid Waste Management Division, Air/Water Compliance Monitoring Divisions) handles power (t), while the Forest Management Services, Land Management Services, and Biodiversity Ecosystems Services handle power (x). A Bangsamoro solid waste act should coordinate with conservation legislation to address environmental issues that span both domains — such as waste disposal impacts on protected areas and water pollution affecting biodiversity.

**Q. What is the relationship between solid waste management and the Clean Air Act's incineration ban?**

A. **RA 8749** (Clean Air Act, 1999), Section 20, bans incineration of municipal, biomedical, and hazardous wastes. This applies in BARMM and significantly constrains solid waste disposal options — waste-to-energy incineration, which is used in some countries as an alternative to landfilling, is **prohibited** under Philippine law. A Bangsamoro solid waste management act must operate within this constraint, focusing on **source reduction**, **segregation**, **recycling**, **composting**, and **engineered sanitary landfills** as the disposal hierarchy. The BARMM could explore waste-to-energy technologies that do not constitute "incineration" under RA 8749 (such as gasification or pyrolysis), but would need to navigate the legal definition carefully.

**Q. How do island province conditions affect solid waste management in BARMM?**

A. Basilan and Tawi-Tawi face unique challenges that RA 9003's mainland-oriented framework does not address: (a) **limited land** for sanitary landfill construction; (b) **high transport costs** for moving waste between islands; (c) **marine pollution** from waste entering ocean waters; (d) **dispersed populations** across multiple islands making centralized waste management inefficient. A Bangsamoro solid waste act should include a **dedicated island solid waste management chapter** addressing these conditions — potentially authorizing decentralized waste treatment facilities, marine debris collection programs, and inter-island waste transport coordination mechanisms.

**Q. How does Art. XIII, Sec. 29's scope extend beyond traditional solid waste management?**

A. Art. XIII, Sec. 29 is broader than solid waste management — it covers ***"the use, importation, transit, transport, deposit, disposal, and dumping of toxic or hazardous substances"*** and ***"activities that may adversely impact the environment."*** This encompasses: **hazardous waste management** (chemical waste, medical waste, electronic waste); **toxic substance regulation** (pesticides, industrial chemicals); **environmental permitting** (EIA for projects with potential environmental impact); and **general pollution prevention** (any activity harmful to health, safety, or welfare). A Bangsamoro environmental law could use Art. XIII, Sec. 29 as the basis for a **comprehensive environmental protection code** that goes beyond solid waste management.
