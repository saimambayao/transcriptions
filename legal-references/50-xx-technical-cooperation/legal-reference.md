# (xx) Technical Cooperation for Human Resource Development

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(xx)** — "Technical cooperation for human resource development;"

## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over technical cooperation for human resource development as an enumerated power under Article V, Section 2(xx).[^1] This power is elaborated in Article XII, Section 28, which authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to establish "linkages for cultural exchange and economic and technical cooperation" with countries having diplomatic relations with the Philippines.[^2] Critically, however, Section 28 imposes a national approval requirement: such linkages must be established "upon review and approval of the National Government." Article XII, Section 29 adds that the "Bangsamoro Government shall endeavor to benefit from conventions to which the National Government is a party."[^3]

This power encompasses the Bangsamoro Government's ability to enter into technical cooperation arrangements — partnerships with foreign governments, international organizations, and development partners for the purpose of building the human resource capacity of the Bangsamoro. The scope includes training programs, technology transfer agreements, scholarship exchanges, capacity building projects, and development cooperation frameworks. The power overlaps with the cultural exchange and economic/technical cooperation power under Article V, Section 2(o), sharing the same BOL provisions and national approval requirement.

The national approval requirement makes this one of the more constrained enumerated powers. Unlike most powers where the BOL requires consistency with national law (a standard-setting relationship), Section 28 requires affirmative "review and approval" by the National Government — a gatekeeping function that means the Bangsamoro Government cannot independently enter into international technical cooperation agreements.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Office of the Chief Minister (OCM), funded through BAA 85, Chapter II, leads international linkages and coordination, including technical cooperation with foreign governments and development partners.[^4] The Development Academy of the Bangsamoro (DAB), funded through BAA 85, Chapter XXVII, serves as the primary implementing institution for human resource development, providing capacity building, training, and professional development programs.[^5]

The Parliament has enacted one BAA that directly touches this power: BAA 59, which creates the Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute (BAFTI).[^6] BAFTI is mapped to three powers — agriculture (c), fishery (y), and technical cooperation for HR development (xx) — reflecting how technical cooperation manifests through sector-specific training institutions. Beyond BAA 59, the power is exercised through BAA 13's general mandates for the OCM and DAB.

In practice, BARMM engages extensively with international development partners including JICA, UNDP, World Bank, ADB, Konrad Adenauer Stiftung, and various bilateral aid programs. These partnerships operate through memoranda of understanding and project-level agreements, typically channeled through the OCM or individual ministries. Resolutions 301-302 recognized JICA's contributions to BARMM, and Resolution 318 expressed gratitude to the Malaysian Prime Minister for peace process support — both reflecting the diplomatic dimension of technical cooperation. Resolution 415 thanked the Mennonite Church of Canada for a study visit. These resolutions illustrate the breadth of international partnerships but also highlight that the framework is largely informal and project-driven rather than legislated.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The national law relationship is explicit and constraining. Article XII, Section 28 requires that technical cooperation linkages with foreign countries be established "upon review and approval of the National Government."[^7] This is one of the few enumerated powers where the BOL requires affirmative national government approval rather than merely consistency with national standards. The Department of Foreign Affairs and the Office of the President serve as the national gatekeepers for international agreements, meaning all formal technical cooperation arrangements must pass through national review before they become binding.

Article XII, Section 29 provides a complementary mechanism: the Bangsamoro Government shall "endeavor to benefit from conventions to which the National Government is a party."[^8] This creates a right to participate in international development programs that the Philippines has joined — such as ODA programs, multilateral development frameworks, and treaty-based technical assistance.

No intergovernmental body under Article VI is specifically created for technical cooperation. The Intergovernmental Relations Body (Art. VI, Sec. 2) serves as the general coordination mechanism, and technical cooperation matters that require national approval would be processed through it or through direct coordination with relevant national agencies (DFA, NEDA, the concerned line departments).

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

No comprehensive Bangsamoro Technical Cooperation and Human Resource Development Code exists. The current framework is entirely project-driven and administratively managed through the OCM and individual ministries, without a legislated framework that codifies: the process for seeking national government approval for international technical cooperation agreements, priority areas for human resource development aligned with BDP targets, a standardized framework for managing ODA and development partner projects, an institutional mechanism for coordinating across ministries (which currently each manage their own partnerships), and requirements for knowledge transfer and sustainability of technical cooperation outcomes.

A Bangsamoro Technical Cooperation Act would establish the legal architecture for managing international partnerships. It could create a Bangsamoro International Cooperation Office under the OCM with a clear mandate, formalize the national approval process to reduce delays, establish a development partner coordination framework (preventing duplication and ensuring alignment with the BDP), and codify return-service and knowledge-sharing obligations for officials and professionals who receive international training.

The DAB's role in human resource development also lacks standalone legislation. While funded through BAA 85, the DAB operates under BAA 13's general mandate without a dedicated act defining its specific functions, governance structure, training standards, or coordination role across ministries. A DAB Act would strengthen the institutional foundation for human resource development.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

The BDP 2023-2028 addresses this power in Chapter 8 (Technology and Innovation), which emphasizes the role of international partnerships in building human capital and transferring technology.[^9] Chapter 5 (Governance and Institutional Development) also depends on technical cooperation for building the capacity of government officials and civil servants. Without a legislated framework for technical cooperation, BDP implementation relies on ad hoc project arrangements with development partners, limiting the strategic coherence and sustainability of human resource development investments.

## Legislative Gaps

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type A**: No technical cooperation framework | OCM manages international partnerships through administrative action; no legislated framework for seeking national approval, coordinating development partners, or ensuring sustainability of cooperation outcomes | Enact Bangsamoro Technical Cooperation Act establishing an international cooperation office, formalizing the national approval process, and codifying development partner coordination |
| **Type A**: No DAB standalone act | DAB operates under BAA 13 general mandate without dedicated legislation defining its functions, governance, training standards, or cross-ministry coordination role | Enact Development Academy of the Bangsamoro Act |
| **Type C**: National approval mechanism unclear | Art. XII, Sec. 28 requires "review and approval of the National Government" but no implementing mechanism clarifies which national agency reviews, the timeline for approval, or the process for contested proposals | Clarify through intergovernmental agreement or legislation the process, timeline, and responsible agencies for national approval of technical cooperation linkages |
