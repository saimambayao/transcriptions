# (xx) Technical Cooperation for Human Resource Development

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(xx)** — ***"Technical cooperation for human resource development;"***

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over technical cooperation for human resource development?**

A. Article V, Section 2(xx) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"technical cooperation for human resource development"*** as one of the 55 powers over which ***"the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority."*** This power authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to: (a) enter into **technical cooperation arrangements** — both domestic and international — for the purpose of developing human resources; (b) establish **institutional mechanisms** for skills transfer, capacity building, and workforce development; and (c) build the **human capital** of the Bangsamoro bureaucracy, private sector, and civil society. The power has two dimensions: **domestic technical cooperation** (with national government agencies, academic institutions, NGOs, and the private sector) and **international technical cooperation** (with foreign governments and international organizations — subject to Art. XII, Sec. 28).[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Six provisions elaborate on this power:

   (1) Art. XII, Sec. 28 — the **international cooperation constraint**: ***"The Bangsamoro Government may establish linkages for cultural exchange, economic and technical cooperation with countries having diplomatic relations with the Philippines upon review and approval of the National Government."*** This applies when technical cooperation involves foreign governments or organizations.[^2]

   (2) Art. XII, Sec. 29 — **benefits from international conventions**: the Bangsamoro Government is entitled to ***"equitable"*** benefits from conventions to which the National Government is a party — including technical assistance components of multilateral agreements.[^3]

   (3) Art. IX, Sec. 21 — **science and technology**: the Bangsamoro Government shall give priority to ***"science, research, inventions, technology education, and their development"*** and shall ***"prioritize the acquisition, adoption, innovation, and application of science and technology for development."*** Technical cooperation is the primary mechanism for technology acquisition and transfer.[^4]

   (4) Art. XII, Sec. 26 — **grants and donations**: BARMM agencies may accept grants from domestic and foreign sources; foreign grants from governments and multilateral institutions require ***"prior clearance and approval by the President."*** Technical cooperation frequently involves grant-funded programs.[^5]

   (5) Art. IX, Sec. 16 — **education system**: the Bangsamoro Government shall develop ***"an integrated system of quality education relevant to the needs of the people and society as a subsystem of the national educational system."*** Human resource development through education partnerships is a core dimension of this power.[^6]

   (6) Art. V, Sec. 2(o) — **companion power**: ***"Cultural exchange, and economic and technical cooperation"*** — the broader umbrella under which international technical cooperation falls.[^7]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not name specific legislation for technical cooperation or human resource development. Art. IX, Sec. 21 states that the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall give priority to"*** science and technology development — a directive mandate but without naming a specific code or act. The power is currently exercised through BAA 13 institutional provisions (DAB, BPDA, Liaison Office) and sector-specific legislation (BAA 59 for agriculture/fisheries training).

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL does not directly create a human resource development institution. However, under BAA 13, the Parliament has established the **Development Academy of the Bangsamoro (DAB)** as the ***"central human capital development training and research arm of the Bangsamoro Government"*** (Book IV, Title II, Ch. 3, Sec. 47). The BOL also provides in Art. VII, Sec. 42 that the Parliament ***"may create other offices"*** as it deems necessary — which authorizes additional institutions for this power.

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three limitations:

   (1) **National government review and approval for international cooperation**: Art. XII, Sec. 28 — ***"upon review and approval of the National Government."*** This applies to technical cooperation with foreign countries and international organizations.[^2]

   (2) **Diplomatic relations requirement**: Art. XII, Sec. 28 — only with ***"countries having diplomatic relations with the Philippines."***[^2]

   (3) **Presidential clearance for foreign grants**: Art. XII, Sec. 26 — grants from ***"governments of foreign countries, their agencies and instrumentalities or multilateral institutions or organizations"*** require ***"prior clearance and approval by the President."***[^5]

   **Domestic** technical cooperation — with national agencies, Philippine universities, NGOs, and private sector — is **not subject** to these constraints and may be pursued directly by BARMM agencies within their mandates.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous region framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: All autonomous region powers operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."*** Technical cooperation, especially when international, must respect the national government's authority over foreign affairs.[^8]

   (2) **Science and technology (Art. XIV, Sec. 10)**: ***"Science and technology are essential for national development and progress. The State shall give priority to research and development, invention, innovation, and their utilization; and to science and technology education, training, and services."*** This constitutional mandate supports BARMM's technical cooperation for technology education and skills development.[^9]

   (3) **Human resource development (Art. XII, Sec. 14)**: ***"The sustained development of a reservoir of national talents consisting of Filipino scientists, entrepreneurs, professionals, managers, high-level technical manpower and skilled workers and craftsmen in all fields shall be promoted by the State."*** BARMM's exercise of power (xx) implements this constitutional mandate at the regional level.[^10]

   (4) **Education (Art. XIV, Sec. 1-2)**: The State shall ***"protect and promote the right of all citizens to quality education at all levels"*** and ***"establish and maintain a complete, adequate, and integrated system of education relevant to the needs of the people and society."*** This includes vocational and technical skills training.[^11]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees: (a) the **right to quality education** (Art. XIV, Sec. 1) — which includes technical-vocational training; (b) **equal protection** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — human resource development programs must be accessible without discrimination; (c) **academic freedom** (Art. XIV, Sec. 5(2)) — educational institutions, including those involved in technical cooperation, enjoy institutional academic freedom. These rights establish the **constitutional floor** — BARMM human resource development programs must at minimum satisfy these guarantees.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**

A. Art. XIV, Sec. 10 mandates the State to ***"give priority to... science and technology education, training, and services."*** Art. XIV, Sec. 11 authorizes Congress to ***"provide for incentives, including tax deductions, to encourage private participation in programs of basic and applied scientific research."*** Art. XII, Sec. 14 mandates ***"sustained development"*** of a national talent reservoir. These mandates carry constitutional weight — national laws implementing them (such as RA 7796 creating TESDA) apply suppletorily in BARMM where no BAA provides a regional alternative.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**

A. The national framework is structured around: **RA 7796** (TESDA Act) — the national authority for technical-vocational education and skills development, establishing competency standards, assessment, and TVET qualifications; **RA 8182/RA 8555** (ODA Acts) — governing the acceptance and utilization of official development assistance, a major channel for technical cooperation; **RA 12145** (DEPDEV Act) — reorganizing NEDA into the Department of Economy, Planning and Development, which coordinates national development programming including ODA; **RA 11293** (Philippine Innovation Act) — establishing the national innovation ecosystem; **EO 459, s. 1997** — the approval framework for international agreements. These laws establish the national architecture within which BARMM technical cooperation operates.[^12][^13][^14][^15][^16]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. (1) **RA 7796** (TESDA Act) — applies **suppletorily** for national competency standards and certification. BARMM has its own TVET system under MBHTE, but TESDA national certifications remain the recognized standard for portability.[^12]
   (2) **RA 8182/RA 8555** (ODA Acts) — **directly applicable** for all ODA-funded technical cooperation in BARMM.[^13][^14]
   (3) **EO 459** — **directly applicable** for international technical cooperation agreements requiring national approval.[^15]
   (4) **RA 12145** — **directly applicable** for BARMM coordination with the national development planning agency (DEPDEV).[^16]
   (5) **RA 7157** (Foreign Service Act) — **directly applicable** for embassy/consulate personnel assignments recommended under Art. XII, Sec. 28.[^17]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. **TESDA** — maintains regional presence for TVET standards, assessment, and certification. **DEPDEV** (formerly NEDA) — coordinates national development planning and ODA programming, including technical cooperation projects targeting BARMM. **Department of Foreign Affairs (DFA)** — reviews and approves international technical cooperation linkages. **Civil Service Commission (CSC)** — sets national standards for government personnel development and training. **Development Academy of the Philippines (DAP)** — the national counterpart to the DAB, providing government capacity-building programs.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. Two institutions share primary responsibility: (a) the **Development Academy of the Bangsamoro (DAB)** — the ***"central human capital development training and research arm of the Bangsamoro Government"*** (BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Ch. 3, Sec. 47), attached to the OCM, responsible for **domestic human resource development** — training programs, capacity building, institutional strengthening for BARMM agencies; and (b) the **Office of the Chief Minister (OCM)** — for the **international cooperation** dimension, through the **Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila** (BAA 13, Book IV, Sec. (8)) which serves as ***"coordinating office of foreign scholarship programs"*** and ***"liaison office of the Chief Minister to international offices/embassies."*** BAA 85, Chapters II and XXVII allocate their respective operational budgets.[^18][^19][^20]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **BAA 59** (Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute Act) is the only standalone legislation directly implementing this power — it creates BAFTI as a sector-specific training institution with mandated international cooperation (Sec. 12). No comprehensive Bangsamoro Technical Cooperation Code or Human Resource Development Act has been enacted.[^21]

**Q. What are the DAB's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Chapter 3, Section 47 establishes the DAB with eleven enumerated powers and functions:

   - (a) ***"Provide training programs and technical assistance services to BARMM officials and employees, with the end in view of maintaining high-efficient workforce, output-producing bureaucracy, and socially-responsible institutions;"***
   - (b) ***"Conduct development-oriented research projects and policy studies that promote institutional productivity and address issues affecting government service delivery;"***
   - (c) ***"Conduct knowledge exchange programs with BARMM officials and employees that promote discussions on the best practices and strategies, challenges, and opportunities affecting government service delivery;"***
   - (d) ***"Create, update, and manage a pool of external experts and specialists that shall be tapped for the institutional development needs of agencies of the Bangsamoro government;"***
   - (e) ***"Create, update, and manage a pool of external experts and specialists that shall be utilized as technical resource for training and research services of the Academy;"***
   - (f-g) Call upon any BARMM official, employee, or agency to participate in training and research activities;
   - (h) ***"Call upon provide feedback, and offer assistance to any agency or employee of the Bangsamoro Government regarding its performance and service delivery;"***
   - (i) ***"Adopt and promulgate policies and guidelines governing human capital development of all agencies of the Bangsamoro government designed to strengthen workforce capacity and institutional productivity;"***
   - (j) ***"Adopt and promulgate policies and guidelines governing the design and conduct of capacity-development programs of all agencies of the Bangsamoro government;"***
   - (k) ***"Establish and maintain training and research center/s in the region."***[^18]

   Section 48 provides the organizational structure: five divisions — Finance and Administrative Division (FAD), Center of Training and Assessment (CTA), Center of Research and Policy Development (CRPD), Center of Education and Extension (CEE), and Center of Knowledge Management (CKM). The Executive Director (Salary Grade 26) is appointed by the Chief Minister for a fixed term of six years, with the authority to ***"sign contracts with donor institutions, partners and other institutions relevant to the mandates of the Academy"*** (Sec. 51(e)).[^18]

**Q. What can the lead institutions NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**

A. Without a standalone Technical Cooperation and Human Resource Development Act, the existing institutions cannot: (a) establish a **unified framework** governing how all BARMM agencies engage in technical cooperation — currently each agency pursues cooperation independently; (b) create **binding standards** for human resource development across all ministries — the DAB's guidelines are policy-level, not legislative; (c) establish a **Bangsamoro Technical Cooperation Fund** with dedicated appropriation for co-financing international technical assistance; (d) codify **priority skills areas** and require all ministries to align training programs; (e) define **ODA coordination protocols** between BPDA, the line ministries, and international partners; (f) create a **regional skills qualifications framework** specific to BARMM's needs.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Res. 189 urges ministries to utilize BARMM businesses for capacity building, and Res. 394 encourages participation in values transformation training — both signal interest in human resource development. However, no resolution directly calls for comprehensive technical cooperation or human resource development legislation.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**

A. Yes, with a caveat. The **DAB** is the correct lead for the **domestic human resource development** dimension — its BAA 13 mandate as the ***"central human capital development training and research arm"*** directly corresponds to the power's scope. The **OCM** is correct for the **international technical cooperation** dimension — Art. XII, Sec. 28 requires national government approval, making this an executive function exercised through the Chief Minister's authority. The **BPDA** has a critical supporting role through its mandate to ***"coordinate all regional, national and foreign assisted programs and projects in the Bangsamoro"*** (BAA 13, Sec. 40(e)). The caveat: there is no single coordinator for the full scope of this power — the domestic and international dimensions are split across DAB, OCM, and BPDA without a formal coordination mechanism.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. - **Lead agency (domestic)**: Development Academy of the Bangsamoro (DAB) — BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Ch. 3, Sec. 47-51; BAA 85, Chapter XXVII
   - **Lead office (international)**: OCM — Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila (BAA 13, Book IV, Sec. (8)); BAA 85, Chapter II
   - **Coordinating agency**: BPDA — ODA/Nationally Funded Programs and Projects Coordination Division (BAA 13, Sec. 40-41); BAA 85, Chapter XXIII
   - **Sector-specific**: BAFTI — agriculture and fisheries training (BAA 59); attached to MAFAR
   - **Supporting ministry**: MBHTE — technical-vocational education and skills training
   - **Supporting council**: Bangsamoro Economic and Development Council (BEDC) — reviews development programs; BPDA serves as secretariat (BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Ch. 2, Sec. 13-21)

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. The BOL does not specifically mandate the creation of a dedicated technical cooperation or human resource development institution. The existing institutions (DAB, BPDA, BAFTI) were created by BAA rather than BOL directive. Art. VII, Sec. 42 provides open-ended authority for the Parliament to ***"create other offices"*** — a Bangsamoro Human Resource Development Authority or a Technical Cooperation Office could be established under this provision.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Full exercise of this power would benefit from: (a) a **Bangsamoro Skills and Qualifications Authority** — to develop and maintain a regional skills framework, competency standards, and assessment/certification system distinct from TESDA but interoperable with it; (b) a **Technical Cooperation Coordination Unit** — within the OCM or BPDA to systematically identify, evaluate, and manage all technical cooperation activities across ministries, preventing duplication and maximizing impact; (c) a **Bangsamoro Scholarship and Fellowship Board** — to centralize the management of foreign scholarship programs currently fragmented across the Liaison Office and individual ministries.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **TESDA** maintains regional offices that implement national TVET programs — competency assessments, skills certification, scholarship programs — within BARMM. **DEPDEV** (formerly NEDA) coordinates the national ODA portfolio, including technical assistance projects targeting Mindanao and BARMM specifically. **CSC** administers national civil service training standards and competency-based HR development. **DAP** (Development Academy of the Philippines) offers government capacity-building programs accessible to BARMM officials. International organizations — **UNDP, World Bank, JICA, USAID, ADB, GIZ** — implement technical cooperation projects in BARMM through agreements with the National Government, with BARMM agencies as implementing partners.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**

A. Key programs: (a) **TESDA Scholarship Programs** — training for development, special training for employment program (STEP), community-based training; (b) **NEDA/DEPDEV ODA Portfolio** — coordinates multilateral and bilateral technical cooperation (World Bank Mindanao Trust Fund, JICA BARMM capacity building, UNDP governance strengthening); (c) **CSC Training and Development** programs for government personnel; (d) **CHED Scholarship Programs** — merit-based and targeted scholarships for higher education.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**

A. Devolution is **partial and ongoing**: (a) TVET functions are substantially devolved — BARMM operates its own technical-vocational system under MBHTE, though TESDA national standards remain authoritative for certification; (b) ODA coordination is **partially devolved** — BPDA coordinates foreign-assisted programs within BARMM but the national-level ODA approval and programming remains with DEPDEV; (c) civil service training is **partially devolved** — the DAB provides Bangsamoro-specific capacity development, but CSC national standards still govern. Full devolution would require BARMM institutions to achieve **functional equivalence** with their national counterparts — particularly in skills certification and ODA management.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**

A. Yes — three areas of concurrent operation: (a) **TVET certification** — TESDA national certifications coexist with MBHTE regional programs; (b) **ODA management** — DEPDEV and BPDA both exercise coordination roles for foreign-assisted programs in BARMM; (c) **government capacity building** — DAP national programs and DAB regional programs target the same BARMM personnel pool. These overlaps are **by design** — the BOL establishes a coordination model rather than complete devolution — but the absence of a formal coordination protocol creates risks of duplication.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. The Bangsamoro Government has: (a) **established the DAB** as a permanent institution with five functional divisions for human capital development; (b) **enacted BAA 59** (BAFTI) — the first sector-specific training legislation in BARMM; (c) **established the BPDA's ODA Coordination Division** to manage foreign-assisted technical cooperation; (d) received and implemented **major technical cooperation programs** from JICA, UNDP, World Bank, USAID, ADB, and GIZ covering governance, planning, agriculture, health, and education; (e) managed **foreign scholarship programs** through the Bangsamoro Liaison Office; (f) launched DAB-administered training programs for BARMM officials and employees. The implementation has been **institutional-first** — building the agencies before enacting the comprehensive framework.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has unique advantages in: (a) **culturally grounded training** — DAB and BAFTI can deliver capacity-building programs in local languages, using culturally appropriate methodologies, and incorporating Islamic values (including *amanah* and *maslahah*) that the national system does not offer; (b) **post-conflict human resource development** — BARMM understands the specific capacity gaps created by decades of conflict and can target technical cooperation toward conflict-affected populations (ex-combatants, IDP communities, returning professionals); (c) **Muslim-majority country partnerships** — BARMM's cultural and linguistic connections to OIC member states (Malaysia, Indonesia, Turkey, Gulf states) create natural channels for technical cooperation that the national government's secular approach does not fully leverage; (d) **context-sensitive skills priorities** — BARMM can align technical cooperation to its specific development needs (halal industry, Islamic finance, Shari'ah legal education, madaris quality improvement) rather than accepting one-size-fits-all national programs.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **primary constraint** is the **absence of a unified framework** — technical cooperation activities are fragmented across DAB (government training), BPDA (ODA coordination), Bangsamoro Liaison Office (foreign scholarships), BAFTI (agriculture/fisheries), and individual ministries (sector-specific partnerships), with no coordinating mechanism or shared strategy. The **secondary constraint** is **institutional capacity** — DAB is still building its staff complement and facilities, BPDA's ODA division coordinates but does not drive technical cooperation strategy, and the Bangsamoro Liaison Office is a small office with broad responsibilities. The **binding constraint** is the lack of a **Bangsamoro Human Resource Development Plan** — a strategic document identifying priority skills, target populations, cooperation partners, and resource requirements.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government has: (a) **TESDA** — an established agency with 17 regional offices, 61 provincial offices, 51 district offices, and 123 TESDA Technology Institutions; a comprehensive **Philippine TVET Qualifications Framework** with 236 training regulations covering 20 industry sectors; a national competency assessment and certification system processing hundreds of thousands of assessments annually; (b) **DEPDEV/NEDA** — decades of experience managing a multi-billion-peso ODA portfolio with established relationships with all major bilateral and multilateral partners; (c) **DAP** — 50+ years of government capacity building; graduate degree programs; a network of alumni across government; (d) **CSC** — a comprehensive learning and development framework for the entire national civil service. BARMM's corresponding institutions are orders of magnitude smaller and younger.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national system: (a) does not provide **Shari'ah-compliant** human resource development tracks — no halal industry skills certification, no Islamic finance training standards, no Shari'ah court professional development programs; (b) does not account for **post-conflict** human resource needs — the national skills framework does not address the specific capacity deficits in conflict-affected areas or the reintegration needs of former combatants; (c) does not deliver in **local languages** and through culturally appropriate channels — national programs are designed for the general Filipino population, not specifically for Bangsamoro communities; (d) does not prioritize **Bangsamoro-specific sectors** — the halal industry, Islamic education, and traditional arts/crafts that define BARMM's unique economic and cultural landscape.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. - **Replicate**: National competency standards and assessment methodology — the TVET qualifications framework is sound and portability requires compatibility with national standards
   - **Adapt**: ODA coordination protocols — BARMM needs its own internal process for identifying, proposing, and managing technical cooperation while interfacing with the national system; government training programs — adapt DAP's methodology to Bangsamoro governance context and cultural values
   - **Replace**: Skills priorities — develop a Bangsamoro-specific skills framework that incorporates halal industry, Islamic finance, Shari'ah legal services, madaris management, conflict-sensitive development, and other context-specific domains that the national framework does not cover

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. The Shari'ah dimension is **principled and directional** — not regulatory. Human resource development is governed by administrative and civil service law, not Shari'ah. However, Islamic principles provide strong normative support for this power's exercise: the duty to seek knowledge (*talab al-'ilm*), the trust of public stewardship (*amanah*), mutual cooperation (*ta'awun*), and public interest (*maslahah*). These principles inform the Bangsamoro Government's approach to capacity building, particularly in: (a) **Shari'ah court professional development** — training for Shari'ah judges, lawyers, and public assistance officers; (b) **halal industry skills** — certification and quality standards for halal production; (c) **Islamic finance capacity** — training for Islamic banking and finance professionals; (d) **madaris quality improvement** — curriculum development and teacher training for Islamic educational institutions. These are areas where technical cooperation with OIC member states is particularly valuable [fiqh — not enacted in BARMM law as applied to technical cooperation specifically].

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL is **silent** on Shari'ah in relation to technical cooperation specifically. However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to ***"enact appropriate laws"*** on Shari'ah matters, and Art. X, Sec. 16 authorizes creation of a ***"Shari'ah academy"*** — both of which imply human resource development needs in the Shari'ah domain that technical cooperation can address.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**

A. Shari'ah integration for technical cooperation should be **principled** — legislation guided by Islamic values (*maslahah*, *amanah*, *ta'awun*) without mandating specific compliance mechanisms. For **sector-specific** human resource development — halal industry, Islamic finance, Shari'ah court capacity — a **Shari'ah review** by the Bangsamoro Darul Ifta' or a designated *ulama* advisory body could ensure training curricula and technical cooperation agreements align with Islamic principles. This would be an advisory rather than regulatory function.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**

A. BARMM has **significant space for divergence** in the **domestic dimension** of this power and **limited space** in the **international dimension**. For domestic technical cooperation: the Bangsamoro Government can establish its own human resource development standards, training institutions, and skills framework — the BOL does not require national law consistency for internal capacity building. The constitutional floor identified in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B) — Art. XIV, Sec. 10 (science and technology) and Art. XII, Sec. 14 (national talent development) — supports rather than constrains BARMM's human resource development. BARMM can diverge by: (a) establishing **Shari'ah-specific** training tracks (as established in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E)); (b) creating **context-specific skills domains** (halal industry, Islamic finance, conflict-sensitive development); (c) designing **culturally grounded** training methodologies.

For international technical cooperation: as established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XII, Sec. 28 requires ***"review and approval of the National Government"*** — BARMM cannot independently establish international technical cooperation linkages. The divergence here is limited to **setting internal priorities** and **proposing** cooperation arrangements.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. If BARMM establishes its own skills qualifications framework: (a) **portability** — BARMM-certified skills must be recognized outside the region, requiring mutual recognition agreements with TESDA or a common national standard; (b) **equal protection** — as established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), Art. III, Sec. 1 requires that BARMM training standards do not disadvantage workers seeking employment outside the region; (c) **employers** operating across BARMM and non-BARMM areas need clarity on which certifications are valid. A **Bangsamoro Skills Qualifications Framework** interoperable with the Philippine TVET framework would resolve these cross-boundary issues.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. A Bangsamoro Human Resource Development Act would govern the **internal framework** — institutional structure, skills priorities, training standards, and domestic cooperation mechanisms. National law (RA 7796/TESDA, RA 8182/ODA, EO 459) would continue to govern the **external interfaces** — national skills certification, ODA acceptance, and international agreement approval. Constitutional provisions (Art. XIV, Sec. 10; Art. XII, Sec. 14) would continue to set the **minimum floor** for human resource development as a national priority.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Three jurisdictional ambiguities: (a) **TVET certification** — TESDA national certifications and BARMM training programs coexist without a formal mutual recognition protocol; workers trained in BARMM-specific programs may lack nationally recognized credentials; (b) **ODA coordination** — DEPDEV/NEDA coordinates ODA at the national level while BPDA coordinates within BARMM; the boundary between national programming decisions and BARMM implementation authority is unclear; (c) **government training standards** — the CSC sets national competency-based learning and development standards while the DAB develops Bangsamoro-specific programs; which standard prevails for BARMM civil servants is not legislatively defined. Legislative action — particularly a Bangsamoro Human Resource Development Code and an intergovernmental MOA on skills recognition — would resolve these ambiguities.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **No pending Parliament Bill** comprehensively addresses technical cooperation or human resource development. BAA 59 (BAFTI) addressed the agriculture/fisheries training gap. The broader framework gap — covering all sectors, all agencies, and both domestic and international cooperation — remains open.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP Chapter 8** (Technology and Innovation) targets technology transfer and innovation capacity — achievable only through systematic technical cooperation. **BDP Chapter 5** (Governance and Institutional Development) depends on civil service capacity building — the DAB's core mandate. **BDP Chapter 13** (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) identifies ODA and foreign-assisted programs as a financing channel — technical cooperation is both the vehicle and the outcome. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda**, **Pillar 2 (Good Governance)**, depends on institutional capacity that only structured human resource development can build, while **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)** requires a skilled workforce that technical cooperation programs develop.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a unified framework: (a) **fragmented capacity building** — each ministry pursues training independently, without coordinated skills priorities or quality standards; (b) **underutilized ODA** — technical cooperation opportunities may be missed because no central coordination mechanism identifies needs and matches them to donor offerings; (c) **brain drain** — absence of a structured career development pathway means trained Bangsamoro professionals may leave the region; (d) **skills mismatch** — training programs may not align with BARMM's actual economic and governance needs without a regional skills framework; (e) the **DAB** remains a training provider without the legislative authority to set binding standards across all ministries — it can recommend but not require.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does power (xx) relate to power (o) cultural exchange and economic/technical cooperation?**

A. Power (xx) is the **human resource development dimension** of the broader power (o). Art. XII, Sec. 28 — the core implementing provision — applies to both. The distinction: power (o) covers all three dimensions of international engagement (cultural, economic, technical) while power (xx) focuses specifically on **technical cooperation directed at developing human resources**. In practice, the OCM handles the international cooperation dimension of both powers through the Bangsamoro Liaison Office, while DAB handles the domestic human resource development outcomes. A comprehensive Bangsamoro International Cooperation Act (discussed in the legal reference for power (o)) would serve both powers.

**Q. How does power (xx) relate to power (z) grants and donations?**

A. Technical cooperation frequently involves **grant funding** — foreign governments and multilateral organizations fund capacity-building programs through grants. Art. XII, Sec. 26 governs grants; Art. XII, Sec. 28 governs technical cooperation linkages. When a technical cooperation arrangement includes grant funding from a foreign government, **both provisions** apply — the arrangement requires national government approval (Art. XII, Sec. 28) and the grant component requires Presidential clearance (Art. XII, Sec. 26). BPDA's ODA Coordination Division manages the intersection of technical cooperation and grant management.

**Q. What is the role of BAFTI (BAA 59) as a model for sector-specific technical cooperation?**

A. BAA 59 establishes BAFTI as a precedent — the first standalone legislation creating a **sector-specific training institution** in BARMM. Key features that could be replicated for other sectors: (a) defined powers and functions for training and extension services (Sec. 4); (b) mandated coordination with ***"local and international organizations"*** for enhanced quality (Sec. 12); (c) technology transfer mechanisms (Sec. 4(f)); (d) partnership with CSEA for cooperative-based dissemination (Sec. 7); (e) halal development integration (Sec. 10); (f) dedicated appropriation (Sec. 14). This model could be applied to create training institutes for: halal industry, Islamic finance, digital technology, governance and public administration, and health services — each as sector-specific expressions of power (xx).

**Q. What is the practical significance of the DAB's authority to sign contracts with donor institutions?**

A. BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Ch. 3, Sec. 51(e) grants the DAB Executive Director authority to ***"sign contracts with donor institutions, partners and other institutions relevant to the mandates of the Academy."*** This is significant because it gives the DAB **direct contracting authority** for technical cooperation — the Executive Director can execute partnership agreements with domestic and international training providers, universities, and development organizations. For **international** partnerships, Art. XII, Sec. 28's national government approval requirement still applies — but the DAB can propose and negotiate arrangements. For **domestic** partnerships, the DAB can act independently within its mandate.

**Q. How does the BPDA's ODA coordination function interface with technical cooperation?**

A. BPDA's mandate to ***"coordinate all regional, national and foreign assisted programs and projects in the Bangsamoro"*** (BAA 13, Sec. 40(e)) makes it the **central coordination point** for ODA-funded technical cooperation. The BPDA's Bureau of Policy, Research, and Information houses the **ODA/Nationally Funded Programs and Projects Coordination Division** — which tracks, coordinates, and monitors all foreign-assisted programs in BARMM. In the BEDC framework, BPDA recommends ***"programs and projects for consideration of National Economic Development Authority-Investment Coordinating Committee (NEDA-ICC)"*** (BAA 13, Sec. 15(8)) — now DEPDEV. This is the formal channel through which BARMM technical cooperation proposals enter the national ODA programming pipeline.

**Q. What skills domains are unique to BARMM's human resource development needs?**

A. BARMM has human resource development needs that the national framework does not address: (a) **Shari'ah legal professionals** — judges, lawyers, public assistance officers, and judicial academy faculty for the Shari'ah court system; (b) **halal industry specialists** — certification auditors, quality control officers, halal supply chain managers; (c) **Islamic finance professionals** — Islamic banking officers, Shari'ah compliance officers, *mudarabah*/*musharakah* analysts; (d) **madaris educators** — qualified teachers for the Islamic education system; (e) **peace and security practitioners** — decommissioning program managers, transitional justice facilitators, community reconciliation specialists; (f) **Bangsamoro governance specialists** — parliamentary staff, ministerial policy analysts, LGU development planners trained in the specific Bangsamoro governance framework. Technical cooperation with OIC member states — particularly Malaysia, Indonesia, and Turkey — is uniquely positioned to address domains (a)-(d), while domestic and multilateral cooperation addresses (e)-(f).
