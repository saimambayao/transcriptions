### PART I: GAD LEGAL FRAMEWORK

**A. BOL GAD Architecture**

The BOL embeds Gender and Development across its entire structure — not as an isolated provision but as a cross-cutting governance obligation. Provisions below organized by function.

- A.1 Foundational Recognition and Policy Commitments
  - BOL Preamble, Art. II, Art. IV, Art. IX non-discrimination
- A.2 The Core GAD Mandate (Art. XIII, Sec. 5)
  - Verbatim text + function analysis
  - 5% GAD budget mandate
  - CEDAW commitment
  - Gender-responsive programs obligation
  - Mechanism for consultation with women
- A.3 Political Representation and Participation
  - Parliament sectoral seat for women (Art. VII, Sec. 7c)
  - Council of Leaders women's sector (Art. VI, Sec. 9)
  - Cabinet appointment — at least 1 woman (Art. VI, Sec. 11)
- A.4 Commission on Women Mandate
  - Creation mandate (Art. VI, Sec. 11 — second paragraph)
  - BOL mandate vs. BAA 8 establishment vs. BAA 13 codification
- A.5 Protection of Women's Rights
  - Art. VI, Sec. 12 (Protection of women — employment, exploitation, abuse, CEDAW)
  - Art. IX non-discrimination provisions
  - Art. IV Sec. 10 non-discrimination
- A.6 Concurrent Legislative Powers with GAD Dimension
  - Art. III, Sec. 4(r) — development programs for women
  - Art. III, Sec. 4(mm) — consultation mechanisms for women and marginalized sectors
- A.7 Transition and Institutional Gender Obligations
  - Gender and ethnic balance in hiring (Art. XVI transition provisions)
  - BTA staffing gender balance requirement
- A.8 Development and Fiscal Dimensions
  - ODA gender allocation (5%–30% of ODA for GAD)
  - Gender-responsive budgeting obligations
  - Alignment with 55 powers: which powers carry inherent GAD obligations

## Key Design Decisions

1. **BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 5 is the anchor.** This is the most specific and powerful GAD provision in the entire BOL — it mandates 5% GAD budget, ODA gender allocation, consultation mechanisms, and special development programs. Every other provision radiates from this core.

2. **Cross-cutting reference, not a power-by-power entry.** This document covers GAD as it appears across all 55 powers — unlike the individual power entries in `legal-references/`. It follows the Shari'ah and OBC cross-cutting format.

3. **Legislation-only scope.** Islamic gender justice (Quranic and Sunnah sources) is OUT OF SCOPE for this reference — that dimension belongs to the Shari'ah reference and the `/gender` skill. This document covers formal legislation: BOL, BAAs, national laws, Constitution, pending bills, BDP.

4. **BAA 8 vs. BAA 13.** BAA 8 created the BWC; BAA 13 codified and expanded its mandate. Both must be cited correctly. Do NOT conflate them.

5. **CEDAW as binding legal obligation.** BOL Art. VI, Sec. 12 and BAA 13, Sec. 1 explicitly incorporate CEDAW. This makes CEDAW obligations directly applicable to BARMM governance — not merely aspirational.

6. **Pending legislation noted but not cited as enacted.** PB 336 (BARMM GAD Code) and the Anti-GBV bill are PENDING. They are documented in the Legislative Gaps section only. They are NOT cited as existing law.

7. **5% GAD budget — dual legal basis.** The 5% GAD budget has two independent legal bases: RA 9710, Sec. 36 (national) and BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 5 (regional organic law). Both must be cited separately and correctly.

8. **BHRC as Ombud is distinct from BWC mandate.** The BHRC (Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission) exercises the Gender and Development Ombud function per BAA 13, Sec. 21. This is separate from the BWC's policy-making and coordination mandate. Do NOT conflate them.
