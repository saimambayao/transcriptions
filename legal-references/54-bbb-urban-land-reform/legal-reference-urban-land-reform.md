# (bbb) Urban Land Reform and Land Use

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(bbb)** — ***"Urban land reform and land use;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(bbb) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement. Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) authorizes the Parliament to enact an urban land reform and land use program ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution."*** The constitutional qualifier — not a national law qualifier — is the operative constraint. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (MHSD)** — established under BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI. MHSD houses the **Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division** under its Technical Regulatory Services (BAA 13, Bk. VI, Title VI, Ch. 3, Sec. 9(6)). |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Interior and Local Government (MILG) — general supervision over LGUs that prepare CLUPs; Ministry of Public Works (MOPW) — infrastructure development tied to land use; Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE) — environmental overlays and land classification; Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA) — regional spatial strategy and development planning; Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) — agricultural land conversion authority (BAA 13, Bk. VI, Title I, Sec. 3(z)) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 3 (Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy) — multi-nodal spatial framework, growth centers, regional settlement hierarchy; Chapter 11 (Infrastructure) — Housing and Human Settlement Development Program, land acquisition and development |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution, the Parliament may enact an urban land reform and land use program for the region"*** — Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3). This is a permissive mandate ("may enact"), not a mandatory one ("shall enact"). |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant -> constitutional floor -> national law -> implementation -> Shari'ah dimension -> divergence -> gaps -> development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(bbb) enumerates ***"Urban land reform and land use"*** as a power of the Bangsamoro Government, exercised ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law"*** and ***"without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines."***[^1] This is a **two-part power**: (1) urban land reform — the social justice dimension of equitable access to urban land, particularly for the disadvantaged; and (2) land use — the regulatory dimension of controlling how land within urban areas is utilized, including zoning, development controls, and comprehensive land use planning.

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**
A. The principal elaborating provision is Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3): ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution, the Parliament may enact an urban land reform and land use program for the region."***[^2] This provision is notable for three reasons. First, the constraint is **constitutional**, not national law — the phrase is "subject to the provisions of the Constitution," not "consistent with national laws." Second, the word **"may"** makes this a permissive mandate — Parliament is authorized but not compelled. Third, the term **"program"** suggests comprehensive legislation, not piecemeal regulation.

Additional elaborating provisions include: Art. XIII, Sec. 17(1) on **land classification** — public lands must be classified as alienable and disposable before they can be subject to land reform.[^3] Art. XIII, Sec. 17(2) on **cadastral surveys** — parcel-level data is a prerequisite for land use planning.[^4] Art. IX, Sec. 9 creates the **housing and human settlements agency** — now MHSD — which is the institutional anchor for both urban land reform and land use regulation.[^5] Art. IX, Sec. 8 establishes **affordable and progressive housing projects** as a basic service obligation, for which urban land reform provides the land supply mechanism.[^6]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) uses the permissive ***"may enact"*** — not the mandatory ***"shall enact."*** The Parliament is **authorized** to enact an urban land reform and land use program but is not obligated to do so. This contrasts with mandatory mandates elsewhere in the BOL, such as Art. IX, Sec. 9 (***"The Parliament shall pass a law creating a housing and human settlements agency"***). The permissive nature means the power is currently exercised through BAA 13's institutional mandate and the suppletory application of national law.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**
A. The BOL does not create a specific body for urban land reform and land use. However, Art. IX, Sec. 9 mandates a **housing and human settlements agency** — fulfilled through the establishment of MHSD under BAA 13.[^7] MHSD's BAA 13 mandate expressly includes urban development and land use functions (Sec. 3(a), (b), (g); Sec. 9(6)). Art. VII, Sec. 42 further authorizes Parliament to create offices for **planning and development**, which could encompass a dedicated land use planning authority.[^8]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**
A. Three conditions: (1) Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) — the program must be ***"Subject to the provisions of the Constitution"***;[^9] (2) Art. V, Sec. 2 — all enumerated powers are subject to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** (autonomous regions operate within the framework of the Constitution);[^10] (3) Art. XIII, Sec. 17(1) — **land classification** requires a recommendation to the President, meaning BARMM cannot unilaterally reclassify public lands for urban use. Additionally, the eminent domain component of urban land reform is subject to the constitutional requirement of **just compensation** and **public use** (Art. III, Sec. 9 of the Constitution).

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**
A. The universal constraint — Art. X, Sec. 15 — provides that ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines"*** the organic act shall define the basic structure of government for the autonomous region.[^11] Subject-specific provisions include:
- **Art. XIII, Sec. 9**: ***"The State shall, by law, and for the common good, undertake, in cooperation with the public sector, a continuing program of urban land reform and housing..."***[^12] This is a **constitutional mandate** for urban land reform directed at Congress — it establishes a constitutional floor of protection.
- **Art. XIII, Sec. 10**: ***"Urban or rural poor dwellers shall not be evicted nor their dwelling demolished, except in accordance with law and in a just and humane manner."***[^13] This is an **absolute constitutional protection** — no BARMM legislation may diminish this eviction and demolition safeguard.
- **Art. XII, Sec. 1**: The State shall develop a **self-reliant and independent national economy** effectively controlled by Filipinos — land use regulation must serve productive national economy goals.
- **Art. II, Sec. 9**: ***"The State shall promote a just and dynamic social order..."*** — urban land reform is a **social justice instrument** under this state policy.

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**
A. Three constitutional guarantees directly apply: (1) **Protection against arbitrary eviction** — Art. XIII, Sec. 10 prohibits eviction and demolition except in accordance with law and in a just and humane manner. BARMM urban land reform legislation must incorporate at minimum these safeguards. (2) **Due process and just compensation** — Art. III, Sec. 1 (due process) and Art. III, Sec. 9 (no private property shall be taken for public use without just compensation). Land acquisition for urban reform must comply with both. (3) **Equal protection** — Art. III, Sec. 1. Urban land reform must not discriminate against any group — including non-Moro inhabitants and settlers — in land distribution or access to socialized housing.

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 9 directs the State to undertake ***"a continuing program of urban land reform and housing."*** RA 7279 (UDHA) implements this constitutional mandate at the national level. Because BARMM's urban land reform power is subject to the Constitution, any Bangsamoro program must satisfy at least the **constitutional floor** established by Art. XIII, Secs. 9-10 — even if it need not mirror the specific mechanisms of RA 7279.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**
A. The national urban land reform framework centers on **RA 7279** (UDHA, 1992), as amended by RA 9397 (2007) and RA 10884 (2016). UDHA provides: socialized housing — government-subsidized housing for the underprivileged; balanced housing — developers must allocate a percentage of their projects to socialized housing; eviction and demolition safeguards — Sec. 28 prescribes mandatory conditions before eviction (30-day notice, adequate relocation, no eviction during inclement weather or at night); land disposition — idle lands, government lands, and lands acquired by expropriation for socialized housing; community mortgage program — enabling informal settlers to purchase land collectively. Land use regulation is governed by **RA 7160** (Local Government Code) which mandates CLUPs and zoning ordinances at the LGU level, with **DHSUD** (RA 11201) as the national regulatory and oversight body.

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**
A. **RA 7279** (UDHA) applies **suppletorily** — it governs urban land reform and housing in BARMM pending enactment of a Bangsamoro urban land reform and land use program. RA 7279's eviction and demolition safeguards (Sec. 28) apply directly as they implement the constitutional mandate of Art. XIII, Sec. 10. **RA 7160** (LGC) governs BARMM LGU land use planning suppletorily pending a Bangsamoro Local Government Code. **RA 11201** (DHSUD Act) establishes the national regulatory framework — DHSUD retains regulatory functions in BARMM pending full devolution to MHSD. **RA 6657** (CARL) governs agricultural land conversion — MAFAR exercises this authority within BARMM.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**
A. **DHSUD** — exercises subdivision and condominium regulation, land use and development regulation, and housing standards enforcement in BARMM pending full devolution. **NHA** — implements socialized housing production and resettlement projects, particularly Marawi rehabilitation. **SHFC** — administers community mortgage programs in BARMM. **NHMFC/Pag-IBIG** — provides housing loan programs to BARMM residents. **DAR** — exercises authority over agricultural land conversion (shared with MAFAR in BARMM). Authority transfer from national agencies to MHSD depends on enactment of a standalone Bangsamoro urban land reform and land use act.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**
A. The **Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (MHSD)** is primarily responsible, established under BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI.[^14] MHSD's mandate expressly covers ***"the management of housing, human settlement, and urban development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** and designates it as ***"the main planning and policy-making, regulatory, program coordination, and performance monitoring entity for all housing, human settlements, and urban development concerns."***[^15]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**
A. No standalone urban land reform and land use act has been enacted. The power is exercised through MHSD's BAA 13 mandate and the suppletory application of national law (primarily RA 7279 and RA 7160).

**Q. What are the lead ministry's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI, Chapter 1, Sec. 2 establishes MHSD's mandate: ***"The Ministry of Human Settlements and Development shall act as the primary agency for the management of housing, human settlement, and urban development in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It shall be the main planning and policy-making, regulatory, program coordination, and performance monitoring entity for all housing, human settlements, and urban development concerns."***[^16]

Sec. 3 enumerates 19 powers and functions. Those directly relevant to urban land reform and land use include:
- **(a)** ***"Develop and implement comprehensive and integrated housing, human settlements, and urban and rural economic development plans and programs"***
- **(b)** ***"Institute mechanisms for the proper management of the growth and development of urban and rural communities"***
- **(c)** ***"Coordinate and monitor urban and rural economic development undertakings"***
- **(g)** ***"Implement a single regulatory system that shall govern all activities relative to the planning, development, production, marketing, and management of socialized housing and urban development projects"***
- **(h)** ***"Conduct continuing and comprehensive studies and researches necessary for housing and urban and rural development"***
- **(o)** ***"Develop and establish a sector performance monitoring and assessment mechanism to accurately and independently report on the performance of the constituent local government units in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region on the implementation of housing and urban plans and policies"***
- **(p)** ***"Develop, in partnership with beneficiaries, People's Plans which shall contain site development plans that conform to the CLUP of the local government unit"***
- **(r)** ***"Identify and acquire government lands and private lands through donation, expropriation, and payment of just compensation or any other form of alienation for housing and rural development"***[^17]

Organizationally, Sec. 9(6) creates the **Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division** under Technical Regulatory Services — the dedicated unit for land use regulation within MHSD.[^18]

MAFAR also exercises a related function: BAA 13, Book VI, Title I, Sec. 3(z) grants MAFAR ***"exclusive authority to approve or disapprove conversion of agricultural lands for residential, commercial, industrial, and other land uses."***[^19]

**Q. What can the lead ministry NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13 provides MHSD with institutional existence and general functions but cannot establish: (1) a **comprehensive zoning and land use regulatory code** with specific development control standards, density regulations, setback requirements, and use classifications; (2) **mandatory CLUP compliance mechanisms** — requiring all BARMM LGUs to prepare and update CLUPs conforming to regional standards; (3) **urban land reform instruments** — idle lands taxation, land banking, land acquisition priorities, and beneficiary identification criteria; (4) **eviction and demolition procedures** specific to BARMM context — particularly for conflict-affected and IDP communities; (5) **penalty provisions** for unauthorized land use changes, illegal conversions, and non-compliant developments; (6) **Shari'ah-compliant land disposition** — procedures for dealing with *waqf* properties, Islamic inheritance-affected lands, and *masjid*-adjacent lands in urban reform programs; (7) **interagency coordination framework** — delineating MHSD-MILG-MENRE-MAFAR-BPDA roles in land use governance.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**
A. No resolution specifically directing the drafting of a Bangsamoro urban land reform and land use act has been identified. Resolutions Nos. 405 and 476 address housing and shelter — related but not directly calling for comprehensive land use legislation.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**
A. MHSD is the correct lead for **urban land reform** given its BAA 13 mandate covering housing, human settlements, and urban development. However, the **land use** component of this power straddles multiple agencies: MHSD handles urban development land use through its Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division; **MILG** supervises LGUs that prepare CLUPs and enact zoning ordinances; **BPDA** develops the regional spatial strategy; **MAFAR** controls agricultural land conversion; **MENRE** manages environmental overlays. The land use dimension requires interagency coordination — MHSD leads urban land reform, but land use planning is a **multi-agency function** with no single coordinator.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**
A.
- **Lead ministry**: MHSD — BAA 13, Bk. VI, Title VI; BAA 85 budget allocation; operational with Provincial Human Settlements and Development Offices in every province
- **Supporting ministries**: MILG (LGU supervision including CLUP compliance); MAFAR (agricultural land conversion); MENRE (environmental dimensions); MOPW (infrastructure tied to land use)
- **Planning authority**: BPDA — develops Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy and coordinates development planning
- **LGU-level offices**: Provincial and municipal planning offices responsible for CLUP preparation and zoning ordinance enforcement
- **Commissions**: No dedicated commission for land use exists in BARMM

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**
A. The BOL does not mandate specific institutions for urban land reform and land use beyond the housing and human settlements agency (Art. IX, Sec. 9 — fulfilled through MHSD). Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes Parliament to create offices for ***"planning and development"*** — this could encompass a dedicated land use planning authority, but it is a permissive authorization, not a mandate.[^20]

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**
A. Full exercise of this power would benefit from: (1) a **Bangsamoro Land Use Board or Commission** — a regulatory body to approve CLUPs, adjudicate land use disputes, and enforce regional land use standards (analogous to HLURB/DHSUD at the national level); (2) an **Interagency Land Use Coordination Committee** — formalizing the MHSD-MILG-MENRE-MAFAR-BPDA coordination on land use planning. These are not BOL-mandated but would be created by the standalone BAA.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**
A. **DHSUD** exercises regulatory authority over subdivision and condominium regulation, land use regulation, and housing development standards in BARMM — functions previously held by HLURB. DHSUD reviews and approves subdivision and condominium plans, development permits, and CLUPs within BARMM. **NHA** implements socialized housing projects, including Marawi rehabilitation housing. **SHFC** administers community mortgage programs enabling informal settlers to acquire land titles collectively. **Pag-IBIG Fund (HDMF)** provides housing loans to BARMM residents. **DAR** exercises jurisdiction over agricultural land conversion nationally.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**
A. Key programs include: NHA's Marawi Rehabilitation Housing Program — constructing permanent housing for Marawi IDP families; SHFC's Community Mortgage Program — community-based land acquisition; Pag-IBIG housing loan programs; DHSUD's Subdivision and Condominium Regulation — processing development permits; the National Shelter Program's various components for socialized housing financing.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**
A. Devolution is **incomplete**. MHSD exists and exercises policy and program functions, but regulatory functions — particularly subdivision/condominium regulation, CLUP review and approval, and land use standards enforcement — remain with DHSUD. No executive order or formal devolution agreement specifically transferring DHSUD's land use regulatory functions to MHSD has been issued. NHA continues to implement projects directly in BARMM. The absence of a standalone Bangsamoro urban land reform and land use act perpetuates the regulatory gap.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**
A. Yes — **land use regulation** involves concurrent activity. BARMM LGUs prepare CLUPs (under MILG supervision), but DHSUD reviews compliance with national standards. MHSD develops housing and urban development plans, but NHA implements housing projects directly. MAFAR exercises exclusive agricultural land conversion authority within BARMM, but DAR retains a national oversight role. This concurrent operation is **by default** (devolution incomplete and no BAA clarifies jurisdictional boundaries), not by design.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**
A. MHSD has been operationalized with Provincial Field Offices. The BAA 85 budget allocates P733.6M for the Housing and Human Settlement Development Program, including **land acquisition, development, and survey**. BPDA has developed the Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy (BDP Chapter 3) establishing a multi-nodal spatial framework with classified regional, sub-regional, provincial, and local centers. The MHSD Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division is operational. However, no standalone urban land reform and land use program has been enacted — the permissive mandate of Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) remains unexercised.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**
A. BARMM's potential advantage lies in:
- **Conflict-sensitive design**: BARMM can develop urban land reform that accounts for post-conflict realities — IDPs, contested lands, displacement-driven informal settlements, and the Marawi situation — which the national UDHA framework does not specifically address.
- **Shari'ah integration**: A Bangsamoro program can recognize *waqf* properties, Islamic inheritance patterns, and *masjid*-adjacent land protections — features absent from national law.
- **Institutional proximity**: MHSD and its Provincial Field Offices are closer to the ground than DHSUD, enabling more responsive land use regulation.
These advantages are **potential** — they depend on enacting the standalone legislation.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**
A. The **single biggest constraint** is the absence of a standalone Bangsamoro Urban Land Reform and Land Use Act. Without it: (1) MHSD lacks comprehensive regulatory authority — it operates on BAA 13's general mandate supplemented by national law; (2) there is no Bangsamoro-specific zoning code, land use regulatory framework, or urban land reform mechanism; (3) DHSUD retains regulatory functions that should be devolved; (4) LGUs have no Bangsamoro-specific CLUP standards. Enacting the program authorized by Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) would remove this constraint.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**
A. The national system provides:
- **Legislative depth**: RA 7279 (UDHA) with 30+ years of amendments, IRRs, and jurisprudence — a mature urban land reform framework
- **Institutional infrastructure**: DHSUD with specialized bureaus (Land Use and Development, Housing Regulation, Monitoring and Evaluation); NHA with decades of housing production experience; SHFC with community mortgage expertise
- **Regulatory systems**: CLUP review and approval process; subdivision/condominium development permit system; land use conversion approval framework; housing development standards
- **IT and data**: eCLUP system for digitized land use plans; DHSUD online permit tracking; NHA beneficiary database; SHFC community mortgage tracking

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**
A. The national framework does not account for: (1) **conflict-affected contexts** — UDHA's eviction and relocation provisions do not address the unique situation of IDPs, ex-combatants, and communities on contested lands; (2) **Shari'ah-compliant property arrangements** — national law does not recognize *waqf*, Islamic inheritance-based land division, or the unique property patterns in Muslim communities; (3) **post-Marawi urban reconstruction** — the scale and complexity of Marawi rebuilding exceeds UDHA's standard provisions; (4) **cultural land use patterns** — the relationship between *masjid*, *madrasah*, and residential areas in Bangsamoro communities reflects Islamic urbanism patterns that national zoning categories do not capture.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**
A.
- **Replicate**: UDHA's eviction and demolition safeguards (Sec. 28) — these implement a constitutional floor and should be adopted at minimum. CLUP preparation methodology — the national CLUP framework is technically sound.
- **Adapt**: balanced housing requirements (RA 10884) — adopt the concept but calibrate thresholds for BARMM's development context. DHSUD's regulatory permit system — adapt for MHSD's Housing One-Stop Processing Centers with Bangsamoro-specific requirements.
- **Replace**: land disposition mechanisms — design Bangsamoro-specific land banking, idle lands programs, and socialized housing land acquisition that account for *waqf* properties, Islamic inheritance patterns, conflict-affected land, and IDP resettlement needs. Zoning classifications — develop categories that reflect Bangsamoro settlement patterns and Islamic urbanism.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**
A. Yes — urban land reform and land use have a **significant Shari'ah dimension** in the Bangsamoro context. Islamic property law (*fiqh al-mu'amalat*) governs property rights, transactions, and endowments. *Waqf* properties are inalienable under Islamic law. Islamic inheritance rules (*mawaris*) affect land fragmentation and ownership patterns. The design of urban spaces in Muslim communities reflects Islamic principles — the relationship between *masjid*, *souq* (market), residential areas, and public spaces follows traditional Islamic urbanism patterns.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**
A. The BOL does not directly reference Shari'ah in Art. XIII, Sec. 17 (the urban land reform and land use provision). However, Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to legislate on Shari'ah ***"property and contract law"*** — which intersects directly with urban land reform when dealing with Muslim-owned urban properties.[^21] Art. X, Sec. 1 establishes the Shari'ah justice system's jurisdiction over property disputes between Muslims, meaning Shari'ah courts would adjudicate certain land disputes arising from urban land reform.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**
A. Key principles include:
- ***Hifz al-mal*** (protection of property) — one of the five *maqasid*. Property rights must be respected; expropriation requires just compensation (*ta'wid*).
- ***Waqf*** (endowment) — land dedicated to charitable or religious purposes is **permanently inalienable**. A Bangsamoro land use program must identify and exempt *waqf* properties from land reform redistribution.
- ***Mawaris*** (Islamic inheritance) — Shari'ah inheritance rules produce distinctive land fragmentation patterns. Land use planning must account for multi-heir property situations.
- ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — justifies land use regulation for community welfare, including zoning and development controls.
- ***Ihya' al-mawat*** (reviving dead/idle land) — Islamic principle encouraging productive use of idle land, which parallels urban land reform's idle lands provisions.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**
A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) — Title I, Book Two governs property relations for Muslims, including succession and family property. Shari'ah District Courts have jurisdiction over property disputes between Muslims (BOL Art. X, Sec. 6). No Parliament-enacted Shari'ah legislation on urban property or land use exists. The *Darul Ifta'* has not issued formal opinions (*fatawa*) on urban land reform in the Bangsamoro context.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**
A. Integration should be **principled with specific mandatory protections**: (1) *waqf* properties must be identified and categorically **exempted** from land reform redistribution — this is a mandatory Shari'ah requirement, not optional; (2) Islamic inheritance-affected properties should be subject to **mediation-first approaches** through Shari'ah courts before any land reform disposition; (3) zoning classifications should include an **Islamic institutional zone** category recognizing *masjid-madrasah* complexes; (4) the **Shari'ah Legislative Review Board (SLRB)** should review the urban land reform and land use act for Shari'ah compliance before enactment.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**
A. BARMM has **substantial room for divergence**. The BOL constraint in Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) is "subject to the provisions of the Constitution" — not "consistent with national laws." This means the Bangsamoro urban land reform and land use program need not mirror RA 7279 (UDHA) or any other national law, provided it satisfies the constitutional floor established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B) — particularly Art. XIII, Secs. 9-10 (urban land reform mandate and eviction safeguards). BARMM can: establish **different zoning classifications** reflecting Bangsamoro settlement patterns; create **Shari'ah-compliant land disposition mechanisms** as outlined in the **Shari'ah Considerations** section (Section E); design **conflict-sensitive urban land reform** for IDPs and contested lands; set **different density, setback, and development standards** than the national system. The constitutional floor — not national law — is the binding constraint.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**
A. Property developers operating both inside and outside BARMM would face different regulatory regimes — different CLUP review processes, different development standards, different socialized housing requirements. BARMM legislation should include: **mutual recognition provisions** for development permits issued by national agencies for pre-existing projects; **transitional provisions** for properties currently regulated under national law; **harmonization mechanisms** for cross-boundary developments (e.g., Cotabato City which straddles BARMM and national jurisdiction). Constitutional rights — due process, just compensation, protection against arbitrary eviction — apply uniformly regardless.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**
A. The Bangsamoro urban land reform and land use act would govern within BARMM, superseding the suppletory application of RA 7279 and RA 7160's land use provisions to the extent of any inconsistency. National law would continue to apply suppletorily where the BAA is silent. Constitutional provisions — Art. XIII, Secs. 9-10, Art. III, Secs. 1 and 9 — always apply.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**
A. Three areas of jurisdictional ambiguity exist: (1) **CLUP review and approval** — DHSUD currently reviews BARMM LGU CLUPs, but MHSD has an oversight mandate under BAA 13 Sec. 3(o). No agreement delineates which agency has final approval authority. (2) **Subdivision and condominium regulation** — DHSUD retains regulatory jurisdiction pending formal devolution. MHSD's BAA 13 mandate to ***"implement a single regulatory system"*** (Sec. 3(g)) implies it should assume this function, but no BAA effectuates the transfer. (3) **Agricultural land conversion** — MAFAR exercises "exclusive authority" under BAA 13, but DAR's national jurisdiction has not been formally ceded. A standalone BAA would resolve all three overlaps by legislating clear jurisdictional boundaries.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**
A. No pending PBs addressing a comprehensive Bangsamoro urban land reform and land use program have been identified. The permissive mandate of Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) remains completely unexercised — neither through enacted BAA nor pending legislation.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**
A. **BDP Chapter 3** (Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy) establishes the multi-nodal spatial framework — regional centers, sub-regional centers, growth nodes — that depends on enforceable land use regulation to translate spatial strategy into physical development. **BDP Chapter 11** (Infrastructure) — Target 6.5.1 aims to reduce the housing backlog from 38.88% to 20%, requiring **land acquisition, development, and survey** — which is the operational intersection of urban land reform and land use planning. The Housing and Human Settlement Development Program (P733.6M in BAA 85) is directly enabled by this power.[^22] **Mas Matatag Pillar 3** (Accelerating Social Development) prioritizes housing and settlement development.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**
A. Without a standalone urban land reform and land use act: (1) the **BDP housing backlog reduction target** (38.88% to 20%) cannot be fully achieved — MHSD lacks the comprehensive land acquisition and disposition framework that urban land reform legislation would provide; (2) the **Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy** remains aspirational — without enforceable land use standards, BARMM LGUs are not required to align CLUPs with the regional spatial framework; (3) **Marawi rehabilitation** proceeds under national frameworks that do not account for the conflict-specific and Shari'ah-specific needs of the affected community; (4) **agricultural land conversion** lacks a BARMM-specific regulatory framework, risking uncontrolled conversion that undermines food security (a MAFAR concern); (5) the **devolution of regulatory functions** from DHSUD to MHSD stalls — without enabling legislation, DHSUD has no legal basis to transfer authority.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does power (bbb) — urban land reform and land use — relate to power (cc) — housing and human settlements?**
A. The two powers are **functionally interdependent**: urban land reform provides the **land supply** (through land acquisition, disposition, and land banking) and **regulatory framework** (through zoning and development controls) that housing programs require to deliver affordable shelter. MHSD is the lead ministry for both powers. The BOL recognizes this interconnection — Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) appears in the same section as the land classification and cadastral survey provisions, while Art. IX, Sec. 9 mandates the housing agency. A standalone Bangsamoro Urban Land Reform and Land Use Act would work in tandem with a Bangsamoro Housing Code — one providing the land framework, the other the housing program.

**Q. How does this power relate to power (aaa) — urban and rural planning development?**
A. Power (aaa) provides the **planning framework** (CLUPs, spatial strategy, urban and rural plans) while power (bbb) provides the **regulatory and reform instruments** (zoning enforcement, land reform redistribution, development controls). Both depend on the same spatial data, overlap in institutional coverage (MHSD, BPDA, MILG), and should be addressed in coordinated or combined legislation. The BOL lists them as separate powers (Art. V, Sec. 2(aaa) and (bbb)), but operationally they are two dimensions of the same governance function.

**Q. What is the significance of Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3) using "may" instead of "shall"?**
A. The permissive "may" means Parliament is **not constitutionally compelled** to enact an urban land reform and land use program — unlike the mandatory mandates in Art. IX, Sec. 9 (housing agency — "shall") or Art. XIII, Sec. 24 (cooperative code — "shall adopt and implement"). This gives Parliament **discretion on timing** — it can choose to address other legislative priorities first while relying on national law suppletorily. However, the practical consequence is that without exercising this "may," MHSD operates under BAA 13's general mandate without the comprehensive regulatory tools that urban land reform requires.

**Q. What unique challenges does urban land reform face in the Bangsamoro context?**
A. Five challenges distinguish the Bangsamoro context from the national framework: (1) **Post-conflict land tenure disputes** — decades of armed conflict displaced populations and created overlapping land claims, particularly in Marawi and Maguindanao; (2) **IDP resettlement** — over 300,000 Marawi IDPs alone require permanent resettlement, straining available urban land; (3) **Informal settlements on contested lands** — many informal settlements occupy land with unclear title, including ancestral domain claims; (4) ***Waqf* property protections** — significant urban land in Bangsamoro communities is dedicated to religious and charitable endowments that cannot be redistributed; (5) **Fragmented land due to Islamic inheritance** — *mawaris* rules produce multi-heir properties that resist conventional land use planning approaches.

**Q. Should the Bangsamoro urban land reform and land use program be a standalone BAA or part of a broader land governance code?**
A. Given the functional interdependence of powers (bbb), (cc), and (aaa), Parliament has two options: (1) a **standalone Bangsamoro Urban Land Reform and Land Use Act** — focused on land reform instruments and land use regulation, cross-referencing the housing and planning frameworks; or (2) a **comprehensive Bangsamoro Land Governance and Housing Code** combining urban land reform, land use regulation, housing, and urban/rural planning into one integrated statute. The latter approach would mirror the national UDHA's breadth while addressing all three related powers in a coordinated legislative package. Either approach fulfills the permissive mandate of Art. XIII, Sec. 17(3).
