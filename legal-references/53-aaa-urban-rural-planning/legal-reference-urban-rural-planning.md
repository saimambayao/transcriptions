# (aaa) Urban and Rural Planning Development

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(aaa)** — ***"Urban and rural planning development;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(aaa) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | No express consistency requirement with national planning law. The BOL does not subject this power to any "consistent with national laws" or "in accordance with" qualifier. However, Art. V, Sec. 2 is ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** and ***"this Organic Law"*** — establishing a constitutional floor. Art. XIII, Sec. 6 requires Bangsamoro participation in national development planning through NEDA, indicating coordination but not subordination. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (MHSD)** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI, Secs. 1-12 (urban and rural development planning, land use regulation, and human settlements management); **Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA)** — BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Secs. 39-42 (socio-economic development planning and coordination) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Ministry of Interior and Local Government (MILG) — supervision over LGU development planning; Ministry of Public Works (MPW) — infrastructure planning; Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE) — environmental planning constraints; Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) — agricultural land use conversion |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 3 (Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy) — multi-nodal spatial development, growth centers, functional roles of provinces; Chapter 11 (Scaling Up Strategic, Adequate, and Climate-Resilient Infrastructure) — infrastructure planning alignment |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | No express legislative mandate naming a specific "urban and rural planning code." However, Art. XIII, Sec. 4 mandates the **Bangsamoro Economic and Development Council (BEDC)** as the planning, monitoring, and coordinating agency — created through BAA 6. Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes Parliament to create an office for **planning and development**. Art. XIII, Sec. 17 authorizes Parliament to enact an ***"urban land reform and land use program"*** — this is the closest legislative directive (covered under power (bbb) but directly relevant to planning). |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government in this area?**
A. Art. V, Sec. 2(aaa) grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over ***"Urban and rural planning development."***[^1] This is an **enumerated power** under the general welfare authority of the Bangsamoro Government. The grant covers the full spectrum of planning activity — spatial planning, physical framework planning, land use planning, zoning, settlement pattern management, and the coordination of urban and rural development strategies within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**
A. Multiple provisions elaborate on the planning mandate:

- **Art. XIII, Sec. 2** requires that development in the Bangsamoro ***"shall be carefully planned taking into consideration the ecological balance and the natural resources that are available for its use and for the use of future generations."***[^2] This establishes **sustainability as a planning obligation**, not merely a policy preference.

- **Art. XIII, Sec. 3** mandates a ***"comprehensive framework for sustainable development"*** that guides program and policy mechanisms focusing on ***"the environment dimensions of social and economic interventions."***[^3]

- **Art. XIII, Sec. 4** creates the **BEDC** as the ***"planning, monitoring, and coordinating agency for all development plans, programs, and projects"*** and requires that plans ***"take into account the development plans of the provinces, cities, municipalities, and barangays."***[^4] This is the apex planning provision — all urban and rural planning flows through this institutional framework.

- **Art. XIII, Sec. 6** mandates Bangsamoro participation in **national development planning** through NEDA representation.[^5]

- **Art. XIII, Sec. 17** authorizes Parliament to enact an ***"urban land reform and land use program for the region"***[^6] — the legislative basis for comprehensive land use planning.

- **Art. VII, Sec. 42** authorizes Parliament to create offices for ***"planning and development"*** — implemented through the BPDA.[^7]

- **Art. VI, Sec. 8** creates the **Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board** for integrating economic, social, and environmental considerations.[^8]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**
A. The BOL does not name a specific "urban and rural planning code" or equivalent. However, Art. XIII, Sec. 4 mandates the establishment of the BEDC (fulfilled through BAA 6), and Art. XIII, Sec. 17 authorizes (but does not mandate) Parliament to ***"enact an urban land reform and land use program."***[^6] The permissive "may enact" language means this is an authorization, not an obligation — but it signals clear legislative intent that Parliament should act.

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**
A. Yes. The BOL directly creates or mandates:
- **BEDC** (Art. XIII, Sec. 4) — the apex planning body[^4]
- **Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board** (Art. VI, Sec. 8) — intergovernmental sustainable development coordination[^8]
- **Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board** (Art. VI, Sec. 6) — coordinates national and Bangsamoro infrastructure plans[^9]
- **Planning and development office** authority (Art. VII, Sec. 42) — implemented as BPDA under BAA 13[^7]
- **Ministry of Human Settlements and Development** (Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a)(l)) — the line ministry for settlements planning[^10]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**
A. The following qualifications apply:
- The chapeau of Art. V, Sec. 2 subjects this power to ***"Section 20, Article X of the Constitution"*** (the constitutional framework clause) and ***"this Organic Law"***[^1]
- Art. XIII, Sec. 17 subjects the land use program to ***"the provisions of the Constitution"***[^6] — invoking constitutional property rights and social justice provisions
- Art. XIII, Sec. 6 requires Bangsamoro development plans to be ***"reflected in the national development plans"*** through NEDA participation[^5] — a coordination requirement
- Presidential **general supervision** applies as stated in the chapeau of Art. V, Sec. 2

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to this subject area?**
A. The following constitutional provisions apply:

- **Universal constraint (applies to all 55 powers)**: Art. X, Sec. 15 — autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution."***[^11]

- **Art. XII, Sec. 1** (National Economy and Patrimony) — ***"The goals of the national economy are a more equitable distribution of opportunities, income, and wealth; a sustained increase in the amount of goods and services produced by the nation for the benefit of the people."***[^12] Urban and rural planning must advance equitable distribution of development opportunities.

- **Art. XII, Sec. 5** — The State shall protect ***"the rights of indigenous cultural communities to their ancestral lands to ensure their economic, social, and cultural well-being."***[^13] Planning in areas with indigenous peoples must respect ancestral domain claims.

- **Art. XIII, Sec. 1** (Social Justice) — ***"The Congress shall give highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all the people to human dignity."***[^14] Planning must prioritize social justice — equitable access to services, shelter, and livelihood.

- **Art. XIII, Sec. 9** (Urban Land Reform and Housing) — ***"The State shall, by law, and for the common good, undertake, in cooperation with the public sector, a continuing program of urban land reform and housing which will make available at affordable cost decent housing and basic services to underprivileged and homeless citizens."***[^15] This is a mandatory state obligation — BARMM planning must include affordable housing and urban reform.

- **Art. XIII, Sec. 10** — ***"Urban or rural poor dwellers shall not be evicted nor their dwelling demolished, except in accordance with law and in a just and humane manner."***[^16] A direct constraint on planning enforcement actions — demolitions and relocations must follow due process.

- **Art. II, Sec. 9** (Declaration of Principles) — ***"The State shall promote a just and dynamic social order..."***[^17] Planning serves as a tool for this state policy.

- **Art. III, Sec. 1** (Bill of Rights) — ***"No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law..."***[^18] Zoning and land use restrictions that diminish property value or restrict use must satisfy due process. Just compensation applies to takings through eminent domain.

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**
A. The Constitution guarantees: (1) **due process** in all land use regulation — property owners must be heard before zoning reclassification or condemnation (Art. III, Sec. 1);[^18] (2) **equal protection** — planning standards must not discriminate (Art. III, Sec. 1);[^18] (3) **social justice** in housing — underprivileged citizens have a right to affordable housing within a continuing program (Art. XIII, Sec. 9);[^15] (4) **protection against arbitrary eviction** — urban and rural poor may not be evicted except in accordance with law (Art. XIII, Sec. 10);[^16] (5) **ancestral domain rights** of indigenous peoples must be respected in all planning activities (Art. XII, Sec. 5).[^13]

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for legislation or institutions in this area?**
A. Art. XIII, Sec. 9 mandates a ***"continuing program of urban land reform and housing"*** — this is a constitutional obligation, not discretionary.[^15] Congress (and by extension, Parliament under the BOL) must enact measures implementing this mandate. Art. XIII, Sec. 10 requires that any eviction or demolition be ***"in accordance with law"*** — requiring legislation governing planning enforcement.[^16]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for this subject area?**
A. The national framework provides a comprehensive planning architecture:

- **RA 7160** (Local Government Code) establishes the **Comprehensive Land Use Plan (CLUP)** as the mandatory planning instrument for every LGU — covering land allocation, zoning, infrastructure priorities, and development controls.[^19]
- **RA 7279** (UDHA) as amended by **RA 10884** creates the national urban development and housing program — requiring LGUs to identify socialized housing sites, regulate demolitions, and integrate housing into CLUPs.[^20]
- **RA 11201** creates the **DHSUD** as the national apex agency for housing, human settlements, and urban development — absorbing HLURB, HUDCC, NHA, and shelter agency functions.[^21]
- **RA 10587** regulates the environmental planning profession — requiring licensed planners for CLUP preparation.[^22]
- **EO 72 (1993)** operationalizes the CLUP mandate — prescribing preparation, approval, and implementation guidelines.[^23]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**
A. **RA 7160** applies suppletorily to BARMM LGUs for CLUP preparation pending a Bangsamoro Local Governance Code. **RA 7279** (UDHA) applies suppletorily for urban development and housing pending BARMM housing legislation. **RA 11201** (DHSUD Act) applies nationally but MHSD exercises regional authority under BAA 13. **RA 10587** (Environmental Planning Act) applies directly — planners must be licensed nationally. **EO 72** CLUP guidelines apply suppletorily to BARMM LGUs.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**
A. **DHSUD** retains national regulatory functions including subdivision and condominium regulation, national housing programs, and CLUP review/approval support. **NEDA** coordinates national development planning including BARMM's integration into the Philippine Development Plan. **DPWH** implements nationally-funded infrastructure under Art. XIII, Sec. 37. These national agencies' functions within BARMM are subject to coordination with BARMM counterparts — devolution remains incomplete for planning regulatory functions.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**
A. Two BARMM institutions share primary responsibility:

1. **BPDA** — BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Secs. 39-42.[^24] The BPDA is the ***"planning, coordinating, and monitoring agency for all development plans, policies, programs and projects of the Bangsamoro Government"*** and serves as the ***"counterpart of the National Economic Development Authority (NEDA) in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^24] The BPDA handles macro-level socio-economic and spatial planning.

2. **MHSD** — BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI, Secs. 1-12.[^25] The MHSD is the ***"primary agency for the management of housing, human settlement, and urban development"*** and the ***"main planning and policy-making, regulatory, program coordination, and performance monitoring entity for all housing, human settlements, and urban development concerns."***[^25] The MHSD handles operational urban and rural development planning, land use regulation, and settlements management.

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**
A. No standalone urban and rural planning code has been enacted. The power is currently exercised through: (1) **BAA 6** (BEDC establishment); (2) **BAA 13** (Administrative Code — BPDA and MHSD mandates); (3) **BAA 31** (Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board).

**Q. What are the lead institutions' mandates and functions under BAA 13?**

**BPDA (BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Secs. 39-42):**[^24]

The BPDA's functions include:
- a. Coordinate the formulation, and undertake monitoring and evaluation, of socio-economic development policies and plans
- b. Review, evaluate and recommend short, medium and long-term development plans, and proposed programs and projects for the BEDC
- c. Coordinate the annual and multi-year investment programming of ministries, line agencies and offices
- d. Provide technical assistance to ministries, agencies, LGUs and development partners in identifying and developing plans, programs and projects
- e. Coordinate all regional, national and foreign-assisted programs and projects
- f. Conduct research, statistics administration, knowledge product development, and information management in support of planning
- g. Perform such other appropriate tasks as may be assigned by the Council

The BPDA's organizational structure includes a **Planning and Policies Bureau (PPB)** with divisions for Macro-Economic Planning, Economic Planning, Social Planning, **Infrastructure Planning**, Peace/Public Order/Safety/Security, Monitoring and Evaluation, and **Local Planning and Coordinating Division** — the last two being directly relevant to urban and rural planning coordination with LGUs.

**MHSD (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI, Secs. 1-12):**[^25]

The MHSD's mandate declares that it shall ***"formulate, adopt, execute, and promote comprehensive, integrated, and sustainable regional housing, urban and rural development policies, plans, programs, and projects."***[^25] Key functions include:
- a. Develop and implement comprehensive and integrated housing, human settlements, and **urban and rural economic development plans** in coordination with LGUs
- b. Institute mechanisms for proper management of **growth and development of urban and rural communities**
- c. Coordinate and monitor urban and rural economic development undertakings
- g. Implement a **single regulatory system** governing activities relative to planning, development, production, marketing, and management of socialized housing and urban development projects
- h. Conduct continuing and comprehensive studies for **housing and urban and rural development**
- p. Develop **People's Plans** containing site development plans conforming to the CLUP of the LGU

The MHSD structure includes a **Technical Regulatory Services** division with an ***"Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division"***[^26] — the unit specifically responsible for land use regulation and urban development oversight. The **Operations Management Services** includes a ***"Planning and Design Division"***[^26] for operational planning functions.

**Q. What can the lead institutions NOT do without a standalone BAA beyond BAA 13?**
A. BAA 13 creates the institutions and assigns general functions. Without a standalone urban and rural planning code, the institutions cannot:
- **Establish a regional CLUP framework** — prescribing mandatory contents, approval processes, review cycles, and compliance mechanisms for LGU comprehensive plans within BARMM
- **Create a regional zoning system** — defining zoning categories, permitted uses, conditional uses, prohibited uses, and variance procedures specific to BARMM context
- **Impose regulatory penalties** — for non-compliance with planning standards, unauthorized land use conversion, or violation of zoning ordinances
- **Establish a regional spatial framework plan** — a binding regional-level spatial plan that guides LGU CLUPs (the BDP Spatial Strategy in Chapter 3 is a policy framework, not a legislative instrument)
- **Create a planning review and approval authority** — a regulatory body that reviews and approves LGU CLUPs, subdivision plans, and development permits under regional standards
- **Codify the relationship between BPDA and MHSD** on planning — their respective jurisdictions over macro-spatial vs. operational-urban planning are defined in BAA 13 but not legislatively formalized with clear delineation

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**
A. **PR No. 720** (proposed) urges a moratorium on agricultural land conversion and alignment with the BARMM Regional Land Use Policy, signaling parliamentary awareness of land use governance gaps. **PB No. 352** (Lake Lanao Area Management Act) includes spatial planning, zoning, and land use provisions for the Lake Lanao watershed — a sector-specific model for area-based planning legislation.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the assigned lead MOA the correct one for this power?**
A. Urban and rural planning development has a **dual institutional assignment** that is appropriate but requires legislative clarity:

- **BPDA** correctly handles macro-level development planning — the Bangsamoro Development Plan, Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy, investment programming, and NEDA coordination. Its BAA 13 mandate explicitly covers ***"all development plans, policies, programs and projects."***[^24]
- **MHSD** correctly handles operational urban and rural planning — settlements planning, land use regulation, housing site designation, and urban development management. Its BAA 13 mandate explicitly covers ***"urban and rural development policies, plans, programs, and projects."***[^25]

The challenge is **jurisdictional overlap**: both institutions claim planning authority, with no statutory mechanism resolving conflicts between the BPDA's macro-spatial plans and the MHSD's operational urban plans. **MILG** also has a role through its supervision of LGU development planning (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VIII, Sec. 3(d) — ***"Establish and prescribe plans, policies, programs, and projects to strengthen the administrative, technical, and fiscal capabilities of local government offices"***).[^27]

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**
A.
- **Lead planning authority**: BPDA (BAA 13, Book IV, Title II) — macro-level planning, BEDC secretariat
- **Lead urban/rural development ministry**: MHSD (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VI) — housing, settlements, urban and rural development, land use regulation
- **LGU oversight**: MILG (BAA 13, Book VI, Title VIII) — supervision over LGU development planning
- **Intergovernmental bodies**: BEDC (BAA 6), Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BAA 31), Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board (Art. VI, Sec. 6)
- **BEDC Sectoral Committees**: Regional Land Use Committee (RLUC) — specifically responsible for land use policy within the BEDC structure
- **Field offices**: Provincial Human Settlements and Development Offices (BAA 13, Sec. 10) — provincial-level planning and implementation

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**
A. Art. VI, Sec. 6 mandates the **Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board** — its operational status is unclear. The BOL-mandated BEDC, BPDA, Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board, and MHSD have all been established through BAA 6, BAA 13, and BAA 31.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**
A. Yes. Full exercise of this power would benefit from:
- A **Regional Land Use Board** or equivalent — a regulatory body with authority to review and approve LGU CLUPs, hear zoning appeals, and enforce planning compliance within BARMM (analogous to the former HLURB role that DHSUD now performs nationally)
- A **Regional Spatial Planning Office** within BPDA or as a standalone unit — dedicated to maintaining the Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy as a binding instrument, not just a BDP chapter
- An **inter-ministry planning coordination mechanism** formalizing BPDA-MHSD-MILG-MAFAR coordination on land use, settlement, agricultural land conversion, and infrastructure planning

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**
A. **DHSUD** exercises land use regulatory functions including review of subdivision and condominium projects, though MHSD increasingly exercises regional authority. **NEDA** coordinates the integration of BARMM development plans into the Philippine Development Plan. **DPWH** implements national infrastructure projects that shape urban development patterns. The **Land Registration Authority (LRA)** manages land titling that underpins all planning enforcement.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for this area?**
A. NEDA coordinates the Philippine Development Plan process including BARMM integration through Art. XIII, Sec. 6. DHSUD provides CLUP technical assistance and reviews subdivision applications. The National Housing Authority (NHA) — now under DHSUD — operates housing programs in BARMM for IDPs and socialized housing. DPWH's infrastructure projects determine settlement growth patterns.

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power — has authority been transferred from national agencies to BARMM?**
A. Devolution is **partial and informal**. MHSD exercises urban and rural development functions under BAA 13, but DHSUD has not formally transferred all HLURB regulatory functions (CLUP approval, subdivision regulation, zoning appeal adjudication). BPDA operates as the NEDA counterpart but NEDA retains oversight of ODA-funded programs. The absence of a standalone planning code means the boundary between DHSUD and MHSD jurisdiction remains undefined by BARMM legislation.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate concurrently?**
A. Yes — (1) **CLUP review and approval**: both DHSUD (national) and MHSD (regional) provide technical assistance, with no clear statutory delineation of which has final approval authority for BARMM LGU CLUPs; (2) **Subdivision and housing regulation**: DHSUD retains national regulatory authority while MHSD claims regional regulatory authority under BAA 13; (3) **Development plan integration**: BPDA formulates the BDP while NEDA coordinates the PDP, with Art. XIII, Sec. 6 creating a coordination (not subordination) relationship; (4) **Agricultural land conversion**: MAFAR exercises regional authority under BAA 13, while the Department of Agrarian Reform retains national conversion authority.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**
A. Key accomplishments include: (1) **Bangsamoro Development Plan** — the 2nd BDP (2023-2028) includes a comprehensive **Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy** (Chapter 3) establishing multi-nodal growth centers, functional roles for provinces, and spatial development principles;[^28] (2) **BEDC establishment** through BAA 6 with five sectoral committees including the **Regional Land Use Committee (RLUC)** — the dedicated body for land use policy;[^29] (3) **BPDA operationalization** with a Planning and Policies Bureau, Infrastructure Planning Division, and Local Planning and Coordinating Division; (4) **MHSD establishment** with an Environment, Land Use, and Urban Development Division and provincial field offices; (5) **BAA 31** establishing the Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board for environmental-development integration.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**
A. BARMM's unique advantages include: (1) **conflict-sensitive planning** — MHSD's mandate specifically targets ***"victims of conflicts and atrocities"*** and IDPs,[^25] a population the national DHSUD framework does not specifically address; (2) **bottom-up integration** — Art. XIII, Sec. 4 requires LGU development plans to be integrated into regional plans, creating a more participatory framework than the national CLUP review system; (3) **Shari'ah and cultural sensitivity** — BARMM can design planning systems that account for *waqf* lands, Islamic inheritance patterns, and Bangsamoro settlement traditions that the national framework ignores; (4) **dedicated spatial strategy** — the BDP's Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy provides regionally specific development direction that the PDP cannot offer at this granularity.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**
A. The **single biggest constraint** is the **absence of a standalone urban and rural planning code**. Without this code: (1) BARMM LGUs default to RA 7160 and EO 72 procedures designed for the national context; (2) MHSD's regulatory authority over land use and subdivision development lacks a dedicated legislative basis beyond BAA 13's general mandate; (3) the Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy remains a policy document, not a binding legislative instrument; (4) there is no regional CLUP framework prescribing BARMM-specific planning standards; (5) the jurisdictional boundary between BPDA, MHSD, and MILG on planning functions remains unclear. Enacting a **Bangsamoro Urban and Rural Planning Code** would resolve all five constraints.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**
A. The national system provides: (1) **DHSUD** (RA 11201) — a comprehensive housing, human settlements, and urban development department with decades of institutional infrastructure from its HLURB, HUDCC, and NHA predecessors; (2) **CLUP framework** — established through RA 7160 and EO 72 with standardized CLUP guidelines, review procedures, and technical manuals that have been refined over 30 years; (3) **Environmental Planning profession regulation** (RA 10587) — requiring licensed planners for official planning documents; (4) **Subdivision and condominium regulation** — RA 4726 (Condominium Act) and PD 957 (Subdivision and Condominium Buyer's Protective Decree) with established registration, licensing, and adjudication systems; (5) **National Urban Development and Housing Framework** (RA 7279) with balanced housing requirements (RA 10884).

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**
A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in several areas: (1) **no conflict-sensitive planning standards** — the CLUP guidelines do not address post-conflict settlement patterns, IDP resettlement, or security-sensitive development zones; (2) **no Shari'ah accommodation** — national planning frameworks do not recognize *waqf* land classifications, Islamic property succession patterns, or the need for prayer facility zoning requirements; (3) **no island and archipelagic planning models** — the standard CLUP format is designed for mainland municipalities and does not adequately address the unique spatial challenges of Sulu and Tawi-Tawi's archipelagic geography; (4) **cultural mismatch** — national planning processes assume standard LGU capacity and participation mechanisms that do not account for Bangsamoro traditional governance structures and community decision-making patterns.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**
A.
- **Replicate**: the CLUP requirement for every LGU — every BARMM municipality needs a comprehensive plan updated on a regular cycle. The national environmental planning profession standards (RA 10587) should apply directly.
- **Adapt**: the CLUP content and review process — modify for BARMM context by adding *waqf* land classification, conflict-sensitive zoning, archipelagic planning standards, Islamic facility requirements, and post-conflict resettlement planning. Adapt the DHSUD CLUP review procedure into a BARMM regional planning review process under MHSD.
- **Replace**: the national housing shelter agency model — MHSD already replaces DHSUD's regional functions and should develop BARMM-specific socialized housing standards that prioritize IDP shelter and conflict-affected communities over the national framework's general urban poor orientation.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**
A. Yes, though it is indirect rather than central. Urban and rural planning intersects with Shari'ah through: (1) **property law** — Islamic inheritance creates distinctive land fragmentation patterns requiring specific planning responses; (2) ***waqf*** **endowments** — dedicated lands that cannot be rezoned or alienated for purposes contrary to the endowment; (3) **community planning standards** — Islamic settlements traditionally organize around the *masjid* (mosque) as the community center, with distinct spatial patterns for residential, commercial, and religious areas; (4) ***maslahah*** — the principle of public welfare justifies government regulation of land use and development.

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**
A. Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to ***"enact any Shari'ah-based legislation on...property"***[^30] — which could include Shari'ah-compliant property planning and zoning provisions. No BOL provision specifically references Shari'ah in relation to urban and rural planning. However, the general Shari'ah legislative authority encompasses property-related planning regulation.

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**
A.
- ***Hifz al-Mal*** (protection of property) — planning regulations must balance public interest against Islamic property protections. Zoning restrictions that amount to regulatory takings must account for Islamic property rights.
- ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — the overarching justification for planning regulation. Government authority to zone land, restrict development, and plan infrastructure is grounded in the duty to protect public welfare.
- ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — public land and resources are held in trust for the community. Planning officials exercise stewardship, not ownership, over the public domain.
- ***Waqf*** management — endowment properties require specific planning treatment. A standalone planning code should include a *waqf* land classification that protects endowed properties from rezoning or condemnation contrary to the endowment purpose.
- ***Darar*** (harm prevention) — the Islamic law principle prohibiting harm to neighbors provides a jurisprudential basis for nuisance zoning, setback requirements, and environmental buffers.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**
A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws) governs Muslim property succession and *waqf* management — its property provisions inform how planning must accommodate Islamic land ownership patterns.[^31] No Shari'ah court jurisprudence specifically addressing planning disputes has been identified, though Shari'ah District Courts have jurisdiction over ***"all cases involving disposition, distribution, and settlement of the estate of deceased Muslims"***[^32] — which includes land disputes arising from inheritance-driven fragmentation that planning seeks to address.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro legislation for this power?**
A. A **principled approach** is appropriate — Bangsamoro planning legislation should be guided by Islamic values (*maslahah*, *amanah*, *darar* prevention) and include specific provisions for *waqf* land classification and Islamic property protections, without requiring full Shari'ah compliance certification for every planning decision. Specific integration points: (1) mandatory *waqf* land identification in CLUPs; (2) mosque and prayer facility zoning standards in all settlement plans; (3) Islamic inheritance-sensitive land consolidation mechanisms; (4) Shari'ah review of planning legislation through the Shari'ah Legislative Review Board.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national law and policy in this area?**
A. The BARMM has **strong authority to diverge** from the national planning framework. Synthesizing from the preceding sections:

- The BOL contains **no express consistency requirement** with national planning laws — unlike powers such as civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39) where the BOL mandates consistency.
- The constitutional floor established in Section B (Constitutional Considerations) requires **due process, equal protection, social justice in housing, and protection against arbitrary eviction** — these are minimum standards, not maximum limits.
- As established in Section C (National Law Considerations), national planning laws (RA 7160, EO 72, RA 7279) currently apply **suppletorily** — they fill gaps where BARMM has not legislated, but they do not bind BARMM once a BAA is enacted.
- As established in Section E (Shari'ah Considerations), the Shari'ah dimension provides an **additional basis for divergence** — BARMM can create *waqf* land classifications, Islamic settlement planning standards, and Shari'ah-sensitive zoning provisions that have no national equivalent.
- Art. XIII, Sec. 6 requires only **coordination** with national planning, not conformity.

In practice, BARMM can: (1) create a regional planning code with different CLUP requirements than EO 72; (2) establish planning standards that exceed national minimums; (3) create new land use categories (*waqf*, conflict-affected zones, IDP resettlement areas) not found in the national framework; (4) establish a regional CLUP review authority replacing DHSUD's role; (5) design archipelagic and conflict-sensitive planning methodologies.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**
A. Property owners in BARMM would be subject to BARMM planning standards rather than national ones — this could create different zoning categories, development permits, and compliance requirements than those in adjacent non-BARMM areas. Developers operating in both BARMM and non-BARMM jurisdictions would need to comply with two different regulatory regimes. Cross-boundary infrastructure (roads, utilities connecting BARMM to non-BARMM areas) would require coordination through the Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board (Art. VI, Sec. 6). Subdivision developments straddling BARMM and non-BARMM boundaries would face dual regulation. Mutual recognition provisions in a standalone BAA would mitigate these complications.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**
A. The BAA governs within BARMM. National planning laws (RA 7160 CLUP provisions, EO 72 guidelines, RA 7279 urban development provisions) would apply suppletorily only where the BAA is silent. Constitutional provisions (due process, equal protection, social justice, anti-eviction protections) always apply regardless of the BAA's content.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**
A. Yes — three critical overlaps exist:

1. **CLUP approval authority**: Both DHSUD (national) and MHSD (regional) claim authority over CLUP review and technical assistance for BARMM LGUs. No BAA or MOA delineates the boundary. A standalone planning code should assign CLUP review authority exclusively to MHSD or a new regional planning board.

2. **Subdivision and housing regulation**: DHSUD retains national regulatory authority under RA 11201 while MHSD exercises regional authority under BAA 13, Sec. 3(g). Both can regulate subdivision projects within BARMM — creating potential for conflicting approvals or regulatory gaps.

3. **Agricultural land conversion**: MAFAR exercises regional authority under BAA 13 while the Department of Agrarian Reform retains national conversion authority under RA 6657/RA 9700. Land use conversion is a core planning function — unclear jurisdiction delays conversions and creates legal uncertainty for developers.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**
A. **PB No. 352** (Lake Lanao Area Management Act) addresses area-specific planning for the Lake Lanao watershed — including zoning, spatial planning, and management planning — but does not address the broader regional planning framework. **PR No. 720** signals intent on land use governance but is a resolution, not legislation. **No pending PB addresses the comprehensive urban and rural planning code gap** — this remains the most significant unaddressed legislative need for this power area.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**
A. **BDP Chapter 3** (Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy) directly depends on this power — the BSS establishes multi-nodal growth centers across BARMM's six provinces and Cotabato City, with spatial strategy principles of **agglomeration, connectivity, and vulnerability reduction**.[^28] Without a planning code, the BSS remains advisory rather than binding.

**BDP Chapter 11** (Infrastructure) depends on planning to guide infrastructure investments — road networks, utilities, and public facilities must follow spatial development plans.

The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** depends on this power through: **Pillar B** (Mas Matatag na Kabuhayan) — economic zoning, BIMP-EAGA node development, and halal economy hub strategies all require spatial planning legislation to designate and develop zones; **Pillar C** (Mas Matatag na Pamayanan) — infrastructure and community development require effective settlement management and urban/rural planning.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**
A. Without a standalone planning code: (1) the Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy cannot be enforced — growth center designations and functional role assignments remain aspirational without binding CLUPs; (2) BARMM LGUs will continue following national CLUP guidelines not designed for post-conflict, archipelagic, or Islamic settlement contexts; (3) agricultural land conversion will proceed without a coherent regional framework, threatening food security; (4) IDP resettlement planning will lack legally mandated spatial provisions; (5) economic zone designation under the Matatag agenda cannot proceed through planning legislation; (6) cross-boundary infrastructure coordination will lack a regional planning framework to guide BARMM's priorities.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. How does power (aaa) — Urban and Rural Planning Development relate to power (bbb) — Urban Land Reform and Land Use?**
A. These powers are **inseparable complements**. Power (aaa) addresses the **process and institutional framework** — how planning is done, who does it, what instruments are used (CLUPs, spatial plans, zoning). Power (bbb) addresses the **substantive program** — what the plans contain regarding land reform and land use allocation. In practice, a standalone BAA would likely address both powers simultaneously, since a planning code without land use provisions is meaningless, and a land use program without a planning framework is unimplementable. Art. XIII, Sec. 17's authorization to enact an ***"urban land reform and land use program"***[^6] bridges both powers.

**Q. How does the Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy operate without a legislative mandate?**
A. The BDP's Bangsamoro Spatial Strategy (Chapter 3) operates as a **policy framework approved by the Parliament through BDP adoption** — not as a binding legislative instrument. The BSS identifies multi-nodal growth centers, functional roles for provinces, and spatial development principles (agglomeration, connectivity, vulnerability reduction).[^28] However, LGUs are not legally required to conform their CLUPs to the BSS. A standalone planning code could elevate the BSS from policy to law — requiring LGU plans to conform to the regional spatial framework, similar to how national law requires LGU CLUPs to conform to the Philippine Development Plan.

**Q. What is the role of the Regional Land Use Committee (RLUC) within the BEDC?**
A. The RLUC is one of the BEDC's **five sectoral committees plus one special committee** — specifically responsible for land use policy formulation within the BEDC structure.[^29] It participated in the BDP 2023-2028 formulation process and provides the institutional mechanism for regional land use policy coordination. However, like the BEDC itself, the RLUC's authority is advisory and coordinating — it cannot issue binding land use regulations without a legislative basis.

**Q. How should BARMM address the unique planning challenges of its archipelagic provinces?**
A. Sulu and Tawi-Tawi's archipelagic geography presents planning challenges the national CLUP framework does not address — scattered island settlements, maritime connectivity as a planning variable, limited buildable land, and marine resource-dependent economies. A standalone planning code should include an **archipelagic planning module** — prescribing settlement planning standards for island municipalities, maritime transport corridor integration in spatial plans, marine and coastal zone management coordination, and modified CLUP requirements appropriate for small island LGUs with limited technical capacity.

**Q. What planning considerations are specific to conflict-affected areas within BARMM?**
A. Post-conflict planning requires specialized provisions not found in the national framework: (1) **IDP resettlement zone designation** — CLUPs must identify and reserve land for permanent resettlement of displaced populations; (2) **demilitarized and normalization zone planning** — areas subject to normalization programs require special planning treatment; (3) **war damage reconstruction zones** — areas like Marawi City after the 2017 siege require urban renewal and reconstruction planning provisions; (4) **security-sensitive infrastructure planning** — critical infrastructure placement must account for security considerations unique to BARMM; (5) **land dispute sensitive planning** — planning processes must account for competing ancestral domain, *waqf*, and private title claims over the same land — a pervasive issue in Bangsamoro areas.
