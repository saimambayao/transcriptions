# (m) Creation of Government-Owned or Controlled Corporations and Pioneer Firms

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(m)** — ***"Creation of government-owned or controlled corporations and pioneer firms;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(m) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Yes — Art. XII, Sec. 30 requires ***"compliance with the provisions of Republic Act No. 10149"*** (GOCC Governance Act of 2011) and creation ***"upon coordination with the National Government"*** |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Bangsamoro Parliament (BTA)** — legislative charter authority; **Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management (MFBM)** — fiscal oversight |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Governance Commission for GOCCs (GCG) — national oversight; Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board (IFPB) — determines BARMM participation in existing GOCCs; Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) — alternative incorporation pathway |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 7 (Economic Development); Chapter 13 (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) |
| **Priority Code** | None named — but a Bangsamoro GOCC Act would fill the regulatory framework gap |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over GOCCs and pioneer firms?**

A. Article V, Section 2(m) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"creation of government-owned or controlled corporations and pioneer firms"*** as one of the 55 powers. Art. XII, Sec. 30 elaborates with detailed operational provisions. This power encompasses: (1) **creating new GOCCs** by legislative charter or Corporation Code incorporation; (2) **creating pioneer firms** and other business entities for economic development; (3) **exercising authority** over existing GOCCs operating within BARMM (Art. XII, Sec. 31); and (4) **securing representation** on boards of national GOCCs operating substantially in the region (Art. XII, Sec. 32).[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. Five provisions elaborate:

   (1) Art. XII, Sec. 30 defines the **creation mechanism**: ***"Upon coordination with the National Government, the Parliament shall have the power to create government-owned or controlled corporations in compliance with the provisions of Republic Act No. 10149 ... by a grant of legislative charter, or under Batas Pambansa Blg. 68."*** GOCCs registered with SEC ***"may likewise operate outside the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** A separate paragraph authorizes ***"pioneering firms and other business entities to boost economic development."***[^2]

   (2) Art. XII, Sec. 31 grants ***"authority and control over existing government-owned or controlled corporations operating exclusively in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** — conditioned on **IFPB feasibility determination**. Existing GOCCs may transfer operations outside BARMM.[^3]

   (3) Art. XII, Sec. 32 guarantees **board representation** in national GOCCs operating substantially in BARMM, with a minimum of ***"at least one (1) seat in the governing boards."*** The IFPB determines the extent of participation and BARMM's share from operations.[^4]

   (4) Art. XII, Sec. 33 specifically names **AAIIBP** and **SPDA** — requiring IFPB to determine BARMM participation ***"within six (6) months from the establishment of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority."***[^5]

   (5) Art. XII, Sec. 6(f) classifies GOCC dividends — both from Bangsamoro GOCCs and from national GOCCs — as an enumerated **revenue source**.[^6]

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. The BOL does not name a specific "GOCC code" or "GOCC governance act" by title. The authority to create GOCCs is self-executing through the legislative charter mechanism. However, Art. XII, Sec. 30 requires compliance with RA 10149, which effectively imports that law's framework. A standalone **Bangsamoro GOCC Act** would provide region-specific governance, dividend, and oversight provisions.[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body for this power?**

A. The BOL does not create a specific Bangsamoro GOCC governance body. It assigns the **IFPB** — an intergovernmental body (Art. VI, Sec. 4; Art. XII, Sec. 37-41) — to determine BARMM participation in existing GOCCs. Nationally, the **GCG** (created by RA 10149) exercises oversight over all GOCCs, including those created by Parliament.[^7]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. Three express conditions:

   (1) **National Government coordination**: Art. XII, Sec. 30 requires GOCC creation ***"upon coordination with the National Government"*** — this is a procedural requirement, not a veto power. The nature of "coordination" (notification, consultation, or approval) is not specified.[^2]

   (2) **RA 10149 compliance**: Bangsamoro GOCCs must comply with the **GOCC Governance Act** — governance standards, board composition requirements, compensation limits, dividend obligations, and GCG oversight.[^2]

   (3) **IFPB determination**: BARMM's authority over existing GOCCs (Art. XII, Sec. 31), board representation (Art. XII, Sec. 32), and participation in AAIIBP/SPDA (Art. XII, Sec. 33) all require IFPB action.[^3][^4][^5]

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to GOCC creation?**

A. Three categories:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: The universal constitutional boundary on all 55 powers.[^8]

   (2) **GOCC Creation (Art. XII, Sec. 16)**: The Constitution requires that GOCCs be created by ***"a special law"*** (legislative charter). The 1987 Constitution prohibits the creation of private corporations by special law — but permits GOCCs to be chartered by legislation. The BOL's provision for legislative charter creation is consistent with this constitutional requirement.[^9]

   (3) **Accountability of Public Officers (Art. XI)**: GOCC officers are public officers subject to constitutional accountability standards — including SALN filing, anti-graft provisions, and Ombudsman jurisdiction. This applies to Bangsamoro GOCC officers.[^10]

**Q. What constitutional mandates exist for this area?**

A. The Constitution does not mandate GOCC creation — it permits it. Art. XII, Sec. 16 permits the creation of GOCCs by special law. The constitutional fiscal provisions (Art. XII, Sec. 29 — no public money spent except by appropriation) apply to all GOCCs, requiring that their budgets go through the appropriations process.

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for GOCCs?**

A. **RA 10149** (GOCC Governance Act) establishes a comprehensive framework: (1) **GCG** as the central oversight body with authority to review GOCC charters, appoint directors, set compensation, and evaluate performance; (2) **governance standards** — board composition requirements (independent directors, fit-and-proper test), compensation and position classification, performance evaluation, dividend remittance; (3) **charter review** — GCG reviews all existing and proposed GOCC charters; (4) **reporting and transparency** requirements. **RA 7656** requires GOCCs to declare at least 50% of net earnings as dividends to the National Government. **RA 11232** (Revised Corporation Code) provides the alternative SEC-registration pathway.[^11][^12][^13]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. (1) **RA 10149** — applies **directly** by express BOL mandate (Art. XII, Sec. 30). GCG oversight extends to Bangsamoro-created GOCCs. (2) **RA 11232** — applies directly as the alternative incorporation pathway. (3) **RA 7656** — applies to national GOCCs operating in BARMM; applicability to Bangsamoro-created GOCCs depends on whether they fall under the national dividend requirement framework. (4) **PD 690** (SPDA) and **RA 6848** (AAIIBP) — apply directly per Art. XII, Sec. 33.[^11][^12][^13]

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. (1) **GCG** — exercises oversight over all GOCCs including those created by Parliament, per RA 10149. (2) **SEC** — registers GOCCs incorporated under the Corporation Code. (3) **IFPB** — determines BARMM participation in existing GOCCs (Arts. XII, Sec. 31-33). (4) **Commission on Audit (COA)** — audits all GOCCs per constitutional mandate (Art. XII, Sec. 2 of the BOL reiterating Art. IX-D of the Constitution).

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which institution is primarily responsible?**

A. The **Bangsamoro Parliament** is the primary actor — exercising the legislative charter authority. **MFBM** provides fiscal oversight. BAA 13 does not assign a specific ministry for GOCC creation management — this is a gap. BAA 85, Chapter III allocates budget to MFBM for fiscal management functions.[^14]

**Q. What standalone legislation has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **PB 376** (Bangsamoro Enterprise Support Corporation — BESC) is the first and only GOCC created by Parliament as of this writing. No comprehensive GOCC governance framework has been enacted.

**Q. What are MFBM's relevant functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13 assigns MFBM responsibility for fiscal management, including budget formulation, revenue administration, and financial oversight. While MFBM does not have an express GOCC oversight function under BAA 13, its fiscal management role positions it as the natural counterpart to GCG for Bangsamoro GOCCs — managing dividend remittance, capital structure review, and fiscal performance monitoring.

**Q. What can the Bangsamoro Government NOT do without a standalone GOCC Act?**

A. Without a dedicated Bangsamoro GOCC Act, the government cannot: (1) establish a **Bangsamoro GOCC governance body** equivalent to GCG that exercises region-specific oversight; (2) define **dividend policy** for Bangsamoro-created GOCCs (whether dividends flow to the Bangsamoro treasury, the national government, or both); (3) establish **GOCC performance standards** tailored to BARMM's development priorities; (4) create a **clear coordination protocol** with the National Government for the Art. XII, Sec. 30 "coordination" requirement; (5) define the **relationship between Bangsamoro GOCCs and GCG** — whether GCG exercises full oversight or whether BARMM creates a complementary oversight mechanism.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is the institutional assignment correct for this power?**

A. Partially. The **Parliament** correctly exercises the legislative creation function. However, there is no dedicated **executive-level GOCC oversight body** within BARMM. Nationally, GCG fills this role. MFBM is the logical home for a Bangsamoro GOCC oversight function, but BAA 13 does not expressly assign it. A Bangsamoro GOCC Act should clarify the institutional architecture.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. (1) **Bangsamoro Parliament** — exercises legislative charter authority; (2) **MFBM** — fiscal oversight (BAA 13, BAA 85 Ch. III); (3) **IFPB** — intergovernmental body determining BARMM participation in existing GOCCs (Art. XII, Sec. 37-41); (4) **BESC** — the first Bangsamoro GOCC (PB 376). No dedicated GOCC oversight office exists within the Bangsamoro executive branch.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. The national government retains significant authority: (1) **GCG** — reviews GOCC charters (including those enacted by Parliament), evaluates governance compliance, sets compensation standards, and monitors performance under RA 10149; (2) **SEC** — registers GOCCs incorporated under the Corporation Code; (3) **COA** — audits all GOCCs; (4) **National GOCCs operate within BARMM** — NPC/NGCP (power), NFA (food), PhilHealth, SSS, GSIS, and other national GOCCs deliver services within the region.

**Q. What is the status of IFPB determinations under Art. XII, Sec. 33?**

A. The IFPB was mandated to determine BARMM participation in **AAIIBP** and **SPDA** ***"within six (6) months from the establishment of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority."*** The BTA was established in March 2019 — the 6-month deadline has long passed. **Res. No. 153** called on OCM to nominate BARMM representatives to GOCC boards, and **Res. No. 168** requested a report on SPDA assets. [UNVERIFIED: The exact status of IFPB determinations for AAIIBP and SPDA could not be confirmed from available local sources.]

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has BARMM accomplished so far?**

A. (1) Enacted **PB 376** (BESC) — the first Bangsamoro GOCC, demonstrating the legislative charter mechanism; (2) Adopted **Res. 153** — calling for BARMM board representation in national GOCCs; (3) Adopted **Res. 168** — requesting SPDA asset accounting; (4) Established the institutional framework (Parliament, MFBM) for future GOCC creation. However, no comprehensive GOCC governance framework has been enacted.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. The **primary constraint** is the absence of a **Bangsamoro GOCC Act** establishing the governance framework, coordination protocols with the National Government and GCG, dividend policy, and oversight mechanisms. The secondary constraint is the **incomplete IFPB determinations** — BARMM's participation in AAIIBP and SPDA (Art. XII, Sec. 33) and representation in national GOCC boards (Art. XII, Sec. 32) depend on IFPB action that has not been fully completed.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built that BARMM uses?**

A. The national system provides: **RA 10149's comprehensive GOCC framework** — governance standards, board requirements, compensation limits, dividend obligations, and GCG oversight; **SEC's incorporation system** for non-charter GOCCs; **COA's audit framework** for GOCC financial accountability; and **30+ years** of GOCC operational experience with established regulatory patterns.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. (1) **No Shari'ah-compliant GOCC governance track** — RA 10149 does not address Islamic governance principles for GOCCs in the financial or halal industry sectors; (2) **No post-conflict economic development GOCC model** — the national framework does not provide for GOCCs designed specifically for conflict-affected regions; (3) **IFPB implementation delays** — the intergovernmental mechanism for GOCC participation determination has not delivered timely results; (4) **GCG oversight** is Manila-centric — its capacity to review and monitor Bangsamoro GOCCs is limited by geographic distance and institutional priorities.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. The Shari'ah dimension is **sector-specific**. GOCCs in the financial sector (particularly Islamic banking — Art. V, Sec. 2(ii)) must comply with Shari'ah principles prohibiting *riba* (interest), *gharar* (excessive uncertainty), and *maysir* (gambling). **AAIIBP** (Art. XII, Sec. 33) is already a Shari'ah-compliant GOCC. The general *amanah* (trust/stewardship) principle applies to all GOCCs — officers managing public funds bear fiduciary obligations consistent with both civil law and Islamic law. For GOCCs in non-financial sectors, the Shari'ah dimension is limited to the general principles of *maslahah* (public welfare) and *'adl* (justice).

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro GOCC legislation?**

A. A **principled approach** is recommended rather than mandatory Shari'ah compliance for all GOCCs. GOCCs operating in financial services should be required to offer **Shari'ah-compliant products** alongside conventional ones. GOCCs in other sectors should operate under general governance standards informed by *amanah* principles. Any Bangsamoro GOCC Act should include provisions for a **Shari'ah Advisory Board** for financial-sector GOCCs, consistent with Art. XIII, Sec. 34 (Shari'ah Supervisory Board for Islamic banks).

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can BARMM diverge from national GOCC law?**

A. BARMM's divergence is **moderately constrained**. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), Art. XII, Sec. 30 expressly requires ***"compliance with the provisions of Republic Act No. 10149"*** — this is the BOL's clearest national law consistency requirement for this power. BARMM **cannot**: create GOCCs that fail RA 10149 governance standards, exempt GOCCs from GCG oversight, or bypass the coordination requirement with the National Government. BARMM **can**: define additional governance requirements beyond RA 10149's minimums (e.g., Shari'ah Advisory Boards for financial-sector GOCCs), establish a Bangsamoro GOCC oversight body that works alongside GCG, define the dividend policy for Bangsamoro GOCCs, and create pioneer firms with fewer constraints than GOCCs (the BOL does not subject pioneer firms to RA 10149).

**Q. What are the implications for GOCCs operating across boundaries?**

A. GOCCs registered with SEC ***"may likewise operate outside the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region"*** (Art. XII, Sec. 30). This means SEC-registered Bangsamoro GOCCs are subject to national corporate law (RA 11232) and RA 10149 everywhere they operate. Legislative-chartered GOCCs are governed by their charter, which operates within BARMM territory. Cross-boundary GOCCs face dual oversight — GCG nationally and any Bangsamoro GOCC oversight body within BARMM.

### G. Legislative Gaps

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions?**

A. Yes. Three overlaps: (1) **GCG vs. Bangsamoro oversight** — does GCG exercise full RA 10149 authority over Parliament-created GOCCs, or does BARMM create a complementary oversight body? (2) **Dividend policy** — do Bangsamoro GOCC dividends flow to the Bangsamoro treasury (Art. XII, Sec. 6(f)) or to the National Government (RA 7656), or both? (3) **"Coordination" meaning** — Art. XII, Sec. 30's ***"upon coordination with the National Government"*** is undefined — is this notification, consultation, or approval?

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB 376** (BESC) has been enacted — addressing the first instance of GOCC creation. No comprehensive GOCC governance bill has been identified in the pending bill index. The primary gap — a **Bangsamoro GOCC Act** — remains unaddressed.

### H. Development Connection

**Q. Which BDP goals and Matatag pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 7 (Economic Development) depends on GOCCs and pioneer firms to drive state-led economic development in sectors where private enterprise is insufficient or nonexistent — economic zones, halal industry infrastructure, Islamic banking expansion, enterprise support. Chapter 13 (Plan Financing and Fiscal Strategy) identifies GOCC dividends as a revenue source under Art. XII, Sec. 6(f). The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028), **Pillar 3 (Economic Resilience)**, relies on enterprise support (BESC) and potentially future GOCCs in strategic sectors.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if gaps remain?**

A. Without a **Bangsamoro GOCC Act**: the governance framework for future GOCCs relies entirely on RA 10149 (which is not tailored to BARMM); dividend policy remains unclear — potentially losing revenue for the Bangsamoro treasury; the **AAIIBP participation** (critical for Islamic banking) and **SPDA asset disposition** remain unresolved; and BARMM cannot create a pipeline of strategic GOCCs for economic development (economic zones, halal certification, development banking) without a clear governance and oversight framework.

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. What is the difference between a GOCC and a pioneer firm under the BOL?**

A. Art. XII, Sec. 30 treats them differently. **GOCCs** must be created ***"in compliance with the provisions of Republic Act No. 10149"*** and ***"upon coordination with the National Government"*** — either by legislative charter or SEC incorporation. **Pioneer firms** are authorized by a separate paragraph: ***"The Parliament may create pioneering firms and other business entities to boost economic development."*** The BOL does not subject pioneer firms to RA 10149 compliance or National Government coordination — they are a more flexible instrument for economic development. A pioneer firm could be a corporation, partnership, joint venture, or other business entity. The distinction matters because pioneer firms have **fewer regulatory constraints** than GOCCs.

**Q. What is the IFPB's role and why does it matter?**

A. The **Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board** (Art. VI, Sec. 4; Art. XII, Sec. 37-41) is the gatekeeper for three GOCC-related decisions: (1) **feasibility determination** for BARMM to assume authority over existing GOCCs operating within BARMM (Art. XII, Sec. 31); (2) **extent of participation** and revenue share from national GOCCs (Art. XII, Sec. 32); (3) **AAIIBP and SPDA participation** (Art. XII, Sec. 33). Without IFPB action, BARMM cannot exercise authority over existing GOCCs, secure board representation, or participate in AAIIBP/SPDA. The 6-month deadline for the AAIIBP/SPDA determination has long passed.[^7]

**Q. Can the Bangsamoro Parliament create GOCCs for sectors outside BARMM?**

A. Only if the GOCC is registered with SEC under the Corporation Code. Art. XII, Sec. 30 provides: ***"those duly registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission may likewise operate outside the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** A legislative-chartered GOCC (created by Parliament act without SEC registration) operates within BARMM territory. If the Parliament wants a GOCC to operate nationally or internationally, it must use the SEC incorporation pathway.[^2]
