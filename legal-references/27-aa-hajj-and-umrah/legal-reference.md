## Legal Briefer

### What This Power Covers

The BOL grants the Bangsamoro Government authority over Hajj and Umrah as an enumerated power under Article V, Section 2(aa).[^1] This power is elaborated in Article VII, Section 42, which authorizes the Parliament to create "a Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority which shall exercise jurisdiction over *Hajj* and *Umrah* matters affecting pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."[^2] This is the BOL's sole elaboration of the Hajj and Umrah power beyond the Art. V enumeration, making it one of the more concisely defined powers.

The scope encompasses the full cycle of pilgrimage management: pre-departure preparation and documentation, travel coordination, in-Kingdom support services, post-return processing, and the regulation of pilgrimage service providers. Hajj (the annual pilgrimage to Mecca, one of the Five Pillars of Islam) and Umrah (the lesser pilgrimage that can be performed at any time) are religious obligations with significant logistical, financial, and regulatory dimensions. The Bangsamoro Government's authority covers the administrative and regulatory aspects of pilgrimage for residents of BARMM, not the religious observance itself.

The BOL does not impose a national law consistency requirement on this power. However, because Hajj and Umrah require international travel, the power necessarily involves coordination with national agencies — the Department of Foreign Affairs (for passport and consular services), the Bureau of Immigration (for travel documentation), the Bureau of Quarantine (for health requirements), and the NCMF (which historically managed Filipino pilgrimage affairs at the national level). The extent of BPA's jurisdiction vis-a-vis the NCMF's national mandate has practical implications for BARMM pilgrims.

### How the Bangsamoro Government Exercises This Power Today

The Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority (BPA) is the lead institution, funded through BAA 85, Chapter XXV.[^3] The BPA was established through BAA 13 (Bangsamoro Administrative Code) as one of the offices the Parliament is authorized to create under Article VII, Section 42. No standalone BAA creating the BPA with comprehensive powers, governance structure, and regulatory framework has been enacted — the BPA operates under its BAA 13 general mandate.

The BPA coordinates pilgrimage services for BARMM residents, including Hajj registration, pre-departure orientation, travel logistics, and in-Kingdom support. However, the absence of a standalone BPA Act means the authority operates without a legislated framework for regulating pilgrimage service providers (travel agencies, tour operators), establishing fee schedules and financial transparency requirements, protecting pilgrims from fraud and substandard services, and coordinating with the NCMF and Saudi Arabian authorities.

Parliamentary resolutions signal awareness of pilgrimage issues. Resolution 375 urged the NCMF and Bureau of Quarantine to minimize the burden on pilgrims from island provinces regarding Hajj vaccination requirements — highlighting the practical coordination challenges that BARMM pilgrims face when navigating both national and regional requirements.[^4] Resolution 376 requested the President to investigate irregularities during the 2023 Hajj — indicating incidents of mismanagement that stronger regulation could prevent.[^5] Bill 282, which proposes 30 calendar days of paid leave for government employees undertaking pilgrimage, addresses the employment dimension but has not been enacted.

### How National Law Interacts with This Power

The BOL does not require this power to be exercised in accordance with national law, and no Article VI intergovernmental body exists for Hajj and Umrah. However, the practical exercise of this power intersects with several national frameworks. The NCMF, created by Executive Order No. 27 (s. 1987) and strengthened by various executive orders, has historically managed Filipino Hajj operations at the national level, including Saudi Arabia Hajj quota allocation, accreditation of Hajj operators, and coordination with the Royal Saudi Embassy.

The BPA's jurisdiction is defined by the BOL as covering "pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region," creating a geographic delineation that coexists with the NCMF's national jurisdiction. In practice, this means BARMM pilgrims may be served by both the BPA (for regional pilgrimage services) and the NCMF (for national-level Hajj quota allocation and Saudi coordination). The boundary between these jurisdictions has not been formally defined through legislation or an intergovernmental agreement, creating potential for overlap or gaps in service.

Passport, immigration, and quarantine requirements for international travel remain under national government jurisdiction. The Bureau of Quarantine's health requirements for Hajj pilgrims (vaccination, health certificates) apply nationally and cannot be modified by the BPA unilaterally, as Resolution 375's appeal to the NCMF and BOQ illustrates.

### What Is Missing — Gaps and Legislative Opportunities

The most critical gap is the absence of a standalone Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority Act. While the BPA exists and is funded, it operates under BAA 13's general authority without a comprehensive legislative framework. A standalone BPA Act would codify: the BPA's powers and functions, including regulatory authority over pilgrimage service providers; registration, licensing, and accreditation requirements for Hajj and Umrah tour operators in BARMM; a pilgrim protection framework addressing fraud prevention, service standards, complaint mechanisms, and emergency repatriation; fee schedules and financial transparency requirements; a Hajj fund or pilgrimage savings program to make Hajj financially accessible to lower-income Muslims; and the coordination mechanism between the BPA and the NCMF for Saudi Arabia Hajj quota allocation.

The pilgrimage leave bill (Bill 282) addresses an important but narrow dimension — workplace accommodation for pilgrimage. A broader legislative package would also address financial assistance (a Hajj savings or subsidized loan program), health preparation (coordination with MOH for pre-departure medical screening), and post-Hajj reintegration support.

Resolution 376's call to investigate 2023 Hajj irregularities underscores the need for stronger regulatory oversight. Without a legislated accreditation framework for Hajj service providers, pilgrims remain vulnerable to substandard services, overcharging, and fraud by unlicensed operators.

### BDP 2023-2028 Connection

Hajj and Umrah are addressed within the BDP 2023-2028 Chapter 6 (Cultural Diversity and Heritage), which recognizes the centrality of Islamic religious practice to Bangsamoro identity and social life.[^6] The BDP's goals for cultural preservation and religious freedom depend on the Bangsamoro Government's ability to manage pilgrimage effectively. Without a standalone BPA Act, the BDP's aspiration for high-quality pilgrimage services and pilgrim protection lacks the legal infrastructure for systematic implementation.

| Gap Type | Description | Recommended Action |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|
| **Type A**: No standalone BPA Act | BPA exercises its mandate through BAA 13 only; no dedicated legislative framework for pilgrimage service regulation, operator accreditation, pilgrim protection, or fee governance | Enact Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority Act codifying BPA powers, regulatory framework for operators, pilgrim protection standards, and Hajj fund mechanism |

| **Type C**: BPA-NCMF jurisdiction overlap | BPA jurisdiction over BARMM pilgrims and NCMF national Hajj management coexist without a formal agreement defining roles, quota allocation responsibility, and coordination protocols | Formalize BPA-NCMF coordination through intergovernmental agreement defining respective roles, Hajj quota allocation for BARMM, and service delivery responsibilities |
