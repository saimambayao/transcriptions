# (aa) Hajj and Umrah

> **BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(aa)** — ***"Hajj and Umrah;"***

## Power Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| **BOL Basis** | Art. V, Sec. 2(aa) — enumerated power |
| **National Law Consistency Required** | Intergovernmental coordination required — BAA 13, Sec. 43 mandates BPA to ***"act in close coordination with the National Commission on Muslim Filipinos on Hajj and Umrah matters."*** No express "consistent with" qualifier in the BOL itself. |
| **Lead Ministry/Office** | **Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority (BPA)** — attached to the Office of the Chief Minister (BAA 13, Sec. 43) |
| **Supporting Agencies** | Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' (Islamic guidance and fatwa); Ministry of Health (Hajj vaccination coordination); Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office (travel document legal support) |
| **BDP Chapter(s)** | Chapter 5 (Governance — moral governance and institutional capacity); Chapter 9 (Social Development — cultural and religious services) |
| **Named Legislative Mandate** | None. The BOL does not name a specific Hajj and Umrah code by title. Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes creation of BPA; BAA 13, Sec. 43-46 implements it. No unfulfilled named legislative mandate exists for this power. |

## Legal Briefer

A granular Q&A analysis. Answers stay as close as possible to the language of the law. All verbatim statutory text in ***bold italic***. Key terms in **bold**. Themes follow the legal hierarchy and analytical flow: BOL grant → constitutional floor → national law → implementation → Shari'ah dimension → divergence → gaps → development.

### A. BOL Grant and Scope

**Q. What power does the BOL grant the Bangsamoro Government over Hajj and Umrah?**

A. Article V, Section 2(aa) of Republic Act No. 11054 enumerates ***"Hajj and Umrah"*** as one of the 55 powers over which the Bangsamoro Government ***"shall exercise its authority."*** The chapeau of Art. V, Sec. 2 states: ***"Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines."*** This power covers the **administration**, **supervision**, and **facilitation** of the annual Muslim pilgrimage (Hajj) and minor pilgrimage (Umrah) for pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^1]

**Q. What specific BOL provisions elaborate on this power beyond Art. V, Sec. 2?**

A. One provision directly elaborates on this power:

   Art. VII, Sec. 42 states: ***"The Parliament may create other offices such as for the youth, women, settler communities, disaster risk reduction and management, and planning and development, including a Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority which shall exercise jurisdiction over Hajj and Umrah matters affecting pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."***[^2]

   This provision is notable for three reasons: (1) **BPA is named** — it is one of the few offices the BOL identifies by name in Art. VII, Sec. 42, signaling the importance the BOL assigns to pilgrimage administration; (2) **jurisdiction is geographically defined** — limited to ***"pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro"***, which establishes the boundary with the national agency (NCMF); and (3) the language is **permissive** (***"may create"***), not mandatory — Parliament was authorized but not required to create BPA. Parliament exercised this authority through BAA 13.

**Q. Does the BOL mandate the Parliament to enact specific legislation for this power?**

A. No. Unlike powers such as cooperatives (Art. XIII, Sec. 24 — ***"Bangsamoro cooperative code"***) or education (Art. IX, Sec. 14-17), the BOL does not name a specific Hajj and Umrah statute by title. Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorizes BPA's creation — which has been fulfilled through BAA 13 — but does not mandate a standalone pilgrimage code. This does not mean legislation is unnecessary; it means Parliament has **discretion** over whether and when to enact comprehensive Hajj and Umrah legislation beyond the BAA 13 general mandate.[^2]

**Q. Does the BOL create any specific body, office, or institution for this power?**

A. The BOL **authorizes** (but does not require) the creation of the ***"Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority."*** Art. VII, Sec. 42 expressly names BPA and defines its jurisdiction. BPA was established through **BAA 13**, Book VI, Title I, Chapter 3, Section E (Sec. 43-46), and receives budget allocation under **BAA 85**, Chapter XXV.[^2][^3][^4]

**Q. What conditions, limitations, or qualifications does the BOL place on this power?**

A. The BOL places two types of limitations:

   (1) **Geographic jurisdiction**: Art. VII, Sec. 42 limits BPA's jurisdiction to ***"pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."*** Pilgrims from outside BARMM remain under NCMF's jurisdiction. This is a geographic boundary, not a substantive one — BPA has full authority over pilgrimage matters for Bangsamoro-origin pilgrims.

   (2) **Intergovernmental coordination**: While the BOL itself does not impose a coordination requirement, BAA 13 Sec. 43 operationalizes this power with a coordination mandate: BPA ***"shall act in close coordination with the National Commission on Muslim Filipinos on Hajj and Umrah matters to ensure that the pilgrims from the Bangsamoro are provided with the same level of services as those from outside."***[^3] This coordination obligation is self-imposed through BAA 13, not BOL-mandated — but it reflects the practical reality that Hajj administration requires engagement with both national authorities and the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia.

### B. Constitutional Considerations

**Q. What constitutional provisions apply to Hajj and Umrah?**

A. Four categories of constitutional provisions apply:

   (1) **Autonomous Region Framework (Art. X, Sec. 15)**: Autonomous regions operate ***"within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."*** The BOL chapeau (Art. V, Sec. 2) reiterates this universal constraint.[^5]

   (2) **Free Exercise of Religion (Art. III, Sec. 5)**: ***"No law shall be made respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof. The free exercise and enjoyment of religious profession and worship, without discrimination or preference, shall forever be allowed."*** Hajj is a core religious obligation — government facilitation of Hajj serves the **free exercise clause** by removing practical barriers to the fulfillment of a fundamental Islamic duty. At the same time, the **non-establishment clause** means government Hajj programs must be structured as facilitation of citizen rights, not as state endorsement of a particular religion.[^6]

   (3) **Right to Travel (Art. III, Sec. 6)**: ***"Neither shall the right to travel be impaired except in the interest of national security, public safety, or public health, as may be provided by law."*** Pilgrimage is travel for religious purposes — restrictions on pilgrim travel (such as excessive processing requirements or mandatory purchases from BPA) must satisfy this constitutional standard. BAA 13 Sec. 45's protection of free choice of travel agencies and airlines reflects this right.[^7]

   (4) **Tax Exemption for Religious Institutions (Art. VI, Sec. 28(3))**: ***"Charitable institutions, churches and parsonages or convents appurtenant thereto, mosques, non-profit cemeteries, and all lands, buildings, and improvements, actually, directly, and exclusively used for religious, charitable, or educational purposes shall be exempt from taxation."*** Pilgrimage-related *awqaf* properties held in trust by BPA for religious purposes may fall within this exemption.[^8]

**Q. What rights and privileges does the Constitution guarantee in this area?**

A. The Constitution guarantees:

   (1) **Free exercise of religion** — Hajj is the fifth pillar of Islam. Any regulation that unduly burdens or obstructs the performance of Hajj for financially and physically capable Muslims infringes upon the free exercise clause. Government regulation must **facilitate**, not frustrate, this right.

   (2) **Right to travel** — pilgrims have the constitutional right to travel abroad for Hajj. BAA 13 Sec. 45's guarantee that ***"pilgrims shall be accorded free choice of travel agencies and airlines or other means of transportation"*** implements this constitutional protection.

   (3) **Equal protection and due process** (Art. III, Sec. 1) — Bangsamoro pilgrims must receive services comparable to those available to other Muslim Filipino pilgrims. BAA 13 Sec. 43's coordination mandate specifically addresses this: ensuring ***"the same level of services as those from outside."***

**Q. What constitutional mandates for Hajj legislation or institutions exist?**

A. No express constitutional mandate exists for Hajj-specific legislation or institutions. Unlike cooperatives (Art. XII, Sec. 15 — congressional mandate for a cooperative agency) or education (Art. XIV, Sec. 1 — state protection of education), the Constitution does not contain a Hajj-specific provision. However, the **free exercise clause** (Art. III, Sec. 5) creates an **implicit obligation** on the State to remove barriers to religious observance — which, for Muslim Filipinos, includes the Hajj. RA 9997's creation of NCMF with express Hajj functions (Sec. 8(q)-(r)) and the BOL's enumeration of Hajj as a Bangsamoro power reflect the legislative implementation of this constitutional value.[^6]

### C. National Law Considerations

**Q. What does the current national legal framework provide for Hajj administration?**

A. The national framework is centered on **RA 9997** (National Commission on Muslim Filipinos Act of 2009). Section 8(q) mandates NCMF to ***"administer all programs, projects and activities, formulate the necessary rules and regulations, and coordinate with pertinent offices to ensure the success of the annual Hajj (pilgrimage) to Mecca, Kingdom of Saudi Arabia."*** Section 8(r) mandates NCMF to ***"promote the development of a Hajj Assistance Fund."*** Section 10(e) creates the **Bureau of Pilgrimage and Endowment** within NCMF — responsible for administering the annual pilgrimage, processing travel documents, collecting fees, accrediting sheikhs, and administering *awqaf* properties. Section 16 authorizes the President to appoint the NCMF Secretary as ***"Amirul Hajj"*** — the head of the Muslim Filipino pilgrimage delegation to Saudi Arabia. NCMF also has the power to ***"enter into agreements"*** for pilgrimage purposes and to establish ***"regional and provincial Hajj coordinating and monitoring units"*** (Sec. 10).[^9][^10]

**Q. What national laws currently apply in BARMM?**

A. Two national laws apply:

   (1) **RA 9997** — NCMF retains national-level Hajj administration. The coordination between BPA and NCMF is mandated by BAA 13 Sec. 43. NCMF's authority applies **directly** to pilgrims from outside BARMM and **in parallel** with BPA for coordination of the overall Hajj program.

   (2) **PD 1083** — Code of Muslim Personal Laws. Does not directly regulate Hajj but provides the **legal recognition of Islamic religious obligations** within the Philippine legal system. Applies directly.

**Q. What national institutions exercise authority in BARMM for this area?**

A. The **National Commission on Muslim Filipinos (NCMF)** exercises national-level Hajj authority through its **Bureau of Pilgrimage and Endowment**. NCMF coordinates the annual Hajj program, processes the Saudi Arabia Hajj quota allocation, and serves as the Philippine government's primary interlocutor with the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia's Ministry of Hajj and Umrah. The **Bureau of Quarantine (BOQ)** exercises health screening authority over outbound pilgrims, including vaccination requirements — a function that prompted Resolution No. 375 due to disproportionate burden on island-province pilgrims. NCMF retains authority even within BARMM because: (a) the Saudi Arabian government allocates Hajj quotas to **national governments**, not subnational regions; (b) the **Amirul Hajj** designation is a Presidential appointment under RA 9997, Sec. 16; and (c) international agreements on pilgrimage are conducted at the national level.

### D. Implementation

#### D.1 How the Bangsamoro Government Implements This Power

**Q. Which ministry or office is primarily responsible for exercising this power?**

A. The **Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority (BPA)** is the lead agency. See the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A) for BPA's institutional basis in the BOL. BAA 13, Book VI, Title I, Chapter 3, Section E (Sec. 43-46) defines BPA's institutional status, mandate, functions, and organizational structure. BAA 85, Chapter XXV allocates its operational budget of P278,837,239.00.[^3][^4]

**Q. What standalone legislation (BAAs) has the Parliament enacted for this power?**

A. **No standalone BAA** has been enacted for Hajj and Umrah as of BAA 89. This power is exercised entirely through BPA's **BAA 13 general mandate** and **BAA 85 budget allocation**.

**Q. What are BPA's mandate and functions under BAA 13?**

A. BAA 13, Book VI, Title I, Chapter 3, Section E (Sec. 43-46) defines BPA comprehensively:

   **Institutional status and jurisdiction** (Sec. 43): ***"The Bangsamoro Government shall have primary jurisdiction over Hajj and Umrah matters affecting pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro."*** BPA ***"shall act in close coordination with the National Commission on Muslim Filipinos on Hajj and Umrah matters to ensure that the pilgrims from the Bangsamoro are provided with the same level of services as those from outside."***[^3]

   **Mandate** (Sec. 44): BPA is ***"primarily responsible for the administration of the annual Muslim pilgrimage to Mecca, Kingdom of Saudi Arabia of pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro."*** It ***"shall formulate and implement programs, projects and activities for the efficient and effective administration and supervision of the conduct of pilgrimage activities of Bangsamoro pilgrims."***[^11]

   **Functions** (Sec. 45) — BPA exercises seven categories of functions:

   (a) **Travel document processing**: ***"formulate the necessary guidelines to ensure the timely processing of the Bangsamoro pilgrim's travel documents and the equitable and reasonable collection of fees"***

   (b) **Sheikh accreditation**: ***"accredit qualified and deserving sheikhs"***

   (c) **Hajj timetable**: ***"formulate, subject to the approval of the Chief Minister, the time table of annual Hajj activities which must be posted in all areas accessible to Bangsamoro Muslims"***

   (d) **Fee limitation**: Collections ***"shall be limited to mutawiff and passporting and/or processing fees only"*** — deposited in a **special trust fund** subject to existing auditing laws

   (e) **Free choice protection**: ***"pilgrims shall be accorded free choice of travel agencies and airlines or other means of transportation"*** and ***"shall be free to choose on where to purchase or acquire other logistics, materials and supplies for the pilgrimage"*** — with the proviso that ***"in no instance shall the pilgrims be forced to purchase such logistics, materials and supplies from the Authority or its authorized agents or representatives"***

   (f) **Saudi engagement**: ***"The Authority shall directly engage and enter into agreements with the appropriate authorities in the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, upon consultation with, and prior approval by, the Chief Minister"***

   (g) **Round-trip ticket requirement**: All pilgrims must be ***"legitimate holders of round-trip tickets to and from the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia"***[^12]

   **Organization and composition** (Sec. 46): Headed by an **Executive Director** (Director II rank) assisted by a **Deputy Executive Director** (Director I rank), both appointed by the Chief Minister (coterminous). Three divisions:

   (a) **Pilgrimage Operations Division** — administers the actual conduct of Hajj and Umrah operations
   (b) **Endowment Administration Division** — manages *awqaf* and endowment properties
   (c) **Administration and Finance Division** — provides administrative and financial support
   (d) ***"Such other divisions as may be created by the office with the concurrence of the Chief Minister"***[^13]

**Q. What can BPA NOT do without a standalone Hajj and Umrah Act?**

A. While BAA 13 gives BPA a functional mandate, it does not provide: a **comprehensive regulatory framework** with detailed standards for Hajj operator accreditation, pilgrim protection, and service quality requirements; **penalty provisions** for fraud, overcharging, or exploitation of pilgrims; a **clear jurisdictional protocol** with NCMF defining which functions are BPA's, which are NCMF's, and how the Hajj quota allocation works; a **Hajj Assistance Fund** with statutory basis, eligibility criteria, and repayment terms (the P200M Hajj Financing and Support Program exists in the GAA but lacks an enabling code); an ***awqaf*** **regulatory framework** for endowment administration (BPA has an Endowment Administration Division but no statutory framework for managing endowment assets); or a **pilgrimage leave entitlement** for employees (pending as PB No. 282). BAA 13 creates the institution but not the **regulatory code** it needs to fully exercise its authority.

**Q. Have any parliamentary resolutions signaled intent to legislate in this area?**

A. Two resolutions signal Parliamentary concern: **Resolution No. 375** — urging NCMF and BOQ to minimize vaccination burden on island-province pilgrims — and **Resolution No. 376** — requesting the President to investigate 2023 Hajj irregularities. These resolutions demonstrate active **oversight** but do not constitute a formal call for comprehensive legislation. No resolution expressly calls for a standalone Hajj and Umrah Code.

#### D.1.A Institutional Validation

**Q. Is BPA the correct lead institution for this power?**

A. Yes. BPA's assignment is unambiguous — the **BOL names BPA** (Art. VII, Sec. 42), BAA 13 defines its mandate and functions (Sec. 43-46), and BAA 85 provides its budget (Chapter XXV). The power is geographically bounded (***"pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro"***) and functionally specific (Hajj and Umrah administration). No other BARMM institution claims jurisdiction over this power.

**Q. What BARMM institutions currently exist for this power?**

A. Three institutions are relevant:

   - **Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority (BPA)**: lead agency (BAA 13 Sec. 43-46; BAA 85 Ch. XXV). Operational with three divisions, Executive Director, and FY 2026 budget of P278.8M.
   - **Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta'**: consultative body for Islamic jurisprudence (BAA 13 Sec. 52-54). Relevant for **fatwa** guidance on Hajj-related questions — e.g., permissibility of specific travel arrangements, Hajj financing structures, conditions of *istita'ah*.
   - **Ministry of Health (MOH)**: supports Hajj vaccination and health screening coordination. BAA 85 allocates P38.4M for vaccines ***"in connection with the conduct of Hajj in coordination with the Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority."***

**Q. What institutions does the BOL mandate that have NOT been created yet?**

A. None for this power. Art. VII, Sec. 42 authorized BPA — and BPA has been created through BAA 13. No other BOL-mandated institution specifically for Hajj and Umrah remains unestablished.

**Q. Does this power require creating NEW institutions that the BOL does not specifically name?**

A. Potentially: (1) a **Hajj Assistance Fund Board** or similar governance body to administer the P200M Hajj Financing and Support Program with transparent eligibility criteria, selection processes, and repayment terms; (2) a **Shari'ah compliance mechanism** (potentially through the Darul-Ifta') for reviewing Hajj financial products and ensuring conformity with Islamic principles. Neither requires a separate institution — both could be established within BPA through a standalone Hajj and Umrah Act.

#### D.2 How the National Government Implements This Power

**Q. How does the national government currently exercise authority in this area within BARMM?**

A. **NCMF** exercises national-level Hajj authority through its Bureau of Pilgrimage and Endowment. NCMF's functions in BARMM include: (a) **Hajj quota coordination** — the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia allocates a national Hajj quota to the Philippines; NCMF manages this quota, including allocation to BARMM; (b) **Amirul Hajj designation** — the NCMF Secretary, appointed by the President as Amirul Hajj (RA 9997, Sec. 16), heads the national pilgrimage delegation; (c) **Hajj Assistance Fund** — NCMF administers the national fund; and (d) **regulatory coordination** — NCMF's rules and regulations on Hajj apply alongside BPA's. The **Bureau of Quarantine (BOQ)** exercises health screening authority over all outbound pilgrims, including vaccination requirements.

**Q. What national government programs and services operate in BARMM for Hajj?**

A. (1) **NCMF Hajj program** — annual pilgrim registration, travel document processing, and Saudi coordination; (2) **Hajj Assistance Fund** — financial assistance for deserving Muslim Filipino pilgrims (RA 9997, Sec. 8(r)); (3) **BOQ vaccination and health clearance** — required pre-departure health screening for all international travelers including pilgrims; (4) **DFA passport services** — passport processing for pilgrims (BPA facilitates but DFA issues).

**Q. What is the status of devolution for this power?**

A. Devolution is **partially complete**. BPA exercises primary jurisdiction over Bangsamoro pilgrims per BAA 13 Sec. 43. However, three critical functions remain with national agencies: (a) **Hajj quota allocation** — managed by NCMF as the national government's interlocutor with Saudi Arabia; (b) **Amirul Hajj designation** — Presidential appointment under RA 9997, Sec. 16; (c) **international agreements** — the Philippine government's pilgrimage agreements with Saudi Arabia are national-level. BAA 13 Sec. 45 authorizes BPA to ***"directly engage and enter into agreements with the appropriate authorities in the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia"*** — but this requires ***"prior approval by, the Chief Minister"*** and operates within the national framework.

**Q. Are there areas where both national and Bangsamoro agencies operate in parallel?**

A. Yes. The primary concurrent area is **Hajj administration itself** — NCMF and BPA both administer Hajj for the same Muslim population. The geographic boundary (BPA for Bangsamoro pilgrims, NCMF for others) is operationally blurred because: (a) the Saudi Hajj quota is allocated **nationally** and BPA must coordinate with NCMF on BARMM's share; (b) Bangsamoro Muslims residing **outside BARMM** may fall under NCMF, not BPA; (c) health screening (BOQ) and passport processing (DFA) are national functions regardless of origin. BAA 13 Sec. 43's coordination mandate acknowledges this concurrent operation.

#### D.3 BARMM Implementation

**Q. What has the BARMM accomplished so far in exercising this power?**

A. BPA has been **operationally established** under BAA 13 with an Executive Director, three divisions, and significant budget allocation. Key accomplishments include: (a) **Hajj facilitation** — BPA assisted approximately 3,000 pilgrims during the 2023 Hajj season (per BAA 32 performance targets); (b) **Saudi engagement** — BPA personnel deployed to the Royal Saudi Embassy and entered into contracts with foreign suppliers; (c) **Hajj Awareness Program** — information drives through radio airing and distribution of 3,000 handbooks; (d) **Sheikh training** — Training of Trainers for 50 sheikhs; (e) **Decommissioned combatant subsidies** — 27 decommissioned combatants subsidized for Hajj performance (BAA 32); (f) **Hajj portal registration** — 4 staff oriented on the digital registration system; (g) **Hajj kit distribution** — 3,000 Hajj kits distributed to pilgrims; (h) **Substantial budget** — FY 2026 allocation of P278.8M including P200M for the Hajj Financing and Support Program.

**Q. Where is the BARMM stronger than the national government in this area?**

A. BARMM has three unique advantages:

   (1) **Geographic and cultural proximity** — BPA serves pilgrims who share the Bangsamoro Muslim cultural context. Hajj preparation, guidance, and sheikh accreditation can be conducted in local languages (Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, Yakan) and with culturally appropriate approaches. NCMF's national programs operate in Filipino and English.

   (2) **Institutional focus** — BPA is a dedicated Hajj and Umrah authority, while at NCMF the Bureau of Pilgrimage and Endowment is one of several bureaus serving a broader mandate. BPA can concentrate its entire institutional capacity on pilgrimage administration.

   (3) **Conflict-sensitive design** — BAA 32's allocation for decommissioned combatant Hajj subsidies demonstrates BPA's capacity to integrate **post-conflict reconciliation** into pilgrimage programs. This has no national counterpart.

**Q. What is holding BARMM back from full implementation?**

A. Three binding constraints:

   (1) **No standalone Hajj and Umrah Act** — BPA operates on BAA 13's general mandate, which lacks detailed regulatory provisions for operator accreditation, pilgrim protection, penalty enforcement, and *awqaf* management.

   (2) **Dependence on NCMF for Hajj quota allocation** — the Saudi Hajj quota is allocated nationally through NCMF; BPA has no independent mechanism to negotiate its share or engage Saudi authorities without the national framework.

   (3) **Amirul Hajj function remains national** — the designation is a Presidential appointment under RA 9997, Sec. 16, limiting BPA's ability to lead the Bangsamoro pilgrimage delegation independently.

#### D.4 National Government Implementation

**Q. What has the national government built for this area that BARMM does not yet have?**

A. The national government, through NCMF, has built:

   - **Legislative depth**: RA 9997 provides a comprehensive statutory framework with specific Hajj functions (Sec. 8(q)-(r)), an institutional structure (Bureau of Pilgrimage and Endowment), and an *awqaf* administration framework (Sec. 8(s)-(t)).
   - **International relationships**: NCMF maintains the government-to-government relationship with the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia's Ministry of Hajj and Umrah, including quota negotiations and bilateral agreements.
   - **Amirul Hajj protocol**: The Presidential appointment of the Amirul Hajj (RA 9997, Sec. 16) gives the national delegation official government representation at the highest level.
   - **Hajj Assistance Fund**: A statutory fund under RA 9997, Sec. 8(r) — though BPA has a larger program (P200M under BAA 85), it lacks a statutory enabling framework comparable to the national fund.

**Q. Where does the national system fall short for BARMM?**

A. The national framework fails BARMM's needs in three areas:

   (1) **Centralized operations** — NCMF's Hajj administration is centralized in Manila. Pilgrims from Basilan, Tawi-Tawi, and Maguindanao face significant logistical challenges in accessing NCMF services. Resolution No. 375's call to minimize vaccination burdens on island-province pilgrims reflects this geographic disadvantage.

   (2) **One-size-fits-all approach** — NCMF serves all Muslim Filipinos nationwide. The specific needs of Bangsamoro pilgrims — including post-conflict community support, local language services, and regional sheikh accreditation — are not prioritized in the national program.

   (3) **Limited accountability to Bangsamoro constituents** — NCMF reports to the Office of the President, not to the Bangsamoro Parliament. Resolution No. 376's call for investigation of 2023 Hajj irregularities reflects the accountability gap — Parliament can pass resolutions but cannot exercise direct oversight over NCMF operations.

**Q. What should BARMM replicate, adapt, or replace from the national system?**

A. For each major element:

   - **Replicate**: **Hajj Assistance Fund statutory framework** — adopt a Bangsamoro-specific fund with statutory eligibility criteria and governance, modeled on RA 9997 Sec. 8(r). Also replicate **audit and accountability standards** for pilgrim fee collections and the special trust fund.
   - **Adapt**: **Operator accreditation and pilgrim protection** — take NCMF's regulatory approach and adapt for BARMM's context with local-language requirements, regional sheikh accreditation, and conflict-sensitive provisions. Adapt the ***awqaf*** **administration framework** (RA 9997, Sec. 8(s)-(t)) for regional endowment management.
   - **Replace**: **Service delivery model** — build BPA's own direct service delivery infrastructure rather than depending on NCMF's centralized Manila-based operations. BPA already has the authority to ***"directly engage and enter into agreements with the appropriate authorities in the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia"*** (BAA 13 Sec. 45) — a standalone Act would strengthen this independent capacity.

### E. Shari'ah Considerations

**Q. Does this power have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A. Yes — **Shari'ah is central** to this power. Hajj and Umrah is the only enumerated power in Art. V, Sec. 2 that is **directly and exclusively a religious obligation**. Hajj is the fifth pillar of Islam — *fard* (obligatory) once in a lifetime for every Muslim who meets the conditions of *istita'ah* (capacity). The Shafi'i school, predominant among Bangsamoro Muslims, also considers Umrah obligatory. Government administration of pilgrimage is not merely regulatory — it is the exercise of a power rooted in the most fundamental obligations of the Islamic faith. Every aspect of BPA's mandate — from fee collection to sheikh accreditation to Saudi engagement — must be evaluated against Islamic principles to ensure that government action **facilitates** rather than **obstructs** the fulfillment of a divine command.[^14]

**Q. What does the BOL say about Shari'ah in relation to this power?**

A. The BOL does not mention Shari'ah specifically in Art. V, Sec. 2(aa) or Art. VII, Sec. 42. However, the power itself is inherently Shari'ah-grounded — Hajj is a religious obligation defined by Islamic law, not by statute. Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact Shari'ah-based legislation on commercial and civil matters; to the extent Hajj financing involves commercial transactions (*e.g.*, operator contracts, financial assistance structures), this provision supports Shari'ah-compliant regulation. Art. X, Sec. 1 establishes that ***"the dispensation of justice in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall be in consonance with the Constitution, Shari'ah, traditional or tribal laws, and other relevant laws"*** — suggesting a general principle that Bangsamoro governance should harmonize with Islamic law where applicable.[^15]

**Q. What Shari'ah principles are relevant to legislation in this area?**

A. Five Islamic legal principles directly govern Hajj and Umrah legislation:

   (1) **Fifth Pillar of Islam** — Hajj as *fard* (obligation). Legislation must not create barriers to *istita'ah* (capacity). Excessive fees, burdensome documentation, or forced purchases violate the facilitative purpose.

   (2) ***Istita'ah*** (capacity/ability) — financial and physical ability to perform Hajj. Government programs that reduce financial barriers (such as the P200M Hajj Financing and Support Program) directly expand the number of Bangsamoro Muslims who achieve *istita'ah*. However, any **financing mechanism** must itself comply with Shari'ah — an interest-bearing Hajj loan would be impermissible under the prohibition of *riba*.

   (3) ***Amanah*** (trust/stewardship) — BPA officers managing pilgrim funds are *amin* (trustees). BAA 13 Sec. 45's requirement that fees be deposited in a **special trust fund** subject to auditing reflects this principle. Resolution No. 376's call for investigation of 2023 irregularities is an enforcement mechanism for *amanah*.

   (4) ***Maslahah*** (public interest/welfare) — government investment in Hajj administration serves the common good by facilitating a core religious obligation.

   (5) ***Awqaf*** (endowment) — BPA's Endowment Administration Division administers religious endowments. *Awqaf* management must follow Shari'ah principles — assets held in perpetuity, income used for stated charitable/religious purposes, no disposal of the original endowment asset.

**Q. What existing Shari'ah legislation or jurisprudence applies?**

A. **PD 1083** (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) does not specifically address Hajj or Umrah. *Awqaf* (endowment) is partially addressed in PD 1083 (Art. 110 et seq.) as it relates to Muslim personal property — but the comprehensive endowment framework for institutional *awqaf* (such as those BPA administers) is not covered. No Bangsamoro legislation specifically addresses Hajj under Shari'ah law. The **Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta'** (BAA 13 Sec. 52-54) has the authority to promulgate *fatwa* (religious edicts) that could address Hajj-related questions — such as permissibility of specific financing arrangements, conditions for Hajj delegation leadership, or rulings on disputed pilgrimage practices.

**Q. How should Shari'ah compliance be integrated into Bangsamoro Hajj and Umrah legislation?**

A. Shari'ah compliance should be **mandatory** for all aspects of Hajj and Umrah administration — unlike cooperatives (where a dual-track conventional/Shari'ah approach is appropriate), Hajj is a purely Islamic religious obligation and any government administration of it must inherently conform to Islamic principles. Specific requirements: (a) the **Hajj Financing and Support Program** must use Shari'ah-compliant financial structures (*qard hasan* — benevolent loan without interest, or grant-based assistance) — not interest-bearing loans; (b) **sheikh accreditation** should include assessment by the Darul-Ifta' or recognized *ulama*; (c) ***awqaf*** **management** must follow Shari'ah principles of perpetuity and purpose restriction; (d) all **BPA regulations** should be subject to **Shari'ah review** by the Darul-Ifta' before promulgation.

### F. Divergence Analysis

**Q. To what extent can the BARMM diverge from national Hajj administration?**

A. BARMM has **substantial operational authority** to diverge because: (1) Art. V, Sec. 2(aa) enumerates Hajj and Umrah as a Bangsamoro power — this is congressional authorization; (2) Art. VII, Sec. 42 creates BPA by name with defined jurisdiction; (3) the BOL imposes no "consistent with national law" qualifier on this power. As established in the **BOL Grant and Scope** section (Section A), BPA's authority is geographically bounded (pilgrims from within the Bangsamoro) but substantively broad. As established in the **Constitutional Considerations** section (Section B), the constitutional framework (Art. III, Sec. 5 free exercise; Art. III, Sec. 6 right to travel) sets the floor.

   However, three structural factors constrain divergence:

   (a) **Saudi Arabia's national quota system** — the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia allocates Hajj visas to **national governments**, not subnational regions. BPA cannot independently negotiate with Saudi Arabia outside the national framework. BAA 13 Sec. 45 authorizes Saudi engagement ***"upon consultation with, and prior approval by, the Chief Minister"*** — but this operates within the Philippine national quota.

   (b) **Amirul Hajj remains a Presidential function** — RA 9997, Sec. 16 assigns this to the NCMF Secretary. Changing this would require amending a national law.

   (c) **International agreements** — pilgrimage bilateral agreements are conducted between national governments. BPA can supplement but not replace the national framework.

   In practice, BARMM can: establish its **own regulatory standards** for pilgrim services; develop **Shari'ah-compliant financing** models that NCMF does not offer; accredit **sheikhs** under its own criteria; operate a **regional Hajj Assistance Fund** with its own eligibility standards; and administer ***awqaf*** properties under its own framework — all within the national Hajj quota.

**Q. If BARMM enacts different standards, what are the implications for rights holders and cross-boundary operations?**

A. (1) **For Bangsamoro pilgrims**: They could gain services not available through NCMF — particularly Shari'ah-compliant Hajj financing, local-language support, sheikh accreditation by the Darul-Ifta', and region-specific pilgrim protection standards.

   (2) **For Bangsamoro Muslims outside BARMM**: Art. VII, Sec. 42's geographic limitation means Bangsamoro Muslims residing outside BARMM fall under NCMF, not BPA — even if they are ethnically Bangsamoro. This creates a service gap that only intergovernmental coordination can address.

   (3) **For cross-boundary coordination**: The annual Hajj requires unified national coordination with Saudi Arabia. A standalone Bangsamoro Hajj Act should include **coordination protocols** with NCMF to ensure that differentiated standards do not create confusion in quota allocation, travel documentation, or Saudi engagement.

**Q. What happens to national law when the Parliament enacts a BAA on this subject?**

A. A Bangsamoro Hajj and Umrah Act would govern BPA's regulatory authority within BARMM; RA 9997 would continue to apply for national-level coordination (Hajj quota, Amirul Hajj, international agreements). Constitutional provisions always apply.

### G. Legislative Gaps and Recommended Legislation

**Q. Are there overlapping or unclear jurisdictions between Bangsamoro and national authority?**

A. Yes. The primary overlap is between **BPA** and **NCMF's Bureau of Pilgrimage and Endowment**. Currently: (a) the **Hajj quota** is nationally allocated by Saudi Arabia through NCMF — BPA's share within this quota is determined by coordination, not by statute; (b) **pilgrim registration** may be processed through both BPA and NCMF systems; (c) ***awqaf*** administration falls under both BPA's Endowment Administration Division and NCMF's Sec. 8(s)-(t) mandate; (d) **sheikh accreditation** could be exercised by both agencies. A standalone Hajj and Umrah Act should include a **jurisdictional protocol** (or MOA framework) with NCMF defining which functions are BPA's, which are NCMF's, and how quota allocation is determined.

**Q. What pending Parliament Bills address the gaps?**

A. **PB No. 282** (30 calendar days paid leave for pilgrimage) addresses the **employment barrier** to Hajj. No pending PB proposes a comprehensive Hajj and Umrah Code, a Hajj Assistance Fund statutory framework, or an *awqaf* management act. The most critical legislative gap — a **Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Act** — has no pending bill.

### H. Development and Policy Connection

**Q. Which BDP 2023-2028 goals and Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda pillars depend on this power?**

A. **BDP 2023-2028**, Chapter 5 (Governance) identifies **moral governance** as the overarching principle — and Hajj administration demonstrates the Bangsamoro Government's capacity to deliver distinctly Islamic public services that the national government cannot match. Chapter 9 (Social Development) connects Hajj to **cultural and religious identity** as a social development outcome. The **Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda** (2026-2028, OCM EO No. 2, s. 2026), **Pillar 1 (Good Governance)**, depends on transparent and accountable Hajj administration to build **public trust** — particularly in light of Resolution No. 376's concerns about 2023 irregularities. **Pillar 5 (Moral Governance)** directly encompasses government facilitation of religious observance.

**Q. What development outcomes are at risk if the legislative gaps remain unaddressed?**

A. Without a standalone Hajj and Umrah Act: (a) the **P200M Hajj Financing and Support Program** operates without statutory eligibility criteria, selection processes, or Shari'ah-compliance requirements — creating risks of arbitrary allocation and potential *riba*-bearing financial structures; (b) **pilgrim protection standards** depend entirely on BAA 13's general provisions — no penalty framework exists for operators who exploit pilgrims; (c) ***awqaf*** properties managed by BPA lack a statutory governance framework — endowment assets are vulnerable to mismanagement without clear legal rules for administration, investment, and beneficiary rights; (d) the **jurisdictional boundary with NCMF** remains undefined by statute — creating coordination gaps and potential duplication that reduce efficiency and service quality; (e) **public trust** in BPA is undermined when the regulatory framework is too thin to ensure accountability (as Resolution No. 376 demonstrated).

### I. Power-Specific Questions

**Q. Why is Hajj and Umrah one of the 55 enumerated powers — and what distinguishes it from other powers?**

A. Hajj and Umrah is the **only enumerated power** that is directly and exclusively a religious obligation. All other powers — from administration of justice to trade and industry to health — are governmental functions that serve diverse purposes. Hajj is a pillar of Islam. The BOL's enumeration of this power reflects three realities: (1) the **Bangsamoro population is predominantly Muslim** and the Hajj obligation affects a substantial portion of the population; (2) the **ARMM precedent** — the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao had pilgrimage administration functions, and the BOL continues this authority; (3) **self-governance over religious life** is a core component of Bangsamoro autonomy — the BOL's recognition of Islamic identity (Art. II, Sec. 1; Art. III) extends to ensuring that the Bangsamoro Government can facilitate the most important religious journeys of its constituents.

**Q. What is the relationship between BPA and NCMF — will one replace the other within BARMM?**

A. BPA does **not replace NCMF** within BARMM — unlike CSEA replacing CDA for cooperatives. The relationship is **coordinative**, not substitutive. BAA 13 Sec. 43 mandates ***"close coordination"*** because: (a) the Saudi Arabian Hajj quota is a **national allocation** that NCMF manages — BPA cannot independently obtain Hajj visas; (b) the **Amirul Hajj** is the NCMF Secretary (RA 9997, Sec. 16), not a BPA official — the head of the national pilgrimage delegation is a Presidential appointee; (c) **international agreements** on pilgrimage are national-level. BPA exercises **primary jurisdiction** for Bangsamoro-origin pilgrims (BAA 13 Sec. 43) — meaning BPA handles registration, preparation, sheikh accreditation, fee collection, and service delivery for Bangsamoro pilgrims. But BPA operates **within** the national Hajj framework, not independently of it. A standalone Hajj and Umrah Act should define this relationship by statute rather than leaving it to ad hoc coordination.

**Q. How does BPA's authority to engage Saudi Arabia directly (BAA 13, Sec. 45) work in practice?**

A. BAA 13 Sec. 45 authorizes BPA to ***"directly engage and enter into agreements with the appropriate authorities in the Kingdom of Saudi Arabia, upon consultation with, and prior approval by, the Chief Minister."*** This provision is significant because it grants BPA a degree of **external affairs authority** unusual for a subnational government agency. In practice, this means BPA can: (a) negotiate **operational arrangements** with Saudi authorities for Bangsamoro pilgrim services (housing, transportation, medical support in Saudi Arabia); (b) enter into **contracts with Saudi service providers** (BPA signed 3 contracts with foreign suppliers during the 2023 Hajj per BAA 32 targets); and (c) deploy **BPA personnel to the Royal Saudi Embassy** for coordination. However, this external engagement operates within the **Philippine national Hajj quota** — BPA cannot independently negotiate for additional Hajj visas beyond the national allocation. The Chief Minister's "prior approval" requirement ensures this external engagement is governed at the highest level of the Bangsamoro executive.

**Q. What should a Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Act contain that BAA 13 does not provide?**

A. A standalone act should cover: (1) **Hajj operator accreditation** — standards, application process, monitoring, and revocation for private Hajj operators and travel agencies; (2) **Pilgrim Bill of Rights** — codifying the free-choice protections in BAA 13 Sec. 45 and adding enforceable standards for service quality, information disclosure, refund rights, and complaint mechanisms; (3) **Hajj Financing and Support Fund** — statutory framework with eligibility criteria, application process, selection committee, financial structure (Shari'ah-compliant *qard hasan* or grant-based), repayment terms, and accountability mechanisms; (4) **Sheikh accreditation** — qualifications, training requirements, Darul-Ifta' review, and periodic reaccreditation; (5) ***Awqaf* management framework** — legal rules for administration of endowment properties, investment, beneficiary rights, and accountability (complementing BPA's Endowment Administration Division); (6) **Penalty provisions** — for fraud, overcharging, exploitation of pilgrims, unauthorized fee collection, and forced purchases; (7) **BPA-NCMF jurisdictional protocol** — statutory definition of which functions are BPA's, which are NCMF's, and how Hajj quota allocation is determined; (8) **Pilgrimage leave** — mandating paid leave for employees performing Hajj (if PB No. 282 is not enacted separately); (9) **Shari'ah compliance mechanism** — requiring Darul-Ifta' review of BPA regulations and financial products.

**Q. How does the Hajj Financing and Support Program (P200M under BAA 85) operate without enabling legislation?**

A. The P200M allocation under BAA 85, Chapter XXV is an **appropriation**, not an enabling framework. This means: (a) the funds exist but the **eligibility criteria** for beneficiaries are determined administratively by BPA, not by statute; (b) the **financial structure** — whether the assistance is a grant, a loan, or a subsidy — is not defined by law; (c) if the assistance is structured as a **loan**, the question of whether it bears interest or is structured as *qard hasan* (interest-free benevolent loan) depends on BPA policy, not statutory requirement; (d) **selection processes** may lack the transparency that statutory provisions would mandate; and (e) the program's **continuity** depends on annual appropriation rather than a permanent statutory fund. A standalone Hajj and Umrah Act would transform this from an administrative program to a **statutory entitlement** with defined standards.
