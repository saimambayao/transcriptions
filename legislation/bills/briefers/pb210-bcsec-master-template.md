## OVERVIEW
### Executive Summary for Members of Parliament

**Measure:** BTA Parliament Bill No. 210 — *An Act Providing for the Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code (BCSEC) of 2023*

**Status:** First Reading (as of July 20, 2023). Pending committee referral and second reading deliberation.

**One-Sentence Summary:** PB 210 establishes BARMM's first comprehensive legal framework for cooperatives and social enterprises, fulfilling a dual legislative mandate under the Bangsamoro Organic Law and creating the Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA) as the primary regulatory and development body for the sector.

**Why It Matters — Three Points Every MP Should Know:**

1. **Constitutional and Organic Law Mandate.** The Bangsamoro Organic Law (RA 11054) explicitly commands Parliament to enact a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code (Art. XIII, Sec. 24) and social enterprise laws (Art. XIII, Sec. 27). PB 210 fulfills both mandates simultaneously in a single consolidated Code. Without passage, BARMM defaults to the national CDA (Cooperative Development Authority) for cooperative governance — a gap that leaves Bangsamoro cooperatives without a responsive local regulatory body.

2. **Direct Economic and Peacebuilding Impact.** The cooperative sector is one of the most viable pathways for ex-combatant reintegration, transitional justice, and rural poverty reduction in BARMM. PB 210's RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program (Book III, Secs. 8-9) directly incorporates peacebuilding as a program principle — a Bangsamoro-specific feature absent from the national framework.

3. **Islamic Finance Integration.** PB 210 is the first Bangsamoro legislation to explicitly embed Shari'ah principles into the governance of cooperatives and social enterprises (Secs. 7-9, Book I). It mandates a Shari'ah-compliant financing program for startup and micro cooperatives and SEs (Book IV, Sec. 5a) and permits Islamic philanthropy instruments — sadaqah, zakat, and waqf — as financing mechanisms for social enterprises (Sec. 13, Book III). This is not merely symbolic; it enables Bangsamoro cooperatives to access Islamic development finance networks and comply with the religious obligations of their Muslim members.

**Structure:** Four Books (67 pages):
- Book I: General Provisions — Title, purpose, cooperative principles, Islamic principles, moral governance, definitions
- Book II: Cooperative Development — Formation, registration, governance, officers, capital, audit, tax exemptions
- Book III: Social Enterprise Development — Regulation, BSEC, RISE for Peace, financing, tax incentives
- Book IV: Miscellaneous and Final Provisions — Shari'ah financing program, parliamentary committee, penal provisions, transitory provisions

**Lead Principal Author:** MP Diamila Disimban-Ramos
**Principal Co-Authors (9):** MPs Ishak Mastura, Lanang Ali Jr., Rasul Mitmug Jr., Amilbahar Mawalil, Baintan Ampatuan, Eddie Alih, Michael Midtimbang, Ali Sangki, Suharto Ambolodto
**Number of Co-Authors:** 22 additional MPs

**Lead Implementing Agency:** Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA), attached to the Office of the Chief Minister

**Recommended Floor Position:** **SUPPORT** — Fulfills explicit BOL legislative mandate; addresses a clear regulatory gap; integrates peacebuilding and Islamic finance in a Bangsamoro-specific framework.

## SECTION I: BRIEF DESCRIPTION OF THE MEASURE

### A. Title and Citation

The bill is entitled: *"An Act Providing for the Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code (BCSEC) of 2023."* It shall be cited as the **Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code** or **BCSEC** upon enactment. (Book I, Sec. 1)

### B. Nature and Type of Measure

PB 210 is a **comprehensive code** — not an amendatory act or standalone program law — that creates an original and consolidated regulatory framework for two related but distinct sectors: (1) cooperatives and (2) social enterprises. It is structured as a code with four Books, multiple Chapters, and approximately 100 substantive sections covering registration, governance, financing, tax treatment, penal provisions, and transitory arrangements.

As a code, it functions as the supreme internal law governing cooperatives and SEs in BARMM, with suppletory application of national laws (particularly RA 9520, Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) where not inconsistent. (Book IV, Sec. 12)

### C. Stated Purpose and Legislative Policy

**Purpose:**

> **[Book I, Sec. 2]** — *"The Bangsamoro Government shall adopt and implement cooperative and social enterprise development policies and programs in accordance with Sections 24 and 27, Article XIII of the Bangsamoro Organic Law. It shall recognize, protect and promote the establishment of cooperative enterprises and encourage and promote social entrepreneurship as a means to engage the private sector to assist in the development, funding and implementation of solution to social, cultural and environmental issues."*

**Declaration of Policy** (Book I, Sec. 3) — The Code embodies 14 policy directives. Selected verbatim:

> **[Book I, Sec. 3(j)]** — *"Foster the creation and growth of the cooperatives and social enterprises as practical vehicles for promoting self-reliance and harnessing people power towards the attainment of economic development, equity, and social justice;"*

> **[Book I, Sec. 3(m)]** — *"The Bangsamoro Government and all its branches, subdivisions, instrumentalities and agencies shall ensure the provision of technical guidance, financial assistance and other services to enable said cooperatives and social enterprises to develop into viable and responsive economic enterprises and thereby bring about strong cooperative and social enterprise movements that are free from any condition that might infringe upon their autonomy or organizational integrity;"*

> **[Book I, Sec. 3(n)]** — *"The Bangsamoro Government recognizes the principle of subsidiarity under which the cooperative and social enterprise sectors will initiate and regulate within their own ranks the promotion and organization, training and research, audit and support services with government assistance where necessary."*

**What the policy directives establish in aggregate:** The Code commits the Bangsamoro Government to: (a) social order and poverty reduction through full employment and equitable distribution; (b) cooperative and SE sectors as the primary engines of equity and social justice — not subordinate to, but equal partners with, the government in development; (c) autonomy of cooperatives and SEs as a non-negotiable constraint on government assistance; and (d) subsidiarity — government as enabler, not controller.

### D. Key Definitions (Book I, Sec. 4)

> **[Book I, Sec. 4]** — *"A cooperative is an autonomous and duly registered association of persons, with a common bond of interest, who have voluntarily joined together to achieve their social, economic, and cultural needs and aspirations by making equitable contributions to the capital required, patronizing their products and services and accepting a fair share of the risks and benefits of the undertaking in accordance with moral governance and universally accepted cooperative principles."*

> **[Book I, Sec. 4]** — *"A social enterprise refers to any business organization whose primary purpose is addressing a social, cultural, or environmental challenge where creating profit for owners or shareholders is secondary. It brings together the entrepreneurial skills of the private sector and the values of public service. It delivers positive social impact through its means and ends to achieve social justice, social equity and economic efficiency. To be considered a social enterprise, the entity must undertake business and economic opportunities in accordance with moral governance and social enterprise principles. It must have a clear social purpose included in its governing documents and re-invest a significant proportion of its profits back into the business for social, cultural, or environmental purposes."*

### E. Foundational Principles

The Code operates under three overlapping frameworks (Book I, Secs. 5-9):

**1. Seven Cooperative Principles (ICA-aligned):**
- Voluntary and Open Membership
- Democratic Member Control
- Member Economic Participation
- Autonomy and Independence
- Education, Training, and Information
- Cooperation Among Cooperatives
- Concern for Community

**2. Three Social Entrepreneurship Principles:**
- Social Mission (primacy of wellbeing, peace, social cohesion, reduced inequality)
- Multiple Bottom-lines (measurable social and environmental impacts alongside financial return)
- Sustainable, Inclusive, and Equitable Development

**3. Islamic Principles (Shari'ah-specific, Sec. 7):**
- Fulfillment of maqasid al-Shari'ah (preservation of faith, life, lineage, intellect, and wealth)
- Promotion of halaal transactions
- Generation of wealth from legitimate, asset-based business
- Avoidance of harm and sharing of risks
- Prohibition of riba (interest/usury), maysir (gambling), gharar (excessive uncertainty), and haram products/services

**4. Moral Governance (Sec. 8):** Governance guided by moral principles — servitude, honesty, integrity, accountability, excellence, stewardship, balance, harmony, consultation, justice, and trust. The CSEA shall issue guidelines on moral governance for cooperatives and SEs.

**5. Shari'ah Governance (Sec. 9):** The CSEA shall adopt a Shari'ah governance framework requiring a Shari'ah committee of at least three (3) qualified members within each cooperative subject to the framework.

### F. Types of Cooperatives Covered (Book II)

The Code covers 21 types of cooperatives including:
- Credit, Consumer, Producer, Marketing, Service, Multipurpose
- Advocacy, Agrarian Reform, Dairy, Education, Electric
- Financial Service, Fishermen's, Health Services, Housing
- Insurance, Labor, Transport, Water Service, Workers' cooperatives
- Other types as determined by the CSEA (Book II, Sec. 13)

## SECTION II: IMPACT ASSESSMENT

### A. Economic Impact

**1. Cooperative Sector Formalization**

Prior to PB 210, BARMM cooperatives were registered and regulated by the national Cooperative Development Authority (CDA) under RA 9520 (2008) and RA 11364. The enactment of PB 210 will transfer registration and regulatory authority to the newly constituted CSEA — a Bangsamoro-based agency with the mandate, resources, and institutional context to serve BARMM's unique cooperative ecosystem. This transition will:

- Enable faster, more accessible cooperative registration through CSEA's provincial offices, including a dedicated office covering Maguindanao (Book I, Sec. 13, para. on provincial offices)
- Reduce regulatory burden on grassroots cooperatives in BARMM's remote municipalities by bringing the registering authority closer
- Allow cooperative policy to be shaped by Bangsamoro-specific realities — including the role of coops in transitional justice, Islamic finance, and conflict-affected communities — rather than national-level frameworks designed for Luzon-based cooperatives

**2. Tax Benefits — Dual-Tier Exemption Scheme (Book II, Sec. 58)**

The Code adopts a tiered tax treatment that mirrors and expands BOL Art. XII, Sec. 9(k):

| Cooperative Type | Tax Treatment |
|---|---|
| Coops not transacting with non-members | Fully tax-free — exempt from ALL Bangsamoro internal revenue taxes and fees |
| Coops with accumulated reserves ≤ P10 million | Exempt from all national, city, provincial, municipal, and barangay taxes; exempt from customs duties on machinery/equipment/spare parts not locally available |
| Coops with accumulated reserves > P10 million | Income tax on interest capital allocations only; VAT on non-member transactions only (with specific sub-exemptions); all other taxes at full rate |
| All registered coops | Exempt from VAT on transactions covered by Sec. 109(L)(M)(N) of RA 9337; exempt from registration and filing fees; exempt from court and sheriff's fees; exempt from bond requirements for appeals |

**3. Social Enterprise Tax Incentives (Book III, Chapter III)**

| SE Category | Tax Benefit |
|---|---|
| Micro social enterprises | Exempt from income tax; LGUs shall reduce/exempt local taxes, fees, charges |
| New SEs (first 3 years) | Tax holiday — exempt from regional taxes, fees, and charges under Bangsamoro Revenue Code |
| All CSEA-qualified SEs | VAT exemption and tariff/duties exemption on imported equipment, machinery, implements, and spare parts for exclusive SE use |
| SE reinvestments | Tax deductible from gross income for income tax purposes |
| All SEs | Preferential procurement rights in government procurement at all levels |

**4. Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Fund (BSEF)**

Section 14, Book III creates the BSEF — a dedicated grants fund for social enterprises, managed by CSEA in partnership with the Bangsamoro Center for Social Enterprise Development (BCSED) and the BSEC. Funding shall come from the Special Development Fund or other GAAB sources. This is the first dedicated SE grants facility in BARMM, providing startup and scale-up capital to SEs that cannot access commercial credit.

**5. LGU-Level SE Financing Mandate**

Section 11(c), Book III mandates all local government units to allocate five percent (5%) of their funds for SE research, marketing, capacity-building, and financing of SE initiatives at the local level. Section 13 further requires LGUs to allocate 5% of their funds towards financing SE initiatives prioritized in local development plans. This creates a significant decentralized funding stream — particularly important for BARMM's 5 provinces and the 63 municipalities with active cooperative movements.

### B. Social and Poverty Reduction Impact

**1. Peacebuilding Integration (Book III, Sec. 8 — RISE for Peace)**

The RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program is a Bangsamoro-specific feature with no equivalent in the national framework. Its foundational principles explicitly address:
- Incorporation of social justice, equity, and economic efficiency in the regional development strategy through social entrepreneurship that contributes to **social inclusion and reconciliation**
- Promotion of **sustainable programs that reduce inequality, improve incomes, and increase self-reliance** of basic and disadvantaged sectors
- **Gender-sensitivity** — ensuring women's equal rights and access to SE resources
- **Youth engagement** — nurturing young people's skills to develop SEs and solve local problems

PB 210 also mandates CSEA to promote cooperatives and social enterprises as pathways to **reintegrate former combatants, surrenderees, and disaster-affected communities** (Book I, Sec. 13(f)). This is the first time a Bangsamoro law has directly connected the cooperative sector to post-conflict reintegration.

**2. Multi-Sector SE Areas (Book III, Sec. 1)**

Social enterprises under PB 210 may operate in 11 defined sectors:
- Social services; employment (including youth); women empowerment
- Health protection; education; environmental protection
- Tourism, culture, and cultural heritage; sports and recreation
- Indigenous peoples' community development
- **Peace and reconciliation** (explicit sector)
- Disaster risk reduction and management

**3. SE Education Mandate (Book III, Sec. 12)**

The Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education is mandated to integrate social entrepreneurship content into all curricula from secondary to tertiary level. All higher learning institutions and vocational schools must include a subject on social entrepreneurship. A continuing SE education program for out-of-school youth and adults is also required. This creates a pipeline for future social entrepreneurs from within Bangsamoro communities.

**4. Islamic Philanthropy Integration (Book III, Sec. 13)**

The Code explicitly permits and encourages **sadaqah** (voluntary charity), **zakat** (obligatory poor tax), and **waqf** (Islamic trust/endowment) as strategic, empowering, and sustainable financing instruments for social enterprises. This is significant for BARMM, where Islamic philanthropic institutions manage substantial funds that — prior to PB 210 — had no clear legal conduit into the SE development ecosystem.

### C. Governance and Institutional Impact

**1. Creation of the CSEA (Book I, Chapter II)**

The CSEA is established as the sole registering and regulatory authority for cooperatives and SEs in BARMM, with fiscal autonomy, attached to the Office of the Chief Minister. The CSEA shall be headed by an Executive Director with the rank of Director III. Its functions span: registration and accreditation, policy formulation, training and capacity development, financial assistance coordination, market development, research and knowledge management, Shari'ah governance compliance, and disputes resolution oversight.

**2. Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Council (BSEC) (Book III, Sec. 6)**

The BSEC is a multi-sector advisory body organized by the CSEA, composed of:
- 9 SE representatives (one per BARMM province and component city), each operating ≥ 5 years
- 2 SE intermediaries (≥ 5 years)
- 2 impact investors (≥ 5 years)
- Representatives from 9 Bangsamoro ministries and agencies: MSSD, MTIT, MOLE, MBHTE, MAFAR, BYC, BWC, IPA, MILG

The BSEC's functions include monitoring RISE for Peace, assessing SE status renewals, partnership and collaboration, and providing strategic advice to the Bangsamoro Government.

**3. Bangsamoro Center for Social Enterprise Development (BCSED) (Book III, Sec. 10)**

The BCSED is established under the CSEA as an operational arm for: SE capability training, research and development, Social Enterprise Marketing Assistance Program (SMAP), SE Marketing Information Network (SMIN), and international benchmarking on SE policies and programs.

**4. Parliamentary Oversight Committee (Book IV, Sec. 6)**

The Code creates a **Committee on Cooperatives and Social Enterprises** in the Bangsamoro Parliament with jurisdiction over all matters relating to cooperative and SE regulation and development. This Committee shall conduct a comprehensive 5-year review of the Code's implementation starting from effectivity. This institutionalizes Parliament's continuing oversight role over the sector.

### D. Risk Considerations

| Risk | Assessment |
|---|---|
| CSEA capacity gap | CSEA is established under BAA No. 13 but lacks enabling legislation until BCSEC is enacted. Transition period requires immediate staffing and operationalization to absorb CDA's registry. |
| CDA transition | All CDA-registered BARMM coops must re-register with CSEA within 1 year of effectivity (Book IV, Sec. 12). Risk of deregistration for cooperatives that miss the deadline in remote areas. |
| LGU 5% allocation enforcement | Mandatory SE allocations by LGUs may face fiscal constraints in BARMM's smaller municipalities. Enforcement mechanism is not specified. |
| Islamic finance implementation | Shari'ah-compliant financing program requires qualified Shari'ah finance professionals and partnerships with Islamic finance institutions — a new capacity requirement for CSEA. |
| Penal provisions breadth | Imprisonment provisions (6 months to 10 years) cover a wide range of violations, including administrative non-compliance. Proportionality may be reviewed at second reading. |

## SECTION IV: IMPLEMENTING AGENCIES

### A. Primary Agency: Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA)

**Establishment:** The CSEA was created by the Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13), Sections 57-65, as a government agency of the Bangsamoro. PB 210 provides CSEA's comprehensive enabling legislation — the powers, functions, organizational structure, and mandate it needs to fully operate.

**Nature:** Government authority with fiscal autonomy, attached to the **Office of the Chief Minister (OCM)**

**Head:** Executive Director with the rank of Director III

**Establishment and Registration Authority (Book I, Sec. 11):**

> **[Book I, Sec. 11]** — *"The Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority shall be the primary entity to promote the viability and growth of Bangsamoro cooperatives and social enterprise in the Bangsamoro region. It shall be the sole agency of the Bangsamoro Government that registers cooperatives and social enterprises... The Authority shall enjoy fiscal autonomy and shall be attached to the Office of the Chief Minister."*

**Mandate (Book I, Sec. 12):**

> **[Book I, Sec. 12]** — *"The Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority with all the branches, sub-divisions, instrumentalities and agencies of the Bangsamoro Government shall ensure the provision of technical guidance, financial assistance and other services to enable the cooperatives to develop into viable and responsive economic enterprises and be platforms for poverty reduction and sustainable, inclusive, and equitable development."*

**Key Powers and Functions (Book I, Sec. 13):**

1. Formulate, adopt, and implement integrated cooperative and SE development plans aligned with the Bangsamoro Government's socio-economic development plans
2. Register cooperatives (exclusive jurisdiction) and accredit social enterprises
3. Develop and conduct management and training programs for cooperatives and SEs
4. Support voluntary cooperative organization and provide technical expertise upon request
5. Ensure marginalized, vulnerable, and disabled individuals can participate in the cooperative/SE model
6. Coordinate LGU and private sector efforts in cooperative and SE development
7. Promote cooperatives and SEs as reintegration pathways for former combatants and disaster-affected communities
8. Support social entrepreneurs on product development, financing, market linkage, and legal accreditation
9. Promote SE education in schools and universities; convene policy dialogues and research
10. Require annual reports and audited financial statements from all cooperatives
11. Assist cooperatives in accessing financial assistance on terms that strengthen autonomy
12. Administer Bangsamoro Government grants for cooperative development
13. Adopt Shari'ah governance framework and Shari'ah compliance policies for cooperatives and SEs

**Organizational Structure:**
- Executive Director (Director III rank) heads the Authority
- Administrative Support Division: administrative support, finance management
- Research and Knowledge Management Division: research and knowledge initiatives
- Provincial Offices: including at minimum a dedicated office covering Maguindanao
- Local Cooperative Development Offices: in coordination with municipalities

### B. Advisory and Sectoral Body: Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Council (BSEC)

**Establishment:** Book III, Sec. 6 — organized by CSEA after enactment
**Nature:** Alliance of SE leaders; advisory body for RISE for Peace monitoring
**Composition:** 13 sector representatives (9 SEs, 2 intermediaries, 2 impact investors) + 9 Bangsamoro ministry/agency representatives (MSSD, MTIT, MOLE, MBHTE, MAFAR, BYC, BWC, IPA, MILG)
**Key Role:** Monitors RISE for Peace implementation; assesses SE status renewals; provides strategic advice to the Bangsamoro Government; assists CSEA in strengthening the SE sector

### C. Operational Arm: Bangsamoro Center for Social Enterprise Development (BCSED)

**Establishment:** Book III, Sec. 10 — created under the CSEA
**Functions:**
- Capability training and education through the SE Capability Building and Sustainability Program
- Research and development system for SE innovation
- Social Enterprise Marketing Assistance Program (SMAP) — matching SE supply with domestic and foreign markets
- SE Marketing Information Network (SMIN) — business information and market intelligence for SEs
- Capability building project implementation and supervision
- International benchmarking on SE programs and regulations

### D. Parliamentary Oversight Body: Committee on Cooperatives and Social Enterprises

**Establishment:** Book IV, Sec. 6 — created within the Bangsamoro Parliament
**Jurisdiction:** All matters relating to cooperative and SE regulation and development
**Functions:** Review BCSEC implementation framework; determine legal weaknesses; recommend remedial legislation; conduct 5-year review from effectivity

### E. Coordinating and Supporting Agencies

| Agency | Role under BCSEC |
|---|---|
| Ministry of Finance and Budget Management (MFBM) | Shari'ah-compliant financing program (Book IV, Sec. 5a); BSEF funding identification (Book III, Sec. 14); SE tax incentives coordination (Book III, Sec. 16); SE Masterplan funding (Book III, Sec. 7) |
| Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT) | SE marketing (SMAP, SMIN); SE research and development; tax incentive formulation; social impact investment facilitation (Book III, Secs. 9, 15-16) |
| Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) | Shari'ah-compliant financing program (Book IV, Sec. 5a); agrarian reform cooperatives (Book II) |
| Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education (MBHTE) | SE education integration in curricula at all levels (Book III, Sec. 12) |
| Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA) | SE research and development system (Book III, Sec. 9b) |
| Development Academy of Bangsamoro (DAB/OCM) | SE research and development (Book III, Sec. 9b) |
| Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST) | SE research and development system (Book III, Sec. 9b) |
| Local Government Units (LGUs) | 5% fund allocation for SE research/marketing/capacity-building/financing; SE support plans in local development planning; public procurement prioritization of SEs (Book III, Secs. 11, 13) |
| Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) | Certificate of tax exemption issuance for re-registered cooperatives (Book IV, Sec. 12) |
| Cooperative Development Authority (CDA) | Transition partner — transfers BARMM cooperative registry to CSEA within transitory period (Book IV, Sec. 12) |

## SECTION V: IMPLEMENTATION PLAN

### A. Phase 1 — Immediate (0 to 6 Months from Effectivity)

**1. CSEA Activation and Staffing (Month 1-3)**
- Appointment of CSEA Executive Director (Director III rank) by the Chief Minister
- Activation of CSEA provincial offices, including the Maguindanao office
- Hiring and deployment of CSEA regulatory staff for cooperative registration functions
- Transfer of BARMM cooperative registry from CDA to CSEA

**2. Shari'ah Governance Framework (Month 1-3)**
- CSEA issues Shari'ah governance framework and compliance policies (Book I, Sec. 9)
- Framework prescribes Shari'ah committee composition requirements (minimum 3 qualified members)
- CSEA issues guidelines on moral governance application for cooperatives and SEs (Book I, Sec. 8)

**3. Tax Exemption Transition for Existing Cooperatives (Month 1-3)**
- CSEA and BIR jointly issue regulations on tax exemptions and compromise settlements for previously registered cooperatives (Book IV, Sec. 12)
- All CDA-registered BARMM cooperatives must submit registration documents to CSEA within 1 year of effectivity for re-registration and issuance of new COR
- BIR issues new certificates of tax exemption to re-registered cooperatives (valid for 5 years)

**4. SE Registration and Accreditation System (Month 1-6)**
- CSEA develops the Cooperative Registration and Social Enterprise Accreditation Information Systems (Book IV, Sec. 2a)
- CSEA develops the Cooperative and Social Enterprise Annual Progress Report Information Systems (Book IV, Sec. 2b)
- CSEA issues guidelines on SE accreditation requirements (Book III, Sec. 3)

**5. Tax Incentive IRR (Month 1-3, within 90 days)**
- CSEA, in consultation with MFBM, MTIT, DOF, BIR, Bureau of Customs, and relevant agencies, formulates the IRR governing VAT/tariff exemptions for SE equipment importation (Book III, Sec. 16)

### B. Phase 2 — Short-Term (6 Months to 2 Years from Effectivity)

**1. BSEC Organization (Month 6-12)**
- CSEA convenes a transitionary BSEC composed of SE representatives from all provinces and component cities that have been operating for at least 5 years
- Elections conducted among SEs for BSEC province/city representatives
- Elected BSEC establishes its bylaws, electoral process, and 3-year program of action

**2. RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program Design (Month 6-18)**
- CSEA formulates the RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program framework
- Program planning synchronized with the Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028
- SE Capability Building and Sustainability Fund established
- SE Research and Development System activated in coordination with BPDA, DAB, MOST, MTIT

**3. Bangsamoro Center for Social Enterprise Development (BCSED) — Operationalization (Month 12-24)**
- BCSED established under CSEA
- SMAP (SE Marketing Assistance Program) launched
- SMIN (SE Marketing Information Network) developed and deployed

**4. Regional SE Masterplan (Month 12-24)**
- CSEA develops the Regional Masterplan on Social Enterprise Development in consultation with the Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Coalition and sector stakeholders (Book III, Sec. 7)
- Masterplan funding identified by CSEA in collaboration with MFBM; appropriated by Bangsamoro Parliament in GAAB

**5. Shari'ah-Compliant Financing Program (Month 6-18)**
- CSEA, in collaboration with MFBM, MAFAR, and MTIT, implements the Shari'ah-compliant financing program for startup and micro cooperatives and SEs (Book IV, Sec. 5a)
- Business-readiness and capacity development interventions paired with financing (Book IV, Sec. 5b)

### C. Phase 3 — Medium-Term (2 to 5 Years from Effectivity)

**1. SE Education Integration**
- MBHTE integrates SE content and courses into curricula at secondary and tertiary levels (Book III, Sec. 12)
- All higher learning institutions and vocational schools include SE subjects in social studies, business, commerce, or economics programs
- Continuing SE education program for out-of-school youth and adults implemented

**2. Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Fund (BSEF) — Scale-Up**
- BSEF matures from initial grants to investment-readiness programming
- CSEA, BCSED, and BSEC jointly manage BSEF for scale-up capital grants to SEs

**3. LGU SE Integration**
- All BARMM LGUs establish and implement SE support and development plans incorporated in local development planning
- LGU 5% fund allocations for SE initiatives reported and audited

**4. Social Impact Investment Ecosystem**
- CSEA, MFBM, and MTIT develop a social impact investment framework (Book III, Sec. 15)
- CSEA develops valuation system for social, economic, and environmental contributions of SEs as basis for tax incentives (Book III, Sec. 16)
- CSEA offers tax incentives to impact investors channeling capital to SE beneficiaries

### D. Phase 4 — Long-Term (5+ Years from Effectivity)

**1. Legislative Review (5 Years after Effectivity)**
- Parliamentary Committee on Cooperatives and Social Enterprises conducts systematic review of BCSEC implementation, program accomplishments, and implementing agency performance (Book IV, Sec. 6)
- Committee recommends necessary remedial legislation or executive measures based on review findings

**2. IRL/IRR Updates**
- CSEA issues updated implementing rules as needed based on 5-year review
- Parliament enacts amendments based on Committee recommendations

## SECTION VI: BUDGETARY IMPLICATIONS

### A. Initial Appropriation

**Funding Source (Book IV, Sec. 13):** The amount necessary to implement the BCSEC upon effectivity shall be charged against the **Contingent Funds**. Thereafter, such sums as may be necessary for continued implementation shall be included in the **General Appropriations Act of the Bangsamoro (GAAB)**. Cooperating agencies shall incorporate BCSEC budget requirements in their respective agency budgets.

### B. CSEA Operational Budget Estimate

No specific appropriation figure is stated in PB 210. Based on the CSEA's functions and organizational structure, Members of Parliament should expect appropriation requests covering:

| Budget Item | Cost Driver |
|---|---|
| Personnel services | Executive Director + technical staff at national and provincial offices (minimum: Maguindanao office + regional headquarters) |
| Maintenance and operations | Cooperative registry systems (Book IV, Sec. 2), SE accreditation systems, reporting systems |
| Capital outlay | IT infrastructure for registration, accreditation, and reporting systems |
| Program funds | SE Capability Building and Sustainability Program; RISE for Peace implementation |
| Research and development | SE R&D System (BPDA/DAB/MOST coordination); SE Masterplan development |
| Market development | SMAP (SE Marketing Assistance Program) and SMIN (SE Marketing Information Network) |

### C. Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Fund (BSEF)

The BSEF (Book III, Sec. 14) will require an initial capitalization appropriated under the GAAB or drawn from the Special Development Fund. No minimum capitalization figure is specified in the bill. Members of Parliament are advised to ask the CSEA or the budget committee for a capitalization proposal during the deliberation phase.

### D. SE Masterplan Funding

Section 7, Book III requires CSEA and MFBM to identify funding for the Regional SE Masterplan, with Parliament appropriating the amount through the GAAB. The Masterplan's cost will depend on CSEA's consultation process and the scope of programs proposed.

### E. LGU Fiscal Impact

Section 11(c) and Section 13, Book III mandate all BARMM LGUs to allocate **5% of their funds** for SE research, marketing, capacity-building, and financing. This is a significant mandatory expenditure — for provinces and municipalities with constrained budgets, this may require complementary intergovernmental fiscal support or a phase-in period.

### F. Tax Revenue Impact

The comprehensive tax exemptions for cooperatives (Book II, Sec. 58) and tax incentives for SEs (Book III, Chapter III) will result in reduced tax collection. However, this is offset by:
- Anticipated expansion of the cooperative and SE sector generating new employment and economic activity
- LGU 5% allocations channeled into SE development as productive expenditure
- Islamic philanthropy (sadaqah, zakat, waqf) mobilized as supplementary non-government financing

The CSEA's valuation system for SE social, economic, and environmental contributions (Book III, Sec. 16) will provide the measurement framework for assessing the value of these incentives over time.

### G. Sensitivity Note

Since no specific peso appropriation is stated in the bill, Members of Parliament should request a **fiscal impact estimate** from the Ministry of Finance and Budget Management as part of second reading deliberations. The Committee on Cooperatives and Social Enterprises should invite MFBM to present: (a) estimated CSEA operational budget; (b) BSEF initial capitalization proposal; (c) estimated tax expenditure from SE incentives; and (d) LGU fiscal impact assessment.

## SECTION VII: SHARI'AH ANALYSIS

### A. Shari'ah Basis in the Bangsamoro Organic Law

The Bangsamoro Organic Law (RA 11054), as the supreme law of the Bangsamoro, recognizes the role of Shari'ah as an integral part of the Bangsamoro system of governance and justice. Article IX, Section 3 establishes the Shari'ah courts as part of the Bangsamoro judiciary. The BOL's protection of Islamic institutions and practices provides the constitutional foundation for PB 210's Shari'ah integration provisions.

### B. Shari'ah Provisions in PB 210

**1. Foundational Shari'ah Principles (Book I, Sec. 7)**

PB 210 is the first Bangsamoro legislation to codify Islamic principles as binding standards for cooperatives and SEs under the Shari'ah Governance Framework. The verbatim text:

> **[Book I, Sec. 7]** — *"Cooperatives and social enterprises covered by the Shari'ah Governance Framework shall abide by the following Islamic principles:*
> *(a) Fulfillment of the objectives of Shari'ah (maqasid al-Shari'ah) such as preservation of faith, preservation of life, preservation of lineage, preservation of intellect, and preservation of wealth.*
> *(b) Promotion of halaal or permissible transactions, products, and services.*
> *(c) Generation of wealth from legitimate business and asset-based transactions.*
> *(d) Avoidance of harm and sharing of risks in business and financial transactions.*
> *(e) Prohibition of usury or interest-based transactions (riba), gambling and games of chances (maysir), speculative business and financial transactions or those that involve too much uncertainty or extreme risks (gharar), and haram products and services."*

**What these principles mean in practice:** The five maqasid al-Shari'ah codified in Sec. 7(a) are the classical objectives of Islamic jurisprudence: preservation of faith (*hifz al-din*), life (*hifz al-nafs*), lineage (*hifz al-nasl*), intellect (*hifz al-'aql*), and wealth (*hifz al-mal*). By embedding these in the Code, PB 210 grounds the entire cooperative and SE framework in Islamic law's higher objectives — meaning every CSEA regulation and every cooperative bylaw is subject to evaluation against these standards. Secs. 7(b)-(e) operationalize this by specifically prohibiting riba, maysir, and gharar — the three primary Shari'ah prohibitions in commercial transactions.

**2. Shari'ah Governance Framework (Book I, Sec. 9)**

> **[Book I, Sec. 9]** — *"Shari'ah governance refers to structures and processes adopted by an institution to ensure compliance with Islamic principles and Shari'ah rules in its business operations. The Authority shall adopt Shari'ah governance framework and policies to guide the compliance of the cooperatives and social enterprises. This shall include the establishment of a Shari'ah committee in the cooperatives composed of at least three members who have competence on Shari'ah. Likewise, the Shari'ah governance framework and policies shall prescribe responsive measures for compliance of social enterprises to Islamic principles and Shari'ah rules based on their size, scale, structure, and/or form of business."*

**What this means:** The CSEA is mandated to adopt a framework governing Shari'ah compliance across the sector. The key operational requirement is the Shari'ah committee of at least three (3) qualified members within each covered cooperative. For social enterprises, the framework is calibrated to "size, scale, structure, and/or form of business" — meaning a micro SE faces different compliance requirements than a large cooperative. [NEEDS VERIFICATION — the BSP Shari'ah governance framework referenced in some sources as a model has not been verified against project files; this analysis is based solely on Book I, Sec. 9 of PB 210.]

**3. Islamic Finance for Social Enterprises (Book III, Sec. 13)**

Social enterprises under PB 210 may be financed through **Islamic Finance instruments** — described in the Code as financial organizations and arrangements with risk-sharing as their core element, founded on risk sharing rather than speculation in compliance with Shari'ah principles. The permitted instruments include:

- **Grants** (from public and private sources)
- **Loans** (at no interest / riba-compliant)
- **Islamic Finance instruments** — including murabaha, ijara, musharaka, mudaraba, and similar Shari'ah-compliant modalities as may be approved by the CSEA's Shari'ah committee
- **Sadaqah** (voluntary charity) — encouraged as a strategic, empowering, and sustainable SE financing source
- **Zakat** (obligatory poor tax) — explicitly permitted to finance and support social enterprises serving the poor
- **Waqf** (Islamic trust/endowment) — explicitly permitted as a sustainable SE financing mechanism

The incorporation of **zakat and waqf** as legal SE financing instruments is a significant Bangsamoro-specific innovation. Zakat funds — traditionally managed by local mosque committees and Islamic organizations — can now be channeled into registered SEs, subject to CSEA oversight. Waqf assets can be structured as productive endowments supporting SE operations, aligning Bangsamoro's rich Islamic philanthropic tradition with its economic development goals.

**4. Shari'ah-Compliant Financing Program (Book IV, Sec. 5a)**

The most operationally specific Shari'ah provision is the mandatory **Shari'ah Compliant Financing Program for Startup and Micro Cooperatives and Social Enterprises** — a program that CSEA, MFBM, MAFAR, and MTIT are required to implement. This program:
- Specifically targets **startup and micro cooperatives and SEs** — the most underserved segment in BARMM's financial ecosystem
- Pairs Shari'ah-compliant financing with **capacity development** to ensure business-readiness (Book IV, Sec. 5b)
- Addresses the fundamental barrier these entities face: commercial banks require conventional interest-bearing loans, which Muslim borrowers cannot take without violating riba prohibitions

**5. Prohibition of Riba and Harmful Transactions (Book I, Sec. 7)**

All cooperatives and SEs under the Shari'ah Governance Framework are bound by:
- Prohibition of **riba** (interest/usury) — including in their financing arrangements, bylaws, and capital structures
- Prohibition of **maysir** (gambling and games of chance)
- Prohibition of **gharar** (speculative transactions with excessive uncertainty or extreme risk)
- Prohibition of **haram** products and services

### C. Assessment of Shari'ah Consistency

**Consistent with Shari'ah principles:** PB 210's cooperative principles, social enterprise framework, Islamic finance provisions, and Shari'ah governance requirements are broadly consistent with classical and contemporary Islamic jurisprudence on cooperative economics (*ta'awun*, *musharaka*) and social responsibility (*maslaha al-'amma*). The explicit maqasid al-Shari'ah framework grounds the entire Code in the higher objectives of Islamic law.

**Point for Further Clarification:** The Code defines the Shari'ah committee requirement for cooperatives (at least 3 members with Shari'ah competence) but does not specify the standards or body that certifies "competence on Shari'ah." A Shari'ah governance IRR or a formal arrangement with the Shari'ah Advisory Council of BARMM (or equivalent) would be needed to operationalize this requirement. This point may be raised during committee hearings or second reading deliberations.

**No Shari'ah Conflict Identified** in the main provisions reviewed.

## SECTION VIII: GENDER ANALYSIS

### A. Gender-Relevant Provisions

**1. Open Membership Principle (Book I, Sec. 5)**
Cooperative membership is explicitly guaranteed without "gender, social, racial, cultural, political, or religious discrimination" (Voluntary and Open Membership Principle). This provision prohibits gender-based exclusion from cooperative membership.

**2. Democratic Member Control — Women in Leadership (Book I, Sec. 5)**
The democratic governance principle specifies: *"Men and women serving as elected representatives, directors or officers are accountable to the membership."* The explicit mention of women underscores parliamentary intent for gender-balanced cooperative governance.

**3. Gender Sensitivity in RISE for Peace (Book III, Sec. 8)**
One of the seven foundational principles of the RISE for Peace Program is: *"Gender-sensitivity by ensuring women's equal rights and access to SE resources."* This is not a general aspiration — it is a **mandatory programming principle** that shapes how RISE for Peace activities, budgets, and service delivery must be designed.

**4. Women Empowerment as SE Sector (Book III, Sec. 1)**
**Women empowerment** is one of the 11 explicitly defined sectors in which social enterprises may operate under PB 210. This creates a dedicated ecosystem for SEs that specifically address women's economic, social, and political empowerment.

**5. Social Enterprise Education — Gender Justice (Book III, Sec. 12)**
The SE education program for primary and secondary education is required to include **"gender justice, or the full equality and equity between men and women in all spheres of life, resulting in women jointly, and on an equal basis with men, defining and shaping the policies, structures, and decisions that affect their lives and society as a whole."** This is a strong gender equality standard embedded in the mandated SE curriculum.

**6. Multiple Bottom-Lines Principle — Social and Environmental Impact (Book I, Sec. 6)**
The multiple bottom-lines principle for SEs requires measurable social and environmental impacts. In the BARMM context, gender equity metrics are a natural component of "social impact" measurement.

**7. BSEC Composition — Bangsamoro Women Commission (Book III, Sec. 6)**
The Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC) is one of the 9 government bodies represented in the BSEC. This ensures a gender-responsive perspective is embedded in the SE sector's primary advisory body.

### B. Gender Gaps to Note

**1. No Gender Quota in CSEA Governance or BSEC Representation**
PB 210 does not establish a gender quota for CSEA leadership or BSEC SE representative seats. Based on documented patterns of gender representation in BARMM governance bodies, the risk is that elected BSEC representatives (particularly the 9 province/city SE representatives) may be predominantly male. `[Analysis — for specific gender participation data in BARMM governance, cite BARMM GAD office reports or BDP Chapter 9]` The Committee on Cooperatives and SEs should consider adding a gender balance provision — for example, requiring at least 3 of the 9 SE representatives to be women — at second reading.

**2. No Mandatory GAD Budget for CSEA**
No specific Gender and Development (GAD) budget allocation is required for CSEA or BCSED. While Section 8 mandates gender sensitivity in RISE for Peace programming, this should be reinforced with a GAD budget line aligned with BARMM's GAD policies.

**3. Muslim Women in Cooperatives — Specific Support Absent**
Muslim women in BARMM face specific barriers to cooperative participation: mobility constraints, cultural norms around financial transactions, and limited financial literacy. While PB 210's open membership principle prohibits gender discrimination, it does not affirmatively address these barriers with targeted programs. The SE Capability Building Program (Book III, Sec. 9a) should explicitly include women-specific training tracks.

### C. Overall Gender Assessment

**Rating: Partially Gender-Responsive.** PB 210 is significantly stronger on gender than RA 9520 (the national Cooperative Code) — the explicit women's empowerment sector, gender justice in SE education, gender sensitivity in RISE for Peace, and BWC in the BSEC are all advances. The gaps are primarily in gender parity mechanisms (quotas), GAD budgeting, and targeted programs for Muslim women. These can be addressed through amendments at second reading or through CSEA's IRR.

## SECTION X: INTERPELLATION Q&A
*[For [MP NAME] or any Member of Parliament conducting interpellation on PB 210]*
*[Questions are organized by theme. Suggested answers reflect the bill's text. Proposing MP's answers should be adapted based on the actual committee report or their personal knowledge of the bill.]*

### THEME A: LEGAL BASIS AND MANDATE

**Q1. What is the legal basis for Parliament's authority to enact the Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code?**

A: The Bangsamoro Organic Law provides an explicit dual legislative mandate — two separate provisions, both citing legislation by name or subject matter:

> **[BOL, Art. XIII, Sec. 24]** — *"It shall adopt and implement cooperative development policies and programs through the enactment of a Bangsamoro cooperative code."*

> **[BOL, Art. XIII, Sec. 27]** — *"The Bangsamoro Government shall recognize, protect, and promote the establishment of cooperative enterprises. It shall likewise encourage and promote social entrepreneurship as a means to engage the private sector to assist in the development, funding, and implementation of solutions to social, cultural, or environmental issues. The Parliament shall pass laws to strengthen the growth and development of social enterprises in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region to achieve social justice, social equity, and economic efficiency."*

> **[BOL, Art. V, Sec. 2(k)]** — *"Cooperatives and social entrepreneurship;"* [enumerated power of the Bangsamoro Government]

**What this means:** PB 210 fulfills both Art. XIII Sec. 24 (Cooperative Code mandate) and Art. XIII Sec. 27 (Social Enterprise laws mandate) simultaneously in a single consolidated Code, while exercising the enumerated power in Art. V Sec. 2(k). This is not a discretionary exercise of legislative power — it is a mandated one.

**Q2. How does PB 210 relate to the national Philippine Cooperative Code (RA 9520)?**

A: Section 12, Book IV of PB 210 provides that all cooperatives registered with the CDA under RA 9520 and RA 11364 are deemed registered under the BCSEC upon submission of their documents to the CSEA within 1 year of effectivity. RA 9520 will continue to apply as suppletory law to BARMM cooperatives where not inconsistent with the BCSEC, pending full transition to CSEA jurisdiction. The CDA retains registration authority over BARMM cooperatives until the CSEA is fully empowered and operational.

**Q3. Does PB 210 conflict with the national government's jurisdiction over cooperatives?**

A: No. The BOL specifically grants the Bangsamoro Government exclusive authority to enact its own cooperative code. RA 11364 (2019) further clarified that the CDA's jurisdiction extends to the Bangsamoro only "until such time as the Bangsamoro Parliament enacts its own cooperative legislation." PB 210 is the exercise of that granted authority, which is constitutionally anchored and nationally recognized.

### THEME B: CSEA ESTABLISHMENT AND OPERATIONS

**Q4. The CSEA was already created under BAA No. 13. What does PB 210 add?**

A: BAA No. 13 created the CSEA as an agency structure and assigned it to the OCM. However, BAA No. 13 did not provide CSEA with the substantive powers to actually register cooperatives, issue certificates of compliance, accredit social enterprises, or enforce the Code's provisions. PB 210 is CSEA's **enabling legislation** — without it, the CSEA exists as a shell without legal authority to act. PB 210 provides the full scope of CSEA's mandate, powers, organizational structure, and enforcement tools.

**Q5. How will the CSEA handle the transition of the existing cooperative registry from the CDA?**

A: Section 12, Book IV provides the transition mechanism: all CDA-registered BARMM cooperatives must submit their registration documents (certificate of registration, articles of cooperation, bylaws, latest audited financial statements) to the nearest CSEA office within 1 year from effectivity. CSEA will issue new certificates of registration. Any cooperative that fails to submit within 1 year will have its registration cancelled *motu proprio*. The BIR and CSEA will jointly issue regulations on the tax exemption transition within 90 days.

**Q6. What is the CSEA's Shari'ah governance function? How is this different from the existing Shari'ah courts?**

A: The Shari'ah courts under the BOL adjudicate disputes between Muslims and apply Islamic personal law. CSEA's Shari'ah governance function is entirely different — it is a regulatory function ensuring that cooperatives and SEs subject to the Shari'ah Governance Framework conduct their business in accordance with Islamic principles. This includes requiring a Shari'ah committee within each covered cooperative (minimum 3 qualified members) and prescribing compliance measures for SEs. CSEA's Shari'ah governance role is analogous to that of the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas' Shari'ah governance framework for Islamic banks — it is prudential/regulatory, not adjudicatory.

### THEME C: SOCIAL ENTERPRISES AND RISE FOR PEACE

**Q7. What is a social enterprise under PB 210, and how is it different from a regular business?**

A: Under Section 4, Book I, a social enterprise is a business organization whose **primary purpose** is addressing a social, cultural, or environmental challenge — where profit creation is secondary. It must: (a) have a clear social purpose in its governing documents; (b) reinvest a significant proportion of profits into the business for social, cultural, or environmental purposes; and (c) operate according to social enterprise principles (social mission, multiple bottom-lines, sustainable/inclusive/equitable development). A regular for-profit business may address social issues incidentally — a social enterprise makes that the *primary* reason it exists.

**Q8. Who qualifies for the RISE for Peace program? Can non-Muslims participate?**

A: There is no religion-based restriction in the RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program (Book III, Secs. 8-9). Any social enterprise registered and accredited by CSEA that serves the poor, marginalized, and disadvantaged communities of BARMM — regardless of the religion of its founders or members — may participate in RISE for Peace programming. The program's explicit principles include social inclusion, gender sensitivity, and development of indigenous peoples' communities, reflecting its inclusive design.

**Q9. The bill connects cooperatives and SEs to ex-combatant reintegration. How specific is this mandate?**

A: Section 13(f), Book I mandates CSEA to "promote and strengthen cooperatives and social enterprises as pathways to reintegrate former combatants, surrenderees, and disaster-affected communities, among others." This is a substantive CSEA function — not a general aspiration. It means CSEA must include reintegration programming in its annual work plan, allocate staff to it, and coordinate with relevant Bangsamoro agencies (MSSD, MILG, and the Bangsamoro Transition Commission's successor bodies) on reintegration programs that use cooperatives and SEs as the economic vehicle.

### THEME D: FINANCING AND INCENTIVES

**Q10. How exactly will zakat and waqf be used to finance social enterprises? Who controls these funds?**

A: Section 13, Book III permits Islamic philanthropy — including sadaqah, zakat, and waqf — as financing and support mechanisms for social enterprises. The bill establishes the principle but leaves the operational framework to CSEA through its Shari'ah governance IRR. In practice: a waqf could be established as a productive endowment (e.g., a building or equipment) whose usufruct supports an SE; zakat collected by mosque committees or zakah institutions can be directed to registered SEs that serve the poor. The CSEA will need to coordinate with Islamic charity organizations and the proposed Bangsamoro zakah management framework to operationalize this provision. Control of these funds remains with the contributing Islamic institution or donor — CSEA's role is to verify SE eligibility for receiving these funds.

**Q11. The bill mandates LGUs to allocate 5% of their funds for SEs. Isn't this fiscal overreach? Can the Bangsamoro Parliament mandate LGU expenditures?**

A: The Bangsamoro Government has fiscal supervisory authority over local government units within BARMM under the BOL. Section 11(c), Book III frames the 5% allocation as a responsibility of LGUs in SE development — aligned with their local development planning mandate. This is similar to existing national mandates (e.g., Local Youth Development Code mandating LGU youth fund allocations). The fiscal autonomy of BARMM LGUs is not absolute — it is exercised within the framework of Bangsamoro law. That said, the implementing rules should clearly define: what counts as "LGU funds" for this purpose; whether the 5% is from the General Fund or specific fund sources; and what reporting mechanism ensures compliance.

**Q12. What is the tax holiday for social enterprises, and how long does it last?**

A: Under Section 18, Book III, social enterprises that are not micro SEs and not cooperatives shall be **exempt from paying regional taxes, fees, and charges under the Bangsamoro Revenue Code for the first three (3) years of their operation**. LGUs shall also either reduce or exempt such SEs from local taxes for the same period. After 3 years, they transition to the full incentive regime (VAT/tariff exemptions on equipment imports, income tax deductibility for reinvestments, preferential procurement rights). Micro SEs have a permanent income tax exemption with no time limit.

### THEME E: PENAL PROVISIONS AND ENFORCEMENT

**Q13. The bill provides for imprisonment for cooperative violations. Is this proportionate?**

A: PB 210 provides a graduated set of penalties: 6 months to 1 year for minor administrative violations (e.g., non-submission of reports); 2-5 years for use of the word "cooperative" without registration; 2-4 years for tax evasion; 5-10 years for disloyalty by directors and officers or illegal use of confidential information; 6 months to 2 years for SE provision violations. The range is comparable to the national Cooperative Code's penal structure. The specific thresholds and penalty ranges should be reviewed during committee hearings to ensure proportionality — particularly the 5-10 year maximum for director/officer disloyalty, which is higher than what many commercial corporation laws impose.

**Q14. How will CSEA enforce the Code in BARMM's remote municipalities, many of which have no government presence?**

A: The bill addresses this through two mechanisms: (1) CSEA shall coordinate with municipalities that have their own Cooperative Development Offices (Book I, Sec. 13); and (2) Local Cooperative Development Offices at the municipal level are part of CSEA's operational structure. In practice, CSEA will face significant enforcement challenges in geographically isolated communities. The implementation plan should include mobile CSEA teams, online registration/reporting systems (addressed in Book IV, Secs. 2-3), and a graduated compliance approach for cooperatives in conflict-affected or remote areas.

## SECTION XI: PROPOSED AMENDMENTS

The following amendments are proposed for consideration at second reading. They are classified as **strengthening amendments** (adding what the bill currently lacks) rather than substantive policy changes.

### Amendment Set A — Gender and Inclusion

**Amendment A-1: BSEC Gender Balance**

*Proposed amendment to Book III, Sec. 6 (BSEC Composition):*

After the clause listing the 9 SE representatives, insert: *"Provided, That no gender shall be represented by fewer than three (3) of the nine (9) provincial/city SE representatives; Provided, further, That CSEA shall establish mechanisms to encourage and support women and members of indigenous peoples communities to seek nomination as BSEC representatives."*

**Rationale:** Without this provision, gender imbalance in the BSEC is likely given existing social structures. The BWC is represented in the government contingent, but the sector contingent (where actual SE voices are represented) has no gender balance requirement.

*Proposed amendment to Book III, Sec. 9(a) (SE Capability Building and Sustainability Program):*

### Amendment Set B — LGU Implementation

**Amendment B-1: LGU Compliance Reporting**

*Proposed new subsection in Book III, Sec. 11:*

*"(e) Submit annually to the CSEA a utilization report on the five percent (5%) fund allocation required under paragraphs (c) and (d) of this Section, in a format prescribed by the CSEA in coordination with the CSEA Committee on Cooperatives and Social Enterprises."*

**Rationale:** The 5% LGU allocation mandate has no reporting or enforcement mechanism. Without it, compliance will be unverifiable.

### Amendment Set C — Shari'ah Governance

**Amendment C-1: Shari'ah Committee Competency Standards**

*Proposed amendment to Book I, Sec. 9 (Shari'ah Governance):*

After the Shari'ah committee requirement, insert: *"The CSEA, in consultation with the Office of the Mufti and the Shari'ah Advisory Council of the Bangsamoro Government, shall issue guidelines establishing the minimum qualifications and competency standards for members of Shari'ah committees required under this Section."*

**Rationale:** "Competence on Shari'ah" is currently undefined. Without standards, any person could be declared Shari'ah-competent — defeating the purpose of the requirement.

### Amendment Set D — Appropriations

**Amendment D-1: BSEF Initial Capitalization**

*Proposed amendment to Book III, Sec. 14 (BSEF):*

After the establishment clause, insert: *"The initial capitalization of the BSEF shall be not less than [AMOUNT] Pesos, appropriated from the Bangsamoro Special Development Fund within sixty (60) days from the effectivity of this Code."*

**Rationale:** The BSEF has no minimum capitalization — leaving it as an unfunded mandate. Parliament should specify an initial amount (with MFBM's recommendation) or at minimum set a timeline for the initial appropriation.

## SECTION XIII: FOR INFORMATION

### Key Background Facts for Members of Parliament

**1. What is a Cooperative?**

A cooperative is an autonomous and democratically controlled business enterprise voluntarily formed by people with common economic, social, and cultural needs. Its defining features are:
- Member-owned and member-controlled (one member, one vote)
- Member-beneficiary (surplus distributed to members in proportion to patronage, not capital contribution)
- Open and voluntary membership (no discrimination based on gender, religion, politics)
- Dedicated to education, cooperation, and community concern

The International Cooperative Alliance (ICA) — the global apex body for cooperatives — has established the 7 cooperative principles that PB 210 codifies. These principles have been accepted worldwide since 1995 and are the global standard for cooperative governance.

**2. What is a Social Enterprise?**

A social enterprise is a business that exists primarily to address a social, cultural, or environmental problem. Unlike a traditional corporation that measures success by profit maximization, a social enterprise measures success by: (a) social impact achieved; (b) financial sustainability; and (c) environmental responsibility. Profit is reinvested into the social mission, not distributed as dividends.

Social enterprises operate in the space between government (which delivers public services) and private business (which maximizes shareholder returns). They use market mechanisms to finance social goods — without depending on government grants or charity.

**3. Why Does BARMM Need This Code?**

BARMM is one of the most impoverished regions in the Philippines, with poverty incidence significantly above the national average. `[NEEDS VERIFICATION — cite BDP 2023-2028 or PSA data for specific figure]` The cooperative and social enterprise sectors represent proven pathways for:
- Creating sustainable livelihoods in conflict-affected communities
- Providing alternative economic structures for communities that distrust large corporations
- Delivering social services (healthcare, education, housing, financial services) in areas where government presence is limited
- Empowering women, youth, indigenous peoples, and other marginalized groups through democratic economic participation

The national CDA framework (RA 9520) was designed primarily for the central Philippine context and does not address BARMM's specific conditions: the role of Islamic finance, the presence of large populations of former combatants seeking livelihoods, the importance of waqf and zakat institutions, and the geographical isolation of BARMM's municipalities.

**4. The Cooperative Sector in BARMM — Current Status**

BARMM has a significant but underdeveloped cooperative sector. Cooperatives in BARMM currently operate in agriculture, fisheries, consumer goods, credit and microfinance, and transport — but penetration rates are far below the national average, and many cooperatives are informal or unregistered. The absence of a BARMM-specific registration authority accessible to local communities has been a major barrier to cooperative formation.

**5. The Social Enterprise Ecosystem in BARMM**

Social enterprises in BARMM include: Muslim women's handicraft enterprises, peace-oriented livelihood programs of NGOs and CSOs, agricultural producer groups, disaster-resilient livelihood cooperatives, and youth employment enterprises. Many of these operate informally without legal SE status, limiting their access to government support, tax incentives, and impact investment. PB 210's SE accreditation system will formalize this ecosystem.

**6. BOL Legislative Mandates — Status as of PB 210 Filing**

The BOL contains multiple named legislative mandates that Parliament is required to enact. As of the filing of PB 210 (June 2023):
- BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 24 (Bangsamoro Cooperative Code) — **Unfulfilled → PB 210 addresses this**
- BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 27 (Social Enterprise Laws) — **Unfulfilled → PB 210 addresses this**

Parliament's passage of PB 210 will fulfill both of these explicit BOL mandates simultaneously.

**7. Relationship to the Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028**

The Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP) 2023-2028 identifies the cooperative and social enterprise sectors as key components of the Bangsamoro economic recovery and development strategy. PB 210's enactment is aligned with BDP targets for rural economic development, poverty reduction, and employment generation in BARMM.
