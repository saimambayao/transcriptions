EXPLANATORY NOTE

On March 6, 1906, 800 to 1,000 Tausug villagers were massacred inside the 50-foot
crater of Bud Dajo — a dormant volcano six to seven kilometers away from Jolo, Sulu. The
victims were a community of Tausugs who fled to Bud Dajo in defiance of the United States’
occupation of Mindanao.

Despite being out-gunned and overpowered, the freedom-loving Tausugs chose to fight,
and sacrifice their lives, rather than submit to American rule by paying taxes or surrendering
their weapons. Seldom in history do we find such bravery as that displayed by the Tausugs at
Bud Dajo, in their adherence to defending “hulah, bangsa, iban agama (homeland, nation, and
faith).”

Based on reports at the time, the American colonial government preferred to call the
massacre a “battle” but many American personalities, such as social critic Samuel Clemens--
who is also known by his pen name, Mark Twain--and American Historian Vic Hurley, called
the encounter a massacre. American soldiers stormed the high mountain peak to kill 1,000
Muslims, an assault that also resulted in the deaths of 21 American soldiers. Whether it was a
battle or massacre, this was certainly the bloodiest engagement of the Tausug resistance against
foreign invaders.

Clemens wrote a scathing piece in 1906 about the Bud Dajo massacre, in which he said
"'Slaughter' is a good word. Certainly, there is not a better one in the Unabridged Dictionary
for this occasion."

According to Hurley's account: “After the engagement the crater was a shamble. Moros
were piled five-deep in the trenches where they had been mowed down by the artillery and rifle
fire. The American attack had been supported by two quick-firing guns from the gunboat
Pampanga and examination of the dead showed that many of the Moros had as many as 50
wounds. Of the 1,000 Moros who opened the battle two days previously, only six men survived
the carnage.”

This fight to the death for freedom resonates the main aspiration of any human: To be
free, and to hold that freedom to the last breath, with all the courage one can muster to do so.

Today, the Bud Dajo massacre continues to inspire the Tausugs in their struggle for
self-determination and the quest for peace. This bill seeks to rectify the government’s neglect
in marking this important historical event. The government should hold commemorative
activities of the massacre so that the people, especially the youth, can better appreciate the
history of our struggle.

Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament in session:
Section 1. Short Title. — This Act shall be known as the “Bud Dajo Day Act of 2022.”

Section 2. Declaration of Policy. — The Bangsamoro Government hereby recognizes
the significant importance of transitional justice mechanisms as tools in addressing the
legitimate grievances of the Bangsamoro people which can contribute to achieving the broader
objectives of prevention of further conflict, peacebuilding and reconciliation.

Towards this end, it shall seek to identify legitimate causes to address the underlying
source of conflict and entrenched forms of marginalization of the Bangsamoro towards
recognizing their dignity, restoring confidence and preventing the recurrence of similar human
rights violations.

As such, it is hereby declared to be the policy of the Bangsamoro Government to take
account of the origins of Bangsamoro conflicts and recognize the preservation of significant
Bangsamoro historical events, such as the heroism that was displayed by the Bangsamoro
people against American military attacks on Bud Dajo, Sulu in 1906. In recognition of the
bravery that was displayed, March 6 of every year is hereby declared as “Bud Dajo Day.”

Section 3. The following activities shall be undertaken in observance of this occasion:

a. Heads of BARMM government agencies and instrumentalities, government-owned
and controlled corporations, together with their employees, shall organize and
participate in activities designed to raise public awareness on the events in Bud
Dajo, Sulu on March 6, 1906, and its continuing significance to the Bangsamoro
people; and

b. The Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage (BCPCH)
and the Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education, in coordination with
non-governmental organizations advocating for human rights, shall lead public and
private school communities at all levels in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in
organizing consciousness-raising activities on Bud Dajo Day.

Section 4. Implementing Agency. Pursuant to the observance of the Bud Dajo Day,
the annual program of activities shall be prepared and implemented by all BARMM
government agencies, and by the concerned high schools, colleges and universities, which shall
be monitored by the Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage and
the Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education, as the case may be.

Section 5. Implementing Rules and Regulations. The BCPCH, within ninety (90)
days from the approval of this Act, shall promulgate the implementing rules and regulations
for the effective implementation of this Act, in consultation and coordination with the Ministry
of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education and non-governmental organizations advocating for
human rights.

Section 6. Effectivity. This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days following its
publication in a newspaper of regional circulation.
