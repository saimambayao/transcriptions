The Philippines is a country prone to natural calamities and disasters,
especially with the worsening effects of global warming and climate change. In the
Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, it is a woeful reality that we are beset by mother
nature’s worst: typhoons, floods, landslides, earthquakes, droughts, and the like, man-

made hazards like widespread fires, and even more sadly, the very worst of human-
induced disasters, the terrible tribulations brought about by extremist militant groups.

As an awful offshoot of these disasters, so many people lose their livelihood,
their loved ones, and their homes. We have seen so many people flee from their homes
because of these disasters, and as a result, they become what are called “internally
displaced persons” or IDPs. According to the United Nations’ Office of the High
Commissioner on Human Rights (OHCHR), citing the Guiding Principles on Internal
Displacement, IDPs are persons or groups of persons who have been forced or obliged
to flee or to leave their homes or places of habitual residence, in particular as a result
of or in order to avoid the effects of armed conflict, situations of generalized violence,
violations of human rights or natural or human-made disasters, and who have not
crossed an internationally recognized border.”!

A prime example and one of the worst cases of displacement that our region
has experienced began in May of 2017, when an awful crisis dawned upon the City of
Marawi. According to Bermudez, et. al., in an article written in 2018 titled “A disaster
approach to displacement: IDPs in the Philippines” by the Inter-American Court of Human
Rights, an armed encounter between the Philippines’ armed forces and the Islamic
State-inspired Maute Group that lasted for five months displaced around 360,000
people from Marawi. In the same article, it is acknowledged that the Philippines has
no laws relating directly to IDPs and instead, there are only legal guidelines for the
State’s response in cases of displacement, based on Republic Act (R.A.) No. 10121,
otherwise known as the Philippine Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Act of
2010. However, the law has its limitations, like being about structures and response
actors, and not enough about displaced persons.”

This proposed legislative measure attempts to address an essential question
that directly pertains to displaced persons. Given that they are forced to flee their
homes and as they wait for their area to be declared safe to return to or while
rebuilding or restoring efforts are still ongoing, where do displaced persons go?
Where can they seek shelter? Where can they be kept safe whilst they are away from
their homes?

R.A. No. 11054 or the Bangsamoro Organic Law (BOL), under Article V, Section
2(s), vests authority to the Bangsamoro Government over matters involving disaster
risk reduction and management, without prejudice to the general supervision of the
President. Moreover, Section 3 of the same article provides for the important provision
on general welfare. It provides:

Section 3. General Welfare. - The Bangsamoro Government shall exercise the powers expressly
granted, those necessarily implied therefrom, as well as powers necessary, appropriate, or
incidental for its efficient and effective governance and those which are essential to the
promotion of general welfare. Within its territorial jurisdiction, the Bangsamoro Government
shall ensure and support, among other things, the preservation and enrichment of culture,
promote health and safety, enhance the right of the people to a balanced ecology, encourage

In relation to this provision, Article VIL, Section 5{a) of the BOL cites as one of the

and ensure the general welfare of the Bangsamoro people and other inhabitants in the

Section 8. Right of Access to Basic Services.— The Bangsamoro Government shall provide,
maintain, and ensure the delivery of basic and responsive health programs, quality education,
appropniate services, livelihood opportunities, affordable and progressive housing projects,

maintain appropriate disaster-preparedness units for immediate and effective relief services

calamity-affected areas and victims of calamities.

More than these provisions, Article XIII, Section 7 of the same law states “[t]he
Bangsamoro Government recognizes the importance of disaster risk reduction and
management and climate change adaptation.”

To answer the basic questions cited before, in current practice, most often,
schools, gymnasiums, covered courts, and multipurpose buildings serve as
evacuation centers during disasters. Furthermore, there are times when temporary
tents made of indigenous materials and recycled items such as used tarpaulins, and
plastic items are being used as temporary shelter of IDPs during disasters especially
human-induced disasters, with limited access to water, plumbing, and sanitation.
These are not conducive places to temporarily take shelter in.

IDPs are already suffering enough with so many losses on their part. Their
family life has been disrupted, their security is at risk, the children’s education is
interrupted, elderly people and people with special needs are all the more challenged
with their disabilities, and their health is compromised from being exposed to such
disasters places them in a very vulnerable position. The least that the government can
do for them and their dignity is to provide clean, safe, and spacious places for them to
temporarily stay at. They should have access to plumbing and to clean water, to
facilities that cater to women, especially mothers who are breastfeeding, and elderly
people and people with special needs, and overall, to an acceptable temporary living
situation.

In view of the foregoing, the approval of this measure is earnestly sought.

Be it enacted by the Parliament of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim
Mindanao assembled:

Section 1. Title. - This Act shall be known as the “Bangsamoro Evacuation
Center Act of 2022”.

Section 2. Declaration of Policy. - It is a declared policy of the Bangsamoro
Government to ensure the safety and general welfare of its constituents especially in

Section 3. Objectives. - This Act generally aims to strengthen disaster
preparedness and response of the Bangsamoro Government. Specifically, it has the
following objectives:

a) Come up with a standard design of evacuation centers in the Bangsamoro
Autonomous Region, based on the building code of the Philippines;

f} Serve as the safe space for the communities affected by disaster especially
within the area of jurisdiction.

Section 4. Establishment of Evacuation Centers. - The Bangsamoro
Government shall establish in every municipality or city a standard evacuation center
for disaster preparedness and response. The standard design and construction shall

be prepared by the Ministry of Public Works (MPW) in Bangsamoro Autonomous
Region.

Each evacuation center shall be located in safe and accessible spaces of the
municipality or city.

The evacuation center shall accommodate at least 100 to 200 internally
displaced persons (IDPs) with due consideration on the standard protocols for

Section 5. Facilities in the Evacuation Centers. - The evacuation centers shall
be equipped with proper ventilation, kitchen, water, plumbing, sanitation support
structures, and facilities that will cater to women, especially breastfeeding mothers, as
well as for the elderly and those with special needs.

Section 6. Evacuation Center's Management. - The evacuation centers shall be
under the direct supervision of the Ministry of Social Services and Development
(MSSD) and shall be managed by the Municipal or City Social Welfare Officers
(M/CSWDOs) in their respective areas.

The center shall have a designated office for the M/CSWDOs, which will serve
as their municipal or city office.

Section 7. Complementation for the Operation and Maintenance. — The local
government unit shall complement the manpower of the MSSD through collaboration
with the Municipal Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Officer (MDRRMO) for
the operation and maintenance of the evacuation center.

Section 8. Coverage. - This Act shall cover all municipalities and cities within
the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. In case of the Department of Social Welfare and
Development in the province of Basilan and the city of Marawi, which services were
already devolved to the local government units, the said LGUs are encouraged to
implement this Act.

Section 9. Appropriations. - The funds necessary for the establishment of
evacuation centers and the corresponding operation and maintenance shall be
included in the annual appropriations of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.

Section 10. Separability Clause. - If for any cause, any part of this Act is
declared unconstitutional or contrary to the provisions of Bangsamoro Organic Law,
the rest of the provisions shall remain in force and effect.

Section 11. Repealing Clause. - All laws, orders, rules, and regulations which
are inconsistent with the provisions of this Act, are hereby repealed, modified,
superseded, or amended accordingly.

Section 12. Effectivity. - This Act shall take effect after fifteen (15) days
following its full publication in a newspaper of general circulation in the Bangsamoro
Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao.
