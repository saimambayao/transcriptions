EXPLANATORY NOTE

Physical fitness is an essential component of overall health and well-being. Research
shows that regular physical activity not only improves physical health but also contributes to
mental and emotional well-being. Taking up exercise reduces the risk of developing mental
illness. It also helps in treating some mental health conditions, like depression and anxiety. For
example, for mild-moderate depression, research suggests physical activity can be as effective
as antidepressants or psychological treatments like cognitive behavioral therapy. Exercise can
also be a valuable addition to other treatment options.’

The Bangsamoro Government, through the Bangsamoro Organic Law, promises to

protect and promote the right to health of the people and instill health consciousness among
them.”

It is also enshrined under Republic Act No. 11036 (R.A. 11036), or the “Mental Health

Employees render a minimum of 40 work hours a week. Due to the fatigue proximately

themselves for their physical conditioning.

As the Bangsamoro Region breaks the stigma surrounding mental health in the
workplace, it is necessary to establish programs that not only raise awareness about mental
health issues but also provide solutions to this longstanding problem. And thus, the
submission of this bill.

This Act seeks to promote a culture of physical fitness among the workers of
Bangsamoro Region, through its ministries, offices, and agencies. It aims to encourage
individuals to engage in regular physical activity, which can lead to improved health outcomes,
increased productivity and enhanced overall quality of life. It is hoped that this measure will
not only improve the health and well-being of individuals but also contribute to the overall
development of the Bangsamoro region.

Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority in Parliament assembled:
SECTION 1. Short Title. This act shall be known as the “PHYSICAL FITNESS ACT OF 2024”

SECTION 2. Declaration of Policy and Principles. The Bangsamoro Government shall protect
and promote the right to health of its employees and instill health consciousness among them.

To guarantee the delivery of efficient and effective public service for the
Bangsamoro people, the health, safety, and welfare of the Bangsamoro officials and
employees must be given paramount consideration. Thus, the Bangsamoro government
must establish measures to ensure good physical condition and mitigate the risk of illness to
its officials and employees.

The Bangsamoro ministries, offices, and agencies shall conduct weekly Physical
Fitness Activities (PFA) that can lead to improved mental health, increased productivity, and
enhanced overall quality of life.

SECTION 3. Definition of Terms. For purposes of this act, the following terms shall be defined
as follows:

a. Mental Health - refers to a state of well-being in which the individual realizes
one's abilities and potentials, scopes adequately with the normal stresses of
life, displays resilience in the face of extreme life events, works productively
and fruitfully, and can make a positive contribution to the community.

b. Physical Conditioning — refers to any form of exercise that may develop physical
fitness.

c. Physical Fitness — is a set of attributes that are either health — or skill-related.

d. Skeleton workforce — a minimum number of employees needed to maintain
the operations of the ministries, offices, and agencies.

e. Wellness — the condition of good physical and mental health, especially when
actively maintained by proper diet, exercise, and avoidance of risky behavior.

SECTION 4. Scope and Coverage. This Act shall cover all Bangsamoro government employees
regardless of their status and nature of appointment.

SECTION 5. Physical Conditioning Program. All ministries, offices, and agencies in the
Bangsamoro region are hereby enjoined to conduct weekly physical conditioning activities.

The activity shall not be less than thirty (30) minutes and shall be conducted in the
afternoon of Friday, preferably at 4:30 p.m.: Provided, That the quality service of the
respective ministries, offices, and agencies will not be interrupted or be prejudiced.

There shall be a skeleton workforce to remain in the office during the conduct of physical
fitness activities. The workforce shall perform and provide the services needed to be
discharged by the respective ministries, offices, and agencies.

Bangsamoro ministries, offices, and agencies unable to accommodate physical conditioning
activities at the designated time and day may create a schedule that suits their office needs
in order to adhere to this act.

SECTION 6. Implementing Rules and Regulations. All heads of Bangsamoro ministries,
offices, agencies, and offices within the scope of this Act shall promulgate rules and
regulations to implement the provisions of this Act within 90 days from the date of its
approval.

AN ACT PROVIDING A HAVEN AND REHABILITATION CENTER FOR
WOMEN WHO ARE VICTIMS OF GENDER-BASED VIOLENCE,
PROVIDING FUNDS THEREFOR, AND FOR OTHER PURPOSES

EXPLANATORY NOTE

Gender-based violence remains a significant concern in our society, with women
often bearing the brunt of its impact. The country has established firm laws that
safeguard women's rights. There is the Magna Carta for Women, which seeks to
eliminate discrimination through the recognition, protection, fulfillment, and promotion
of the rights of Filipino women, especially those belonging to the marginalized sectors of
society. Also, there is the "Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act of 2004,”
which protects women and children from any form of violence.

The Bangsamoro Region has Bangsamoro Organic Law upholding and protecting
the fundamental rights of women, including the right to be protected from exploitation,
abuse, or discrimination.

Despite the existence of concrete laws addressing gender-based violence
concerns, VAW persists.

According to the2022 National Demographic and Health Survey
(NDHS) conducted by the Philippine Statistics Authority, 17.5% of Filipino women aged
15-49 have experienced any form of physical, sexual, and emotional violence from their
intimate partners. As of 2021, there were eight thousand three hundred ninety-nine
(8,399) reported cases of physical violence, one thousand seven hundred ninety-one
(1,791) of rape, and one thousand five hundred five (1,505) of acts of lasciviousness.

Societal norms and traditions dictate people to think that men are the leaders,
pursuers, and providers and take on the dominant roles in society. At the same time,
women are the nurturers, men’s companions, and supporters, and they take on
subordinate roles in society. This perception leads men to gain more power over women.
Hence, violence becomes a form of men’s expression of control over women to retain
power. '

Because of this long-imprinted belief, men subject women to be housekeepers,
completely dependent upon them for support. This leads women to hesitate in taking
action against the abuse they endure, fearing that leaving their homes would leave them
with nowhere to go and no resources to survive. This dependency not only perpetuates
the cycle of abuse but also traps women in harmful situations.

The implementation of this act offers a refuge for abused women seeking safety
from possible retaliation by their abusers. These centers provide a secure and
confidential environment where women can access protection without fear of being
harmed. Furthermore, during their stay, residents will have access to skills training
programs to equip them with the necessary skills to support themselves after they leave.

The Women’s Rehabilitation Center Act provides women with safe havens where
they can receive the necessary care, support, and rehabilitation to recover from their
traumatic experiences and rebuild their lives.
