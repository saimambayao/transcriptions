EXPLANATORY NOTE

The rights of the Settlers in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao are
guaranteed by Republic Act 11054 (The Organic Law for the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in
Muslim Mindanao). Sections 6-7, Article IV (General Principles and Policies) of this law
particularly provides that “[t]he Bangsamoro Government shall promote unity, peace, justice, and
goodwill among all peoples” and “shall ensure every Filipino citizen in its territorial jurisdiction
the provision of the basic necessities and equal opportunities in life xxx.” Section 10, Article IV
further provides that “[t]he freedom of choice of all people within the Bangsamoro Autonomous
Region shall be respected” and that “[t]here shall be no discrimination on the basis of identity,
religion, and ethnicity.”

Some of the basic rights of the Settlers are also embodied under Article [IX of RA 11054.
A relevant and important provision of which is that on religious freedom. Section 5 therewith
provides that “[t]he Bangsamoro Government shall guarantee religious freedom and the free
exercise thereof pursuant to the Constitution, national laws, and principles of International Law.
The Bangsamoro Government shall protect all persons from harassment or any undue pressure,
coercion, and violence on account of religion. Any establishment and institution shall be free to
implement policies and undertake activities pursuant to their respective religious beliefs and
values. Finally, Section 15, Article IX provides that “[t]he Bangsamoro Government shall ensure

However, despite the provisions of RA //054 that guarantee the rights of the Settlers in the
BARMM, there are still misconceptions and misapprehensions among some Filipino citizens
within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region with regard to the existence of the said rights or the
belief of the lack thereof. For instance, there are tendencies among some misinformed individuals
from both the Bangsamoro and the Settlers to believe that the lands owned and duly possessed or
occupied by Settlers are now owned by the Bangsamoro government by virtue of the passage of
the Bangsamoro Organic Law, and can accordingly be taken away by the latter even against the
will of the former. Thus, there is still a need for a law that will recognize, define, elaborate and
promulgate such rights.

Along this line, there is also a need to reinforce the existing Office for the Settler
Communities that is created under the Bangsamoro Autonomous Act No. 13, or the Bangsamoro
Administrative Code and to further promulgate the extent and the scope of its mandate, its powers
and functions, as well its organization and composition. Based on its experiences and operations
under its current setup for the past years since it was created under the Bangsamoro the
Administrative Code, the existing Office has encountered challenges and difficulties that have
hampered its administrative and technical operations necessary in the implementation of its
programs, activities and projects for the Settlers. With the ultimate goal of enabling it to become
a more effective agency in fulfilling its mandate and in performing its powers and functions, as
well as in implementing the said programs, activities and projects, it is a necessary for the Office
to be strengthened and in particular, to bestow it with fiscal autonomy and expanding its
administrative section to become an administrative and finance division, as well as the various
offices under it. This necessarily entails the hiring of additional personnel but with this, together
with the fiscal empowerment, it is believed that the OSC will be able to hurdle the said challenges
and difficulties, thereby truly serving as the primary agency responsible in promoting the welfare
and addressing issues and concerns of settlers in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.

In addition, there is a need to change the name Office for Settler Communities to Office of
Settlers’ Affairs. While Section 15, Article IX of RA 11054 mandated that x x x “the Parliament
shall create an office for settler communities to promote the welfare and address issues and
concerns of settlers in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region,” the name of such office is not
necessarily Office for Settler Communities. As can be seen from the quoted provision, the term
“office for settler communities” is a generic or common name and not the proper name of the
office as it was not capitalized therein. It merely mandated the Parliament to create an office for
settler communities but the name of such office can be anything as long as it signifies that it is for
the Settlers. Moreover, the preposition “for” may also be replaced with “of” to observe uniformity
in the use of this preposition in other agencies or ministries in the BARMM such as the Office of
Muslim Affairs or the Ministry of Indigenous Peoples’ Affairs. Thus, the name Office of Settlers’
Affairs is seen as a more appropriate name of the Office.

Finally, the term “Settlers” ought to be the term to use when referring to “settler
communities” not merely for the sake of consistency but in accordance to the principle that only
natural or juridical persons can possess rights. With this, settler communities cannot possess rights

ensuring the necessary conditions for enduring peace and sustained socio-economic development
suitable to the systems of life, needs, and aspirations of its peoples by providing services to
communities, and ensuring multi-stakeholder participation. Accordingly, we will be able to
promote an inclusive and meaningful governance in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, in the
interest of its tri-people.

B. Declaration of Basic Principles and Policies

Section 1. Peace and order, rule of law, solidarity, social justice, general welfare, protection of
life, liberty and property, basic necessities and equal opportunities. — The Bangsamoro
Government shall uphold peace and order and the rule of law, promote ethnic, cultural and
religious solidarity, work for the advancement of social justice and the general welfare, afford full
protection to life, liberty, and property of all the peoples within in its territorial jurisdiction, and
ensure the provision of their basic necessities and equal opportunities in life.

Section 2. Protection of human rights. — The Bangsamoro Government ensures the protection of
human rights recognized under international law, the 1987 Philippine Constitution, Republic Act
No. 11054, otherwise known as the Organic Law for the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in
Muslim Mindanao, all national laws of the Philippines, and other laws as may be enacted by
Bangsamoro Parliament.

OF THEIR WELFARE AND CULTURAL INTEGRITY

A. Political, Civil, Economic, Social, Cultural and Religious Rights

Section 1. Settlers’ rights under existing laws. — Pursuant to paragraph 4, Section 1, Article X of
Republic Act No. 11054, Shari'ah or Islamic law shall apply exclusively to cases involving
Muslims. Where a case involves a Settler, Shari‘ah law may apply only if the Settler voluntarily
submits to the jurisdiction of the Shari'ah court. Accordingly, all existing national laws, including
subsequent laws, vesting political, civil, economic, social and cultural rights shall govern the rights
of Settlers.

Consequently, acts and omissions in violation of Settlers’ rights shall be prosecuted under
existing laws with penal provisions.

Section 2. Settlers’ social, cultural, and religious rights. — The Bangsamoro Government shall
respect the integrity of the Settlers’ values, practices and institutions. Laws contrary to the cultural
and religious beliefs of Settlers shall not be applicable to them. Accordingly, the Settlers shall have
the right to freely pursue their economic, social and cultural development.® In communion with

C. Social Justice and Human Rights

Section 1. Equal Protection and Non-discrimination of Settlers. — Pursuant to the equal protection
clause of the 1987 Constitution of the Republic of the Philippines, as well as relevant international
instruments, the Bangsamoro Government shall accord to the Settlers the rights, protections and
privileges enjoyed by the rest of the citizenry. It shall extend to them the same employment rights,
opportunities, basic services, educational and other rights and privileges available to every member
of the Bangsamoro society.' It shall ensure that there is no discrimination regarding hiring and
appointment of Settler professionals in the Bangsamoro ministries and offices. The security of
tenure of Settler employees shall be assured in the actual Bangsamoro governance. !

Section 2. Right in case of dislocation due to calamities. — In case dislocation occurs as a result
of natural or man-made calamities, the Bangsamoro Government shall endeavor to relocate the
dislocated Settlers in suitable areas where they can have temporary life support systems: Provided,
That the dislocated Settlers shall have the right to return to their abandoned lands until such time
that the normalcy and safety of such lands shall be determined: Provided, further, That should their
lands or immovable properties cease to exist and normalcy and safety of the previous settlements
are not possible, the Bangsamoro government shall endeavour to relocate the Settlers to tenable
areas as their permanent place of residence. Provided, furthermore, That basic services and
livelihood shall be provided to them to ensure that their needs are adequately addressed.'*

Section 3. Settlers’ representation to Peace and Order Council of the LGUs. —The Bangsamoro
Government shall ensure that the employment of any form of force or coercion against the Settlers
shall be dealt with by law. To these ends, it shall work for Settlers’ representation to the Peace and
Order Council of the local government units.'>

Section 4. Freedom from discrimination and right to equal opportunity and treatment. — It shall
be the right of the Settlers to be free from any form of discrimination, with respect to recruitment
and conditions of employment, such that they may enjoy equal opportunities for admission to
employment, medical and social assistance, safety as well as other occupationally-related
benefits. !

Section 5. Basic services. — The Bangsamoro Government shall guarantee the right of the Settlers
to government’s basic services which shall include, but not limited to, water and electrical
facilities, education, health, and infrastructure.!”

D. Promotion of Settlers’ Cultural Integrity

Section 1. Protection of Settlers’ culture, traditions and institutions. — The Bangsamoro
Government shall respect, recognize and protect the right of the Settlers to preserve and protect
their culture, traditions and institutions.'®

Section 2. Educational systems. — The Bangsamoro Government shall provide equal access to
various cultural opportunities to the Settlers through the educational system, public or private
cultural entities, scholarships, grants and other incentives without prejudice to their right to
establish and control their educational systems and institutions by providing education in
accordance to the national standards.

Section 3. Recognition and Promotion of cultural diversity with sensitivity to Settlers’ culture. —
The Bangsamoro Government shall take effective measures to eliminate prejudice and
discrimination and to promote tolerance, understanding and good relations among the various
peoples in the Bangsamoro Region. To this end, it shall endeavor to have the dignity of the cultures,
traditions, histories and aspirations of the Tri-people appropriately reflected in all forms of
education, public information and cultural-educational exchange and take effective measures to
ensure that all the mass media within the Bangsamoro Region duly reflect cultural diversity with
sensitivity to the Settlers’ culture.

Section 4. Rights to religious sites and ceremonies. — The Settlers shall have the right to manifest,
practice, develop, and teach their spiritual and religious traditions, customs and ceremonies; the
right to maintain, protect and have access to their religious sites; the right to use and control of
ceremonial objects; and, the right to the repatriation of human remains. Accordingly, the
Bangsamoro Government shall take effective measures, in cooperation with the Settlers concerned,
to ensure that their sacred places, including burial sites, be preserved, respected and protected.!?

B. Fiscal Autonomy

Section 1. Granting of fiscal autonomy to the OSA. — The OSA is hereby granted fiscal autonomy.
It shall have an annual and other budgetary allocations from the General Appropriations Act of
Bangsamoro, equitably considering the percentage of Settlers’ population in the Bangsamoro
Region. Such annual and other budgetary allocations shall not be reduced from the allocated
budget in the preceding fiscal year. The compensation, emoluments, privileges, or any other
benefits enjoyed by the OSA officials and employees shall not be decreased.”

Section 2. Automatic release of funds from the Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management
to the OSA. — The Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management shall automatically release
to the OSA its approved annual budget upon the submission of the required documents.

Section 3. Authority of the OSA to create its sources of revenues. — The OSA shall have the power
to create its sources of revenues consistent with the policies embodied in the BOL. Also, it may
forge linkages and formal partnerships with both local and international organizations through
Memorandum of Agreements (MOAs).

Section 4. Settlers’ Empowerment Fund (SEF). — There shall be a Settlers’ Empowerment Fund
(SEF) equivalent to five percent (5%) annually of the approved General Appropriations Act of
Bangsamoro of the preceding fiscal year. The SEF is seen as integral to the protection of Settlers’
rights and the promotion of their welfare in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.

ARTICLE IV: FINAL PROVISIONS

Section 1. Penal clause. — Any act and omission in violation of Settlers’ rights and any provision
of this Act shall be prosecuted under existing laws with penal provisions.

Section 2. Supplementary provision. — If there is no provision in this Act applicable to a specific
case or proceeding, the generally accepted principle of the national and international laws shall
apply suppletorily.

Section 3. Implementing Rules and Regulations. — Within sixty (60) days immediately after the
effectivity of this Act, the OSA shall issue the necessary rules and regulations for the effective
implementation of this Act.

Section 4. Separability clause. — If any provision of this Act is held unconstitutional, other
provisions not affected thereby shall remain valid and binding.

Section 5. Repealing clause. — All other regional acts, decrees, instructions, rules, and regulations
or parts thereof inconsistent with the provisions of this Act are hereby repealed or modified

accordingly.

Section 6. Effectivity clause. — This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in
a newspaper of regional circulation in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.
