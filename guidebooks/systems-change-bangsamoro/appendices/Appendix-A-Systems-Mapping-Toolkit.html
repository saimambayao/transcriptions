Appendix A

Systems Mapping Toolkit

A step-by-step guide for creating systems maps for any BARMM policy issue

Purpose

This toolkit equips BARMM teams with practical methods for creating three essential systems maps: causal loop diagrams, stakeholder maps, and incentive maps. These visual tools reveal hidden dynamics, power structures, and behavioral drivers that shape policy outcomes in the Bangsamoro.

When to Use This Toolkit

  • Before designing any major reform or program
  • When diagnosing why a policy or program is underperforming
  • When planning a new policy intervention
  • When multiple stakeholders disagree on problem definitions
  • During strategic planning sessions for ministries or LGUs
Time Needed
2-4 hours per mapping session
Participants
5-15 people with diverse perspectives
Materials
Large paper or whiteboard, markers (3+ colors), sticky notes
Outputs
Visual system map, identified leverage points, action priorities
Facilitator Tip

Start with causal loop diagrams to understand system dynamics, then use stakeholder mapping to identify who can act on leverage points, and finally use incentive mapping to design interventions that align actor motivations with desired outcomes.

Step-by-Step Guide: Causal Loop Diagrams

Causal loop diagrams (CLDs) show how variables in a system influence each other, revealing the feedback loops that drive system behavior over time.

1

Define the System Boundary

Decide what is inside your system and what is outside. Be explicit about scope.

  • Write a clear problem statement (e.g., "Low budget utilization in BARMM education programs")
  • List what is in scope (provincial education offices, MBHTE programs, LGU budgets)
  • List what is out of scope (DepEd national policy, private schools, international aid)
  • Draw a boundary line on your paper — everything inside is your system
2

Identify Key Variables

Variables are things that change — they can go up or down. Write each on a sticky note.

  • Use nouns or noun phrases, not actions (e.g., "poverty rate" not "reducing poverty")
  • Aim for 8-15 variables — too few misses dynamics, too many creates confusion
  • Include both measurable variables (enrollment rate, budget utilization) and soft variables (trust in government, teacher morale)
Example Variables for Education

Teacher quality • Student enrollment • Budget allocation • School infrastructure • Parent engagement • Poverty rate • Employment opportunities • Cultural relevance of curriculum • Teacher morale • Community trust in education

3

Draw Causal Links

Connect variables with arrows showing how one influences another. Mark each arrow with polarity:

+ Same direction: When A increases, B increases (or when A decreases, B decreases)
− Opposite direction: When A increases, B decreases (or when A decreases, B increases)
  • Ask: "If this variable goes up, what happens to the connected variable?"
  • Each arrow represents a causal claim — be ready to defend it
  • Add time delays with a double line through the arrow (||) for effects that take months or years
4

Identify Feedback Loops

Trace closed paths through the diagram. Two types:

Type Symbol Behavior How to Identify
Reinforcing R Amplifies change — growth or collapse Even number of negative (-) links in the loop, or all positive (+)
Balancing B Resists change — seeks equilibrium Odd number of negative (-) links in the loop
5

Name the Loops

Give each loop a descriptive name that captures its story. This helps communicate the insight.

  • Reinforcing: "Poverty Trap," "Brain Drain Spiral," "Trust-Building Flywheel"
  • Balancing: "Budget Ceiling," "Capacity Constraint," "Accountability Check"
6

Identify Leverage Points

Look for places where a small intervention could shift the system's behavior:

  • Variables that appear in multiple loops — changing them affects several dynamics
  • Variables with time delays — early intervention here prevents larger problems
  • The "weakest link" in a reinforcing loop — breaking or strengthening it changes everything
  • Balancing loops that are too strong or too weak — adjust their power
Worked Example: The Education-Poverty Cycle in Your Province

System boundary: Education and economic outcomes in one BARMM province

Key variables: Poverty rate, school enrollment, education quality, employment opportunities, household income, teacher availability, school infrastructure, community investment in education

Two key loops identified:

R1 "Poverty Trap": High poverty → low enrollment (+) → low education quality (+) → low employment (+) → low household income (+) → high poverty (+). Reinforcing loop that traps communities.

R2 "Investment Flywheel": Education quality → employment (+) → household income (+) → community investment (+) → school infrastructure (+) → teacher availability (+) → education quality (+). Reinforcing loop that builds prosperity.

Leverage point: Teacher availability — it appears in both loops. Increasing teacher supply and quality can weaken R1 while strengthening R2.

Rate Influence

For each stakeholder, assess their power to affect the outcome:

  • High: Can approve, block, or significantly alter the initiative (e.g., Chief Minister, BTA Parliament)
  • Medium: Can shape implementation or redirect resources (e.g., Ministry directors, LGU executives)
  • Low: Affected by the outcome but has limited power to change it (e.g., individual citizens, barangay workers)
3

Rate Interest

For each stakeholder, assess how much they care about the issue:

  • High: Directly affected; actively engaged; this is a priority for them
  • Medium: Somewhat affected; occasionally engaged; one of many priorities
  • Low: Minimally affected; rarely engaged; not a current priority

Key Insight

In the Bangsamoro context, many governance challenges involve mixed incentive structures where formal rules point one direction while informal norms (clan loyalty, patronage networks, traditional authority) point another. Your incentive map should capture both formal and informal incentives to be useful.
