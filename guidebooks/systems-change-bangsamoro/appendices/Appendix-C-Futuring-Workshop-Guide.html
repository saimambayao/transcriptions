Appendix C

Futuring Workshop Guide

A facilitation guide for Theory U sessions with BARMM leaders

Workshop Overview

Duration
2 full days (approximately 14 hours total)
Participants
20-50 BARMM leaders (MPs, ministers, directors, LGU executives, CSO heads, ulama, youth)
Facilitator Requirements
Familiarity with Theory U methodology and systems thinking; experience with multi-stakeholder dialogues
Workshop Outputs
Shared vision document, prototype action plans, peer accountability pairs, personal commitment statements

Materials Preparation Checklist

Large paper sheets / Manila paper (50+)
Markers in multiple colors (5+ per table)
Sticky notes (4 colors, 10 pads minimum)
Index cards (100+)
Masking tape for wall displays
Printed participant handouts
Timer / bell for session transitions
Box or container for "letting go" exercise
Projector and screen
Audio system for group sessions
Prayer mats / designated prayer area
Refreshments (halal)

Theory U Framework

Theory U, developed by Otto Scharmer at MIT, describes a process of going down the left side of the U (opening mind, heart, and will to let go of old patterns) and coming up the right side (crystallizing vision, prototyping action, and embodying new practices). This workshop adapts Theory U for the Bangsamoro governance context.

Room Setup

Arrange tables in groups of 5-6 participants. Ensure each table has a mix of seniority levels, sectors, and geographic representation. Avoid placing people from the same ministry or LGU at the same table. Leave wall space for gallery walks and poster displays.

Day 1: Going Down the U

Opening Mind • Opening Heart • Opening Will
1
Open Mind — Current State Assessment
2 hours • Morning Session 1

Objective: Surface and examine the mental models that shape how participants understand BARMM governance. Challenge assumptions that may be limiting innovation.

Suggested Timeline

0:00 - 0:15
Opening du'a and Bismillah. Facilitator introduces Theory U and the workshop purpose.
0:15 - 0:45
Individual reflection: each participant writes 3 assumptions about BARMM governance they have never questioned.
0:45 - 1:30
Table exercise: share assumptions, then challenge each other using structured dialogue protocol.
1:30 - 2:00
Plenary debrief: each table shares their most surprising challenged assumption.
Activity: Assumption Surfacing

Each participant receives 3 index cards. On each card, write one assumption about BARMM governance that you hold as true but have never examined critically. Examples:

  • "Budget utilization is low because of lack of capacity"
  • "Traditional leaders and modern governance cannot coexist"
  • "The transition period is too short to build real institutions"
Group Exercise: Structured Dialogue

For each assumption shared at the table, follow this protocol:

  1. Present — the author reads their assumption aloud (1 min)
  2. Probe — other members ask clarifying questions only, no judgments (3 min)
  3. Challenge — each member offers one counter-example or alternative explanation (5 min)
  4. Reflect — the author states what they now see differently (1 min)
2
Open Heart — Perspective Taking
2 hours • Morning Session 2

Objective: Build empathy by experiencing issues from perspectives different from one's own role. Break down silos between levels of government and between government and citizens.

Activity: Role Reversal Exercise

Participants are assigned a role different from their actual position:

  • Ministers and MPs take the role of barangay captains
  • LGU executives take the role of ordinary citizens seeking government services
  • CSO leaders take the role of ministry directors managing competing demands
  • Youth leaders take the role of ulama navigating tradition and modernity

In their assigned roles, participants discuss a common BARMM challenge (e.g., delivering basic services in remote areas) and advocate from their assigned perspective.

Exercise: "Walk in Their Shoes"

Each table selects one concrete problem (e.g., school construction delays, health worker shortages, IRA distribution). Then maps the same problem from 3 different stakeholder perspectives:

  1. The citizen experiencing the problem — what do they see, feel, need?
  2. The frontline government worker — what constraints do they face?
  3. The policy maker — what trade-offs must they navigate?

Each perspective is written on a different colored sticky note and posted on the table's paper.

3
Open Will — Letting Go
2 hours • Afternoon Session

Objective: Identify and consciously release ARMM-era patterns, habits, and institutional behaviors that persist in the BARMM and hinder systems change.

Activity: "What Must We Let Go Of?"

Each table identifies 3 ARMM-era patterns that still persist in the BARMM today. These could be:

  • Institutional habits (e.g., centralized decision-making, patronage-based hiring)
  • Mental models (e.g., "autonomy means independence from Manila")
  • Behavioral norms (e.g., avoiding confrontation with senior officials)
  • Structural patterns (e.g., parallel systems that duplicate effort)

Write each pattern on a large card with: (1) the pattern, (2) why it persists, and (3) what it costs us.

Exercise: Symbolic Letting Go Ceremony

After the table discussions, each participant writes one personal pattern they commit to releasing on a card. In a facilitated ceremony:

  1. Each participant stands and reads their card aloud to their table (optional: to the whole group)
  2. They place the card in a communal box at the center of the room
  3. The facilitator closes with a reflection: "These patterns served us once. We honor what they were. We release them to make room for what must emerge."

Note: This can be paired with a du'a for guidance and renewal.

Facilitator Note

This session requires psychological safety. Emphasize that naming patterns is not blaming individuals. Frame it as "institutional archaeology" — understanding what we inherited so we can choose what to carry forward. Allow silence. Do not rush the ceremony.

Day 2: Coming Up the U

Crystallizing • Prototyping • Embodying
4
Crystallizing — Vision
2 hours • Morning Session 1

Objective: Transform the insights from Day 1 into a concrete, shared vision for what the Bangsamoro can become by 2030.

Activity: "BARMM 2030"

Small groups (one per sector) design their vision for their assigned sector in 2030. Each group produces a visual poster answering:

  • What does success look like for citizens in this sector by 2030?
  • What institutions and systems are in place?
  • What is different from today?
  • What does the Bangsamoro feel like to live in?

Encourage visual representation — drawings, diagrams, and symbols alongside text.

Exercise: Gallery Walk

Groups post their vision posters on the walls. All participants do a silent gallery walk (15 min), reading each poster and placing gold sticky dots on elements that resonate with them. Then:

  1. Each group presents their vision (5 min per group)
  2. Plenary discussion: What common themes emerge across all four sectors? (15 min)
  3. Facilitator captures shared themes on a master sheet
5
Prototyping — Action
2 hours • Morning Session 2

Objective: Move from vision to action by designing concrete prototype interventions using systems thinking frameworks from the guidebook.

Activity: Prototype Design

Each table designs one prototype intervention using the template below. The intervention should address a real BARMM challenge and apply systems thinking principles from the guidebook.

Prototype Intervention Template

Problem Statement
System Map

Sketch the key feedback loops (use Appendix A methods)

Leverage Point
Proposed Intervention
Expected Feedback Loop

What reinforcing or balancing loop will this create?

Measurement

How will you know it's working? What will you measure at 3, 6, and 12 months?

6
Embodying — Commitment
2 hours • Afternoon Session

Objective: Translate group insights and prototypes into personal commitments and peer accountability structures that will sustain momentum after the workshop.

Activity: Personal Commitment Statements

Each participant writes a personal commitment statement completing the sentence: "In the next 90 days, I will..."

The commitment should be:

  • Specific — a concrete action, not a vague intention
  • Within their authority — something they can do without waiting for approval
  • Systems-oriented — aimed at changing a pattern, not just completing a task
  • Measurable — they will know whether they did it
Exercise: Peer Accountability Pairs

Participants pair with someone from a different sector or level of government. Each pair:

  1. Shares their commitment statement with their partner
  2. Exchanges contact information
  3. Agrees on a check-in schedule (suggested: every 2 weeks for 90 days)
  4. Identifies one way they can support each other's commitment
Closing: Shared Vision Statement

The facilitator guides the group to draft a collective vision statement for systems change in the Bangsamoro. Process:

  1. Review the common themes captured during the gallery walk (5 min)
  2. Each table proposes one sentence for the statement (10 min)
  3. Facilitator weaves the sentences into a draft on screen (10 min)
  4. Group reads the statement aloud together (5 min)
  5. Closing du'a and group photo

Facilitation Notes

Managing Power Dynamics

Senior officials and youth at the same table: Use structured protocols (timed speaking, round-robin) to ensure equal voice. Consider having senior officials speak last in rounds so they don't anchor the conversation.
Cross-rank dialogue: Establish a "workshop bubble" norm at the start — within the workshop, participants relate as co-learners, not by rank. The facilitator should model this by addressing everyone the same way.
Silent voices: Use written exercises before verbal sharing to give introverts and junior participants time to formulate their thoughts. Collect written cards anonymously when discussing sensitive patterns.

Handling Disagreement

Productive vs. unproductive conflict: Disagreement about ideas is healthy; personal attacks are not. If tension rises, redirect to the shared purpose: "We all want a better Bangsamoro. Let's explore where this disagreement comes from."
Political sensitivities: Some topics (IRA distribution, political appointments, peace process compromises) may trigger strong reactions. Prepare for these by establishing ground rules early: "We discuss systems and patterns, not individuals or parties."
When stuck: Use the "Third Option" technique — when two positions are in opposition, ask: "What is a third option that neither side has considered?" This shifts from debate to co-creation.

Islamic Opening and Closing Protocols

Opening: Begin with Bismillahir Rahmanir Rahim. Invite a respected alim or ustadz among the participants to offer the opening du'a. Include a brief Quranic reflection relevant to change and renewal (e.g., Surah Ar-Ra'd 13:11 on people changing their condition).
Prayer breaks: Schedule the workshop around salah times. Provide a designated prayer area. Build in 15-20 minute breaks for Dhuhr and Asr prayers. Do not schedule sessions during Jumu'ah (Friday noon prayer).
Closing: Close with a collective du'a for the success of the Bangsamoro transition, for wisdom in governance, and for the welfare of the Bangsamoro people. Allow a participant to lead this.

Language Considerations

Working language: Default to Filipino/Tagalog or English as the plenary language, depending on participant comfort. Allow table discussions in local languages (Maguindanaon, Meranaw, Tausug, etc.).
Written materials: All handouts and templates should be in English. Facilitator instructions and slide presentations can be bilingual (English/Filipino).
Translation support: If the group includes participants who are more comfortable in local languages, assign bilingual table facilitators who can bridge between plenary (English/Filipino) and table discussions (local languages).
