# Chapter 8 — Review, Approval, and Implementation

Your draft MOP is complete. The procedures are documented. The workflow tables are built. The forms are designed. Now you must validate the MOP through review, secure approval from the appropriate authority, and implement it across your agency. This chapter covers each step.

## 8.1 Circulating Drafts for Feedback

A MOP written in isolation by the development team will contain blind spots. The people who execute the procedures daily will catch errors, omissions, and impracticalities that the drafting team missed. Circulate your draft through three review rounds.

### 8.1.1 Internal Review Process

Route the draft MOP to every division and unit within its scope. Give reviewers two weeks minimum for a full MOP and one week for individual chapter updates.

**Who reviews:**
- All Division Chiefs and Section Heads within the MOP's scope
- Selected Staff Officers (especially those with the most experience in key procedures)
- The Administrative Officer (for administrative procedures)
- The Finance Officer (for financial management procedures)
- The HR Officer (for human resource procedures)
- The Procurement Officer (for procurement procedures)

**What to ask reviewers:**

Provide each reviewer with a feedback form that asks specific questions:

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Are the procedures described accurately? | Verifies the MOP matches actual practice |
| Are any steps missing? | Identifies gaps |
| Are any steps unnecessary? | Identifies over-documentation |
| Are the forms appropriate and usable? | Tests form design |
| Can a new employee follow these procedures without oral explanation? | Tests clarity and completeness |
| Are the turnaround times realistic? | Validates performance standards |
| Are the responsible persons correctly identified? | Verifies accountability assignments |

### 8.1.2 External Expert Review

For a first-time MOP or a major revision, bring in external reviewers with expertise your internal team may lack:

- **Legal reviewer.** A lawyer or legal officer who can verify legal compliance, particularly with BAA 13, BAA 17, BAA 84, and your enabling BAA.
- **Operations management expert.** A professional with experience in government operations manuals who can evaluate the MOP's structure, completeness, and usability.
- **COA representative.** If possible, invite an auditor to review the financial management and accountability chapters. Their feedback now prevents audit findings later.

External review typically takes two to four weeks. Budget for this time in your work plan.

### 8.1.3 Managing Conflicting Feedback

Different reviewers will give different -- sometimes contradictory -- feedback. The Staff Officer thinks the procedure is too detailed. The Division Chief thinks it is not detailed enough. The Legal Expert wants more legal references. The Technical Writer wants fewer.

Manage conflicts with a structured approach:

1. **Compile all feedback** in a single matrix (see Section 8.2.1)
2. **Categorize each item** as factual correction, preference, or substantive disagreement
3. **Accept all factual corrections** without discussion
4. **Note preferences** but do not automatically adopt them -- consistency matters more than individual preference
5. **Escalate substantive disagreements** to the Team Leader for resolution
6. **Consult the approving authority** when disagreements involve policy decisions

## 8.2 Incorporating Feedback

### 8.2.1 Feedback Tracking Matrix

Track every piece of feedback in a structured matrix:

| No. | Reviewer | Chapter/Section | Feedback | Category | Decision | Revised? |
|-----|----------|----------------|----------|----------|----------|----------|
| 1 | Admin Division Chief | Ch. VI, Sec. 6.2 | Step 3 should specify two-hour turnaround | Substantive | Accepted | Yes |
| 2 | Finance Officer | Ch. IV, Sec. 4.3 | Voucher form number is wrong | Factual correction | Accepted | Yes |
| 3 | Staff Officer A | Ch. VI, Sec. 6.1 | Too many steps. Can we combine Steps 4-5? | Preference | Rejected (different responsible persons) | No |
| 4 | Legal Expert | Ch. III, Sec. 3.2 | BAA 17 citation is incorrect | Factual correction | Accepted | Yes |
| 5 | External Reviewer | Ch. VII | Missing procurement threshold reference | Substantive | Accepted | Yes |

This matrix serves two purposes: it tracks the disposition of every comment (so no feedback is lost), and it documents why specific decisions were made (so future reviewers understand the rationale).

### 8.2.2 Revision Protocols

Apply revisions systematically:

1. **Batch revisions by chapter.** Address all feedback for one chapter before moving to the next. This prevents inconsistencies that arise from piecemeal editing.
2. **Track changes.** Use tracked changes (in word processors) or redline notation (in markdown) so reviewers can verify their feedback was addressed.
3. **Re-verify cross-references.** After incorporating revisions, re-check all cross-references. A change in one chapter often ripples to others.
4. **Second review.** After major revisions, circulate the affected chapters for a second review. This should be faster (one week) because reviewers are only checking their specific feedback.

## 8.3 Authority Approval

### 8.3.1 Who Approves the MOP

The approving authority depends on the MOP's scope:

| MOP Scope | Approving Authority |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Ministry-wide MOP | Minister (or equivalent agency head) |
| Division MOP | Undersecretary or Assistant Secretary |
| Office MOP | Director II or Division Chief |
| Cross-agency MOP (e.g., shared services) | Chief Minister or designated coordinating authority |

The approving authority should receive:

- The final MOP document
- The Feedback Tracking Matrix showing all feedback received and how it was addressed
- The Mandate-to-Procedure Traceability Matrix (Chapter 6)
- A one-page executive summary highlighting key features, changes from previous versions (if applicable), and implementation recommendations

### 8.3.2 Approval Documentation

Document the approval formally. The approving authority issues:

1. **An approval memorandum or office order.** This document states: (a) the MOP is approved, (b) the effective date, (c) which previous versions or SOPs it supersedes, and (d) the requirement for all staff to comply.
2. **A signed MOP cover page.** The approving authority signs the MOP's cover page or approval page, affirming that the document has been reviewed and approved.

Keep the signed approval documents in a permanent file. They establish the MOP's official status and effective date.

## 8.4 Staff Orientation and Training

An approved MOP is worth nothing if staff do not know it exists, cannot find it, or do not understand how to use it. Training is not optional. It is the bridge between documentation and implementation.

### 8.4.1 Designing the Orientation Program

Design a structured orientation program that covers:

| Session | Duration | Audience | Content |
|---------|----------|----------|---------|
| **MOP overview** | 2 hours | All staff | Purpose of the MOP, overall structure, how to navigate it, where to find it, how to request amendments |
| **Division-specific procedures** | 4 hours | Division/unit staff | Detailed walkthrough of each procedure in the staff's functional area, including hands-on practice with forms |
| **Supervisory procedures** | 3 hours | Division Chiefs, Section Heads | Review and approval procedures, quality criteria, performance monitoring, escalation protocols |
| **Administrative procedures** | 2 hours | Admin and support staff | Reception, routing, tracking, filing, and delivery procedures |

### 8.4.2 Training Sessions by Role

Tailor training content to each role:

**For Staff Officers (execution level):**
- Focus on the workflow tables they will execute
- Walk through each step of their assigned procedures
- Practice filling out the forms they will use
- Review the quality criteria for their outputs
- Clarify where to find help when they encounter unusual situations

**For Division Chiefs (review level):**
- Focus on the review criteria and quality standards
- Practice evaluating draft outputs against the quality criteria tables
- Review the escalation protocols for delays
- Discuss how to use performance KPIs to manage their division

**For the Director and Agency Head (approval level):**
- Focus on the strategic purpose of the MOP
- Review the turnaround time standards and escalation protocols
- Discuss performance monitoring and quarterly review processes
- Clarify delegation of authority for MOP-related approvals

### 8.4.3 Hands-On Practice with Forms and Workflows

Lecture-based training does not build competence. Hands-on practice does. Include these exercises in every training session:

**Exercise 1: Complete the forms.** Give each participant blank copies of every form they will use. Walk through each field. Have them fill in the form using a sample scenario.

**Exercise 2: Walk the workflow.** Simulate a complete procedure from Reception to Delivery. Assign roles. Give the "Administrative Officer" a sample request. Have them log it, attach the Referral and Action Slip, and forward it. Continue through each step until the output is "delivered."

**Exercise 3: Find it in the MOP.** Give participants scenarios and ask them to find the relevant procedure in the MOP. This builds familiarity with the document's structure and navigation.

## 8.5 Rollout Plan

### 8.5.1 Phased vs. Full Implementation

Choose your rollout approach based on agency size and readiness:

**Full implementation** works for small agencies (fewer than 50 staff) where training can reach everyone within two weeks. Set an effectivity date. On that date, the MOP applies to all operations.

**Phased implementation** works for larger agencies. Roll out by division or by chapter:

| Phase | Timeline | Scope |
|-------|----------|-------|
| Phase 1: Weeks 1-2 | Administrative procedures (Ch. I, II, VIII) | All divisions |
| Phase 2: Weeks 3-4 | HR and finance procedures (Ch. III, IV) | All divisions |
| Phase 3: Weeks 5-8 | Service delivery procedures (Ch. VI) | One division at a time |
| Phase 4: Weeks 9-10 | Procurement and planning (Ch. V, VII) | All divisions |
| Phase 5: Week 11-12 | Full integration and monitoring | All divisions |

Phased rollout allows you to identify and fix problems before they affect the entire agency.

### 8.5.2 Distribution and Access

Make the MOP accessible. Inaccessible MOPs are unused MOPs.

**Physical copies:**
- Print at least one copy per division
- Place copies in accessible locations (not locked in the Division Chief's cabinet)
- Use binders that allow easy replacement of updated pages

**Digital copies:**
- Upload to the agency's shared drive or intranet
- Ensure all staff know the file location
- Use PDF format for the official version (prevents unauthorized edits)
- Maintain an editable version (Word or Markdown) for authorized amendments

**Quick reference materials:**
- Print laminated one-page summaries of the most common procedures
- Post workflow diagrams for high-frequency processes on office walls
- Create pocket-sized cards with frequently used form references

### 8.5.3 Communication Strategy

Announce the MOP's implementation through multiple channels:

1. **Agency-wide memorandum** from the head announcing the approved MOP, its effective date, and the requirement for compliance
2. **Division briefings** where Division Chiefs explain the MOP to their staff
3. **Bulletin board postings** with the MOP overview and key procedures
4. **Digital notification** via email or group chat to all staff

> **The 90-day rule.** Expect full compliance within 90 days of implementation, not immediately. During the first 90 days, focus on guidance and coaching rather than enforcement. After 90 days, the MOP becomes the standard against which all operations are measured. This grace period builds buy-in and allows staff to develop new habits.
