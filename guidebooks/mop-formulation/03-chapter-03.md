# Chapter 3 — Information Gathering

The quality of your MOP depends on the quality of your information. Rushing through this phase produces procedures that describe an idealized version of your agency -- not the agency that actually exists. This chapter provides systematic methods for collecting accurate, complete information.

## 3.1 Reviewing Existing Policies and Procedures

Begin with what already exists on paper. Every BARMM agency has some form of documentation, even if it is scattered, outdated, or incomplete. A thorough review of existing documents prevents you from reinventing procedures that already work and reveals gaps that need attention.

### 3.1.1 Collect All Existing Documents

Request the following from every division and office within your MOP's scope:

- Current MOPs or standard operating procedures (SOPs)
- Office orders and special orders
- Memoranda and circulars
- Process flowcharts
- Forms, templates, and checklists
- Training materials and orientation guides
- Job descriptions and position descriptions
- Previous audit reports and management letters
- Organizational charts (current and historical)

Cast a wide net. Check physical filing cabinets and digital drives. Ask long-serving staff where documents are stored. Many agencies discover useful documentation buried in archives that current staff have never seen.

### 3.1.2 Verify Currency and Validity

For each document you collect, verify three things:

1. **Date.** When was it last updated? Documents older than three years are likely outdated.
2. **Authority.** Who approved it? Documents without clear approval authority have uncertain status.
3. **Status.** Is it currently in effect, or has it been superseded by a newer document?

Mark each document as current, outdated, or unknown. Focus your detailed review on current documents, but keep outdated ones for reference -- they often contain useful procedural detail that newer documents omit.

### 3.1.3 Identify Conflicting Provisions

When two documents prescribe different steps for the same process, you have a conflict. Conflicts create confusion. Staff follow whichever version they encountered first, producing inconsistent results.

Document each conflict in a comparison table:

| Process | Document A | Document B | Conflict |
|---------|-----------|-----------|----------|
| Leave application | Office Order No. 12: Submit to Division Chief | Memo No. 45: Submit to Admin Officer | Different initial recipient |
| Procurement request | SOP v1: Three quotes required | SOP v2: Three quotes for purchases above 50,000 | Different threshold |

Resolve conflicts during the drafting phase (Chapter 4). For now, document them accurately.

### 3.1.4 Map Document Hierarchy

Establish which documents take precedence. The hierarchy for BARMM agencies is:

1. **Republic Act No. 11054** (Bangsamoro Organic Law) -- supreme authority
2. **Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts** (BAA 13, BAA 17, BAA 84, etc.) -- legislative mandates
3. **Executive Orders and Administrative Orders** -- executive directives
4. **Department Orders and Memorandum Circulars** -- Ministry-level directives
5. **Office Orders and SOPs** -- operational-level directives

When lower-level documents conflict with higher-level ones, the higher-level document controls. Your MOP must comply with all documents above it in the hierarchy.

### 3.1.5 Note Undocumented Practices

Many agencies operate through undocumented practices -- procedures that staff follow by convention but that exist nowhere in writing. These practices are not necessarily wrong. Some represent efficient adaptations to real-world conditions. Others represent unauthorized shortcuts.

Identify undocumented practices by comparing written procedures against observed practice. Note each one for evaluation during the drafting phase. Decide whether to formalize, modify, or eliminate each practice.

### 3.1.6 Assess Compliance with BAA 13

Compare your agency's existing documents against the organizational structure and functions defined in BAA 13. Check whether:

- Your organizational chart matches the structure prescribed by BAA 13
- Your documented functions align with the mandates listed in BAA 13
- Your procedures reference the correct legal authorities
- Your forms and templates comply with administrative requirements

Flag any discrepancies. These must be resolved in the new MOP.

### 3.1.7 Catalog Forms and Templates

Create a complete inventory of every form and template your agency uses. For each form, record:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Form name** | Official name of the form |
| **Form number** | Assigned number (if any) |
| **Purpose** | What the form is used for |
| **Origin** | Who created it and when |
| **Frequency** | How often it is used |
| **Location** | Where blank copies are stored |
| **Status** | Current, modified, or obsolete |

This inventory becomes the basis for the Annexes chapter of your MOP (Chapter IX in the standard structure).

### 3.1.8 Document Findings in a Policy Inventory Matrix

Consolidate all findings from steps 3.1.1 through 3.1.7 into a single Policy Inventory Matrix:

| Document Title | Date | Authority | Status | Scope | Conflicts | BAA 13 Compliance | Notes |
|---------------|------|-----------|--------|-------|-----------|-------------------|-------|
| SOP on Procurement | 2022-03-15 | Admin Division Chief | Current | Division-wide | Conflicts with BAA 84 threshold | Partial | Needs update for BAA 84 |
| Office Order No. 12 | 2020-07-01 | Minister | Outdated | Ministry-wide | None identified | Compliant | Superseded by OO No. 34 |

This matrix becomes your primary reference for the drafting phase.

## 3.2 Conducting Stakeholder Interviews

Documents tell you what should happen. People tell you what actually happens. Stakeholder interviews bridge the gap between written procedures and operational reality.

### 3.2.1 Identify Interview Subjects

Interview at least one person from each of these categories:

- **Agency head or deputy** -- strategic perspective, priorities, and expectations
- **Division chiefs** -- division-level operations, inter-division coordination
- **Section chiefs or unit heads** -- day-to-day workflow management
- **Staff officers** -- hands-on procedural execution
- **Administrative staff** -- records management, filing, correspondence flow
- **Clients or service recipients** -- external perspective on service delivery
- **Partner agencies** -- inter-agency coordination procedures

Prioritize people with the most direct knowledge of procedures. A division chief may know the policy, but a staff officer knows the actual steps.

### 3.2.2 Prepare Interview Guides

Build a semi-structured interview guide with questions organized by topic. Use open-ended questions that encourage detailed responses:

**Process questions:**
- "Walk me through what happens when you receive a [specific request]."
- "What is the first thing you do when this document arrives on your desk?"
- "How do you know when this task is complete?"

**Responsibility questions:**
- "Who decides whether to approve or reject this request?"
- "What happens when the usual approver is not available?"
- "Who do you go to when you are unsure how to handle something?"

**Form and document questions:**
- "What forms do you use for this process?"
- "Do you ever modify the form or add extra information?"
- "Where do you file the completed form?"

**Problem questions:**
- "What is the most common delay in this process?"
- "What causes the most errors?"
- "If you could change one thing about this procedure, what would it be?"

### 3.2.3 Conduct the Interviews

Schedule 30-60 minutes per interview. Conduct interviews in the interviewee's workspace whenever possible -- this allows them to show you the actual forms, files, and tools they use.

Follow these guidelines:

- Begin by explaining the purpose of the interview and how the information will be used
- Ask the interviewee to describe their processes step by step, as if teaching a new employee
- Listen more than you talk
- Ask follow-up questions when steps seem unclear or incomplete
- Request copies of filled-out forms as examples
- Thank the interviewee and explain the next steps

### 3.2.4 Document Responses Systematically

Record each interview using a standard template:

| Field | Content |
|-------|---------|
| **Interviewee** | Name, position, division |
| **Date** | Interview date |
| **Interviewer** | Your name |
| **Topic** | Process or function discussed |
| **Key findings** | Bullet-point summary of main points |
| **Forms mentioned** | List of forms and documents referenced |
| **Gaps identified** | Missing steps, unclear responsibilities, bottlenecks |
| **Recommendations** | Interviewee's suggestions for improvement |

### 3.2.5 Validate Findings Across Sources

Cross-check what one interviewee tells you against what others say. When three people describe the same process differently, you have discovered a standardization gap that your MOP must address.

Create a validation matrix:

| Process Step | Interviewee A | Interviewee B | Interviewee C | Consistent? |
|-------------|--------------|--------------|--------------|-------------|
| Who receives the request? | Admin Officer | Division Secretary | Guard on duty | No |
| Who assigns the task? | Division Chief | Section Head | Division Chief | Mostly |
| How long does review take? | 1-2 days | 3-5 days | Same day | No |

Inconsistencies are not problems to hide. They are problems to solve. Document them honestly. Resolve them during drafting through consultation with the approving authority.

### 3.2.6 Identify Informal Workarounds

Every agency has workarounds -- informal procedures that staff use to get things done when the formal process is too slow, unclear, or impractical. Common workarounds include:

- Verbal approvals before written documentation
- Personal follow-up calls to track document movement
- Unofficial forms created by staff to fill gaps
- Informal queuing systems based on personal relationships
- Shortcut routing that bypasses review steps

Identify these workarounds without judgment. Some workarounds are efficient innovations that should be formalized. Others are risky shortcuts that should be eliminated. The drafting phase is where you make those decisions.

### 3.2.7 Synthesize Interview Data

After completing all interviews, consolidate your findings into a synthesis report organized by functional area:

1. **Common themes** across all interviews
2. **Process descriptions** as described by practitioners (not as written in documents)
3. **Gaps and inconsistencies** between documented and actual procedures
4. **Recommended improvements** from staff
5. **Forms and tools** in actual use

This synthesis becomes a primary input to the drafting phase.

## 3.3 Observing Operations

Interviews capture what people say they do. Observation captures what they actually do. People are not dishonest -- they simply cannot articulate every micro-step in their workflow from memory. Observation fills those gaps.

### 3.3.1 Shadowing Key Personnel

**Shadowing** means following a staff member through their workday, observing how they handle tasks from start to finish. Shadow at least one person per key procedure.

**How to shadow effectively:**

1. Explain your purpose before you begin. Assure the person that you are observing the process, not evaluating their performance.
2. Sit or stand where you can see what they are doing without interfering.
3. Note every step, including steps they do automatically and would not mention in an interview.
4. Record the time each step takes.
5. Note tools, forms, and systems they use.
6. Ask clarifying questions during natural pauses -- not during the task.
7. Debrief with the person after the observation. Ask, "Did I miss anything? Was today typical?"

### 3.3.2 Time and Motion Studies

A **time and motion study** measures how long each step in a procedure takes. This data is essential for setting realistic turnaround time standards in your MOP (Chapter 7).

For each procedure you study, record:

| Step | Action | Start Time | End Time | Duration | Wait Time | Notes |
|------|--------|-----------|----------|----------|-----------|-------|
| 1 | Receive document | 9:00 | 9:02 | 2 min | 0 | Logged in registry |
| 2 | Attach action slip | 9:02 | 9:05 | 3 min | 0 | Standard form used |
| 3 | Route to Division Chief | 9:05 | 9:05 | 0 min | 45 min | Waited for chief's arrival |
| 4 | Chief reviews and assigns | 9:50 | 10:05 | 15 min | 0 | Reviewed with section head |

Separate **active time** (time spent doing work) from **wait time** (time the document sits idle). In most government processes, wait time accounts for 60-80% of total processing time. This is where efficiency improvements hide.

### 3.3.3 Process Mapping from Observation

Convert your observations into visual process maps. Use simple notation:

- **Rectangle** -- action step ("Review document")
- **Diamond** -- decision point ("Is document complete?")
- **Arrow** -- direction of flow
- **Oval** -- start or end point
- **Parallel lines** -- wait or delay

Process maps from observation often look different from process maps derived from interviews or documents. The differences reveal where actual practice has drifted from intended practice.

## 3.4 Researching Best Practices

Look beyond your agency. Other BARMM agencies, national government agencies, and international organizations have developed MOPs for similar functions. Study their approaches.

**Sources to check:**

- **PRLS MOP.** The Policy Research and Legal Services MOP is the gold standard within the Bangsamoro Parliament. Chapter 5 analyzes its format in detail.
- **Other BARMM MOAs.** Request copies of MOPs from peer agencies. The Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG) and the Ministry of Finance, Budget, and Management (MFBM) often have well-developed operational manuals.
- **National government agencies.** The Department of Budget and Management (DBM) and the Civil Service Commission (CSC) publish operations manuals and citizen's charters that provide useful formatting models.
- **International frameworks.** ISO 9001 (Quality Management Systems) provides a structured approach to documenting processes that many government agencies worldwide follow.

Adapt best practices to your context. Copy the format, not the content. Your procedures must reflect your agency's specific mandate, organizational structure, and operating environment.

## 3.5 Analyzing Data for Gaps

After gathering information from documents, interviews, and observation, analyze your data to identify root causes of operational problems. Three tools are particularly useful.

### 3.5.1 Root Cause Analysis with the 5 Whys

The **5 Whys** technique asks "why?" repeatedly until you reach the root cause of a problem. It prevents you from addressing symptoms instead of causes.

**Example:**

| Why | Question | Answer |
|-----|----------|--------|
| 1 | Why are procurement requests delayed? | Because approvals take too long |
| 2 | Why do approvals take too long? | Because the approving officer is often unavailable |
| 3 | Why is the approving officer unavailable? | Because they travel frequently for field work |
| 4 | Why is there no alternate approver? | Because the delegation of authority memo does not include procurement |
| 5 | Why does the delegation memo not include procurement? | Because it was written before procurement was decentralized to divisions |

**Root cause:** The delegation of authority memo is outdated. The fix is not to speed up approvals but to update the delegation memo to include a designated alternate approver for procurement requests.

Your MOP should address the root cause: include a provision for alternate approvers and reference the updated delegation of authority.

### 3.5.2 Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagrams

A **fishbone diagram** maps all possible causes of a problem into categories. The standard categories for government processes are:

- **People** -- staffing levels, skills, availability
- **Process** -- steps, sequencing, decision points
- **Policy** -- rules, regulations, mandates
- **Technology** -- systems, tools, equipment
- **Resources** -- budget, materials, facilities
- **Communication** -- information flow, coordination

Draw the problem at the head of the fish. Draw each category as a bone. List specific causes under each category. This visual tool helps your team see the full picture and prioritize which causes your MOP can address.

### 3.5.3 Gap Analysis Matrix

Consolidate your findings into a Gap Analysis Matrix that directly feeds the drafting phase:

| Functional Area | Current State | Desired State | Gap | Root Cause | MOP Chapter Affected |
|----------------|--------------|--------------|-----|-----------|---------------------|
| Document routing | Verbal routing, no tracking | Referral and Action Slip system | No standard routing form | No form exists | Ch. VI: Service Delivery |
| Budget preparation | Each division uses different format | Standardized budget template | Inconsistent formats | No template exists | Ch. IV: Financial Management |
| Staff onboarding | Informal orientation by colleagues | Structured 5-day orientation | No orientation program | No SOP for onboarding | Ch. III: HR Management |
| Procurement | Three quotes for all amounts | Three quotes above 50,000; simplified below | Over-documentation | Outdated SOP | Ch. VII: Procurement |

This matrix becomes your drafting roadmap. Each gap maps to a specific chapter and procedure in your MOP. When the matrix is complete, you are ready to begin drafting.
