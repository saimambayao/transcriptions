# Introduction

## How to Use This Manual

This manual is a working tool. It belongs on your desk, not on a shelf. Keep it within reach when you sit down to draft a bill, review a committee report, or answer a question about how Bangsamoro legislation works.

The structure follows the life of a bill. It begins with the legal foundations of the Bangsamoro Parliament and moves through the drafting process from policy concept to enrolled act. Each chapter builds on the one before it, but you do not need to read the manual cover to cover. If you are drafting a revenue measure, go to the chapter on fiscal provisions. If you are struggling with a definitions section, go to the chapter on structure and style. If the Chief Minister has certified a bill as urgent, go to the chapter on the legislative process and find the rules that apply.

A few conventions will help you get the most out of it:

- **Verbatim BOL quotations** appear in blockquotes. These are taken directly from Republic Act No. 11054. When the manual quotes the Bangsamoro Organic Law, it reproduces the text exactly. Do not paraphrase legal text in your own drafts — if the BOL says something, quote it or cite it precisely.
- **Drafting rules** are stated plainly. Where a rule exists ("every bill shall embrace only one subject"), the manual tells you what it is and how to follow it. Where the rule leaves room for judgment, the manual explains what good judgment looks like.
- **Examples** are drawn from actual Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts. When a provision illustrates a point — good or bad — the manual names the BAA so you can read it yourself.
- **Practical guidance** sections appear throughout. These are labeled clearly and addressed to you. They tell you what to do, not what to think.

This manual does not replace the Parliament's Rules of Procedure. It does not replace the Constitution, the BOL, or the advice of the Attorney General's Office. It works alongside those authorities, not above them.

### What This Manual Covers

The manual is organized into chapters that track the drafting process:

1. **The Bangsamoro Parliament and its legislative power.** What the Parliament can legislate on, what it cannot, and how to tell the difference.
2. **Types of legislative measures.** Bills, resolutions, codes, and appropriations — how they differ and when to use each.
3. **The anatomy of a bill.** The parts of a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act, from title to effectivity clause, and the rules that govern each one.
4. **Drafting style and language.** How to write clearly. Plain English. Consistent usage. The conventions that make Bangsamoro legislation readable.
5. **Common provisions.** Definitions, repealing clauses, separability clauses, appropriations, effectivity — the provisions that appear in nearly every bill and the mistakes drafters make with them.
6. **Special types of legislation.** Revenue measures, appropriations bills, codes, and local government measures each have their own rules. These chapters cover them.
7. **The legislative process.** From filing to enrollment. What happens to your bill at each stage and what the Rules of Procedure require.
8. **Amendment drafting.** How to amend existing BAAs without creating confusion.
9. **Working with the committee system.** How committee deliberations shape your draft and what a committee report should contain.

### What This Manual Does Not Cover

This manual does not teach substantive policy. It does not tell you whether a particular tax rate is sound fiscal policy or whether a proposed program will work. Those are questions for policy analysts, economists, and the Members of Parliament who will vote on the bill. The drafter's job is to take a policy decision and express it in language that is clear, enforceable, and consistent with the Constitution and the BOL.

The manual also does not cover litigation strategy, judicial proceedings, or executive rulemaking in any depth. Those are separate disciplines. Where they intersect with drafting — judicial doctrines that affect what the Parliament can legislate, for example — the manual addresses them, but only to the extent they shape your work at the drafting table.

## A Note to the Drafter

You are writing law for the Bangsamoro people.

That sentence deserves a moment of reflection. The bills you draft will affect farmers in Maguindanao del Sur and fishermen in Tawi-Tawi. They will shape how schools operate in Lanao del Sur and how businesses form in Cotabato City. They will determine the rights of indigenous peoples in the Special Geographic Area, the protections available to women across the region, and the structure of local government in communities where people have waited decades for genuine self-governance.

The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region is young. The Bangsamoro Organic Law was ratified in 2019. The Bangsamoro Transition Authority is still building the institutions that a future elected Parliament will inherit. Every BAA the Parliament enacts is, in a real sense, a first draft of the region's legal architecture. Some of these laws will be amended over time. Others will stand for generations. The quality of your drafting matters.

### Three Practical Principles

Three principles should guide every bill you draft:

**Write for the person who must follow the law.** A bill is not a press release. It is not a policy statement. It is a set of instructions — to government officials, to private citizens, to courts — about what must be done, what may be done, and what is prohibited. Before you write any provision, ask: will the person who reads this know exactly what is required?

A farmer applying for an agricultural loan should be able to understand the eligibility requirements. A municipal treasurer collecting a tax should be able to determine the rate without guessing. A judge applying a penalty provision should find the elements of the offense stated clearly enough to apply.

If the language is ambiguous, somebody will have to interpret it. That interpretation will happen in a committee hearing, an executive order, a court proceeding, or a conversation at a barangay hall. Each layer of interpretation introduces the possibility of error. The fewer layers your bill requires, the better it serves the people it governs.

**Respect the limits of legislative power.** The Bangsamoro Parliament has broad authority. Article V of the BOL lists fifty-five subject areas. The general welfare clause adds more. But the Parliament's power is not unlimited. The Constitution sets boundaries. The BOL sets boundaries. National laws, where they apply, set boundaries. Judicial decisions — including recent Supreme Court rulings on redistricting and territorial jurisdiction — set boundaries.

Your job as drafter is to know where those boundaries are. When a policy idea pushes against a constitutional limit, say so. When a proposed provision conflicts with national law, flag it. When you are not sure whether the Parliament has authority over a subject, research it before you draft. Chapter 1 of this manual addresses these questions in detail, but no manual can substitute for the drafter's own diligence.

**Be precise, not clever.** Legal writing rewards precision. It does not reward elegance, novelty, or complexity for its own sake. Use short sentences when you can. Use defined terms consistently. Repeat the same word when you mean the same thing — do not use synonyms for variety. A bill that reads like a technical document is doing its job. A bill that reads like literature is probably doing it poorly.

This does not mean legal writing should be impenetrable. The opposite. The clearest legal writing is the writing that uses ordinary words in their ordinary meanings, structured in a way that eliminates doubt. When you must use a technical term, define it. When you must create a complex provision, break it into subsections. When a sentence runs past three lines, split it.

### The Drafter's Role

In the Bangsamoro Parliament, as in most legislative bodies, the drafter often works behind the scenes. The Members of Parliament set policy. The committees deliberate. The Chief Minister and Cabinet bring executive priorities. The drafter takes all of that — the political mandate, the policy goals, the committee discussions — and turns it into a text that will carry the force of law.

This means you will sometimes draft provisions you disagree with. That is part of the job. Your obligation is not to impose your own policy preferences but to express the Legislature's intent as clearly and accurately as you can. When you see a legal problem — a constitutional issue, a conflict with an existing BAA, a gap in the enforcement mechanism — you should raise it. That is part of the job too. But the final policy decision belongs to the Members of Parliament.

It also means you must be willing to tell a committee or a Member that something cannot be done the way they want it done. Not every good policy idea translates into good legislation. Some objectives are better achieved through executive action, through intergovernmental coordination, or through amendments to existing law rather than new legislation. The drafter who identifies these distinctions earns the trust of the Parliament.

### The MP Office and Its Legislative Staff

You work in an MP office. Understanding where you fit in the office structure helps you understand who reviews your work, who approves it, and who you coordinate with.

The standard MP office includes these roles:

| Role | Position Title | Primary Function |
|------|---------------|-----------------|
| **MP** | Member of Parliament | Sets policy direction, issues legislative directives, approves all final outputs |
| **Chief of Staff** | Supervising Political Affairs Officer (SPAO) III or PAO VI | Overall supervision and management, final clearance, political coordination |
| **Legislative Chief** | Supervising Legislative Staff Officer (SLSO) I | Leads all legislative operations — drafting, research, briefers, calendar, Parliament coordination |
| **Legislative Staff Officers** | Legislative Staff Officer I-IV | Conduct research, prepare initial drafts, gather data, support the Legislative Chief |
| **Legislative Support Staff** | Contract of Service or Job-Order | Provide research assistance, document formatting, filing, transmittal, and administrative support |
| **Consultants** | Legal or Legislative Consultant | Provide specialized legal analysis, policy research, or drafting expertise on specific measures |

Not every office has all these positions filled. In a smaller office, one person may serve as both Chief of Staff and Legislative Chief. What matters is that the functions are covered: someone researches, someone drafts, someone reviews, and the MP decides.

The workflow in this manual follows this chain: **Legislative Staff Officers prepare** the initial work. The **Legislative Chief reviews, revises, and manages** the legislative operations. The **Chief of Staff clears** completed work for the MP — ensuring it meets overall office standards. The **MP approves, modifies, or returns** it. The Chief of Staff focuses on overall supervision and political coordination. The Legislative Chief owns the legislative process day-to-day.

### A Living Instrument

The Bangsamoro legal system is still taking shape. New BAAs are enacted each session. Priority codes are being completed. The courts are developing doctrine on the scope of Bangsamoro legislative power. The Parliament's own practices and precedents are forming in real time.

This manual will need to be updated as that body of law grows. Treat it as a starting point — the best available guidance at the time of writing — not as the last word. When a new Supreme Court decision changes the analysis, update the analysis. When the Parliament adopts a new drafting convention, record it. When experience reveals that a recommendation in this manual does not work in practice, revise it.

The purpose of this manual is to help you draft laws that serve the Bangsamoro people well. Laws that are clear, lawful, and effective. Laws that ordinary citizens can read and understand. Laws that government officials can implement without confusion. Laws that courts can apply as the Parliament intended.

That is the standard. Everything in this manual exists to help you meet it.
