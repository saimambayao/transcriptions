Manual of Operations for Bill Drafting in the Bangsamoro Parliament

Chapter I — Introduction

1.1 Purpose

This Manual of Operations standardizes how MP offices handle legislative staff work. It covers every step — from the moment you receive a directive from your Member of Parliament to the filing of the bill, resolution, or amendment with the Bills and Index Division.

The Bill Drafting Guidebook for the Bangsamoro Parliament tells you how to draft. This MOP tells you how to manage the work. It operationalizes the Guidebook into step-by-step procedures: who does what, in what order, using which forms, and to what standard.

Think of it this way. The Guidebook is your reference for the substance of drafting — legal foundations, structure and style, amendment technique. This MOP is your reference for the process around it — assignments, timelines, reviews, coordination, and filing. You need both.

Every MP office in the Bangsamoro Parliament handles the same core functions: research, drafting, review, and filing. But offices differ in size, staffing, and the working style of the MP. This MOP provides a common framework. Your office adapts it to fit your circumstances.

1.2 Scope

This MOP covers:

  • Bill drafting — from legislative directive through enrolled act
  • Resolution drafting — concurrent resolutions, parliamentary resolutions, and proposed resolutions
  • Amendment drafting — committee amendments and floor amendments to pending legislation
  • Legislative briefers — completed staff work (CSW) for plenary and committee deliberations
  • Budget briefers — analysis of appropriations measures and fiscal impact assessments

This MOP applies to:

  • All 80 MP offices of the Bangsamoro Parliament
  • Each MP adopts this MOP for their own office
  • Modifications for office-specific workflows are permitted under Section 1.5

This MOP does NOT cover:

  • Parliamentary procedure on the floor — see Resolution No. 268 (Rules, Procedures, and Practices of the Bangsamoro Parliament) [Resolution 268]
  • Executive branch operations — rulemaking, implementation, and administrative processes belong to the ministries and the Office of the Chief Minister
  • Judicial proceedings — Shari’ah court operations, litigation, and case management fall outside the legislative function

1.3 Legal Basis

This MOP draws its authority from the following instruments:

  1. Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054), Article VII — establishes the Bangsamoro Parliament, defines its legislative power, prescribes requirements for the passage of bills, and sets the framework for the parliamentary system [BOL Art. VII]
  2. Resolution No. 268 — Rules, Procedures, and Practices of the Bangsamoro Parliament — governs parliamentary rules of procedure, committee operations, the Order of Business, and the stages of legislation from first reading to enrollment [Resolution 268]
  3. BAA No. 13 — Bangsamoro Administrative Code — establishes the general administrative framework of the Bangsamoro Government, including the organizational structure of ministries, offices, and agencies that implement enacted legislation [BAA 13]
  4. Bill Drafting Guidebook for the Bangsamoro Parliament (Saidamen R. Mambayao, 2026) — the reference manual for drafting standards, legislative structure, style conventions, and common provisions used in Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts

1.4 Definition of Terms

Term Definition
BAA Bangsamoro Autonomy Act. A law enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament. The equivalent of a national statute at the regional level.
BEP Budget Execution Plan. The document that details how an appropriated budget will be spent, broken down by program, activity, and project.
Bill A proposed law filed before the Parliament. It becomes a BAA upon passage on third reading, enrollment, and approval by the Chief Minister.
Bills and Index Division The office within the Parliament Secretariat responsible for receiving filed bills and resolutions, assigning numbers, and maintaining the legislative index.
BOL Bangsamoro Organic Law. Republic Act No. 11054 — the foundational law that established the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao.
Briefer (Legislative) A concise document that summarizes a bill’s background, key provisions, policy implications, and recommendation. It supports the MP’s decision-making during committee hearings or plenary sessions.
Briefer (Budget) A concise document that analyzes an appropriations measure or fiscal provision, including cost estimates, funding sources, and budget impact.
Committee Report The formal report issued by a standing committee after deliberating on a referred bill. It contains findings, the committee’s recommended version of the bill, and a recommendation for passage or non-passage.
Completed Staff Work (CSW) A finished product — a draft bill, briefer, amendment, or analysis — that has been researched, drafted, reviewed, and is ready for the MP’s decision. The MP should only need to approve, disapprove, or return it for revision.
Enrolled Bill The final, authenticated text of a BAA after passage on third reading. This is the version signed by the Speaker and the Chief Minister.
Explanatory Note The narrative section of a bill that explains the policy rationale, the problem being addressed, the proposed solution, and the legal basis for the measure. It is not part of the enacted law but accompanies the bill at filing.
First Reading The stage where a filed bill is read by title, entered into the Journal, and referred to the appropriate committee. No debate occurs.
Interpellation The period during second reading when Members of Parliament may ask questions about the bill to the sponsor or committee chair. Interpellation follows the sponsorship speech.
Legislative Directive An instruction from the MP to staff to research, draft, or prepare a legislative measure or document. This is the starting point of the workflow.
MOP Manual of Operations. This document.
Order of Business The agenda of the plenary session, prepared by the Parliament Secretariat, listing the items to be taken up in the prescribed sequence under Resolution No. 268.
Plenary A session of the full Parliament. Distinguished from committee meetings, which involve only committee members.
Resolution A formal expression of the Parliament’s will that does not carry the force of law. Types include concurrent resolutions, parliamentary resolutions, and proposed resolutions.
Second Reading The stage where a bill is debated on the floor. It includes the sponsorship speech, interpellation, period of amendments, and voting on the bill’s approval on second reading.
Third Reading The final voting stage. The bill is voted on in its entirety, with no further amendments. Passage requires a majority of Members present constituting a quorum, unless the BOL or Rules prescribe a higher threshold.

1.5 Amendment Procedures

This MOP is a living document. As your office gains experience and the Parliament’s practices evolve, the MOP should evolve with them.

Who may propose amendments:

  • Any staff member in the MP office may propose amendments to this MOP. Amendments may address new workflows, corrections, improvements, or changes required by updated parliamentary rules.

Review and approval process:

  1. The staff member submits the proposed amendment in writing, identifying the section to be changed and the reason for the change.
  2. The Chief of Staff or Legislative Chief reviews the proposal for consistency with the Guidebook and Resolution No. 268.
  3. The MP approves or disapproves the amendment.
  4. Approved amendments are recorded in the Amendment Log and take effect immediately unless the MP specifies otherwise.

Amendment Log:

Date Section Amended Description of Change Approved By

Annual review. The Chief of Staff conducts a mandatory annual review of this MOP, no later than the first month of each calendar year. The review identifies sections that need updating based on changes in parliamentary rules, office experience, or the enactment of new legislation affecting the drafting process.

Chapter II — MP Office Structure and Roles

2.1 Standard MP Office Model

No two MP offices are identical. Some have twenty staff members. Some have three. The workflow remains the same — only the distribution of tasks changes.

This MOP assigns duties to roles, not to positions or individuals. In a large office, each role may be filled by a dedicated person. In a small office, one person may fill two or three roles. What matters is that every role is covered and every task has a clear owner.

The standard MP office model includes these roles:

  • Member of Parliament (MP) — the principal. Sets policy direction, issues legislative directives, and approves all final outputs.
  • Chief of Staff (Supervising Political Affairs Officer [SPAO] III or PAO VI) — manages office operations, conducts final internal review of all documents before they reach the MP, and coordinates with other MP offices and Parliament bodies.
  • Legislative Chief (Supervising Legislative Staff Officer [SLSO] I) — leads research and drafting, supervises legislative staff, and ensures all outputs conform to the Bill Drafting Guidebook.
  • Legislative Staff Officers — conduct research, prepare initial drafts, gather data, and support the Legislative Chief on assigned measures.
  • Legislative Support Staff (Contract of Service or Job-Order arrangement) — provide research assistance, data gathering, document formatting, and administrative support for legislative operations.
  • Legal or Legislative Consultants — provide specialized legal analysis, policy research, or drafting expertise on specific measures as engaged by the MP.
  • Other Support Staff — handle document management, filing, transmittal to the Bills and Index Division, scheduling, and office coordination.

Scaling the model. In a smaller office, one person may fill multiple roles — the Chief of Staff may also function as Legislative Chief, or a single Legislative Staff Officer may handle both research and drafting. In a larger office, you might have multiple Legislative Staff Officers assigned to different committees, dedicated consultants for complex legislation, and full administrative support. The model adapts. The roles stay constant.

2.2 Role Descriptions

Member of Parliament (MP)

Primary responsibilities:

  • Issues legislative directives to initiate the drafting process
  • Sets policy direction and priorities for the office’s legislative agenda
  • Approves all final outputs: bills, resolutions, amendments, briefers, and interpellation questions
  • Sponsors bills and resolutions on the floor
  • Represents the office in plenary and committee proceedings

Authority level: Final decision-maker on all legislative outputs. Approves or returns all documents. Only the MP can direct the filing of a bill or resolution.

Reporting line: None. The MP is the principal.

Key outputs: Approved bills, approved resolutions, sponsorship speeches, voting decisions.

Chief of Staff (SPAO III / PAO VI)

Primary responsibilities:

  • Provides overall supervision and management of the MP office
  • Ensures the office’s work plan and priorities align with the MP’s legislative agenda
  • Conducts final clearance of documents before presenting them to the MP
  • Manages political coordination — relationships with other MP offices, party matters, and external stakeholders
  • Oversees administrative operations — budget, personnel, office logistics
  • Serves as the primary liaison to the Parliament leadership (Speaker’s Office, Floor Leader)

Authority level: Can clear documents for MP review. Can return outputs to the Legislative Chief for revision. Does not draft or revise legislative content — that authority belongs to the Legislative Chief. Cannot approve final outputs for filing — that authority belongs to the MP.

Reporting line: Reports directly to the MP.

Key outputs: Cleared documents for MP decision, office work plans, political coordination memoranda, staff performance oversight.

Legislative Chief (SLSO I)

Primary responsibilities:

  • Leads and supervises all legislative operations — research, drafting, briefer preparation, and amendment work
  • Supervises Legislative Staff Officers, support staff, and coordinates with consultants
  • Manages the legislative calendar — tracks committee hearings, plenary schedules, filing deadlines, and co-authorship timelines
  • Ensures all drafts comply with the Bill Drafting Guidebook — structure, style, legal basis, and common provisions
  • Conducts legal and policy review of drafts for consistency with the BOL, existing BAAs, and national law
  • Coordinates with Parliament bodies — Bills and Index Division (filing), Committee Secretariat (hearings), and other MP offices (co-authorship, consolidated bills)
  • Prepares completed staff work (CSW) packages for Chief of Staff clearance

Authority level: Can assign tasks to Legislative Staff Officers, support staff, and consultants. Can revise and finalize drafts. Can coordinate directly with Parliament bodies on legislative matters. Submits completed work to the Chief of Staff for clearance before it reaches the MP.

Reporting line: Reports to the Chief of Staff.

Key outputs: Draft bills, draft resolutions, draft amendments, legislative briefers, budget briefers, research memoranda, CSW packages, legislative calendar.

Legislative Staff Officers

Primary responsibilities:

  • Conduct background research — existing BAAs, national laws, BOL provisions, comparative legislation, and policy data
  • Prepare initial drafts of bills, resolutions, briefers, and amendments
  • Gather data and evidence for explanatory notes and policy justifications
  • Maintain the office’s legislative research files and reference materials
  • Assist the Legislative Chief during committee hearings — prepare notes, track amendments, record proceedings

Authority level: Can prepare and submit initial drafts for review. Cannot finalize or clear any document independently.

Reporting line: Report to the Legislative Chief.

Key outputs: Research memoranda, initial draft bills, initial draft briefers, data compilations, hearing notes.

Legislative Support Staff (COS / JO)

Primary responsibilities:

  • Provide research assistance — data gathering, source compilation, document formatting
  • Assist Legislative Staff Officers in preparing draft documents and presentations
  • Handle document management — filing, reproduction, digital organization
  • Manage transmittal of bills and resolutions to the Bills and Index Division
  • Track the status of filed measures — committee referrals, hearing schedules, plenary calendar
  • Manage office correspondence, scheduling, and logistics
  • Maintain the Amendment Log for this MOP (Section 1.5)

Authority level: Can transmit documents to the Bills and Index Division upon instruction from the Chief of Staff or Legislative Chief. Cannot modify substantive content of any document independently.

Reporting line: Report to the Legislative Chief or Chief of Staff as assigned.

Key outputs: Transmittal records, filing confirmations, status tracking reports, formatted documents, correspondence logs.

Legal or Legislative Consultants

Primary responsibilities:

  • Provide specialized legal analysis on complex legislative matters (constitutional issues, Shari’ah considerations, fiscal implications)
  • Draft or review technical provisions requiring subject-matter expertise
  • Conduct policy research on specific topics as directed by the MP or Legislative Chief
  • Advise on legislative strategy — timing, co-authorship, committee dynamics
  • May assist in preparing interpellation questions and proposed amendments for specialized bills

Authority level: Advisory. Consultants may draft or revise documents but cannot clear or approve them. All consultant outputs pass through the Legislative Chief and Chief of Staff review chain.

Reporting line: Report to the MP or Chief of Staff as specified in the engagement terms.

Key outputs: Legal opinions, policy analyses, specialized draft provisions, advisory memoranda.

2.4 Coordination with Parliament Bodies

Your office does not work in isolation. The Legislative Chief is the primary coordinator with Parliament bodies on legislative matters. The Chief of Staff handles political coordination (coalition building, party matters, Speaker’s Office liaison). Build working relationships with each body.

Bills and Index Division

The Bills and Index Division is your primary point of contact for filing. It is the office within the Parliament Secretariat that receives all bills and resolutions, assigns official numbers, and maintains the master legislative index.

Key coordination points:

  • Filing requirements. Before you file, confirm the Division’s current requirements for format, number of copies, and accompanying documents (explanatory note, sponsorship speech, cost estimates where required). Requirements may change by session.
  • Number assignment. The Division assigns the official bill or resolution number upon filing. You do not choose your own number.
  • Status tracking. The Division can confirm whether a measure has been referred to committee, scheduled for hearing, or placed on the Order of Business.
  • Consolidated bills. When multiple MPs file similar measures, the Division tracks related filings. Coordinate early to avoid duplicating another office’s work.

Committee Secretariat

Each standing committee has a Committee Secretariat that manages committee operations. When your bill is referred to committee, the Committee Secretariat becomes your key coordination partner.

Key coordination points:

  • Referral notification. After first reading, the Speaker refers the bill to the appropriate committee. The Committee Secretariat will schedule hearings.
  • Hearing schedules. Monitor the committee calendar. Your office should prepare a briefer before each hearing and ensure the MP or a designated staff member attends.
  • Technical working groups. Committees often form technical working groups to refine bills. Your Legislative Chief should participate or coordinate closely with the TWG.
  • Committee reports. The Committee Secretariat prepares the committee report. Review the reported version of the bill carefully — the committee may have adopted amendments that change your original text.

Parliament Secretariat

The Parliament Secretariat manages the overall operations of the Parliament, including the plenary calendar.

Key coordination points:

  • Plenary calendar. The Secretariat sets the plenary session schedule and the Order of Business. Track when your bill is calendared for second reading or third reading.
  • Session journals. The Secretariat produces the session journal — the official record of plenary proceedings. Use it to verify votes, amendments adopted on the floor, and the exact text approved on second reading.
  • Administrative requirements. The Secretariat handles administrative matters — session attendance, office allocations, and staff accreditation.

Other MP Offices

Legislative work often involves coordination with other Members.

Key coordination points:

  • Co-authorship. When another MP joins as co-author, coordinate on the final text before filing. All co-authors should agree on the version that goes to the Bills and Index Division.
  • Consolidated bills. When your bill and another MP’s bill address the same subject, the committee may consolidate them. Coordinate with the other office early to align provisions and avoid unnecessary conflict during deliberations.
  • Coalition building. Before filing a major bill, your office may consult with other MPs to secure support. The Chief of Staff handles this political coordination. The Legislative Chief handles the technical alignment of bill provisions.
  • Amendment coordination. During second reading, coordinate with allied offices on proposed amendments. Uncoordinated amendments from friendly offices can create confusion on the floor.

Chapter III — Legislative Drafting Operations

3.1 Overview

This is the operational core of the Manual. Every legislative measure your office produces follows a defined workflow with assigned responsibilities, deliverables, and timelines.

The Bangsamoro Parliament works with three types of legislative measures:

  1. Bills — create, amend, or repeal law. Seven-stage process.
  2. Resolutions — express sentiment, opinion, or request. Five-stage process.
  3. Amendments — modify a pending bill during committee or plenary. Four-stage process.

Each workflow below identifies the responsible party, timeline, output, and Guidebook reference. Adapt timelines based on complexity.

3.2 Bill Drafting Workflow

The total timeline ranges from 15 to 45 working days depending on complexity.

Stage 1: Legislative Directive

The MP issues a policy direction. Capture it, document it, and get approval to proceed.

Procedure:

  1. Receive the MP’s directive (written brief, verbal instruction, or relayed through Chief of Staff).
  2. Listen for five essentials: the policy problem, target beneficiaries, urgency, prior attempts, and non-negotiables.
  3. Prepare the Initial Legislative Template capturing: working title, policy problem, proposed solution, target beneficiaries, scope, lead author, potential co-authors, target filing date, related legislation, and special instructions.
  4. Submit the template to the MP for review and approval.
  5. Record the approved template in the office legislative tracker.
Field Detail
Responsible MP (directs) –> Legislative Chief (documents) –> Chief of Staff (coordinates)
Timeline Day 0 (single meeting plus 24-hour written follow-up)
Output Approved Initial Legislative Template
Form Legislative Directive Form (Appendix H)
Reference Guidebook Chapter 2, Section 2.2

Key rule: Do not begin research until the MP approves the template. A documented starting point prevents scope creep and misaligned expectations.

Stage 2: Research and Analysis

Build the legal, policy, and factual foundation for the bill.

Procedure:

  1. Define research questions from the approved template: constitutional authority, BOL powers, existing BAAs, national laws, policy data, fiscal implications, Shari’ah considerations.
  2. Submit a written research request to the Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS) with a copy of the template.
  3. Conduct parallel research: (a) 1987 Constitution, (b) BOL Articles V, IX, XII, (c) national laws and IRRs, (d) existing BAAs and resolutions, (e) policy data and comparative legislation, (f) implementing agency capacity and fiscal implications.
  4. Prepare the Legislative Research Briefer: brief description, related laws, policy and legal analysis, impact assessment, Shari’ah and gender considerations, recommendations.
  5. Present the briefer to the MP for approval.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Staff Officers (conduct research) –> Legislative Chief (reviews and compiles briefer) –> MP (approves)
Timeline 3-10 working days depending on complexity
Output Approved Legislative Research Briefer
Reference Guidebook Chapter 2, Sections 2.3.1-2.3.4

Complexity guide: - Simple (narrow amendatory bill, single-issue bill): 3-5 days - Moderate (new regulatory framework, multi-agency bill): 5-7 days - Complex (new code, comprehensive reform, cross-cutting policy): 7-10 days

Stage 3: Consultation

Test the bill concept against stakeholders. Consultation catches implementation problems that desk research cannot.

Procedure:

  1. Identify stakeholders: implementing agencies, affected LGUs, CSOs, subject-matter experts, communities.
  2. Conduct consultations. Document each meeting: date, attendees, issues raised, recommendations.
  3. Coordinate with the relevant committee secretariat early.
  4. Assess gender and human rights impact. If the bill affects indigenous peoples, consult their representatives per customary processes.
  5. Compile a Consultation Report: feedback, consensus areas, unresolved issues.
  6. Present to the MP with recommended adjustments.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Chief (leads consultations) –> Legislative Staff Officers (support, documents) –> Chief of Staff (coordinates agency access)
Timeline 5-15 working days
Output Consultation Report
Reference Guidebook Chapter 2, Section 2.5

Tip: Schedule consultations during Stage 2. Research and consultation can overlap.

Stage 4: Drafting

Write the bill following the Guidebook’s standard anatomy and style conventions.

Procedure:

  1. Prepare a working outline listing every section in order.
  2. Draft the bill: caption, long title, enacting clause, preliminary provisions (short title, declaration of policy, definitions, scope), substantive provisions (general-to-specific, rights-to-obligations, prohibitions-to-penalties), administrative provisions (implementing agency, IRR timeline, reporting), appropriation clause, penal provisions, final provisions (separability, repealing, effectivity).
  3. Prepare the Comparative Matrix (bill vs. existing laws).
  4. Apply style rules: active voice, present tense, “shall/may/must” used correctly, numbers in words and figures, gender-neutral language.
  5. Draft the Explanatory Note: problem, why legislation is needed, what the bill does, BOL authority.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Chief (drafts bill and explanatory note) –> Legislative Staff Officers (assist with research integration and formatting)
Timeline 3-7 working days
Output Draft bill text + Explanatory Note + Comparative Matrix
Reference Guidebook Chapters 3 (Anatomy), 4 (Explanatory Note), 5 (Resolutions — if resolution provisions are included), 6 (Legislative Style)

Stage 5: Internal Review

Review the draft systematically before the MP sees it.

Procedure:

  1. Run the Pre-Drafting Checklist (Appendix D) — confirm all preparatory work is complete.
  2. Run the Quality Review Checklist (Appendix E) — four categories: legal authority (14 items), structure and completeness (28 items), language and style (18 items), internal consistency (10 items).
  3. Conduct the Interpellation Test — read the bill as a skeptical MP. Ask: What problem does this solve? Who benefits? Will it work? Could it be challenged?
  4. Conduct specialized reviews: gender sensitivity, human rights, BOL conformity (Guidebook Ch. 11, Sections 11.4-11.6).
  5. Chief of Staff reviews for policy alignment with the MP’s intent.
  6. Compile comments and revise.
Field Detail
Responsible Chief of Staff (reviews for policy alignment) –> Legislative Chief (conducts technical review, revises) –> Legislative Staff Officers (assist with checklist verification)
Timeline 2-3 working days
Output Reviewed draft with revisions incorporated
Reference Guidebook Chapter 11; Appendices D and E

Stage 6: MP Approval

Present the final draft to the MP for decision.

Procedure:

  1. Prepare a briefing package: revised bill text, explanatory note, legislative research briefer (summary), consultation report (summary), and a one-page decision memo highlighting key choices and any unresolved issues.
  2. Schedule a presentation meeting with the MP.
  3. Walk the MP through the bill section by section. Flag provisions where the MP must make a policy choice.
  4. The MP decides: approve as-is, modify specific provisions, or return for further work.
  5. If modifications are requested, incorporate them and re-run the relevant checklist items.
  6. Obtain the MP’s final approval to proceed to filing.
Field Detail
Responsible MP (decides) –> Chief of Staff (presents briefing) –> Legislative Chief (incorporates final changes)
Timeline 1-3 working days
Output MP-approved bill ready for filing
Reference Guidebook Chapter 2, Section 2.6

Stage 7: Filing

Prepare the filing package and submit to the Bills and Index Division.

Procedure:

  1. Prepare the filing package:
  2. Signed bill text (printed copy + electronic copy)
  3. Explanatory Note (signed by the principal author)
  4. Co-authorship letters (if applicable)
  5. Secure co-authors if planned: circulate the bill to target MPs, brief them on the bill’s purpose, and obtain signed co-authorship confirmations.
  6. File with the Secretary-General (Bills and Index Division). The Secretary-General assigns the bill number. Note: the printed copy prevails over the electronic copy in case of discrepancy.
  7. Track the bill’s assignment — confirm committee referral after Second Reading.
  8. Update the office legislative tracker with the bill number, filing date, and committee referral.
  9. Coordinate with the Majority Floor Leader’s office regarding calendar scheduling.
Field Detail
Responsible Support Staff (prepares and files package) –> Legislative Chief (coordinates co-authorship and committee referral) –> Chief of Staff (manages political coordination)
Timeline 1 working day for filing; co-authorship may take 1-5 additional days
Output Filed bill with assigned number
Reference Guidebook Chapter 2, Section 2.7; Chapter 7, Sections 7.2-7.3

Post-filing: Prepare for committee hearings — anticipate questions, draft talking points, and prepare potential committee amendments. Monitor progress through the stages of legislation.

3.3 Resolution Drafting Workflow

Resolutions follow a shorter workflow. Simple and concurrent resolutions can be filed and adopted in a single session. Speed does not excuse carelessness.

Types of Resolutions

Type Definition Process
Simple Sentiment, opinion, or request. Most common. Simplified — plenary
Concurrent Cabinet and Parliament on shared matters. Simplified
Joint Committee Two or more committees. Majority of all. Fuller deliberation

When to Use a Resolution (Not a Bill)

A resolution is the right tool when the goal is to express, urge, commend, condemn, or request. A bill is the right tool when the goal is to create, require, prohibit, or appropriate. If the measure creates a legal obligation, use a bill.

5-Stage Process

Stage 1: Directive

  1. Receive the MP’s instruction. Resolutions typically begin with a brief oral or written request — less formal than a bill directive.
  2. Confirm: Is a resolution the correct instrument? Check the five warning signs that a bill is needed instead (creates a government body, imposes penalties, appropriates funds, requires action from private citizens, amends an existing BAA).
  3. Document the directive with the topic, purpose, and target timeline.
Field Detail
Responsible MP (directs) –> Legislative Chief (documents)
Timeline Day 0

Stage 2: Research and Verification

  1. Verify the facts. If the resolution congratulates someone, confirm the full name, achievement, ranking, and date. If it condemns an incident, verify date, location, and confirmed facts.
  2. Verify the legal basis. If citing the BOL, Constitution, or a national law, confirm the exact article, section, and wording.
  3. Check for prior resolutions. Search the Parliament’s records to ensure no duplicate resolution already exists on the same subject.
  4. Confirm the right instrument. If the MP’s intent requires creating a legal obligation, redirect to the bill workflow.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Staff Officers (verify) –> Legislative Chief (reviews)
Timeline 1-3 working days
Output Verified facts and citations
Reference Guidebook Chapter 5, Section 5.3.1

Stage 3: Drafting

  1. Draft the resolution following the standard anatomy:
  2. Caption (Parliament identification, session)
  3. Resolution number (assigned upon filing — leave blank in draft)
  4. Title (begins with “A Resolution Urging…,” “A Resolution Commending…,” etc.)
  5. Author line (MP name and signature)
  6. WHEREAS preamble — recitals in deductive order:
    • Legal and constitutional foundation (broadest basis first)
    • Background and context
    • Specific facts (names, dates, places)
    • Parliament’s interest
    • Conclusion and transition
  7. “NOW, THEREFORE, be it” transition
  8. Operative clause (“RESOLVED, as it is hereby resolved by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament, to…”)
  9. Additional operative clauses (RESOLVED, FURTHER / FURTHERMORE / FINALLY)
  10. Furnishing clause (RESOLVED, FINALLY — copies to specific recipients)
  11. Adoption line (both Islamic and Gregorian calendar dates — filled in after adoption)
  12. Certification blocks (Secretary-General certifies correct; Speaker attests)
  13. Apply formatting: WHEREAS and RESOLVED in bold; semicolons after each recital (colon after the final one); consistent capitalization of institutional names.
  14. Keep it proportionate: 3-5 WHEREAS clauses for a straightforward commendation; more for complex policy resolutions.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Chief (drafts) –> Legislative Staff Officers (assist with formatting)
Timeline 1-2 working days
Output Draft resolution
Reference Guidebook Chapter 5, Sections 5.4.1-5.4.3

Stage 4: Review

  1. Run the Resolution Drafting Checklist (Guidebook Chapter 5, end of chapter):
  2. Content: Names correct? Dates verified? Legal citations verified? No duplicate resolution? Does not attempt to legislate?
  3. Structure: Correct session caption? Authors listed? Title in all capitals? WHEREAS recitals in deductive order? Standard operative formula used? Furnishing clause present? Islamic and Gregorian dates? Certification blocks present?
  4. Format: WHEREAS bold? RESOLVED bold? Correct punctuation? Consistent capitalization?
  5. Chief of Staff or MP reviews for accuracy and tone.
Field Detail
Responsible Chief of Staff (reviews) –> Legislative Chief (revises)
Timeline 1 working day
Output Final draft resolution
Reference Guidebook Chapter 5, Drafting Checklist

Stage 5: Filing

  1. The MP signs the resolution.
  2. File with the Secretary-General (printed + electronic copy). The Secretary-General assigns the proposed resolution number.
  3. Secure co-authors if applicable.
  4. Track when the resolution is reported in plenary during “Resolutions, Messages, Memorials and Communications.”
  5. Coordinate with the Majority Floor Leader if plenary action is expected in the same session.
Field Detail
Responsible Support Staff (files) –> Legislative Chief (coordinates)
Timeline 1 working day
Output Filed resolution with assigned number
Reference Guidebook Chapter 5, Section 5.3.3; Chapter 7, Section 7.1.3

3.4 Amendment Drafting Workflow

Amendments refine legislation during committee or plenary deliberation. Every amendment reduces to three operations: insertion, deletion, or substitution.

Types of Amendments

Type Origin Timing
Committee Committee that scrutinized the bill Committee stage; before floor amendments
Floor Individual MP during plenary Period of amendments, after committee amendments
Substitute bill Replaces entire bill text Committee or floor; must cover same subject

Key Rules (Resolution No. 268)

  • Germaneness: No riders. The amendment must relate to the bill’s subject matter.
  • Specificity: Identify the exact page, line, section, and subsection being amended.
  • Writing requirement: Substitution amendments must be submitted in writing. Best practice: put all amendments in writing.
  • Withdrawal: Any amendment may be withdrawn before the vote.

4-Stage Process

Stage 1: Identify Issues

  1. Review the bill text — use the committee report version, not an earlier draft.
  2. Identify the specific provision that needs change. Classify the type of change: insertion, deletion, or substitution.
  3. Determine whether this is a committee amendment (arising from committee scrutiny) or a floor amendment (to be proposed by the MP during plenary).
  4. For committee amendments: coordinate with the committee chair and secretariat.
  5. For floor amendments: register with the Majority Floor Leader to secure recognition during the period of amendments.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Chief (identifies issues from bill review or MP instruction) –> MP (confirms intent)
Timeline 1-2 working days; triggered by committee briefer or plenary preparation

Stage 2: Draft Amendment Text

  1. Get the exact text of the provision being amended — from the committee report version as modified by any already-adopted amendments.
  2. Draft the amendment using the standard formulations:
Operation Standard Language
Insert words “On page [X], line [Y], after the word ‘[existing word],’ insert the words ‘[new words].’”
Insert subsection “A new subsection (x) is hereby inserted in Section [X] to read as follows:”
Insert section “A new Section [X] is hereby inserted after Section [Y] to read as follows:”
Delete words “On page [X], line [Y], delete the words ‘[words to be deleted].’”
Delete subsection “Subsection (x) of Section [X] is hereby deleted.”
Delete section “Section [X] is hereby deleted.”
Substitute words “On page [X], line [Y], the word ‘[old word]’ is hereby amended to read ‘[new word].’”
Substitute subsection “Subsection (x) of Section [X] is hereby amended to read as follows:”
Substitute section “Section [X] is hereby amended to read as follows:”
  1. Match the style of the bill — same terminology, same verb forms, same naming conventions.
  2. Account for renumbering if inserting or deleting subsections or sections. State redesignation explicitly.
  3. Check cross-references — if renumbering changes a section number referenced elsewhere, update or note the adjustment.
  4. Keep each amendment focused: one amendment, one change. Do not bundle multiple changes.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Chief (drafts amendment text) –> Legislative Staff (verifies cross-references and renumbering)
Timeline 1 working day; same-day for urgent floor amendments
Output Written amendment text
Reference Guidebook Chapter 10, Sections 10.4.1-10.4.3

Stage 3: Justify

  1. Prepare a brief justification (one paragraph) explaining: the problem with the current text, the proposed solution, and why the new text is better.
  2. For committee amendments: include justifications in the committee report alongside the proposed changes. Use narrative format or comparative matrix format (original vs. amended).
  3. For floor amendments: brief the MP so they can explain the amendment when recognized by the Speaker.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Chief (prepares justification) –> MP (delivers on the floor)
Timeline Same day as drafting
Output Amendment justification note
Reference Guidebook Chapter 10, Sections 10.2-10.3

Stage 4: Submit

  1. For committee amendments: Include in the committee report presented to plenary. The sponsor presents them during the period of amendments; they are taken up before floor amendments.
  2. For floor amendments: Submit the written text to the Secretary-General and the Majority Floor Leader. The MP is recognized during the period of amendments to present and move the amendment.
  3. Track the outcome: accepted, modified, or rejected. If adopted, verify the amendment is correctly incorporated into the bill text for Third Reading.
Field Detail
Responsible Legislative Chief (submits written text) –> MP (moves amendment on the floor) –> Support Staff (tracks outcome)
Timeline Committee amendments: included in committee report. Floor amendments: submitted before or during the plenary session.
Output Submitted amendment; recorded outcome
Reference Guidebook Chapter 10, Sections 10.2-10.3; Chapter 7, Section 7.5

# Chapter IV — Legislative Briefer and CSW Operations

4.1 Overview

Briefers are decision-support documents. They give the MP everything needed to participate in a legislative proceeding — the analysis, the questions, the recommendations, and the prepared materials. The MP reads the briefer, decides, and acts.

All briefers follow the Completed Staff Work (CSW) framework. The staff does the full analysis. The MP makes the decision. If the MP has to go look something up after reading your briefer, the staff work is incomplete.

Two types of briefers are covered in this chapter:

  • Legislative briefers — for bills and resolutions at any stage of the lawmaking process
  • Budget briefers — for the annual Bangsamoro Expenditure Program (BEP) deliberation

Both types use the same CSW standards. Both pass through the same quality pipeline (Chapter V). The difference is scope: legislative briefers analyze a single measure; budget briefers analyze an agency’s entire budget proposal.

Reference: Guidebook Chapter 8 (Legislative Briefer), Chapter 9 (Budget Briefer), Appendix F (Briefer Template), Appendix G (Budget Template).

4.2 Legislative Briefer Workflow

When to Prepare a Briefer

Prepare a legislative briefer in any of these situations:

  • For bills your office drafted — defense preparation before committee hearings or plenary
  • For bills filed by other MPs — analysis and position-taking on referred measures
  • For committee referrals and hearings — any measure scheduled before a committee the MP sits on
  • For interpellation and plenary deliberation — when a bill reaches second reading

Not every bill needs a full briefer. Use this rule: if the MP will speak, vote, or take a public position, prepare a briefer.

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1 — Receive the trigger. A bill is filed. A committee hearing is scheduled. A plenary date is set. The Chief of Staff identifies the trigger and initiates the briefer assignment.

Step 2 — Determine briefer type.

Type When to Use Depth
Full Complex, priority, or controversial bill; MP-sponsored; budget >PhP10M All 15 sections (App. F)
Short-form Routine measure, admin adjustment, minor amendment Sections 1-3, 7, 12 minimum

Step 3 — Assign staff. The Legislative Chief assigns the briefer to specific staff. One person owns the briefer. Other staff contribute research to specific sections.

Step 4 — Complete the briefer sections.

The 15 sections of Appendix F are grouped into three blocks:

Analysis Block (Sections 1-6):

Section Content
1. Brief Description Bill snapshot — number, title, author, status, next proceeding
2. Related Laws BOL provisions, existing BAAs, national laws, Shari’ah considerations
3. Impact Assessment Alignment with MP’s agenda, BDP goals, GAD assessment
4. Implementing Agencies Who implements, capacity gaps, coordination requirements
5. Implementation Analysis Timeline, phasing, IRR requirements, transition provisions
6. Fiscal Impact Cost estimates, funding source, budget implications

Proceeding Preparation Block (Sections 7-11):

Section Content
7. Previous Proceedings What happened at earlier hearings, positions taken, amendments adopted
8. Interpellation Questions Prepared questions the MP can ask during deliberation
9. Proposed Amendments Draft amendment text with justification for each
10. Speech / Manifestation Draft sponsorship speech or 1-minute manifestation
11. Vote Statement Recommended position — support, oppose, or abstain — with rationale

Administrative Block (Sections 12-15):

Section Content
12. Matters for MP’s Information Political context, media coverage, stakeholder positions
13. Action Log Date-stamped record of all actions taken on this briefer
14. Supervisory Review Chief of Staff sign-off confirming completeness and quality
15. MP Approval MP’s decision — approved, modified, or returned

Step 5 — Submit for review. The drafter submits the completed briefer to the Legislative Chief, who reviews for accuracy and compliance with the Guidebook. The Legislative Chief then forwards it to the Chief of Staff for supervisory review.

Step 6 — Present to the MP. The Chief of Staff presents the briefer to the MP. The MP reviews and decides: approve, modify, or return for further work. Mark the decision in Section 15.

Step 7 — Post-proceeding update. After the hearing or plenary session, annotate the briefer with actual outcomes. Record what questions were asked, what amendments passed, what position the MP took, and what follow-up is needed. This becomes the institutional record.

Timeline

Briefer Type Deadline
Full briefer 5-10 working days before the proceeding
Short-form briefer 2-3 working days before the proceeding

Build backward from the proceeding date. If the committee hearing is on a Thursday, a full briefer should reach the MP by the prior Monday at latest — giving the MP time to read, ask questions, and request changes.

4.3 Budget Briefer Workflow

When to Prepare a Budget Briefer

The trigger is the annual BEP submission. When the Chief Minister submits the Bangsamoro Expenditure Program to the Parliament, every MP office prepares budget briefers for the agencies the MP will scrutinize.

Prepare one briefer per agency. The MP’s portfolio typically includes:

  • Agencies under the MP’s committee assignments
  • Agencies relevant to the MP’s legislative agenda or advocacy areas
  • Agencies the Chief of Staff identifies as strategic priorities

Step-by-Step Process

Step 1 — Receive the BEP. The Parliament distributes the Bangsamoro Expenditure Program to all MP offices. The Chief of Staff secures a copy and begins assignment planning.

Step 2 — Identify agencies. The Chief of Staff determines which agencies the MP will review based on committee assignments, advocacy areas, and strategic priorities. Create a master list.

Step 3 — Assign staff. Assign one briefer per agency to a specific staff member. For large agencies (MFBM, MOH, MBHTE), the Legislative Chief may lead the briefer personally.

Step 4 — Complete the briefer sections.

The 16 sections of Appendix G are grouped into four blocks:

Agency Profile Block (Sections 1-4):

Section Content
1. Agency Profile Name, mandate, organizational structure, key officials
2. Legal Mandate Enabling legislation, BOL provisions, relevant BAAs
3. Strategic Goals Agency goals aligned with the BDP 2023-2028
4. Key Performance Indicators KPIs, targets, and actual performance for the past 2-3 years

Budget Analysis Block (Sections 5-7):

Section Content
5. Budget Comparison 3-year trend; PS/MOOE/CO breakdown; absorption rate; variance analysis
6. Existing PPAS (Tier 1) Continuing programs, activities, and projects — are they performing?
7. New PPAS (Tier 2) Proposed new spending — is it justified, costed, and ready to implement?

Scrutiny Block (Sections 8-14):

Section Content
8. Beneficiary Analysis Who benefits from the agency’s programs? Distribution across provinces?
9. Laws for Implementation Which enacted BAAs require funding through this agency’s budget?
10. GAD Budget Review Does the agency meet the 5% GAD allocation? Is the GAD plan substantive?
11. Special Provisions Analysis of any special provisions attached to the agency’s budget
12. Budget Scrutiny Questions Prepared questions for the Committee on Finance hearing
13. Recommendations Staff recommendation — approve, reduce, increase, or realign specific items
14. 1-Minute Manifestation Draft statement the MP can deliver during plenary budget deliberation

Administrative Block (Sections 15-16):

Section Content
15. Action Log Date-stamped record of all actions taken on this briefer
16. Approval Supervisory review and MP sign-off

Step 5 — Submit for review. Same process as the legislative briefer: drafter to Legislative Chief to Chief of Staff.

Step 6 — Present to the MP. The MP reviews the complete set of budget briefers. The Chief of Staff may schedule a dedicated briefing session for all agency budgets at once.

Step 7 — Post-hearing update. After the Committee on Finance hearing, annotate the briefer with actual outcomes — questions raised, commitments made by the agency, amendments adopted, and follow-up items.

Timeline

Milestone Deadline
Budget briefers completed 2 weeks before the Committee on Finance hearing
Data gathering begins The day the BEP is filed with Parliament

Budget briefers take longer than legislative briefers because of the data requirements. Start early. Waiting until the week before the hearing guarantees incomplete analysis.

Key Budget Thresholds to Check

Every budget briefer should verify these statutory benchmarks (from BAA No. 84 and the annual BBM):

Threshold Standard Source
PS cap Not more than 45% of total revenue source BBM
Development spending floor At least 20% of total annual appropriation BBM
DRRM allocation At least 5% of total appropriations (70% agency / 30% QRF) BBM
GAD allocation At least 5% of each agency’s budget BAA No. 84, Sec. 17
Education, health, social services Highest budgetary priority BAA No. 84, Sec. 17

When an agency’s numbers fall outside these thresholds, flag it. That is a ready-made scrutiny question.

4.4 CSW Standards

Every briefer — legislative or budget — must meet these seven standards before it reaches the MP:

  1. Accuracy — Every fact is verified. Every citation is checked. Every number traces to a source document.
  2. Completeness — All required sections are filled. No placeholders. No “TBD” items.
  3. Clarity — The MP can read and understand the briefer without asking for explanation. Plain language. Short sentences.
  4. Timeliness — Delivered before the proceeding. A briefer that arrives after the hearing is useless.
  5. Alignment — Consistent with the MP’s legislative agenda and policy positions.
  6. Review — Passed through supervisory review (Legislative Chief and Chief of Staff) before reaching the MP.
  7. Presentation — Professionally formatted, properly cited, and ready for immediate use.

The CSW test. Before you submit any briefer, ask yourself: “Is this a finished product that requires minimal to no further modification from the MP?” If the answer is no, it is not Completed Staff Work. Go back and finish it.

Chapter V — Quality Standards and Review

5.1 Overview

Quality is not optional. A briefer with wrong BAA numbers damages the MP’s credibility in committee. A bill with a misspelled official name embarrasses the office on the floor. A budget analysis with outdated figures undermines the MP’s scrutiny questions.

This chapter establishes the quality pipeline that every legislative output must pass through — bills, resolutions, amendments, briefers, and budget analyses. No document leaves the office without completing this pipeline.

5.2 Quality Pipeline

Every document passes through four steps, in sequence. No step may be skipped.

Step 1 — Self-Check

The drafter reviews their own work using the Quality Review Checklist (Guidebook Appendix E). The checklist covers:

  • Legal authority — BOL compliance, no conflict with Constitution or national law
  • Structure — all required sections present, logical organization
  • Language — clear, precise, no archaic legalisms, gender-inclusive
  • Completeness — no missing sections, no placeholders
  • Formatting — consistent numbering, correct citation format

Do not submit a draft without completing the self-check. The checklist exists precisely for this step.

Step 2 — Peer Review

A colleague reviews the document. Fresh eyes catch what familiarity hides. The drafter is too close to the text to see every error.

The peer reviewer checks for:

  • Factual errors the drafter missed
  • Unclear or ambiguous language
  • Missing cross-references
  • Inconsistencies between sections
  • Typographical and formatting errors

The peer reviewer does not rewrite the document. They flag problems. The drafter fixes them.

Step 3 — Supervisory Review

The Chief of Staff or Legislative Chief reviews the document for:

  • Policy alignment — does this reflect the MP’s position and legislative agenda?
  • Legal accuracy — are BOL citations correct? Are BAA references accurate?
  • Factual accuracy — are names, titles, dates, and numbers correct?
  • Section completeness — are all required sections filled with substantive content?
  • CSW compliance — is this truly a finished product ready for the MP’s decision?

The supervisory reviewer has authority to return the document to the drafter for revision. A document is not “cleared” until the supervisory reviewer signs off.

Step 4 — MP Review and Approval

The MP reviews the final product and decides:

  • Approve — the document is accepted as-is
  • Modify — the MP directs specific changes; staff implements and resubmits
  • Return — the document needs substantial revision; it goes back to Step 1

Record the MP’s decision in the document’s approval block. Date-stamp every decision.

5.3 Fact-Checking Standards

These categories must be verified before any document reaches the MP:

Category Check Source
People Full names, current titles Officials reference
Legislation BAA/RA numbers, sections Bills and Index Division; BOL
Dates Events, deadlines, enactment Official Gazette; journal
Numbers Stats, budgets, percentages BEP; COA; PSA BARMM
Organizations Names, acronyms BAA 13
Places Provinces, municipalities Note: Mag. del Norte vs. del Sur

Rule of thumb: If a fact appears in the document, it must trace to a source. If you cannot identify the source, do not include the fact.

5.4 Version Control

Every document is versioned from the moment it is created.

Versioning convention:

Version Meaning
v1.0 Initial draft
v1.1, v1.2 Minor revisions (corrections, formatting, clarifications)
v2.0 Major revision (new sections, substantive policy changes, structural reorganization)

Requirements:

  • Date-stamp every version in the document header
  • Maintain a revision log for each document:
Date Version Changes Made Author
  • Keep all versions. Never delete a previous draft. Previous versions are part of the institutional record.
  • Label clearly. The filename or header must indicate the current version. Never leave staff or the MP guessing which version is current.

5.5 Filing and Records Management

Master File System

Maintain a master file for each legislative measure the office handles — whether your office authored it or is analyzing another office’s bill.

Each master file contains:

  • All drafts (every version, from initial to final)
  • Research memoranda and data compilations
  • Consultation reports and stakeholder inputs
  • Legislative briefers (all versions, including post-proceeding annotations)
  • Correspondence (co-authorship agreements, committee communications)
  • Supporting documents (COA reports, agency data, comparative legislation)

Annex B — Bill Drafting Workflow Flowchart

The bill drafting workflow follows seven stages in sequence. Two stages include decision points that may loop back to an earlier stage.

Stage 1 — Legislative Directive. The MP issues a written or verbal directive. Staff records it using Annex A.

Stage 2 — Research and Pre-Drafting. Staff conducts legal research, gathers data, consults stakeholders, and completes the Pre-Drafting Checklist (Guidebook Appendix D).

Stage 3 — First Draft. The Legislative Chief or assigned staff produces the initial draft, following the Guidebook’s structural and style requirements.

Stage 4 — Internal Review Cycle. The draft passes through self-check, peer review, and supervisory review (Chapter V quality pipeline).

Stage 5 — MP Review. [DECISION POINT] The MP reviews the draft. - If approved: proceed to Stage 6. - If returned with modifications: return to Stage 3 or Stage 4, depending on the scope of changes.

Stage 6 — Final Preparation and Filing. Staff prepares the final package — bill text, explanatory note, sponsorship speech — and transmits to the Bills and Index Division.

Stage 7 — Post-Filing Tracking. Staff tracks the bill through committee referral, hearings, plenary deliberation, and voting. Prepare briefers at each stage as needed.

Decision loop at Stage 5: If the MP returns the draft, the workflow loops back. Minor modifications loop to Stage 4 (review). Major changes loop to Stage 3 (redraft). The workflow continues forward only when the MP approves.
