# Bangsamoro Bill-Drafting Manual

## Chapter 4: The Legal Framework of the Bangsamoro Parliament

### 4.1 Introduction

Every bill drafted for the Bangsamoro Parliament must be situated within a defined legal hierarchy. The drafter's first and most consequential obligation is to ensure that the proposed legislation does not conflict with higher-ranking legal authorities. This chapter sets out the hierarchy of laws applicable to the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM), explains the division of powers between the National Government and the Bangsamoro Government, and identifies the substantive compliance checks that every bill must satisfy before it may properly be filed.

### 4.2 The Hierarchy of Laws

The legal system governing the BARMM is organized in a clear hierarchy. A provision in a lower-ranking authority that conflicts with a higher-ranking one is void to the extent of the inconsistency. The hierarchy, from highest to lowest, is as follows:

**1. The 1987 Philippine Constitution.** The Constitution is the supreme law of the land. It prevails over all other legislation, whether national or regional. Article X of the Constitution provides the foundation for the creation of autonomous regions in Muslim Mindanao and the Cordilleras, and Section 20 of Article X enumerates the minimum powers that the organic act must grant to the autonomous government. No Bangsamoro Autonomy Act may contravene any provision of the Constitution, and any bill drafter must treat the Constitution as the first and final standard of validity.

**2. Republic Act No. 11054 — The Bangsamoro Organic Law (BOL).** The BOL is the foundational charter of the BARMM. It was enacted by the Congress of the Philippines pursuant to Article X of the Constitution and ratified by the people of the Bangsamoro in a plebiscite held in January and February 2019. The BOL defines the territory of the BARMM, establishes its parliamentary form of government, enumerates the powers and competencies of the Bangsamoro Government, creates the intergovernmental relations mechanisms, and prescribes the rights and freedoms of the Bangsamoro people. All Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts must be consistent with the BOL; a bill that exceeds the powers granted by the BOL or violates any of its provisions is ultra vires and unenforceable.

**3. National Legislation (Republic Acts Applicable to the BARMM).** Republic Acts enacted by the Congress of the Philippines that are of general application continue to apply within the BARMM, unless the BOL expressly provides otherwise or the subject matter falls within the exclusive competencies of the Bangsamoro Government. Examples include the Local Government Code (Republic Act No. 7160), the Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083), the Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act (Republic Act No. 8371), and the Code of Conduct and Ethical Standards for Public Officials and Employees (Republic Act No. 6713). The bill drafter must identify all national legislation that is relevant to the subject matter of a proposed bill and ensure that the bill does not create an irreconcilable conflict with those national laws, unless the subject falls within the BARMM's exclusive legislative competence.

**4. Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts (BAAs).** These are laws enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament in the exercise of the legislative powers conferred by the BOL. BAAs are subordinate to the Constitution, the BOL, and applicable national legislation. Within their sphere of competence, however, BAAs have the force and effect of law throughout the BARMM. A later BAA may amend or repeal an earlier one, provided the amendatory bill complies with the procedural requirements of the Parliamentary Rules (Resolution No. 268).

**5. Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR).** Many BAAs delegate to the executive branch the authority to promulgate rules that give operational detail to the statute's general provisions. The IRR must be consistent with its parent BAA and cannot expand, diminish, or modify the rights and obligations established by the statute. The bill drafter should include in each bill a clear provision authorizing the promulgation of implementing rules, specifying which ministry or body shall issue them, and setting a deadline for their issuance.

**6. Executive Orders, Administrative Issuances, and Other Subordinate Instruments.** The Chief Minister may issue executive orders to implement BAAs and to organize the executive branch. Ministries may issue administrative orders, memorandum circulars, and other issuances within their areas of competence. These instruments occupy the lowest tier of the hierarchy and must be consistent with all higher-ranking authorities.

### 4.3 Constitutional Limits on BARMM Legislation: The Residual Clause

The BOL does not grant the Bangsamoro Government plenary legislative power. Article V, Section 1 establishes a residual clause: "All powers, functions, and responsibilities not granted by the Constitution or by national law to the Bangsamoro Government shall be vested in the National Government." Any matter not enumerated in Article V, Section 2 or covered by the general welfare clause in Section 3 falls outside the Parliament's legislative competence.

The following areas fall under the National Government by operation of this residual clause and are beyond the reach of Bangsamoro legislation:

- **National defense and security.** Article XI of the BOL expressly provides that the defense and security of the BARMM shall be the responsibility of the National Government. The Armed Forces of the Philippines retains command over all military operations within the region.
- **Foreign affairs.** The conduct of relations with foreign states and international organizations remains a national power. The Bangsamoro Government may, however, engage in cultural exchange and economic and technical cooperation as provided in the BOL.
- **Currency and monetary policy.** The issuance and regulation of currency, and the formulation of monetary policy, are functions of the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas under national law.
- **Customs duties.** The collection of customs duties, registration fees of vessels and wharfage, tonnage dues, and all other customs fees, charges, and dues remain with the National Government, with limited exceptions for vessels registered with the Bangsamoro Government and wharves it constructs and maintains.
- **Citizenship and immigration.** The determination of who are citizens of the Philippines and the regulation of immigration are national powers under the Constitution.
- **Postal service.** The national postal system is operated by the National Government.
- **Income taxation.** The power to levy income taxes remains with the National Government, except when levied on banks and other financial institutions within the BARMM.
- **Creation of legislative districts.** Article VI, Section 10 of the BOL explicitly states that nothing in the Organic Law shall be construed to allow the Bangsamoro Government to create legislative districts.

A bill that purports to legislate on any of these matters is unconstitutional and ultra vires. The bill drafter must scrutinize every provision of a proposed bill to ensure it does not encroach upon domains that the BOL leaves to the National Government.

### 4.4 Areas of Overlapping Authority

In several areas, the Bangsamoro Government's enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2 overlap with matters where national legislation also operates. These areas of shared jurisdiction require coordination and, in some cases, the establishment of joint bodies. Key areas of overlapping authority include:

- **Disaster risk reduction and management.** Both the National Government and the Bangsamoro Government cooperate and coordinate in this area, as provided in Article XIII of the BOL.
- **Environmental protection and natural resources.** While the Bangsamoro Government has authority over environment, parks, forest management, wildlife, and nature reserves conservation, the National Government retains regulatory authority over matters of national scope.
- **Taxation.** The Bangsamoro Government has broad taxing powers under Article XII of the BOL, including the power to levy taxes, fees, and charges, but the Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board resolves disputes over the determination of taxable elements in relation to taxes where taxable elements are situated both within and outside the Bangsamoro territory.
- **Education.** The Bangsamoro Government exercises authority over education and skills training, but national standards and the role of the Commission on Higher Education and the Department of Education continue to apply.
- **Health.** National programs such as PhilHealth and retained hospitals of the Department of Health continue to be funded by the National Government, without prejudice to the power of the Bangsamoro Government to provide supplemental funding.
- **Public order and safety.** The Bangsamoro Government may legislate on policing, fire and jail management, but matters of national security remain with the National Government.

When drafting a bill in an area of overlapping authority, the drafter must identify the existing national legislation, determine the extent of the Bangsamoro Government's competence under Article V, Section 2, and frame the bill so that it complements rather than contradicts the national framework. The intergovernmental relations mechanisms established by Article VI of the BOL — including the Intergovernmental Relations Body, the Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board, and the Philippine Congress-Bangsamoro Parliament Forum — provide the institutional channels for resolving conflicts in areas of shared jurisdiction.

Beyond the BOL's text, the Supreme Court has established additional constitutional constraints on Bangsamoro legislation through its rulings. Two decisions are particularly significant for the bill drafter:

**National election laws prevail over regional legislation.** In *Ali et al. v. BTA* (G.R. No. E-02219, September 30, 2025), the Supreme Court nullified BAA No. 77 in part because it was enacted during the election period in violation of the Voter's Registration Act, a national law. The drafter must ensure that any bill touching on electoral matters — redistricting, voter registration, party accreditation, or election administration — does not conflict with national election laws or COMELEC regulations.

**Redistricting must meet BOL geographic standards.** The same decision struck down BAA No. 77 for creating parliamentary districts that were not contiguous, compact, and adjacent as required by Article VII, Section 10 of the BOL. Any future redistricting legislation must strictly observe these geographic requirements, and the drafter should verify that proposed district boundaries satisfy all three criteria.

**Territorial scope is bound by democratic consent.** In *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea* (G.R. Nos. 242255 et al., September 9, 2024), the Supreme Court ruled that only areas whose voters approved the BOL in the plebiscite are part of the BARMM, applying Article X, Section 18 of the Constitution. Any bill that proposes programs, infrastructure, or administrative measures for the BARMM must confine its territorial scope to the region's constituent units as determined by the plebiscite results and subsequent judicial rulings.

#### The General Welfare Clause

Article V, Section 3 extends the Parliament's legislative authority beyond the enumerated list. The provision reads in full:

> "The Bangsamoro Government shall exercise the powers expressly granted, those necessarily implied therefrom, as well as powers necessary, appropriate, or incidental for its efficient and effective governance and those which are essential to the promotion of general welfare. Within its territorial jurisdiction, the Bangsamoro Government shall ensure and support, among other things, the preservation and enrichment of culture, promote health and safety, enhance the right of the people to a balanced ecology, encourage and support the development of appropriate and self-reliant scientific and technological capabilities, improve public morals, enhance economic prosperity and social justice, promote full employment among its residents, maintain peace and order, and preserve the comfort and convenience of its inhabitants."

The general welfare clause serves two functions for the drafter. First, it confirms that the Parliament may legislate not only on the fifty-five enumerated matters but also on matters "necessarily implied therefrom" and matters "necessary, appropriate, or incidental" for governance. A bill whose subject matter is not expressly listed in Section 2 may still fall within the Parliament's authority if it can be shown to be incidental to an enumerated power or essential to general welfare. Second, the clause articulates the broad objectives — culture, health, ecology, economic prosperity, social justice, peace and order — that the Parliament is mandated to pursue, and that may be cited in a bill's policy declaration as the legislative purpose.

In all these areas, the Bangsamoro Parliament has legislative authority, subject to the Constitution and the BOL. The drafter has broad latitude, but must still ensure consistency with the fundamental rights guaranteed by both the Constitution and Article IX of the BOL, and must assess whether national legislation also operates in the same subject area (see Section 4.4 above).

### 4.6 The "One Subject, One Title" Rule

Section 25(a) of Article VII of the BOL mandates that "every bill passed by the Parliament shall embrace only one subject which shall be expressed in the title thereof." This requirement is reinforced by Section 13 of Rule XVII of Resolution No. 268, which provides that "a bill shall only have one (1) subject matter, and an amendment which seeks the inclusion of a provision not germane to the purpose of a bill (rider) shall not be allowed."

The purposes of this rule are threefold:

1. **Prevention of log-rolling.** The rule prevents the practice of combining unrelated provisions in a single bill to secure passage of measures that would not survive independent consideration.
2. **Fair notice.** The title of a bill must give legislators and the public fair notice of the bill's contents. A title that fails to indicate the actual scope of the bill's provisions is defective.
3. **Orderly legislation.** The rule promotes coherence and logical organization in the statute book.

The bill drafter must ensure that every provision of the bill is germane to the single subject expressed in the title. The title need not be a detailed catalogue of every provision, but it must be comprehensive enough to encompass all matters treated in the bill. When in doubt, the drafter should draft the title broadly enough to cover all related provisions, while remaining specific enough to give meaningful notice. If a proposed bill addresses two or more distinct subjects, the drafter must separate them into distinct bills.

### 4.7 Gender Sensitivity and Human Rights Compliance

Survey data from Members of Parliament reveal that gender sensitivity and human rights compliance are treated as essential elements of the legislative review process. On a scale of 1 to 5, MPs rated their attention to gender sensitivity at 4.14 and their attention to human rights conformity at 4.12. These high ratings reflect an institutional commitment to mainstreaming gender and rights considerations in all legislation.

Every bill drafter should apply the following checks:

**Gender sensitivity review.** The drafter should examine whether the proposed bill:
- Uses gender-neutral or gender-inclusive language throughout
- Accounts for the differential impact of the proposed law on women, men, and gender-diverse persons
- Includes provisions that promote the participation and representation of women, consistent with the BOL's requirement that the Bangsamoro Government ensure the inclusion of women's agenda and the involvement of women in political processes (Article VII, Section 9)
- Avoids reinforcing harmful gender stereotypes or creating discriminatory outcomes

**Human rights compliance review.** The drafter should examine whether the proposed bill:
- Is consistent with the bill of rights guaranteed by the Constitution (Article III)
- Respects the basic rights and freedoms enumerated in Article VIII of the BOL
- Complies with international human rights treaties and agreements binding upon the Philippines, as required by Section 8, Article IV of the BOL
- Includes adequate safeguards, due process protections, and remedies for persons whose rights may be affected by the law
- Protects the rights of vulnerable groups, including indigenous peoples, women, children, persons with disabilities, and the elderly

### 4.8 Shari'ah Considerations in Bangsamoro Legislation

The BOL recognizes *Shari'ah* as part of the plural justice system of the Bangsamoro. Article IX of the BOL provides that the dispensation of justice in the BARMM shall be in consonance with the Constitution, *Shari'ah*, traditional or tribal laws, and other relevant laws. *Shari'ah* is defined as "Islamic law derived from religious precepts of Islam, particularly the *Qur'an* and *Sunnah*" and applies exclusively to cases involving Muslims, unless a non-Muslim voluntarily submits to the jurisdiction of the *Shari'ah* court.

The Parliament has the power, under Section 4, Article IX of the BOL, to enact laws pertaining to *Shari'ah* on personal, family, and property law jurisdiction. It may also enact laws governing commercial and other civil actions not provided for under the Code of Muslim Personal Laws (Presidential Decree No. 1083), and criminal jurisdiction on minor offenses punishable by *arresto menor* or *ta'zir* equivalent to *arresto menor*.

The sources of *Shari'ah* recognized by the BOL are:

**Principal Sources:**
- *Al-Qur'an* (the Holy Book)
- *Al-Sunnah* (the Prophetic Tradition)

**Subsidiary Sources:**
- *Al-Ijma* (Consensus of Scholars)
- *Al-Qiyas* (Analogy)

When drafting a bill that touches upon matters within the *Shari'ah* jurisdiction, the bill drafter must:

1. Consult with *Ulama* and scholars of Islamic jurisprudence to ensure that the proposed provisions are consistent with recognized principles of *Shari'ah*
2. Ensure that the bill does not impose *Shari'ah* obligations on non-Muslims, consistent with the BOL's provision that *Shari'ah* applies exclusively to Muslims
3. Clearly delineate the jurisdictional boundaries between *Shari'ah* courts and regular courts
4. Preserve the rights of non-Muslims and non-indigenous peoples, as required by Article IX of the BOL
5. Ensure consistency with the Code of Muslim Personal Laws and any existing BAAs on *Shari'ah*-related matters

## Chapter 5: The Lawmaking Process and Flowchart of the Bangsamoro Parliament

### 5.1 Types of Legislative Measures

The Parliamentary Rules (Resolution No. 268) recognize three principal types of legislative measures:

#### 5.1.1 Bills

A bill is a proposed measure, or an amendment or repeal of an existing law, presented to the Parliament for its consideration (Rule XVII, Section 1). When enacted, a bill becomes a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act (BAA) — a binding law applicable throughout the BARMM. Bills are the primary instruments of legislation and must pass through all the prescribed stages before they may take effect.

Resolution No. 268 classifies bills into three categories:

**Government Bills** (also called Cabinet Bills). A Government bill is a legislative initiative that the Government of the Day submits to Parliament for approval or amendment (Rule XVII, Section 2(a)). Government bills originate from the executive branch: a ministry identifies the need for legislation, drafts the bill, and submits it to the Chief Minister and the Cabinet. If the Cabinet approves the measure, it is introduced before the Parliament as a Government bill. Government bills have precedence over Private Member bills in the Order of Business, reflecting the parliamentary system's emphasis on the government's legislative program.

**Private Member Bills.** A Private Member bill is a legislative initiative introduced by a Member of the Parliament who is not a Minister (Rule XVII, Section 2(b)). Any MP who does not hold a Cabinet position may file a Private Member bill. While Private Member bills may address any subject within the Parliament's competence, they yield precedence to Government bills in the legislative calendar, without prejudice to consolidation with related Government bills.

**Popular Initiative.** A Popular Initiative is a bill proposed directly by the people as part of participatory democracy in the BARMM (Rule XVII, Section 2(c), referencing BAA No. 13). The Parliament regulates the process for popular initiative, and bills submitted through this mechanism are subject to the same stages of legislation as Government and Private Member bills.

#### 5.1.2 Resolutions

A resolution is a document expressing the sentiment or opinion of the Parliament. Unlike a bill, a resolution does not create or modify a law (Rule XVII, Section 4). The Parliamentary Rules recognize three types:

- **Simple Resolution.** A measure used by the Parliament to take action affecting its own procedures, to address urgent matters requiring immediate action, or to express an opinion, sympathy, or commendation.
- **Concurrent Resolution.** A resolution issued concurrently by both the Bangsamoro Cabinet and the Parliament expressing their sentiments on certain matters.
- **Joint Committee Resolution.** A resolution of two or more committees expressing the will or action of Members on certain matters, requiring the majority vote of all committees concerned.

Simple and concurrent resolutions are not subject to the ordinary stages of legislation. The Parliament or the committees concerned act on them promptly in plenary session.

### 5.2 The Pre-Legislative Stage

Before a bill is drafted, a substantial amount of preparatory work should take place. Survey data from Members of Parliament indicate strong support for structured pre-legislative processes: MPs rated the importance of policy development before bill drafting at 4.52 out of 5, the highest-rated pre-legislative activity.

#### 5.2.1 Policy Development

The policy development stage involves identifying the problem that the proposed legislation seeks to address, analyzing its causes, evaluating alternative solutions (including non-legislative options), and selecting the most appropriate policy response. This stage should produce a clear policy statement that sets out the objectives of the proposed legislation, the target beneficiaries, the expected outcomes, and the estimated costs and resources required for implementation.

For Government bills, this stage typically takes place within the originating ministry, often in coordination with the Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority. For Private Member bills, the MP may draw upon research staff, committee resources, or external expertise.

#### 5.2.2 Stakeholder Consultation

MPs rated the importance of consulting affected stakeholders at 4.30 out of 5. Pre-legislative consultation serves to:

- Identify the interests and concerns of persons and communities who will be affected by the proposed law
- Gather data and evidence that inform the bill's provisions
- Build public understanding and support for the proposed measure
- Identify potential implementation challenges before the bill is drafted

Consultation may take the form of public hearings, focus group discussions, written submissions, or field visits. The results of stakeholder consultation should be documented and reflected in the bill's explanatory statement.

#### 5.2.3 Research and Data Gathering

Evidence-based legislation requires the drafter to ground the bill's provisions in reliable data. This includes statistical data on the scope of the problem, comparative analysis of how other jurisdictions have addressed similar issues, legal analysis of the constitutional and statutory framework, and fiscal impact assessment. The Parliament's committee secretariats, the Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority, and external research institutions can provide support at this stage.

#### 5.2.4 Drafting Instructions

Survey data reveal that most drafting instructions in the Bangsamoro Parliament are communicated orally (rated 3.49 out of 5) rather than in writing (rated 3.25 out of 5). This practice, while common, carries risks: oral instructions may be incomplete, ambiguous, or misunderstood. Best practice calls for written drafting instructions that specify:

- The policy objectives of the bill
- The principal provisions to be included
- The existing laws to be amended, repealed, or referenced
- The ministry or body responsible for implementation
- The fiscal implications and proposed funding source
- Any specific constitutional or BOL provisions that must be observed

Even where the initial instruction is given orally, the drafter should prepare a written summary of the instruction and seek confirmation from the instructing authority before proceeding with the draft.

### 5.3 Filing and First Reading

Once the bill has been drafted, the legislative process proper begins with filing and the First Reading.

**Filing.** The bill, signed by its author or authors and accompanied by an electronic copy, is filed with the Secretary-General of the Parliament (Rule XVII, Section 9). The Secretary-General assigns a Proposed Bill Number in the order the bill is received. The Secretary-General will not accept a bill unless it is accompanied by an electronic copy; in case of discrepancy between the printed and electronic copies, the printed copy prevails. The bill is then forwarded to the Office of the Speaker.

**First Reading.** The Secretary-General reports the bill to the Parliament for its First Reading (Rule XVIII, Section 2(a)). The First Reading involves only the reading of the number, title, and author of the bill, followed by a declaration by the Speaker as to whether the bill is a Government bill or a Private Member bill. If it is a Private Member bill, the author may propose the inclusion of additional authors by requesting the Majority Floor Leader.

**Second Reading and Referral.** Immediately following the First Reading, the bill is read in full on Second Reading (unless copies have been distributed to Members), and the proponent delivers the authorship speech — the explanatory statement of the bill. The Majority Floor Leader then refers the bill to the appropriate committee. If the committee to which the bill is referred believes it should be reassigned, the Chairperson may make a written request to the Committee on Rules for a change of referral, and the Committee on Rules must decide within five days.

*Note on terminology:* The Bangsamoro Parliamentary Rules structure the readings differently from the bicameral Congress. In the Bangsamoro Parliament, the authorship speech and referral to committee occur at the Second Reading stage, and what follows after committee deliberation is the Consideration and Deliberation Stage (which functions as the plenary debate stage) and the Third Reading or Final Stage.

### 5.4 Committee Stage

The committee stage is the most intensive phase of the legislative process. It is here that the bill receives detailed, line-by-line scrutiny.

**Referral and Jurisdiction.** The Speaker refers each bill to the committee with jurisdiction over its subject matter. Resolution No. 268 establishes seventeen Statutory committees and seven Parliamentary committees, each with defined jurisdictional mandates. If the bill's subject matter straddles two or more committees, the Committee on Rules may establish an ad hoc joint committee.

**Committee Hearings and Public Consultations.** The committee examines, scrutinizes, and deliberates the bill in detail (Rule XVIII, Section 2(c)). It may consult, invite, and solicit opinions from experts, relevant ministries and offices, the public, and other interested parties. Committee meetings are held in the mornings of Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday during the regular session, though they may be scheduled at other times with appropriate authorization. Members of the Cabinet may attend committee hearings and respond to questions on matters within their cognizance.

**Committee Deliberation.** The committee considers the bill provision by provision. It may propose amendments, revisions, or a complete substitute bill. The committee may also consolidate related bills into a single measure. If the committee determines that the bill should not proceed, it may report it unfavorably.

**Committee Report.** Upon completing its deliberation, the committee prepares a committee report (Rule XII, Section 16). The report contains the discussions and views expressed by individual Members and all invited resource persons and experts, the amendments introduced and their justifications, and the committee's recommendation. Members who participated in the final voting must sign the report, indicating whether they vote in favor, with reservation, or in dissent. The committee report is printed, distributed, and forwarded to the Office of the Secretary-General. It is also made available to the public.

The committee report is the single most important document in determining the bill's fate. A favorable committee report brings the bill back to the plenary for debate. An unfavorable report effectively kills the bill unless the plenary overrides the committee's recommendation.

### 5.5 Consideration and Deliberation Stage (Second Reading in Plenary)

Once the committee reports the bill favorably, it returns to the plenary for the Consideration and Deliberation Stage. This stage comprises three periods:

**Period of Sponsorship.** The committee chairperson or designated sponsor presents the committee report to the plenary, explaining the bill's objectives, the committee's findings, and the amendments it has adopted. The sponsor delivers a speech setting out the rationale for the bill as reported by the committee.

**Period of Interpellation.** Members of Parliament who are not part of the sponsoring committee may question the sponsor on every provision of the bill. The Majority Floor Leader maintains a list of interpellators, and Members must register in writing with the Secretary-General to be recognized. Interpellators and proponents of opposing views are recognized alternately, with the Majority and Minority taking turns as far as practicable. A Member may not speak more than once on the same question during the same day without leave of the Parliament. Individual speaking time is limited to one hour, although the sponsor may consume as much time as necessary.

Importantly, any committee Member who failed to enter an objection or record a dissenting vote at the committee level may not speak against the bill during the plenary debate, though the Member may propose and speak on amendments.

**Period of Amendments.** After interpellation, the bill enters the amendment period. Two types of amendments are recognized:

- *Committee Amendments* — alterations or changes offered by the reporting committee
- *Floor or Individual Amendments* — alterations offered by individual Members

Amendments must be presented concretely and specifically, indicating the page and line in which the amendment is proposed. No amendment by substitution is entertained unless submitted in writing. A bill may not be amended by substituting it with another that covers a subject distinct from the original. Both committee amendments and floor amendments are debated and voted upon.

**Approval on Second Reading.** After the period of amendments, the bill is submitted to a vote. Approval requires a majority of those present, provided a quorum exists.

### 5.6 Third Reading (Final Stage)

The Third Reading is the decisive vote on the bill in its final form.

**Distribution of Final Text.** Printed copies of the bill in its final form must be distributed to all Members of Parliament at least three days before the vote (Rule XVIII, Section 12). This requirement ensures that every Member has an opportunity to read the final text before casting a vote. The three-day requirement is waived only when the Chief Minister certifies the necessity of immediate enactment to meet a public calamity or emergency.

**Final Debate.** During the Third Reading, there shall be a final debate on whether or not to pass the bill. No amendments may be considered at this stage. The sponsor of the bill has the right to close the debate.

**Recorded Vote.** The Speaker calls for a roll call vote. Members vote by show of hands or by standing; any Member who wishes to abstain manifests the abstention verbally without the need for explanation. Members may make a verbal statement on the final vote lasting not more than five minutes, or submit a short written statement for inclusion in the minutes.

**Majority Required for Passage.** In accordance with the BOL, bills must be approved by a majority of all Members of the Parliament — not merely a majority of those present. This higher threshold ensures that enacted laws have the support of a genuine majority of the parliamentary body.

### 5.7 Post-Passage

Once a bill passes the Third Reading, a series of steps must be completed before it becomes an enforceable law.

**Enrolled Bill.** The bill is prepared in its final enrolled form and authenticated by the Secretary-General, who certifies and affixes the seal of the Parliament.

**Speaker's Signature.** The Speaker signs the enrolled bill as required by Rule IV, Section 4(k), which provides that the Speaker shall sign all acts, resolutions, and bills that may be issued by or upon order of the Parliament.

**Presentation to the Chief Minister.** The Speaker presents the enrolled bill to the Chief Minister (Section 25(c), Article VII of the BOL, and Rule IV, Section 4(n)). The Chief Minister has three options:

1. **Sign the bill into law.** The Chief Minister signifies approval by signing the bill within thirty days after the date of receipt.
2. **Veto the bill.** The Chief Minister may decline to sign. Under the BOL, if the bill is not signed within thirty days, it becomes law as if the Chief Minister had signed it; there is no pocket veto.
3. **Inaction.** If the Chief Minister takes no action within thirty days after receipt, the bill becomes law by operation of the BOL, without the Chief Minister's signature.

**Publication.** The enacted law must be published in the Bangsamoro Gazette or in at least one newspaper of general circulation within the BARMM.

**Effectivity.** The law takes effect after fifteen days following its complete publication, unless the law itself provides for a different effectivity date (Rule XVIII, Section 2(g)).

**Certified Copy to the President and Congress.** The Speaker shall, within ten working days from approval, submit to the President and to the Congress of the Philippines a certified true copy of all laws and resolutions approved by the Parliament (Section 25(d), Article VII of the BOL).

### 5.9 The Order of Business: Where Bills Fit in a Session Day

Resolution No. 268, Rule VIII, Section 1 prescribes the daily Order of Business for each session of the Parliament. Understanding this order is essential for the bill drafter and sponsor because it determines when and how a bill will be taken up on the floor. The Order of Business is as follows:

| Order | Item | Description |
|-------|------|-------------|
| (a) | Call to Order | Speaker opens the session |
| (b) | Invocation or Prayer | |
| (c) | National Anthem | Philippine National Anthem |
| (d) | Bangsamoro Hymn | |
| (e) | Roll Call | Secretary-General calls attendance; quorum determined |
| (f) | Reading and Approval of Journal | Journal of the previous session |
| (g) | Reference of Business | **(1) Bills on First Reading** — new bills are read by title and number; **(2) Resolutions, Messages, Memorials and Communications** |
| (h) | Bills on Second Reading | **(1) Committee Reports on Bills** — sponsors present committee reports; **(2) Period of Interpellations**; **(3) Period of Amendments**; **(4) Approval of Second Reading** |
| (i) | Resolutions Referred to Committee | Committee reports on resolutions, interpellation, amendments, and approval |
| (j) | Unfinished Business | Matters carried over from a previous session |
| (k) | Privilege Hour | Up to 60 minutes; held every Thursday of regular sessions |
| (l) | Question Hour | 60 minutes every first Wednesday of the month (when no Chief Minister's Hour) |
| (m) | Bills on Third Reading | Final vote on bills in their approved final form |

**Special sessions:** On the first Wednesday of every quarter, the Order of Business includes the Chief Minister's Hour immediately after the Roll Call, during which the Chief Minister or a designated Cabinet member reports on government accomplishments.

**Plenary sessions** are held at 1:00 p.m. on Mondays through Thursdays of the third and fourth week of each month, unless the Parliament decides otherwise. **Committee meetings and hearings** are held in the mornings of Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday during the regular session.

The bill drafter should note the implications of this schedule:

- A new bill enters the Order of Business at item (g)(1) — Reference of Business, Bills on First Reading.
- After referral and committee deliberation, the bill returns to plenary at item (h) — Bills on Second Reading, where the committee report is presented and debated.
- The final vote takes place at item (m) — Bills on Third Reading, which comes last in the Order of Business, underscoring the finality and gravity of the vote.

### 5.10 Special and Expedited Procedures

Two exceptional procedures allow the Parliament to accelerate the legislative process:

**Certified Bills (Public Calamity or Emergency).** When the Chief Minister certifies to the necessity of a bill's immediate enactment to respond to a public calamity or emergency, the Chief Minister forwards the certified measure to the Speaker. Within twenty-four hours of receipt, the Parliament must act for the bill's immediate passage. The three-day distribution requirement for the final printed text is waived, and the Third Reading vote may take place on the same day the bill is approved on Second Reading.

**Swift Passage Procedure.** In exceptional cases, the Parliament may adopt the Swift Passage Procedure, which allows a bill to be disposed of in seven working days by skipping the detailed committee scrutiny stage. This procedure permits cross-party or free conscience voting.

Both procedures are designed for extraordinary circumstances. The bill drafter should not design a bill's legislative strategy around the assumption that an expedited procedure will be available. The standard stages of legislation remain the norm, and the use of expedited procedures should be reserved for genuine emergencies.
