# PART ONE: CONTEXT AND FRAMEWORK

# Chapter 1

# Introduction

## 1.1 Purpose and Scope

This manual exists to serve a single, practical end: to improve the quality of legislation produced by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament. Legislation is the primary instrument through which the Bangsamoro Government exercises its mandate under the Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054). Every Bangsamoro Autonomy Act shapes the lives of nearly five million people across the provinces of Basilan, Lanao del Sur, Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, and Tawi-Tawi, the cities of Cotabato, Marawi, and Lamitan, and the sixty-three barangays of the Special Geographic Area. The stakes of legislative drafting, therefore, are not merely technical. They are democratic.

The purpose of this manual is threefold. First, it seeks to provide uniformity of style and format so that all bills presented to the Parliament conform to a consistent standard that aids comprehension and reduces ambiguity. Second, it is intended to assist in the training and professional development of bill drafters — a function that interviews with Members of Parliament have identified as both urgent and under-resourced. Third, it aims to promote accuracy, clarity, and consistency in the text of legislation, so that the laws enacted by the Parliament can be faithfully interpreted and effectively implemented by the executive ministries, the judiciary, local government units, and the public at large.

This manual does not prescribe policy. It takes no position on the substance of any bill. Its concern is with form, structure, and the rules of legal expression. A well-drafted bill may advance a wise policy or an unwise one, but a poorly drafted bill will undermine any policy, however sound. The task of this manual is to equip drafters with the tools to translate policy decisions into legislation that is precise, readable, and enforceable.

## 1.2 Who This Manual Is For

This manual is addressed to every person involved in the preparation and review of legislative text within the Bangsamoro Parliament. Its primary audience includes the following:

**Members of Parliament.** Under the parliamentary system established by the BOL, Members of Parliament bear dual responsibility as legislators and, when appointed to the Cabinet, as executives. Whether filing original bills, proposing amendments on the floor, or reviewing committee reports, MPs require a working understanding of bill-drafting conventions. This manual is intended to support that understanding.

**Chiefs of Staff and Legislative Offices.** The offices of individual MPs and of the Speaker are often the first point of drafting. Chiefs of staff and their legal teams frequently prepare the initial text of bills before they are refined by the Committee on Rules and the Office of the Secretary General. This manual provides a common reference for that initial drafting stage.

**Legal Officers and Committee Staff.** The standing committees of the Parliament — particularly the Committee on Rules, the Committee on Ways and Means, and the various sectoral committees — employ legal officers who review bills for technical correctness, consistency with existing law, and conformity with the BOL. This manual codifies the standards against which such review should be conducted.

**Parliamentary Staff of the Office of the Secretary General.** The institutional staff who maintain the Parliament's records, prepare committee reports, and manage the legislative calendar play an essential role in ensuring that bills move through the legislative process in proper form.

**Consultants from International Non-Governmental Organizations.** The Bangsamoro Parliament has benefited from the support of international partners in the areas of legislative strengthening and institutional development. This manual provides external consultants with a locally authoritative reference for the conventions and requirements of Bangsamoro bill drafting, reducing dependence on generic drafting guides that may not account for the region's unique legal framework.

## 1.3 The Capacity-Building Challenge

The Bangsamoro Parliament is a young institution. It was inaugurated in 2019 following the ratification of the BOL and operated during its initial years under three successive compositions of appointed members (see Section 3.3). Throughout this formative period, the Parliament enacted over eighty Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts and adopted hundreds of resolutions, building a legislative record under conditions that would challenge even established legislatures.

Interviews conducted with Members of Parliament revealed a consistent finding: bill-drafting capacity-building efforts, while valued, have been short-term and not sustainable. The technical skill required to draft legislation competently is not acquired in a single workshop. By most accounts, it takes eighteen months to two years of sustained practice for a legislative staff member to develop genuine expertise in bill drafting. Workshops of one to three days, however well designed, cannot substitute for this extended learning process.

When asked what a bill-drafting manual should contain, MPs identified three priorities above all others. "Language and tone guidance" was the most frequently cited need, mentioned nine times across interviews — reflecting a recognition that legislative language operates under conventions distinct from ordinary legal writing and that inconsistency of tone across bills has been a recurring problem. "Step-by-step workflow" was the second most cited need, with eight mentions, indicating a demand for process clarity from policy conception through committee deliberation to floor passage. "References to existing laws" was the third priority, cited six times, underscoring the challenge of drafting within a layered legal framework where Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts must coexist with the BOL, national statutes, and, in matters of personal law, the Shari'ah.

A survey of Members of Parliament confirmed the strength of demand for this resource. When asked whether a bill-drafting manual would improve their legislative capacity, MPs registered a mean agreement score of 4.52 out of 5.00 — the highest-rated item in the entire survey instrument. This finding establishes not merely that a manual is desired, but that it is perceived as the single most impactful capacity-building intervention available to the Parliament.

This manual is a response to that finding. It is designed not as a substitute for mentorship and practice, but as a permanent reference that can anchor both formal training programs and the daily work of drafting offices.

## 1.4 The Nature of This Manual: Guide, Not Rules

A preliminary observation is warranted about the nature and authority of this manual. Legislative drafting manuals serve as guides — they recommend rather than prescribe. As one comparative study of Commonwealth drafting offices observed, a good manual is one that tends to recommend rather than prescribe. Drafting guidelines are helpful; drafting rules are unhelpful. The reason is straightforward: legislative drafting is both art and science, and no set of rigid rules can anticipate every drafting situation that will arise.

As art, legislative drafting involves understanding and clarifying policy, conceiving a legislative scheme, working collaboratively with policy makers, and managing the scarce resource of parliamentary time. These dimensions require judgment, creativity, and adaptability that cannot be reduced to mechanical rules. As science, legislative drafting demands legal knowledge — constitutional, statutory, and international — governmental and political knowledge of the legislative process, and technical knowledge of drafting conventions and formulations. This manual addresses the science; the art is developed through practice and mentorship.

This manual has no legal authority. It is not a source of law. It is an internal document designed to assist drafters in producing legislation that is accurate, clear, and consistent. No court will cite this manual as authority for the interpretation of a statute, and no Member of Parliament is bound by its recommendations. Its authority derives entirely from the quality of its guidance — guidance that the drafter is free to depart from when the circumstances of a particular bill so require.

The practical consequence of this principle is that the formulations and structures described in the chapters that follow should be understood as established conventions, not as mandatory rules. Where this manual says "the drafter should," it is making a recommendation. Where it says "the standard formulation is," it is describing the practice observed in enacted Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts. In either case, the drafter retains the professional judgment to adapt the guidance to the requirements of the bill at hand.

## 1.5 The Three Principles: Accuracy, Brevity, and Clarity

The drafting of legislation is governed by principles that transcend any particular jurisdiction. The Oregon Bill Drafting Manual, one of the most widely referenced drafting guides in the common law tradition, articulates these principles with directness: legislation must be accurate, brief, and clear. G.C. Thornton, in his foundational treatise on legislative drafting, and V.C.R.A.C. Crabbe, in his work on legislative procedure, echo the same imperative. Reed Dickerson's writings on the interpretation of statutes return to this theme repeatedly: ambiguity in legislation is not a failure of style alone; it is a failure of governance, because it transfers the power to determine the meaning of the law from the legislature that enacted it to the courts and agencies that must interpret it.

These three principles — collectively referred to in drafting literature as the ABC of legislative drafting — form the foundation of this manual.

**Accuracy** means that the text of a bill must faithfully express the policy intent of its author. Every provision must do what it is meant to do, neither more nor less. Accuracy requires the drafter to understand not only the policy being enacted but also the existing legal landscape into which the new law will be inserted. A bill that inadvertently repeals an existing provision, creates an unintended conflict with the BOL, or fails to account for a concurrent national statute is inaccurate, regardless of how elegantly it is written.

**Brevity** means that the text of a bill must accomplish its purpose in as few words as the subject allows. Brevity is not minimalism. A complex subject requires a detailed bill. But no bill should use ten words where five will do, and no provision should repeat what is already stated elsewhere in the same bill or in an existing law that can be incorporated by reference. Legislative verbosity does not add precision; it multiplies the opportunities for contradiction and confusion.

**Clarity** means that the text of a bill must be intelligible to every person who is required to read it — not only judges and lawyers, but also the administrators who must implement it, the local government officials who must comply with it, and the citizens whose rights and obligations it defines. Clarity requires plain language, logical organization, and the disciplined use of defined terms. A bill that can only be understood by its drafter has failed the test of clarity.

These three principles are interdependent. Accuracy without clarity produces legislation that means the right thing but communicates it poorly. Brevity without accuracy produces legislation that is concise but wrong. Clarity without brevity produces legislation that is readable but bloated. The drafter's task is to achieve all three simultaneously, and the chapters that follow provide the practical guidance to do so.

## 1.6 How to Use This Manual

This manual is organized in two parts.

**Part One** — comprising Chapters 1 through 3 — provides the contextual foundation that every drafter must understand before putting pen to paper. Chapter 1 (this chapter) sets out the purpose, audience, and guiding principles of the manual. Chapter 2 describes the democratic functions of the Bangsamoro Parliament, including the legislative process by which bills become Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts, so that the drafter understands the institutional context within which drafted bills will be considered. Chapter 3 traces the legal evolution of the Bangsamoro Government from the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao to the present Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, explaining the layered legal framework within which all Bangsamoro legislation must operate.

**Part Two** — comprising Chapters 4 through 11 — addresses the technical craft of bill drafting. Chapter 4 presents the anatomy of a bill, describing each structural component from the title to the effectivity clause. Chapters 5 through 8 address the substantive elements of a bill in sequence: the title and policy declaration; definitions and interpretation clauses; the operative provisions that constitute the body of the law; and the penal, transitory, and final provisions that close it. Chapter 9 treats the rules of language, style, and grammar that govern legislative text. Chapter 10 addresses the special problems of amending and repealing existing legislation. Chapter 11 describes the workflow from policy conception through committee review, floor deliberation, and enactment.

The manual concludes with a glossary of legislative terms used throughout the text.

Drafters are encouraged to read Part One in its entirety before beginning work on any bill. Part Two may be consulted as needed, chapter by chapter, depending on the drafting problem at hand. Throughout both parts, the manual uses examples drawn, where possible, from enacted Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts, so that the guidance offered is grounded in the Parliament's own legislative practice.

# Chapter 2

# Democratic Functions of the Parliament

## 2.1 The Parliament Under the Bangsamoro Organic Law

The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao is the only region in the Philippines that operates under a parliamentary form of government. Article IV, Section 3 of the Bangsamoro Organic Law mandates that "the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall have a parliamentary form of government," a departure from the presidential system that prevails in the rest of the country and from the unitary structure that governed the former Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao. This parliamentary system is not merely an organizational preference; it is a foundational element of the BOL's design, intended to produce closer integration between the legislative and executive functions of government and, consequently, more responsive governance.

Under Article VII, Section 2 of the BOL, the powers of government are vested in the Parliament, which exercises those powers and functions "expressly granted to it in this Organic Law, and those necessary for, or incidental to, the proper governance and development of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region." The Parliament is thus the supreme governing body of the region. It sets policy, enacts legislation, approves the budget, and elects the Chief Minister who exercises executive authority on its behalf. This concentration of authority in a single body distinguishes the Bangsamoro system from the separation-of-powers model that characterizes the national government.

The Parliament is composed of eighty members, as provided in Article VII, Section 6 of the BOL, unless otherwise increased by the Congress of the Philippines. Under the BOL's design for regular parliamentary elections, these seats are allocated among three categories: party representatives elected through proportional representation (one-half of the membership), district representatives elected through direct plurality vote (not more than forty percent), and reserved seats and sectoral representatives constituting at least ten percent, including seats for non-Moro indigenous peoples, settler communities, women, youth, traditional leaders, and the Ulama.

During the transition period prior to the first parliamentary elections, all eighty members were appointed by the President of the Philippines. This arrangement, provided for in Article XVI of the BOL, vested the Bangsamoro Transition Authority (BTA) with both legislative and executive powers — a dual mandate that ends when a regularly elected Parliament convenes.

## 2.2 The Legislative Function: How Bills Become Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts

The process by which a bill becomes a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act is prescribed by the BOL and elaborated in the Rules of the Parliament. Article VII, Section 25 of the BOL establishes the essential requirements.

**The Three-Readings Rule.** No bill shall become a law in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region unless it has passed three readings on separate days, and printed copies thereof in its final form have been distributed to members of the Parliament three days before its passage. The sole exception to this rule arises when the Chief Minister certifies to the necessity of a bill's immediate enactment to meet a public calamity or emergency.

The three readings proceed as follows. On first reading, the bill is filed, numbered, and referred to the appropriate committee or committees. The committee deliberates on the bill, may conduct public hearings, and produces a committee report with its recommendation — whether to approve, amend, or reject the bill. On second reading, the bill as reported by the committee is presented to the plenary for debate and amendment. Members may propose amendments from the floor, and the bill is voted upon section by section or in its entirety, as the Parliament may determine. On third reading, the bill in its final form is voted upon without further amendment. A majority of all the members of the Parliament constitutes a quorum to transact business, as provided in Article VII, Section 26 of the BOL.

**Presentment to the Chief Minister.** Every bill passed by the Parliament must, before it becomes law, be presented to the Chief Minister. The Chief Minister shall sign the bill within thirty days after the date of its receipt; otherwise, it shall become a law as if the Chief Minister had signed it. This provision, found in Article VII, Section 25(c) of the BOL, establishes a form of executive review that, while not identical to the presidential veto power in the national government, ensures that the Chief Minister has the opportunity to assess legislation before it takes effect.

**Transmittal.** The Speaker of the Parliament shall, within ten working days from approval, submit to the President and to the Congress of the Philippines a certified true copy of all laws and resolutions approved by the Parliament. This requirement, set out in Article VII, Section 25(d), ensures transparency and maintains the intergovernmental relationship between the Bangsamoro Government and the national government.

**Publication.** Laws enacted by the Parliament take effect upon compliance with the publication requirements established by the Parliament's rules and applicable law.

Upon completion of this process, a bill becomes a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act and is assigned a sequential BAA number. As of the current legislative period, the Parliament has enacted eighty-one BAAs, ranging from the foundational Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13) to sectoral legislation on education, health, agriculture, and cultural heritage.

## 2.3 Bills and Resolutions: A Distinction

The Parliament's legislative output is not confined to bills. The BOL and the Rules of the Parliament recognize a distinction between two categories of legislative measures: bills and resolutions.

**Bills** are legislative proposals that, upon passage through the three-readings process and approval by the Chief Minister, become Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts with the force and effect of law. A bill creates, amends, or repeals legal obligations, establishes institutions, appropriates funds, or otherwise alters the legal landscape within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The bill is the primary instrument of the Parliament's legislative power.

**Resolutions** are expressions of the Parliament's will that do not carry the force of law. A resolution may express the sense of the Parliament on a matter of public interest, request executive action, commend individuals or organizations, or authorize internal parliamentary business such as the creation of committees or the adoption of rules. Because resolutions do not create binding legal obligations, they follow a simplified procedural track and do not require presentment to the Chief Minister for signature.

Within the category of resolutions, the Parliament further distinguishes between adopted resolutions — those that have been approved by the plenary — and proposed resolutions that remain pending before committees. The Parliament has adopted over five hundred resolutions since its inauguration, reflecting the breadth of matters on which the legislative body has been called to express its institutional position.

The distinction between bills and resolutions is not merely procedural. It has consequences for the drafter. A bill requires the full apparatus of legislative drafting: a title, a policy declaration, definitions, substantive provisions, penal clauses where appropriate, and effectivity provisions. A resolution, while it should be drafted with care and clarity, follows a simpler format. Drafters must be attentive to this distinction from the outset, because the choice of instrument — bill or resolution — determines the structure, language, and procedural requirements of the entire document.

## 2.4 The Relationship Between Executive and Legislative Authority

The parliamentary system established by the BOL creates a relationship between the executive and legislative branches that is fundamentally different from the separation of powers at the national level. In the national government, the President is elected independently of Congress and may belong to a different political party than the legislative majority. In the Bangsamoro Government, the Chief Minister is elected by and from among the members of the Parliament, and the Cabinet is composed of ministers, the majority of whom must come from the Parliament.

This design has several implications for bill drafting.

First, the executive and legislative branches share a common political mandate. The Chief Minister formulates a platform of government that is subject to the approval of the Parliament, as provided in Article VII, Section 32(d) of the BOL. This means that the government's legislative program — the set of bills it considers priorities — flows from a policy agenda that the Parliament has endorsed. The drafter working on government-priority legislation is, in a practical sense, serving both branches simultaneously.

Second, the Chief Minister exercises executive authority on behalf of the Parliament, not independently of it. Article VII, Section 4 of the BOL provides that the executive function shall be exercised by the Cabinet headed by the Chief Minister. The Chief Minister appoints the heads of ministries, agencies, bureaus, and offices of the Bangsamoro Government, as well as officers of Bangsamoro-owned or controlled corporations. This power of appointment means that the implementing agencies named in any bill are, ultimately, accountable to the same Parliament that enacts the legislation.

Third, the Parliament retains the power to dissolve the government through a vote of no confidence. Article VII, Section 36 of the BOL provides that a vote of no confidence by at least two-thirds of all the members of the Parliament against the government of the day shall lead to the dissolution of the Parliament and the calling of a new parliamentary election. This mechanism ensures ongoing accountability of the executive to the legislature and reinforces the principle that legislative authority is supreme.

For the bill drafter, the practical consequence of these arrangements is that the coordination between the bill's policy intent and its implementing machinery is closer than in a presidential system. The drafter should ensure that the institutional structures created or empowered by a bill are consistent with the existing administrative organization of the Bangsamoro Government as established by the Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13), which provides for fifteen ministries, the Office of the Chief Minister, and their attached agencies, commissions, and offices.

## 2.5 The Oversight Function

Beyond its legislative and executive roles, the Parliament performs a critical oversight function. Oversight is the process by which the Parliament monitors the implementation of laws it has enacted, scrutinizes the use of public funds, and holds executive officers accountable for their performance.

The oversight function operates through several mechanisms. Standing committees conduct regular reviews of the ministries and agencies within their jurisdiction, summoning officials to report on program implementation, budget execution, and policy compliance. The Committee on Ways and Means exercises particular authority over fiscal matters, reviewing the annual budget proposal and monitoring revenue generation and expenditure.

The Parliament's Question Hour provides an additional channel for oversight. During Question Hour, Members of Parliament may direct questions to members of the Cabinet on matters within their respective portfolios. This mechanism, common to parliamentary systems worldwide, ensures that executive officials remain directly answerable to the legislative body that elected the Chief Minister and approved the Cabinet.

Oversight committees may also conduct investigations in aid of legislation, as authorized by Article VII, Section 5(d) of the BOL, which provides that the Parliament may "conduct inquiries in aid of legislation in accordance with its rules" while respecting the rights of persons appearing in or affected by such inquiries.

The oversight function is relevant to bill drafting because every bill should be drafted with implementation in mind. A bill that creates new obligations but provides no mechanism for monitoring compliance, that establishes a program but includes no reporting requirements, or that imposes penalties but designates no enforcement agency has been drafted without adequate attention to the oversight dimension. Effective bills anticipate the Parliament's need to monitor implementation and build appropriate accountability mechanisms into the text.

## 2.6 Parliamentary Procedures Under Resolution No. 268

The detailed procedural rules governing the Parliament's legislative business are contained in Resolution No. 268, which constitutes the Rules of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament. These rules govern the order of business, the committee system, the conduct of plenary sessions, the amendment process, and the voting procedures that determine how a bill moves from filing to enactment.

Drafters must be familiar with Resolution No. 268 because the procedural requirements it establishes have direct implications for the form and content of bills. For example, the rules prescribe the manner of referral to committees, which affects how a bill's subject matter should be framed to ensure appropriate committee jurisdiction. The rules also govern the amendment process on second reading, which means that drafters should anticipate the kinds of amendments that may be proposed and structure the bill's provisions in a manner that facilitates clear and orderly amendment.

Resolution No. 268 should be consulted alongside this manual. Where this manual addresses the substance and form of the bill as a document, Resolution No. 268 addresses the procedural pathway that the bill must travel. Together, they form the complete framework within which legislation is produced.

# Chapter 3

# The Legal Evolution of the Bangsamoro Government

## 3.1 From ARMM to BARMM: The Peace Process Journey

The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao did not emerge from an abstract exercise in constitutional design. It is the product of a peace process that spans decades and reflects the long struggle of the Bangsamoro people for self-determination within the framework of the Philippine Republic.

The modern Bangsamoro struggle is rooted in a history that predates the Philippine state itself. Before Spanish colonization, the Bangsamoro homeland was organized into independent sultanates — the Sultanate of Sulu, established in 1457; the Sultanate of Maguindanao; the Sultanate of Buayan; the Sultanate of Kabuntalan; and the Pat a Pangampong (Four Lake Kingdoms) of Ranao, among others. These polities maintained Islamic legal traditions, conducted international trade, and exercised effective sovereignty over their territories. Over more than three centuries of Spanish colonial rule, the Bangsamoro were never fully conquered, maintaining a distinct political and cultural identity throughout.

During the American period (1898-1946), Mindanao was administered separately as the "Moro Province." Despite the vehement resistance of the Bangsamoro, the region was integrated into the Philippine Commonwealth under the 1935 Constitution. The post-independence period saw continued advocacy for self-governance, which eventually took the form of armed resistance movements and, ultimately, peace negotiations.

Three milestones define the modern peace process. First, the 1996 Final Peace Agreement between the Government of the Philippines and the Moro National Liberation Front, signed under the Ramos administration, led to the creation of the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao under Republic Act No. 6734, as amended by Republic Act No. 9054. Second, the 2012 Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro between the Government of the Philippines and the Moro Islamic Liberation Front, signed under the Aquino III administration, established the framework for a new autonomous entity that would replace the ARMM. Third, the 2014 Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro consolidated the peace framework through four annexes covering transitional arrangements and modalities, revenue generation and wealth-sharing, power-sharing, and normalization.

The transition from ARMM to BARMM represented a fundamental institutional transformation. The ARMM operated under a unitary system of governance with twenty-five regional departments and limited fiscal autonomy. The BARMM, by contrast, was designed with a parliamentary system, a restructured bureaucracy of fifteen ministries, and genuine fiscal autonomy under the BOL. This transformation was not merely administrative. It reflected a new political settlement between the Bangsamoro people and the Philippine state, grounded in the recognition that the ARMM had failed to deliver the self-governance and development that the Bangsamoro sought.

## 3.2 The Bangsamoro Organic Law (RA 11054)

Republic Act No. 11054, the Bangsamoro Organic Law, was signed by President Rodrigo Roa Duterte on July 27, 2018. It was ratified through a two-part plebiscite in January and February 2019. The BOL replaced Republic Act No. 6734, as amended by Republic Act No. 9054, and established the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao as a new political entity with substantially greater autonomy than its predecessor.

The BOL is the foundational law of the Bangsamoro Government. All Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts must be consistent with it, just as all national legislation must be consistent with the Philippine Constitution. For the bill drafter, the BOL occupies the same position in the Bangsamoro legal hierarchy that a constitution occupies in a sovereign state: it is the supreme reference against which the validity of all subordinate legislation is measured.

The BOL is organized into eighteen articles covering the following subjects: the name and purpose of the autonomous region (Article I); Bangsamoro identity (Article II); territorial jurisdiction (Article III); general principles and policies (Article IV); powers of government (Article V); intergovernmental relations (Article VI); the Bangsamoro Government, including the Parliament, the Chief Minister, and the administrative organization (Article VII); the Wali (Article VIII); basic rights (Article IX); the justice system, including Shari'ah jurisdiction (Article X); economy, trade, investment, and tourism (Article XI); fiscal autonomy (Article XII); transitory provisions (Article XIII); amendments and revisions (Article XIV); the plebiscite (Article XV); the transition authority (Article XVI); general provisions (Article XVII); and effectivity (Article XVIII).

Of these, the articles most frequently relevant to bill drafting are Article V (powers of government), which defines the scope of the Parliament's legislative authority; Article VII (Bangsamoro Government), which prescribes the legislative process and the structure of governance; Article IX (basic rights), which establishes rights that legislation must respect and may not diminish; Article X (justice system), which governs the Shari'ah jurisdiction that intersects with many areas of Bangsamoro law; and Article XII (fiscal autonomy), which provides the fiscal framework within which all appropriations legislation must operate.

The drafter should also be aware of subsequent judicial interpretations that have shaped the BOL's application.

In *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea* (G.R. Nos. 242255, 243246, 243693, decided September 9, 2024; finalized November 26, 2024), the Supreme Court unanimously upheld the constitutionality of the BOL but ruled that the Province of Sulu is not part of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The Court held that the BOL's provision treating the former ARMM provinces as a single geographical unit for the ratification plebiscite violated Article X, Section 18 of the 1987 Constitution, which requires that only areas voting favorably shall be included. Because Sulu voters rejected the BOL by 54.3%, the province was constitutionally excluded. The Court applied the doctrine of operative fact to protect the legal validity of acts and policies performed while Sulu was presumed to be part of the BARMM. Executive Order No. 91, signed by President Marcos on July 30, 2025, implemented the ruling by realigning Sulu under Region IX (Zamboanga Peninsula).

This ruling has three consequences for the bill drafter. First, any bill that proposes programs, infrastructure, or administrative measures must exclude the Province of Sulu from its territorial scope. Second, the ruling required the redistricting of parliamentary seats — a process that ultimately produced BAA No. 86 (Parliamentary District Act of 2026), establishing thirty-two single-member parliamentary districts across the remaining constituent units. Third, the doctrine of operative fact, as applied in this case, provides a precedent for drafting transitional provisions that protect acts performed under a legal framework that is subsequently altered or invalidated.

Every drafter should maintain ready access to the full text of the BOL. No bill should be drafted without first verifying that its subject matter falls within the powers granted to the Bangsamoro Government and that its provisions are consistent with the rights, structures, and procedures that the BOL establishes.

## 3.3 The Transition Period

The BOL provided for a transition period during which the Bangsamoro Transition Authority (BTA) would exercise both legislative and executive powers in lieu of an elected Parliament. This transition was originally scheduled to conclude in 2022. Republic Act No. 11593, enacted in 2021, extended it to 2025. Republic Act No. 12064 further extended it to 2028.

During the transition, the BTA operated under three compositions:

- **First BTA (February 2019 – September 2022).** Appointed by President Duterte following the ratification of the BOL. The Parliament enacted its foundational legislation, including the Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13, enacted October 2020), the Bangsamoro Civil Service Code (enacted February 2021), and the Bangsamoro Education Code (enacted May 2021) — three of the seven priority codes identified in the BOL's transition framework.

- **Second BTA (September 2022 – February 2025).** Reconstituted by President Marcos pursuant to Republic Act No. 11593. This period saw the continuation of the legislative program, the enactment of additional Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts, and the passage of a fourth priority code — the Bangsamoro Electoral Code (BAA No. 35, enacted 2023).

- **Third BTA (March 2025 – 2028).** Appointed by President Marcos following the enactment of Republic Act No. 12064. This composition carried the mandate of completing the transition by enacting the remaining priority codes — the Revenue Code, the Local Government Code, and the Investment Code — and building the institutional capacity needed for the first parliamentary elections.

Legislative measures filed during one BTA composition were not archived when a new composition was appointed; they continued to be deliberated by the succeeding composition. This principle of **legislative continuity** meant that bills remained live instruments across BTA compositions and could be taken up, amended, consolidated, or enacted without refiling.

The transition period culminated in the preparation for the first Bangsamoro parliamentary elections. This process was shaped by two landmark Supreme Court decisions and a series of legislative responses:

- In *Ali et al. v. BTA* (G.R. No. E-02219) and *Macapaar et al. v. COMELEC* (G.R. No. E-02235), decided September 30, 2025, the Supreme Court *en banc* declared BAA No. 77 (Bangsamoro Redistricting Act of 2025) unconstitutional on the grounds that it was enacted during the election period in violation of the Voter's Registration Act, that its districts failed the BOL's requirement for contiguous, compact, and adjacent territorial jurisdictions, and that it contained procedural defects. The Court also ruled that BAA No. 58 could not be revived because its framework still included Sulu's seven districts. The Court ordered the Parliament to enact a constitutionally compliant districting law and directed the COMELEC to conduct elections no later than March 31, 2026.

- The Parliament responded by enacting BAA No. 86 (Parliamentary District Act, signed January 20, 2026), which established thirty-two single-member districts apportioned across the region's constituent units based on the 2024 population census: Lanao del Sur (9), Maguindanao del Norte (5), Maguindanao del Sur (5), Basilan (4), Tawi-Tawi (4), Cotabato City (3), and the Special Geographic Area (2). The Special Geographic Area comprises eight municipalities created from the sixty-three North Cotabato barangays that voted to join the BARMM in the 2019 plebiscite. The Parliament also enacted BAA No. 87 and BAA No. 88 (January 2026), amending the Bangsamoro Electoral Code to address election logistics.

- The Philippine Congress enacted legislation resetting the first parliamentary elections to September 2026, with elected Members of Parliament to assume office in October 2026.

These SC rulings established constitutional guardrails that constrain the Parliament's legislative power beyond the transition period. The drafter must ensure that any future redistricting legislation complies with the BOL's geographic requirements for parliamentary districts (contiguous, compact, adjacent) and that electoral legislation does not conflict with national election laws, including the prohibition on precinct alteration during the election period.

For the bill drafter, the transition period presented both opportunities and constraints. The opportunity lay in the breadth of the BTA's mandate: because the BTA exercised both legislative and executive power, coordination between policy development and legislative drafting could be closer and more efficient than under an elected government where coalition dynamics may complicate the legislative process. The constraint lay in the provisional nature of the BTA itself. Legislation enacted during the transition was drafted with awareness that a regularly elected Parliament would amend, repeal, or build upon it. Drafters were therefore encouraged to strive for legislation that was robust and well-structured — capable of serving as a durable foundation rather than a temporary expedient. This principle of durable draftsmanship applies with equal force under the regular Bangsamoro Government.

## 3.4 Legislative Powers of the Bangsamoro Parliament

Article V of the BOL defines the powers of government through a two-part allocation between the Bangsamoro Government and the National Government.

**Powers of the Bangsamoro Government.** Article V, Section 2 provides that "the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters" and enumerates over fifty subject areas, from (a) administration of justice through (ccc) water supply and services, flood control, and irrigation systems. These include: administrative organization; creation of sources of revenue; ancestral domain and natural resources management; social services; education and skills training; environmental protection; agriculture, livestock, and food security; disaster risk reduction and management; culture and language; cooperatives and social entrepreneurship; public works; tourism development; and many others. Article V, Section 3 supplements these enumerated powers with a general welfare clause, granting the Bangsamoro Government "the powers expressly granted, those necessarily implied therefrom, as well as powers necessary, appropriate, or incidental for its efficient and effective governance." When a bill addresses a subject that falls within these enumerated or incidental powers, the drafter may proceed with confidence that the Parliament has authority to legislate on the matter.

**Powers of the National Government.** Article V, Section 1 establishes a residual clause: "All powers, functions, and responsibilities not granted by the Constitution or by national law to the Bangsamoro Government shall be vested in the National Government." This means that any matter not enumerated in Section 2 or covered by the general welfare clause in Section 3 falls outside the Parliament's legislative competence. The drafter must ensure that a bill does not encroach upon matters that the BOL leaves to the National Government.

In practice, some of the enumerated Bangsamoro powers overlap with areas where national legislation also operates — such as education, labor, and disaster management — creating shared jurisdiction that requires careful harmonization. The drafter should always refer to the actual text of Article V when analyzing the Parliament's legislative authority.

The practical consequence for the drafter is that every bill must begin with a jurisdictional analysis. Before drafting a single provision, the drafter should identify the specific power or powers under Article V, Section 2 that authorize the Parliament to legislate on the bill's subject matter, and assess whether national legislation also operates in the same area. This analysis should be documented, at minimum informally, and in many cases should be reflected in the bill's policy declaration or "whereas" clauses, which cite the constitutional and organic law basis for the legislation.

## 3.5 The Relationship with National Legislation

The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region exists within the framework of the Philippine Republic, and its legislation operates within a layered legal system. Understanding this layering is essential for the drafter.

At the apex of the legal hierarchy stands the Philippine Constitution, which is the supreme law of the land and binds the Bangsamoro Government as it binds all other units of government. Article IV, Section 1 of the BOL affirms that "the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region is an integral, indivisible, and inseparable part of the territory of the Republic of the Philippines" and that "the Bangsamoro people shall uphold the Constitution as the fundamental law of the land."

Below the Constitution sits the Bangsamoro Organic Law itself, which functions as the regional equivalent of a constitution. The BOL defines the powers, structures, and rights of the Bangsamoro Government, and all Bangsamoro legislation must be consistent with it.

Below the BOL sit the Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts enacted by the Parliament. These are the primary legislative output of the Bangsamoro Government and constitute the body of regional law that governs the autonomous region's internal affairs.

National legislation — laws enacted by the Congress of the Philippines — continues to apply within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in all matters that fall outside the enumerated and incidental powers of the Bangsamoro Government, and in areas where Bangsamoro and national authority overlap but no Bangsamoro law has yet been enacted. In areas where the Bangsamoro Government has enumerated authority under Article V, Section 2, national laws that were in effect prior to the BOL continue to apply in a suppletory capacity until superseded by Bangsamoro legislation.

This legal architecture creates a specific drafting challenge. The drafter must be aware not only of the BOL and existing BAAs but also of national statutes that may apply to the bill's subject matter. For example, a bill establishing labor standards within the region must account for the Labor Code of the Philippines, since labor is enumerated in Article V, Section 2(jj) as a Bangsamoro power but also regulated nationally. A bill on environmental protection must consider the relationship between the Bangsamoro Government's enumerated power over environmental management within its territory (Article V, Section 2(x)) and the national government's regulatory framework under the Philippine Clean Air Act, the Ecological Solid Waste Management Act, and related statutes.

The drafter must also be attentive to the intergovernmental relations mechanisms established by Article VI of the BOL, which create institutional channels for resolving jurisdictional questions. The Intergovernmental Relations Body, the Philippine Congress-Bangsamoro Parliament Forum, the Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board, and related bodies all play a role in harmonizing national and Bangsamoro legislation. Bills that touch on areas of shared jurisdiction should, where appropriate, include provisions for coordination with the relevant national agencies or intergovernmental mechanisms.

Finally, the drafter should be aware that Shari'ah law constitutes an additional layer within the Bangsamoro legal system. Article X of the BOL establishes Shari'ah courts with jurisdiction over personal law matters — marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody, and property relations among Muslims — with the Qur'an and Sunnah as principal sources. Bills that touch on matters within the Shari'ah jurisdiction must be drafted with sensitivity to the coexistence of civil and religious legal traditions within the region, ensuring that provisions do not inadvertently conflict with established Shari'ah principles or encroach upon the jurisdiction of the Shari'ah courts.

The layered character of the Bangsamoro legal system demands a drafter who is not merely a wordsmith but a legal analyst. The chapters that follow in Part Two of this manual provide the technical tools for that work. But no amount of technical skill can substitute for the drafter's obligation to understand the legal framework within which the Parliament operates — a framework shaped by history, peace, and the aspirations of the Bangsamoro people for self-governance under the rule of law.
