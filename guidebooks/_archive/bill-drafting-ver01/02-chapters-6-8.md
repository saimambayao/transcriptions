# Chapter 6: Bill Drafting — The Process

The legislative drafter's task is to translate policy intent into precise legal language. This chapter sets out the step-by-step process from the moment a drafting instruction is received to the moment a finished bill is submitted for filing. Each stage is designed to prevent the common errors that stall bills in committee or invite damaging interpellation on the floor.

## 6.1 Legal Writing and Legal Drafting: A Distinction

Before addressing the drafting process, the drafter must understand what distinguishes bill drafting from other forms of legal writing. Legal writing and legal drafting are related skills that serve fundamentally different purposes.

| Legal Writing | Legal Drafting |
|---|---|
| Office memoranda | Contracts |
| Motion memoranda | Statutes |
| Appellate briefs | Local ordinances |
| Judicial opinions | Administrative regulations |
| Client letters | Court rules |
| Demand letters | Organizational bylaws |
| Other *analytical or persuasive* documents | Other *governing* documents |

The core distinction: legal writing is **rule explanation** — it analyzes, interprets, and argues about existing rules. Legal drafting is **rule creation** — it produces the rules themselves. A legal memo persuades; a statute commands. A brief explains what the law means; a bill determines what the law will be.

This distinction has practical consequences. Legal writing may employ rhetorical devices, persuasive language, and argumentative structure. Legislative drafting must avoid all of these. A statute that argues for its own existence is a statute that failed to resolve the argument before enactment. The drafter's voice must be neutral, precise, and authoritative — never persuasive, never apologetic.

Drafting skills cannot be fully taught in a classroom or academic setting. They are developed through sustained practice under the guidance of experienced practitioners. It takes eighteen months to two years of consistent practice before a drafter achieves reliable competence. This manual accelerates that process by codifying the conventions and principles that experienced drafters apply instinctively, but it is not a substitute for practice.

## 6.2 What a Bill Can Do

Every bill accomplishes one or more of five legislative actions:

1. **Impose duties** or confer **rights** or **privileges** — requiring or permitting someone to do or refrain from doing something
2. **Create** a political or administrative **entity** — establishing a commission, office, or body
3. **Grant authority** — giving a person or body ministerial (mandatory) or discretionary power to act
4. **Make a declaration** — declaring a status, condition, or fact to be legally true

Each of these actions may be qualified by attaching a **test** (a factual criterion that must be met), a **condition** (a circumstance that must exist), or an **exception** (a carve-out that limits the action's scope).

Understanding this taxonomy prevents a common drafting error: producing sections that read well as prose but create no legal effect. Every substantive section of a bill must accomplish at least one of these five actions. If a section does not impose a duty, confer a right, create an entity, grant authority, or make a declaration, it is not a normative provision and should be reexamined. It may belong in the Explanatory Note rather than the bill itself.

### Thornton's Five Stages of Bill Drafting

The bill-drafting process, as described by the legislative scholar G.C. Thornton, proceeds through five stages:

1. **Understanding the proposal** — comprehending the policy the bill seeks to implement, the problem it seeks to solve, and the questions that must be answered before drafting begins
2. **Analyzing the proposal** — conducting policy analysis, legal analysis, and practicality assessment; continuing consultation with the proponent
3. **Designing the law** — developing the hierarchical structure and outline, guided by the parliamentary rules and the bill's complexity
4. **Composing and developing the draft** — writing the legislative text section by section
5. **Scrutiny and testing the draft** — systematic review of cross-references, numbering, defined terms, language, form, and substance

The ten-step pre-drafting checklist that follows operationalizes the first three stages for the Bangsamoro context. Stages 4 and 5 are addressed in sections 6.6 and 6.7.

## 6.3 The Pre-Drafting Checklist

Before a single word of legislative text is written, the drafter must complete ten preliminary steps. These steps are not optional — they are the foundation upon which a sound bill is built. Skipping any one of them risks producing a bill that is unconstitutional, ultra vires, redundant, or unimplementable.

### Step 1: Ascertain the Purpose

The drafter must determine with exactitude what the requester — whether a Member of Parliament, a committee, the Chief Minister, or a ministry — wants to accomplish. A vague instruction produces a vague bill. The drafter should ask: What is the specific problem this bill is meant to solve? What outcome is desired? Who are the intended beneficiaries? What powers or obligations will be created? If the requester cannot articulate the purpose clearly, the drafter must press for clarification before proceeding.

### Step 2: Check Constitutionality

Every Bangsamoro Autonomy Act must conform to the 1987 Philippine Constitution. The Bangsamoro Organic Law itself derives its authority from the Constitution, and no act of the Parliament may exceed those constitutional limits. The drafter should examine whether the proposed bill implicates any constitutional provision — particularly the Bill of Rights, the provisions on local autonomy, and the limitations on legislative power. A bill that violates the Constitution is void from the beginning, regardless of how many readings it passes.

### Step 3: Check BOL Authority

Republic Act No. 11054, the Bangsamoro Organic Law, defines the legislative powers of the Bangsamoro Parliament. Not every subject is within the Parliament's jurisdiction. Article V, Section 2 enumerates the matters over which the Bangsamoro Government exercises authority, while Article V, Section 1 establishes a residual clause vesting all other powers in the National Government. The drafter must verify that the subject matter of the proposed bill falls within the Parliament's legislative competence under Article V. A bill on a subject that falls outside the enumerated and incidental powers — such as foreign affairs, national defense, or the monetary system — is ultra vires and cannot be enacted.

### Step 4: Research Existing BARMM Law

The Bangsamoro Parliament has enacted over eighty Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts to date. The drafter must search the full corpus of enacted BAAs to determine whether existing legislation already covers the subject. If so, the drafter must decide whether the proposed bill should amend the existing law, repeal and replace it, or supplement it with additional provisions. Drafting a new act on a topic already governed by an existing BAA without addressing the relationship between the two creates a conflict that courts and implementing agencies will struggle to resolve.

### Step 5: Research National Legislation

Many subjects within the Bangsamoro's legislative competence are also governed by national laws — Republic Acts, Presidential Decrees, and Executive Orders. The drafter must identify all relevant national legislation to ensure that the proposed bill aligns with or properly departs from national standards. The BOL provides that in case of conflict between a BAA and a national law on a matter within the Bangsamoro's enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2, the BAA prevails. On matters where Bangsamoro and national authority overlap, however, the relationship is more nuanced, and the drafter must navigate these boundaries carefully.

### Step 6: Search for Conflicts

Beyond checking existing BARMM and national laws individually, the drafter must examine whether the proposed bill will contradict specific provisions of other statutes. This is particularly important when a bill touches multiple areas of law. For example, a bill creating a new Bangsamoro agency must not conflict with the organizational structure established in the Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13), the fiscal management framework under the Bangsamoro appropriations acts, or the civil service provisions in the Bangsamoro Civil Service Code (BAA No. 17). Conflicts between laws breed litigation and administrative paralysis.

### Step 7: Search for Analogues

The drafter should determine whether similar legislation has been filed — either in the Bangsamoro Parliament, the Philippine Congress, or other autonomous or federal legislatures. An existing bill or enacted law on a comparable subject can serve as a model, saving time and ensuring that tested formulations are adopted rather than untested ones. This is not an invitation to copy blindly; every analogue must be adapted to the Bangsamoro context, the BOL's requirements, and the specific policy objectives of the requester.

### Step 8: Outline the Bill

Before drafting any provision, the drafter must plan the bill's architecture. This means determining how many sections or articles the bill requires, in what order they should appear, and how the substantive provisions relate to one another. A well-planned outline prevents the most common structural defect in legislative drafts: provisions that are out of sequence, duplicative, or missing entirely. Section 6.5 below addresses the outline process in detail.

### Step 9: Identify the Implementing Agency

Every bill that creates new powers, duties, or programs must designate the ministry, office, or agency responsible for implementation. In the BARMM, the implementing agency must be one that has the mandate and capacity to carry out the law's requirements. BAA No. 20 (the Bangsamoro Dual Calendar Act), for instance, designated the Office of the Chief Minister through the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' as the implementing office. If no existing agency has the mandate, the bill may need to create a new office or expand an existing one's functions — a decision that carries fiscal and administrative implications the drafter must flag.

### Step 10: Determine Fiscal Impact

Does the proposed bill require the expenditure of public funds? If so, the drafter must estimate the fiscal impact and include an appropriations provision. The Bangsamoro Parliament cannot create unfunded mandates without accounting for how they will be financed. BAA No. 4 (the Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission Act) and BAA No. 20 both included specific funding provisions. Even when a bill does not directly appropriate funds, the drafter should consider whether it will impose costs on implementing agencies, local government units, or the private sector, and disclose these costs in the explanatory note.

## 6.4 The Three Principles: Accuracy, Brevity, Clarity

All legislative drafting rests on three principles — Accuracy, Brevity, and Clarity — known collectively as the ABC of drafting. These principles are not aspirational ideals; they are practical necessities. The consequences of violating them are tangible: bills that say one thing when another was intended, provisions so wordy that their meaning is buried, and language so ambiguous that opposing interpretations are equally plausible.

### Accuracy

Accuracy means saying exactly what is meant — no more and no less. Every word in a statute carries legal force. A provision that inadvertently includes persons it was not intended to cover, or excludes persons it was meant to protect, fails the test of accuracy. The drafter must choose words whose legal meanings match the intended scope precisely. Where a term has a settled legal meaning, the drafter should use it in that settled sense. Where a term might be understood differently in ordinary usage than in law, the drafter must define it in the Definition of Terms section.

### Brevity

Brevity means using the fewest words that achieve accuracy. Brevity is not an end in itself — a provision that is short but inaccurate is worse than one that is long but precise. Rather, brevity serves accuracy by eliminating surplus words that might introduce unintended meanings. The drafter should prefer short sentences to long ones, active voice to passive, and single words to circumlocutions. "Shall" is shorter than "is hereby required to." "May" is shorter than "is hereby authorized and empowered to." Every unnecessary word is a potential source of ambiguity.

### Clarity

Clarity means writing so that a person reading in good faith understands the provision, and a person reading in bad faith cannot misunderstand it. Clarity is the most difficult of the three principles because it requires the drafter to anticipate how language will be received by diverse readers — legislators deliberating the bill, agencies implementing it, courts interpreting it, and citizens complying with it. The drafter must write for all of these audiences simultaneously.

The importance of these principles is not merely academic. A survey of Members of Parliament conducted during the preparation of this manual revealed that precision in word choice is paramount in parliamentary deliberation. As one respondent observed, "one wrong word will spell a difference." Multiple respondents noted that compound sentences — provisions that attempt to pack several ideas into a single grammatical unit — are among the chief causes of delay during deliberation, because Members of Parliament stop to seek clarification on individual words and phrases before they can consider the provision as a whole.

The practical implication is straightforward: prefer short, declarative sentences. Express one rule per sentence. If a provision contains multiple conditions or consequences, break them into separate subsections or paragraphs rather than connecting them with conjunctions.

## 6.5 Drafting the Outline

The outline is the blueprint of the bill. It determines the order in which provisions appear, the level of structural organization required, and the logical flow from general principles to specific rules to final provisions.

### Simple Structure vs. Complex Structure

The drafter must first choose between two structural approaches:

**Simple structure (Section-based).** A simple bill is organized entirely by numbered sections, without grouping sections into articles. This structure is appropriate for short, single-subject bills. BAA No. 1 (the Bangsamoro Flag Act) uses this structure: it contains five sections — Adoption, Declaration of Policy, Design, Hoisting and Display, and Effectivity — arranged in a straightforward sequence without articles or chapters.

**Complex structure (Article-based).** A complex bill groups related sections into articles, each with its own heading. This structure is necessary when a bill addresses multiple subject areas or contains fifteen or more sections. BAA No. 4 (the Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission Act) uses an article-based structure, dividing its provisions into articles on Title, Policy and Principles, the Commission's creation, powers and functions, and final provisions. The most complex BAAs — such as BAA No. 13 (the Bangsamoro Administrative Code) and BAA No. 17 (the Bangsamoro Civil Service Code) — further organize articles into books and chapters.

The general rule: use the simplest structure that accommodates the bill's content. Do not impose articles on a bill that needs only sections, and do not force a bill with thirty substantive provisions into a flat section-only format.

### The Standard Section Sequence

Whether the bill uses a simple or complex structure, its provisions should follow a standard sequence:

1. **Short Title** — the popular name by which the act will be cited
2. **Declaration of Policy** — the broad policy objectives the bill serves
3. **Purpose or Objective** — the specific aims of the bill
4. **Definition of Terms** — definitions of words used in a technical or specialized sense
5. **Scope or Coverage** — who and what the bill applies to
6. **Substantive Provisions** — the operative rules that create rights, duties, prohibitions, or powers
7. **Administrative Provisions** — the agencies, offices, and mechanisms for implementation
8. **Enforcement Provisions** — penalties, sanctions, and remedies for violations
9. **Final Provisions** — appropriations, transitory provisions, repealing clause, separability clause, and effectivity

This sequence is not arbitrary. It reflects the logical order in which a reader — whether a legislator, an implementing officer, or a citizen — needs to encounter the bill's provisions. The reader must first know the law's name, then its purpose, then the meaning of its terms, then who it applies to, before encountering the substantive rules themselves. And the administrative machinery and penalties make sense only after the substantive provisions have established what must be done.

### When to Use Articles

Articles should be used when a bill has fifteen or more sections covering multiple distinct subject areas. The threshold is not rigid, but it reflects the point at which a flat list of sections becomes difficult to navigate. Each article should group sections that address a coherent topic. The standard article organization for a complex bill is:

- Article I: General Provisions (Short Title, Declaration of Policy, Purpose, Definition of Terms, Scope)
- Article II: Substantive Provisions
- Article III: Administrative Provisions
- Article IV: Enforcement Provisions
- Article V: Final Provisions

This five-article framework can be expanded as needed. BAA No. 5 (the Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office Act) uses articles to separate its preliminary provisions from its provisions on functions and powers, organizational structure, and final matters.

## 6.6 Drafting Section by Section

With the outline complete, the drafter proceeds to draft the bill provision by provision, in the order established by the outline. This sequential approach ensures that each provision builds logically on those that precede it. A definition introduced in Section 3 can be relied upon in Section 10; a power established in Section 7 can be enforced in Section 15.

The drafter should work through each provision carefully, applying the ABC principles at every stage. For each section, the drafter should ask:

- **Is this provision accurate?** Does it say exactly what the requester intended, without inadvertent overreach or gaps?
- **Is this provision brief?** Can any words be removed without changing the meaning?
- **Is this provision clear?** Will the reader understand it on a first reading? Could a hostile reader twist its meaning?

Where the drafting instruction calls for collaborative development — as is common with committee bills — the drafter should present each section or group of sections for review before proceeding to the next. This iterative approach catches errors early and ensures that the requester's intent is faithfully captured. Where a complete draft is requested in one delivery, the drafter should still review the entire bill against the following quality checklist before submission:

**The Quality Review Checklist:**

1. Does the Short Title accurately reflect the bill's content?
2. Does the Declaration of Policy align with the BOL and the Constitution?
3. Are all specialized terms defined in the Definition of Terms?
4. Are defined terms used consistently throughout the bill?
5. Is the scope provision clear about who is covered and who is not?
6. Do the substantive provisions accomplish the stated purpose?
7. Is an implementing agency designated, and does it have the capacity to act?
8. Are enforcement provisions proportionate and enforceable?
9. Is the appropriations provision adequate for implementation?
10. Does the repealing clause correctly identify laws being repealed or modified?
11. Is the separability clause included?
12. Is the effectivity clause properly formulated?

## 6.7 The Interpellation Test

Before submitting a final draft, the drafter should mentally subject each provision to the questions most likely to arise during interpellation — the formal questioning that occurs during the second reading of a bill. Interpellation is the moment when a bill's weaknesses are exposed on the floor. A drafter who anticipates these questions can preempt them by strengthening the bill's text or by ensuring that the sponsor is prepared with answers.

The common interpellation questions, and the drafting responses they demand, are as follows:

**"What is the legal basis for this provision?"** Every substantive provision should be traceable to a specific constitutional provision, BOL section, or existing statute. The drafter should ensure that the Declaration of Policy section cites the relevant legal authorities and that individual provisions, where necessary, reference their statutory foundation. BAA No. 20, for example, expressly invokes the Bangsamoro Government's right to self-governance under Republic Act No. 11054 as the basis for mandating the dual calendar system.

**"How does this interact with existing BAAs?"** The drafter must be prepared to explain the relationship between the proposed bill and every existing BAA that touches the same subject. If the bill amends an existing act, this relationship should be explicit in the text. If the bill operates independently, the drafter should verify that no conflict exists.

**"What is the fiscal impact?"** Members of Parliament regularly inquire about the cost of implementation. The drafter should ensure that the appropriations provision identifies the funding source and amount, as BAA No. 20 does in its Section 9, which allocates Ten Million Pesos from available Bangsamoro Government funds for implementation.

**"Which agency implements this, and do they have the capacity?"** A bill that assigns implementation to an agency without the mandate, personnel, or resources to carry it out is a bill that will remain a dead letter. The drafter should verify the implementing agency's readiness and, where necessary, include provisions for capacity-building or the creation of new organizational units.

**"What happens if someone violates this provision?"** Every prohibition or obligation should have a corresponding consequence. If the bill creates a duty, the drafter must address what occurs when that duty is breached — whether through administrative sanctions, civil liability, or criminal penalties.

**"Was there public consultation?"** While this is not a drafting question in the strict sense, the drafter should be aware that Members of Parliament may ask whether stakeholders were consulted. The explanatory note is the appropriate place to document any consultation process.

A bill that survives the interpellation test on paper will survive it on the floor.

# Chapter 7: Parts of a Bill

Every Bangsamoro bill is composed of distinct parts, each serving a specific function. Understanding these parts — their purpose, their placement, and their relationship to one another — is essential to producing a bill that meets parliamentary standards and communicates its content effectively.

## 7.1 Overview: The Two-Part Structure

A bill filed in the Bangsamoro Parliament consists of two principal parts:

- **Part A: The Explanatory Note** — a narrative document that sets out the reasons for the bill, its salient features, and its policy context.
- **Part B: The Main Body** — the operative provisions that, upon enactment, become law.

Both parts share the same caption (heading block), which identifies the bill's number, author, and long title. The caption appears at the top of the document and is described in full in Chapter 8.

The explanatory note precedes the main body when a bill is filed, but it is the main body alone that constitutes the enacted law. The explanatory note has no legal force after enactment; it serves instead as a legislative history document that courts and implementing agencies may consult for interpretive guidance.

## 7.2 The Explanatory Note

### Purpose

The explanatory note sets out the reasons for the bill. It is the author's opportunity to explain, in narrative prose, why the bill is needed, what problem it addresses, what solution it proposes, and what benefits it is expected to deliver. Although the explanatory note is not a mandatory requirement of the Bangsamoro Parliament — a bill without one may still be filed — it is strongly advantageous to include one. A detailed explanatory note serves multiple purposes: it orients committee members who will review the bill, it provides the sponsor with a ready-made framework for the sponsorship speech, and it creates a record of legislative intent that can guide future interpretation.

### Form and Structure

There is no prescribed form for the explanatory note. However, the best explanatory notes share a common structure that addresses the reader's natural questions in a logical order:

1. **Problem Statement.** What is the existing condition that requires legislative intervention? What harm is being suffered, what gap exists in the law, or what policy objective is not being met?

2. **Proposed Solution.** How does the bill address the problem? What mechanisms, powers, duties, or institutions does it create or modify?

3. **Expected Benefits.** What outcomes will result from enactment? Who will benefit, and how?

4. **Background and Context.** What is the legal, social, economic, or historical background that supports the bill? Are there constitutional or BOL mandates that compel action? Have other jurisdictions addressed this issue?

5. **Salient Features.** What are the bill's most important provisions? This section provides a brief summary of the bill's key operative sections.

The level of detail in the explanatory note should be proportionate to the bill's complexity. A simple bill adopting an official symbol may require only a few paragraphs. A comprehensive code — such as the Bangsamoro Administrative Code or the Civil Service Code — may warrant an explanatory note of several pages.

### Signatory

The explanatory note is signed by its author. For a private member bill, this is the Member of Parliament who authored or filed the bill. For a committee bill, it may be signed by the committee chairperson. For a cabinet bill — one originating from the Bangsamoro Government of the Day — it is signed by the Chief Minister.

### Example: BAA No. 20

The bill that became BAA No. 20, the Bangsamoro Dual Calendar Act, illustrates the structure of a well-crafted explanatory note. The bill addressed the policy of mandating the dual use of Hijri and Gregorian calendars across all BARMM institutions. Its explanatory note would have set out the problem (the exclusive use of the Gregorian calendar in official documents, which did not reflect the Islamic heritage of the Bangsamoro people), the proposed solution (mandating dual calendar use in all official correspondence and documents), the legal basis (the Bangsamoro Government's right to self-governance under RA 11054 and the BOL's provisions on cultural identity), and the expected benefits (inclusiveness, cultural recognition, and fulfillment of Islamic observances). The bill's main body then translated these policy objectives into twelve operative sections covering scope, definitions, implementation, and funding.

## 7.3 The Main Body — Three Groups of Provisions

The main body of a bill — the part that becomes law upon enactment — is organized into three groups of provisions: General Provisions, Main Provisions, and Final Provisions. Together, these three groups form a complete legislative instrument.

### Group 1: General Provisions (Preliminary)

General provisions introduce the bill. They set the stage for the operative rules that follow by establishing the law's name, purpose, vocabulary, and scope. The general provisions typically include:

**Short Title.** The popular name by which the act will be cited. Every BAA should have one. The short title is usually the bill's first section. Examples:

- "This Act shall be known as the Bangsamoro Hymn Act of 2020." (BAA No. 7, Section 1)
- "This Act shall be known as 'The Bangsamoro Dual Calendar Act of 2021.'" (BAA No. 20, Section 1)
- "This Act shall be known and cited as the 'Bangsamoro Administrative Code.'" (BAA No. 13, Section 1)
- "This Act shall be known as the 'Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office Act of 2019.'" (BAA No. 5, Section 1)

**Declaration of Policy.** A statement of the broad policy principles that animate the bill. This provision connects the bill to the constitutional and BOL framework and articulates the values the bill serves. BAA No. 7 declares: "In the exercise of its right to self-governance, the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao is free to adopt its Bangsamoro Hymn that conveys and preserves the peoples' identity, history, heritage, and aspirations." BAA No. 17 frames its Declaration of Policy around ensuring merit and fitness in civil service appointments, promoting human resource administration, and adopting measures for morale and professionalism.

**Purpose or Objective.** Where the Declaration of Policy states broad principles, the Purpose provision states specific aims. BAA No. 13 provides: "This Code is promulgated to prescribe the structural, functional and procedural principles and rules of governance of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao." Some bills combine the Declaration of Policy and Purpose into a single provision; others separate them. The drafter should use separate provisions when the policy principles and the specific objectives are distinct enough to warrant independent treatment.

**Definition of Terms.** This section assigns precise meanings to words used in a specialized or technical sense throughout the bill. A well-drafted Definition of Terms section prevents interpretive disputes. BAA No. 20 defines "Hijri Calendar," "Gregorian Calendar," and "Hijrah" — terms that are central to the bill's operation. BAA No. 5 defines "Attorney-General," "Employee," "Legal officer," "Legal service," "Chief Minister," "Office," and "Officer" — each term carefully distinguished from related concepts. The definitions should be arranged alphabetically and should cover every term that might be misunderstood or that the drafter uses in a sense different from ordinary usage.

**Interpretation.** Some bills include an interpretation provision that establishes rules for construing the act — for instance, that the act shall be liberally interpreted to achieve its purpose, or that headings are for convenience only and do not affect interpretation.

**Scope, Coverage, or Applicability.** This provision identifies who and what is subject to the act. BAA No. 20 specifies that covered agencies include "all ministries, bureaus, offices, commissions, administrations, boards, committees, institutes, corporations whether performing governmental or proprietary function, or any other units of the BARMM, as well as provincial local government units, city local government units, municipal local government units, barangay local government units, and public and private schools, including basic, higher and technical education." Precision in the scope provision is critical — it determines the bill's reach and, by implication, its limits.

### Group 2: Main Provisions

Main provisions constitute the heart of the bill. They are the provisions that create rights, impose duties, establish institutions, authorize expenditures, and regulate conduct. They are subdivided into three categories:

**Substantive Provisions.** These are the provisions that accomplish the bill's principal purpose. They create the rules that the bill is designed to establish. In BAA No. 4, the substantive provisions create the Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission, define its composition, establish its mandate, and enumerate its powers. In BAA No. 20, the substantive provisions mandate dual calendar use, specify the format for writing dates, and enumerate the act's specific objectives. In BAA No. 1, the substantive provision adopts the official Bangsamoro flag and prescribes its design in precise detail — colors specified by HEX code, dimensions stated in feet and fractions, and symbols enumerated with their corresponding meanings.

**Administrative Provisions.** These provisions establish the mechanisms for implementing the substantive provisions. They designate the implementing agency, define its powers and functions, prescribe organizational structure, and provide for staffing and budgets. BAA No. 20 designates the Office of the Chief Minister through the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' as the implementing office and enumerates its powers and functions, including promulgating religious verdicts, leading interfaith dialogue, and performing moon-sighting for the Hijri calendar. BAA No. 5 devotes substantial provisions to the organizational structure, staffing, and operations of the Attorney-General's Office.

**Enforcement Provisions.** These provisions prescribe what happens when the law is violated. They may establish administrative penalties, civil liability, criminal sanctions, or a combination of these. Not every bill requires enforcement provisions — a bill adopting an official hymn, for instance, may not need them — but any bill that creates prohibitions or mandatory obligations should include enforcement mechanisms proportionate to the seriousness of the obligation.

### Group 3: Final Provisions

Final provisions address the administrative and transitional matters necessary to bring the law into operation and to manage its relationship with existing legislation. The standard final provisions, in the order they should appear, are:

**Oversight.** A provision designating a parliamentary committee to oversee the law's implementation, where appropriate.

**Reporting or Review.** A provision requiring the implementing agency to submit periodic reports to the Parliament or to conduct a review of the law's effectiveness after a specified period.

**Rule-Making (IRR).** A provision directing the implementing agency to issue Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR) within a specified period after enactment. The IRR provision should specify who issues the rules, what process must be followed (e.g., public consultation), and the deadline for issuance.

**Appropriation.** A provision allocating funds for implementation. BAA No. 20 provides: "For the operation and implementation of this law, the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' shall have an initial funding of Ten Million Pesos (Php 10,000,000.00) which shall be sourced from any available funds of the Bangsamoro Government and every year thereafter shall be included in the regular budget of agency subject to usual accounting, audit rules, and regulations."

**Transitory Provisions.** Provisions that manage the transition from the old legal regime to the new one — for instance, providing that existing regulations remain in force until the IRR is issued, or that incumbent officers continue in their positions until replacements are appointed.

**Sunset Clause.** A provision specifying that the act or certain of its provisions will expire on a stated date or upon the occurrence of a stated event, unless renewed by Parliament.

**Saving Clause.** A provision preserving rights, obligations, or proceedings that accrued or commenced under the prior law.

**Separability Clause.** A provision ensuring that if any provision of the act is declared invalid or unconstitutional, the remaining provisions continue in force. BAA No. 20 provides the standard formulation: "If any provision or part hereof is held invalid or unconstitutional, the remainder or the provisions not otherwise affected shall remain valid and subsisting."

**Repealing Clause.** A provision identifying which existing laws, or provisions thereof, are repealed or modified by the new act. BAA No. 20 uses a general repealing clause: "Any law or issuance, executive order, letter of instruction, administrative order, rule or regulation contrary to, or inconsistent with, the provisions of this Act are hereby repealed, modified or amended accordingly." Where possible, a specific repealing clause — naming the exact provisions repealed — is preferable because it leaves less room for dispute.

**Effectivity Clause.** A provision stating when the act takes effect. The standard formulation in Bangsamoro legislation provides that the act "shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in a newspaper of general or regional circulation," as in BAA No. 20 and BAA No. 1. Some acts take effect immediately, as in BAA No. 14, which states: "This Act shall take effect immediately after its publication in a newspaper of regional circulation in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."

```
    ├── Separability Clause
    ├── Repealing Clause
    └── Effectivity Clause
```

Not every bill will contain all of these provisions. A simple bill may omit the Oversight, Reporting, Sunset, and Saving provisions. But every bill must contain, at minimum, a Short Title, substantive provisions that accomplish its purpose, a Separability Clause, and an Effectivity Clause. The Repealing Clause should also be included in any bill that touches upon subjects governed by existing law.

# Chapter 8: The Caption of a Bill

The caption is the heading block that appears at the top of every bill filed in the Bangsamoro Parliament. It identifies the bill's origin, its place in the parliamentary process, its authorship, and its subject. A properly formatted caption is not merely a formality — it is the first thing a Member of Parliament reads, the basis on which the bill is catalogued and tracked, and the foundation for the long title that defines the bill's scope.

## 8.1 Elements of the Caption

The caption of a Bangsamoro bill contains seven elements, presented in the following order:

### Enacting Political Entities and Seat of Government

The caption begins with a block identifying the political entities under whose authority the bill is filed and the seat of government. The standard formulation is:

> Republic of the Philippines
> Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao
> BANGSAMORO PARLIAMENT
> Bangsamoro Government Center, Cotabato City

This block establishes two things: first, that the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region exists within the Republic of the Philippines and exercises its legislative power as part of the Philippine constitutional order; and second, that the Bangsamoro Parliament, seated in Cotabato City, is the legislating body. The formulation has varied slightly across enacted BAAs — earlier acts used "BARMM Compound, Cotabato City" while later acts use "Bangsamoro Government Center, Cotabato City" — but the essential elements remain constant.

### Parliament Identification and Legislating Authority

The next element identifies which body is legislating and under what authority. During the transition period, bills used:

> BANGSAMORO TRANSITION AUTHORITY

Under the regular Bangsamoro Government, this element reflects the elected Parliament. The drafter should use the designation that is current at the time of filing.

### Session Identification

The caption identifies the parliamentary session during which the bill is filed:

> (FIRST REGULAR SESSION)
> (SECOND REGULAR SESSION)
> (THIRD REGULAR SESSION)

BAA Nos. 1, 4, 5, and 7 were enacted during the First Regular Session. BAA Nos. 13, 14, and 17 were enacted during the Second Regular Session. BAA No. 20 was enacted during the Third Regular Session. Special sessions, if convened, are identified accordingly.

### Bill Number

Each bill receives a unique number upon filing. The numbering prefix indicates the bill's origin:

- **Cabinet Bill No. ___** — A bill originating from the Bangsamoro Government of the Day (the executive branch), submitted to Parliament through the Chief Minister.
- **Committee Bill No. ___** — A bill produced by a parliamentary committee in the exercise of its legislative function.
- **Private Member Bill No. ___** — A bill filed by an individual Member of Parliament.

The bill number is assigned by the Office of the Secretary-General upon filing and is used to track the bill through the legislative process. It is distinct from the BAA number, which is assigned only upon enactment.

### Sponsorship

The caption identifies the bill's author and, where applicable, its co-authors:

> Introduced by: [Name of MP / Committee / Government of the Day]
> Co-Authors: [Names of co-authoring MPs]

For a cabinet bill, the introduction line typically states that the bill is introduced by the Government of the Day. For a private member bill, it names the filing MP. For a committee bill, it names the committee. Co-authors are listed separately.

### Long Title

The long title is the formal description of the bill's subject matter. It appears in ALL CAPS and begins with the words "AN ACT." The long title is discussed in detail in Section 8.2 below.

### Enacting Clause

The enacting clause appears immediately before the first section of the bill's main body. It is discussed in Section 8.3 below.

## 8.2 The Long Title

The long title is one of the most important elements of a bill. It defines the bill's subject matter in general terms, in plain language. It serves three functions: it informs readers of the bill's content, it constrains the scope of what the bill may contain (a provision not covered by the long title may be challenged as a rider), and it provides courts with interpretive guidance on the bill's purpose.

### General Rules

The long title must describe the bill's subject accurately and completely, yet in general terms. It must be broad enough to encompass all of the bill's provisions but specific enough to give a meaningful indication of the bill's content. A long title that is too narrow will fail to cover all provisions; one that is too vague will provide no useful information.

The long title always begins with "AN ACT" followed by a gerund (a present participle verb form) that describes the bill's primary action. It is written in ALL CAPS. Every word in the long title carries significance — courts will construe the scope of the act with reference to its long title.

### Three Types of Long Titles

**New Law.** The long title for a new law uses a gerund that describes the law's principal action. Common formulations include:

- **"AN ACT ADOPTING..."** — used when the bill adopts or establishes something. BAA No. 1: "AN ACT ADOPTING THE OFFICIAL FLAG OF THE BANGSAMORO AUTONOMOUS REGION IN MUSLIM MINDANAO." BAA No. 7: "AN ACT ADOPTING THE OFFICIAL HYMN OF THE BANGSAMORO AUTONOMOUS REGION IN MUSLIM MINDANAO."

- **"AN ACT CREATING..."** — used when the bill establishes a new institution, office, or body. BAA No. 5: "AN ACT CREATING THE BANGSAMORO ATTORNEY-GENERAL'S OFFICE, DEFINING ITS FUNCTIONS AND POWERS, APPROPRIATING FUNDS THEREFOR, AND FOR OTHER RELATED PURPOSES."

- **"AN ACT OPERATIONALIZING..."** — used when the bill implements a mandate from the BOL or another existing law. BAA No. 4: "AN ACT OPERATIONALIZING THE ESTABLISHMENT OF THE BANGSAMORO HUMAN RIGHTS COMMISION, PROVIDING FOR ITS MANDATE, POWERS, AND FUNCTIONS, APPROPRIATING FUNDS THEREFOR, AND FOR OTHER PURPOSES."

- **"AN ACT MANDATING..."** — used when the bill imposes a new requirement. BAA No. 20: "AN ACT MANDATING THE DUAL USE OF HIJRI AND GREGORIAN CALENDARS IN ALL MINISTRIES, AGENCIES, LOCAL GOVERNMENT UNITS, OFFICES AND INSTITUTIONS IN THE BANGSAMORO AUTONOMOUS REGION IN MUSLIM MINDANAO (BARMM), APPROPRIATING FUNDS THEREFOR AND OTHER PURPOSES."

- **"AN ACT PROVIDING..."** — used when the bill establishes a comprehensive framework. BAA No. 13: "AN ACT PROVIDING FOR THE BANGSAMORO ADMINISTRATIVE CODE AND FOR OTHER RELATED PURPOSES." BAA No. 17: "AN ACT PROVIDING FOR THE BANGSAMORO CIVIL SERVICE CODE OF THE BANGSAMORO AUTONOMOUS REGION IN MUSLIM MINDANAO (BARMM)."

**Amendatory Law.** When a bill amends an existing act, the long title must specifically identify the act being amended. The key verb is "TO AMEND" or "AMENDING," and the long title must cite the act number and, where relevant, the specific section being modified. BAA No. 14 provides a clear example: "AN ACT EXTENDING THE AVAILABILITY OF THE 2020 BANGSAMORO APPROPRIATIONS TO DECEMBER 31, 2021, EXCEPT PERSONNEL SERVICES, AMENDING FOR THE PURPOSE SECTION 47 OF THE GENERAL PROVISIONS OF BANGSAMORO AUTONOMY ACT NO. 03, THE BANGSAMORO APPROPRIATIONS ACT OF FISCAL YEAR 2020." This long title identifies: (a) the principal action (extending the availability of appropriations), (b) the exception (personnel services), (c) the specific section being amended (Section 47), and (d) the act being amended (BAA No. 03).

When a bill amends multiple acts, semicolons should be used to separate references to different acts within the long title.

**Appropriations Law.** An appropriations bill uses the formulation "AN ACT APPROPRIATING..." followed by a description of the funds and their purposes. As the annual general appropriations act, this type of long title typically describes the total amount appropriated, the fiscal year covered, and the general purposes of the appropriation.

### The Phrase "AND FOR OTHER PURPOSES"

The trailing phrase "AND FOR OTHER PURPOSES" (or "AND FOR OTHER RELATED PURPOSES") is commonly appended to long titles, but it should not be used indiscriminately. This phrase is appropriate only when a bill has more than two significant purposes and listing all of them in the long title would make it unwieldy. It functions as a catchall that covers ancillary provisions — such as administrative mechanisms, penalties, or transitory measures — that support the bill's principal purpose but do not warrant individual mention in the long title.

For a single-purpose bill, this phrase is unnecessary and should be omitted. BAA No. 1 (the Flag Act) and BAA No. 7 (the Hymn Act), which each accomplish a single, clear objective, do not use the phrase. By contrast, BAA No. 4 and BAA No. 5, which create institutions, define their powers, appropriate funds, and establish administrative mechanisms, properly include the phrase because they accomplish multiple distinct purposes.

The drafter should never use "AND FOR OTHER PURPOSES" as a license to insert provisions that are unrelated to the bill's stated subject. The phrase covers ancillary matters, not unrelated ones. A provision that cannot be connected to the bill's primary subject does not belong in the bill, regardless of what the long title says.

### Examples from Enacted BAAs

The following long titles from enacted BAAs illustrate the range of formulations used in Bangsamoro legislation:

**BAA No. 1:** "AN ACT ADOPTING THE OFFICIAL FLAG OF THE BANGSAMORO AUTONOMOUS REGION IN MUSLIM MINDANAO."
— Single purpose, no trailing phrase. Clear and complete.

**BAA No. 7:** "AN ACT ADOPTING THE OFFICIAL HYMN OF THE BANGSAMORO AUTONOMOUS REGION IN MUSLIM MINDANAO."
— Same structure as BAA No. 1. Single purpose, cleanly stated.

**BAA No. 13:** "AN ACT PROVIDING FOR THE BANGSAMORO ADMINISTRATIVE CODE AND FOR OTHER RELATED PURPOSES."
— A comprehensive code with multiple subject areas. The trailing phrase is appropriate because the Administrative Code covers organizational structure, governance principles, ministerial powers, and numerous other subjects.

**BAA No. 14:** "AN ACT EXTENDING THE AVAILABILITY OF THE 2020 BANGSAMORO APPROPRIATIONS TO DECEMBER 31, 2021, EXCEPT PERSONNEL SERVICES, AMENDING FOR THE PURPOSE SECTION 47 OF THE GENERAL PROVISIONS OF BANGSAMORO AUTONOMY ACT NO. 03, THE BANGSAMORO APPROPRIATIONS ACT OF FISCAL YEAR 2020."
— An amendatory act. Identifies the specific section and act being amended. No trailing phrase because the bill has a single, specific purpose.

**BAA No. 20:** "AN ACT MANDATING THE DUAL USE OF HIJRI AND GREGORIAN CALENDARS IN ALL MINISTRIES, AGENCIES, LOCAL GOVERNMENT UNITS, OFFICES AND INSTITUTIONS IN THE BANGSAMORO AUTONOMOUS REGION IN MUSLIM MINDANAO (BARMM), APPROPRIATING FUNDS THEREFOR AND OTHER PURPOSES."
— Multiple purposes (mandating calendar use and appropriating funds). The trailing phrase covers additional provisions on implementation, powers, and penalties.

## 8.3 The Enacting Clause

The enacting clause is the formal statement that asserts the Parliament's authority to legislate. It appears immediately after the long title and immediately before the first section of the bill's main body. Its exact formulation is:

> *Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament in session assembled:*

This clause is mandatory. No bill is properly constituted without it. The enacting clause performs a constitutional function: it declares that the provisions that follow are enacted by the body vested with legislative power under the Bangsamoro Organic Law. Without this declaration, the text that follows would be merely a document, not a legislative act.

The formulation has appeared with minor variations in enacted BAAs. BAA No. 1 and BAA No. 5 use "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament in session assembled:" while BAA No. 13 and BAA No. 17 use "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority in Parliament assembled:" The variation is in the placement of "Parliament" — either as part of the body's name ("Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament") or as a description of the setting ("in Parliament assembled"). Both formulations are found in enacted law and both are acceptable, but the drafter should adopt whichever formulation is current in parliamentary practice at the time of drafting. Consistency across bills filed in the same session is desirable.

The enacting clause is followed by a colon, and the first section of the bill begins on the next line. Nothing — no preamble, no recital, no additional narrative text — should intervene between the enacting clause and the first section.
