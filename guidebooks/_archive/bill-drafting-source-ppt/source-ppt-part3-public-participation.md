## THE NATURE OF LEGISLATION

- The law, provides the framework within which a society functions.
- The rights and obligations of individuals and organizations in a society are determined by law.
- Laws are tools for ordering economic and social activities in a country.
- They are designed to solve or prevent problems, or to promote welfare.

---

## Policymaking

- Policy making is the process whereby the objectives of governmental action and laws are established.
- It involves selecting and prioritizing the issues to be addressed and deciding how to handle them. Thus, the first step in the legislative drafting process is identifying the objectives to be achieved and the policies to be implemented.

---

## Where Do Laws Originate?

- As legislation is the result of policy being translated into legislative form, before any legislation can be drafted, the underlying policy must be determined.

---

## Policymaking Process

- **identify** the problem or issue;
- **decide** the goals and objectives of the policy;
- **analyze** the options available to the government;
- **consult** interested persons and bodies ('stakeholders') both within and outside government;
- **recommend** which option will most effectively achieve the goals and objectives; and
- **set out** how the policy is to be implemented, monitored and evaluated.

---

## What Must be Considered in Policymaking

- the **effect** of the proposal on existing laws and financial legislation;
- who is to be **affected** or bound;
- the **appropriateness** of the proposed legislation
- the need for **consultations** both within and outside government.

---

## Drafting Instructions

- The Drafting Instructions is the document (or set of documents) that sets out in the proposed policy framework.

---

## Content of Draft Instruction

- historical background information
- legal means of achieving government policies.
- the impact and implications of the proposal on the existing circumstances and law.

---

## CHECKLIST FOR DRAFTING INSTRUCTIONS

The table below is a brief overview of the best structure for a set of instructions. At the core of this proposed structure is the following analysis--

- This is something we want to happen (the desired outcome).
- This is why it cannot happen without legislation (the mischief).
- This is how we think the law needs to be changed in order for it to happen (the remedy).

For further details please refer to the full version of this document.

| **Section** | **Purpose and main content** | **Matters to be covered** |
|---|---|---|
| *Introduction* | To outline the factual and political context in which legislation is being proposed; the general purpose of the changes that are being proposed; and the principal reasons for legislating. | - the political and factual context; - a paragraph introducing the devolution issues that are likely to arise; - a warning about any significant EU or ECHR issues the Bill is likely to give rise to; - any conventions that have been adopted in the instructions (eg acronyms or abbreviations); - any information about whether the instructions are contingent (eg on collective agreement), and about any risk that they will change; and - any other information about the current state of the project. |
| *Description of the existing law* | To describe the relevant existing law, its application in practice. | - a description of the legislative provisions, relevant common law rules and case law, and also (where appropriate) examples of how all that operates in practice; - a brief history of statutory provisions that are being described; - an explanation of why a previous round of amendments occurred; - any recent developments affecting the law being described and anything in the pipeline (eg relevant SIs in preparation); and - what is known about any Law Officers' advice that relates to the background law or to the matters to be dealt with in the Bill, and anything about the contents of any other legal advice given by or within Government or pending litigation that could be relevant. |
| *Description of the mischief* | To describe the respects in which, and extent to which, the existing law prevents the implementation of the department's policy. | - how the existing law prevents the implementation of the proposed policy; - different circumstances will require different contents, discuss the contents with Counsel. |
| *Description of the proposed remedy* *(It may be appropriate to interweave this with the above section)* | To describe the legal changes to which the Bill is to give effect in order to provide a remedy for the mischief and to ensure that the correct analysis underlies the operative part of the instructions. | - an outline of all the specific things that need to be achieved by way of addition to or other amendment of the law; - a description of the substance of any specific changes to the effect of the existing law that need to be made to bring that about and why those changes will have that effect. |
| *Incidental and supplemental matters* | To describe the incidental and supplemental provisions needed to support the remedy or need to be considered during the drafting process. | The following are the matters that need to be considered in this way: - service of documents under the Bill, including a consideration of electronic service; - any impact of the Electronic Commerce Directive (2000/13/EC) on the proposals; - any impact of the Services Directive (2006/13/EC) or the Technical Services Directive (1998/34/EC) on the proposals; - other EU issues including ECHR compatibility; - territorial extent and application; - devolution issues; - application of the Statutory Instruments Act, Parliamentary etc. control and incidental, supplemental, transitional and consequential powers for SIs under the Bill; - the implications of the proposals for Government finances and how are they going to be met; - vicarious liability for criminal offences and also the liability of company directors etc. for offences by companies etc.; - application of criminal offences etc. to armed services personnel; - application to the Crown; - any impact of the proposals on matters covered by Parliamentary privilege; - disclosure and confidentiality of information obtained under the Bill and information sharing; - extent to which powers conferred under the Bill may be delegated; - consequential amendments and repeals in other legislation; - commencement provisions; - transitional and transitory provisions and savings. |
| *Parliamentary and other handling matters* | To discuss any Parliamentary or other handling matters relevant to the drafting, particularly in relation to Parliamentary procedure. | The principal matters to be mentioned are: - the timetable and arrangements for pre-legislative scrutiny of a draft Bill; - any timetable or arrangements for PB Committee and introduction; - the proposals for or progress of any consultation on aspects of the Bill; - matters on which further provisions may be needed and the plans for instructing on them, including areas where the need for concessions in the House can be predicted. Departments may also wish to raise any questions they may have about the likely scope of the Bill; - financial matters; - other Parliament Acts matters; - issues that give rise to concern about the potential hybridity either of provisions of the Bill or of affirmative instruments made under a power proposed for the Bill; - concerns have about proposals affecting the prerogatives or interests of the Crown and the implications for any requirement for Queen's or Prince's consent; - any comments on short and long Bill titles. |
| *Housekeeping matters* | To outline key information to support effective project management. | - the contact details and availability of the Bill team members; - arrangements for Bill training events for the team; and - arrangements for correspondence between OPC and the department and consultation with the devolved administrations. |

**Consultation**: Consulting with stakeholders both within and outside government.

**Publication**: The task of having the statute formally published in the Gazette and news paper of regional circulation.

## Rights, Duties, Privileges, Authority, Create, Declaration

**Rights, Duties, & Privileges**. Duty requires someone to do something or to refrain from doing it. Rights entitlement. Privilege conditional entitlement.

**Authority** is the power to act with or without the duty to act. Discretionary or the ministerial.

**Create** political or administrative entity and provide functions for public purpose.

A **declaration** is a statement declaring that something is true.

## Thornton's Structure on Bill Drafting

1. understanding the proposal
2. analyzing the proposal
3. designing the law
4. composing and developing the draft
5. scrutiny and testing of the draft.

---

## 1. Understanding the proposal

- compilation and reception of the drafting instruction.
- clear picture of the purposes of the proposed legislation, and the defect it intends to correct.
- multitude of often clashing rules and conventions.
- identify the most relevant set of circumstances applicable to the problem
- communication between policy makers and the drafter leads to good understanding of the proposal.

---

## 2. Analyzing the proposal

- detail examination of detail: new law or amending or by administrative action or not
- how it affects the Constitution, laws, personal rights, private property rights, and treaties
- consider past and present and how it will affect the society as legal norms
- examine the gaps and fill them.
- raise issues whenever there is to the proponent
- advice on possible legislative solutions to any inconsistencies that emanate from such analysis.

---

## 3. Designing the Law

- hierarchical structure for the proposal an outline structure
- the design is already provided by the Rules of Parliament
- Importance of design: organized, logical form, coherent, facilitates effective communication of its content, ensure its legality, distinct and different matters is not joined,

---

## 4. Composing And Developing The Draft

1. composition
2. drafting techniques
3. challenges

---

## 4.1. Composition

- From draft the letters of the bill in substance and form with precision, accurately reflect to the drafting instructions, and imbued with clarity.
- He must constantly and continuously confer with the instructing officers when composing sentences, which are required to be clear, precise and unambiguous."
- The Composition of a draft should follow an elaborate and logical structure, with a clear purpose, a correct function, a connection between conditions or consequences and the relevant behavioral directive, good knowledge and fit of the statute into its systems.

---

## Five Steps of Composition

1. Preparation of the draft,
2. Revision of the draft,
3. Cross-checking,
4. Cross-checking with others, and
5. Polishing the results.

---

## 4.2. Drafting Techniques

- finding the right words
- putting words in the right order
- concrete words
- plain language principles
- gender-neutral language,
- culture sensitivity

---

## 4.3. Challenges

- responsibility to interpret and construe the meaning of words in legislative texts
- precision and clarity free from ambiguity
- transforming government policies with legal effects
- political subserviency to the proponent

---

## 5. Scrutiny and Testing of the Draft

- Did the draft text reflect the policy rules forming the basis of the legislative text?
- Did it fully comply with the drafting instructions?
- The drafting process takes the form of much revisionary work by the policy makers and drafters
- The draft is properly, seriously and systematically checked by the drafter who cross-checks cross-references, numbering, lettering, defined terms, language, the form and substance of the draft.
- Policy makers should study the draft and offer comments.
- The drafter amends draft from time to time to meet certain changes in instructions and it is only settled after repeated revisions when he is satisfied with it in substance, form, language and content.

## BILL DRAFTING MANUAL

*(Section title slide)*

---

## Bill Drafting Manual

- establish conventions concerning language, style, and format to guide the bill-drafting process.
- as a guide to the creation of an accurate, clear, and uniform legislative product
- help ensure that new statutes are consistent with the Constitution and BOL
- useful for ensuring consistency

---

## Against Manual

- Requiring consistency with outdated modes of expression dooms legislation to irrelevancy and anachronism.
- useful for the junior drafter, or for the jurisdiction that is new to the practice of drafting.
- prevent the exercise of judgement and balance in drafting
- hinder innovativeness in an office as the draftsperson is bound to the Manual and established guidelines

---

## MANUAL: GUIDE OR RULES

- a good manual was one that tended to recommend rather than to prescribe.
- drafting guidelines are helpful but drafting rules are unhelpful.
- Manuals work best when they are a source of ideas and guidance, but they restrict quality and retard development when they are conceived of as binding authority

---

## BINDING EFFECT OF MANUAL

- cannot be a legal reference
- it is an internal document
- designed to assist drafters. It has no democratic legitimacy as a source of law.

---

## ART vs. SCIENCE

| **AS ART** | **AS SCIENCE** |
|---|---|
| drafting as subjective, creative, and flexible | objective, fixed, and certain |
| rejection of manuals | accepts manual |

| - Conceiving a legislative scheme. | - Governmental and political knowledge (legislative process etc.). |
| - Working in a team. | - Technical knowledge (drafting conventions etc.). |
| - Managing scarce resource | |

## WHAT IS PUBLIC PARTICIPATION?

- means the **chance** of all those concerned **to present or stand up** for their interests or concerns in the **development** of plans, programs, policies, or legal instruments.

- is the deliberative process by which interested or affected citizens, civic society organizations, and government actors are involved in policy-making before a political decision is taken.

- as the redistribution of power that enabled the have-not citizens, presently excluded from the political and economic processes, to be deliberately included.

---

## "PUBLIC" MEANING

- The public is an open and unlimited circle of persons comprising all members and organizational forms of a society. The term public encompasses individuals just as much as groups of persons.

---

## MECHANISM OF PARTICIPATION

- **VOTE**: can be limiting as participation is only interpreted as elections once in every three to five years.

- **VOICE**: continue dialogue with the government where citizens have the power to influence 'the making, implementation, monitoring, and evaluation of government's decision making

---

## PRINCIPLES OF PUBLIC PARTICIPATION

- as partners and with joint responsibility for the community.
- a means of communication.
- an integral part of administration.

---

## BENEFITS OF PUBLIC PARTICIPATION

- Improved service delivery by public officers.
- Increased credibility between public officers and community on important issues and services.
- Creates citizens who feel they belong and have trust in their community and local government.
- Provides a platform for greater diversity of citizens to contribute to public debate and decision making.
- Provides an opportunity for focused and prioritized community concerns to be addressed by public officials.
- Creates citizens who are more aware of their community needs and how government responds to those needs.

---

## OBJECTIVES OF PUBLIC PARTICIPATION

- **On Information** — to promote the exchange of information and experiences; a process for educating decision makers the government on their issues

- **On Empowerment** — increases the sense of citizenship; improves the political position of marginalized or vulnerable groups; recognition of people's responsibility to take action.

- **On Decision-making** — to enhance the quality and transparency of decisions; to enhance the acceptance government decision-making; to strengthen people's trust in politics and public administration; to provide broader bases of decision-making for political and administrative decision-makers.

- **On Transparency and Accountability** — a core element of the transparent and citizen-oriented administrative practice; Promote accountability; results in better implementation of development programs.

---

## SPECTRUM OF PUBLIC PARTICIPATION

| **INFORM** | **CONSULT** | **INVOLVE** | **COLLABORATE** | **EMPOWER** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| To provide the public with balanced and objective information to assist them in understanding the problem, alternatives, opportunities and/or solutions | To obtain public feedback on analysis, alternatives and/or decisions | To work directly with the public throughout the process to ensure that public concerns and aspirations are consistently understood and considered | To partner with the public in each aspect of the decision including the development of alternatives and the identification of the preferred solution | To place final decision making in the hands of the public. |

## CHALLENGES TO PUBLIC PARTICIPATION

**APOLITICAL SOCIETY** — Most members of society are reluctant to be political and acts of public engagement are often seen as political; it is often difficult to relate engagement to positive change in everyday life.

**LACK OF CAPACITY TO ENGAGE** — There is usually only limited knowledge within society of policy-making processes; limited knowledge and skills to communicate constructively with decision makers.

**LIMITED COMMITMENT** — The commitment and continuity to act to effect intended changes is usually limited; Perception that public participation is not meaningful to them; citizens are often reluctant to devote time and effort to understand the intricacies of public issues, as indicated by the chronically low attendance of public hearings.

**LOW PUBLIC TRUST** — A low level of trust in public institution and low perception regarding government officials; low public trust in government for not fully taking into consideration community ideas on priority areas for development; not fulfilling promises they have made in community meeting; skepticism towards participation as a means to deliver change.

**EXCLUSION** — Many consultative processes are seen to be ways of reinforcing the view of the dominant groups and exclusion of most marginalized and vulnerable groups, which are often left out of political processes.

## LAWMAKING PUBLIC CONSULTATIONS

- Public consultation is an important tool for ensuring that new legislation is effective in achieving its goals, well written and fully understood by all interested parties, and, crucially, fully compliant with human rights obligations.
- Initial consultations should happen when different policy options are still being considered, that is, before a decision is made on whether to go ahead with developing a new law.
- Public consultations provide policy makers with vital information that may not be otherwise available to them.
- Meaningful public consultations improve the implementation of adopted legislation.

---

## WITHOUT PUBLIC CONSULTATION IN LAWMAKING, IT MAY PRODUCE

- an ineffective, incomplete, contradictory or/and in breach of human rights.
- poor legislation that need to be amended (often many times) or even replaced.
- they can also lead to potentially costly legal challenges initiated by victims and interest groups.
- It may not be responsive
- It may not have impact to the intended subjects

## PUBLIC PARTICIPATION UNDER THE BTA RULES

- **Petition** (formal requests submitted by individuals, organizations, local government units and other entities to redress certain grievances, address specific stand and other concerns)
- **Public Consultation** (the purpose is obtaining more information and facts and a wider view on any prospective legislative measures from professional and expert opinions, organizations, and the public.)
- **Public Hearing** (same definition)
- **Position Paper** (**not in the rules**)

---

## DESIGNING PUBLIC PARTICIPATION

- effective participation is designed and implemented not only by the public institution; it is continuously developed within the framework of permanent interaction between public administration and community through active information, consultation, and participation.
- This interaction will not necessarily be achieved through regulations, but through a proactive involvement and the will of the both parties — community and public institution.
- Moreover, this kind of relationships and interactions cannot be developed without citizens' trust.
