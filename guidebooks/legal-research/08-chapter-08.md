# Chapter 8 — National Laws and Implementing Frameworks

Chapters 4 and 5 covered the Constitution and BOL. Chapters 6 and 7 covered BAA 13 and other Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts. This chapter covers two tiers that originate *outside* the Bangsamoro Parliament: **national laws** enacted by the Philippine Congress (Tier 5) and **executive issuances** from both the Chief Minister and national officials (Tier 6). Jurisprudence (Tier 7) is treated separately in Chapter 10.

These tiers are where most researchers make their costliest mistakes. National laws get ignored because "BARMM is autonomous." Executive issuances get treated as optional guidance. Each error can invalidate an entire research memorandum.

The challenge at Tiers 5-6 is not finding the sources — they are generally more accessible than BAAs. The challenge is determining *whether and how* they apply within the Bangsamoro. A national law that applies everywhere else in the Philippines may not apply in BARMM. This chapter teaches you to navigate these determinations systematically.

---

## 8.1 Purpose

After completing this chapter, you will be able to:

1. **Determine whether a national law applies in BARMM** using the three-mode applicability test (direct, suppletory, express adoption)
2. **Search for national laws** across four online databases and evaluate which version is authoritative
3. **Distinguish four types of executive issuances** and identify their legal limits

The skills in this chapter build directly on the national law relationship framework from Chapter 3. If you have not completed the national law relationship test for your research question (Section 3.3), go back and do it now. The result — whether the power requires national law consistency, requires no national law consistency, or is a residual national power (or a legislative gap exists) — determines how you approach every source at Tiers 5-6.

## 8.2 Tier 5: National Laws

### 8.2.1 Three Ways National Laws Apply in BARMM

Not every Republic Act applies in BARMM. Not every Republic Act is excluded. The Bangsamoro is autonomous, not independent. The BOL creates a system where national laws interact with Bangsamoro legislation through three distinct mechanisms. Your research must identify which mechanism applies — because the legal effect differs for each one.

**Mode 1: Direct Application**

A national law applies **directly** when the subject falls outside the BOL's 55 enumerated powers — that is, when the subject is a **residual national power** under Article V, Section 1.[^1] In these areas, the Bangsamoro Parliament has no legislative competence. National law governs fully and exclusively.

**Examples of direct application:**
- **National defense and security** — The Armed Forces of the Philippines and the Philippine National Police operate under national law within BARMM. The Bangsamoro Government does not have military or police powers (though Art. XI, Sec. 4 provides for police coordination).[^2]
- **Foreign affairs** — Treaty-making, diplomatic relations, and consular operations are exclusively national.
- **Immigration and citizenship** — The Bureau of Immigration enforces national immigration law throughout BARMM. Parliament cannot create a separate immigration regime.
- **Currency and monetary policy** — The Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas operates under national law. BARMM cannot issue currency or set monetary policy.
- **Customs and tariff** — Customs operations at BARMM ports follow national customs law, though Art. XII, Sec. 29 provides for the sharing of customs revenues.

**How to identify direct application:** If your national law relationship test (Chapter 3, Section 3.3) returned **"Residual National Power"** at Step 1 — the subject is not within the 55 enumerated powers — then any national law on that subject applies directly. Your research at Tier 5 is straightforward: find the applicable national law and apply it.

**Mode 2: Suppletory Application**

A national law applies **suppletorily** when the subject is within the 55 enumerated powers (Art. V, Sec. 2) but the Parliament has not yet enacted a BAA on that subject. The national law fills the gap until the Parliament exercises its power.[^3]

The concept comes from legal principle: when a specialized law is silent on a matter, the general law applies to fill the silence. In the BARMM context, this means national laws serve as the default framework for any enumerated power that Parliament has not yet legislated on.

**Examples of suppletory application:**
- **Cooperatives** — Art. V, Sec. 2(k) lists "Cooperatives and social entrepreneurship" as an enumerated power where the BOL is silent on national law consistency. No BAA specifically codifying cooperative law has been enacted as of March 2026. RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) therefore applies suppletorily within BARMM.[^4]
- **Labor** — Art. V, Sec. 2(jj) lists "Labor, employment, and occupation" as an enumerated power. BAA 82 (Bangsamoro Labor and Employment Code), enacted January 2026, is the regional labor framework. The national Labor Code (PD 442, as amended) applies suppletorily where BAA 82 is silent.
- **Tourism** — Art. V, Sec. 2(aaa) lists "Tourism development" as an enumerated power. BAA 13 creates the Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT), but no standalone tourism code exists. RA 9593 (Tourism Act of 2009) applies suppletorily.

**How to identify suppletory application:** If your national law relationship test returned **"Legislative Gap"** at Step 3 — the subject is an enumerated power (Art. V, Sec. 2) where no national law consistency is required, but no BAA has been enacted — the applicable national law fills the gap. Your research must cover both Tier 4 (confirming the gap) and Tier 5 (identifying the suppletory national law).

**The suppletory application finding is itself a research output.** When you discover a legislative gap filled by suppletory national law, document it explicitly in your Research Memorandum. This is valuable institutional knowledge. It tells decision-makers: *Parliament has the power to legislate on this subject but has not done so. Until it does, this national law governs.*

**Mode 3: Express Adoption**

A national law applies through **express adoption** when the Parliament enacts a BAA that explicitly incorporates or aligns with a national framework. The BAA is the controlling authority, but it draws its substantive provisions from the national law — either by copying the framework, referencing it, or declaring consistency with it.

**Examples of express adoption:**
- **RA 12009 (New Government Procurement Act)** — No Bangsamoro Procurement Act has been enacted as of March 2026. RA 12009 applies directly to all BARMM procurement. This is a **legislative gap** — the BOL grants budgeting authority (Art. V, Sec. 2(f)) and Art. VII, Sec. 28 requires budget consistency with national laws, but Parliament has not yet exercised its power to enact a regional procurement framework. COA audits all BARMM procurement against RA 12009 standards.[^5]
- **BAA 84 and national budget law** — BAA 84 (Budget System Act) establishes the Bangsamoro budget process consistent with the BOL's requirement that the budget conform to "existing laws, rules and regulations of the National Government" (Art. VII, Sec. 28).[^6]
- **Civil service legislation** — When the Parliament legislates on civil service matters, Art. VII, Sec. 39 requires consistency with national civil service laws. Any Bangsamoro civil service legislation therefore constitutes express adoption of the national framework to the extent required by the BOL.[^7]

**How to identify express adoption:** Check the BAA's text. Does it reference a specific national law? Does it use language like "consistent with," "in accordance with," "pursuant to," or "shall apply suppletorily"? If so, your research must cover both the BAA (Tier 4) and the adopted national law (Tier 5), and your Comparative Matrix must map the relationship between them.

### The Applicability Decision Flowchart

For any national law you encounter during research, apply this test:

**Step 1: Is the subject a residual national power?**
- **YES** → National law applies **directly**. No BAA can override it. Stop here.
- **NO** → The subject is within the 55 enumerated powers. Proceed to Step 2.

**Step 2: Does the BOL require consistency with national law on this subject?**
- **YES** → National law consistency is required. National law sets the floor or ceiling. The BAA must be consistent with it. Both apply. Stop here.
- **NO** → Proceed to Step 3.

**Step 3: Has Parliament enacted a BAA on this subject?**
- **YES** → Check whether the BAA expressly adopts or references the national law.
  - **BAA expressly adopts** → Both apply; BAA is the controlling authority but national law fills gaps (Mode 3).
  - **BAA does not reference the national law** → The **BAA governs** within BARMM for this subject. The national law does not apply.
- **NO** → Legislative gap. National law applies **suppletorily** until Parliament acts (Mode 2).

### 8.2.2 Key Universal National Laws

Some national laws affect nearly every MOA regardless of the national law relationship determination. These are **laws of general application** — they apply to all government entities, including BARMM, because they address cross-cutting obligations (ethics, anti-corruption, gender, data protection) rather than sector-specific subjects covered by the 55 enumerated powers.

Include these in your research whenever your question involves government operations, personnel conduct, procurement, or public service delivery:

| National Law | Short Title | Why It Matters for BARMM |
|-------------|------------|-------------------------|
| **RA 6713** | Code of Conduct and Ethical Standards for Public Officials and Employees | Applies to all public officials including BARMM appointees. Defines norms of conduct, disclosure requirements, and prohibited acts. |
| **RA 3019** | Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices Act | Applies to all public officers. Defines corrupt practices and penalties. COA and Ombudsman enforce this within BARMM. |
| **RA 12009** | New Government Procurement Act | Applies directly to BARMM (no regional procurement BAA enacted). COA audits all BARMM procurement against RA 12009 standards. |
| **RA 9710** | Magna Carta of Women | Requires at least 5% of total budget for gender-responsive programs. See also BOL Art. XIII, Sec. 5 (gender equality).[^8] |
| **RA 10173** | Data Privacy Act of 2012 | Applies to all government processing of personal data, including BARMM civil registration, health records, and social welfare programs. |
| **RA 11032** | Ease of Doing Business and Efficient Government Service Delivery Act of 2018 | Applies to all government agencies. Prescribes processing times and anti-red tape measures that BARMM agencies must follow. |
| **RA 8371** | Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act (IPRA) | BOL Art. IX, Sec. 3: IP rights "shall not be diminished." NCIP retains jurisdiction over ancestral domain claims within BARMM.[^9] |
| **RA 7160** | Local Government Code of 1991 | BOL Art. VI, Sec. 10: privileges enjoyed by LGUs "shall not be diminished." LGU powers, elections, and fiscal transfers operate under this framework.[^10] |

**A practical warning:** Do not assume that a national law applies simply because it is a "good idea" or "should" apply. Confirm the legal basis. A national law on agriculture, for example, does not automatically apply in BARMM because agriculture is under Art. V, Sec. 2(c) — an enumerated power where the BOL is silent on national law consistency. If the Parliament has enacted a BAA on the subject, that BAA controls. The eight laws listed above are universal because they address cross-cutting government obligations, not because of any blanket rule that national laws always prevail.

### 8.2.3 How to Search for National Laws

Four primary databases contain Philippine national legislation. Each has strengths and limitations.

**1. Official Gazette (officialgazette.gov.ph)**

- **Content:** Published Republic Acts, Presidential issuances, proclamations
- **Strength:** Official publication. Laws published here have legal force under the Tañada v. Tuvera doctrine — a law takes effect only after publication.[^11]
- **Limitation:** Not all laws are immediately uploaded. Search function is basic. Older laws may be missing.
- **Best for:** Confirming official publication and effectivity dates

**2. LawPhil Project (lawphil.net)**

- **Content:** Republic Acts, Presidential Decrees, Supreme Court decisions
- **Strength:** Comprehensive collection of Philippine laws and jurisprudence. Free access. Well-organized by law number.
- **Limitation:** Unofficial source. No guarantee that amendments are reflected in the posted text. Cross-reference against the Official Gazette for critical citations.
- **Best for:** Initial searches and reading the full text of laws

**3. Chan Robles Virtual Law Library (chanrobles.com)**

- **Content:** Statutes, jurisprudence, executive issuances, treaties
- **Strength:** Extensive database. Often includes annotations and related cases.
- **Limitation:** Unofficial. Some content is behind registration. Formatting can be inconsistent.
- **Best for:** Finding related cases and annotations for a specific law

**4. Supreme Court E-Library (elibrary.judiciary.gov.ph)**

- **Content:** Supreme Court decisions, court rules, some statutes
- **Strength:** Official source for jurisprudence. Searchable by G.R. number, case title, date, and ponente.
- **Limitation:** Primarily focused on jurisprudence rather than legislation. Search can be slow.
- **Best for:** Finding the official text of Supreme Court decisions (Tier 7) and court-cited versions of statutes

**5. Congress website (congress.gov.ph)**

- **Content:** Enrolled bills, committee reports, session proceedings
- **Strength:** Official source for legislative history. Shows pending bills and their status.
- **Limitation:** Search is limited. Not all enrolled bills are immediately posted. Historical records may be incomplete.
- **Best for:** Tracking pending national legislation that may affect BARMM; understanding legislative intent through committee reports

**Search strategy for national laws:**

1. **Start with the RA number** if you know it. Search by number across LawPhil or Chan Robles for the full text.
2. **If you do not know the RA number,** search by subject on LawPhil or Chan Robles. Use the same search terms from your Issue Analysis (Chapter 2, Step 1).
3. **Check for amendments.** Many RAs have been amended multiple times. RA 7160 (Local Government Code), for example, has been amended by RA 11292, RA 11463, and others. LawPhil sometimes indicates amendments; the Official Gazette does not always. Search for "amending RA [number]" to find subsequent amendments.
4. **Verify against the Official Gazette** for effectivity dates and official publication status.
5. **Check the implementing rules.** Many national laws have IRRs that provide the operational detail. Search for "[Law name] IRR" or "[Law name] implementing rules and regulations."

**The verification imperative.** National law databases are unofficial sources maintained by private organizations (except the Official Gazette and the SC E-Library). Text may contain errors, omissions, or outdated versions. When a national law provision is critical to your research answer, verify the text against the Official Gazette version or a certified copy from the National Printing Office. Chapter 13 covers verification procedures in detail.

## 8.3 Tier 6: Executive Issuances

Executive issuances are the operational layer of the legal system. They translate legislation into action. A BAA or national law creates the authority; an executive issuance tells MOAs *how to exercise that authority* — what forms to use, what timelines to follow, what standards to apply.

For the legal researcher, Tier 6 matters because it contains the rules that people actually follow day-to-day. A Ministry officer building a program will refer to the IRR and the implementing MCs long before opening the BOL. Your research is incomplete if you stop at the BAA without checking whether an IRR, EO, or MC has been issued under it.

### 8.3.1 Types: EOs, IRRs, MCs, AOs

Four types of executive issuances operate within the BARMM legal framework:

**Executive Orders (EOs)**

Executive Orders are directives issued by the Chief Minister on regional matters, or by the President on national matters affecting BARMM.

- **Chief Minister EOs** govern the internal operations of the Bangsamoro Government — reorganizing offices, creating task forces, directing ministries to implement specific policies. Example: OCM Executive Order No. 2, s. 2026, which established the implementation framework for the Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028).[^12]
- **Presidential EOs** affecting BARMM typically concern intergovernmental relations, administrative boundary adjustments, or national policies that extend to BARMM. Example: Executive Order No. 91, s. 2025, which realigned the Province of Sulu under Region IX following the Supreme Court's ruling in *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea*.[^13]

**How EOs rank in the hierarchy:** An EO cannot contradict the BAA, BOL provision, or national law it implements. It is subordinate to legislation. But within the space the legislation allows, an EO can direct how government agencies execute policy. Treat EOs as *operational authority* — they tell you how things work in practice, not whether they are permitted in law.

**Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRRs)**

IRRs provide the detailed implementation guidelines for a specific BAA or national law. When Parliament enacts a BAA, it often includes a provision directing the implementing ministry to promulgate an IRR within a specified period (typically 90 days).

**Key rules for IRRs:**
- An IRR **cannot expand the scope** of the law it implements. If a BAA authorizes a ministry to regulate a specific activity, the IRR cannot extend that regulation to activities not covered by the BAA. This is the *ultra vires* doctrine — an IRR that goes beyond the law's grant of authority is void to that extent.[^14]
- An IRR **cannot contradict** the BAA. If the IRR imposes a requirement that conflicts with the BAA's text, the BAA prevails.
- An IRR **interprets ambiguous provisions** in the BAA. When the BAA uses general language (e.g., "appropriate safeguards"), the IRR defines what that means in practice (e.g., specific documentation requirements, timelines, penalties).

**For your research:** When you find a relevant BAA at Tier 4, always ask: *Has an IRR been promulgated?* If yes, the IRR may contain the operational detail that answers your specific question. If no IRR exists, that itself is a finding — it means the BAA's implementation provisions may be incomplete.

**Memorandum Circulars (MCs)**

Memorandum Circulars are policy directives issued by the Office of the Chief Minister (OCM), ministries, or agencies. They communicate specific guidance on how to comply with existing laws and regulations.

- **OCM MCs** apply government-wide — all ministries and agencies must comply.
- **Ministry MCs** apply within the issuing ministry's mandate. An MC from the Ministry of Finance, Budget, and Management (MFBM) applies to budget execution across government; an MC from the Ministry of Health (MOH) applies to health programs.

MCs occupy the lowest rung of binding authority. They can direct processes, set deadlines, and clarify requirements, but they cannot create new obligations not supported by a BAA or EO.

**Administrative Orders (AOs)**

Administrative Orders are internal directives within specific ministries. They govern internal procedures — personnel assignments, office workflows, reporting requirements — and do not typically create external legal obligations.

**For your research:** AOs are relevant when your question concerns the *internal operations* of a specific ministry. If you are researching how a ministry processes applications, for example, the AO governing that process is primary operational authority. AOs are rarely relevant to questions about legal authority or institutional mandates.

### 8.3.2 Validity and Publication Requirements

An executive issuance is not legally effective until it has been properly published. This is a constitutional requirement flowing from the Tañada v. Tuvera principle: *Laws, to be valid and enforceable, must be published in the Official Gazette or in a newspaper of general circulation.*[^15]

**The BARMM publication rule:** For Bangsamoro legislation, the standard effectivity clause provides that a BAA "shall take effect after fifteen (15) days from its publication in the Bangsamoro Gazette or in one (1) newspaper of regional circulation."[^16] The same principle applies to executive issuances with legal effect — EOs, IRRs, and MCs that impose obligations on the public or on government agencies must be published.

**The Bangsamoro Gazette** is the official publication vehicle of the Bangsamoro Government. Publication in the Gazette satisfies the constitutional requirement.

**What to check in your research:**
- **Was the EO, IRR, or MC published?** If not, it may not be legally effective.
- **When was it published?** The issuance takes effect 15 days after publication (unless a different date is specified).
- **Where was it published?** The Bangsamoro Gazette or a newspaper of regional circulation.

**A practical challenge:** Not all executive issuances are easily accessible. Unlike BAAs, which are compiled and indexed, EOs and MCs from individual ministries may not be centrally catalogued. When your research requires a specific executive issuance:

1. **Check the issuing ministry's records** — contact the ministry directly if needed.
2. **Check the OCM for Chief Minister EOs** and government-wide MCs.
3. **Check the BTA Parliamentary Service** — they may have copies if the issuance was submitted to Parliament.
4. **Search online** — some ministries publish issuances on their websites or social media pages.

The difficulty of finding executive issuances is itself a finding. If a critical IRR has not been promulgated or published, that constitutes a gap in the implementation framework. Document it in your Research Memorandum.

### 8.3.3 Testing an Executive Issuance's Validity

When you find an executive issuance relevant to your research question, apply this three-part validity test:

**Test 1: Authority.** Does the issuing official or office have the authority to issue this type of directive?
- A Chief Minister EO on a regional matter: Valid authority.
- A ministry MC on a matter within the ministry's mandate: Valid authority.
- A ministry MC on a matter outside its mandate: Lacks authority.

**Test 2: Consistency.** Does the issuance contradict the law it implements?
- If an IRR imposes a requirement not found in the BAA: Potentially ultra vires.
- If an MC contradicts an EO on the same subject: The EO prevails (higher authority).
- If a Chief Minister EO contradicts a BAA: The BAA prevails.

**Test 3: Publication.** Has the issuance been published as required?
- If the issuance imposes obligations on the public or government agencies and has not been published: May not be legally effective.
- If the issuance concerns purely internal administrative matters (e.g., an AO on office hours): Publication may not be required.

If the issuance fails any of these tests, flag it in your Research Memorandum. A questionable executive issuance does not disappear from your research — you must still document it, but with a notation about the validity concern. The decision-maker needs to know that the issuance exists *and* that its legal standing is questionable.

## 8.4 Shari'ah Checkpoint

Tiers 5-6 present specific Shari'ah intersection points that the researcher must check:

**Tier 5 — National laws and Shari'ah jurisdiction.** National family law (the Family Code, EO 209) does not apply to Muslims whose personal law matters fall under Shari'ah court jurisdiction (PD 1083, BOL Art. X). When your research involves personal law (marriage, divorce, succession, property relations between Muslims), confirm whether Shari'ah law or national family law applies. The answer depends on the religious identity of the parties and the subject matter.[^8]

**Tier 5 — IPRA and traditional/customary law.** RA 8371 protects indigenous peoples' customary law, which may include elements of Shari'ah or pre-Islamic customary practices. When IP rights intersect with Shari'ah, the researcher must trace both legal tracks — they are not always consistent.

**Tier 6 — Executive issuances implementing Shari'ah.** Check whether any executive issuance establishes standards for halal certification, Islamic finance, waqf management, or zakat administration. These issuances may come from the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta (the office of Islamic legal opinion) or the Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (for waqf properties).

**The checkpoint rule remains:** If your research question involves personal law, family relations, succession, property among Muslims, Islamic finance, halal standards, or waqf — Chapter 9 (Shari'ah and Customary Law) is not optional. Complete the Shari'ah analysis there before finalizing your Tier 5-6 research.

## 8.5 Worked Example: Does RA 9520 Apply in BARMM?

**Research question:** A cooperative development officer in the Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT) asks: "What law governs cooperatives in BARMM — is it the national Cooperative Code or do we have our own?"

### Step 1: National Law Relationship Test

Apply the three-step test from Chapter 3, Section 3.3.

**Is cooperatives within the 55 enumerated powers?** Yes. Article V, Section 2(k) lists "Cooperatives and social entrepreneurship" as an enumerated power of the Bangsamoro Government.[^9]

**Does the BOL require national law consistency?** No. Unlike civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39), budget (Art. VII, Sec. 28), or elections (Art. IV, Sec. 4), the BOL does not contain a provision requiring cooperative legislation to be consistent with national law.

**Has Parliament enacted a BAA on cooperatives?** No. As of March 2026, no BAA specifically codifying cooperative law has been enacted. The 2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan (2023-2028) confirms this gap: "The pending codification of the Cooperative Code and Social Enterprise Code currently slows down the full operationalization and institutionalization of both Cooperatives and SE."[^10]

**Classification: ENUMERATED POWER — NO NATIONAL LAW CONSISTENCY REQUIRED — LEGISLATIVE GAP.**

### Step 2: National Law Applicability Test

Applying the three-mode test from Section 8.2.1:

- **Direct application?** No — cooperatives is not a residual national power; it is an enumerated Bangsamoro power (Art. V, Sec. 2(k)).
- **Power where BOL requires national law consistency?** No — no BOL provision requires consistency with national cooperative law.
- **Legislative gap?** Yes — Parliament has the enumerated power but has not exercised it.

**Result: RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) applies suppletorily** within BARMM until Parliament enacts a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code.

### Step 3: Research Answer

RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) currently governs cooperatives in BARMM by suppletory application. The Bangsamoro Parliament has the enumerated power to legislate on cooperatives under Article V, Section 2(k) of the BOL, but has not yet enacted a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code. This legislative gap means:

1. **RA 9520 applies** until Parliament acts — cooperative registration, governance standards, and regulatory oversight follow the national framework.
2. **The power to replace RA 9520 exists** — Parliament can enact a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code that supersedes RA 9520 within the region.
3. **No BOL provision requires consistency** with RA 9520 — unlike civil service or budget law, the Bangsamoro Cooperative Code does not need to mirror the national framework. Parliament has full legislative discretion.
4. **The BDP flags this as a priority** — the 2nd BDP identifies the pending cooperative code as a constraint on sector development.

## 8.6 Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Assuming national law does not apply because "BARMM is autonomous."**

This is the most common Tier 5 error. Autonomy does not mean independence. National laws apply directly in residual power areas, suppletorily in legislative gap areas, and as the floor or ceiling in areas where the BOL requires national law consistency. The only situation where a national law does not apply at all is when Parliament has enacted a BAA on a subject that is (a) within the 55 enumerated powers and (b) not subject to a BOL consistency requirement — and even then, laws of general application (anti-graft, data privacy, gender) still apply.

**Pitfall 2: Assuming national law always applies because "the Philippines is a unitary state."**

The opposite error. Some researchers default to national law because they are unfamiliar with the Bangsamoro framework or because national law is easier to find. But in enumerated power areas where a BAA exists and no national law consistency is required, the BAA governs. Citing the national Labor Code without mentioning BAA 82 (Bangsamoro Labor and Employment Code), for example, is incomplete research — BAA 82 is the controlling regional framework for labor matters.

**Pitfall 3: Citing an IRR without checking the BAA it implements.**

An IRR derives its authority from the law it implements. If the BAA has been amended, the IRR may be outdated. If the IRR imposes requirements not found in the BAA, those requirements may be ultra vires. Always trace the IRR back to its parent law and verify consistency.

**Pitfall 4: Missing the suppletory application finding.**

When no BAA exists on an enumerated power, many researchers simply stop: "No BAA found." That is an incomplete finding. The complete finding is: "No BAA found; national law applies suppletorily; this is a legislative gap." The suppletory application is an affirmative legal determination, not a void. Document it as such.
