# Chapter 3 — The BARMM Legal Hierarchy: A Researcher's Guide

Two existing BARMM guidebooks present the legal hierarchy — and they present it differently. The Bill Drafting Guidebook places national laws at Tier 3, above Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts. The Strategic Planning Guidebook separates the Administrative Code (BAA 13) as its own tier and places national laws below other BAAs. Both are right, and both are incomplete, because the hierarchy is not a fixed ladder. It is a context-dependent structure where the order changes based on the subject matter.

This chapter gives you the definitive model: **seven tiers with three relationships to national law** that determine which tier controls for any given research question. Master this framework and you will never again ask "which law prevails?" without being able to answer it.

---

## 3.1 The Seven Tiers

The BARMM legal system draws authority from seven categories of legal sources, listed here from highest to lowest in their *default* order of precedence:

| Tier | Source | Authority |
|------|--------|-----------|
| **1** | **1987 Philippine Constitution** | Supreme law; no other source may contradict it |
| **2** | **Bangsamoro Organic Law (RA 11054)** | Organic act; defines BARMM powers and structure |
| **3** | **Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13)** | Organizational structure and governance framework |
| **4** | **Other Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts** | Sector-specific legislation enacted by Parliament |
| **5** | **Applicable National Laws** | National RAs that apply within BARMM |
| **6** | **Executive Issuances** | EOs, IRRs, MCs, AOs from CM or ministries |
| **7** | **Jurisprudence** | Supreme Court decisions interpreting BARMM authority |

Two things about this table require explanation.

**First, the word "default."** Tiers 1, 2, 6, and 7 always occupy their positions. The Constitution always prevails. The BOL always prevails over BAAs. Executive issuances always yield to the BAAs they implement. Jurisprudence always provides binding interpretation. But the relationship between Tiers 3-5 — BAA 13, other BAAs, and national laws — depends on the subject matter. This is where the three relationships to national law come in (Section 3.2).

**Second, BAA 13 gets its own tier.** The Bill Drafting Guidebook groups BAA 13 with other BAAs. The Strategic Planning Guidebook separates it as Tier 3. This guidebook follows the Strategic Planning approach for a practical reason: BAA 13 (the Bangsamoro Administrative Code) is not just another BAA. It prescribes the structural, functional, and procedural principles of the entire Bangsamoro Government.[^1] It establishes all 15 ministries, defines the powers of the Chief Minister, creates BPDA and BEDC, and provides the governance framework within which all other BAAs operate. For the legal researcher, BAA 13 is the first place to look for institutional mandates and organizational authority — before sector-specific BAAs, before national laws. It is the government's operating manual.

## 3.2 Three Relationships to National Law

The BOL does not create a simple hierarchy where Bangsamoro law always prevails over national law (or vice versa). Instead, it establishes three different relationships between Bangsamoro legislation and national law, and the hierarchy changes depending on which relationship applies to the research question.

### 3.2.1 No National Law Consistency Required

**Definition:** Subjects where the BOL enumerates the power under Article V, Section 2 and is silent on national law. In these areas, the Parliament has primary legislative competence. BAAs govern, and national law applies suppletorily only where no BAA has been enacted (legislative gap).

**The 55 Enumerated Powers.** Article V, Section 2 lists 55 subjects over which the Bangsamoro Government exercises authority:[^2]

> Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines:
>
> (a) Administration of justice; (b) Administrative organization; (c) Agriculture, livestock, and food security; (d) Ancestral domain and natural resources; ... (ccc) Water supply and services, flood control, and irrigation systems.

The full list runs from (a) to (ccc) — 55 areas covering everything from administration of justice to water supply. Appendix A provides the complete enumeration.

**What this means for research:** When your research question falls within one of the 55 enumerated powers and the BOL is silent on national law, BAAs are the controlling authority. If Parliament has enacted a BAA on the subject, that BAA governs within BARMM. If no BAA has been enacted, the power exists but has not been exercised — you have identified a legislative gap (which may be significant for your research).

**The general welfare clause.** Article V, Section 3 extends the Bangsamoro Government's authority beyond the literal text of the 55 enumerated powers:[^3]

> The Bangsamoro Government shall exercise the powers expressly granted, those necessarily implied therefrom, as well as powers necessary, appropriate, or incidental for its efficient and effective governance and those which are essential to the promotion of general welfare.

This means the Bangsamoro Government may also exercise **implied powers** — those necessarily implied from the express grants — and **incidental powers** — those necessary for efficient governance and general welfare. However, implied powers must be traceable to an express grant. You cannot use the general welfare clause to claim authority that no enumerated power supports.

### 3.2.2 National Law Consistency Required

**Definition:** Subjects where the BOL explicitly requires consistency with national law. The BOL enumerates the power but also specifies that the Bangsamoro Government must act consistently with national law in that area. In these areas, **national law sets the framework** and BAAs must be consistent with it.

The BOL identifies these areas by specific provisions scattered across multiple articles — not by a single list. The researcher must know where to find them:

| BOL Provision | Subject | What It Says |
|---------------|---------|-------------|
| Art. VII, Sec. 39 | **Civil service** | "In case of conflict with the national laws, the Constitution and existing national civil service laws, rules, and regulations shall prevail"[^4] |
| Art. VII, Sec. 28 | **Budget** | Must be "consistent with existing laws, rules and regulations of the National Government"[^5] |
| Art. IV, Sec. 4 | **Elections** | Must be "consistent with national election laws"[^6] |
| Art. XII, Sec. 34 | **Audit** | COA remains the exclusive auditor of the Bangsamoro Government |
| Art. IX, Sec. 3 | **Indigenous peoples' rights** | Rights under RA 8371 (IPRA) "shall not be diminished"[^7] |
| Art. XII, Sec. 1 | **Fiscal autonomy** | Block grant and revenue sharing follow nationally-determined formulas |
| Art. VII, Sec. 3 | **Parliamentary powers** | Parliament has "the authority to enact laws on matters that are within the powers and competencies of the Bangsamoro Government" |

**What this means for research:** When your research question falls into one of these areas, you must search **both** the BAA framework and the applicable national law. The national law sets a floor (or sometimes a ceiling) that BAAs cannot breach. A BAA on civil service cannot contradict the national Civil Service Commission's rules. A BAA on procurement must be consistent with RA 12009 to the extent the BOL requires. Your Comparative Matrix (Chapter 2, Section 2.4.3) is essential here — it reveals where the BAA and the national law diverge.

**Critical nuance:** The specific provisions above are the only areas where the BOL *expressly* requires national law consistency. In all other enumerated powers where the BOL is silent on national law applicability, the Parliament has primary legislative competence. Article VII, Section 39's "national civil service laws shall prevail" applies **only to civil service law** — it is not a blanket rule that national law always prevails over BAAs.[^8]

### 3.2.3 Residual National Power

**Definition:** Subjects not enumerated in Article V, Section 2. The National Government retains authority because the power has not been granted to the Bangsamoro Government. Under Article V, Section 1:[^9]

> All powers, functions, and responsibilities not granted by the Constitution or by national law to the Bangsamoro Government shall be vested in the National Government.

This is the residual clause. It means the default is national authority. Only the powers expressly granted to BARMM (the 55 enumerated powers) and those necessarily implied from them fall within Bangsamoro legislative competence. Everything else — national defense, foreign policy, citizenship, immigration, customs, currency — remains with the National Government.

**What this means for research:** If your research question concerns a subject not among the 55 enumerated powers and not necessarily implied from them, the Bangsamoro Parliament has no legislative competence. Your research should confirm this boundary rather than look for authority that does not exist. This is a finding — the research answer is: *The Bangsamoro Government does not have legislative competence over this subject.*

## 3.3 The National Law Relationship Test

For any research question, apply this three-step test to determine which tier controls:

**Step 1: Is the subject within the 55 enumerated powers (Art. V, Sec. 2)?**

Read the full list of 55 powers (Appendix A). Does your subject match one of them — either directly or through the general welfare clause (Art. V, Sec. 3)?

- **YES** → Proceed to Step 2.
- **NO** → This is a **residual national power**. National law governs. The Bangsamoro Parliament has no legislative competence. Stop here.

**Step 2: Does the BOL explicitly require consistency with national law on this subject?**

Check the provisions listed in Section 3.2.2 above. Does the BOL contain a specific provision requiring consistency with national law for this subject?

- **YES** → **National law consistency is required**. Both BAAs and national laws apply. National law sets the floor or ceiling. Search Tiers 2-5 thoroughly and build your Comparative Matrix.
- **NO** → Proceed to Step 3.

**Step 3: Has Parliament enacted a BAA on this subject?**

Search the BAA archive (Tier 4). Has Parliament exercised its power by enacting legislation?

- **YES** → The **BAA governs**. No national law consistency is required for this subject (though national laws of general application — anti-graft, data privacy, women's rights — may still apply as cross-cutting obligations).
- **NO** → The power exists but has not been exercised. This is a **legislative gap**. In the interim, national laws may apply suppletorily until Parliament acts.

## 3.4 Conflict Resolution Rules

When provisions from different tiers conflict, apply these eight rules in order:

**Rule 1: The Constitution prevails over all other sources, including the BOL itself.**
No provision of the BOL, no BAA, and no executive issuance can contradict the Constitution. If a BAA violates a constitutional provision, it is void — regardless of whether the subject is within the 55 enumerated powers.[^10]

**Rule 2: The BOL prevails over all BAAs.**
If a BAA contradicts a provision of the BOL, the BOL controls. BAAs are enacted *under* the BOL's grant of authority. They cannot exceed that grant.

**Rule 3: For national laws vs. BAAs — apply the National Law Relationship Test.**
This is the rule that both existing guidebooks acknowledge but neither reduces to a simple formula. Use the three-step test from Section 3.3. The answer depends on the subject.

**Rule 4: BAA 13 organizes government; specific BAAs prevail on their subject matter.**
BAA 13 (the Administrative Code) provides the general organizational framework. When a specific BAA addresses the same subject in greater detail, the specific BAA prevails on its subject matter (*lex specialis*). Example: BAA 13 assigns the general mandate of MBHTE, but BAA 18 (the Education Code) defines the educational system in detail. For education matters, BAA 18 controls.

**Rule 5: Later BAAs prevail over earlier BAAs on the same subject (*lex posterior*).**
When two BAAs conflict on the same subject, the more recently enacted BAA prevails — unless the earlier BAA contains a provision that the later BAA does not expressly repeal.

**Rule 6: Specific BAAs prevail over general BAAs on overlapping topics (*lex specialis*).**
When a general BAA (like BAA 13) and a specific BAA (like BAA 84 on the budget system) overlap, the specific BAA governs on its subject matter.

**Rule 7: Executive issuances cannot exceed the authority of the BAA they implement.**
An executive order, administrative order, or memorandum circular cannot contradict the BAA it implements. It also cannot expand the BAA's scope beyond Parliament's intent. If an IRR adds requirements not found in the BAA, those additional requirements may be challenged as *ultra vires*.

**Rule 8: Jurisprudence provides binding interpretation of all other sources.**
Supreme Court decisions define the boundaries of BARMM authority. When the SC rules on the validity of a BAA, the scope of BOL provisions, or the limits of regional autonomy, that ruling is binding on all government bodies. The SC's interpretation becomes part of the legal landscape — your research must account for it.[^11]

## 3.5 Shari'ah Checkpoint: The Parallel Dimension

Before completing any hierarchy analysis, ask: **Does this research question have a Shari'ah dimension?**

Shari'ah is not a separate tier. It is a parallel legal dimension that intersects all seven tiers. The Constitution enables it (Art. X, Sec. 20, which empowers the organic act to create a Shari'ah-based justice system). The BOL codifies it (Art. X, Secs. 1-20). BAAs may implement it. National law (PD 1083) predates the BOL's Shari'ah provisions. Jurisprudence interprets its boundaries.

A Shari'ah dimension exists when the subject involves:
- Personal law for Muslims (marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody)
- Property relations between Muslims
- Commercial or civil actions between Muslims not covered by PD 1083
- Minor criminal offenses under Shari'ah court jurisdiction (*ta'zir*)
- Islamic finance, halal regulation, waqf (endowment), or zakat (alms)

If a Shari'ah dimension exists, add a Shari'ah column to your Comparative Matrix and proceed to Chapter 9 for the full research methodology. The National Law Relationship Test still applies — you still need to determine which tier controls — but you also need to determine how Shari'ah interacts with the secular legal framework at each tier.

Chapter 9 covers this in detail. For now, the checkpoint ensures you do not complete your hierarchy analysis without asking the question.

## 3.6 Worked Example

**Research question:** *What law governs public procurement in BARMM?*

### Step 1: National Law Relationship Test

**Is procurement within the 55 enumerated powers?** Art. V, Sec. 2 does not list "procurement" as a named power, but several enumerated powers necessarily involve procurement: (b) Administrative organization, (f) Budgeting, (qq) Public works and infrastructure, (bb) Health, etc. The general welfare clause (Art. V, Sec. 3) extends authority to powers "necessary, appropriate, or incidental for its efficient and effective governance." Procurement is necessarily incidental to every government function. **Result: YES — implied power.**

**Does the BOL require national law consistency?** Art. VII, Sec. 28 requires the budget to be "consistent with existing laws, rules and regulations of the National Government." Procurement is closely linked to budget execution. Additionally, RA 12009 (Government Procurement Reform Act) is a national law that applies to all government entities, including GOCCs and LGUs. **Result: YES — national law consistency required.**

**Classification: NATIONAL LAW CONSISTENCY REQUIRED.** No Bangsamoro procurement framework has been enacted as of March 2026. RA 12009 (Government Procurement Reform Act) applies directly. The BOL's budget consistency requirement (Art. VII, Sec. 28) reinforces this. The Comparative Matrix must cover the BOL, RA 12009, and any executive issuances on procurement.

### Research Answer

Public procurement in BARMM is governed by **RA 12009** (Government Procurement Reform Act), as no Bangsamoro procurement framework has been enacted as of March 2026. National law consistency is required — the BOL grants budgeting authority (Art. V, Sec. 2(f)) but Art. VII, Sec. 28 requires budget consistency with national laws, and COA audits procurement using national standards. The researcher must search Tier 2 (BOL fiscal provisions), Tier 5 (RA 12009 and its IRR), and Tier 6 (any OCM or MFBM procurement circulars). This is also a **legislative gap** — Parliament has the power to enact a regional procurement framework but has not yet done so.

## 3.7 Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Assuming national law always prevails over BAAs.**
This is the most common hierarchy error. The BOL enumerates 55 power areas. In those areas where the BOL does not require national law consistency, BAAs govern. National law prevails only where the BOL expressly requires consistency (civil service, budget, elections, audit, IP rights) and in residual national powers. Use the National Law Relationship Test, not assumptions.

**Pitfall 2: Assuming BAAs always prevail over national laws.**
The opposite error. Some researchers, knowing BARMM has enumerated powers, assume BAAs always control. But where national law consistency is required, national law sets the floor. A BAA on civil service that contradicts the national Civil Service Commission's rules is invalid to the extent of the inconsistency.

**Pitfall 3: Citing the wrong BOL article.**
Article misattribution is the number one error in BARMM documents. The BOL has **18 articles**, not 13. The most dangerous swaps: Art. IX (Basic Rights) cited when Art. X (Justice System) is correct; Art. V (Powers) cited when Art. VI (Intergovernmental Relations) is correct; Art. VIII (Wali) cited when Art. XVI (Transition) is correct; Art. XII, Sec. 9 cited for the Block Grant when the correct reference is Secs. 15-16.[^12] Always verify against the verbatim BOL text.

**Pitfall 4: Treating the hierarchy as fixed and universal.**
The hierarchy is not a single ladder that applies the same way to every question. It is a context-dependent structure. The relationship between Tiers 3-5 changes based on the relationship to national law. A researcher who memorizes "Constitution > BOL > National Laws > BAAs" (the Bill Drafting model) will get the wrong answer for areas where no national law consistency is required. A researcher who memorizes "Constitution > BOL > BAA 13 > BAAs > National Laws" (the Strategic Planning model) will get the wrong answer where national law consistency is required. Use the National Law Relationship Test, not a memorized sequence.

**Pitfall 5: Forgetting the Shari'ah checkpoint.**
If your research question involves personal law, property relations between Muslims, Islamic finance, or any matter where Shari'ah court jurisdiction applies, the hierarchy analysis is incomplete without the Shari'ah dimension. Chapter 9 is not optional for these questions.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The hierarchy is context-dependent.** There is no single fixed order that applies to every question. Use the National Law Relationship Test to determine the correct order for your specific research question.

2. **Three relationships, three hierarchies.** No national law consistency required: BAAs govern. National law consistency required: national law and BAAs both apply. Residual national power: national law governs exclusively.

3. **The 55 enumerated powers are the gateway.** Everything begins with Article V, Section 2. If the subject is not there (or necessarily implied from there), the Bangsamoro Parliament has no authority.

4. **National law consistency is required only where the BOL says so.** The areas where the BOL expressly requires consistency with national law are listed in specific sections — civil service, budget, elections, audit, IP rights. They are not a general rule. They are specific provisions.

5. **BAA 13 is the government's operating manual.** It deserves its own tier because every institutional question starts there — who has the mandate, what are the functions, how is the government organized.

6. **Always check the Shari'ah dimension.** It is not a separate tier but a parallel system that intersects all seven tiers.
