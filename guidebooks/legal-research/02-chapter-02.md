# Chapter 2 — The Legal Research Process: A Five-Step Methodology

You have a legal question. Maybe a Minister asks whether the MOA has authority to regulate a new activity. Maybe a committee chair needs to know what laws already cover a bill's subject matter. Maybe a planning officer needs the legal basis for a proposed program. The question is clear. Now what?

This chapter gives you a structured process that works for any legal research question in the Bangsamoro context. It has five steps. Each step produces a specific output. Together, they transform a question into a Research Memorandum — the documented inventory of all applicable legal authority, verified and organized for use.

---

## 2.1 Overview: From Question to Research Memorandum

The five steps are:

1. **Issue Analysis** — Frame the question. Identify the jurisdiction. Generate search terms.
2. **Landscape Review** — Understand the legal and policy terrain before searching specific provisions.
3. **Systematic Source Search** — Search each tier of the legal hierarchy methodically, building a Comparative Matrix as you go.
4. **Provision Chain Trace** — Follow what provisions reference until you have found all related authority.
5. **Verification** — Confirm every provision is current, correctly cited, and accurately quoted.

The steps are sequential in design but iterative in practice. Step 3 may reveal that your research question from Step 1 was too broad. Step 4 may send you back to Step 3 to search a tier you thought was irrelevant. Step 5 may reveal that a key provision was repealed, forcing you to re-trace the chain. This is normal. The structure keeps you oriented; the iteration keeps you thorough.

The output of the complete process is a **Research Memorandum** — a structured document listing every applicable authority by tier, with verbatim provisions, source paths, and verification status. Chapter 12 covers the full Research Memorandum format. This chapter teaches you how to produce the content that goes into it.

---

## 2.2 Step 1 — Issue Analysis

Issue analysis converts a vague request into a researchable question. This is the most neglected step. Researchers who skip it waste hours searching for provisions they do not need, or worse, miss provisions they do need because they searched with the wrong terms.

### 2.2.1 Formulating the Research Question

A good research question has three qualities:

**Answerable.** The question must be answerable by reading legal sources. "What is the best approach to IDP protection?" is a policy question — it requires judgment, not just research. "Does the Bangsamoro Parliament have authority to legislate on IDP protection?" is a legal research question — it can be answered by examining the BOL's enumerated powers and relevant national laws.

**Specific.** The question identifies the legal issue precisely. "What laws apply to education in BARMM?" is too broad — there are dozens. "Does BAA 18 (Education Code) authorize the Ministry to regulate private madaris?" is specific enough to research efficiently.

**Tied to a legal outcome.** The question connects to a decision someone needs to make. "What does Article XII say about fiscal autonomy?" is descriptive but purposeless. "Can the Bangsamoro Government impose an excise tax on cement manufactured within the region?" ties the research to a concrete institutional decision.[^1]

### Formulating Technique: The Three-Part Frame

For any research request, extract three elements:

| Element | Question | Example |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Subject** | What legal topic are you researching? | Regulation of ride-hailing services |
| **Jurisdiction** | BARMM only, national only, or both? | Both — transport is among the BOL's enumerated powers but national agencies also regulate |
| **Action** | What does the requester want to know? | Whether the Bangsamoro Parliament can enact a regional ride-hailing regulatory framework |

The three-part frame produces a precise research question: *Does the Bangsamoro Parliament have authority to enact a regional framework for regulating ride-hailing services, given existing national regulation under RA 11659 and the BOL's enumerated powers?*

> *Before reading further: take the last research request you received and apply the three-part frame. Does the reframed question change what you would search for?*

### 2.2.2 Identifying the Jurisdiction

Every research question has a jurisdictional dimension. In the Bangsamoro context, three possibilities exist:

1. **Enumerated Bangsamoro power (no national law consistency required)** — The subject falls within the 55 enumerated powers in BOL Article V, Section 2, and the BOL does not require consistency with national law. Example: administrative organization of regional MOAs.

2. **National law consistency required** — The subject involves both Bangsamoro and national authority. Either the BOL explicitly requires national law consistency (civil service, budget, elections, audit, indigenous peoples' rights), or the subject involves overlapping competencies. Example: procurement (RA 12009 applies directly; BOL Art. VII, Sec. 28 requires budget consistency with national laws).

3. **Residual national power** — The subject involves powers not granted to the Bangsamoro Government (Art. V, Sec. 1). Example: national defense, foreign policy, citizenship.

Identifying the jurisdiction at the outset determines which tiers of the hierarchy you need to search. If the subject is an enumerated Bangsamoro power with no national law consistency required, you focus on Tiers 2-4 (BOL, BAA 13, other BAAs). If national law consistency is required, you must also cover Tier 5 (national laws) thoroughly. If the subject is a residual national power, the Bangsamoro Parliament has no legislative competence, and your research should confirm this boundary rather than look for authority that does not exist.

Chapter 3 covers the National Law Relationship Decision Matrix that resolves jurisdictional questions systematically.

### 2.2.3 Generating Search Terms

Legal provisions do not always use the terms you expect. Generate a search term list that includes:

- **Primary terms** — the subject's standard legal terminology. Example: "ride-hailing," "transportation network company," "TNC."
- **Statutory synonyms** — terms used in specific laws. Example: the BOL uses "cooperatives and social entrepreneurship" (Art. V, Sec. 2(k)), not "cooperative governance" or "coop regulation."
- **Broader concepts** — umbrella terms that might capture your subject. Example: "trade and industry," "commerce," "public convenience."
- **Related subjects** — adjacent topics that might intersect. Example: "labor" (driver employment), "consumer protection" (passenger rights), "data privacy" (rider data).

Write the search terms down. You will use them at every tier in Step 3.

## 2.3 Step 2 — Landscape Review

Before diving into specific provisions, understand the terrain. The landscape review is a quick scan of secondary sources that tells you what kind of legal environment you are entering.

### 2.3.1 Secondary Sources and Governance Context

**Governance context.** Invoke your understanding of BARMM's institutional structure. Which MOA is responsible for this subject? Where does it sit in the organizational hierarchy? What are its mandated powers and functions under BAA 13? This context tells you where to look for sector-specific BAAs and executive issuances.

**Existing research.** Has anyone in the government already researched this question? Check:
- PRLS policy notes and research papers
- Committee reports from relevant parliamentary committees
- Legal opinions issued by the Attorney-General's Office (BAA 5)
- Previous legislative research briefers on related bills

**Commentary and doctrine.** For subjects with significant jurisprudential history — autonomy law, constitutional limitations, Shari'ah jurisdiction — academic commentary and legal doctrine help you understand the landscape before searching for specific provisions.

The landscape review takes 30 minutes to an hour. It is not deep research — it is orientation. You are looking for landmarks, not destinations. The destinations come in Step 3.

### 2.3.2 Development Plan Context

If the research question relates to a policy or program, check the **2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP) 2023-2028**. The BDP provides development context — which BDP goals and strategies relate to your subject, which chapters cover the sector, and what targets the Bangsamoro Government has committed to.

BDP context matters because:
- A program that aligns with a BDP goal has stronger institutional support
- The BDP may identify policy gaps that your research question touches on
- BPDA evaluation of MOA strategic plans is based on BDP alignment[^2]

This step is optional for purely legal questions ("Does the BOL authorize X?") but essential for mandate or policy questions ("What is the legal basis for this development program?").

## 2.4 Step 3 — Systematic Source Search

This is the core of legal research. You work through each tier of the seven-tier BARMM legal hierarchy, searching for every provision that bears on your question. Chapters 4-7 cover each tier in detail. This section teaches the overall methodology.

### 2.4.1 Working Through the Seven Tiers

Search in this order:

| Tier | Source | What You Are Looking For |
|------|--------|------------------------|
| 1 | 1987 Philippine Constitution | Constitutional provisions relevant to the subject; constitutional constraints on Bangsamoro legislative authority |
| 2 | Bangsamoro Organic Law (RA 11054) | Enumerated powers, general welfare clause, structural requirements, Shari'ah provisions, fiscal provisions |
| 3 | Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA 13) | MOA mandates, powers and functions, organizational structure, governance framework |
| 4 | Other Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts | Creating/charter BAAs, cross-cutting BAAs (17 civil service, 84 budget, 49 local governance), sector-specific BAAs |
| 5 | Applicable National Laws | National RAs that apply directly, suppletorily, or through adoption; IRRs |
| 6 | Executive Issuances | Chief Minister EOs, ministerial MCs, AOs, IRRs for specific BAAs |
| 7 | Jurisprudence | Supreme Court decisions interpreting BARMM authority or the subject matter |

**The order matters.** Higher tiers constrain lower tiers. If the Constitution prohibits something, no BOL provision can authorize it. If the BOL grants an enumerated power over a subject with no national law consistency requirement, national laws do not apply (except where the BOL requires consistency). Starting at the top ensures you understand the boundaries before searching the details.

**Do not skip tiers.** Even if you think a tier is irrelevant, scan it. Constitutional provisions in unexpected articles (Art. XIII on social justice, Art. XIV on education) often apply. National laws in unexpected domains (data privacy, anti-graft, women's rights) often have BARMM implications. The five minutes you spend confirming a tier is irrelevant may save you from missing a controlling provision.

### 2.4.2 Recording What You Find

For each provision you find, record these fields:

| Field | What to Enter |
|-------|-------------|
| **Tier** | Which tier of the hierarchy (1-7) |
| **Legal Source** | Full name and number (e.g., "Republic Act No. 11054") |
| **Provision** | Specific article, section, subsection (e.g., "Art. V, Sec. 2(oo)") |
| **Verbatim Text** | The exact text of the provision — copy from the source, do not paraphrase |
| **Authority/Mandate** | Concise statement of what the provision authorizes, requires, or prohibits |
| **Actor** | Who has the obligation or authority — the Bangsamoro Government, the National Government, a specific MOA, or both |
| **Status** | In force / Amended by [law] / Repealed by [law] / Under review |
| **Source Path** | Where you read this provision — file path, URL, or document reference |

> *This recording discipline is non-negotiable. If you cannot point to the exact provision, the exact text, and the exact source where you read it, your research is unverifiable. Paraphrases drift. Verbatim citations do not.*

### 2.4.3 Building the Comparative Matrix as You Search

As you work through the seven tiers, you will often find that **multiple sources address the same subject differently**. The Constitution may establish a broad principle. The BOL may grant specific power. A national law may impose requirements. A BAA may operationalize the power. These provisions do not just coexist — they interact, sometimes in conflict.

The **Comparative Matrix** is the tool that makes these interactions visible. You build it *during* the search, not after. Each time you find a provision at a new tier that addresses the same subject, you add a column to the matrix.

**How to use it:**

1. **Start at Tier 1.** When you find a constitutional provision relevant to your subject, enter it in the Constitution column for the applicable row (authority, scope, limitations, etc.).

2. **Add each tier as you search.** When you reach Tier 2 and find BOL provisions, fill in the BOL column. When you reach Tiers 3-4 and find BAAs, fill in the BAA column. National laws go in the National Law column.

3. **Fill the Conflict/Gap column as differences appear.** The matrix reveals:
   - **Conflicts** — where two tiers say different things about the same subject. Example: a national law requires registration with a national agency, but a BAA assigns registration to a regional agency.
   - **Gaps** — where a tier is silent on an aspect that another tier addresses. Example: the BOL grants power over transportation, but no BAA has been enacted to operationalize it.
   - **Suppletory relationships** — where a national law applies because BARMM legislation is silent. Example: RA 12009 (Procurement) applies suppletorily where BAA 38 does not cover a specific procedure.
   - **Evolution** — how the legal treatment of a subject has changed over time (from ARMM-era law to BTA-era legislation).

4. **Use the matrix to identify which tier controls.** The Conflict/Gap column feeds directly into the national law relationship analysis (Chapter 3). When you find a conflict, you apply the national law relationship test: Is this an enumerated power with no national law consistency requirement? Does the BOL require national law consistency? The matrix gives you the raw material; the national law relationship analysis gives you the answer.

**Why build it during the search, not after?**

Three reasons.

First, **the matrix guides your search.** When you fill in the Constitution column and see that the Constitution imposes a limitation (say, "no money shall be paid out of the treasury except in pursuance of an appropriation made by law" — Art. VI, Sec. 29), you know to look for the appropriation provisions at every subsequent tier. The matrix tells you what to search for next.

Second, **the matrix catches conflicts early.** If you build it after the search, you may not notice that a BAA contradicts a BOL provision until you compile the results. If you build it during the search, you catch the conflict the moment you read the BAA — and you can immediately trace the conflict to determine which provision controls.

Third, **the matrix becomes a deliverable.** When completed, the Comparative Matrix is one of the five standard legal research outputs (Chapter 12). Building it during the search means you produce a deliverable as a byproduct of the process, rather than as an extra step at the end.

> *The matrix does not need to cover every aspect for every question. If your research question is narrow — "Can BARMM impose an excise tax on locally manufactured goods?" — the matrix might have only two rows (authority granted, limitations). If the question is broad — "What is the legal framework for cooperative regulation in BARMM?" — the matrix might have six or eight rows. Scale it to the question.*

### Two Types of Comparative Matrix

The tier-by-tier matrix above compares how **different levels of the legal hierarchy** treat the same subject. There is a second type that compares how **different laws at the same level** treat the same subject.

**The Verbatim Section-by-Section Matrix** compares multiple bills or laws provision by provision. This is the format used in committee work when the Parliament has several competing bills on the same topic. It goes beyond passive comparison — it is a **decision-support tool** that produces actionable consolidation recommendations.

The OOBC's Verbatim Matrix on three OBC bills (394, 205, 203) demonstrates the full format.[^5] It uses six columns:

The matrix compared 18 sections across all three bills — from short title through effectivity — and revealed three categories of findings:

- **Critical divergences**: Bill 394 creates a new 21-member Bangsamoro Development Assistance and Management Council (BDAMC) chaired by the Chief Minister, fundamentally altering OOBC's institutional position from "lead agency" (Bills 205/203) to "technical secretariat." The Analysis column flagged this as "CRITICAL DIVERGENCE" and the Position column recommended a hybrid governance model.
- **Coverage gaps**: Only Bill 394 had explicit prioritization criteria and community determination procedures (BPA/BPC/SDA classification). Neither Bill 205 nor 203 provided comparable targeting mechanisms. The Position column recommended adopting Bill 394's framework with enhancements.
- **Unique provisions**: Bill 203's Peace and Culture Centers (physical infrastructure for OBC service delivery) and co-management model with OBC organizations had no equivalent in Bills 394 or 205. The Position column recommended incorporating this into Bill 394's BDAP framework.

This matrix format is the standard for parliamentary committee consolidation work. When a committee receives multiple bills on the same topic, the verbatim matrix is how the committee secretariat presents the options to the committee chair.

**When to use which:**

| Matrix Type | When to Use | What It Reveals |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Tier-by-tier** (hierarchy) | Researching a legal question across the hierarchy | Conflicts between tiers, gaps, suppletory relationships |
| **Section-by-section** (verbatim) | Comparing multiple bills or laws on the same topic | Structural differences, coverage gaps, consolidation opportunities |

Both types are standard legal research outputs. Chapter 12 covers the formal templates for each. Appendix E provides the annotated Comparative Legal Matrix template.

### 2.4.4 The Shari'ah Checkpoint

At the end of Step 3, before moving to Step 4, ask: **Does this research question have a Shari'ah dimension?**

A Shari'ah dimension exists when:
- The subject involves personal law matters (marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody, family relations) for Muslims
- The subject involves property relations between Muslims
- The subject involves minor criminal offenses that fall under Shari'ah court jurisdiction
- The subject intersects with Islamic finance, halal certification, waqf (endowment), or zakat (alms)
- A provision references "Shari'ah," "Islamic law," or "Muslim personal law"

If yes, you need to search Chapter 9's Shari'ah research sources in addition to the seven tiers. Add a Shari'ah column to your Comparative Matrix.

If no, note it in your Search Record ("Shari'ah dimension: None identified") and proceed.

## 2.5 Step 4 — Provision Chain Trace

Step 3 finds the provisions that directly address your question. Step 4 follows what those provisions *reference* — tracing the chain of authority, rights, obligations, and definitions across multiple sources until you have found all related authority.

This is the step that separates surface-level research from thorough research. Chapter 11 covers the full methodology. Here is the overview.

**Why chain tracing matters.** A provision rarely stands alone. BOL Article VI, Section 12 says the Bangsamoro Government shall "provide assistance to enhance their economic, social, and cultural development" for communities outside the region. But what does "economic, social, and cultural development" mean? To answer that, you must trace through Article IX (Basic Rights), Article XIII (Regional Economy), and the relevant sections of BAA 13. Without the trace, your research is grounded in one provision. With it, your research maps dozens of interconnected provisions.

**The five-step trace:**

1. **Extract traceable concepts** — from each provision found in Step 3, identify terms that point to other provisions: "rights," "powers," "functions," "in coordination with," named populations, named institutions.

2. **Full-source scan** — read the entire relevant source document (not just keyword matches). For BOL provisions, read all five chapter files. For BAA 13, read the relevant Books.

3. **Relevance filter** — given your specific research question, determine which traced provisions actually apply. Not every "rights" provision is relevant to every question.

4. **Depth limit** — follow chains up to three levels deep. Stop when the concept is fully defined with verbatim text, three levels of chaining have been reached, or no further references exist.

5. **Output as Provision Chain Map** — record each chain in a structured table:

| Source Provision | Traceable Concept | Related Provisions Found | Relevance to Research Question |
|-----------------|-------------------|------------------------|-------------------------------|

The Provision Chain Map becomes a section of your Research Memorandum.

## 2.6 Step 5 — Verification

Every provision you cite must be verified. Verification answers three questions:

**Is it current?** Has the provision been repealed, amended, or superseded by later legislation? The Bangsamoro Parliament passes new BAAs regularly. National laws are amended. Executive issuances expire. A provision that was good law last year may not be good law today.

**Is it correctly cited?** Does the article number, section number, and subsection match the source? BOL Article misattributions are the number one error in BARMM documents — Art. IX (Basic Rights) cited when Art. X (Justice System) is correct, Art. XII Sec. 9 cited when Secs. 15-16 is correct.[^3] Verify every citation against the verbatim source text.

**Is it accurately quoted?** Does the text in your Research Memorandum match the source word-for-word? Paraphrases introduce errors. "Shall take into consideration" is not the same as "shall ensure the protection." Copy the text from the source. Do not retype from memory.

### Verification Checklist

For each provision in your Research Memorandum:

- [ ] Read the source file or document containing the provision (not from memory — actually read it)
- [ ] Confirm the article, section, and subsection numbers match
- [ ] Confirm the quoted text is verbatim
- [ ] Check whether the provision has been amended by a later BAA, national law, or executive issuance
- [ ] Check whether the provision has been the subject of a Supreme Court ruling
- [ ] Record the verification source path (where you read it)
- [ ] Mark status: In force / Amended by [law] / Repealed / Under review

### Source Hierarchy for Verification

Verify provisions in this order of reliability:

1. **Local markdown transcriptions** — verbatim-accurate, searchable, fast. BOL at `~/Vault/bangsamoro/bangsamoro-laws/bol-ra-11054/`. BAAs at `~/Vault/bangsamoro/bangsamoro-laws/BAA-{N}.md`.
2. **Original PDF source documents** — authoritative but slower to search
3. **Official Gazette** — for national laws and recent BAAs not yet transcribed
4. **Web sources** — LawPhil, SC E-Library. Label as `[Source: web — verify against official copy]`

Never cite a provision you have not read in the source. Never verify from memory.

## 2.7 The Search Record

Maintain a Search Record alongside your Research Memorandum. The Search Record documents *how* you searched — not just what you found. It serves two purposes: reproducibility (someone else can repeat your search) and completeness audit (you can verify you searched all relevant sources).

The Search Record should include:

| Field | What to Record |
|-------|---------------|
| **Research question** | The formulated question from Step 1 |
| **Search terms used** | The complete list from Step 1, including terms that yielded no results |
| **Tiers searched** | Which of the 7 tiers you covered, and confirmation of coverage |
| **Sources searched** | Specific files, databases, websites, and documents consulted |
| **Provisions found** | Count of provisions recorded in the Research Memorandum |
| **Shari'ah dimension** | Identified / None identified |
| **Chains traced** | Count of provision chains followed in Step 4 |
| **Verification status** | All verified / [N] pending verification |
| **Date** | When the research was completed |
| **Researcher** | Your name and position |

The Search Record is an appendix to your Research Memorandum. It does not need to be long — half a page is typical. But it must be honest. If you did not search Tier 7 (jurisprudence), say so. A known gap is better than an unacknowledged one.

## 2.8 When to Stop Researching

Research can expand indefinitely. There is always one more provision to check, one more chain to trace, one more national law that might apply. You need a stopping rule.

**Stop when:**

1. **All seven tiers have been searched** for your specific question (or you have documented why a tier was excluded).
2. **All provision chains have been traced** to their logical endpoints or the three-level depth limit.
3. **All provisions have been verified** as current.
4. **Your Comparative Matrix** shows no unresolved conflicts — every conflict has been classified using the national law relationship test (Chapter 3).
5. **Your research question has been answered.** You can state, with citations, what the law says about the subject.

**Do not stop when:**

- You have found one supporting provision but have not checked for contradicting ones
- You have searched some tiers but not all
- You have unverified provisions in your memorandum
- You have an open Conflict/Gap cell in your Comparative Matrix that you have not investigated

## 2.9 Worked Example

**Research question:** *Does the Bangsamoro Parliament have authority to enact a regional cooperative code?*

### Step 1: Issue Analysis

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Subject** | Regulation of cooperatives within BARMM |
| **Jurisdiction** | Enumerated power — BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(k) lists "Cooperatives and social entrepreneurship"; no national law consistency required |
| **Action** | Determine whether Parliament can enact a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code, and what law currently governs |
| **Search terms** | "cooperatives," "social entrepreneurship," "cooperative development," "cooperative code," "CDA," "social enterprise" |

### Step 2: Landscape Review

- **Governance context**: Cooperative development may fall under MTIT (trade, investments, tourism) per BAA 13. The BOL mandates a "favorable environment for cooperatives" (Art. XIII, Sec. 24).
- **National context**: RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) is the national cooperative framework. The Cooperative Development Authority (CDA) regulates cooperatives nationally.
- **BDP context**: BDP Ch. 7 (Economy) covers cooperative development as part of economic strategy.

### Step 3: Systematic Source Search (with Comparative Matrix)

**Tier 1 — Constitution:**
- Art. XII, Sec. 15: "The Congress shall create an agency to promote the viability and growth of cooperatives." Establishes national cooperative framework.

**Tier 2 — BOL:**
- Art. V, Sec. 2(k): Bangsamoro Government has enumerated power over "Cooperatives and social entrepreneurship" (no national law consistency required).
- Art. V, Sec. 3: General welfare clause — implied powers for cooperative regulation.
- Art. XIII, Sec. 24: "The Bangsamoro Government shall create a favorable environment for cooperatives."

**Tier 3 — BAA 13:**
- MTIT mandate includes trade, investments, and tourism — cooperatives may fall under this ministry.

**Tier 4 — BAAs:**
- No BAA specifically codifying cooperative law has been enacted as of March 2026. This is a **legislative gap**.

**Tier 5 — National Laws:**
- RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) — national cooperative framework; applies suppletorily in absence of a BAA.

### Step 4: Provision Chain Trace

Starting from BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(k), the traceable concept is "cooperatives and social entrepreneurship." Tracing through:
- Art. XIII, Sec. 24: Affirmative mandate — "favorable environment for cooperatives"
- Art. XIII, Sec. 27: Social enterprises recognized alongside cooperatives
- BAA 13 (MTIT mandate): What specific cooperative functions does MTIT have?

**Finding**: The BOL does not require national law consistency for cooperatives — unlike civil service (Art. VII, Sec. 39) or budget (Art. VII, Sec. 28). Cooperatives is an enumerated power with no national law consistency requirement, where a future BAA would govern.

### Step 5: Verification

All provisions verified against source files:
- Art. V, Sec. 2(k): Verified — `bol-ra-11054/01-articles-I-to-V.md` line 156
- Art. XIII, Sec. 24: Verified — `bol-ra-11054/04-articles-XIII-to-XV.md`
- RA 9520: Verified — national law
- BAA index: No cooperative BAA exists (confirmed)
- Status: All in force

### Research Answer

The Bangsamoro Parliament has **explicit enumerated authority** under BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(k) to regulate cooperatives, with no national law consistency requirement. However, **no BAA has been enacted** to exercise this power. RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code) applies suppletorily until Parliament enacts a Bangsamoro Cooperative Code. The researcher should note this as a **legislative gap** — the power exists, the mandate is clear (Art. XIII, Sec. 24), but Parliament has not yet acted.

## 2.10 Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Starting with the answer.** If you begin research knowing what conclusion you want to reach, you will find only supporting provisions and ignore contradicting ones. Research answers "what does the law say?" — not "does the law support my position?" Search all seven tiers before forming a conclusion.

**Pitfall 2: Paraphrasing from memory.** You read a provision yesterday and think you remember what it says. You write it into your memorandum without re-reading the source. The paraphrase drifts — "shall ensure" becomes "shall promote," "Bangsamoro Government" becomes "regional government," "Section 12" becomes "Section 9." Verify every provision against the source text every time.

**Pitfall 3: Searching only the obvious tier.** A researcher looking for BARMM authority over education goes straight to BAA 18 (Education Code) and stops. But the complete picture requires Tier 1 (Constitution Art. XIV on education), Tier 2 (BOL Art. V, Sec. 2(v) on education and skills training, plus Art. IX on basic rights including education), Tier 5 (national education laws that apply suppletorily), and possibly Tier 7 (SC decisions on educational autonomy). One BAA is not the whole answer.

**Pitfall 4: Ignoring the actor.** A provision says "The National Government shall ensure the protection of the rights of Bangsamoro people outside the region." The researcher attributes this obligation to the Bangsamoro Government. The actor matters. Misattributing an obligation is a serious error — it creates a legal basis that does not exist.[^4]

**Pitfall 5: Skipping the Comparative Matrix.** Searching tier by tier without comparing how different tiers treat the same subject means you miss conflicts, gaps, and suppletory relationships. The matrix catches what sequential reading misses.

**Pitfall 6: Not recording the search.** Six months later, a committee member asks "did you check whether any national law applies?" If you have no Search Record, you cannot answer. Record your search process — not just your findings.

---

## 2.11 Quality Checklist

Before submitting your Research Memorandum, confirm:

- [ ] The research question is documented (three-part frame: subject, jurisdiction, action)
- [ ] All seven tiers of the legal hierarchy have been searched (or exclusions documented)
- [ ] Every provision is recorded with: tier, source, provision number, verbatim text, authority/mandate, actor, status, source path
- [ ] A Comparative Matrix has been built for subjects addressed by multiple tiers
- [ ] The Shari'ah checkpoint has been completed (dimension identified or confirmed absent)
- [ ] Provision chains have been traced for all key provisions (up to 3 levels)
- [ ] Every provision has been verified against the source text (not from memory)
- [ ] No provision is cited that has been repealed, amended, or superseded without noting the change
- [ ] The Search Record is complete: terms, tiers, sources, date, researcher
- [ ] The Research Memorandum is organized by tier, with verbatim text and full citations
- [ ] The Comparative Matrix conflicts and gaps have been classified using the national law relationship test (Chapter 3)
