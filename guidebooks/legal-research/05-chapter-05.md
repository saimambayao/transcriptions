# Chapter 5 — The Bangsamoro Organic Law (RA 11054)

The Bangsamoro Organic Law (RA 11054) is the instrument that created the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, defined its powers, and established its government. It is Tier 2 of the BARMM legal hierarchy — subordinate only to the Constitution (Chapter 4). Every power the Bangsamoro Government exercises traces back to this organic act.

This chapter teaches you how to navigate the BOL's 18-article structure without error, how to work with the 55 enumerated powers, the general welfare clause, and the residual clause, how to distinguish between explicit and implied authority, how to trace BOL provisions to their constitutional basis, and how to identify when the BOL delegates subjects to Parliament for further legislation. These are skills you will use in every research task.

---

## 5.1 Tier 2 — The Bangsamoro Organic Law (RA 11054)

The BOL has **18 articles** — not 13, not 16, not "around 15." Getting the article count right is the single most basic accuracy test for any BARMM legal document. The following table is your essential reference:

### 5.1.2 The Three Power Clauses: Residual, Enumerated, and General Welfare

Chapter 3 introduced the three relationships to national law (enumerated powers with no consistency requirement, powers requiring national law consistency, residual national powers). This section focuses on the three BOL provisions that define the scope of Bangsamoro authority. You must be able to find, quote, and apply all three.

**The Residual Clause — Article V, Section 1:**

> All powers, functions, and responsibilities not granted by the Constitution or by national law to the Bangsamoro Government shall be vested in the National Government.[^6]

This is the boundary line. If a power is not granted to the Bangsamoro Government by the Constitution (through Article X, Section 20) or by national law (through the BOL), it remains with the National Government. The practical effect: when you search the BOL for authority over a subject and find nothing, your answer is not "the BOL is silent." Your answer is "the power is with the National Government under the residual clause."

**The 55 Enumerated Powers — Article V, Section 2:**

> Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines:[^7]

This section lists 55 areas from (a) "Administration of justice" through (ccc) "Water supply and services, flood control, and irrigation systems." Every one of the 55 enumerated powers is a subject on which the Parliament can enact a BAA. The list is your primary tool for answering the question "Does the Bangsamoro Government have authority over this subject?"

**The General Welfare Clause — Article V, Section 3:**

> The Bangsamoro Government shall exercise the powers expressly granted, those necessarily implied therefrom, as well as powers necessary, appropriate, or incidental for its efficient and effective governance and those which are essential to the promotion of general welfare.[^8]

This clause extends authority beyond the literal 55 enumerated powers in two ways:

1. **Implied powers** — powers necessarily implied from an express grant. Example: the power over "education and skills training" (v) necessarily implies the power to certify educational institutions and accredit teachers operating within BARMM.

2. **Incidental powers** — powers necessary, appropriate, or incidental for efficient governance and the promotion of general welfare. Example: the power over "health" (bb) combined with the general welfare clause supports the creation of a regional health insurance scheme, even though "health insurance" is not explicitly listed.

The general welfare clause is powerful but bounded. It cannot create authority where no express power supports it. If none of the 55 enumerated powers relates to a subject — say, national defense — the general welfare clause cannot fill that gap. Article V, Section 1 (the residual clause) sends that power to the National Government.

### 5.1.3 Article-by-Article Research Guide

This section provides a research-oriented summary of each BOL article. Use it when you need to quickly locate which article addresses a particular subject.

**Article I — Name and Purpose (Secs. 1-3)**

Establishes the political entity's name ("Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao") and its purpose: "meaningful self-governance within the framework of the Constitution." Cite Article I, Section 3 when you need the purpose clause as a foundation for interpreting other BOL provisions.

**Article II — Bangsamoro Identity (Secs. 1-2)**

Defines the Bangsamoro people ("those who, at the advent of the Spanish colonization, were considered natives or original inhabitants of Mindanao and the Sulu archipelago") and authorizes the Parliament to adopt the official flag, emblem, and hymn. Section 1 is essential for research involving the definition of who qualifies as Bangsamoro.

**Article III — Territorial Jurisdiction (Secs. 1-5)**

Defines the geographic scope of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region — the areas included, the municipal and regional waters (up to 15 and 19 kilometers respectively), and the constituent units. Cite this article when your research involves questions about geographic jurisdiction — where Bangsamoro laws apply and where they do not.

**Article IV — General Principles and Policies (Secs. 1-10)**

Contains foundational policy declarations: territorial integrity (Sec. 1), self-governance (Sec. 2), parliamentary form of government (Sec. 3), social justice (Sec. 7), IP rights (Sec. 9), freedom of choice (Sec. 10). These are interpretive guides — they shape how courts and officials read the rest of the BOL.

**Article V — Powers of Government (Secs. 1-3)**

The heart of the BOL for legal researchers. Section 1 (residual clause), Section 2 (55 enumerated powers), Section 3 (general welfare clause). Almost every research question about BARMM authority starts here.

**Article VI — Intergovernmental Relations (Secs. 1-13)**

Creates the intergovernmental mechanisms: the Intergovernmental Relations Body (Sec. 2), the Philippine Congress-Bangsamoro Parliament Forum (Sec. 3), the Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board (Sec. 4), and the Council of Leaders (Sec. 9). Also covers national programs that continue in BARMM (Sec. 13). Cite this article when your research involves coordination between BARMM and the National Government.

**Article VII — Bangsamoro Government (Secs. 1-42)**

The longest article. Covers: the Parliament's powers (Sec. 5), composition (Secs. 6-10), legislative procedures (Sec. 25), the appropriations power (Sec. 27), the Chief Minister's election and powers (Secs. 30-34), the Deputy Chief Ministers (Sec. 35), vote of no confidence (Sec. 36), civil service (Sec. 39), and accountability (Sec. 41). This article is the source for anything about how the Bangsamoro Government is structured and how it operates.

**Article VIII — Wali (Secs. 1-6)**

Covers the Wali — the ceremonial head. Appointment (Sec. 1), qualifications (Sec. 2), duties (Sec. 3), term (Sec. 4), per diem (Sec. 5), removal (Sec. 6). Short and narrowly scoped. **Do not confuse this article with Article XVI (Transition).**

**Article IX — Basic Rights (Secs. 1-26)**

A broad article covering: transitional justice (Sec. 1), IP rights (Sec. 3), religious freedom (Sec. 5), human rights (Sec. 6), social justice and access to basic services (Sec. 8), labor rights (Sec. 10), women's participation (Sec. 11), settler communities (Sec. 15), education (Secs. 16-19), science and technology (Sec. 21), health (Sec. 22), arts and culture (Secs. 24-26). **Do not confuse this article with Article X (Justice System).**

**Article X — Justice System (Secs. 1-20)**

Covers the Bangsamoro justice system: Shari'ah courts (Sec. 2), their jurisdiction (Secs. 5-7), the Shari'ah High Court (Sec. 7), judicial qualifications (Sec. 8), traditional/tribal justice (Sec. 17), and alternative dispute resolution (Sec. 20). Cite this article for questions about the court system, Shari'ah jurisdiction, and dispute resolution.

**Article XI — National Defense and Security (Secs. 1-7)**

Short and clear: national defense is a National Government responsibility (Sec. 1). The PNP creates a Police Regional Office in BARMM (Sec. 2). The BJMP and BFP establish regional offices (Secs. 5-6). The Philippine Coast Guard establishes a regional office (Sec. 7). This article is relevant when researching the boundary between BARMM authority and National Government security powers.

**Article XII — Fiscal Autonomy (Secs. 1-41)**

The most section-heavy article, covering: revenue sources (Sec. 6), taxing power limitations (Sec. 9), tax sharing (Sec. 10), the block grant (Secs. 15-16), automatic appropriation (Sec. 17), block grant allocation rules (Sec. 19), general spending limitations (Sec. 20), natural resource revenue sharing (Sec. 34), and the Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board's functions (Secs. 37-38). Cite this article for all fiscal, budget, and revenue questions. **Block grant provisions are in Sections 15-16 — not Section 9.**

**Article XIII — Regional Economy and Patrimony (Secs. 1-36)**

Covers: sustainable development (Secs. 2-3), the BEDC (Sec. 4), gender and development (Sec. 5), natural resources (Secs. 8-16), the Zones of Joint Cooperation (Secs. 18-21), inland waters management (Sec. 22), agriculture and fisheries (Sec. 23), trade and industry (Secs. 24-28), *halal* program (Sec. 30), energy (Secs. 31-36). Cite this article for research involving economic development, natural resources, and patrimony.

**Article XIV — Rehabilitation and Development (Secs. 1-2)**

Short. Establishes the Special Development Fund (Sec. 2) for rehabilitation, reconstruction, and development. Cite this article for research involving post-conflict development and the SDF.

**Article XV — Plebiscite (Secs. 1-3)**

Governs the plebiscite for ratification of the BOL. Mostly historical relevance now, but cite it when tracing the legal basis for the BOL's effectivity.

**Article XVI — Transition (Secs. 1-14)**

Covers the BTA: creation (Sec. 2), powers during transition (Sec. 3), priority legislation (Sec. 4 — the five priority codes: Administrative Code, Revenue Code, Electoral Code, Local Government Code, Education Code), the transition plan (Sec. 6), the interim cabinet of 15 ministries (Sec. 8), disposition of ARMM personnel (Sec. 10), dissolution upon the first Parliament's election (Sec. 12). **Do not confuse with Article VIII (Wali).**

**Article XVII — Amendments (Sec. 1)**

One section: amendments, revisions, or repeals of the BOL must be enacted by Congress. The Parliament cannot amend the BOL on its own.

**Article XVIII — Final Provisions (Secs. 1-5)**

Covers: subsisting regional laws (Sec. 1), existing LGUs (Sec. 2), separability clause (Sec. 3), amendatory clause (Sec. 4), and effectivity (Sec. 5). Cite this article when researching the continued validity of pre-BOL ARMM laws or the BOL's relationship with other national laws.

## 5.2 Researching BOL Authority: Explicit vs. Implied

When a research question asks "Does the Bangsamoro Government have authority over X?" you must conduct a two-stage analysis: first check for explicit authority, then assess implied authority.

### 5.2.1 Explicit Authority Analysis

**Step 1: Search Article V, Section 2.** Scan the 55 enumerated powers for a direct match. Use the grouped list in Section 5.1.3 of this chapter to narrow your search by subject category.

**Step 2: Search the subject-specific article.** The 55 enumerated powers in Article V are broad labels. The detail is in the subject-specific articles. For example, the enumerated power over "education and skills training" (Art. V, Sec. 2(v)) is just a label. The substance — what the Bangsamoro Government can actually do with that power — is in Article IX, Sections 16-19, which cover the integrated education system, Madaris education, the tribal university system, and sports development.

**Step 3: Check for conditions and limitations.** Many BOL provisions grant authority with conditions attached. Article VII, Section 39 grants authority over civil service — but "in case of conflict with the national laws, the Constitution and existing national civil service laws, rules, and regulations shall prevail."[^9] Article XII, Section 6 grants taxing power — but Section 9 lists 12 categories of taxes the Bangsamoro Government cannot impose.[^10] Record both the grant and its conditions.

**Step 4: Check Article XVI, Section 4 for BTA-specific mandates.** If you are researching during the transition period, Article XVI, Section 4 identifies priority legislation: the Bangsamoro Administrative Code, Revenue Code, Electoral Code, Local Government Code, and Education Code.[^11] These are subjects the BOL explicitly delegated to the BTA for further legislation.

### 5.2.2 Implied Authority Analysis

If explicit authority does not resolve your question, assess whether the general welfare clause (Art. V, Sec. 3) supports the proposed action.

Apply this three-part test:

1. **Is there an express power that relates to the subject?** Implied authority must be traceable to an express grant. If no enumerated power touches the subject, the general welfare clause cannot create authority from nothing.

2. **Is the proposed action "necessarily implied" from the express power?** If yes, it is an implied power. Example: the express power over "health" (bb) necessarily implies the power to license hospitals within BARMM.

3. **Is the proposed action "necessary, appropriate, or incidental" for efficient governance?** If yes, it is an incidental power under the general welfare clause. This is broader than "necessarily implied" but still requires a connection to an express power.

If the answer to all three questions is "no," the power does not exist for the Bangsamoro Government. The residual clause (Art. V, Sec. 1) assigns it to the National Government.

## 5.3 Tracing BOL Provisions to Their Constitutional Basis

Every BOL power has a constitutional source. Tracing that source strengthens your research because it (a) confirms that the BOL provision is constitutionally valid, (b) reveals the constitutional limits that shape the BOL provision, and (c) provides a stronger legal foundation for any document you produce.

### 5.3.1 The Tracing Method

Follow this three-step sequence:

**Step 1: Identify the BOL provision.** Start with the BOL article and section you are researching.

**Step 2: Find the constitutional authorization.** Ask: "Which constitutional provision authorizes or relates to this BOL provision?" Use the six-article map from Section 4.2.2 as your guide.

Common traces:

| BOL Provision | Constitutional Basis |
|---------------|---------------------|
| Art. V, Sec. 2 (55 enumerated powers) | Art. X, Sec. 20 (minimum powers for organic acts) |
| Art. VII, Sec. 2 (Parliament's legislative authority) | Art. X, Secs. 1, 15, 18 (creation, organic act, self-governance) |
| Art. VII, Sec. 27 (appropriations requirement) | Art. VI, Sec. 29 (no money from Treasury without appropriation) |
| Art. VII, Sec. 41 (public office as public trust) | Art. XI, Sec. 1 (accountability of public officers) |
| Art. IX, Sec. 3 (IP rights) | Art. X, Sec. 20 + Art. XIII (social justice) |
| Art. IX, Sec. 16 (education) | Art. XIV, Secs. 1-5 (education provisions) |
| Art. XII, Secs. 15-16 (block grant) | Art. X, Secs. 17, 20 (tax sharing, minimum powers) |
| Art. XII, Sec. 2 (COA as exclusive auditor) | Art. IX-D (COA's constitutional mandate) |

**Step 3: Note the constitutional constraint.** The constitutional provision does not just authorize — it also limits. Article X, Section 20 lists nine minimum power areas but also states the organic act must be "within the framework of this Constitution." Article XIV guarantees academic freedom — so a BAA on education cannot restrict what universities teach. Record both the authorization and the constraint.

### 5.3.2 When the Trace Reveals a Gap

Sometimes you will find a BOL provision that does not trace neatly to a specific constitutional provision. This does not automatically mean the provision is unconstitutional. Article X, Section 20(9) includes a catch-all: "such other matters as may be authorized by law for the promotion of the general welfare within the region."[^12] This is the constitutional hook for BOL powers that go beyond the eight specific areas listed in Section 20.

However, if the BOL provision conflicts with a specific constitutional prohibition — such as the Bill of Rights or the fiscal provisions of Article VI — the constitutional prohibition controls regardless of the catch-all.

## 5.4 Identifying BOL Delegations to Parliament

The BOL does not resolve every legal question. In many areas, it deliberately delegates subjects to Parliament for further legislation. Recognizing these delegations is critical because they tell you two things: (1) Parliament has authority to legislate on the subject, and (2) the BOL's provisions on the subject are not self-executing — they require implementing legislation.

### 5.4.1 How to Spot a Delegation

Delegation clauses use characteristic language. Look for these phrases:

- "**The Parliament shall enact a law...**" — direct delegation.
- "**...as may be provided by law**" — signals that a future BAA must provide the details.
- "**...as the Parliament may provide**" — same effect.
- "**...shall be defined by the Parliament**" — delegation of definitional authority.
- "**...in accordance with a law to be passed by the Parliament**" — condition precedent.

### 5.4.2 Key Delegations in the BOL

The BOL contains dozens of delegations. Here are the most significant:

| BOL Provision | Subject Delegated | Status |
|---------------|-------------------|--------|
| Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) | Bangsamoro Administrative Code | Enacted (BAA 13) |
| Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) | Bangsamoro Revenue Code | Not yet enacted |
| Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) | Bangsamoro Electoral Code | Enacted (BAA 35) |
| Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) | Bangsamoro Local Governance Code | Enacted (BAA 49) |
| Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) | Bangsamoro Education Code | Enacted (BAA 18) |
| Art. VII, Sec. 39 | Bangsamoro Civil Service Code | Enacted (BAA 17) |
| Art. IX, Sec. 1 | Transitional justice mechanism | Enacted (BAA 89) |
| Art. IX, Sec. 9 | Housing and human settlements agency | Enacted through BAA 13 provisions |
| Art. X, Sec. 4 | Shari'ah laws (personal, family, property) | Pending |
| Art. X, Sec. 17 | Traditional/tribal justice systems law | Pending |
| Art. XIII, Sec. 22 | Inland waters management law | Pending |

When your research identifies a delegation that has been fulfilled by a BAA, your next step is to read that BAA (Tiers 3-4, covered in Chapters 5-6). When the delegation remains unfulfilled, you have identified a legislative gap — the BOL authorizes Parliament to act, but no BAA yet exists on the subject.

## 5.5 Shari'ah Checkpoint for Tiers 1 and 2

At the end of every tier-level research session, apply the Shari'ah Checkpoint. For Tiers 1 and 2, answer these three questions:

**Question 1: Does the constitutional provision invoke or affect religious freedom?**

Article III, Section 5 of the Constitution guarantees free exercise of religion. Article XIV, Section 3(3) allows religious instruction in public schools at the option of parents or guardians. If your research touches religious practice, religious education, or religious institutions, flag these provisions.

**Question 2: Does the BOL provision relate to a subject that intersects with Shari'ah?**

The BOL explicitly recognizes Shari'ah in multiple articles. Article X establishes the Shari'ah court system and grants Parliament power over Shari'ah law on personal, family, and property matters (Sec. 4). Article IV, Section 3 establishes the parliamentary form of government. Article IX, Section 5 guarantees religious freedom within the Bangsamoro context. If your research question falls within any of these areas, note the Shari'ah dimension for deeper analysis in later tiers (the Shari'ah analysis framework is covered in Chapter 9).

**Question 3: Does the subject involve customary law provisions?**

The BOL separately recognizes customary law alongside Shari'ah. Article IV, Section 9 requires the Bangsamoro Government to recognize IP rights. Article V, Section 2(q) enumerates "customary laws" as a Bangsamoro power. Article X, Section 17 calls for laws to promote traditional/tribal justice systems. If your research question involves indigenous communities, land, or disputes, both Shari'ah and customary law dimensions may be present.

Record your Shari'ah Checkpoint findings in your Research Memorandum (Chapter 12 format).

## 5.6 Worked Example: Education Authority

This section traces the Bangsamoro Government's authority over education from the Constitution through the BOL to enacted legislation.

### The Research Question

"What is the legal basis for the Bangsamoro Government's authority over basic education within its territory?"

### Step 1: Constitutional Basis (Tier 1)

Search Article XIV (Education, Science, Technology, Arts, Culture, Sports).

**Article XIV, Section 1:**
> The State shall protect and promote the right of all citizens to quality education at all levels, and shall take appropriate steps to make such education accessible to all.[^13]

This establishes the constitutional duty. It applies to the Bangsamoro Government as part of the Philippine state.

**Article XIV, Section 2(1):**
> The State shall establish, maintain, and support a complete, adequate, and integrated system of education relevant to the needs of the people and society.

**Article XIV, Section 3(2):**
> Educational institutions, other than those established by religious groups and mission boards, shall be owned solely by citizens of the Philippines or corporations or associations at least sixty per centum of the capital of which is owned by such citizens.

**Article X, Section 20(7):**
The organic act must grant the autonomous region power over "educational policies." This is the constitutional delegation that empowers the BOL to assign education authority to BARMM.

### Step 2: BOL Provisions (Tier 2)

**Article V, Section 2(v):** "Education and skills training" is one of the 55 enumerated powers. This is the express grant.

**Article IX, Sections 16-19** provide the substance:

- **Section 16 (Integrated System of Quality Education):** "It shall be a top priority of the Bangsamoro Government to establish, maintain, and support a complete and integrated system of quality education, which shall be a subsystem of the national education system."[^14] Note the phrase "subsystem of the national education system" — it means BARMM's education system operates within, not outside, the national framework. The section also requires conformity with "minimum standards set by the National Government."

- **Section 17 (Vocational, Technical, Nonformal, and Special Education):** Extends coverage to vocational and nonformal education, again conforming to national minimum standards.

- **Section 18 (Madaris Educational System):** Authorizes the Bangsamoro Government to "establish, maintain, and supervise *Madaris* education" in coordination with DepEd, CHED, and TESDA.[^15] This is a uniquely Bangsamoro power — no other region has explicit BOL authority over Madaris education.

- **Section 19 (Tribal University System):** Directs Parliament to create a tribal university system for non-Moro indigenous peoples.

**Article XVI, Section 4(a):** Lists the "Bangsamoro Education Code" among the BTA's priority legislation. This delegation has been fulfilled: Parliament enacted **BAA 18** (Bangsamoro Education Code).

### Step 3: Trace the Chain

Here is the complete authority chain:

| Level | Source | Provision |
|-------|--------|-----------|
| Constitutional duty | Art. XIV, Sec. 1 | State shall protect and promote quality education |
| Constitutional delegation | Art. X, Sec. 20(7) | Organic act shall grant power over educational policies |
| BOL express power | Art. V, Sec. 2(v) | "Education and skills training" enumerated |
| BOL substantive provisions | Art. IX, Secs. 16-19 | Integrated education system, Madaris, tribal university |
| BOL delegation to Parliament | Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a) | Priority: enact Bangsamoro Education Code |
| Enacted legislation (Tier 4) | BAA 18 | Bangsamoro Education Code |

This chain shows how constitutional authorization flows through the organic act to enacted legislation. The same method works for any subject within the 55 enumerated powers.

### Shari'ah Checkpoint

Does the education research involve Shari'ah or customary law? Yes. Article IX, Section 18 covers Madaris education — the Islamic educational system. The Shari'ah dimension is present. Flag it for deeper treatment when researching Tiers 3-4 (specifically BAA 18's provisions on Islamic and Arabic studies).

## 5.7 Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Citing the wrong BOL article number.**
Prevention: check every article citation against the 18-article table in Section 5.1.1. Treat this as a mandatory verification step, not a courtesy check.

**Pitfall 2: Paraphrasing the BOL from memory instead of quoting verbatim.**
Prevention: always work with the BOL text open in front of you. Quote the actual language. If you cannot access the text, mark your citation as "[UNVERIFIED]" and verify before finalizing.

**Pitfall 3: Assuming the general welfare clause creates unlimited authority.**
Prevention: the general welfare clause (Art. V, Sec. 3) extends existing powers — it does not create new ones. Always trace the implied or incidental power back to a specific enumerated power.

**Pitfall 4: Treating all constitutional provisions as self-executing.**
Prevention: distinguish between self-executing provisions (Bill of Rights) and directive principles (social justice aspirations). Only self-executing provisions override BAAs directly. Directive principles require implementing legislation.

**Pitfall 5: Ignoring the conditions attached to BOL grants of authority.**
Prevention: when you find a power grant, read the full section. Look for provisos, conditions, and cross-references to other articles. Many grants come with conditions (consistency with national law, compliance with minimum standards, COA oversight).

**Pitfall 6: Skipping Article XVI when researching BTA-era questions.**
Prevention: if your research involves the transition period, start with Article XVI before looking at Articles V and VII. The BTA's powers are defined through Article XVI's lens.

**Pitfall 7: Citing Art. XII, Sec. 9 for the Block Grant.**
Prevention: Section 9 covers taxing power *limitations*. The Block Grant is in Sections 15-16. Memorize this distinction — it appears in BARMM documents frequently, and it is wrong every time.

## 5.9 Quality Checklist

Before concluding your Tier 1-2 research, verify all of the following:

- [ ] **Constitutional provisions identified.** You have found all constitutional articles relevant to your research question, not just Article X.
- [ ] **Constitutional provisions classified.** Each is marked as self-executing or directive.

- [ ] **Dangerous swaps checked.** You have specifically verified that you have not confused Art. IX/X, Art. V/VI, Art. VII/VIII, Art. VIII/XVI, or Art. XII Sec. 9/Secs. 15-16.
- [ ] **Express authority analyzed.** You have searched Article V, Section 2 for the relevant enumerated power and read the corresponding subject-specific article.
- [ ] **Implied authority assessed.** If express authority is insufficient, you have applied the three-part test from Section 5.2.2.
- [ ] **Conditions and limitations recorded.** You have noted every proviso, consistency requirement, and national law reference attached to the BOL provisions you found.
- [ ] **Constitutional basis traced.** Each BOL provision in your research is linked to its constitutional authorization.
- [ ] **Delegations identified.** You have noted whether the BOL delegates the subject to Parliament and whether that delegation has been fulfilled.

- [ ] **All quotations are verbatim.** No paraphrased BOL or constitutional text appears as a direct quote.
