# Chapter 5 — Researching at Tiers 3 and 4: BAA 13 and Other Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts

Chapter 3 introduced the seven-tier legal hierarchy and the three relationships to national law that determine which tier controls for a given question. Chapter 4 taught you how to research Tiers 1 and 2 — the Constitution and the Bangsamoro Organic Law. This chapter takes you into the heart of Bangsamoro legislation: **Tier 3 (BAA 13, the Bangsamoro Administrative Code)** and **Tier 4 (all other Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts)**. These two tiers contain the operational law that governs daily government work in BARMM.

If the Constitution sets the outer boundary and the BOL defines the playing field, then BAA 13 is the playbook and the other BAAs are the specific plays. Your ministry's mandate, your office's powers, your agency's organizational structure — they live here. Master these two tiers and you can answer the question every government official eventually asks: *"Do we have the authority to do this?"*

## 5.1 Tier 3: BAA 13 — The Bangsamoro Administrative Code

### 5.1.1 What BAA 13 Is

BAA 13, formally titled "An Act Providing for the Bangsamoro Administrative Code and for Other Related Purposes," is the structural law of the Bangsamoro Government. Its purpose, stated in Introductory Provisions Section 2, is "to prescribe the structural, functional and procedural principles and rules of governance of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao."[^1]

Think of BAA 13 as the government's **organizational charter**. It does four things:

1. **Defines the territory, symbols, and foundational principles** of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region (Book I)
2. **Establishes intergovernmental relations** between BARMM, the National Government, and local government units (Book II)
3. **Creates the government structure** — the Parliament, the Wali, the Chief Minister, and the Cabinet (Books III, IV, V)
4. **Organizes every ministry and office** — specifying mandates, powers and functions, and organizational structures (Book VI)

BAA 13 is not just another BAA. It is the **creating law** for most ministries, offices, and agencies in the Bangsamoro Government. When a minister needs to know the legal basis for their ministry's existence, they start with BAA 13. When a researcher needs to determine who has authority over a specific government function, they start with BAA 13.

### 5.1.2 The Seven-Book Structure

BAA 13 is organized into seven Books. Each Book addresses a distinct dimension of governance:

| Book | Title | Content | Key Use for Researchers |
|------|-------|---------|------------------------|
| **I** | Bangsamoro Autonomy and Administration | Territory, symbols, legal principles, public officers, immunity from suit, oaths, reports | Foundational rules — definitions, effectivity of laws, the Bangsamoro Gazette, legal periods |
| **II** | Intergovernmental Relations | IGRB, Council of Leaders, IGR principles, national-regional coordination | Authority boundaries between BARMM and the National Government |
| **III** | The Bangsamoro Autonomous Government | Powers of government, the Wali, the Parliament, the Chief Minister, the judiciary | Separation of powers — who does what at the highest level |
| **IV** | The Office of the Chief Minister | Chief Minister powers, OCM organization, executive offices (BAGO, Senior Minister, Cabinet Secretary), BMSS | Executive authority, appointment power, executive issuances, OCM-attached offices |
| **V** | General Functions of the Cabinet | Cabinet Ministers' collective functions | Cabinet-level decision-making authority |
| **VI** | Ministries and Offices | All 15 ministries plus commissions and bodies — each with Declaration of Policy, Mandate, Powers and Functions, Organizational Structure | **The most-used Book** — where you find institutional mandates and specific powers |
| **VII** | General Provisions | Applicability, effectivity, transitory provisions | Scope and effective date |

**Book VI is your primary research destination.** It contains 20 Titles covering every ministry, commission, and body in the Bangsamoro Government. Each Title follows a consistent internal structure:

- **Chapter 1: General Provisions** — Declaration of Policy, Mandate, Powers and Functions
- **Chapter 2: Ministry Proper** — Office of the Minister, powers of the Minister
- **Additional Chapters** — Bureaus, offices, attached agencies, organizational details

This consistent structure means once you learn how to read one ministry's section, you can navigate all of them.

### 5.1.3 The Mandate and Powers/Functions Distinction

Every ministry section in Book VI contains two critical provisions that researchers must distinguish:

**The Mandate** defines *what the ministry exists to do* — its reason for being, stated in broad terms.

**The Powers and Functions** define *what the ministry is authorized to do* — the specific actions it can take to carry out its mandate.

These are not the same thing. The mandate is the mission statement. The powers and functions are the legal authorities. A ministry can have a broad mandate but limited enumerated powers. When your research question asks "Does Ministry X have authority to do Y?", look at the **Powers and Functions** section, not just the mandate.

**Example: Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE)**

The MENRE mandate (Book VI, Title III, Section 2) states:

> "The Ministry shall be primarily responsible for the exploration, utilization, management, conservation, protection and sustainable development of the region's environment, natural resources and energy sources."[^2]

The powers and functions (Section 3) then list 19 specific authorities, from recommending regional environmental laws (item a) to promulgating rules for controlling pollution (item m) to performing "such other acts as may be necessary, proper or incidental to the attainment of its mandates" (item s).[^3]

If someone asks, "Can MENRE regulate water pollution in Lake Lanao?", the answer comes from the **powers and functions** — specifically items (c) and (m), which authorize promulgating rules governing "activities tending to cause the depletion and degradation of our environment" and "the control of water, air and land pollution within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region." The mandate alone is too general to give a definitive answer.

### 5.1.4 When BAA 13 Creates vs. When a Standalone BAA Creates

BAA 13 is the creating law for most government entities. But not all of them. Some entities were created by standalone BAAs *before* BAA 13 was enacted, and BAA 13 later incorporated references to them. Others were created by standalone BAAs *after* BAA 13 because the entity required more detail than a section within an administrative code could provide.

Here is how to tell the difference:

| Entity | Creating Law | BAA 13 Role |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) | **BAA 13**, Book VI, Title I | Creating law — mandate and powers defined here |
| Ministry of Finance, Budget and Management (MFBM) | **BAA 13**, Book VI, Title IV | Creating law |
| All 15 Ministries | **BAA 13**, Book VI, Titles I-XV | Creating law for each |
| Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (BHRC) | **BAA 4** | BAA 13 references BAA 4 in Book VI, Title XVII, Chapter 1, Sec. 2 |
| Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office (BAGO) | **BAA 5** | BAA 13 provides detail in Book IV, Title II, Sec. 10-24, but references BAA 5 as the creating law |
| Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC) | **BAA 8** | BAA 13 provides detail in Book VI, Title XVI |
| Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC) | **BAA 10** | BAA 13 references BYC in Book VI, Title XVII |
| Bangsamoro Sports Commission (BSC) | **BAA 12** | BAA 13 references BSC in Book VI, Title XVII |
| Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BSDB) | **BAA 31** | Created after BAA 13; not in the original administrative code |
| Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission | **BAA 83** | Created after BAA 13 |
| Bangsamoro Transitional Justice Commission | **BAA 89** | Created after BAA 13 |

**The research rule:** Always check **both** BAA 13 and any standalone creating BAA. BAA 13 may provide the organizational structure while the standalone BAA provides additional powers, funding provisions, or operational details not found in the administrative code. When the two conflict, apply the **lex specialis** rule (Section 5.2.5): the specific standalone BAA prevails over the general administrative code for matters it specifically addresses.

### 5.1.5 How to Search BAA 13 Systematically

BAA 13 is a large document — over 5,000 lines of text covering the entire government structure. You cannot skim it and expect to find what you need. Use this systematic approach:

**Step 1: Identify the relevant Book.**

| Your Question Concerns... | Start With... |
|---------------------------|--------------|
| Territory, symbols, legal definitions, public officers | Book I |
| BARMM-National Government relations, IGR bodies | Book II |
| Parliament powers, Wali, Chief Minister, judiciary | Book III |
| Chief Minister powers, OCM offices, executive issuances | Book IV |
| Cabinet functions | Book V |
| A specific ministry's mandate, powers, or structure | Book VI |
| General applicability and effectivity | Book VII |

**Step 2: Identify the relevant Title within the Book.**

For Book VI (your most common destination), the 20 Titles correspond to specific ministries and bodies:

| Title | Ministry/Body |
|-------|--------------|
| I | Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR) |
| II | Basic, Higher, and Technical Education (MBHTE) |
| III | Environment, Natural Resources and Energy (MENRE) |
| IV | Finance, Budget and Management (MFBM) |
| V | Health (MOH) |
| VI | Human Settlements and Development (MHSD) |
| VII | Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIPA) |
| VIII | Interior and Local Government (MILG) |
| IX | Labor and Employment (MOLE) |
| X | Public Order and Safety (MPOS) |
| XI | Public Works (MOPW) |
| XII | Science and Technology (MOST) |
| XIII | Social Services and Development (MSSD) |
| XIV | Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT) |
| XV | Transportation and Communications (MTC) |
| XVI | Bangsamoro Commissions (BWC and others) |
| XVII | Other Commissions and Bodies (BHRC, BYC, BSC) |
| XVIII | Administrative Services |
| XIX | National Government Agencies in the BARMM |
| XX | Government-Owned or -Controlled Corporations |

**Step 3: Within the Title, locate the specific provision.**

For ministry research, follow this order:

1. **Section 1 (Declaration of Policy)** — Read first for context. What policy goals does the ministry serve?
2. **Section 2 (Mandate)** — The ministry's mission. What is it created to do?
3. **Section 3 (Powers and Functions)** — The specific legal authorities. **This is where most research answers live.**
4. **Organizational Structure sections** — Who within the ministry handles the function you are researching?

**Step 4: Cross-reference with other Books.**

Your research rarely ends within a single Title. After finding the ministry-specific provisions:

- Check **Book IV** if the function involves the Chief Minister's power (appointment, executive orders, eminent domain)
- Check **Book II** if the function involves coordination with the National Government
- Check **Book III** if the function involves Parliament's legislative authority or the judicial system
- Check **Book I** for definitions, legal principles, and procedural rules that apply across all ministries

### 5.1.6 BAA 13 Amendments

BAA 13 has been amended once: **BAA 37** changed Book I, Chapter 2, Section 9 to establish the seat of government in Parang, Maguindanao del Norte (the original provision placed it in Cotabato City "unless otherwise provided by the Bangsamoro Parliament in a subsequent law").[^4]

Always check whether a provision you cite from BAA 13 has been amended. As of February 2026, BAA 37 is the only amendment — but more may follow as the Bangsamoro Government matures and the administrative code evolves.

## 5.2 Tier 4: Other Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts

### 5.2.1 The BAA Archive as a Searchable Corpus

As of February 2026, the Bangsamoro Parliament has enacted **89 BAAs**.[^5] These form the Bangsamoro legislative archive — the body of enacted law that governs everything from the official flag (BAA 1) to transitional justice and reconciliation (BAA 89).

Five BAA numbers — 56, 66, 67, 68, and 69 — have no titles on public record as of this writing. The remaining 84 BAAs with known titles cover 13 domain categories. Understanding these categories is your first step toward efficient research.

### 5.2.2 Categories of BAAs

Not all BAAs serve the same legislative purpose. Sort them into six functional categories to guide your research:

#### Category 1: Creating/Charter BAAs

These BAAs **establish new government entities** — commissions, boards, offices, and institutions not created by BAA 13. Each creating BAA typically defines the entity's mandate, powers and functions, composition, and funding.

| 5 | Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office (BAGO) |
| 6 | Bangsamoro Socio-Economic Development Planning System |
| 8 | Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC) |
| 10 | Bangsamoro Youth Commission (BYC) |
| 12 | Bangsamoro Sports Commission (BSC) |
| 31 | Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BSDB) |
| 40 | Bangsamoro Science High School System |
| 50 | Bangsamoro Kulliyyah for Islamic Studies |
| 57 | Office of the Bangsamoro Mujahideen |
| 59 | Bangsamoro Agriculture and Fisheries Training Institute |
| 83 | Bangsamoro Nutrition Commission |
| 89 | Bangsamoro Transitional Justice and Reconciliation Program |

**Research tip:** When researching an entity's authority, check both its creating BAA and its section in BAA 13. The creating BAA usually provides more detailed powers and operational provisions. BAA 13 provides the organizational framework and interrelationships.

#### Category 2: Comprehensive Code BAAs

These BAAs establish **complete regulatory frameworks** for entire sectors. They are the most substantive pieces of Bangsamoro legislation — often running dozens or hundreds of sections — and typically define rights, duties, penalties, and administrative procedures.

| BAA | Code |
|-----|------|
| 13 | Bangsamoro Administrative Code |
| 17 | Bangsamoro Civil Service Code |
| 18 | Bangsamoro Education Code |
| 35 | Bangsamoro Electoral Code |
| 49 | Bangsamoro Local Governance Code |
| 82 | Bangsamoro Labor and Employment Code |
| 84 | Bangsamoro Budget System Act |

These are the BAAs you will consult most often. Each represents a sector where Parliament has exercised comprehensive legislative authority. If your research question falls within one of these sectors, the relevant code is your primary source — not national law (unless the BOL requires consistency, as with civil service under Article VII, Section 39).

**What has NOT been enacted:** As of February 2026, the Bangsamoro Parliament has **not** enacted a Revenue Code, an Environment Code, a Procurement Code, a Health Code, or a Housing Code. These are legislative gaps — areas where the BOL grants power but Parliament has not yet exercised it comprehensively. Identifying these gaps is itself a research finding. When you discover that no comprehensive code exists for your research topic, document this as a gap and identify the applicable national laws that operate suppletorily in the absence of Bangsamoro legislation.

These BAAs address **specific issues within a sector** without creating a comprehensive code. They expand mandates, fill gaps, or address specific needs.

### 5.2.5 Determining Whether a BAA Is Still Good Law

A BAA may have been amended, superseded, or struck down. Before citing any BAA as authority, verify its status using this three-step process:

**Step 1: Check the Amendment Chain.**

Scan the full BAA index for any later BAA that expressly amends your target BAA. The following amendment relationships exist as of February 2026:

| Original BAA | Amending BAA | What Changed |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| BAA 3 (GAA 2020) | BAA 14 | Extended fund availability |
| BAA 13 (Admin Code) | BAA 37 | Changed seat of government |
| BAA 23 (GAA 2022) | BAA 33 | Extended fund availability |
| BAA 32 (GAA 2023) | BAA 52 | Extended fund availability |
| BAA 35 (Electoral Code) | BAA 87, BAA 88 | Removed NOTA; other amendments |
| BAA 54 (Datu Sinsuat Balabaran) | BAA 70 | Amended Section 5 |
| BAA 55 (Sheik Abas Hamza) | BAA 71 | Amended Section 5 |
| BAA 58 (Parliamentary Districts) | BAA 77 | Reconstituted districts |
| MMAA 17 (Muslim Holidays) | BAA 39 | **Repealed** MMAA 17 entirely |
| MMAA 164 (Madrasah Grad Ed) | BAA 50 | **Repealed** MMAA 164 entirely |

**Step 2: Check for judicial invalidation.**

The Supreme Court can strike down a BAA for unconstitutionality or for exceeding the BOL's grant of authority. As of February 2026, the most significant judicial action concerns BAA 58 and BAA 77, both invalidated in October 2025. BAA 86 was enacted as the replacement.

Check Supreme Court decisions and Philippine legal databases (LawPhil, Supreme Court E-Library) for any ruling affecting the BAA you are citing.

**Step 3: Check for implied repeal.**

A later BAA may not expressly repeal an earlier one but may cover the same subject so comprehensively that the earlier BAA is impliedly superseded. Implied repeal is disfavored in statutory construction — courts presume both laws can coexist unless they are irreconcilably conflicting. But if the later BAA contains an "all inconsistent laws are hereby repealed" clause, provisions of the earlier BAA that conflict with the later one are superseded.

### 5.2.6 Two Interpretive Rules for Resolving BAA Conflicts

When two BAAs appear to conflict, two rules of statutory construction determine which prevails:

**Lex posterior derogat legi priori** — the later law prevails over the earlier law.

When Parliament enacts a BAA that is inconsistent with an earlier BAA on the same subject, the later BAA prevails. This is the standard rule for legislative succession.

**Example:** BAA 58 created parliamentary districts. BAA 77 amended BAA 58 by reconstituting those districts. BAA 77, as the later law, prevailed over BAA 58's original district configuration. (Both were then struck down by the Supreme Court, and BAA 86 replaced them — demonstrating that lex posterior applies in sequence.)

**Lex specialis derogat legi generali** — the specific law prevails over the general law.

When a general BAA (like BAA 13, the Administrative Code) and a specific BAA (like BAA 5, creating BAGO) address the same subject, the specific BAA prevails on matters it specifically addresses. The general BAA still applies to matters the specific BAA does not cover.

**Example:** BAA 13, Book IV, Title II, Sections 10-24 describe the Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office in detail. BAA 5 is the standalone creating law for BAGO. If BAA 5 provides a power or function that BAA 13 does not mention, BAA 5 governs. If BAA 13 provides administrative detail not found in BAA 5, BAA 13 supplements. If the two conflict on a specific point, BAA 5 — as the specific law — prevails.

**Combined application:** When the two rules point in the same direction (the later law is also the more specific law), the answer is clear. When they point in opposite directions (the later law is the general one, and the earlier law is the specific one), courts generally favor lex specialis — the specific law prevails — unless the later general law contains language clearly intended to supersede the earlier specific provision.

## 5.3 The BAA Research Process

### 5.3.1 Start With the Question, Not With BAA 13

A common mistake is opening BAA 13 and scrolling until something looks relevant. This is slow and unreliable. Instead, start with your research question and use it to navigate directly to the right provisions.

**From Chapter 2's methodology, you already have:**
- A framed research question (Step 1 — Issue Analysis)
- An understanding of the legal landscape (Step 2 — Landscape Review)
- The correct tier identified through the national law relationship test (Chapter 3)

If the national law relationship test confirms you are in **enumerated Bangsamoro power** territory (no national law consistency required), your systematic source search at Tiers 3 and 4 follows this sequence:

### 5.3.2 The Five-Point BAA Search Sequence

**Point 1: BAA 13 — Find the relevant ministry.**

Identify which ministry holds the mandate for your research subject. Use the Book VI Title index (Section 5.1.5 above). Read the ministry's Declaration of Policy, Mandate, and Powers and Functions in that order.

**Point 2: BAA 13 — Cross-reference with other Books.**

After identifying the ministry, check whether your question also touches:
- The Chief Minister's executive authority (Book IV)
- Intergovernmental coordination (Book II)
- Parliamentary legislative authority (Book III)
- Administrative services or civil service rules (Book VI, Titles XVIII-XIX)
- Government corporations (Book VI, Title XX)

**Point 3: Check for a standalone creating BAA.**

Using the creating/charter BAA table (Section 5.2.2, Category 1), determine whether the relevant entity was created by a standalone BAA. If so, read that BAA for additional powers and operational provisions.

**Point 4: Check for a comprehensive code BAA.**

Using the code BAA table (Section 5.2.2, Category 2), determine whether Parliament has enacted a comprehensive code on your subject. If so, the code is your primary source — not BAA 13's summary provisions.

**Point 5: Search for sector-specific BAAs.**

Scan the full BAA index for any BAA that addresses your specific topic. Use the category tables in Section 5.2.2 as your guide, but also do a keyword search of the full index — a BAA's relevance may not be obvious from its category alone.

### 5.3.3 Documenting Your BAA Research

As you work through the five-point sequence, build a **BAA Research Tracker** — a table that records which BAAs you have checked, what you found, and whether the provision is current:

| BAA | Relevant? | Provision(s) | What It Says | Current? | Notes |

| 31 | Yes | Sections 1-5 | Creates Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board | Yes | Board, not a code — limited to sustainable development planning |
| 35 | No | — | Electoral Code — not relevant to environmental research | — | Skipped |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

This tracker becomes part of your Research Memorandum (Chapter 9). It documents your diligence and ensures no BAA is overlooked.

## 5.4 Shari'ah Checkpoint

Before concluding your Tier 3 and 4 research, ask these questions:

**Does BAA 13 reference Shari'ah in the relevant ministry's provisions?**

Several BAA 13 provisions incorporate Shari'ah considerations. Book IV, Title II, Section 17(3)(d) requires the BAGO's Legal Research and Opinion Division to "formulate and review policies on Shari'ah matters to ensure compliance with Shari'ah rules and principles."[^6] Book I, Chapter 5, Section 17 defines public office as a "sacred public trust" and requires public officers to "serve as 'khalifa'" — a concept rooted in Islamic governance.[^7]

**Does any relevant BAA invoke Shari'ah explicitly?**

Some comprehensive code BAAs include Shari'ah provisions. Search for the terms "Shari'ah," "Islamic," "Halal," "khalifa," and "moral governance" within any BAA you cite.

**Does the Bangsamoro Darul Ifta' have a relevant ruling?**

BAA 13, Book I, Chapter 3, Section 14 provides that the Bangsamoro Information Office shall translate relevant documents in Arabic "in coordination with the Bangsamoro Darul Ifta'."[^8] The Darul Ifta' issues fatwas (religious rulings) that may bear on your research topic. These are not BAAs, but they carry persuasive authority within the Bangsamoro governance framework.

**Does your research subject involve moral governance?**

BAA 13's Introductory Provisions, Section 3(j) commits the Bangsamoro Government to "maintain honesty, integrity, transparency and accountability" and take "positive and effective measures against graft and corruption."[^9] The Oath of Moral Governance in Book I, Chapter 7, Section 24 is mandatory for all public officers.[^10] If your research touches procurement, contracting, personnel management, or any area susceptible to corruption concerns, the moral governance framework is a relevant overlay.

## 5.5 Worked Example: Researching Environmental Regulation Authority in BARMM

A policy advisor asks: *"Does the Bangsamoro Government have authority to enact comprehensive environmental regulations? What legal authority exists now, and what gaps remain?"*

### Step 1: Frame the Question

Using Chapter 2's three-part frame:
- **Subject:** Environmental regulation
- **Jurisdiction:** BARMM (checking national law applicability)
- **Action:** Determine whether comprehensive regulatory authority exists and identify gaps

### Step 2: National Law Relationship (Chapter 3)

Check BOL Article V, Section 2. Item (x) lists: "Environment, parks, forest management, wildlife, and nature reserves conservation."[^11] This is an **enumerated Bangsamoro power with no national law consistency requirement**. BAAs are the controlling authority.

### Step 3: BAA 13 — Find the Relevant Ministry (Point 1)

Open Book VI. Title III is "Environment, Natural Resources and Energy." Read:

- **Section 1 (Declaration of Policies):** Three policy pillars — natural resource development, renewable energy, and forest/coastal conservation.[^12]
- **Section 2 (Mandate):** MENRE is "primarily responsible for the exploration, utilization, management, conservation, protection and sustainable development of the region's environment, natural resources and energy sources."[^13]
- **Section 3 (Powers and Functions):** 19 specific powers, including recommending environmental laws, promulgating rules on pollution control, regulating natural resource development, and formulating conservation strategies.[^14]

**Finding:** MENRE has the institutional mandate and 19 enumerated powers covering environmental regulation. BAA 13 establishes the ministry's authority but does not itself constitute a comprehensive environment code.

### Step 4: Cross-Reference with Other Books (Point 2)

- **Book II, Section 2(f):** The Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board is listed as an IGR body.[^15]
- **Book IV:** No specific environmental provisions in the Chief Minister's powers.

### Step 5: Check for Standalone Creating BAAs (Point 3)

- **BAA 31** creates the Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board (BSDB). This is a **board** — a coordinating and advisory body, not a regulatory agency. It does not constitute an environment code or a comprehensive regulatory framework. It addresses sustainable development planning, not environmental law enforcement.[^16]

### Step 6: Check for Comprehensive Code BAAs (Point 4)

Search the BAA index for any BAA titled "Environment Code," "Environmental Protection," or similar. **Result: None found.** No comprehensive environment code has been enacted as of February 2026.

### Step 7: Search for Sector-Specific BAAs (Point 5)

Scan the full 89-BAA index for any BAA touching environmental subjects:

- **BAA 6** (Socio-Economic Development Planning System) — touches environment only as part of broader planning
- **BAA 31** (Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board) — already checked
- **BAA 60, 61** (memorial sites) — not environmental regulation

**Finding:** No sector-specific environment BAA exists.

### Step 8: Check National Laws Applying Suppletorily

Since no comprehensive Bangsamoro environment code exists, national environmental laws apply suppletorily within BARMM:

- **RA 9729** (Climate Change Act) and its IRR
- **RA 9003** (Ecological Solid Waste Management Act)
- **RA 9275** (Philippine Clean Water Act)
- **RA 8749** (Philippine Clean Air Act)
- **PD 1586** (Environmental Impact Statement System)

These national laws operate in the absence of Bangsamoro environmental legislation but would yield to a future BAA on subjects within the BOL's enumerated powers.

### Step 9: Shari'ah Checkpoint

Islamic stewardship (*khalifah*) principles support environmental protection. The concept of humanity as trustee of the earth is consistent with MENRE's conservation mandate. No specific Darul Ifta' ruling on environmental regulation was found, but the principle strengthens the policy basis for comprehensive legislation.

### Research Conclusion

> **Authority exists but has not been fully exercised.** The BOL grants enumerated power over environment and natural resources (Art. V, Sec. 2(x)), with no national law consistency requirement. BAA 13 establishes MENRE with a mandate and 19 enumerated powers. BAA 31 creates an advisory board for sustainable development. But **no comprehensive Bangsamoro Environment Code has been enacted**. This is a legislative gap. National environmental laws apply suppletorily. The Bangsamoro Parliament has the authority to enact a comprehensive environment code — it has simply not yet done so.

## 5.6 Researching Bills and Proposed Resolutions

Your research at Tiers 3 and 4 should not stop at enacted BAAs. The legislative landscape includes **filed bills and proposed resolutions** that have not yet been enacted. These are not law, but they are research-relevant for three reasons:

1. **They signal legislative intent.** A filed bill tells you that Parliament is considering acting on a subject. If your research identifies a gap (like the environment code gap above), a filed bill may show that the gap is about to be filled.

2. **They may affect your timeline.** If you are drafting a policy that relies on a legislative gap (e.g., applying national law because no BAA exists), a pending bill may close that gap and change your legal analysis.

3. **They provide comparative text.** A filed bill's provisions show how Parliament's committees are thinking about a subject. This is useful context even if the bill has not been enacted.

**Where to find bills and resolutions:**

- The Bangsamoro Parliament's official records (Committee Secretariat)
- The Bill Drafting Guidebook, which tracks the legislative agenda
- Published indexes of filed bills (e.g., the Parliament's website, if available)

**How to document them:** In your Research Memorandum, list pending legislation in a separate section labeled "Pending Legislation" — distinct from enacted law. Note the bill number, title, author, committee referral, and status (filed, in committee, plenary, etc.). Never cite a pending bill as enacted law.

## 5.7 Common Pitfalls

### Pitfall 1: Confusing BAA Numbers

BAA numbers are sequential, not topical. BAA 25 is not the Education Code — it upgrades the Datu Blah Sinsuat District Hospital. BAA 60 is not BEZA — it declares a memorial site. BAA 11 is not the Education Code — it defines the power of appointment.

**Prevention:** Always verify BAA numbers against the official index. Never assume a BAA's content from its number alone. The BAA Quick-Reference (categorized by domain) is faster than the sequential index for topical searches.

### Pitfall 2: Citing BAA 13 Without Checking for a Standalone BAA

BAA 13 provides summary provisions for entities that have their own creating laws. If you cite BAA 13's description of BAGO without also checking BAA 5, you may miss critical powers or functions that only BAA 5 provides.

**Prevention:** For any entity referenced in BAA 13, check whether a standalone creating BAA exists. The table in Section 5.1.4 is your quick reference.

### Pitfall 3: Assuming a BAA Is Still Good Law

BAAs can be amended (BAA 37 amending BAA 13), superseded (BAA 86 replacing BAA 58/77), or struck down (Supreme Court invalidating BAA 58 and BAA 77). Citing an invalidated BAA as authority is a research failure.

**Prevention:** Run the three-step verification (Section 5.2.5) for every BAA you cite: check amendments, check judicial decisions, check for implied repeal.

### Pitfall 4: Treating the Mandate as Sufficient Legal Authority

A ministry's mandate in BAA 13 says *what it exists to do*. The powers and functions say *what it can do*. Citing only the mandate to justify a specific action is legally insufficient — the action must fall within one of the enumerated powers or be necessarily implied from them.

**Prevention:** Always cite the specific power or function, not just the mandate, when answering questions about institutional authority.

### Pitfall 5: Missing Legislative Gaps

Not finding a BAA on your subject is itself a finding. If the BOL grants power but no BAA has been enacted, you have identified a **legislative gap**. Document it. National law applies suppletorily in the gap. Your research memorandum should state: "No BAA has been enacted on this subject as of [date]. National law applies."

**Prevention:** Treat absence of legislation as a conclusion, not as a failure to find something.

### Pitfall 6: Ignoring Appropriations BAAs

The GAA's General Provisions section creates binding rules for the fiscal year — spending limits, earmarks, restrictions on fund transfers. If your research concerns government spending or program funding, the latest GAA is a legal source, not just a budget document.

**Prevention:** When your research touches any financial question, add the current GAA (BAA 85 for FY 2026) and the Budget System Act (BAA 84) to your BAA Research Tracker.

### Pitfall 7: Conflating BAAs with National Laws That Sound Similar

BAA 17 is the Bangsamoro Civil Service Code. It is not the same as the national Civil Service law (EO 292, Book V). BAA 82 is the Bangsamoro Labor and Employment Code. It is not the same as the national Labor Code (PD 442). These operate in parallel, and the BOL determines which prevails when they conflict.

**Prevention:** In your Research Memorandum, always specify whether you are citing a BAA or a national law. Use the full formal title on first reference.

## Key Takeaways

1. **BAA 13 is your first stop for institutional authority.** It creates most ministries and offices and defines their mandates and powers. Always start here.

2. **Distinguish mandate from powers and functions.** The mandate says why the entity exists. The powers and functions say what it can legally do. Cite the powers, not just the mandate.

3. **Always check for standalone creating BAAs.** BAA 13 is the general framework; standalone BAAs provide specific detail. Use both.

4. **Categorize BAAs before searching.** Knowing the six categories (creating, codes, sector-specific, appropriations, municipalities, symbols) helps you navigate 89 BAAs efficiently.

5. **Verify every BAA is current.** Check for amendments, judicial invalidation, and implied repeal before citing any BAA as authority.

6. **Document legislative gaps.** Not finding a BAA is itself a finding. The absence of comprehensive legislation on a subject within the BOL's enumerated powers is a gap worth reporting.

7. **Lex posterior and lex specialis resolve conflicts.** Later law prevails over earlier law. Specific law prevails over general law. When the two rules conflict, specific law generally wins.
