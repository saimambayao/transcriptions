# Chapter 7 — Researching Shari'ah and Customary Law

Every chapter in this guidebook has ended with a Shari'ah Checkpoint — a single question: *Does this research question have a Shari'ah dimension?* If you answered "yes" at any checkpoint, this chapter tells you what to do next.

Shari'ah is not a niche topic that only affects family law disputes. It is a parallel legal dimension woven into the constitutional fabric of the Bangsamoro. The BOL dedicates 20 sections across Article X to the justice system, Shari'ah courts, traditional law, and alternative dispute resolution. PD 1083 — the Code of Muslim Personal Laws, enacted in 1977 — remains the primary codification of Muslim personal law in the Philippines. The Shari'ah court system forms part of the Philippine judicial system under Supreme Court supervision. Any legal researcher working in BARMM will encounter Shari'ah questions, whether the research involves inheritance, family property, commercial contracts between Muslims, or the constitutional limits of Parliament's legislative power over Islamic law.

This is the only standalone chapter on Shari'ah research methodology in any BARMM guidebook. The Bill Drafting Guidebook addresses Shari'ah for drafters — how to write legislation consistent with Islamic law. This chapter addresses a different question: **how to research Shari'ah legal questions systematically**, using the same five-step methodology from Chapter 2 while navigating the unique features of Islamic legal sources.

After completing this chapter, you will be able to:

5. **Use** the Maqasid al-Shari'ah framework to assess whether proposed legislation aligns with Islamic objectives
6. **Research** traditional and tribal law questions involving indigenous peoples within BARMM
7. **Locate** Shari'ah legal sources — from local codifications to international Islamic law standards

## 7.2 Shari'ah as a Parallel Legal Dimension

Chapter 3 introduced the seven-tier legal hierarchy and the three relationships to national law. Shari'ah does not sit at any single tier. It cuts across all of them.

**Tier 1 — Constitution.** Article X, Section 15 of the 1987 Constitution recognizes regional autonomy, including the power to establish justice systems appropriate to the cultural heritage of autonomous regions. Article X, Section 20 grants autonomous regions legislative powers "subject to the provisions of this Constitution and national laws."[^1]

**Tier 2 — BOL.** Article X of RA 11054 dedicates 20 sections to the Bangsamoro justice system. Sections 1-16 cover Shari'ah courts, their jurisdiction, sources of law, qualifications of judges, and the power of Parliament to legislate. Sections 17-18 cover traditional and tribal justice. Sections 19-20 cover regular courts and alternative dispute resolution.[^2]

**Tier 3 — BAA 13.** The Bangsamoro Administrative Code establishes the institutional framework within which Shari'ah-related agencies operate, including the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta.

**Tier 4 — Other BAAs.** Parliament may enact BAAs that codify Shari'ah provisions — on Islamic finance, halal regulation, waqf (endowment), zakat (alms), or family law matters not covered by PD 1083. As of March 2026, no comprehensive Shari'ah-specific BAA has been enacted, but the legislative authority exists under Article X, Section 4.

**Tier 5 — National law.** Presidential Decree No. 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws, 1977) is the existing national codification of Muslim personal law. It covers marriage, divorce, inheritance, family relations, and property rights for Muslims. The BOL explicitly references PD 1083 in multiple provisions, and Article X, Section 4 grants Parliament power to legislate on matters "*not provided for*" under PD 1083.[^3] This means PD 1083 continues to apply for subjects it covers.

**Tier 6 — Executive issuances.** Ministerial circulars and administrative orders may implement Shari'ah-related provisions of existing law.

**Tier 7 — Jurisprudence.** Supreme Court decisions define the boundaries of Shari'ah court jurisdiction. Because all Shari'ah courts are part of the Philippine judicial system, their decisions are reviewable by the Supreme Court on questions of law.[^4]

**The practical implication:** When your research question has a Shari'ah dimension, you do not abandon the seven-tier methodology. You apply it — but you add a Shari'ah column to your search at every tier. The hierarchy still determines which source prevails. The national law relationship analysis still applies. The difference is that you are now tracking an additional legal stream that runs parallel to the secular one.

## 7.3 BOL Article X: The Constitutional Framework

Article X of the BOL is titled "The Justice System in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region" (Sections 1-16 cover Shari'ah; Sections 17-18 cover tribal justice; Sections 19-20 cover regular courts and ADR). This is your primary source for Shari'ah research. Know it thoroughly.

### 7.3.1 Sources of Shari'ah

Section 3 defines the sources of Shari'ah recognized by the BOL:[^5]

**Principal Sources:**
- **(a) Al-Qur'an** — the holy book of Islam; the primary and supreme source
- **(b) Al-Sunnah** — the traditions of Prophet Muhammad (SAW); the second primary source

**Secondary Sources:**
- **(a) Al-Ijma** — consensus of Islamic scholars on a legal question
- **(b) Al-Qiyas** — reasoning by analogy from established principles to new situations

This hierarchy of sources matters for research. When assessing whether a proposed BAA is consistent with Shari'ah, you check the principal sources first. If the Qur'an and Sunnah address the subject directly, that settles the question. If they do not address it directly, you look to Ijma and Qiyas — scholarly consensus and analogical reasoning.

**A critical limitation for researchers:** The BOL recognizes these sources but does not codify their content. The Qur'an is a religious text, not a statute. Applying it to a legal question requires Islamic jurisprudential expertise (*fiqh*). The legal researcher's role is to identify when Shari'ah applies, map the jurisdictional framework, and flag questions that require consultation with Islamic legal scholars — not to independently interpret Quranic provisions. When your research reaches a point where Shari'ah interpretation is needed, document the question and recommend referral to the **Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta (BDI)**, the region's Islamic religious authority for juristic opinions (*fatawa*).

### 7.3.2 Scope: Exclusively Muslim

Section 1 of Article X states a clear boundary:[^6]

> *Shari'ah shall apply exclusively to cases involving Muslims. Where a case involves a non-Muslim, Shari'ah law may apply only if the non-Muslim voluntarily submits to the jurisdiction of the Shari'ah court.*

This is a **hard limit**. The researcher must confirm two elements:

1. **Are the parties Muslim?** If all parties are Muslim, Shari'ah court jurisdiction may apply (depending on the subject matter). If no party is Muslim, Shari'ah does not apply.
2. **If a non-Muslim is involved, has that party voluntarily submitted?** The non-Muslim must affirmatively consent. There is no deemed submission, no constructive submission. The consent must be voluntary.

Section 1 also protects the rights of non-Muslims: "The provisions of this Article shall not prejudice the rights of non-Muslims and nonindigenous peoples."[^7]

**Research implication:** Before diving into Shari'ah sources, confirm that the parties or subjects of your research question are Muslims (or that a non-Muslim has voluntarily submitted). If neither condition is met, Shari'ah does not apply to the question, and your research stays within the secular framework of Chapters 3-6.

### 7.3.3 The Three-Tiered Court System

The BOL establishes three levels of Shari'ah courts, each with defined jurisdiction. All three form part of the Philippine judicial system under Supreme Court supervision.[^8]

**Shari'ah Circuit Courts** (Section 5) — the courts of first instance for most Shari'ah matters. They handle personal law disputes (marriage, divorce, dower, property, maintenance), minor offenses, communal property disputes, and civil actions within specified monetary thresholds.

**Shari'ah District Courts** (Section 6) — the intermediate courts. They handle custody, guardianship, legitimacy, estate settlement, corrections to Muslim Registries, customary and Shari'ah contracts, higher-value civil actions, and exercise appellate jurisdiction over Circuit Court decisions. They also exercise special powers: mandamus, prohibition, injunction, certiorari, and habeas corpus.[^9]

**Shari'ah High Court** (Section 7) — the appellate court for the entire system. Composed of five justices including the Presiding Justice (Section 9). It exercises exclusive appellate jurisdiction over all District Court cases and exclusive original jurisdiction over annulment of District Court judgments and auxiliary writs. Its decisions are **final and executory** — except on questions of law, which may be elevated to the Supreme Court.[^10]

The hierarchy of review:

```
Shari'ah Circuit Court
        ↓ (appeal)
Shari'ah District Court
        ↓ (appeal)
Shari'ah High Court
        ↓ (only on questions of law)
Supreme Court of the Philippines
```

**Pending the complete organization of the Shari'ah High Court**, the BOL provides that District Court decisions shall be appealable to the Court of Appeals.[^11] The researcher must verify the operational status of the Shari'ah High Court when assessing the current appellate pathway.

### How to Use the Matrix

**Step 1:** Identify the **subject matter** of the dispute or case in your research question.

**Step 2:** Match it against the exclusive original jurisdiction column. Start at the Circuit Court level. If the subject appears there, that is the court of first instance. If not, check the District Court level.

**Step 3:** For **civil actions**, check the monetary thresholds. Real property cases split at P400,000 (assessed value). Other civil actions split at P200,000 (demand or claim).

**Step 4:** For **criminal matters**, confirm the penalty level. Shari'ah courts have criminal jurisdiction ONLY over offenses punishable by *arresto menor* (imprisonment of one day to thirty days) or the *ta'zir* equivalent. This is a hard limit — Section 4 restricts criminal jurisdiction to "minor offenses punishable by *arresto menor* or *ta'zir* which must be equivalent to *arresto menor* or fines commensurate to the offense."[^12]

**Step 5:** Identify the **appellate pathway**. Circuit Court decisions go to the District Court. District Court decisions go to the High Court. High Court decisions are final — except on questions of law, which go to the Supreme Court.

## 7.5 PD 1083 and the BOL

Presidential Decree No. 1083, the Code of Muslim Personal Laws of the Philippines, was enacted on February 4, 1977 — more than four decades before the BOL. It remains the foundational codification of Muslim personal law in the Philippine legal system. Understanding the relationship between PD 1083 and the BOL is essential for Shari'ah research.

### The Continuing Force of PD 1083

The BOL references PD 1083 repeatedly throughout Article X:

- **Section 5(a):** Circuit Courts have jurisdiction over "offenses defined and punished under Presidential Decree No. 1083"[^13]
- **Section 5(b):** Circuit Courts handle disputes involving parties "married in accordance with Article 13 of Presidential Decree No. 1083"
- **Section 6(a):** District Courts handle cases "arising under Presidential Decree No. 1083"
- **Section 6(c):** District Courts handle Muslim Registry corrections "mentioned in Title VI of Book Two of Presidential Decree No. 1083"

These references confirm that PD 1083 is not superseded by the BOL. It continues to operate as the substantive law governing Muslim personal relations. The BOL builds upon it.

### Parliament's Supplementary Power

Section 4 grants the Parliament power to legislate in two directions:[^14]

1. **Personal, family, and property law** — Parliament can enact laws in these areas (subject to the Constitution and existing law)
2. **Commercial and civil actions not provided for under PD 1083** — Parliament can fill gaps that PD 1083 does not cover

The key phrase is "not provided for." Where PD 1083 already covers a subject, it remains the governing law. Parliament's power is supplementary — it addresses what PD 1083 left out, not what it already covers.

### When PD 1083 and a Future BAA Overlap

If Parliament enacts a BAA that covers the same subject as PD 1083, three scenarios arise:

**Scenario 1: The BAA supplements PD 1083.** The BAA addresses a gap — a subject PD 1083 does not cover. Both laws coexist. Example: If Parliament enacts an Islamic finance BAA, it covers territory PD 1083 never addressed. No conflict.

**Scenario 2: The BAA is consistent with PD 1083 but more detailed.** The BAA provides additional procedural or substantive rules that do not contradict PD 1083. Both laws apply, with the BAA providing the additional detail. The national law relationship test determines whether the subject requires national law consistency or not.

**Scenario 3: The BAA contradicts PD 1083.** This is the hard case. PD 1083 is a national law (Tier 5). The BAA is a Bangsamoro law (Tier 4). The national law relationship test from Chapter 3 determines which prevails. If the subject is within the 55 enumerated powers and the BOL does not require national law consistency, the BAA governs within BARMM. If the BOL requires consistency with national law for the subject, PD 1083 prevails to the extent of the conflict.

### Research Protocol for PD 1083

When your research question touches Muslim personal law:

1. **Start with PD 1083.** It is the existing codification. Read the relevant title and articles.
2. **Check the BOL.** Does Article X grant additional authority or modify the PD 1083 framework?
3. **Search for BAAs.** Has Parliament enacted any legislation supplementing PD 1083?
4. **Check for pending bills.** Is there legislation in the pipeline that would change the current framework?
5. **Build the Comparative Matrix.** Place PD 1083, BOL Article X, any relevant BAA, and any applicable national law (e.g., the Family Code for mixed marriages) in adjacent columns.

## 7.6 Maqasid al-Shari'ah: The Objectives Framework

Beyond the specific jurisdictional rules, there is a broader framework for assessing whether legislation aligns with Islamic principles. The **Maqasid al-Shari'ah** — the higher objectives of Islamic law — provides that framework.

The Bangsamoro Shari'ah Legislation Framework proposes using Maqasid as the guiding philosophy for public policy: a "purpose-driven approach" that is "rational and universally accessible" and "compatible with the legal pluralism required by the BOL."[^15] The five objectives are:

**1. Protection of Faith (Din)**
Safeguarding religious freedom, practice, and worship. Legislation should not impede the free exercise of Islam. Example: A BAA regulating public events should not restrict Islamic religious gatherings.

**2. Protection of Life (Nafs)**
Preserving life, health, and physical well-being. Legislation should advance the safety and health of persons. Example: A public health BAA aligns with this objective when it provides healthcare access for underserved Muslim communities.

**3. Protection of Intellect (Aql)**
Safeguarding reason, education, and knowledge. Legislation should promote learning and prohibit what impairs rational capacity. Example: The Bangsamoro Education Code (BAA 18) advances this objective by establishing the educational framework for the region, including madrasah education.

**4. Protection of Lineage (Nasl)**
Preserving family, progeny, and social bonds. Legislation should strengthen the family unit and protect the welfare of children. Example: PD 1083's provisions on marriage, divorce, and custody serve this objective.

**5. Protection of Property (Mal)**
Safeguarding wealth, economic rights, and fair transactions. Legislation should protect property rights and ensure just economic dealings. Example: A future Islamic finance BAA would advance this objective by establishing Shari'ah-compliant financial instruments.

### How Researchers Use Maqasid

The Maqasid framework is not a source of binding law — no court will strike down a BAA solely for violating Maqasid. But it serves three research functions:

**First, as a policy alignment test.** When researching the legal basis for proposed legislation, assess which Maqasid objective(s) the proposal advances. A bill that protects none of the five objectives raises a red flag — it may not align with Islamic principles, even if it is technically within Parliament's jurisdiction.

**Second, as a legislative intent framework.** When analyzing the purpose of an existing BAA that has a Shari'ah dimension, the Maqasid framework helps articulate the underlying policy rationale in Islamic terms.

**Third, as a comparative lens.** When comparing BARMM legislation with Islamic law frameworks in other jurisdictions (Malaysia, Indonesia, Brunei), the Maqasid framework provides a common analytical vocabulary.

Add a **Maqasid Assessment** row to your Comparative Matrix whenever the research question involves Shari'ah. Identify which of the five objectives are engaged and how the legal provisions under review advance (or fail to advance) them.

## 7.7 Nested Sovereignty and Legal Pluralism

To research Shari'ah effectively in BARMM, you need to understand the structural model within which it operates. The Bangsamoro Shari'ah Legislation Framework describes this as **"nested sovereignty"** — the BARMM exercises "culturally-specific sovereignty that is nested within, and ultimately subordinate to, the national sovereignty of the Philippine Republic."[^16]

Three principles follow from this model:

### 7.7.1 Constitutional Supremacy

All Shari'ah legislation must comply with the 1987 Constitution. The Constitution is the supreme law. The BOL, which authorizes Shari'ah courts and Shari'ah legislation, is itself subject to the Constitution. A BAA implementing Shari'ah provisions cannot violate constitutional rights — due process, equal protection, freedom of religion, or any other constitutional guarantee.

**Research implication:** When assessing proposed Shari'ah legislation, always check for constitutional compatibility. A provision may be consistent with Islamic law but unconstitutional. The Constitution prevails.

### 7.7.2 Judicial Integration

Shari'ah courts are **part of the Philippine judicial system**, not separate from it. Section 2 of Article X states this directly: "Shari'ah courts within the Bangsamoro territorial jurisdiction shall form part of the Philippine judicial system subject to the supervision of the Supreme Court."[^17]

The Supreme Court:
- **Supervises** all Shari'ah courts (Section 2)
- **Promulgates** the Shari'ah Rules of Court (Section 11)
- **Administers** the Shari'ah Special Bar Examinations (Section 12)
- **Appoints and disciplines** Shari'ah court personnel (Section 14)
- **Reviews** Shari'ah High Court decisions on questions of law (Section 7)

**Research implication:** When searching for Shari'ah jurisprudence, the Supreme Court E-Library is your primary database for appellate decisions. Shari'ah court rulings that have been appealed on questions of law appear in the SC's case database.

### 7.7.3 Legal Pluralism

Three legal traditions coexist in BARMM:
- **The Philippine Constitution and national statutory law** — the overarching framework
- **Shari'ah** — Islamic law applicable to Muslims
- **Traditional or tribal law** — customary law applicable to indigenous peoples

These three are not competitors. They apply to different populations and different subject matters, with the Constitution as the ceiling for all. The researcher's task is to determine which tradition (or combination) applies to a given question — and how they interact when they overlap.

## 7.8 Researching Traditional and Tribal Law

Article X does not address only Shari'ah. Sections 17-18 establish the framework for traditional and tribal justice.

### 7.8.1 The BOL Framework

**Section 1** states that "traditional or tribal laws shall be applicable to disputes of indigenous peoples within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."[^18]

**Section 17** directs Parliament to "enact laws to promote and support the traditional or tribal justice systems that are appropriate for the indigenous peoples." It recognizes the right of indigenous peoples to "use their own commonly accepted justice systems, conflict resolution institutions, peace building processes or mechanisms, and other customary laws and practices within their respective communities and as may be compatible with the national legal system and with internationally recognized human rights."[^19]

**Section 18** creates the Office for Tribal Justice System to oversee the study, preservation, and development of tribal justice within BARMM.[^20]

### 7.8.2 The IP Rights Anchor

BOL Article IX, Section 3 states that rights under RA 8371 (Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act, or IPRA) "shall not be diminished."[^21] This means that any BARMM legislation affecting indigenous peoples must be consistent with IPRA protections. The Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIPA) has institutional jurisdiction over IP-related matters.

### 7.8.3 The Research Challenge: Uncodified Law

Traditional law is fundamentally different from statutory law. Most tribal and customary practices are **uncodified** — they exist as oral traditions, community practices, and decisions by tribal councils, not as written legislation.

This creates a unique research challenge. You cannot search for traditional law the way you search for a BAA or a Supreme Court decision. The research methods include:

1. **Consult MIPA records.** MIPA may maintain documentation of customary practices for specific indigenous groups within BARMM.
2. **Consult the Office for Tribal Justice System.** If operational, this office is mandated to study and document tribal justice systems.
3. **Review IPRA documentation.** RA 8371 and its IRR describe processes for recognizing customary law, particularly in the context of ancestral domain claims and community governance.
4. **Consult community leaders.** For specific questions about customary practice, the authoritative sources may be tribal elders, *datus*, or council leaders — not written documents.
5. **Check academic sources.** Ethnographic studies, university research, and publications by organizations like NCIP (National Commission on Indigenous Peoples) may document customary practices.

**Documentation standard:** When your research relies on traditional or customary law that is not codified in any written source, mark it clearly: **[UNVERIFIED — based on customary practice, not codified law]**. This notation alerts the reader that the legal basis rests on community practice rather than enacted legislation, and that independent verification with community leaders may be needed.

### 7.9.3 International and Comparative Sources

When your research requires comparative analysis with other Islamic legal systems, these sources are relevant:

- **AAOIFI Standards** (Accounting and Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions) — international standards for Islamic finance, banking, and insurance
- **OIC Fiqh Academy Resolutions** — juristic opinions from the Organisation of Islamic Cooperation's International Islamic Fiqh Academy on contemporary legal questions
- **Malaysian Shari'ah court decisions** — Malaysia operates a dual-track legal system (civil and Shari'ah) similar in some respects to BARMM's legal pluralism
- **Indonesian Islamic law framework** — the world's largest Muslim-majority country with a codified *Kompilasi Hukum Islam* (Compilation of Islamic Law) for personal matters
- **Academic journals** — *Journal of Islamic Law and Culture*, *Islamic Law and Society*, *Journal of Muslim Minority Affairs*

**Caution:** International sources provide **comparative context**, not binding authority. The BARMM Shari'ah framework is defined by the Philippine Constitution, the BOL, and PD 1083. A fatwa from the OIC Fiqh Academy or a Malaysian court ruling may be persuasive but is never controlling in a Philippine legal context. Always cite international sources as comparative references, not as primary authority.

## 7.10 Worked Example

**Research question:** *What is the legal framework governing Muslim inheritance in BARMM?*

### Step 1: Issue Analysis

Apply the Three-Part Frame from Chapter 2:

| Element | Answer |
|---------|--------|
| **Subject** | Muslim inheritance (succession) |
| **Jurisdiction** | Both BARMM and national — personal law for Muslims, but estate tax is national |
| **Action** | Map the complete legal framework: who has authority, what law governs distribution, what law governs taxation |

**Shari'ah dimension?** Yes — inheritance is a core area of Muslim personal law, explicitly within Shari'ah court jurisdiction.

### Step 2: National Law Relationship

**Is inheritance within the 55 enumerated powers?** Article V, Section 2 includes (a) "Administration of justice" and multiple powers related to personal law. Article X, Section 4 grants Parliament power over "personal, family, and property law." **Result: YES.**

**Does the BOL require consistency with national law?** No specific provision in Article X requires consistency with national tax law for inheritance distribution. However, national tax law (estate tax under the NIRC) is a residual national power — the BOL does not grant BARMM the power to impose or exempt estate tax. **Result: The subject straddles two relationships to national law.** The *distribution* of the estate (who inherits what share) is within Bangsamoro jurisdiction (no national law consistency required). The *taxation* of the estate transfer is a residual national power.

### Step 3: Systematic Source Search

**Tier 1 — Constitution:** Article III (Bill of Rights) protects property rights. Article X, Section 20 authorizes autonomous regions to legislate on "personal, family, and property relations."

**Tier 2 — BOL:** Article X, Section 4 grants Parliament power over personal and property law. Article X, Section 6 gives the Shari'ah District Court exclusive original jurisdiction over "disposition, distribution, and settlement of the estate of deceased Muslims, probate of wills, issuance of letters of administration, or appointment of administrators or executors regardless of the nature or the aggregate value of the property."[^22]

**Tier 4 — BAAs:** Search the BAA archive for any enacted legislation on inheritance or succession. As of March 2026: none found.

**Tier 5 — National law:**
- **PD 1083, Title II (Succession):** This is the existing codification of Muslim inheritance law. It prescribes the shares of heirs according to Islamic law (*faraid*).
- **National Internal Revenue Code (NIRC), as amended by RA 10963 (TRAIN Law):** Estate tax of six percent (6%) on the net estate above P5,000,000. This applies to all decedents regardless of religion.

**Tier 7 — Jurisprudence:** Search the SC E-Library for decisions on Shari'ah court jurisdiction over estate settlement and for any rulings on the interaction between PD 1083 and the Family Code in inheritance matters.

### Step 5: Research Answer

Muslim inheritance in BARMM is governed by three laws operating simultaneously:

1. **PD 1083 (Title II)** provides the substantive rules — the prescribed shares for each category of heir under Islamic law.
2. **BOL Article X, Section 6(b)** vests exclusive jurisdiction in the Shari'ah District Court for estate settlement, regardless of the aggregate value of the property.
3. **The NIRC (as amended by TRAIN Law)** imposes estate tax on the net estate — this is a national tax law that applies regardless of the religious affiliation of the decedent.

No BAA has been enacted on Muslim inheritance as of March 2026. If Parliament enacts one, Article X, Section 4 limits its scope to matters "not provided for" under PD 1083 — it can supplement PD 1083 but should not contradict it. The Shari'ah principles underlying PD 1083's inheritance rules are rooted in the Maqasid of protecting property (*Mal*) and lineage (*Nasl*).

## 7.11 Common Pitfalls

**Pitfall 1: Treating Shari'ah as a separate tier.**
Shari'ah is not Tier 8. It is a parallel dimension that intersects all seven tiers. When you isolate Shari'ah from the hierarchy, you miss the interactions — how the Constitution constrains it, how the BOL frames it, how national law coexists with it. Always research Shari'ah within the seven-tier framework, not outside it.

**Pitfall 2: Assuming Parliament can expand Shari'ah criminal jurisdiction beyond arresto menor.**
Article X, Section 4 limits criminal jurisdiction to "minor offenses punishable by *arresto menor* or *ta'zir* which must be equivalent to *arresto menor* or fines commensurate to the offense."[^23] A bill imposing imprisonment beyond *arresto menor* (one day to thirty days) through the Shari'ah court framework exceeds the BOL's grant. The researcher must flag any proposed legislation that attempts to expand beyond this limit.

**Pitfall 3: Applying Shari'ah to non-Muslims.**
Section 1 states that Shari'ah applies "exclusively to cases involving Muslims." A non-Muslim is subject to Shari'ah jurisdiction *only* upon voluntary submission. No law, BAA, or executive issuance can extend Shari'ah jurisdiction to non-Muslims without their consent. If your research question involves non-Muslim parties who have not submitted to Shari'ah jurisdiction, the Shari'ah framework does not apply.

**Pitfall 4: Interpreting Shari'ah sources independently.**
The legal researcher identifies when Shari'ah applies, maps the jurisdictional framework, and locates the applicable provisions. The researcher does **not** independently interpret Quranic verses or Hadith to derive legal rules. That is the domain of Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*) and should be referred to the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta or qualified Islamic legal scholars. Overstepping this boundary produces unreliable research.

**Pitfall 5: Ignoring PD 1083.**
PD 1083 is nearly five decades old, but it remains the primary codification of Muslim personal law. The BOL builds on it rather than replacing it. Researchers who skip PD 1083 and jump straight to the BOL miss the substantive law — the BOL provides jurisdictional authority, but PD 1083 provides the rules.

**Pitfall 6: Conflating traditional/tribal law with Shari'ah.**
Traditional law and Shari'ah are distinct legal traditions. Shari'ah derives from Islamic religious sources. Traditional law derives from indigenous customs and practices. They apply to different populations — Shari'ah to Muslims, traditional law to indigenous peoples. Sections 17-18 of Article X address traditional law separately from Sections 1-16 on Shari'ah. Do not treat them as interchangeable.

**Pitfall 7: Citing international Islamic sources as binding authority.**
AAOIFI standards, OIC Fiqh Academy resolutions, and comparative Shari'ah jurisprudence from other countries are persuasive but not binding. The BARMM Shari'ah framework is defined by Philippine law — the Constitution, the BOL, and PD 1083. Always cite the Philippine sources as primary authority and international sources as comparative context.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Shari'ah is parallel, not separate.** It intersects all seven tiers of the legal hierarchy. Research it within the seven-tier framework using the same five-step methodology.

2. **Three laws govern most Shari'ah questions simultaneously.** The BOL provides jurisdictional authority. PD 1083 provides the substantive rules. The Constitution provides the ceiling. Apply all three.

3. **Know the hard limits.** Shari'ah applies exclusively to Muslims. Criminal jurisdiction stops at *arresto menor*. The Supreme Court has final authority on questions of law. Parliament legislates on matters not covered by PD 1083.

4. **Researchers map and locate — they do not interpret.** Your role is to identify when Shari'ah applies, which court has jurisdiction, and what the codified law says. Interpretation of Islamic primary sources belongs to qualified Islamic scholars and the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta.

5. **Traditional law is not Shari'ah.** They are distinct traditions with distinct sources, distinct populations, and distinct constitutional bases. Research them separately.

6. **Always complete the Shari'ah Applicability Matrix.** It ensures you do not miss a Shari'ah dimension — and it ensures you do not create one where none exists.
