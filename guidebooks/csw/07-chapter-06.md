# Chapter 6 — Step 3: Research Needed Information (R)

Your problem is defined. Your objectives are set. Now you need evidence. Step 3 is where you build the factual foundation that supports your analysis, options, and recommendation.

Weak research produces weak CSW. If your data is outdated, your sources are unreliable, or your analysis is superficial, the decision-maker cannot trust your conclusions. The quality of your research determines the credibility of everything that follows.

## 6.1 Developing a Research Plan

Do not start researching randomly. Start with a plan. A research plan answers four questions:

| Question | Output |
|----------|--------|
| **What do I need to know?** | List of specific research questions |
| **Where will I find it?** | Sources mapped to each question |
| **How will I collect it?** | Methods for each source (document review, interview, data request) |
| **When will I finish?** | Timeline with milestones |

### Research Plan Template

| Research Question | Source | Method | Responsible | Due Date | Status |
|------------------|--------|--------|------------|---------|--------|
| What is the current number of qualified ECCD teachers in BARMM? | MBHTE Human Resource Division | Data request (formal letter) | Lead analyst | Day 3 | |
| What salary grades apply to daycare teachers? | CSC / LGU plantilla records | Document review | Research assistant | Day 2 | |
| What are best practices for ECCD teacher recruitment in similar contexts? | Academic journals, UNICEF reports | Literature review | Lead analyst | Day 5 | |

The plan keeps your research focused. Without it, you spend three days reading interesting but irrelevant material while the deadline approaches.

> **TIP:** Allocate no more than 30-40% of your total CSW timeline to research. If you have 10 working days, spend 3-4 on research. The remaining time goes to options analysis, writing, review, and revision. Research that consumes the entire timeline leaves no time to think.

## 6.2 Identifying and Evaluating Sources

### 6.2.1 Government Data (PSA-BARMM, MFBM, Implementing Agencies)

Government data is your primary source for BARMM CSW. It has institutional authority and is what decision-makers expect to see.

| Source | What It Provides | How to Access |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| **PSA-BARMM** | Population, poverty incidence, employment, demographic data | PSA regional office, PSA website (psa.gov.ph) |
| **MFBM** | Budget data, expenditure program, financial statements | Formal data request to MFBM planning division |
| **Bangsamoro Expenditure Program (BEP)** | Agency-level budget allocations by year | Committee on Finance and Budget records |
| **COA Annual Audit Reports** | Audit findings, financial observations, compliance issues | COA BARMM office, COA website |
| **Implementing agencies** | Program data, performance indicators, accomplishment reports | Direct coordination or formal request |
| **BDP 2023-2028** | Development targets, baseline indicators, strategic framework | Published document (available in BARMM offices) |

### 6.2.2 Academic and Policy Research

Academic sources add analytical depth and credibility.

**Where to look:**

- **Google Scholar** (scholar.google.com) — Free access to academic papers, many with full text
- **Philippine Institute for Development Studies (PIDS)** — Philippine policy research
- **World Bank Open Knowledge Repository** — Development data and research, often with Philippines-specific reports
- **UNICEF, UNDP, ADB** — Sector-specific reports on education, governance, and development
- **University of the Philippines** — Philippine governance and public administration research

### 6.2.3 Stakeholder Inputs

Stakeholder consultation produces primary data that government statistics cannot capture: lived experience, operational realities, and political context.

Methods for gathering stakeholder inputs:

| Method | When to Use | Sample Size | Time Required |
|--------|------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Key informant interviews** | When you need depth from specific experts | 5-15 individuals | 1-2 hours per interview |
| **Focus group discussions** | When you need community perspectives | 8-12 per group, 2-4 groups | 1.5-2 hours per group |
| **Surveys** | When you need quantitative data from a large group | 50+ respondents | 1-3 weeks for design, deployment, analysis |
| **Written consultation** | When stakeholders are geographically dispersed | Variable | 1-2 weeks for responses |

> **CAUTION:** Evaluate every source for credibility, timeliness, and relevance. A 2018 report on BARMM education may be outdated if significant reforms occurred after 2019 (BOL ratification). Government data from before the transition period may not reflect current BARMM realities. Always check the publication date and note limitations.

## 6.3 Conducting a Literature Review

A literature review is not a reading list. It is a structured synthesis of existing knowledge on your topic.

### How to conduct a literature review for CSW:

**Step 1: Search systematically.** Use 3-5 search terms derived from your problem statement. Search at least three databases or sources. Document your search terms and results.

**Step 2: Screen for relevance.** Read abstracts and executive summaries first. Select 10-20 most relevant sources for full review.

**Step 3: Extract key findings.** For each source, record:

| Element | What to Record |
|---------|---------------|
| **Citation** | Author, title, year, source |
| **Key findings** | 2-3 main conclusions relevant to your problem |
| **Methodology** | How the research was conducted |
| **Limitations** | What the research did not cover |
| **Relevance** | How it applies to your specific BARMM context |

**Step 4: Synthesize.** Do not present sources one by one ("Smith (2023) says... Jones (2024) says..."). Organize by theme. What do the sources collectively say about root causes? About effective interventions? About risks?

**Step 5: Identify gaps.** What has not been studied? What BARMM-specific data is missing? These gaps may require primary data collection (Section 6.4) or may be flagged as limitations in your CSW.

## 6.4 Gathering Primary Data

When existing sources do not answer your research questions, you need primary data.

### 6.4.1 Surveys, Interviews, Focus Groups

**Surveys** produce quantitative data from large groups. Use when you need statistics: satisfaction rates, awareness levels, resource counts.

- Design clear, unambiguous questions
- Keep surveys under 20 minutes
- Use validated scales when available
- Pre-test with 5-10 respondents before full deployment

**Interviews** produce qualitative data with depth. Use when you need explanations, narratives, and expert judgment.

- Prepare a semi-structured interview guide (5-10 questions)
- Allow follow-up questions based on responses
- Record with permission or take detailed notes
- Transcribe key points within 24 hours

**Focus groups** produce group perspectives and surface disagreements. Use when you need to understand how different stakeholders view the same issue.

- Recruit 8-12 participants per group
- Use a skilled facilitator (not the lead analyst)
- Prepare a discussion guide with 4-6 open-ended questions
- Record and document group dynamics, not just content

### 6.4.2 Ethical Guidelines and Data Privacy

All primary data collection must comply with ethical standards and RA 10173 (Data Privacy Act).

**Mandatory requirements:**

- **Informed consent** — Participants must know the purpose, how data will be used, and their right to decline
- **Confidentiality** — Protect participant identities unless they consent to attribution
- **Voluntary participation** — No coercion, no incentives that compromise voluntary choice
- **Data security** — Store collected data securely; limit access to the research team
- **Cultural sensitivity** — Respect Bangsamoro cultural norms, gender protocols, and community protocols when conducting fieldwork

> **BANGSAMORO NOTE:** When collecting data in communities, respect local protocols. Introduce yourself through the barangay chair or community leader. In predominantly Muslim communities, be mindful of gender dynamics during interviews and focus groups. Offer separate-gender sessions when appropriate. Schedule around prayer times.

## 6.5 Analyzing and Synthesizing Information

Data collection is not research. Analysis is.

### Analytical Methods for CSW

| Data Type | Analytical Method | When to Use |
|-----------|------------------|-------------|
| **Quantitative** | Descriptive statistics (averages, percentages, trends) | Budget data, population figures, coverage rates |
| **Quantitative** | Variance analysis (compare actual vs. target) | Budget execution, KPI achievement |
| **Quantitative** | Trend analysis (multi-year comparison) | Budget trends, enrollment trends, staffing trends |
| **Qualitative** | Thematic analysis (identify recurring themes) | Interview transcripts, consultation notes |
| **Qualitative** | Content analysis (categorize document contents) | Policy documents, legislation, reports |
| **Mixed** | Triangulation (cross-validate findings across sources) | Any CSW — strengthen conclusions by showing multiple sources agree |

### Synthesis Framework

Organize your findings around your research questions, not your sources:

| Research Question | Finding | Sources | Confidence Level |
|------------------|---------|---------|-----------------|
| How many qualified ECCD teachers does BARMM have? | 1,738 out of 2,985 positions filled by qualified teachers (58.2%) | MBHTE HR data, Dec 2025 | High — verified government data |
| What is the primary barrier to recruitment? | Salary competitiveness (SG 8-9 vs. private sector SG 11-13 equivalent) | Interviews with 12 MBHTE regional directors; CSC salary schedule | Moderate — qualitative triangulated with salary data |

## 6.6 Identifying Best Practices

Look for models that have solved similar problems in comparable contexts.

**Hierarchy of relevance for best practices:**

1. **Within BARMM** — Solutions already working in other BARMM agencies or LGUs (most directly applicable)
2. **Within the Philippines** — National government or other LGU approaches
3. **Southeast Asian countries** — Similar governance and development contexts
4. **International** — Global best practices (least directly transferable but can inform design)

When presenting best practices in your CSW, always assess **transferability**. A program that works in Metro Manila may fail in island municipalities of BARMM. Explain what would need to adapt, not just what worked elsewhere.

## 6.7 Documenting and Validating Findings

### Documentation Standards

Your research section must be traceable. Every claim needs a source. Every figure needs a citation.

**Citation format for CSW:**

- Government documents: Agency name, document title, year. Example: "MBHTE, Status Report on ECCD Implementation, 2025"
- Legal provisions: Full citation. Example: "BOL, Article VI, Section 13"
- Academic sources: Author (Year). Example: "Santos (2024)"
- Interview data: Position title and date (protect identity when needed). Example: "Interview with MBHTE Regional Director, January 15, 2026"

### Validation

Before finalizing your research findings, validate with subject matter experts.

**Validation questions:**

1. Are the data accurate and current?
2. Are the sources credible?
3. Are there findings that contradict other evidence?
4. Have I missed any critical source?
5. Are my interpretations reasonable, or am I reaching beyond the evidence?

Share draft findings with 2-3 knowledgeable colleagues. Ask them to challenge your conclusions. Revise where warranted.

## 6.8 Why This Step Matters — Consequences of Skipping

When you skip or rush research:

**Your options float on air.** Without evidence, your recommendations are opinions. Decision-makers can and should reject opinions presented as analysis.

**You miss critical information.** A law you did not find, a data point you did not check, a stakeholder perspective you did not seek — any of these can invalidate your entire CSW.

**Your credibility collapses.** If the decision-maker discovers an error in your data, they question everything else in the document. One wrong number taints 30 pages of analysis.

**You repeat past mistakes.** Without reviewing what has been tried before, you may recommend an approach that already failed. Best practice review prevents reinventing broken wheels.

**Real BARMM example of skipping Step 3:**

A legislative staff member prepared a budget briefer for a ministry's FY 2026 request. The briefer recommended approving the full budget. During deliberation, an MP asked about the ministry's utilization rate for the previous year. The staff member had not checked.

The actual utilization rate was 47% — meaning the ministry spent less than half its FY 2025 budget. The committee reduced the FY 2026 allocation by 30% and questioned the staff member's competence.

A 30-minute review of COA expenditure data would have surfaced the utilization rate in Step 3. The briefer would have addressed it proactively, perhaps recommending conditional approval with a utilization improvement plan. Instead, the staff member's credibility and the office's reputation suffered permanent damage.

---

**Chapter Summary**

- Develop a research plan before starting. Map research questions to sources, methods, and deadlines.
- Prioritize government data (PSA, MFBM, implementing agencies) for BARMM CSW.
- Supplement with academic research, stakeholder inputs, and best practices.
- Conduct literature reviews thematically, not source by source.
- Follow ethical guidelines and data privacy requirements for primary data collection.
- Analyze and synthesize findings organized by research questions, not by source.
- Document every finding with traceable citations.
- Validate findings with subject matter experts before finalizing.
- Skipping research produces opinion-based CSW, missed critical data, credibility damage, and repeated failures.
