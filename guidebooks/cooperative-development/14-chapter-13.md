# Chapter 13 -- The CSEA-BARMM and the Cooperative Ecosystem

Your cooperative does not operate in isolation. Behind it stands an entire ecosystem of government agencies, development partners, and fellow cooperatives. Knowing who does what -- and how to access their services -- is as practical as knowing your bylaws. A cooperative that understands the ecosystem gets faster registration, better training, cheaper financing, and stronger legal protection. A cooperative that does not understand it wastes months navigating bureaucracy, misses grant deadlines, and solves problems alone that the system was designed to solve for them.

This chapter maps the ecosystem. You will learn what the CSEA-BARMM does and how it is structured. You will learn what services you can request -- and how to request them. You will learn how your LGU Cooperative Development Officer fits into the picture. You will learn which BTA committees oversee the cooperative sector, how the CDA relates to the CSEA, and which development partners operate in the region. You will also learn about the Combatant Transitional Program -- one of the most important cooperative development pathways in the Bangsamoro.

Treat this chapter as your directory. Return to it every time you need to know **who to call**, **what to file**, or **where to go**.

## 13.1 The Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA-BARMM)

The **Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA)** is the primary government agency responsible for cooperatives and social enterprises in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. Every cooperative you register, every compliance certificate you secure, every training program you request -- all of these go through or involve the CSEA. Understanding this agency is not optional. It is the foundation of your relationship with the Bangsamoro government's cooperative development infrastructure.

### 13.1.1 Mandate, Powers, and Functions (BTA Bill 210, Sections 11-13)

**What the CSEA is.** Under BTA Bill 210, the CSEA is the **sole agency** of the Bangsamoro Government authorized to register cooperatives and social enterprises.[^1] It enjoys **fiscal autonomy** and is attached to the **Office of the Chief Minister**.[^2] This placement is significant. It means the CSEA reports to the highest executive authority in the Bangsamoro government, giving cooperative development a seat at the top of the policy table.

**The CSEA's mandate.** The CSEA, together with all branches and agencies of the Bangsamoro Government, must ensure the provision of **technical guidance**, **financial assistance**, and **other services** to enable cooperatives to develop into viable and responsive economic enterprises.[^3] The operative word is "ensure." The mandate is not merely to offer services. It is to guarantee that cooperatives have what they need to become platforms for **poverty reduction** and **sustainable, inclusive, and equitable development**.

**Powers and functions.** BTA Bill 210, Section 13 grants the CSEA twenty-four enumerated powers. You do not need to memorize all of them. But you do need to know the categories and which ones directly affect your cooperative:

**Planning and policy.** The CSEA formulates, adopts, and implements integrated plans and programs on cooperative and social enterprise development.[^4] These plans must be consistent with the overall socio-economic development plans of the Bangsamoro Government -- meaning the **Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP) 2023-2028** and its successors.

**Training and capability building.** The CSEA develops and conducts management and training programs upon request of cooperatives and social enterprises.[^5] This is demand-driven. Your cooperative must request the training. Do not wait for the CSEA to come to you. Submit a formal request letter to the CSEA Provincial Office or the Cooperative Development Division at the central office.

**Registration and compliance.** The CSEA is the sole registration authority. It processes applications for **Certificates of Registration (COR)** and **Certificates of Compliance (COC)**.[^6] It also requires all cooperatives to submit **annual reports** and **audited financial statements**.[^7]

**Financial access.** The CSEA assists cooperatives in accessing financial and other forms of assistance under terms designed to strengthen their viability and autonomy.[^8] It also administers all grants and donations coursed through the Bangsamoro Government for cooperative development.[^9]

**Dispute resolution.** Upon request of either or both parties, the CSEA mediates and conciliates disputes within a cooperative or between cooperatives.[^10] This is the escalation path when your internal mediation and conciliation mechanisms fail, as discussed in Chapter 12.

**Combatant reintegration.** The CSEA promotes and strengthens cooperatives as pathways to reintegrate **former combatants**, **surrenderees**, and **disaster-affected communities**.[^11] This is a unique Bangsamoro mandate that does not exist at the national level. Section 13.5 of this chapter covers the Combatant Transitional Program in detail.

**Coordination.** The CSEA coordinates the efforts of LGUs and the private sector in the promotion, organization, and development of cooperatives and social enterprises.[^12] Your municipal or city government should be working **with** the CSEA on cooperative programs -- not independently.

**What this means for you.** The CSEA is not just a registration office. It is your cooperative's primary government partner for training, financing, compliance support, dispute resolution, and market development. **Know your CSEA Provincial Director by name.** Keep their contact number in your records. Visit the provincial office at least once per quarter -- even when you do not have a specific problem. Building a relationship during calm times ensures faster response during urgent ones.

### 13.1.2 Organizational Structure: Four Divisions and Provincial Offices

The CSEA's organizational structure is defined by BTA Bill 210, Section 14.[^13] Understanding it tells you exactly who handles what -- so you never send a compliance question to the training division or a legal question to the finance office.

**The Executive Director.** The CSEA is headed by an **Executive Director** with the rank of **Director III**.[^14] The Executive Director oversees all four divisions, sets agency-wide priorities, and represents the CSEA in Cabinet-level discussions. The Executive Director is assisted by four Division Chiefs and supported by Provincial Directors.

**The four divisions.** The CSEA is composed of the following divisions:

| Division | Head | Primary Responsibility |
|---|---|---|
| **Administrative and Finance Division** | Division Chief | Day-to-day operations, budget, administrative support |
| **Cooperative Development Division** | Division Chief | Registration, compliance, training, monitoring, mediation |
| **Social Enterprise Division** | Division Chief | SE promotion, accreditation, product development, education |
| **Shari'ah and Legal Division** | Division Chief | Legal review, Shari'ah compliance, policy research, audits |

**The Administrative and Finance Division** handles the internal machinery of the CSEA -- budget management, human resources, procurement, and administrative support.[^15] You will interact with this division when paying registration fees, requesting official documents, or following up on the administrative status of your applications.

**The Cooperative Development Division** is the division you will deal with most frequently. It processes registration applications and issues **COR** and **COC** certificates.[^16] It provides mediation and arbitration services.[^17] It conducts monitoring and inspection of cooperatives.[^18] It develops and delivers management training programs.[^19] It assists cooperatives with financing and marketing opportunities.[^20] And it works on convergence across different ministries and agencies to deliver a **whole-of-government approach** to cooperative development.[^21]

**The Social Enterprise Division** handles social enterprise promotion, accreditation, product development, and capacity building.[^22] If your cooperative also qualifies or intends to qualify as a social enterprise, this is the division that processes your SE accreditation and connects you to the **RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program**. The Social Enterprise Division also encourages **social procurement** -- the practice of government agencies purchasing goods and services from social enterprises.[^23]

**Provincial Offices.** The CSEA operates **Provincial Offices** in the provinces of Maguindanao, Lanao del Sur, Basilan, and Tawi-Tawi.[^24] Each Provincial Office is headed by a **Provincial Director** with the rank of **Director I**. Cotabato City and the **Special Geographic Area (SGA)** fall under the jurisdiction of the CSEA Provincial Office in Maguindanao.[^25]

Your Provincial Office is your primary point of contact for all routine transactions -- registration filing, compliance submissions, training requests, and field consultations. For complex matters involving legal interpretation, Shari'ah compliance, or policy disputes, the Provincial Office will coordinate with the relevant division at the central office.

**The organizational chart.** Think of the structure this way:

```
Executive Director (Director III)

    |

    |
    |--- Provincial Office - Maguindanao (Provincial Director, Director I)
    |        (includes Cotabato City and SGA)
    |
    |--- Provincial Office - Lanao del Sur (Provincial Director, Director I)

```

**Practical tip.** When filing documents or making inquiries, **always identify which division handles your matter** before visiting. A quick phone call to the central office or provincial office saves you from being redirected multiple times on the day of your visit. Bring a reference number for any pending application.

### 13.1.3 The Shari'ah and Legal Division

The **Shari'ah and Legal Division** deserves its own section because it represents something unique to the BARMM cooperative ecosystem. No other region in the Philippines has an agency division dedicated to ensuring cooperatives comply with **Shari'ah governance**. This division is the institutional guarantee that Bangsamoro cooperatives can operate in full alignment with Islamic law.

The division is headed by a Division Chief and performs five core functions:[^26]

**Legal and regulatory review.** The division assesses whether cooperatives and social enterprises comply with BTA Bill 210, relevant national laws, and regulatory measures issued by the CSEA and other regulatory bodies.[^27] If your cooperative receives a compliance notice, the Shari'ah and Legal Division is the source of the legal analysis behind it.

**Legal and policy research.** The division produces legal opinions, bill reviews, policy analyses, notes, and commentaries that inform policy and regulatory measures.[^28] It also drafts the policies and regulatory directives that the CSEA issues. When a new CSEA circular arrives at your cooperative, this division wrote it -- or at least reviewed it for legal soundness.

**Shari'ah risk management and control.** The division systematically identifies, measures, monitors, and reports **Shari'ah non-compliance risks** within cooperative operations, business activities, and governance.[^29] This is not abstract. If your cooperative's lending practices involve interest (riba), if your investment portfolio includes haram products, or if your contracts contain excessive uncertainty (gharar) -- the Shari'ah and Legal Division is the body that flags the problem and recommends corrective action.

**Shari'ah research.** The division conducts in-depth research and studies on Shari'ah to ensure the CSEA's regulations are responsive to Islamic jurisprudence.[^30] This function keeps the regulatory framework current with evolving interpretations and applications of Shari'ah in the cooperative context.

**Shari'ah review and audit.** The division conducts regular assessments to verify that cooperatives adhere to the Shari'ah governance and compliance framework.[^31] It also evaluates the CSEA's own internal control mechanisms, risk management systems, and governance processes for Shari'ah compliance.

**What this means for your cooperative.** If you operate a **Shari'ah-compliant cooperative** -- and BTA Bill 210 strongly encourages this -- the Shari'ah and Legal Division is both your regulator and your resource. Use it. Request Shari'ah compliance reviews **before** your annual audit, not after. Ask for advisory opinions when you are considering a new financial product or business arrangement. A proactive relationship with this division prevents the kind of compliance surprises that lead to penalties or reputational damage.

## 13.2 CSEA Services Available to Your Cooperative

The CSEA offers three broad categories of services: **regulatory services** (registration and compliance), **development services** (training and technical assistance), and **financial services** (grants, programs, and market access). Each category includes specific services you can request. This section maps them so you know exactly what is available.

### 13.2.1 Registration and Compliance Support

**Registration services.** The CSEA handles the entire cooperative registration process, from name reservation to the issuance of the **Certificate of Registration (COR)**. The specific services include:

- **Cooperative name reservation.** You reserve your proposed cooperative name with the CSEA to ensure it is unique and not already taken. The CSEA maintains a masterlist of all registered cooperatives.
- **Processing of registration applications.** You submit your Articles of Cooperation, Bylaws, Economic Survey, Treasurer's Affidavit, Surety Bond, and PRS Certificate to the Provincial Office. The Cooperative Development Division reviews and processes the application.
- **Issuance of the Certificate of Registration.** Upon approval, the CSEA issues the COR under its official seal. This certificate is conclusive evidence that your cooperative is duly registered.[^32]
- **Certificate of Amendments.** When your cooperative amends its Articles of Cooperation or Bylaws, the CSEA processes and certifies the amendments.

**Compliance services.** After registration, the CSEA monitors your cooperative's compliance and issues the critical **Certificate of Compliance (COC)**. The COC is issued after one year of operation and upon compliance with all CSEA requirements.[^33] Without a valid COC, your cooperative cannot access government programs, enter into contracts with government agencies, or participate in public procurement. The specific compliance services include:

- **Annual report review.** The CSEA reviews your Cooperative Annual Performance Report (CAPR) and Cooperative Annual Financial Statement (CAFS).
- **Inspection and validation.** CSEA field staff conduct on-site inspections to verify that your cooperative's actual operations match its reported activities.
- **Compliance assessment.** The CSEA evaluates whether your cooperative meets all statutory requirements for COC issuance or renewal.
- **Inactive cooperative intervention.** If your cooperative fails to operate within six months of registration, the CSEA intervenes to provide support, assistance, guidance, and close monitoring for the next six months.[^34] Failure to operate for one full year means the CSEA will deny COC issuance, rendering the cooperative officially inactive.

**Practical tip.** Do not wait for the CSEA to contact you about compliance. **Submit your CAPR and CAFS within 120 days after your fiscal year ends.** Hold your General Assembly within 90 days. Track these deadlines on your compliance calendar (see Chapter 9). A cooperative that submits on time gets processed faster and avoids the backlog that hits every provincial office in the final weeks before the deadline.

### 13.2.2 Training and Technical Assistance

Training is one of the most valuable services the CSEA offers -- and one of the most underutilized. Many cooperatives in BARMM do not know they can **request** training from the CSEA. They wait for announcements or invitations that may never come. Do not make this mistake.

**Pre-Registration Seminars (PRS).** The CSEA conducts or accredits institutions to conduct PRS for prospective cooperative members.[^35] The PRS is a mandatory requirement before registration. It covers cooperative principles, governance, financial management, and legal obligations. Your organizing group cannot register without PRS certificates for all members.

**Officer and management training.** The CSEA develops and delivers management training programs for elected officers, board members, committee members, and hired management staff.[^36] These programs cover:

- **Governance training** -- board responsibilities, fiduciary duties, conflict of interest rules
- **Financial management** -- bookkeeping, auditing standards, fund allocation
- **Operations management** -- business planning, supply chain, marketing
- **Compliance training** -- CAPR/CAFS preparation, COC requirements, reporting obligations

**Technical assistance.** Beyond formal training programs, the CSEA provides on-site technical assistance through its Provincial Offices and Cooperative Development Specialists.[^37] Technical assistance includes:

- **Business planning support** -- helping your cooperative develop or refine its business plan
- **Financial management advisory** -- reviewing your books, recommending improvements
- **Governance improvement** -- assessing your board's effectiveness, recommending structural changes
- **Market linkages** -- connecting your cooperative with buyers, suppliers, and partner organizations

**How to request training.** Write a formal letter addressed to the CSEA Executive Director (for central office programs) or the Provincial Director (for provincial programs). State the specific training you need, the number of participants, your preferred schedule, and your cooperative's registration number. Attach your latest COR and COC. Follow up within two weeks if you do not receive a response.

**Partnership training programs.** The CSEA also delivers training through partnerships with other agencies and organizations. One notable example is the **3ZERO Initiative** -- a partnership with the **Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR)**, the **World Food Programme (WFP)**, and **ACTED** -- which conducts the Agri-Fishery Cooperatives' Partners Forum for sustainable and inclusive agriculture. Watch for announcements of these partnership programs. They often provide training at no cost to participating cooperatives.

### 13.2.3 Financial Assistance Programs (PESO Project, LAKAS SE)

The Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028 identifies two flagship programs under the CSEA that provide direct financial and technical support to cooperatives.[^38] Understand these programs. If your cooperative qualifies, they can be transformative.

**The Development of Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Program.** This program aims to strengthen cooperative capacities to ensure profitability and functionality.[^39] It includes two projects:

**The PESO Project (Profitability and Equity Support Oriented).** PESO provides financial and technical assistance to improve cooperative profitability.[^40] The name captures the program's dual focus: it is not just about giving money (financial assistance) but about building the internal capacity (equity support) that makes the money productive. PESO targets cooperatives that are registered and operational but struggling with profitability -- exactly the challenge the BDP 2023-2028 identifies as the central problem facing Bangsamoro cooperatives.

**The LAKAS SE Program (Lakbay Aral sa Social Enterprise).** LAKAS SE is a learning program for social enterprise development.[^41] It provides structured learning experiences -- including study tours, peer exchanges, and mentoring -- for cooperatives and social enterprises. If your cooperative is exploring the social enterprise pathway discussed in earlier chapters, LAKAS SE is the program that will build your SE capability.

**The Combatant Transitional Program.** This program is covered in detail in Section 13.5. It includes two projects -- the **STOCK Project** and the **Cooperative Financial and Material Support Project** -- designed specifically for cooperatives organized by former combatants.

**How to access these programs.** Contact your CSEA Provincial Office and ask about the current call for applications. Financial assistance programs typically have eligibility criteria, application windows, and documentation requirements. Prepare the following before you apply:

1. **Updated COR and COC** -- your registration and compliance must be current
2. **Audited financial statements** -- the CSEA will assess your cooperative's financial health
3. **Business plan** -- you must demonstrate how the financial assistance will be used and how it will improve profitability
4. **Board resolution** -- your board must formally authorize the application
5. **Training records** -- evidence that your officers and members have completed relevant CSEA training programs

**Practical tip.** Programs like PESO and LAKAS SE have limited funding. They prioritize cooperatives that demonstrate **compliance**, **governance quality**, and **clear business plans**. The cooperatives that get funded are not the ones with the biggest problems. They are the ones that have done the preparatory work and can show they will use the assistance productively. Start preparing now, even if the current call has closed.

## 13.3 The Role of LGU Cooperative Development Officers

Your LGU Cooperative Development Officer (CDO) is the government official closest to your cooperative's daily operations. The CDO works at the municipal, city, or provincial level -- right where your cooperative operates. A strong relationship with your CDO makes everything easier: registration support, compliance guidance, access to local programs, and coordination with the CSEA.

### 13.3.1 Functions Under RA 11535 and BTA Bill 210, Section 20

**The legal basis.** Republic Act No. 11535 (2021) mandated all LGUs to appoint **Cooperative Development Officers**.[^42] BTA Bill 210, Section 20 reinforces this mandate and adds functions specific to the Bangsamoro context.[^43] Together, these two laws define what your CDO is supposed to do.

**The CDO's eight core functions under BTA Bill 210.** Section 20 enumerates the following functions:[^44]

**1. Facilitate organizing strategies.** Your CDO helps communities organize cooperatives. If your barangay wants to form a cooperative but does not know where to start, the CDO is the first person to approach. The CDO assists with organizing meetings, identifying potential members, and connecting the group with PRS providers.

**2. Provide customized guidance and technical assistance.** The CDO provides cooperative-specific advice on **financial efficiency**, **operational effectiveness**, and **market responsiveness**.[^45] This is not generic advice. The CDO should know your cooperative's business, your members' challenges, and your local market conditions.

**3. Assess and monitor performance.** The CDO assesses cooperative performance and advises on compliance with laws and regulations.[^46] The results of these assessments must be reported to the CSEA. This means your CDO is both your advisor and a monitoring channel. Cooperate fully with performance assessments. They benefit you more than the CSEA -- the assessment findings tell you what to fix before the CSEA inspection arrives.

**4. Coordinate capacity building.** The CDO coordinates with the CSEA, relevant agencies, and private sector organizations for **capacity-building activities** and **access to funding**.[^47] Think of the CDO as your broker for external resources. The CDO knows what programs are available -- CSEA training, MAFAR agricultural programs, MTIT market linkages, MSSD social protection programs -- and can connect your cooperative to the right opportunities.

**5. Implement specialized programs.** The CDO initiates and implements programs in line with the **Master Plan for Cooperative Development in the Bangsamoro**, with emphasis on **sustainability and resilience**.[^48]

**6. Submit biannual reports.** The CDO compiles and submits comprehensive **biannual reports** to the CSEA about the progress, challenges, opportunities, and evolving needs of cooperatives in the jurisdiction.[^49] Your cooperative's data feeds into these reports. Accurate reporting by the CDO leads to better-targeted CSEA programs for your area.

**7. Foster strategic partnerships.** The CDO facilitates partnerships among cooperatives, industry stakeholders, and other relevant organizations to promote cooperation, mutual support, and collective growth.[^50]

**8. Additional functions.** The CDO performs additional functions as prescribed by the CSEA, the LGU, and other relevant laws, with focus on **emerging trends** and the **dynamic needs** of the cooperative sector.[^51]

**The problem.** The BDP 2023-2028 identifies a critical gap: **most LGUs in the Bangsamoro Region have no appointed Municipal Cooperative Officers.**[^52] Regional cooperative officers find it difficult to coordinate at the LGU level without municipal counterparts. This is one of the root causes of the 11.66 percent compliance rate. Where there is no CDO, cooperatives are essentially on their own.

If your municipality has not yet appointed a CDO, raise the issue with your local government. RA 11535 makes the appointment mandatory. A letter from your cooperative to the municipal mayor -- citing the law and requesting the appointment -- is a legitimate and constructive step.

### 13.3.2 How to Work Effectively with Your LGU-CDO

Having a CDO is one thing. Working effectively with them is another. Here are practical guidelines:

**Introduce your cooperative formally.** Send a courtesy letter to the CDO after registration. Attach your COR, list of officers, and a brief description of your business. This establishes you as a cooperative that communicates professionally.

**Include the CDO in your General Assembly.** Invite the CDO as a resource person or observer at your annual and special general assemblies. The CDO gains firsthand knowledge of your cooperative's condition, and you gain access to real-time government guidance.

**Share your CAPR and CAFS.** Give the CDO a copy of your annual reports. The CDO needs this data for the biannual report to the CSEA. And the CDO can spot compliance issues in your reports before the CSEA does -- giving you time to correct them.

**Request assistance in writing.** When you need help -- training, market linkages, technical advice -- put the request in writing. A written request creates a record, triggers a formal response obligation, and shows the CDO's office that cooperative demand exists. Verbal requests are easily forgotten.

**Attend CDO-organized events.** When the CDO organizes cooperative summits, compliance drives, or training sessions, show up. Active cooperatives get prioritized for resources. Absent cooperatives get overlooked.

**Coordinate, do not depend.** Your CDO is a partner, not your manager. Your cooperative must maintain its own records, meet its own deadlines, and build its own capacity. The CDO supports your efforts. The CDO does not replace them.

## 13.4 The Broader Cooperative Ecosystem

Beyond the CSEA and the LGU-CDO, your cooperative exists within a broader ecosystem of legislative oversight bodies, national agencies, development partners, and civil society organizations. Knowing these actors helps you access resources, anticipate policy changes, and build alliances.

### 13.4.1 The Committee on Cooperatives and Social Enterprises (BTA Parliament)

The **Bangsamoro Transition Authority (BTA) Parliament** exercises legislative oversight over the cooperative sector through its committee system. The committee responsible for cooperative legislation reviews bills, conducts hearings, and recommends policy measures that directly affect your cooperative.

**What this committee does for your cooperative.** The committee reviews and deliberates on proposed legislation affecting cooperatives and social enterprises -- including BTA Bill 210, the proposed Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code that is referenced throughout this guidebook.[^53] When this bill is enacted, it becomes the primary legal framework for your cooperative.

The committee also conducts **oversight hearings** to assess how well the CSEA is performing its mandate. If your cooperative has systemic concerns about CSEA services -- slow processing, unresponsive provincial offices, unclear regulations -- these concerns can be raised during oversight hearings. Your cooperative federation or union is the appropriate channel for communicating these concerns to the committee.

**How you connect to Parliament.** You do not engage the committee directly as an individual cooperative. Engagement happens through your **federation or union**, through the **CSEA** (which is regularly called to committee hearings), or through formal consultations that the committee conducts with the cooperative sector. When the committee announces public hearings on cooperative legislation, attend or submit a written position paper. Your voice matters during the legislative process -- not after the law is passed.

### 13.4.2 CDA and National Government Coordination

The **Cooperative Development Authority (CDA)** is the national government agency that regulates cooperatives across the Philippines under **Republic Act 9520** (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008).[^54] The relationship between the CDA and the CSEA is defined by the Bangsamoro Organic Law and the transition process.

**Before BARMM.** In the former ARMM, the CDA was the sole agency mandated to register cooperatives. CDA policies and guidelines governed all cooperative activities in the region.

**The transition.** With the establishment of BARMM, cooperative regulation transitioned from the CDA to the CSEA. The CSEA is now in its **transition phase** -- building institutional capacity, establishing provincial offices, and developing Bangsamoro-specific policies.[^55] During this transition, several practical realities affect your cooperative:

- **Cooperatives registered under the CDA** continue to be recognized. You do not need to re-register with the CSEA immediately. However, watch for transitory provisions once BTA Bill 210 is enacted -- these may require re-registration or compliance updates within a specified period.
- **National cooperative policies** still apply in areas not yet covered by Bangsamoro-specific regulations. RA 9520 remains in force as the baseline law. BTA Bill 210 will supplement and, in some areas, supersede RA 9520 upon enactment.
- **CDA training and programs** may still be available to BARMM cooperatives, particularly those operated by national government agencies or funded by national programs.

**What the CSEA uniquely has.** A provision-by-provision legal analysis reveals that the CSEA possesses several powers and institutional features that the CDA does not have.[^81] These unique features reflect the Bangsamoro's distinct constitutional status:

| CSEA Unique Feature | Significance |
|---|---|
| **Social enterprise jurisdiction** | The CDA's authority covers cooperatives only. The CSEA regulates **both cooperatives and social enterprises** -- a broader developmental scope.[^82] |
| **Organic-law authority** | The CDA derives its authority from ordinary legislation (RA 11364). The CSEA's authority traces to the **Bangsamoro Organic Law** -- a constitutionally mandated enactment with higher normative weight.[^83] |
| **Islamic banking and Shari'ah integration** | The BOL empowers the Bangsamoro Government to establish **Islamic banking** and **Shari'ah-compliant financial institutions** (BOL Art. XIII, Secs. 29-30). The CDA's national framework does not address Islamic cooperative finance.[^84] |
| **Express poverty reduction mandate** | BAA 13 positions cooperatives as **platforms for poverty reduction**. The CDA's statutory language focuses on cooperatives as instruments of equity and economic development -- without the same targeted poverty reduction framing.[^85] |
| **Asymmetric intergovernmental status** | The CSEA is an **autonomous institution** within an asymmetric governmental framework. CDA regional offices are merely administrative extensions of the national agency.[^86] |

**What the CDA has that the CSEA currently lacks.** The comparative analysis also reveals **critical powers** the CDA exercises under RA 11364 and RA 9520 that the CSEA does not yet possess.[^87] These gaps create regulatory voids that affect your cooperative:

| CDA Power | Impact of CSEA Gap |
|---|---|
| **Quasi-judicial dispute resolution** | The CDA hears and decides inter- and intra-cooperative disputes, with decisions appealable to the Court of Appeals. The CSEA currently **lacks a dedicated regional dispute forum**. |
| **Cease and desist orders** | The CDA can immediately halt harmful cooperative activities. The CSEA **cannot issue cease and desist orders**. |
| **Suspension, cancellation, dissolution, liquidation** | The CDA can terminate non-compliant cooperatives. The CSEA **cannot currently order dissolution** of a non-compliant cooperative. |
| **Conservatorship** | The CDA appoints conservators for illiquid cooperatives to protect member deposits. The CSEA **lacks this rehabilitation mechanism**. |
| **Training institution accreditation** | The CDA accredits non-academic cooperative training institutions. The CSEA **cannot standardize training quality** through accreditation. |
| **Capital assessment authority** | The CDA periodically adjusts paid-up capital requirements. The CSEA **cannot calibrate financial thresholds** for cooperatives. |
| **Apex organization recognition** | The CDA recognizes apex organizations and a national cooperative alliance. The CSEA **lacks authority to recognize hierarchical cooperative structures**. |
| **Merger, consolidation, and division jurisdiction** | The CDA processes structural changes. The CSEA **cannot adjudicate mergers or consolidations**. |

**Why BTA Bill 210 is critical.** The passage of BTA Bill 210 (the **Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code**) is the most important legislative priority for closing these gaps.[^88] Until its enactment, the CSEA's regulatory authority remains **incomplete** relative to the CDA. Your cooperative should monitor this bill's progress through the BTA Parliament. Its enactment will transform the CSEA from an agency with strong organic-law foundations but limited enforcement tools into a fully empowered regulatory and developmental authority.

**Coordination mechanisms.** The CSEA coordinates with the CDA on matters of national policy, data sharing, and cooperative master listing. If your cooperative operates across regions -- for example, a Cotabato-based cooperative with suppliers in Region XII -- you may need to engage both the CSEA and the CDA. In such cases, ask your CSEA Provincial Office for guidance on the jurisdictional boundaries.

### 13.4.3 Development Partners, NGOs, and Civil Society Organizations

The Bangsamoro cooperative sector benefits from significant support by **development partners**, **non-governmental organizations (NGOs)**, and **civil society organizations (CSOs)**. These organizations provide funding, technical assistance, training, and market access that complement government programs.

**Key categories of partners.**

**International development organizations.** Several international organizations have cooperative development programs in BARMM. The **World Food Programme (WFP)** partners with the CSEA and MAFAR on the **3ZERO Initiative**, which supports agri-fishery cooperatives through the Agri-Fishery Cooperatives' Partners Forum.[^56] Other international partners operate in areas such as livelihood development, conflict transformation, and economic resilience.

**National NGOs and CSOs.** Philippine-based organizations provide cooperative training, community organizing, and social enterprise development services in the Bangsamoro. These organizations often work directly with communities and can provide ground-level support that government agencies cannot always deliver at scale.

**Academic institutions.** Universities and training institutions in the region provide **accredited Pre-Registration Seminars**, cooperative management courses, and research on cooperative development. The **Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education (MBHTE)** also supports cooperative education in schools and universities.

**Government agency partners.** Beyond the CSEA, several BARMM ministries have programs relevant to cooperatives:

| Agency | Relevance to Your Cooperative |
|---|---|
| **MAFAR** (Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform) | Agricultural cooperative programs, farmer organization support, agrarian reform beneficiary organizations |
| **MTIT** (Trade, Investments, and Tourism) | Market linkages, trade fairs, export assistance, tourism enterprise development |
| **MSSD** (Social Services and Development) | Social protection programs, community development, poverty reduction |
| **MOLE** (Labor and Employment) | Livelihood programs, employment facilitation, skills training |
| **MOST** (Science and Technology) | Technology transfer, innovation support, research and development |

**How to engage partners.** Do not wait for partners to find you. **Actively seek partnerships** through these channels:

1. **Attend CSEA-organized forums and trade fairs.** These events are specifically designed to connect cooperatives with potential partners.
2. **Join your cooperative federation or union.** Federations have wider networks and regularly engage with development partners on behalf of member cooperatives.
3. **Monitor the CSEA website and social media** for announcements of partnership programs and calls for applications.
4. **Register on the CSEA cooperative masterlist.** This public directory makes your cooperative visible to potential partners searching for local cooperatives.
5. **Participate in LGU development planning.** Your barangay and municipal development plans often include cooperative development components with attached resources.

## 13.5 The Combatant Transitional Program

The **Combatant Transitional Program** is one of the most distinctive features of the Bangsamoro cooperative ecosystem. It exists because the BARMM was born from a peace process. Thousands of former combatants from the **Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF)** and other groups needed a pathway from armed struggle to legitimate economic participation. Cooperatives became that pathway.

This section explains the program, its projects, and what it means for cooperatives organized by or including former combatants.

### 13.5.1 Cooperatives for Former Combatants

**The context.** The BDP 2023-2028 identifies the **Combatant Transitional Program** as one of two priority programs under the Cooperatives and Social Enterprise sub-sector.[^57] The program aims to **support former rebels to organize themselves into cooperatives** in order to legally conduct businesses.[^58] It also provides financial and material support to cooperatives organized by former combatants to aid them in their business operations.

**Why cooperatives for combatants face unique challenges.** The BDP 2023-2028 is candid about the difficulties. The CSEA transitioned from the CDA, and the policies and guidelines for cooperatives were "predominantly aligned with the previous entity" -- meaning the CDA's national framework.[^59] The newly registered cooperatives, which were **mainly composed of former combatants**, encountered difficulties in complying with these requirements. In other words, the rules were designed for a different context. Former combatants -- many of whom have limited formal education, little business experience, and long histories outside the formal economy -- found conventional cooperative compliance requirements difficult to meet.

This is not a failure of the combatants. It is a failure of the system to adapt its requirements to the realities of transitional populations. The CSEA's mandate under BTA Bill 210 explicitly addresses this gap by including **combatant reintegration** as a core function.[^60]

**What this means for your cooperative.** If your cooperative was organized through the Combatant Transitional Program, you are not starting from a position of weakness. You are starting from a position of **policy priority**. The Bangsamoro Government has committed resources, programs, and institutional attention to your success. The challenge is to **convert that priority into operational competence** -- which means investing in training, compliance, and governance from the very beginning.

If your cooperative is not part of the Combatant Transitional Program but operates in a community with former combatants, consider this: **cooperation among cooperatives** is one of the seven ICA Principles. Offering support, partnership, or mentorship to a combatant-transition cooperative is both principled and strategic. It builds community cohesion, expands your network, and demonstrates the values that the cooperative movement is built on.

### 13.5.2 STOCK Project and Financial Support

The Combatant Transitional Program includes two projects designed to address the specific needs of former combatant cooperatives.[^61]

**The STOCK Project (Strengthening of Technical and Operational Knowledge).** STOCK is a capacity-building project that targets the core capability gap facing former combatant cooperatives.[^62] The name captures the approach: before a cooperative can use financial support productively, it must have the **technical knowledge** (bookkeeping, compliance, reporting) and **operational knowledge** (business planning, marketing, supply chain management) to run a viable enterprise.

STOCK provides:

- **Management training** tailored to the experience level and educational background of former combatants
- **Technical skills development** in cooperative governance, financial management, and regulatory compliance
- **Operational mentoring** through hands-on guidance on business operations, market access, and product development

**The Cooperative Financial and Material Support Project.** This project provides **direct financial and material assistance** to cooperatives organized by former combatants.[^63] It complements STOCK by ensuring that newly trained cooperatives have the capital and physical resources to begin or expand operations.

Financial and material support may include:

- **Seed capital or grant financing** for cooperative start-up activities
- **Equipment and materials** for production, processing, or trading operations
- **Operational subsidies** during the initial period of cooperative operations
- **Market linkage support** connecting cooperative products to buyers

**How the two projects work together.** STOCK builds capability. The Financial and Material Support Project provides resources. Together, they address both sides of the equation: a cooperative that receives money without capability wastes the money, and a cooperative that receives training without resources cannot apply the training. The program's design recognizes that both are necessary.

**Eligibility and access.** Cooperatives seeking to access the Combatant Transitional Program should:

1. **Ensure registration with the CSEA** -- your cooperative must hold a valid COR
2. **Demonstrate combatant membership** -- a significant portion of your members must be verified former combatants as defined by the program criteria
3. **Apply through the CSEA Provincial Office** -- the Provincial Director can advise on current program availability, documentation requirements, and application timelines
4. **Participate in STOCK training** -- financial support is typically conditioned on participation in the capacity-building component
5. **Prepare a business plan** -- even grant programs require a clear plan for how funds will be used and what outcomes are expected

**A word about sustainability.** Grant financing and material support are catalysts, not permanent subsidies. The Combatant Transitional Program is designed to **transition** cooperatives from dependence on external support to self-sustaining profitability. Your cooperative should plan from day one for the point when program support ends. Build your capital reserves. Diversify your revenue sources. Invest in member skills. The measure of the program's success is not how much support your cooperative receives -- it is how well your cooperative performs after the support ends.

## Key Takeaways

**The CSEA is your primary government partner.** It registers your cooperative, monitors your compliance, provides training, administers financial programs, mediates disputes, and coordinates with other agencies. Know your Provincial Director. Visit your Provincial Office regularly. Request services in writing.

**Understand the four divisions.** The Administrative and Finance Division handles operations. The Cooperative Development Division handles registration, compliance, training, and monitoring. The Social Enterprise Division handles SE accreditation and development. The Shari'ah and Legal Division handles legal review and Shari'ah compliance. Direct your inquiries to the right division.

**Your CDO is your local government partner.** The LGU Cooperative Development Officer facilitates organizing, provides guidance, monitors performance, and connects you to resources. If your municipality has not appointed a CDO, advocate for the appointment under RA 11535.

**The CSEA has unique powers the CDA lacks -- and vice versa.** The CSEA has social enterprise jurisdiction, organic-law authority, and Islamic banking integration. The CDA has quasi-judicial powers, cease and desist orders, and dissolution authority that the CSEA currently lacks. **BTA Bill 210** closes these gaps. Monitor its progress.

**The ecosystem extends beyond government.** Development partners, NGOs, academic institutions, and fellow cooperatives are all part of the network that supports your success. Actively seek partnerships through the CSEA, your federation, and LGU development planning processes.

**The Combatant Transitional Program is a national priority.** STOCK builds knowledge. Financial support provides resources. Together, they transition former combatant cooperatives from startup to sustainability. Access the program through your CSEA Provincial Office.

**Do not operate in isolation.** The cooperative that knows the ecosystem and uses it well is the cooperative that survives, grows, and fulfills its mission.
