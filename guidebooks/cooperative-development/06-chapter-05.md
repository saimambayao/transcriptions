# Chapter 5 — Governance: The General Assembly, Board, and Committees

A cooperative is only as strong as its governance. The seaweed cooperative in Sitangkai with PHP 5 million in assets and the multi-purpose cooperative in Cotabato City with PHP 50 million share the same governance challenge: how to make decisions that serve all members, not just a few.

Governance is not bureaucracy. It is the system that turns collective ownership into collective benefit. When governance works, a fishing cooperative in Basilan distributes its surplus fairly and elects leaders its members trust. When governance fails, the same cooperative becomes a vehicle for a few insiders who treat member funds as personal capital. The difference is structure -- the rules, meetings, elections, and accountability mechanisms you will build in this chapter.

Your cooperative's governance has three layers. The **General Assembly** sets the direction. The **Board of Directors** steers day-to-day strategy. The **committees** perform oversight, resolve disputes, and run elections. Together, these three layers create a system of checks and balances that protects every member's interest.

This chapter walks you through each layer. By the end, you will know how to run a General Assembly that reaches quorum, build a Board that leads without overreaching, staff your mandatory committees, and conduct elections that your members trust.

---

## 5.1 The General Assembly: Highest Policy-Making Body

The General Assembly is your cooperative's supreme authority. No board resolution, no committee recommendation, and no officer decision can override what the General Assembly decides within its powers. RA 9520 is explicit: "The general assembly shall be the highest policy-making body of the cooperative."[^1] BTA Bill No. 210 carries this forward in identical terms.[^2]

Think of the General Assembly as the cooperative's parliament. Every voting member has a seat. Every member gets one vote. The Assembly decides the biggest questions: who leads, how surplus is distributed, and whether the cooperative merges, dissolves, or amends its foundational documents.

### 5.1.1 Powers of the General Assembly (What Cannot Be Delegated)

The law identifies **three exclusive powers** that the General Assembly can never hand off to the Board or any committee:[^3]

1. **Approve and amend the Articles of Cooperation and Bylaws.** Only the full membership can change the cooperative's foundational documents.
2. **Elect the Board of Directors and remove them for cause.** The Assembly chooses its leaders and removes them if they fail.
3. **Approve the cooperative's developmental plans.** Strategic direction comes from the membership, not from the Board alone.

These three powers are non-delegable. No matter how large your cooperative grows, no matter how dispersed your members become, these decisions must go before the General Assembly.

Beyond the exclusive powers, the General Assembly exercises broad authority over the cooperative's affairs:

| Category | Powers | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Constitutional** | Amend Articles of Cooperation and Bylaws | Protects the cooperative's foundational structure |
| **Electoral** | Elect Board of Directors, Audit Committee, Election Committee; remove officers for cause | Ensures democratic leadership selection |
| **Financial** | Approve annual budget, approve net surplus distribution, approve developmental plans | Keeps money decisions with the membership |
| **Corporate** | Approve mergers, consolidation, voluntary dissolution, major investments | Prevents the Board from making irreversible decisions alone |
| **Oversight** | Receive annual reports, receive audit findings, hold officers accountable | Ensures transparency and accountability |

**Can the General Assembly delegate any of its powers?** Yes -- but only under strict conditions. The law allows delegation by a **three-fourths (3/4) vote of all members with voting rights, present and constituting a quorum**.[^4] Even then, only the non-exclusive powers can be delegated. The three exclusive powers listed above stay with the Assembly, always.

> **Bangsamoro Example.** A multi-purpose cooperative in Lanao del Sur has 500 members spread across 12 barangays. Gathering all members for every decision is impractical. The General Assembly votes -- with 380 of 500 members present, exceeding the 3/4 threshold -- to delegate approval of operating loans under PHP 200,000 to the Board. The Assembly retains authority over budget approval, elections, and bylaws amendments. The delegation is recorded in the minutes and written into the bylaws.

### 5.1.2 Regular and Special Meetings: Notice, Quorum, Voting

**Regular meetings.** Your cooperative must hold at least one General Assembly per year. The law calls this the **Annual General Assembly (AGA)**. Hold it within **90 days after the close of your fiscal year**.[^5] If your fiscal year ends December 31, your AGA must happen by March 31. Fix the date in your bylaws so members can plan ahead.

**Special meetings.** Urgent matters cannot wait for the next AGA. A special meeting can be called in three ways:[^6]

1. **By majority vote of the Board of Directors.** The Board identifies an urgent issue and calls the meeting.
2. **By petition of at least 10% of voting members.** Members themselves force a meeting when the Board will not act. The Board must convene the meeting within one month of receiving the petition.
3. **By order of the Authority (CSEA).** If the Board ignores a valid member petition, CSEA can order the petitioners to call the meeting themselves.

**Notice requirements.** No meeting is valid without proper notice. The rules differ by meeting type:

| Meeting Type | Notice Period | Method | Content |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Annual General Assembly** | As fixed in bylaws (standard: 2 weeks) | Writing, posting, publication, or electronic means | Date, time, place, agenda |
| **Special Meeting** | At least **1 week** before the meeting | Written notice to all voting members | Date, time, place, specific business to be transacted |

**Send notice to all members of record.** Not just active members. Not just members who attend regularly. All members entitled to vote. A cooperative in Maguindanao del Norte that sends notice only through its Viber group chat and misses 30 members without smartphones has failed the notice requirement.

> **Practical Tip.** Create a **member contact registry** and update it every AGA. Include at least three contact methods for each member: mobile number, barangay address, and name of a contact person (spouse, adult child, neighbor). When you send notice, use multiple channels. Post it at the cooperative office, send it by text message, and ask barangay officials to announce it.

**Quorum.** No business can be conducted without a quorum. Under BTA Bill No. 210, a quorum consists of at least **25% of all members entitled to vote**.[^7] If your cooperative has 100 voting members, you need at least 25 present to start business.

Reaching quorum is one of the most common problems cooperatives face. The BDP 2023-2028 identifies the **11.66% compliance rate** of BARMM cooperatives as a critical challenge.[^8] Many cooperatives fail compliance precisely because they cannot hold valid General Assemblies.

**Strategies for reaching quorum:**

- **Schedule the AGA on a day when members are already gathered** -- after Friday prayers, during barangay assemblies, or on market days.
- **Provide clear incentives for attendance** -- not cash payments, but practical value like refreshments, distribution of patronage refunds, or training sessions held immediately before or after the AGA.
- **Use delegates for large cooperatives.** If your cooperative has "numerous and dispersed membership," the General Assembly may be composed of delegates elected by each sector, chapter, or district.[^9] Define the delegate system in your bylaws.

**Voting.** The voting system is straightforward but non-negotiable:

| Rule | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **One member, one vote** | Every member of a primary cooperative gets exactly one vote[^10] |
| **Regular decisions** | Majority of members present and constituting a quorum |
| **Amendments to Articles of Cooperation or Bylaws** | 2/3 of members present |
| **Voluntary dissolution** | 3/4 of all members |
| **Delegation of GA powers** | 3/4 of all members with voting rights, present and constituting a quorum |

**Proxy voting** is permitted only in secondary or tertiary cooperatives, and only if the bylaws explicitly allow it.[^11] In a primary cooperative, every member votes in person. No exceptions. This protects against manipulation -- no officer can collect proxy forms and vote on behalf of absent members.

> **What About Cooperative Banks?** If your cooperative is a cooperative bank, different quorum rules apply under Article 99 of RA 9520. Consult CSEA for specific requirements.

## 5.2 The Board of Directors: Strategic Direction and Oversight

If the General Assembly is the cooperative's parliament, the Board of Directors is its cabinet. The Board makes the strategic decisions between General Assemblies. It hires management, sets policy, approves contracts, and monitors performance. But it answers to the General Assembly -- not the other way around.

### 5.2.1 Composition, Qualifications, and Term of Office

**Size.** Your Board must have at least **5 members** and no more than **15 members**.[^12] The exact number is set in your bylaws. For most BARMM cooperatives, especially those with fewer than 100 members, a Board of 5-7 directors is practical. Larger cooperatives with complex operations may benefit from 9-11 directors. Avoid a Board of 15 unless your cooperative has thousands of members and highly diversified operations.

**Term.** Directors serve a term of **2 years** and hold office until their successors are duly elected and qualified, or until they are removed for cause.[^13] Your bylaws should specify whether the Board turns over entirely every two years or uses staggered terms (half the Board elected each year). Staggered terms preserve institutional memory.

**Qualifications.** To serve as a director, a person must:[^14]

1. Be a **voting member** of the cooperative
2. Be of **legal age** (at least 18 years old)
3. Be a **Filipino citizen**
4. Possess the **qualifications stated in the bylaws**
5. Have completed the **required training** (as prescribed by CSEA)

**Disqualifications.** The following persons cannot serve as directors:[^15]

1. **Any person engaged in a business similar to the cooperative** or who has a conflict of interest with it
2. **Any director who holds a position in the daily operations and management** of the cooperative
3. **Any person previously removed from office for cause**
4. **Any person convicted of an offense involving moral turpitude**
5. **Any person who has not met the training requirements** prescribed by CSEA
6. **Two or more persons related within the third civil degree of consanguinity or affinity** cannot simultaneously serve as officers[^16]

The **separation of governance and management** is critical. Directors set strategy. Managers execute it. A Board member cannot also serve as the General Manager, bookkeeper, or any position in daily operations.[^17] When one person holds both governance and management roles, oversight disappears. This rule exists for a reason -- enforce it.

> **Bangsamoro Example.** A credit cooperative in Cotabato City discovers that its Board chairperson also manages the cooperative's lending window. She approves her own loan applications. She sets the interest rates that benefit her borrowers. No one questions her because she holds both the governance and operations roles. This violates Section 37(3) of BTA Bill No. 210 and Article 39(3) of RA 9520. The General Assembly must require her to choose: chair the Board or manage the lending window. She cannot do both.

**Vacancy.** When a Board seat opens before the term expires -- through resignation, death, disqualification, or removal -- the remaining directors may fill it by majority vote, provided they still constitute a quorum. If the remaining directors no longer form a quorum, the vacancy must be filled by the General Assembly at a regular or special meeting.[^18] The replacement director serves only the unexpired portion of the predecessor's term.

### 5.2.2 Powers and Responsibilities of the Board

The Board of Directors carries four distinct roles:

**1. Strategic Role.** The Board sets the cooperative's direction. It formulates policies, approves strategic plans, and makes decisions that shape the cooperative's future. The General Assembly approves the developmental plan, but the Board translates that plan into annual objectives and quarterly targets.

**2. Oversight Role.** The Board monitors management performance. It reviews financial reports, tracks key performance indicators, and ensures the cooperative complies with laws, regulations, and its own bylaws. The Board does not run the cooperative's daily operations -- it watches over the people who do.

**3. Fiduciary Role.** Directors act as **trustees of the members' interests**. They must exercise a **duty of care** (making informed, diligent decisions) and a **duty of loyalty** (putting the cooperative's interests above their own). This fiduciary responsibility is not optional. It carries legal consequences. Directors who vote for unlawful acts, act with gross negligence, or acquire personal interests in conflict with their duty are **jointly and severally liable** for all damages.[^19]

**4. Administrative Role.** The Board hires and supervises the General Manager, determines officer compensation, and approves major contracts. It decides operational policies -- credit terms, procurement procedures, pricing -- that the Manager then implements.

| Role | What the Board Does | What the Board Does NOT Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategic** | Approves annual plan, sets objectives | Does not draft the business plan (that is management's job) |
| **Oversight** | Reviews monthly financial reports | Does not do the bookkeeping |
| **Fiduciary** | Ensures funds are used properly | Does not sign every check (Treasurer does) |
| **Administrative** | Hires the General Manager | Does not supervise individual staff (the GM does) |

> **The Line Between Board and Management.** The most common governance failure in BARMM cooperatives is a Board that runs the daily operations. When Board members handle procurement, manage the warehouse, or count inventory, they become both the doers and the overseers of their own work. No oversight remains. Draw the line clearly: **the Board decides what to do; the Manager decides how to do it.**

### 5.2.3 Board Meetings: Frequency, Quorum, and Minutes

**Frequency.** The Board of Directors must meet at least **once a month** for primary cooperatives.[^20] Monthly meetings keep the Board connected to the cooperative's operations, financial condition, and member concerns. Many well-run cooperatives meet twice a month -- once for regular business and once for a working session focused on a strategic issue.

**Special meetings.** The Chairperson or a majority of Board members can call a special meeting at any time, provided **written notice specifying the agenda** is given to all directors at least **one week** before the meeting.[^21]

**Quorum.** A majority of Board members constitutes a quorum.[^22] If your Board has 7 members, you need at least 4 present to conduct business. Decisions require a majority vote of the quorum.

**No proxy voting at Board meetings.** Directors cannot attend or vote by proxy.[^23] This rule is absolute. Every director who votes on a decision must be physically present (or present through whatever medium your bylaws allow) and must make their own judgment. Board governance requires personal participation, not delegated authority.

**Minutes.** Keep detailed minutes of every Board meeting. Record:

- Date, time, and place
- Directors present and absent
- Agenda items discussed
- Motions made, seconded, and voted on (record the vote count)
- Resolutions adopted (number them sequentially: Board Resolution No. 2026-001, etc.)
- Action items assigned, with deadlines and responsible persons
- Time of adjournment

**File copies of Board minutes with CSEA** within 30 days of the meeting date as part of your compliance obligations.[^24] Keep the original minutes book at the cooperative's registered office, accessible to members during business hours.

> **Practical Tip.** Assign a **Board Secretary** whose sole responsibility during meetings is to record minutes. Do not rely on a director to both participate in discussion and capture what was said. If your Secretary is also a director, consider appointing a minute-taker from the staff.

## 5.3 Officers of the Cooperative

Officers execute the Board's decisions and manage the cooperative's daily affairs. The law distinguishes between **elected officers** (chosen by the Board from among themselves) and **appointed officers** (selected by the Board from within or outside the Board).

### 5.3.1 Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson, Secretary, Treasurer

**Chairperson.** The Board elects the Chairperson from among themselves.[^25] The Chairperson:

- Presides over Board meetings and General Assemblies
- Represents the cooperative in official dealings
- Signs documents and contracts as authorized by the Board
- Ensures Board resolutions are carried out

The Chairperson is the cooperative's public face. Choose someone who commands respect, communicates clearly, and understands the cooperative's mission. The Chairperson does not manage the cooperative's day-to-day operations -- that is the General Manager's job.

**Vice-Chairperson.** Also elected from among the Board members.[^25] The Vice-Chairperson steps in when the Chairperson is absent, incapacitated, or on leave. In many BARMM cooperatives, the Vice-Chairperson also chairs a major committee (such as the Education and Training Committee) to keep engaged between meetings.

**Secretary.** The Board appoints the Secretary either from among the directors or from outside the Board.[^25] The Secretary:

- Records and keeps minutes of all meetings (Board and General Assembly)
- Maintains the cooperative's records and correspondence
- Sends meeting notices to members and directors
- Certifies Board resolutions and other official documents
- Keeps the membership register current

**Treasurer.** Also appointed by the Board.[^25] The Treasurer:

- Has custody of the cooperative's funds
- Maintains accurate financial records
- Deposits funds in accounts authorized by the Board
- Signs checks and disbursement vouchers (usually co-signed with the Chairperson or Manager)
- Prepares financial reports for the Board and General Assembly
- Executes the **Treasurer's Affidavit** confirming the cooperative's paid-up capital

| Officer | Selected By | From | Key Responsibility |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chairperson** | Board elects | Among directors | Presides meetings, represents cooperative |
| **Vice-Chairperson** | Board elects | Among directors | Acts in Chairperson's absence |
| **Secretary** | Board appoints | Directors or outside | Records, notices, correspondence |
| **Treasurer** | Board appoints | Directors or outside | Funds custody, financial records |

**Surety bond requirement.** Every director, officer, and employee handling funds, securities, or property must be covered by a **surety bond** issued by a duly registered insurance or bonding company.[^26] The Board determines the bond amount. The bond guarantees faithful performance of duties. File the bond with CSEA upon registration and renew it annually.

> **Why the Surety Bond Matters.** A consumer cooperative in Basilan lost PHP 340,000 when its Treasurer absconded with the cooperative's working capital. Because the cooperative had maintained a surety bond, the bonding company compensated the loss. Without the bond, the members would have absorbed the entire loss. Treat the surety bond as insurance, not as paperwork.

### 5.3.2 The General Manager or Chief Executive Officer

The **General Manager (GM)** is hired by the Board to run the cooperative's daily operations.[^27] Unlike the Chairperson and other officers, the GM need not be a Board member. In fact, the GM should not be a Board member -- the GM reports to the Board, and you cannot effectively report to yourself.

The General Manager:

- Manages day-to-day operations
- Implements Board policies and resolutions
- Supervises all staff
- Prepares operational and financial reports for the Board
- Executes contracts and agreements as authorized by the Board
- Recommends policies, budgets, and plans to the Board for approval

**The GM is the bridge between governance and operations.** The Board tells the GM what the cooperative's goals are. The GM figures out how to achieve them and manages the people and resources needed to get there.

**Hiring the right GM.** Look for someone with:

- Management experience (not necessarily in cooperatives, but in managing people and operations)
- Financial literacy (the GM reviews financial reports even if the Treasurer prepares them)
- Communication skills (the GM interfaces with members, CSEA, LGU officials, and suppliers)
- Integrity (the GM handles procurement, inventory, and cash flow daily)

**Compensation.** The Board sets the GM's compensation. It should reflect the cooperative's size, financial capacity, and the local labor market. Do not overpay relative to the cooperative's revenue, but do not underpay and risk losing a competent manager. Tie a portion of compensation to performance metrics -- member growth, patronage levels, compliance milestones -- to align the GM's incentives with the membership's interests.

### 5.3.3 Qualifications and Disqualifications

All officers must meet the general qualifications for cooperative leaders:[^28]

**Qualifications:**
- Voting member of the cooperative (except the GM and staff, who need not be members)
- Of legal age
- Filipino citizen
- Good moral character
- Completed CSEA-required training

**Disqualifications (apply to all officers and directors):**
- Engaged in a business similar to the cooperative or having a conflict of interest
- Previously removed from office for cause in any cooperative
- Convicted of an offense involving moral turpitude
- Related within the third civil degree of consanguinity or affinity to another serving officer[^29]
- Failed to meet training requirements

**The nepotism rule deserves special attention.** The law prohibits two or more persons related within the third degree of consanguinity (blood) or affinity (marriage) from serving simultaneously as **appointive officers**.[^29] In many Bangsamoro communities, extended family networks are tight. A cooperative with 50 members might have 15 members from the same clan. The nepotism rule does not prevent clan members from being ordinary members -- it prevents them from concentrating power in officer positions.

| Degree | Consanguinity (Blood) | Affinity (Marriage) |
|---|---|---|
| **1st degree** | Parent, child | Parent-in-law, child-in-law |
| **2nd degree** | Sibling, grandparent, grandchild | Sibling-in-law |
| **3rd degree** | Uncle/aunt, nephew/niece, great-grandparent | Uncle/aunt-in-law, nephew/niece-in-law |

> **Bangsamoro Example.** In a producer cooperative in Maguindanao del Sur, the Treasurer is the sister of the Secretary. Both are appointed officers. This violates the third-degree consanguinity rule (siblings = second degree). One of them must step down. The Board cannot waive this requirement -- it comes from the law, not from the bylaws.

**Removal protections.** Officers serve during good behavior. They cannot be removed arbitrarily. The law requires:[^30]

- **Cause** -- a valid reason such as misconduct, negligence, or breach of fiduciary duty
- **Due process** -- the officer must be given an opportunity to be heard
- **"Loss of confidence" is not a valid ground** unless evidenced by specific acts or omissions causing loss of confidence in the officer's honesty and integrity

This protection exists to prevent political removal. A Board faction that dislikes a Treasurer cannot remove her simply by declaring they have "lost confidence." They must point to specific acts -- a missed report, an unauthorized disbursement, a financial discrepancy -- and give the Treasurer a chance to explain.

## 5.4 Mandatory and Optional Committees

Committees are the governance structure's working arms. While the Board decides, committees investigate, audit, mediate, and educate. The law requires certain committees and allows your cooperative to create additional ones as needed.

### 5.4.1 Audit Committee, Election Committee, Mediation and Conciliation Committee, Ethics Committee

**Your bylaws must provide for at least four committees:**[^31]

1. **Audit Committee**
2. **Election Committee**
3. **Mediation and Conciliation Committee**
4. **Ethics Committee**

In addition, your bylaws may create an **Executive Committee** and an **Education and Training Committee**, among others. Cooperatives operating under Islamic principles must add a **Shari'ah Committee** (see Section 5.4.2).

#### Audit Committee

The Audit Committee is the cooperative's internal watchdog. It reports directly to the General Assembly -- not to the Board.[^32] This independence is critical. If the Audit Committee reported to the Board, the Board could suppress unfavorable findings.

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Selection** | Elected by the General Assembly |
| **Composition** | At least 3 members; cannot be Board members |
| **Reports to** | General Assembly (not the Board) |
| **Removal** | Only by the General Assembly |

**Functions:**[^33]

1. **Continuously monitor** the adequacy and effectiveness of the cooperative's management control systems
2. **Audit the performance** of the cooperative and its various responsibility centers
3. **Review financial statements** before they are presented to the General Assembly
4. **Investigate irregularities** -- whether reported by members, discovered during audits, or flagged by management
5. **Report findings** directly to the General Assembly
6. **Recommend corrective actions** to the Board and the membership

> **What "Continuously Monitor" Means.** The Audit Committee does not audit once a year. It reviews management controls on an ongoing basis -- checking whether cash handling procedures are followed, whether procurement policies are observed, whether loan disbursements match Board approvals. The annual audit report to the General Assembly is the culmination of year-round oversight.

#### Election Committee

The Election Committee ensures fair and transparent elections. Like the Audit Committee, it is elected by the General Assembly and operates independently of the Board.[^34]

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Selection** | Elected by the General Assembly |
| **Composition** | At least 3 members; cannot be Board members |
| **Reports to** | General Assembly |
| **Tenure** | Until the next election is completed |

**Functions:**

1. **Promulgate election rules and regulations** -- who can run, how campaigns are conducted, how votes are cast
2. **Validate candidate qualifications** against the law and the bylaws
3. **Supervise the conduct of elections** -- set up voting, manage the ballot process, ensure secrecy of vote
4. **Canvass votes and certify results**
5. **Proclaim winning candidates**
6. **Resolve election protests** at the cooperative level

#### Mediation and Conciliation Committee

Internal disputes are inevitable. Members disagree about surplus distribution. Officers accuse each other of misconduct. Suppliers claim unpaid invoices. The Mediation and Conciliation Committee provides a first line of resolution before disputes escalate to CSEA or the courts.[^35]

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Selection** | Appointed by the Board of Directors |
| **Composition** | At least 3 members |
| **Jurisdiction** | Internal disputes only (member-to-member, member-to-officer, member-to-cooperative) |
| **Confidentiality** | All proceedings are confidential |

**Functions:**

1. **Hear complaints and grievances** against members, officers, or staff
2. **Mediate disputes** -- facilitate dialogue, propose compromises
3. **Recommend resolutions** to the Board
4. **Maintain confidentiality** throughout the process

The Committee's power is **recommendatory**. It cannot impose sanctions. It submits its findings and recommendations to the Board, which then decides on the appropriate action. For disputes that involve potential criminal liability or claims beyond the cooperative's internal jurisdiction, refer the matter to the proper authorities.

> **Bangsamoro Context.** In many Bangsamoro communities, dispute resolution follows traditional mechanisms -- the intervention of respected elders, community leaders, or religious authorities. The Mediation and Conciliation Committee can integrate these traditions. Appoint committee members who are respected for their fairness and wisdom. Use consultation (*shura*) as the primary method. The goal is resolution, not punishment.

#### Ethics Committee

The Ethics Committee develops and enforces the cooperative's moral and ethical standards.[^36]

| Element | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Selection** | Appointed by the Board of Directors |
| **Composition** | At least 3 members |
| **Authority** | Recommendatory only; the Board decides on sanctions |

**Functions:**

1. **Develop and maintain the Code of Ethics and Conduct** -- the cooperative's written standard of behavior for officers, staff, and members
2. **Investigate ethical violations** reported by members or detected during monitoring
3. **Conduct hearings** on alleged violations
4. **Recommend sanctions** to the Board (reprimand, suspension, removal recommendation, or other penalties defined in the bylaws)
5. **Promote ethical behavior** through education, orientation, and regular communication

The Ethics Committee works closely with the Mediation and Conciliation Committee. The difference: Mediation handles disputes between parties; Ethics handles violations of the cooperative's moral standards by individuals.

#### Education and Training Committee

Though not always listed among the "mandatory" committees in the Cooperative Code, CSEA guidelines strongly encourage every cooperative to have an Education and Training Committee.[^37] This committee:

1. Plans and implements **education and training programs** for members, officers, and staff
2. Oversees the **Pre-Registration Seminar (PRS)** for prospective members
3. Ensures that officers and directors complete the **mandatory training** prescribed by CSEA
4. Manages the **Cooperative Education and Training Fund (CETF)** -- the portion of net surplus allocated to training
5. Coordinates with **CSEA-accredited trainers** and training institutions

| Committee | Selected By | Reports To | Key Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Audit** | General Assembly | General Assembly | Financial and operational oversight |
| **Election** | General Assembly | General Assembly | Fair and transparent elections |
| **Mediation** | Board of Directors | Board | Internal dispute resolution |
| **Ethics** | Board of Directors | Board | Ethical standards and enforcement |
| **Education & Training** | Board of Directors | Board | Member and officer development |

### 5.4.2 The Shari'ah Committee (BTA Bill 210, Section 9)

This is a provision unique to the Bangsamoro context. BTA Bill No. 210, Section 9 establishes the **Shari'ah Governance Framework** and requires cooperatives that operate under Islamic principles to maintain a **Shari'ah Committee**.[^38]

Section 41(b) of the same bill states directly: "Those who choose to operate under Islamic principles and the Shari'ah governance framework shall include in its Bylaws the creation of a Shari'ah Committee."[^39]

**Composition.** The Shari'ah Committee must have at least **three members who have competence on Shari'ah**.[^38] "Competence on Shari'ah" means members who have formal education or recognized expertise in Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*), Islamic finance, or Shari'ah-compliant business practices.

**Functions of the Shari'ah Committee:**

1. **Review all cooperative transactions** for compliance with Shari'ah principles -- no riba (usury), no maysir (gambling), no gharar (excessive uncertainty), no haram products or services[^40]
2. **Advise the Board** on Shari'ah-compliant alternatives when conventional approaches conflict with Islamic principles
3. **Certify that the cooperative's products and services are halal** and compliant with the Shari'ah Governance Framework
4. **Review contracts and agreements** before execution to ensure they meet Islamic finance standards
5. **Issue Shari'ah compliance reports** to the Board and the General Assembly
6. **Educate members and officers** on Islamic principles relevant to cooperative operations

**When is the Shari'ah Committee required?** The law says "those who choose to operate under Islamic principles." In practice, most cooperatives in BARMM serve predominantly Muslim communities. Even if your cooperative does not formally adopt the Shari'ah Governance Framework, consider establishing a Shari'ah Committee to build member trust and ensure your operations align with the values of your community.

| Conventional Practice | Shari'ah Concern | Compliant Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Interest on loans | Riba (usury) prohibited | **Murabaha** (cost-plus financing) |
| Interest on savings deposits | Riba prohibited | **Wadiah** (safekeeping) or **Mudarabah** (profit-sharing) |
| Conventional insurance | Gharar (uncertainty) | **Takaful** (mutual guarantee) |
| Joint ventures with unclear terms | Gharar prohibited | **Musharakah** (partnership with clear terms) |
| Raffle prizes at AGA | Maysir (gambling) | Member appreciation based on patronage |

> **Bangsamoro Example.** A seaweed marketing cooperative in Tawi-Tawi established a Shari'ah Committee composed of a local ustadz with a degree in Islamic studies, a retired AAIIBP-trained Islamic finance practitioner, and a senior member who completed the CSEA training on Shari'ah-compliant cooperatives. The committee reviewed the cooperative's lending program and found that the "service charge" on loans functioned as interest (riba). The committee recommended converting to a murabaha arrangement where the cooperative purchases supplies on behalf of members and sells them at a disclosed markup. Members agreed -- they understood the difference between a transparent markup and a hidden interest charge.

### 5.4.3 How Committees Report to the Board and GA

Clear reporting lines prevent committees from becoming independent fiefdoms or, worse, becoming powerless bodies that nobody listens to. Here is the reporting structure:

**Committees elected by the General Assembly** (Audit and Election) report directly to the General Assembly. They submit written reports at the AGA. The Board cannot suppress, edit, or delay these reports. If the Audit Committee finds irregularities in the Board's own spending, the report goes to the membership unchanged.

**Committees appointed by the Board** (Mediation, Ethics, Education, Shari'ah, and any optional committees) report to the Board on a regular basis -- typically monthly or quarterly. The Board acts on their recommendations. At the AGA, these committees also present summaries of their work so the membership stays informed.

**Reporting schedule:**

| Committee | Reports To | Frequency | At the AGA |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Audit** | General Assembly | Continuous monitoring; annual report | Full written report presented directly |
| **Election** | General Assembly | As needed during election period | Election results report |
| **Mediation** | Board | After each case resolution | Summary of cases handled (names redacted) |
| **Ethics** | Board | Quarterly or after each investigation | Summary of ethical matters addressed |
| **Education** | Board | Quarterly | Training programs conducted, CETF utilization |
| **Shari'ah** | Board and GA | Quarterly to Board; annual to GA | Shari'ah compliance assessment |

**Document everything.** Every committee should maintain its own minutes book. Record who attended, what was discussed, what was recommended, and what action the Board or GA took on the recommendation. These records become part of the cooperative's institutional memory and CSEA compliance documentation.

## 5.5 Moral Governance in Practice

BTA Bill No. 210, Section 8 introduces a concept that goes beyond the standard cooperative governance framework: **moral governance**.[^41] The bill defines moral governance as "an approach in governance of cooperatives and social enterprises that is guided by moral principles of enjoining what is right and forbidding what is wrong."

This is not a vague aspiration. It is a legal requirement. The bill mandates that cooperatives adopt their own code of moral principles and ethical standards, and that CSEA issue guidelines for applying moral governance to cooperatives and social enterprises in BARMM.

### 5.5.1 Developing Your Cooperative's Code of Moral Principles

The **12 Pillars of Moral Governance** provide the framework for your cooperative's code.[^42] These pillars are drawn from the Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028 and reflect the values that the Bangsamoro government applies to all its institutions. Apply them to your cooperative:

| Pillar | What It Means | How It Applies to Your Cooperative |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Integrity** | No financial or other binding in execution of official duties | Officers do not accept gifts, commissions, or favors from suppliers. Board members declare conflicts of interest before voting on any matter that affects them personally. |
| **2. Accountability** | Open and transparent decisions with documentation | Every decision is recorded in minutes. Every peso spent is documented in financial records. Officers answer questions from members without evasion. |
| **3. Transparency and Honesty** | Public declaration of private interests | Officers submit annual disclosure statements listing their business interests, debts, and relationships that could create conflicts. Financial statements are posted in the cooperative office. |
| **4. Sense of Patriotism** | Investment in heritage and reputation | The cooperative contributes to community development. It supports Bangsamoro cultural activities. It takes pride in being a Bangsamoro institution. |
| **5. Striving for Excellence** | Highest quality with process over results | Do not cut corners in compliance. Submit reports on time. Train officers continuously. Improve services every year. |
| **6. Cultural Understanding and Tolerance** | Awareness of socioeconomic differences | Respect the diversity within your membership -- Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, Iranun, indigenous peoples, and settler communities. Make every member feel welcomed. |
| **7. Unwavering Loyalty to God** | Following rules and regulations guided by faith | For cooperatives in Muslim-majority communities, this means aligning operations with Islamic principles. For all cooperatives, it means operating with a sense of higher purpose beyond profit. |
| **8. Justice** | Serving public without prejudice | Distribute surplus fairly. Apply rules equally to all members. Do not favor relatives, friends, or powerful members. |
| **9. Inclusivity** | Welcoming environment free from discrimination | Open membership to all qualified persons regardless of tribe, gender, political affiliation, or social status. Ensure women can participate fully in governance. |
| **10. Objectivity** | Merit-based decisions in public interest | Hire the best-qualified GM. Elect directors based on competence, not popularity. Award contracts to the best supplier, not the one with connections. |
| **11. Dialogue and Meaningful Engagement** | Community consultation | Consult members before making major decisions. Hold information sessions. Use consultation (*shura*) as a governance practice, not just a formality. |
| **12. Balance Between Practical Life and Moral Side** | Ethical governance | Pursue profitability without compromising values. A cooperative can be financially successful and morally upright. One does not require sacrificing the other. |

**How to adopt a Code of Moral Principles:**

1. **Draft the code.** The Ethics Committee leads this work. Use the 12 Pillars as your framework. Translate each pillar into specific, enforceable standards. "Integrity" becomes "No officer shall accept any gift, commission, or payment from any supplier, contractor, or business partner of the cooperative."

2. **Consult the membership.** Present the draft at a General Assembly or a special consultation meeting. Invite feedback. Members who help write the rules are more likely to follow them.

3. **Adopt the code by General Assembly resolution.** A formal vote gives the code legitimacy and enforceability.

4. **Train all officers and members on the code.** Print copies. Discuss it during orientation for new members. Review it annually.

5. **Integrate the code into your bylaws.** Reference the Code of Moral Principles in your bylaws so that violation of the code is a violation of the bylaws -- which triggers the enforcement mechanisms described in the next section.

### 5.5.2 Accountability Mechanisms: Removal, Liability, and Due Process

Moral governance without enforcement is aspiration without teeth. Your cooperative needs clear mechanisms for holding officers accountable and removing those who violate their duties.

**Removal of Officers**

The removal process has legal safeguards to prevent political abuse:[^43]

**Step 1: Filing the complaint.** Any member may file a written complaint against an elected officer with the Board of Directors. The complaint must state specific acts or omissions that constitute grounds for removal.

**Step 2: Board investigation.** The Board (or its designated committee) investigates. The officer concerned must be given **notice of the complaint** and an **opportunity to be heard** -- this is the due process requirement. The Board may place the officer under **preventive suspension** by majority vote while the investigation is ongoing.

**Step 3: Board recommendation.** If the Board finds **prima facie evidence of guilt** (meaning there is enough evidence on its face to support the charges), it presents its recommendation for removal to the General Assembly.

**Step 4: General Assembly decision.** The officer may be removed by a **three-fourths (3/4) vote** of the regular members present and constituting a quorum at a regular or special meeting called for that purpose.[^44] The officer must be given an opportunity to be heard at this meeting.

**Grounds for removal include:**

- Misappropriation or misuse of cooperative funds
- Gross negligence in the performance of duties
- Violation of the cooperative's bylaws or Code of Moral Principles
- Conviction of an offense involving moral turpitude
- Acquiring personal interests in conflict with the cooperative
- Repeated failure to attend Board meetings without valid excuse
- Acts of disloyalty to the cooperative

> **What "Due Process" Means in Practice.** Due process is not a technicality. It means: (1) the officer knows the charges against them, (2) the officer has time to prepare a response, (3) the officer has a hearing where they can present their side, and (4) the decision is based on the evidence, not on personal animosity. Skip any of these steps, and the removal can be challenged before CSEA or the courts.

**Liability of Directors, Officers, and Committee Members**

The law imposes **joint and several liability** on directors, officers, and committee members who:[^45]

1. **Willfully and knowingly vote for or assent to patently unlawful acts**
2. **Act with gross negligence or bad faith** in directing the cooperative's affairs
3. **Acquire any personal or pecuniary interest in conflict** with their duty

"Joint and several liability" means the cooperative or its members can sue any one of the liable officers for the full amount of damages -- not just their proportionate share.

**Double profits rule.** When a director, officer, or committee member acquires an interest adverse to the cooperative in violation of their duty, they become a **trustee** for the cooperative. They are liable for damages **and** must account for **double the profits** that would have accrued to the cooperative.[^46]

**Disloyalty.** A director who acquires for himself an opportunity that should belong to the cooperative must account for **double the profits** -- even if the director used personal funds in the venture. This can only be ratified by a 3/4 vote of all members with voting rights, present and constituting a quorum, after full disclosure.[^47]

**Self-dealing.** A contract between the cooperative and one of its own directors, officers, or committee members is **voidable** at the cooperative's option unless four conditions are met:[^48]

1. The presence of the interested director was not necessary for quorum
2. The vote of the interested director was not necessary for approval
3. The contract is fair and reasonable
4. For officers and committee members, the contract was previously authorized by the GA or Board

If any of the first two conditions is absent, the contract can still be ratified by a 3/4 vote of all members with voting rights, present and constituting a quorum -- but only after full disclosure of the director's adverse interest.

## 5.6 Election Procedures

Elections are where democratic governance becomes real. Every two years (or annually for staggered terms), your cooperative's members choose who will lead them. Fair elections build trust. Rigged elections destroy cooperatives.

### 5.6.1 Nomination, Campaigning, and Voting Rules

The **Election Committee** manages the entire process. Here is a step-by-step procedure your cooperative should adopt in its bylaws:

**Step 1: Announce the election timeline.** At least 30 days before the AGA, the Election Committee publishes the election schedule: nomination period, qualification review period, campaign period, and election day.

**Step 2: Open nominations.** Members may nominate themselves or be nominated by other members. Set a nomination period of at least two weeks. Require nominees to submit a written acceptance of nomination.

**Step 3: Validate qualifications.** The Election Committee reviews each nominee against the qualifications and disqualifications in the law and bylaws. Disqualified candidates are notified in writing with the specific grounds for disqualification. Give disqualified nominees the right to appeal to the Committee within a set period (5-7 days).

**Step 4: Publish the final list of candidates.** Post it at the cooperative office and distribute it to all members with the AGA notice.

**Step 5: Campaign period.** Allow a brief campaign period (1-2 weeks). Set rules:

- No vote-buying or offering of material inducements
- No use of cooperative funds or resources for campaigning
- No negative campaigning that constitutes harassment or defamation
- Equal access to cooperative communication channels (bulletin boards, group chats) for all candidates

**Step 6: Conduct the election.** On election day (typically at the AGA):

- Verify voter eligibility at the registration table
- Issue ballots (or use whatever voting method the bylaws specify)
- Ensure secrecy of the vote -- use private voting booths or folded ballots
- Count votes in the presence of watchers designated by the candidates
- Record results in the official tally sheet

**Step 7: Canvass and proclaim results.** The Election Committee canvasses the votes, prepares the official results, and proclaims the winning candidates before the Assembly.

**Election Procedure Checklist:**

| Step | Timeline | Responsible |
|---|---|---|
| Announce election schedule | 30+ days before AGA | Election Committee |
| Open nominations | 30-14 days before AGA | Election Committee |
| Close nominations and validate | 14-7 days before AGA | Election Committee |
| Publish final candidate list | 7 days before AGA | Election Committee |
| Campaign period | 7 days before AGA | Candidates |
| Conduct election at AGA | AGA date | Election Committee |
| Canvass and proclaim | Same day | Election Committee |

> **Bangsamoro Tip.** In communities where literacy rates vary, consider using symbols, colors, or photos alongside candidate names on ballots. The functional literacy rate in BARMM is 71.6%, the lowest among Philippine regions.[^49] Make your election accessible to every member, regardless of educational background.

### 5.6.2 Handling Election Disputes

No election is without complaints. Your cooperative needs a clear dispute resolution process:

**Level 1: Election Committee.** All election protests must first be filed with the Election Committee within a period set in the bylaws (typically 5-7 days after proclamation). The Committee reviews the protest, gathers evidence, and issues a ruling. Common grounds for protest:

- Irregularities in the voting process (ballot tampering, counting errors)
- Disqualified candidates allowed to run
- Violation of campaign rules
- Failure to follow the election procedures in the bylaws

**Level 2: Board of Directors.** If the protesting party is not satisfied with the Election Committee's ruling, they may appeal to the Board within a set period. The Board reviews the record and issues a final decision at the cooperative level.

**Level 3: CSEA.** If internal remedies are exhausted and the dispute remains unresolved, either party may bring the matter to CSEA for adjudication. CSEA has the authority to investigate cooperative affairs, order elections, and impose administrative sanctions.

**Level 4: Courts.** As a last resort, election disputes involving legal rights may be brought to the regular courts. But exhaust internal remedies first. A court will typically ask whether the cooperative's own dispute resolution mechanisms were used before accepting the case.

| Level | Body | Timeline | Authority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Election Committee | 5-7 days after proclamation | Investigate and rule on protests |
| **2** | Board of Directors | 7-10 days after ELECOM ruling | Review and final internal decision |
| **3** | CSEA | After internal remedies exhausted | Adjudicate, investigate, order actions |
| **4** | Courts | After CSEA remedies exhausted | Legal rights and claims |

> **Prevention Is Better Than Cure.** Most election disputes arise from poorly defined rules. Write your election rules in detail -- in the bylaws, not in ad hoc Board resolutions. Specify every step: how to nominate, how to qualify, how to vote, how to protest. The Election Committee promulgates rules, but the bylaws should provide the framework. When the rules are clear before the election, disputes after the election become rare.

## Governance Structure at a Glance

Your cooperative's governance flows from the top down, with accountability flowing from the bottom up:

**Organizational Hierarchy:**

| Level | Body | Selected By | Accountable To |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1 (Highest)** | General Assembly | All voting members | The membership itself |
| **2** | Board of Directors | Elected by GA | General Assembly |
| **2** | Audit Committee | Elected by GA | General Assembly |
| **2** | Election Committee | Elected by GA | General Assembly |
| **3** | Chairperson, Vice-Chair | Elected by Board from among directors | Board and GA |
| **3** | Secretary, Treasurer | Appointed by Board | Board |
| **3** | Mediation Committee | Appointed by Board | Board |
| **3** | Ethics Committee | Appointed by Board | Board |
| **3** | Shari'ah Committee | Appointed by Board | Board and GA |
| **3** | Education Committee | Appointed by Board | Board |
| **4** | General Manager | Hired by Board | Board |
| **5** | Staff | Hired by GM | General Manager |

**Three governance principles to remember:**

1. **The General Assembly is supreme.** No body or officer has authority above the General Assembly within the cooperative. The GA sets direction, the Board steers, and management executes.

2. **Governance and management are separate.** Directors direct. Managers manage. Mix the two, and oversight disappears.

3. **Accountability flows upward.** Staff reports to the GM. The GM reports to the Board. The Board reports to the General Assembly. Committees report to whoever selected them. Every person in the governance structure answers to someone.
