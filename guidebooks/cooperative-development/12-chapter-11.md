# Chapter 11 — Growth, Merger, Division, and Dissolution

Every cooperative reaches a crossroads. Growth brings complexity -- your original fifteen members are now three hundred, your single barangay operation now serves three municipalities, and the Articles of Cooperation you filed five years ago no longer describe what your cooperative actually does. Two struggling cooperatives in the same barangay might be stronger as one. A cooperative that has grown too large might serve its members better as two. And a cooperative that has lost its purpose needs a dignified exit -- not an embarrassing cancellation order from the CSEA.

This chapter covers all of these transitions. You will learn how to expand your cooperative's reach, how to merge with or absorb another cooperative, how to divide one cooperative into two, how to join a federation, and how to dissolve your cooperative properly when the time comes. Each of these actions requires specific voting thresholds, legal filings, and member protections. Get them wrong, and the transition fails -- or worse, exposes your officers to personal liability.

The law does not punish cooperatives for changing. It punishes cooperatives that change without following the process.

---

## 11.1 Growing Your Cooperative

Growth is not a single event. It is a series of decisions -- each one requiring a different legal action. Adding twenty new members is straightforward. Expanding into a neighboring municipality requires amending your Articles of Cooperation. Launching a new business line means changing your cooperative type. Each of these changes follows a different path.

### 11.1.1 Expanding Membership and Area of Operation

Your cooperative's **area of operation** is defined in your Articles of Cooperation.[^1] If your Articles say your cooperative operates in Barangay Pagalungan, Municipality of Datu Odin Sinsuat, Maguindanao del Norte -- then that is your legal boundary. You cannot open a branch office in Cotabato City or recruit members from Sultan Kudarat without amending those Articles first.

**When you need to amend your area of operation:**

- You want to recruit members from a neighboring barangay or municipality
- You plan to open a satellite office or collection point outside your registered area
- A local government unit (LGU) requests your cooperative to serve an expanded territory
- Your members have migrated but still want to participate

**The amendment process** requires a **two-thirds (2/3) vote** of all members with voting rights.[^2] This is not two-thirds of those present at the General Assembly. It is two-thirds of your entire voting membership. If you have 150 voting members, you need at least 100 votes in favor.

**Step-by-step: Amending your area of operation**

```
1. DRAFT the proposed amendment
   (specify the new area of operation in full)
         |
2. NOTIFY all members at least 30 days
   before the Special General Assembly
         |
3. PRESENT the amendment at the
   Special General Assembly
         |
4. VOTE — requires 2/3 of ALL members
   with voting rights
         |
5. CERTIFY the amendment
   (secretary and majority of directors,
   under oath)
         |
6. FILE with the CSEA within 30 days
   of approval
         |
7. WAIT for CSEA approval
   (effective upon approval, or after 30
   days if CSEA does not act for reasons
   not attributable to the cooperative)
```

**Practical tip.** Before expanding your area, conduct an **economic survey** of the new territory. How many potential members live there? What services do they need? Can your cooperative's current capital base support the expansion? Growth without preparation is how cooperatives overextend and fail.

> **BARMM example.** A multipurpose cooperative based in Wao, Lanao del Sur, wants to serve members in the neighboring municipality of Bumbaran. The cooperative calls a Special General Assembly, secures a two-thirds vote from its 200 voting members (at least 134 votes needed), certifies the amendment, and files it with the CSEA Lanao del Sur Extension Office. Thirty days later, the amendment takes effect and the cooperative can legally operate in both municipalities.

### 11.1.2 Adding New Business Lines (Amending the AOC)

Your cooperative's **type** determines what business activities you can legally conduct.[^3] A credit cooperative lends money. A consumer cooperative procures and distributes goods. A multipurpose cooperative combines two or more types. If your credit cooperative wants to start a consumer store, you must amend your Articles to become a **multipurpose cooperative** -- or register the store as a separate entity.

The same **two-thirds (2/3) vote** requirement applies.[^4] The same filing process applies. But adding a business line carries additional requirements:

| New Business Line | Additional Requirements |
|---|---|
| **Credit/lending** | Minimum paid-up share capital; compliance with BSP or CSEA lending regulations |
| **Consumer store** | Business permit from the LGU; inventory management system |
| **Production/processing** | FDA or BFAD permits (if food); DENR clearance (if manufacturing) |
| **Insurance** | Insurance Commission accreditation; higher capitalization |
| **Banking** | BSP license; significantly higher capital requirements |

**The critical question: amend or register a new cooperative?** Use this decision guide:

```
Is the new business line closely related
to your current operations?
     |
     +-- YES --> Amend your AOC to become
     |           multipurpose (if not already)
     |
     +-- NO ---> Consider these factors:
                  |
                  +-- Will the new line require
                  |   different membership?
                  |   YES --> Register separately
                  |
                  +-- Will the new line expose
                  |   your existing assets to
                  |   new risks?
                  |   YES --> Register separately
                  |
                  +-- Can your current capital
                      base support both lines?
                      NO --> Register separately
```

**Warning.** Operating a business line not authorized in your Articles of Cooperation is a **compliance violation**. The CSEA can issue a deficiency notice, withhold your Certificate of Compliance, and -- in serious cases -- initiate cancellation proceedings.[^5]

## 11.2 Merger and Consolidation

Two cooperatives become one. The law provides two paths: **merger** (one cooperative absorbs the other) and **consolidation** (both cooperatives dissolve and form an entirely new one).[^6] The legal effects are identical. The practical difference is which name survives.

### 11.2.1 When Merger Makes Sense

Merger is not a sign of failure. It is a strategic decision. Consider merger when:

- **Two cooperatives serve the same community** with overlapping services and competing for the same members
- **One cooperative has strong management but weak capital**, while another has capital but poor governance
- **Membership has declined** in both cooperatives to near the minimum threshold (15 members for a primary)
- **Economies of scale** would reduce operating costs -- one accountant instead of two, one office instead of two, one audit fee instead of two
- **A federation or the CSEA recommends it** after identifying duplication in your area

**When merger does NOT make sense:**

- The cooperatives serve **different types of members** with different needs
- One cooperative carries **undisclosed liabilities** that would burden the surviving entity
- The **cultures are incompatible** -- one is run democratically, the other by a single family
- Members of one cooperative **do not want** to join the other

> **BARMM example.** Two credit cooperatives operate in the same municipality of Upi, Maguindanao del Norte. Each has 40 members, a part-time bookkeeper, and annual net surplus of less than PHP 50,000. Their combined overhead -- rent, audit fees, CSEA filings -- consumes most of their earnings. The CSEA extension officer recommends they merge. After both cooperatives vote separately, they consolidate into a single credit cooperative with 80 members, a full-time bookkeeper, and the financial strength to access institutional lending programs.

### 11.2.2 Legal Requirements and Voting Thresholds

The merger or consolidation process is governed by PB-210, Section 18 (for Bangsamoro cooperatives) and RA 9520, Article 21 (for the national framework).[^7] The requirements are strict because merger permanently ends the existence of at least one cooperative.

**Voting requirement: three-fourths (3/4) of all members with voting rights**, present and constituting a quorum, at **separate** General Assembly meetings of each constituent cooperative.[^8] Each cooperative votes independently. If either cooperative fails to reach the threshold, the merger cannot proceed.

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Who votes** | All members with voting rights in each cooperative, separately |
| **Threshold** | 3/4 of members present, with quorum |
| **Dissenting members** | May withdraw their membership and receive a refund of their share capital[^9] |
| **Filing** | Submit merger plan and resolutions to the CSEA |
| **Effective date** | Upon issuance of the **Certificate of Merger or Consolidation** by the CSEA[^10] |

**Step-by-step: The merger process**

```
1. NEGOTIATE the terms of merger
   (which cooperative survives, how
   assets and liabilities transfer,
   officer positions in the merged entity)
         |
2. DRAFT a Merger Plan
   (include financial statements of both
   cooperatives, proposed AOC of the
   surviving/new cooperative, timeline)
         |
3. NOTIFY all members of BOTH
   cooperatives at least 30 days
   before their respective GAs
         |
4. HOLD SEPARATE General Assemblies
   in each cooperative
         |
5. VOTE in each GA — requires 3/4
   of members present with quorum
         |
6. ALLOW dissenting members to withdraw
   (refund share capital per bylaws)
         |
7. FILE the following with the CSEA:
   - Certified copies of both resolutions
   - Merger Plan
   - Financial statements of both cooperatives
   - Proposed AOC of the surviving/new cooperative
   - List of members of the merged entity
         |
8. CSEA reviews and issues the
   Certificate of Merger or Consolidation
         |
9. MERGER takes effect on the date
   of the Certificate
```

**Practical tip.** The Merger Plan is the most important document. It should address every question a member might ask: What happens to my share capital? Will my loan terms change? Who will be on the new board? Where will the office be? A clear Merger Plan reduces dissent and increases the likelihood of reaching the three-fourths vote.

### 11.2.3 Effects of Merger on Members and Creditors

The law is clear about what happens after merger. Under PB-210, Section 19 and RA 9520, Article 22, five effects occur automatically:[^11]

1. **The constituent cooperatives become one.** In a merger, the surviving cooperative continues. In a consolidation, a new cooperative exists.

2. **The separate existence of absorbed cooperatives ceases.** Their registration numbers become inactive. Their names are removed from the CSEA roster.

3. **The surviving cooperative inherits everything.** All assets, rights, privileges, immunities, and franchises of each constituent cooperative transfer to the surviving or consolidated entity.

4. **The surviving cooperative inherits all liabilities.** Every debt, every obligation, every pending lawsuit. The merger does not erase what was owed. Creditors can pursue the surviving cooperative as if it had incurred those obligations itself.[^12]

5. **Creditor rights are protected.** No lien on property of any constituent cooperative is impaired by the merger. No pending legal action is dismissed. The surviving cooperative steps into the shoes of every constituent.

| What Transfers | What Does NOT Transfer |
|---|---|
| All assets and property | Nothing -- everything transfers |
| All liabilities and debts | |
| All pending lawsuits | |
| All contracts and agreements | |
| All member share capital | |
| All regulatory obligations | |

**Warning for members.** If you vote for merger with a cooperative that has hidden debts, your cooperative absorbs those debts. **Insist on a full financial audit of both cooperatives before voting.** This is not optional. It is the single most important protection you have.

## 11.3 Division of Cooperatives

The opposite of merger. One cooperative becomes two or more. Division is rarer than merger, but the law provides for it because cooperatives sometimes outgrow their original purpose or territory.

### 11.3.1 When and Why to Divide

Consider division when:

- **The cooperative has grown so large** that members in distant areas feel disconnected from governance
- **Two distinct business lines** within the cooperative would each be stronger as independent entities
- **Internal conflict** between geographic or sectoral groups has made unified governance impossible
- **Regulatory requirements** favor smaller, specialized cooperatives over a single large multipurpose entity

> **BARMM example.** A multipurpose cooperative in Cotabato City has 500 members -- half are market vendors running a consumer store, half are government employees running a credit operation. The two groups have different needs, different risk profiles, and different meeting schedules. After years of tension over how to allocate net surplus, the General Assembly votes to divide into two cooperatives: a consumer cooperative for the vendors and a credit cooperative for the employees. Each group gets the assets and liabilities related to its business line.

### 11.3.2 The Three-Fourths Vote Requirement

Division requires a **resolution approved by three-fourths (3/4) of all members with voting rights**, present and constituting a quorum.[^13] The procedure is prescribed by CSEA regulations, but the law imposes two firm conditions:

1. **Each new cooperative must comply with all registration requirements.** Minimum 15 members, minimum paid-up share capital, Articles of Cooperation, bylaws -- everything starts fresh for each new entity.[^14]

2. **No division in fraud of creditors.** You cannot divide a cooperative to dump its debts into one entity and move its assets to another. Any division that prejudices creditors is **void**.[^15]

**Step-by-step: The division process**

```
1. PROPOSE the division
   (identify which members, assets, and
   liabilities go to each new cooperative)
         |
2. DRAFT a Division Plan
   - Asset allocation between new entities
   - Liability allocation (fair and
     proportional, not in fraud of creditors)
   - Member assignment (by choice or
     by business line)
   - Timeline for transition
         |
3. NOTIFY all members at least 30 days
   before the General Assembly
         |
4. HOLD a General Assembly
         |
5. VOTE — requires 3/4 of members
   present, with quorum
         |
6. ALLOW dissenting members to withdraw
         |
7. EACH new cooperative completes the
   full registration process with the CSEA
   (AOC, bylaws, bond, economic survey)
         |
8. CSEA reviews and registers each
   new cooperative
         |
9. The original cooperative ceases
   to exist upon registration of the
   new entities
```

**Asset and liability allocation.** This is the hardest part of any division. The Division Plan must allocate assets and liabilities **proportionally and fairly**. A common approach:

| Allocation Method | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **By business line** | Each new cooperative gets the assets and liabilities of its business line | Multipurpose cooperatives splitting by type |
| **By membership proportion** | Assets and liabilities split based on member count or share capital | Geographic splits |
| **By negotiation** | Members negotiate a custom allocation through the GA | Complex situations with mixed assets |

**Warning.** Creditors must be notified and their claims honored. If Cooperative A owes PHP 500,000 to a bank and divides into Cooperatives B and C, the bank must know which entity assumes the debt -- and must consent to the arrangement. Division without creditor protection is legally void.[^16]

## 11.4 Forming or Joining a Federation

Your cooperative does not have to stand alone. **Federations** and **unions** are secondary and tertiary cooperative structures that allow cooperatives to pool resources, share expertise, and speak with a collective voice.[^17]

### 11.4.1 Requirements for Federation Membership

Under PB-210, Section 21, a **federation** requires a minimum of **three registered cooperatives** of the same type of service within the Bangsamoro region.[^18] Three credit cooperatives can form a credit federation. Three multipurpose cooperatives can form a multipurpose federation. You cannot mix types -- a credit cooperative, a consumer cooperative, and a producer cooperative cannot form a single federation.

**To form a federation, carry out the same formalities required for registering a primary cooperative:**

- Articles of Cooperation for the federation
- Bylaws
- Economic survey demonstrating the need for the federation
- Bond for accountable officers
- List of member cooperatives with their registration numbers

**To join an existing federation**, your cooperative must:

1. Pass a **board resolution** or **General Assembly resolution** (depending on your bylaws) authorizing membership
2. Meet the federation's **admission requirements** (financial standing, compliance status, cooperative type)
3. Pay the federation's **membership fee** and commit to regular **dues**
4. Submit your most recent **CAPR, CAFS, and Certificate of Compliance** as proof of good standing

### 11.4.2 Benefits and Obligations of Federation Membership

A federation is not just a registry. It is a working organization with specific functions defined by law.[^19]

**What your federation does for you:**

| Federation Function | What It Means for Your Cooperative |
|---|---|
| **Complementary business activities** | The federation can run wholesale operations, central warehousing, or bulk purchasing that your cooperative cannot do alone |
| **Education and advisory work** | Training programs, management consulting, technical assistance -- often free or subsidized for members |
| **Bookkeeping and accounting support** | Standardized systems, shared software, audit preparation assistance |
| **Coordination** | Joint marketing, shared logistics, inter-cooperative lending |
| **Joint ventures** | Access to national and international partnerships your cooperative could not pursue alone |
| **Publications** | Newsletters, market information, regulatory updates |

**What you owe your federation:**

- **50% of your Education and Training Fund (ETF)** goes to your chosen federation or union every year[^20]
- **Annual dues** as set by the federation's General Assembly
- **Compliance with federation bylaws** and resolutions
- **Timely submission of reports** the federation needs for its own compliance

**Cooperative Unions** serve a different purpose. While federations are organized by **type of business**, unions are organized by **territory** -- provincial, city, regional, or national.[^21] Unions represent cooperatives of all types within their jurisdiction. They advocate for cooperative interests with the government, conduct research, and promote cooperative principles.

Your cooperative can belong to **both** a federation (by type) and a union (by territory) simultaneously.

> **BARMM example.** The Bangsamoro Cooperative Union represents cooperatives of all types across the region. A credit cooperative in Marawi City might belong to both the Lanao del Sur Credit Cooperative Federation (by type) and the Bangsamoro Cooperative Union (by territory). The federation helps it with lending practices and shared capital. The union represents its interests in regional policy discussions.

## 11.5 Voluntary Dissolution

Sometimes a cooperative must end. Members move away. The business becomes unviable. Internal conflict makes governance impossible. The law provides a structured process for voluntary dissolution -- one that protects members and creditors while allowing the cooperative to close its affairs with dignity.

There are two paths, and the distinction is critical: **dissolution where no creditors are affected** and **dissolution where creditors are affected**.[^22]

### 11.5.1 The Dissolution Process Under Law

**Path 1: No Creditors Affected**

If your cooperative has **no outstanding debts** -- no loans, no unpaid suppliers, no pending obligations -- the dissolution process is simpler.[^23]

| Step | Action | Who |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Board resolution** approving dissolution | Majority of the Board of Directors |
| 2 | **Notice to members** by registered mail or personal delivery, at least 30 days before the GA | Secretary |
| 3 | **Publication** of the notice for 3 consecutive weeks in a local newspaper (or newspaper of general circulation if no local paper exists) | Board |
| 4 | **General Assembly vote** -- requires **3/4 of all members with voting rights**, present and constituting a quorum | Members |
| 5 | **Certification** of the resolution by a majority of directors, countersigned by the board secretary | Directors, Secretary |
| 6 | **Filing** with the CSEA | Chairperson |
| 7 | **CSEA issues Certificate of Dissolution** | CSEA |

**Path 2: Creditors Affected**

If your cooperative **has outstanding debts**, the process adds layers of creditor protection.[^24]

```
1. BOARD RESOLUTION approving dissolution
   (majority of directors)
         |
2. GENERAL ASSEMBLY VOTE
   (3/4 of members present, with quorum)
         |
3. FILE a PETITION for dissolution
   with the CSEA, signed by majority
   of directors, verified by chairperson
   or secretary
   - Must list ALL claims and demands
     against the cooperative
         |
4. CSEA issues an ORDER
   - Sets a hearing date (30-60 days out)
   - Order is published weekly for 3
     consecutive weeks in a local newspaper
   - Order is posted in 3 public places
     in the cooperative's municipality
         |
5. CREDITORS have 5 days to file
   OBJECTIONS after the notice period
         |
6. CSEA HEARING
   - Tries any issues raised in objections
         |
7. If petition is proven, CSEA issues
   ORDER OF DISSOLUTION setting forth:
   - Assets and liabilities
   - Claims of each creditor
   - Number of members
   - Nature and extent of member interests
```

**The critical difference.** In Path 1, the CSEA issues the certificate after receiving your resolution. In Path 2, the CSEA conducts a hearing, allows creditors to object, and only dissolves the cooperative after satisfying itself that creditor rights are protected.

### 11.5.2 Settling Liabilities and Distributing Remaining Assets

Dissolution is not the end. It is the beginning of **liquidation** -- a three-year wind-down period during which the cooperative continues to exist for the sole purpose of settling its affairs.[^25]

**During the three-year liquidation period, the cooperative may:**

- Prosecute and defend lawsuits
- Settle and close its affairs
- Dispose of, convey, and distribute its properties and assets
- Convey all properties to **trustees** for the benefit of members, creditors, and other persons in interest

**The order of distribution matters.** Assets are distributed in this priority:

```
1. TAXES and government obligations
         |
2. SECURED CREDITORS
   (those with liens on specific assets)
         |
3. UNSECURED CREDITORS
   (suppliers, lenders without collateral)
         |
4. MEMBER SHARE CAPITAL
   (refund of paid-up shares)
         |
5. RESERVE FUND
   (does NOT go to members -- must be
   placed in a usufructuary trust for
   the benefit of the cooperative's
   federation or union)[^26]
         |
6. REMAINING NET SURPLUS
   (distributed to members based on
   patronage or as the GA resolves)
```

**Three rules you must not break:**

1. **No asset distribution before all debts are paid.** A cooperative shall only distribute its assets upon lawful dissolution **and after payment of all its debts and liabilities**.[^27]

2. **The Reserve Fund is never distributed to members.** Upon dissolution, the General Assembly may resolve to establish a **usufructuary trust fund** for the benefit of the federation or union, or donate it to a cooperative institution.[^28]

3. **Unclaimed assets go to the federation or union.** If a creditor or member is unknown or cannot be found, their share goes to the federation or union with which the cooperative is affiliated.[^29]

> **BARMM example.** A fishermen cooperative in Tawi-Tawi with 30 members decides to dissolve. It has PHP 200,000 in a bank loan, PHP 50,000 in unpaid supplier invoices, and PHP 800,000 in total assets (including a motorized banca and a cold storage unit). The cooperative files a petition with the CSEA because creditors are affected. After the hearing, the CSEA issues a dissolution order. During the three-year liquidation, the cooperative sells the banca and cold storage, pays the bank (PHP 200,000), pays the supplier (PHP 50,000), refunds member share capital (PHP 300,000), transfers the Reserve Fund (PHP 80,000) to the Tawi-Tawi Fishermen Cooperative Federation, and distributes the remaining PHP 170,000 to members based on patronage.

## 11.6 Involuntary Dissolution and Cancellation of Registration

Not every dissolution is voluntary. The CSEA and the courts have the power to end a cooperative's existence when it violates the law or fails to operate. Understanding these grounds protects you from them.

### 11.6.1 Grounds for Cancellation

The law provides **three paths** to involuntary dissolution.[^30]

**Path 1: Court-Ordered Dissolution**

A competent court may dissolve a cooperative after due hearing on two grounds:

1. **Violation of any law, regulation, or provision of its bylaws**
2. **Insolvency**[^31]

**Path 2: CSEA-Ordered Cancellation of Registration**

The CSEA may suspend or revoke a cooperative's Certificate of Registration, after due notice and hearing, on any of the following grounds:[^32]

| Ground | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Fraudulent registration** | The cooperative obtained its COR through false documents, fake member lists, or misrepresentation |
| **Illegal purpose** | The cooperative exists for a purpose that violates the law -- money laundering, illegal lending, front for other activities |
| **Willful Code or bylaw violations** | The cooperative has been notified of violations and continues to violate despite warning |
| **Willful failure to operate on a cooperative basis** | The cooperative is run as a private business, not according to cooperative principles (one-member-one-vote, democratic governance, etc.) |
| **Failure to meet minimum membership** | The cooperative has fallen below 15 members (for a primary) and has not recruited new members within a reasonable period |

**Path 3: Dissolution by Failure to Organize and Operate**

This is the most common involuntary dissolution in BARMM. Under PB-210, Section 61(a), a cooperative that has **not commenced business within one year** of registration, or has **not carried on its business for one year**, triggers intervention.[^33]

The timeline under PB-210 is more structured than the national law:

```
REGISTRATION DATE
      |
      v
6 MONTHS: If cooperative has not
started operations, CSEA intervenes
with support, guidance, and monitoring
      |
      v
12 MONTHS: If still not operating,
CSEA denies the Certificate of
Compliance (COC) -- cooperative
becomes officially INACTIVE
      |
      v
2 YEARS without operations:
CSEA sends a SHOW CAUSE notice.
Cooperative must explain why it
has not operated.
      |
      v
If no justifiable cause is provided:
CSEA DELETES the cooperative from
the roster. It is deemed DISSOLVED.
```

Under the national framework (RA 9520, Article 68), the threshold is **two years** without commencing business or two consecutive years without operations.[^34] PB-210 is stricter -- it begins intervention at six months and flags inactivity at one year.

**Due process is required.** The CSEA cannot simply delete your cooperative from the roster. It must send a formal notice. It must give you the opportunity to show cause. Only after you fail to provide a justifiable reason will the CSEA proceed with dissolution.[^35]

### 11.6.2 The Inactive Cooperative Problem and Reactivation

BARMM has thousands of registered cooperatives that exist only on paper. They registered, received a COR, and never operated -- or operated for a year and went silent. These **inactive cooperatives** distort the data, occupy registration numbers, and give a false picture of the cooperative movement's strength.

**How cooperatives become inactive:**

| Cause | Frequency in BARMM |
|---|---|
| **Organizers lost interest** after registration | Very common |
| **Key officers migrated** or moved away | Common |
| **Capital was never collected** from members | Common |
| **No viable business** in the community | Occasional |
| **Internal conflict** paralyzed operations | Occasional |
| **Disaster or displacement** disrupted the community | Occasional (but significant in conflict-affected areas) |

**Can an inactive cooperative be reactivated?** Yes -- but only before the CSEA has formally dissolved it. If your cooperative is inactive but still on the CSEA roster, take these steps:

1. **Reconvene the Board of Directors.** If a quorum is not possible, call a Special General Assembly to elect new directors.

2. **Conduct an inventory** of all assets, liabilities, and member records.

3. **File all overdue reports** -- CAPR, CAFS, and any other required submissions. Pay any outstanding penalties.

4. **Adopt a reactivation plan** with a clear business program, updated economic survey, and realistic timeline.

5. **Request CSEA assistance.** Under PB-210, Section 61(a), the CSEA is required to provide support, assistance, guidance, and close monitoring to cooperatives that have not started operations within six months.[^36] Use this mandate. The CSEA extension officer in your area can help you develop a business plan, connect you with training providers, and monitor your progress.

6. **Apply for a new Certificate of Compliance** once you have filed all reports and resumed operations.

**The reactivation decision.** Before investing effort in reactivation, ask honestly:

```
Do you still have at least 15 members
willing to participate actively?
     |
     +-- NO --> Dissolve voluntarily. It is
     |          better to end cleanly than
     |          to accumulate penalties.
     |
     +-- YES
          |
     Is there a viable business
     opportunity for the cooperative?
          |
          +-- NO --> Dissolve voluntarily.
          |          A cooperative without a
          |          business is a club, not
          |          a cooperative.
          |
          +-- YES
               |
          Can you settle all outstanding
          debts and penalties?
               |
               +-- NO --> Negotiate with
               |          creditors first.
               |          Reactivation with
               |          unpaid debts exposes
               |          new officers to old
               |          liabilities.
               |
               +-- YES --> REACTIVATE.
                           File reports,
                           pay penalties,
                           resume operations.
```

> **BARMM example.** A consumer cooperative in Basilan registered in 2022 but never operated because its founding members were displaced by flooding. By 2024, the members have returned and want to start the cooperative. The cooperative is still on the CSEA roster but has not filed any reports. The members reconvene, elect a new board, file their overdue CAPR (reporting zero operations), pay the late filing penalties, and submit a reactivation plan with an updated economic survey to the CSEA Extension Office in Basilan. The CSEA assigns a Cooperative Development Specialist to monitor their progress for six months.

## Summary: Choosing Your Path

Every transition described in this chapter begins with the same question: **What is best for the members?** Not what is easiest for the officers. Not what avoids hard conversations. What genuinely serves the people who entrusted their share capital to this cooperative.

| Situation | Best Path | Key Requirement |
|---|---|---|
| You need to serve a larger area or add services | **Amend your AOC** (Section 11.1) | 2/3 vote of all members |
| Two cooperatives would be stronger as one | **Merge** (Section 11.2) | 3/4 vote in each cooperative, separately |
| One cooperative should become two | **Divide** (Section 11.3) | 3/4 vote; no fraud on creditors |
| You want collective bargaining power and shared services | **Join a federation** (Section 11.4) | Board or GA resolution; good compliance standing |
| The cooperative has no future | **Dissolve voluntarily** (Section 11.5) | 3/4 vote; settle all debts first |
| The cooperative has been inactive for over a year | **Reactivate or dissolve** (Section 11.6) | Honest assessment of viability |

One final principle. The law does not trap you in a cooperative that no longer works. It gives you tools to grow, to combine, to split, and to close. But every tool requires **member consent**, **creditor protection**, and **proper filing**. Skip any of those three, and your transition is not just invalid -- it is a violation.

Use the tools. Follow the process. Protect the members.

[^2]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 15 -- amendment of Articles of Cooperation requires a two-thirds (2/3) vote of all members with voting rights. See also RA 9520, Article 18.

[^3]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 20 -- defines 21 types of cooperatives in the Bangsamoro region, from credit to workers cooperatives.

[^4]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 15 -- the same two-thirds voting requirement applies to any amendment, whether changing the area of operation, business type, or other provisions.

[^5]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(e) -- the CSEA may revoke registration for willful violation of the Code or bylaws despite notice.

[^6]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 18 -- two or more cooperatives may merge into a single cooperative which shall either be one of the constituent cooperatives (merger) or the consolidated cooperative (consolidation). See also RA 9520, Article 21.

[^7]: RA 9520, Article 21 -- Merger and Consolidation of Cooperatives. PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 18 -- Merger and Consolidation of Cooperatives. Both laws contain substantially similar provisions, with PB-210 adapted for the Bangsamoro context under the CSEA.

[^8]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 18(b) -- three-fourths (3/4) vote of all members with voting rights, present and constituting a quorum, at separate general assembly meetings of each constituent cooperative.

[^9]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 18(b) -- dissenting members may withdraw their membership pursuant to Section 27, Chapter II. See also RA 9520, Article 21(2), which references Article 30 on withdrawal rights.

[^10]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 18(c) -- the merger or consolidation shall be effective upon the issuance of the certificate of merger or consolidation by the CSEA.

[^11]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 19 -- Effects of Merger and Consolidation. See also RA 9520, Article 22.

[^12]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 19(e) -- the surviving cooperative shall be responsible for all liabilities and obligations of each constituent cooperative. Neither the rights of creditors nor any lien upon property shall be impaired by the merger.

[^13]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 17 -- division requires a three-fourths (3/4) vote of all members with voting rights, present and constituting a quorum. See also RA 9520, Article 20.

[^14]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 17 -- all requirements set forth in the Code must be complied with by the new cooperatives resulting from division.

[^15]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 17 -- "no division of a cooperative in fraud of creditors shall be valid." See also RA 9520, Article 20.

[^16]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 17. The prohibition against fraudulent division means creditors must be informed and their claims properly allocated among the resulting cooperatives.

[^17]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 21 (Federations) and Section 22 (Unions). See also RA 9520, Articles 24 and 25.

[^18]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 21 -- "Three or more registered cooperatives in the Region of the same type of services may form a federation."

[^19]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 21, paragraphs 1-7 -- enumerates seven functions of a federation, from business activities to coordination to joint ventures. See also RA 9520, Article 24.

[^20]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter VI, Section 76(b) -- the Education and Training Fund allocation, of which 50% goes to the cooperative's chosen federation or union. See also RA 9520, Article 86.

[^21]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter I, Section 22 -- cooperative unions represent the interest and welfare of all types of cooperatives at the provincial, city, regional, and national levels. See also RA 9520, Article 25.

[^22]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(b) and 61(c) -- the two paths for voluntary dissolution. See also RA 9520, Articles 64 and 65.

[^23]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(b) -- voluntary dissolution where no creditors are affected. See also RA 9520, Article 64.

[^24]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(c) -- voluntary dissolution where creditors are affected. See also RA 9520, Article 65.

[^25]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(g) -- liquidation of a cooperative. The cooperative continues to exist for three years after dissolution for the purpose of winding up its affairs. See also RA 9520, Article 69.

[^26]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter VI, Section 76(a) -- upon dissolution, the reserve fund shall not be distributed among members. The General Assembly may resolve to establish a usufructuary trust fund for the benefit of the federation or union.

[^27]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(g)(3) -- a cooperative shall only distribute its assets or properties upon lawful dissolution and after payment of all its debts and liabilities. See also RA 9520, Article 69.

[^28]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter VI, Section 76(a) -- the reserve fund upon dissolution may be placed in a usufructuary trust fund for the benefit of any federation or union to which the cooperative is affiliated.

[^29]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(g)(2) -- upon the winding up of cooperative affairs, any asset distributable to any creditor or shareholder who is unknown or cannot be found shall be given to the federation or union to which the cooperative is affiliated. See also RA 9520, Article 69.

[^30]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(d), (e), and (f) -- three involuntary dissolution paths: court-ordered, CSEA-ordered, and failure to organize and operate.

[^31]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(d) -- involuntary dissolution by court order on grounds of law/bylaw violation or insolvency. See also RA 9520, Article 66.

[^32]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(e) -- five grounds for CSEA cancellation of registration. See also RA 9520, Article 67.

[^33]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(a) -- dissolution by failure to operate, with the CSEA intervention timeline beginning at six months.

[^34]: RA 9520, Article 68 -- dissolution by failure to organize and operate within two years of registration or two consecutive years of non-operation.

[^35]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(a) and (f) -- the CSEA must send a formal notice and allow the cooperative to show cause before deleting it from the roster.

[^36]: PB-210, Book I, Chapter V, Section 61(a) -- "the Authority will intervene to provide support, assistance, guidance and close monitoring to help the cooperative to operate within the next six (6) months."
