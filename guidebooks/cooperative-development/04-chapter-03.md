# Chapter 3 — From Idea to Organizing Group: Pre-Formation

In Sitio Marang, a coastal barangay in Tawi-Tawi, twelve women dry seaweed on bamboo racks every afternoon. They sell to the same middleman. They earn the same low price. They have talked for months about pooling their harvest and selling directly to buyers in Zamboanga. They have the idea. They have the people. What they do not have is a roadmap.

This chapter is that roadmap.

Before you file a single document, before you draft your Articles of Cooperation, before you reserve a name with CSEA -- you must build the foundation. That foundation has four parts: **confirming your common bond**, **assembling your organizing group**, **conducting an economic survey**, and **completing the Pre-Registration Seminar**. Skip any one of these, and your registration will fail. Do them well, and you will walk into CSEA's office with a cooperative that is ready -- not just on paper, but in practice.

---

## 3.1 Is Your Community Ready for a Cooperative?

A cooperative begins long before anyone fills out a form. It begins when a group of people share a problem and believe they can solve it together. But belief alone is not enough. The law requires a specific foundation. Your community must meet it.

### 3.1.1 The Common Bond of Interest Requirement

**The common bond of interest is the legal thread that ties your members together.** Without it, CSEA will not register your cooperative. Section 5 of BTA Bill No. 210 states the requirement directly: organizers must be "natural persons who are Filipino citizens, of legal age, having a common bond of interest and are actually residing or working in the intended area of operation."[^1]

The common bond can take several forms:

| Type of Common Bond | What It Means | Bangsamoro Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Geographic (Residential)** | Members live in the same barangay, municipality, or province | Fisherfolk in Bongao forming a marketing cooperative |
| **Occupational** | Members share the same trade or profession | Seaweed farmers across three barangays in Tawi-Tawi |
| **Institutional** | Members work for the same employer or agency | BARMM ministry employees forming a credit cooperative |
| **Associational** | Members belong to the same organization | Mosque congregation forming a consumer cooperative |

**Choose the common bond that most accurately describes your group.** Do not force a bond that does not exist. If your members are seaweed farmers from five different barangays, your bond is occupational, not geographic. If your members are neighbors who share no livelihood, your bond is residential. The bond you declare must match reality -- CSEA verifies this during the evaluation process.

For our Sitio Marang group, the common bond is clear: **occupational**. All twelve women (and at least three more they plan to recruit) are seaweed farmers. They live in the same barangay, which gives them a geographic bond as well. But the occupational bond is stronger because it connects directly to the cooperative's planned business activity.

**Why the common bond matters beyond compliance.** The common bond is not just a legal box to check. It determines whether your members will actually patronize the cooperative. A cooperative of seaweed farmers will naturally buy drying supplies, sell harvests, and share equipment through the cooperative. A cooperative where the only bond is living near each other may struggle to find services that all members need. The stronger the bond, the higher the patronage. The higher the patronage, the stronger the cooperative.

### 3.1.2 Assessing Community Readiness

Before you invest weeks of organizing work, run a quick assessment. Not every group with a common bond is ready for a cooperative. Readiness depends on four conditions:

**1. Economic viability.** Is there a real economic problem that a cooperative can solve? The women in Sitio Marang sell their seaweed to a single middleman at PHP 35 per kilo of dried seaweed. Direct buyers in Zamboanga pay PHP 55-65 per kilo. That price gap is the economic case for a cooperative. If no price gap, no inefficiency, and no unmet need exists, a cooperative may not be the right vehicle.

**2. Willingness to commit capital.** Every member must contribute share capital. For a primary cooperative, the minimum paid-up capital is **PHP 15,000** across all members.[^2] That means if you have 15 members, each member needs to contribute at least PHP 1,000 to meet the floor. Can your prospective members afford this? If the answer is uncertain, do not abandon the idea -- but plan for how members will build their contributions over time.

**3. Leadership availability.** A cooperative needs leaders who can manage meetings, keep records, handle money, and represent the group to CSEA and other agencies. Look at your organizing group. Who can serve as interim chairperson? Who is good with numbers? Who can write reports? If all the leadership falls on one person, you have a problem. Cooperatives run on shared governance, not one-person management.

**4. Existing informal cooperation.** Groups that already cooperate informally -- sharing boats, pooling harvests, lending each other supplies -- make the strongest cooperative foundations. They already trust each other. They already understand collective action. The cooperative simply formalizes what they are already doing.

#### Community Readiness Checklist

Use this checklist before proceeding to the organizing stage. Mark each item honestly.

| # | Readiness Indicator | Yes/No | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | We have at least 15 people interested in forming a cooperative | | |
| 2 | All prospective members are Filipino citizens of legal age (18+) | | |
| 3 | We share a clear common bond (geographic, occupational, institutional, or associational) | | |
| 4 | We can identify a specific economic problem the cooperative will solve | | |
| 5 | Members are willing to contribute share capital (minimum PHP 1,000 each for 15 members) | | |
| 6 | At least 3-5 members can take on leadership roles | | |
| 7 | No single person dominates all decision-making | | |
| 8 | We already cooperate informally (shared resources, group buying, etc.) | | |
| 9 | We can identify products or services the cooperative will provide | | |
| 10 | We are willing to attend the Pre-Registration Seminar (3 hours 45 minutes) | | |

**Scoring.** If you answered "Yes" to 8-10 items, your group is ready to proceed. If you answered "Yes" to 5-7, you need to strengthen the weaker areas first. Below 5, spend more time on social preparation before organizing.

For our Sitio Marang group: they have 12 members (need 3 more), they share an occupational bond, the price gap creates economic viability, and they already cooperate by sharing drying racks. Their main gap is leadership -- only Hadja Nuriya and Bai Asmina have experience managing group activities. They need to identify at least one more leader before moving forward.

## 3.2 Building the Organizing Group

### 3.2.1 The 15-Member Minimum and What It Really Takes

The law is clear: **at least 15 natural persons** are required to organize a primary cooperative.[^3] All must be Filipino citizens, of legal age, and must share a common bond of interest.

But 15 is the legal minimum, not the practical target.

**Aim for 20-30 founding members.** Here is why. Some members will lose interest during the organizing process. Others will fail to attend the Pre-Registration Seminar. A few may be unable to contribute their share capital on time. If you start with exactly 15 and lose even one, you fall below the legal threshold and cannot register. Start with a buffer.

The ideal founding membership also depends on your cooperative type and planned activities:

| Cooperative Type | Minimum Members | Recommended Founding Members | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Credit** | 15 | 25-30 | More members = larger lending pool |
| **Producer** | 15 | 20-25 | Collective volume drives better prices |
| **Marketing** | 15 | 20-30 | More products to aggregate and sell |
| **Multipurpose** | 15 + additional requirements | 30-50 | Multiple services require broader base |
| **Consumer** | 15 | 25-35 | Purchasing power increases with members |

**Every founding member must complete the PRS.** This is not optional. Under BTA Bill No. 210, Section 5, "a prospective member of a primary cooperative must have completed a Pre-registration Seminar."[^4] If even one of your 15 members lacks a PRS certificate, CSEA will reject the application.

For our Sitio Marang group: They have 12 women. They need at least 3 more. Hadja Nuriya knows five other seaweed farmers in the adjacent sitio who have expressed interest. Recruit them. Aim for 18-20 founding members so the group can absorb any dropouts during the organizing process.

### 3.2.2 Identifying Leaders and Assigning Pre-Formation Roles

Before you hold your first formal organizing meeting, identify who will lead the pre-formation process. You are not yet electing official officers -- that happens after registration, at the organizational meeting.[^5] But you need an **interim organizing committee** to drive the formation forward.

**The three essential pre-formation roles:**

**1. Interim Chairperson.** This person leads the organizing meetings, keeps the group focused, and serves as the point of contact with CSEA. Look for someone who is respected by the group, can facilitate discussions without dominating them, and has enough time to attend multiple meetings and government offices.

**2. Interim Secretary.** This person records the minutes of organizing meetings, maintains the list of prospective members, and manages the paperwork. The secretary will eventually prepare (or coordinate the preparation of) the Articles of Cooperation, bylaws, and economic survey. Look for someone who is literate, organized, and comfortable with documentation.

**3. Interim Treasurer.** This person collects and safeguards initial capital contributions from members. The treasurer will eventually execute the **Treasurer's Affidavit** -- a notarized document certifying that at least 25% of authorized capital has been subscribed and at least 25% of subscriptions have been paid.[^6] Look for someone trustworthy, numerate, and willing to be bonded.

**Warning: Do not let one person hold all three roles.** This defeats the democratic principle of cooperative governance. Even at the pre-formation stage, distribute responsibility. If your group only has one person willing to lead, you are not ready to form a cooperative.

#### Additional Pre-Formation Roles

For larger organizing groups, add these roles:

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Membership Committee Lead** | Recruits new members, verifies eligibility, maintains the roster |
| **PRS Coordinator** | Contacts CSEA to schedule the PRS, ensures all members attend |
| **Capital Campaign Lead** | Tracks share capital commitments and payments from each member |
| **Economic Survey Lead** | Gathers the data needed for the economic survey (market prices, costs, potential revenue) |

For our Sitio Marang group: Hadja Nuriya becomes interim chairperson -- she is respected, experienced, and connected with other seaweed farmers. Bai Asmina, who finished two years of college and keeps clean records for the barangay health station, becomes interim secretary. Sahaya, who managed the funds for their mosque's feeding program for three years, becomes interim treasurer. The three additional women Hadja Nuriya will recruit can take on membership and PRS coordination roles.

## 3.3 Conducting the Economic Survey

The **economic survey** is one of the four mandatory documents you must submit to CSEA for registration.[^7] But it is more than a requirement. It is your cooperative's first business plan. A well-prepared economic survey forces you to answer the hard questions before you commit capital and effort.

### 3.3.1 Area of Operation, Size of Membership, and Viability Data

BTA Bill No. 210, Section 6 requires every group intending to form a cooperative to submit "an economic survey, indicating therein the area of operation, the size of membership, and other pertinent data in a format provided by the Authority."[^8] The same section mandates a "general statement describing, among others the structure and purposes of the proposed cooperative" and requires that "the structure and actual staffing pattern of the cooperative shall include a bookkeeper."

**The economic survey must demonstrate four things:**

**1. Demand for cooperative services.** Who will use the cooperative's products or services? How often? How much will they spend? For a seaweed marketing cooperative, this means documenting how many kilos of dried seaweed your members produce, how often they harvest, and what buyers are willing to pay.

**2. A viable member base.** How many people can you realistically recruit? What is their economic profile? Can they afford the share capital? Will they patronize the cooperative regularly?

**3. Available resources.** What physical, financial, and human resources does the group already have? Drying racks? Storage facilities? A bookkeeper? A truck? What do you still need?

**4. Economic feasibility.** Based on projected revenue and expenses, can the cooperative sustain itself? Will it generate enough income to cover costs, build reserves, and provide benefits to members?

The CDA's official economic survey form organizes this data into four major sections:[^9]

| Section | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **I. General Information** | Type of cooperative, address, area of operation, common bond, membership composition, capital structure |
| **II-A. Economic Aspect** | Existing competitors, member support strategies, transaction scope, planned business activities |
| **II-B. Financial Aspect** | Capital sources, initial operating capital, projected revenue and expenses, investment plans |
| **II-C. Technical Aspect** | Equipment and facilities needed, procurement plans, required skills and training |
| **II-D. Organizational Structure** | Board qualifications, staffing plan, education programs, committee members |

### 3.3.2 The Economic Survey Template (Step by Step)

Walk through each section of the economic survey using the Sitio Marang seaweed cooperative as an example. The official CDA/CSEA template is the authoritative form -- what follows is a guide for filling it out correctly.[^10]

#### Background Section

Write a brief narrative about your community and why a cooperative is needed. Keep it to one paragraph.

**Filled-in example:**

> Sitio Marang is a coastal community of approximately 200 households in Barangay Simunul, Municipality of Simunul, Tawi-Tawi. The primary livelihood is seaweed farming, with approximately 45 households engaged in cultivating, drying, and selling *Kappaphycus alvarezii* (cottonii). Currently, individual farmers sell dried seaweed to a single middleman at PHP 35 per kilo -- well below the PHP 55-65 per kilo offered by direct buyers in Zamboanga City. The lack of organized marketing, limited drying facilities, and absence of collective bargaining power have kept farm-gate prices low. This cooperative aims to aggregate member harvests, improve drying quality, and sell directly to processors and exporters.

#### Rationale Section

Explain why the cooperative form is the best solution. Why not an association? Why not a sole proprietorship?

**Filled-in example:**

> Individual farmers lack the volume, storage capacity, and market connections to negotiate directly with processors. A cooperative enables members to pool harvests (achieving the 5-ton minimum required by most direct buyers), share the cost of drying and storage facilities, and negotiate collectively for better prices. The cooperative structure also allows access to CSEA technical assistance, government livelihood programs, and the Education and Training Fund mandated by law.

#### Section I: General Information

**Capital Structure:**

| Item | Computation | Sitio Marang Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Authorized Capital** | Set by organizers | PHP 100,000.00 |
| **Subscribed Capital** | Must be at least 25% of Authorized | PHP 36,000.00 (36%) |
| **Paid-up Capital** | Must be at least 25% of Subscribed AND at least PHP 15,000 | PHP 18,000.00 (50% of subscribed) |
| **Par Value per Share** | Set by organizers | PHP 500.00 per share |

**How the capital computation works.** BTA Bill No. 210, Section 9(d) requires that at least 25% of authorized share capital be subscribed and at least 25% of subscriptions be paid up, with a floor of PHP 15,000.[^11] For 18 members contributing PHP 1,000 each in initial paid-up capital, total paid-up is PHP 18,000 -- above the PHP 15,000 floor. Each member holds 2 shares at PHP 500 par value.

#### Section II-A: Economic Aspect

**Existing cooperatives in the area.** Research whether any cooperative in your area already offers the same services. If yes, name them. This is not a disqualifier -- but CSEA wants to know you are aware of the competitive landscape.

**Strategies for member support.** Check all that apply from the template: collective purchases, commitment on lending policies, active participation in cooperative affairs, or specify others. For a marketing cooperative, add "collective marketing of member harvests" and "shared access to drying and storage facilities."

**Transaction scope.** Will you transact with members only, or with members and non-members? Most cooperatives choose **members and non-members** -- this allows the cooperative to aggregate volume from non-member farmers (who pay a service fee) while building the case for those farmers to join as members.

**Three-year business activity plan:**

| Year | Planned Activities |
|---|---|
| **Year 1** | Aggregate and market member seaweed harvests; establish quality grading system; acquire basic drying equipment |
| **Year 2** | Expand to include seaweed nursery services; establish direct buyer contracts in Zamboanga; add 10 new members |
| **Year 3** | Begin semi-processed carrageenan production; explore export markets; expand to neighboring barangays |

#### Section II-B: Financial Aspect

**Capital sources.** Check all that apply: share capital subscription, revolving capital, loans, donations/subsidies/grants, fundraising. For most new cooperatives, the primary source is **share capital subscription**. Grants from CSEA programs like the **PESO Project** (Profitability and Equity Support Oriented) may become available after registration.[^12]

**Three-year financial projection:**

| Item | Year 1 | Year 2 | Year 3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Projected Revenue** | PHP 275,000 | PHP 450,000 | PHP 720,000 |
| **Estimated Expenses** | PHP 210,000 | PHP 340,000 | PHP 510,000 |
| **Projected Net Surplus** | PHP 65,000 | PHP 110,000 | PHP 210,000 |
| **Initial Operating Capital** | PHP 18,000 | -- | -- |

**How the Sitio Marang numbers work.** Year 1: 18 members producing an average of 300 kg dried seaweed each per year = 5,400 kg total. Sold at PHP 50/kg (conservative estimate vs. PHP 55-65 direct buyer price) = PHP 270,000 in sales revenue. Add PHP 5,000 from service fees charged to non-member sellers. Expenses include transportation to Zamboanga (PHP 90,000), drying supplies (PHP 40,000), packaging (PHP 25,000), administrative costs (PHP 30,000), and equipment maintenance (PHP 25,000).

List the equipment and facilities your cooperative needs. The template provides checkboxes for common items. For a seaweed marketing cooperative:

**Procurement mode.** For initial equipment: cash purchase from paid-up capital. For larger items (solar dryers): explore grant programs through CSEA, MAFAR, or the **3ZERO Initiative** partnership between CSEA, MAFAR, WFP, and ACTED.[^13]

**Required skills and training.** List the training your members need beyond the PRS: quality grading of dried seaweed, basic bookkeeping, cooperative governance, and marketing skills.

#### Section II-D: Organizational Structure

Describe the qualifications your Board of Directors should possess. Name the officers and employees the cooperative will hire for initial operations.

**Staffing plan for a small cooperative:**

| Position | Name | Status | Compensation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Manager | Hadja Nuriya (interim) | Volunteer | None (first year) |
| Bookkeeper | To be hired | Part-time/Contractual | PHP 3,000/month |

**Certification.** The founding Board of Directors must sign and certify the economic survey. It must then be notarized.[^15]

## 3.4 The Pre-Registration Seminar (PRS)

The **Pre-Registration Seminar** is a mandatory training that every prospective cooperative member must complete before registration. No PRS certificate, no registration. There are no exceptions.

### 3.4.1 PRS Content Requirements Under Law

RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) established the PRS requirement for all cooperatives nationwide.[^16] CDA Memorandum Circular No. 2025-11 -- the **Revised Guidelines for Conducting Pre-Registration Seminars for Prospective Cooperatives** -- sets the current national standards.[^17] In BARMM, the **Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA)** conducts the PRS, applying these standards within the Bangsamoro context.[^18]

**The PRS runs for 3 hours and 45 minutes** and covers five major topics:[^19]

| Module | Content | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Preliminaries** | Opening program, introduction of participants | 10 minutes |
| **2. Objectives and Description of PRS** | Why PRS exists, what participants will learn | 20 minutes |
| **3. The CDA/CSEA and Its Mandate** | Salient provisions of the CDA Charter (RA 11364), CSEA's role in BARMM | 15 minutes |
| **4. Introduction to Cooperatives** | Cooperative identity, types and services, purpose, member rights and responsibilities | 2 hours |
| **5. Requirements and Process of Registration** | Documentary requirements, E-CoopRIS online system, what to expect after registration | 1 hour |
| | **Total** | **3 hours 45 minutes** |

**Module 4 is the heart of the PRS.** In two hours, participants learn what a cooperative truly is -- not just a business, but a values-driven organization built on seven internationally recognized principles.[^20] They learn the difference between a cooperative and a corporation. They learn their rights as members (to vote, to inspect books, to receive patronage refunds) and their responsibilities (to patronize, to attend meetings, to pay capital contributions).

**What the PRS does not cover.** The PRS is an orientation, not a comprehensive training. It does not teach bookkeeping, business planning, or management. Those come later through the **Basic Cooperative Course** (8-16 hours for new members) and **Officer Training** programs (24-40 hours for directors and committee members).[^33] The PRS gives every prospective member enough knowledge to make an informed decision: do I want to join this cooperative?

**Why the PRS matters beyond compliance.** Many organizing groups treat the PRS as a box to check. That is a mistake. The PRS is where your members first learn the **seven cooperative principles** -- the values that distinguish a cooperative from a business. Members who understand these principles make better decisions in General Assembly meetings. They hold their Board of Directors accountable. They patronize the cooperative because they understand how patronage refunds work. Treat the PRS as the first investment in your cooperative's human capital, not just a registration hurdle.

### 3.4.2 Finding an Accredited PRS Provider

In BARMM, the PRS is conducted by **CSEA**, not CDA. Contact CSEA directly to schedule a session for your organizing group.

**CSEA Office:**
JICA Building, BGC
Gov. Gutierrez Avenue
Brgy. RH XII, Cotabato City
Maguindanao 9600

**Contact:** (00) 552-1105[^21]

**How to request a PRS session:**

1. **Contact CSEA** and ask about the PRS schedule. CSEA posts schedules on their official social media platforms.
2. **Request a group session.** MC 2025-11 encourages prospective members to attend as a group.[^22] This is practical -- your entire organizing group completes the requirement at the same time.
3. **Coordinate with your LGU.** Your municipal or city government, through the **Cooperative Development Officer (CDO)**, can help coordinate with CSEA. The CDO can provide the venue, meals, and logistical support for face-to-face sessions.[^23] If your LGU does not have a CDO (many in BARMM do not), contact CSEA directly.
4. **Choose your delivery mode.** PRS sessions can be conducted **online or face-to-face**.[^24] For remote island communities in BaSulTa (Basilan, Tawi-Tawi), online delivery may be more accessible -- but ensure all participants have internet access.

**Cost: Free.** The PRS is conducted free of charge. No fees shall be collected from participants.[^25] For face-to-face sessions, participants cover their own meals. If your LGU provides the venue and meals, participants pay nothing at all.

**Special schedule requests.** If the regular PRS schedule does not work for your group, you may request a special session. This requires prior authorization from the CSEA Regional Director and depends on staff availability.[^26]

### 3.4.3 What Happens If Members Skip the PRS

**The consequence is absolute: your cooperative will not be registered.**

The PRS Certificate is one of the mandatory documentary requirements for registration.[^27] CSEA will not process your application without a valid PRS certificate for every founding member listed in the Articles of Cooperation. There is no workaround.

**The PRS certificate is valid for three years from the date of issuance.**[^28] This means your organizing group has three years from the date they complete the PRS to finalize their registration documents and submit to CSEA. If the certificate expires before you register, every member must retake the PRS.

**Practical tip: Schedule the PRS early in your organizing process.** Do not wait until all documents are ready. Complete the PRS while you are still drafting the economic survey and Articles of Cooperation. This serves two purposes: (1) it gets the legal requirement out of the way, and (2) the PRS content helps your members understand cooperative principles, which improves the quality of your founding documents.

**What if a member joins after the PRS?** If you recruit additional members between the PRS and registration, those new members must also complete a PRS. They can attend a separate session -- they do not need to attend the same session as the original group.

For our Sitio Marang group: Hadja Nuriya contacts CSEA in Cotabato City and learns that a PRS session is scheduled in two weeks. She coordinates with the barangay captain for a venue. The LGU agrees to provide the space and snacks. All 18 prospective members attend the 3-hour-45-minute session. They receive their PRS certificates. The certificates are valid until 2029. The clock is ticking -- but they have plenty of time.

## 3.5 Drafting Your Initial Business Concept

Before you write the Articles of Cooperation, you need clarity on what your cooperative will actually do. The economic survey captures financial projections and operational data. But first, you need a **business concept** -- a clear picture of the problem you solve, for whom, and how.

### 3.5.1 The Lean Canvas for Cooperatives (Adapted)

The **Lean Canvas** is a one-page business modeling tool created by Ash Maurya.[^29] It forces you to think through nine critical elements of your business. Originally designed for startups, it adapts powerfully to cooperatives -- with one key difference. Where a startup's "unfair advantage" might be proprietary technology, a cooperative's unfair advantage is its **community bond and collective action**.

Here are the nine blocks of the Lean Canvas, adapted for cooperative context, with a filled-in example for the Sitio Marang seaweed cooperative:

#### Block 1: Problem

**What problem does your cooperative solve?** List the top three problems your members face.

| # | Problem | Current Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Low farm-gate prices (PHP 35/kg vs. PHP 55-65/kg direct) | Sell individually to a single middleman |
| 2 | Poor drying quality reduces grade and price | Use makeshift bamboo racks exposed to rain |
| 3 | No access to market information or direct buyers | Rely on middleman's price, no negotiation power |

**Always start with the problem.** A cooperative that solves a real, painful problem will attract and retain members. A cooperative formed without a clear problem to solve will struggle from day one.

#### Block 2: Customer Segments

**Who benefits from your cooperative?** For a cooperative, your "customers" are two groups: your **members** (who are also your owners) and your **external buyers** (who purchase what your cooperative sells).

| Segment | Who They Are |
|---|---|
| **Members (primary)** | 18 women seaweed farmers in Simunul, Tawi-Tawi |
| **External buyers** | Seaweed processors and exporters in Zamboanga City; carrageenan factories |
| **Future members** | Estimated 30+ additional seaweed farmers in adjacent barangays |

#### Block 3: Unique Value Proposition

**What makes your cooperative's offer compelling?** Write one sentence that captures your cooperative's unique value.

> *"Aggregated, quality-graded dried seaweed from Tawi-Tawi's top-producing coastal communities -- delivered in bulk directly to processors at competitive prices."*

For members, the value is simple: **higher income per kilo of seaweed**. For buyers, the value is **reliable volume and consistent quality** -- something individual farmers cannot guarantee.

#### Block 4: Solution

**How does your cooperative solve the problem?** Map each problem to a specific solution.

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Low farm-gate prices | Aggregate harvests and sell directly to processors, eliminating the middleman |
| Poor drying quality | Invest in improved solar dryers; train members in quality grading standards |
| No market access | Build direct buyer relationships in Zamboanga; join CSEA trade fairs and the 3ZERO Initiative |

#### Block 5: Channels

**How will you reach your members and buyers?**

| Channel | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Barangay meetings and mosque announcements** | Recruit and communicate with members |
| **CSEA trade fairs and product expos** | Connect with buyers |
| **Direct outreach to Zamboanga processors** | Establish buying contracts |
| **Mobile phone and social media groups** | Coordinate harvests and logistics |

#### Block 6: Revenue Streams

**How does the cooperative earn income?**

| Revenue Source | Description | Projected Annual Amount |
|---|---|---|
| **Marketing margin** | Spread between member farm-gate price and buyer price | PHP 240,000 (Year 1) |
| **Service fees** | Fees charged to non-member farmers who use cooperative marketing services | PHP 15,000 |
| **Drying facility rental** | Fees for use of improved solar dryers | PHP 20,000 |

#### Block 7: Cost Structure

**What are your major costs?**

| Cost Category | Annual Estimate |
|---|---|
| Transportation (to Zamboanga) | PHP 90,000 |
| Drying supplies and equipment maintenance | PHP 40,000 |
| Packaging materials | PHP 25,000 |
| Administrative (bookkeeper, communications, office supplies) | PHP 40,000 |
| Surety bond and registration fees | PHP 15,000 (Year 1 only) |

#### Block 8: Key Metrics

**What numbers tell you whether the cooperative is working?**

| Metric | Target (Year 1) |
|---|---|
| Total volume marketed (kg) | 5,400 kg |
| Average selling price per kg | PHP 50+ |
| Member patronage rate | 80%+ of members selling through the cooperative |
| Net surplus | PHP 65,000+ |
| Membership growth | From 18 to 25 |

#### Block 9: Unfair Advantage

**What do you have that cannot be easily copied?** This is where cooperatives shine. Your unfair advantage is not a patent or proprietary algorithm. It is your **community**.

> *Tightly-knit community of women seaweed farmers who have worked together for years, sharing knowledge, labor, and drying facilities. This trust and social cohesion cannot be replicated by a competing trader or corporation.*

#### How to Use the Lean Canvas in Practice

**Step 1: Gather your organizing committee.** Set aside two hours. Bring a large sheet of paper or use a whiteboard. Draw the nine-block grid.

**Step 2: Fill in the Problem block first.** Do not start with the solution. If your group cannot agree on the top three problems, you are not ready to form a cooperative. Spend as long as needed on this block.

**Step 3: Fill in Customer Segments second.** Who exactly are the members? Who are the buyers? Be specific. "Women seaweed farmers in Simunul aged 25-55" is better than "farmers."

**Step 4: Work through the remaining blocks.** There is no strict order for blocks 3-9, but most groups find it natural to move from Unique Value Proposition to Solution to Channels to Revenue.

**Step 5: Test your canvas against reality.** After completing the canvas, ask three hard questions: (1) Is the problem painful enough that members will pay capital and patronize the cooperative? (2) Can we actually deliver the solution with our current resources? (3) Do the revenue numbers cover the cost numbers? If the answer to any of these is "no," revise the canvas before proceeding.

The Lean Canvas is not a registration requirement. CSEA does not ask for it. But filling it out before you write the economic survey sharpens your thinking. Every number in the economic survey comes from the analysis you do here. The Lean Canvas asks the "why" and "what." The economic survey asks the "how much" and "when."

### 3.5.2 Identifying Your Products, Services, and Target Market

Your cooperative must offer specific products or services. Vague statements like "general trading" or "help our members" will not survive the economic survey, and they will not attract patronage. Define what your cooperative does in concrete terms.

**Start with what your members already produce or need.** The strongest cooperatives build on existing livelihoods. Do not form a coffee cooperative in a community that grows seaweed. Match the cooperative to the community.

**BARMM's top commodities offer proven opportunities for cooperatives:**[^30]

| Commodity | BARMM Share of National Production | Cooperative Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| **Seaweed** | 48.42% (#1 nationally) | Marketing, drying, semi-processing |
| **Cassava** | 41.70% (#1 nationally) | Processing (flour, chips), marketing |
| **Rubber** | Significant producer | Marketing, processing |
| **Coffee** | Growing sector | Quality control, direct trade |
| **Coconut** | Major livelihood crop | Copra marketing, oil processing |
| **Rice** | Staple crop | Production, milling, consumer |
| **Fisheries** | Abundant marine resources | Marketing, cold storage, value-adding |

**Conduct basic market analysis.** Answer four questions:

**1. Who will buy your product or service?** Name specific buyers, not categories. "Zamboanga seaweed processors" is better than "the market." Visit the buyers if possible. Ask what volume they need, what quality standards they require, and what price they offer.

**2. What volume can your members produce?** Be conservative. Calculate based on current production, not aspirational targets. If your 18 members each produce 300 kg of dried seaweed per year, your Year 1 volume is 5,400 kg -- not 10,000 kg.

**3. What is the price gap?** Compare the price your members currently receive (selling individually) with the price the cooperative can negotiate (selling in bulk, directly). If there is no meaningful price gap, the cooperative may not add enough value to justify the effort.

**4. Who is the competition?** Are there middlemen, traders, or other cooperatives serving the same market? What advantages do they have? What can your cooperative offer that they cannot?

**Document your market analysis in the economic survey.** The data you gather here fills in Sections II-A (Economic Aspect) and II-B (Financial Aspect) of the CDA/CSEA economic survey template.

**A note on Shari'ah compliance.** If your cooperative will operate under Islamic principles -- and most cooperatives in BARMM should consider this -- your products, services, and business activities must comply with Shari'ah. Under BTA Bill No. 210, Section 7, this means your cooperative must fulfill the objectives of Shari'ah (*maqasid al-Shari'ah*): preservation of faith, life, lineage, intellect, and wealth.[^31] Practically, this means **no interest-based lending (riba)**, **no gambling (maysir)**, **no speculative transactions (gharar)**, and **no dealing in haram products**.[^32] For a seaweed marketing cooperative, Shari'ah compliance is straightforward -- seaweed is halal, and marketing does not involve interest. For a credit cooperative, you will need Shari'ah-compliant alternatives like **Murabaha** (cost-plus financing) or **Musharakah** (partnership). Chapter 7 covers Shari'ah-compliant financial operations in detail.

## Pulling It All Together: The Pre-Formation Checklist

Before moving to Chapter 4 (Registration), verify that your organizing group has completed every pre-formation requirement. Use this checklist:

| Step | Task | Status | Required For |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Confirmed common bond of interest | ☐ | Registration |
| 2 | Assembled 15+ founding members (recommend 20+) | ☐ | Registration |
| 3 | All founding members are Filipino citizens, 18+ | ☐ | Registration |
| 4 | Assigned interim organizing committee (Chair, Secretary, Treasurer) | ☐ | Internal organization |
| 5 | Completed the economic survey | ☐ | Registration |
| 6 | All founding members completed the PRS | ☐ | Registration |
| 7 | PRS certificates are valid (within 3 years of issuance) | ☐ | Registration |
| 8 | Drafted initial business concept (Lean Canvas or equivalent) | ☐ | Economic survey input |
| 9 | Identified products/services and target market | ☐ | Economic survey input |
| 10 | Capital commitments collected from members (minimum PHP 15,000 total paid-up) | ☐ | Registration |
| 11 | Bookkeeper identified or hired | ☐ | Registration (per BTA Bill 210, Sec. 6) |

If all boxes are checked, you are ready for the next phase: **drafting the Articles of Cooperation and Bylaws, reserving your cooperative name, and submitting your registration package to CSEA.** That is Chapter 4.
