# Chapter 4 -- Registration: From Application to Certificate

Registration is where your cooperative becomes real. Before this moment, you have an idea, an organizing group, a completed economic survey, and a stack of PRS certificates. After this moment, you have a **legal entity** -- one that can open bank accounts, enter contracts, receive grants, and transact business in its own name. Every step in Chapters 2 and 3 was preparation. This chapter is execution.

The registration process has a reputation for being difficult. It is not difficult. It is detailed. There is a difference. A difficult process would require special expertise or connections. This process requires completeness, accuracy, and patience. You will prepare two core documents -- the **Articles of Cooperation (AOC)** and the **Bylaws** -- assemble supporting requirements, file them with CSEA, and wait for your **Certificate of Registration (COR)**. Every one of these steps has clear rules. Follow them, and you will be registered.

This chapter walks you through each step. By the end, you will know exactly what to prepare, exactly how to prepare it, and exactly what to expect once you file.

---

## 4.1 Overview: The Registration Process

### 4.1.1 Timeline: What to Expect from Filing to COR

The registration process runs across three phases. Here is the full sequence from your first organizing meeting through COR issuance:

**Phase 1: Document Preparation (4-8 weeks)**

1. **Reserve your cooperative name** with CSEA (1-3 days)
2. **Draft the Articles of Cooperation** using the prescribed template (1-2 weeks)
3. **Draft the Bylaws** consistent with the AOC (1-2 weeks)
4. **Prepare the economic survey** if not yet finalized from Chapter 3 (1 week)
5. **Collect all PRS certificates** from your members (already completed)
6. **Compute and collect share capital** contributions from all subscribers
7. **Execute the Treasurer's Affidavit** certifying capital received (1 day)
8. **Secure surety bonds** for all accountable officers (1-2 weeks)
9. **Notarize** the AOC and Treasurer's Affidavit (1 day)
10. **Assemble four original copies** of all documents in separate folders

**Phase 2: Filing and Review (up to 60 days)**

11. **Submit the complete application** to the CSEA Provincial Office
12. **CSEA conducts document review** for completeness (5-10 working days)
13. **CSEA evaluates** for compliance with law (20-30 working days)
14. **CSEA conducts on-site validation** of your principal office and operations[^1]

**Phase 3: Registration Decision**

15. **CSEA issues the Certificate of Registration** -- your cooperative acquires juridical personality[^2]

**The 60-day rule.** The law gives CSEA sixty days from filing of complete requirements to act on your application. If CSEA does not act within sixty days and the delay is not your fault, your application is **deemed approved**.[^3] This is a powerful legal protection. Keep your filing receipt and note the date.

### 4.1.2 Where to File: CSEA-BARMM vs. CDA

If your cooperative's principal office is in the **Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM)**, you file with **CSEA -- the Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority**. Not with CDA.

BTA Bill No. 210, Section 11 is explicit: CSEA "shall be the sole agency of the Bangsamoro Government that registers cooperatives and social enterprises."[^4] This authority was established to give BARMM full control over cooperative development within its territory.

**CSEA Provincial Offices.** CSEA maintains provincial offices in the five BARMM provinces: **Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, Lanao del Sur, Basilan, and Tawi-Tawi**. Cotabato City and the Special Geographic Area fall under the CSEA Provincial Office in Maguindanao.[^5] File your application with the provincial office where your cooperative's principal office is located.

**CSEA Central Office.** For questions, name reservations, or issues that provincial offices cannot resolve:

> CSEA Office
> JICA Building, BGC
> Gov. Gutierrez Avenue
> Brgy. RH XII, Cotabato City
> Maguindanao 9600
> Phone: (00) 552-1105[^6]

**What about cooperatives that registered with CDA before CSEA?** Cooperatives registered with CDA prior to CSEA's establishment remain validly registered. Their CDA-issued Certificates of Registration are still legally recognized. However, these cooperatives should coordinate with CSEA for all future compliance filings, amendments, and renewals. CSEA is now the authority for all cooperative matters within BARMM.

**What about cooperatives outside BARMM?** If your cooperative's principal office is outside BARMM, you file with the **CDA Extension Office** having jurisdiction over your area.[^7] The CDA retains authority for the rest of the Philippines. The documentary requirements are substantively the same -- what differs is the filing office and the emphasis on moral governance and Shari'ah compliance that CSEA applies under BTA Bill No. 210.

## 4.2 Preparing the Articles of Cooperation

The **Articles of Cooperation (AOC)** is the founding document of your cooperative. Think of it as the cooperative's birth certificate and constitution combined. It establishes the cooperative's identity, declares its purpose, and sets its capital structure. Once registered, the AOC becomes a binding contract between the cooperative and the registering authority.[^8]

### 4.2.1 The Eight Required Contents (BTA Bill No. 210, Section 9)

BTA Bill No. 210, Section 9(b) lists eight mandatory contents that every AOC must include. Here is each one, explained in plain language:

**1. The name of the cooperative, which shall include the word "cooperative."**[^9]

Your cooperative's name must contain the word **"Cooperative"**, **"Kooperatiba"**, or **"Cooperativa"**. The name must also specify the **type** of cooperative (credit, multipurpose, marketing, etc.) as defined under Section 20 of BTA Bill No. 210.[^10]

| Rule | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Must include | "Cooperative," "Kooperatiba," or "Cooperativa" |
| Must specify | Type of cooperative (e.g., "Credit Cooperative") |
| Must not be | Identical or confusingly similar to an existing cooperative |
| Prohibited words | "Incorporated," "corporation," "company," "partnership" |
| Formatting | Not in ALL CAPS (unless it is an acronym after the full name) |

**Example:** "Sitio Marang Seaweed Farmers Marketing Cooperative" -- this name includes the word "Cooperative," specifies the type (Marketing), and identifies the bond (Seaweed Farmers) and location (Sitio Marang).

**2. The purpose or purposes and scope of business for which the cooperative is to be registered.**[^11]

State your cooperative's **primary business activity** and any related activities. Be specific. Do not write vague language like "to engage in all forms of cooperative enterprise." Instead, write: "To engage in the marketing of dried and processed seaweed products, and to provide members with access to drying equipment, packaging materials, and direct market linkages."

If your cooperative will engage in allied activities, list those too. Allied activities are those necessary and incidental to the primary business -- for example, a marketing cooperative that also provides members with production inputs (nylon line, fertilizer) and basic credit services.

**3. The term of existence of the cooperative.**[^12]

Your cooperative may exist for up to **fifty (50) years** from the date of registration. You choose the term. Most cooperatives set it at fifty years (the maximum). The term can be extended later by amending the AOC, but no extension can be made earlier than five years before the expiry date unless CSEA approves it for justifiable reasons.

Write both words and numbers: "FIFTY (50) years."

**4. The area of operation and the postal address of its principal office.**[^13]

The **area of operation** is the geographic area where your cooperative will recruit members. The **principal office** is the physical address where the cooperative conducts business. The principal office **must be within the area of operation**.

Be specific: "The area of operation shall be the Municipality of Simunul, Province of Tawi-Tawi." Then provide the complete principal office address: Number/Unit, Street, Barangay, Municipality, Province, Zip Code.

**5. The names, nationality, and the postal addresses of the registrants.**[^14]

List **every organizer** (cooperator). For each, provide: full legal name, nationality (Filipino), and complete postal address. All fifteen-or-more organizers must appear in this list. These are the founding members who sign the AOC.

**6. The common bond of membership.**[^15]

Declare the single common bond that ties your members together. Choose one: **residential, occupational, institutional, or associational**. Only one common bond is permitted per cooperative.[^16] Specify the field of membership within that bond -- for example, "Occupational: seaweed farmers in the Municipality of Simunul, Tawi-Tawi."

**7. The list of names of the directors who shall manage the cooperative.**[^17]

List the **elected Board of Directors**. The board must have a minimum of **five (5)** and a maximum of **fifteen (15)** members. An **odd number** is recommended to prevent tie votes.[^18] These directors are elected from among the organizers at the organizing meeting.

**8. The amount of its share capital, the names and residences of its contributors, and a statement of whether the cooperative is primary, secondary, or tertiary.**[^19]

This article declares the cooperative's capital structure:

| Capital Element | What to Specify |
|---|---|
| **Authorized share capital** | Total capital the cooperative is authorized to issue (in words and figures) |
| **Number of shares** | Total shares the authorized capital is divided into |
| **Par value per share** | Value of each share (max PHP 1,000; must be divisible by PHP 1.00)[^20] |
| **Category** | Primary, secondary, or tertiary |

You must also list each subscriber's name, postal address, number of subscribed shares, amount subscribed, number of paid-up shares, and amount paid up.

**Optional additional provisions.** The AOC may contain any other provisions not inconsistent with the law.[^21] For Bangsamoro cooperatives, consider adding a provision declaring adherence to **Shari'ah principles** under Section 7 of BTA Bill No. 210. This is not legally required in the AOC itself, but it signals your cooperative's commitment and provides a legal basis for Shari'ah-compliant operations in the bylaws.

**Preferred shares (optional).** If your cooperative opts to offer preferred share capital alongside common share capital, the capitalization article must be expanded to show both classes separately. Preferred shares cannot exceed **25% of total authorized share capital**.[^21a] Preferred shareholders are associate members -- they cannot vote or be voted upon, but they have preference in receiving interest on share capital and in the distribution of net assets during liquidation. Most new primary cooperatives start with common shares only. Add preferred shares later if your capital needs grow.

### 4.2.2 Line-by-Line Guide to Filling Out the AOC Template

The official CDA/CSEA model template provides a structured form. Here is how to fill it out correctly, using our running example of the Sitio Marang Seaweed Farmers Marketing Cooperative.

**Header: Name of Cooperative**

Write the full name exactly as it appears on your approved **Name Reservation Slip** from CSEA. Do not abbreviate. Do not add words. The name on the AOC must match the reservation slip exactly.

> **Articles of Cooperation of**
> SITIO MARANG SEAWEED FARMERS MARKETING COOPERATIVE

**Article I -- Name of Cooperative**

Repeat the full name: "The name of this Cooperative shall be **Sitio Marang Seaweed Farmers Marketing Cooperative**."

**Article II -- Type and Purpose(s)**

State the type first, then enumerate specific activities:

> "This is a **Marketing** Cooperative organized for the following purposes:
>
> 1. To engage in the collective marketing of dried and processed seaweed products on behalf of its members;
> 2. To provide members with access to drying equipment, packaging materials, and quality control services;
> 3. To negotiate fair market prices and establish direct buyer relationships in Zamboanga City and other markets;
> 4. To provide members with production inputs necessary for seaweed farming."

**Article III -- Bond of Membership**

> "The common bond of membership of this Cooperative is **Occupational**: seaweed farmers in the Municipality of Simunul, Province of Tawi-Tawi."

**Article IV -- Area of Operation**

> "The area of operation shall be the **Municipality of Simunul, Province of Tawi-Tawi**. The principal office of this Cooperative is located at Sitio Marang, Barangay Simunul, Municipality of Simunul, Province of Tawi-Tawi, 7504."

Verify: Is the principal office within the area of operation? Yes -- Sitio Marang is inside Simunul municipality.

**Article V -- Term of Existence**

> "This Cooperative shall exist for a term of **FIFTY (50)** years from the date of registration, unless sooner dissolved in accordance with law."

**Article VI -- Names and Addresses of Cooperators**

Prepare a table listing every organizer:

|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Amina B. Hassan | Filipino | Sitio Marang, Brgy. Simunul, Simunul, Tawi-Tawi 7504 |
| 2 | Fatima T. Abubakar | Filipino | Sitio Marang, Brgy. Simunul, Simunul, Tawi-Tawi 7504 |
| ... | *(continue for all 15+ organizers)* | | |

**Article VII -- Board of Directors**

> "The Board of Directors shall be composed of **SEVEN (7)** members."

Then list the elected directors:

| No. | Name |
|---|---|
| 1 | Amina B. Hassan (Chairperson) |
| 2 | Nurjannah S. Tillah (Vice Chairperson) |

**Article VIII -- Capitalization**

This is where most errors occur. Get the math right.

> "The Authorized Share Capital of this Cooperative is **ONE HUNDRED THOUSAND PESOS (PHP 100,000.00)**, divided into **ONE THOUSAND (1,000)** shares with a par value of **ONE HUNDRED PESOS (PHP 100.00)** per share."

**Article IX -- Subscribed and Paid-up Share Capital**

> "The amount of **THIRTY THOUSAND PESOS (PHP 30,000.00)** of the authorized share capital has been subscribed, and **FIFTEEN THOUSAND PESOS (PHP 15,000.00)** of the total subscription has been paid."

Then list each subscriber:

| Name | Subscribed Shares | Amount Subscribed | Paid-up Shares | Amount Paid |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Amina B. Hassan | 20 | PHP 2,000.00 | 10 | PHP 1,000.00 |
| Fatima T. Abubakar | 20 | PHP 2,000.00 | 10 | PHP 1,000.00 |
| *(15 members x 20 shares each)* | | | | |
| **TOTAL** | **300** | **PHP 30,000.00** | **150** | **PHP 15,000.00** |

**Capital compliance check.** Verify these three conditions before proceeding:

| Requirement | Formula | Your Numbers | Pass? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Subscribed >= 25% of Authorized | 25% x PHP 100,000 = PHP 25,000 | PHP 30,000 | Yes |
| Paid-up >= 25% of Subscribed | 25% x PHP 30,000 = PHP 7,500 | PHP 15,000 | Yes |
| Paid-up >= PHP 15,000 minimum | MC 2016-02 floor | PHP 15,000 | Yes |

If your computed paid-up is less than PHP 15,000, you must still meet the **PHP 15,000 minimum floor** set by MC 2016-02.[^22] For multipurpose cooperatives, the minimum paid-up capital is **PHP 100,000**.[^23]

**Certification Clause**

The elected treasurer's name goes in the first blank. Specify the date, month, year, and place of execution. All cooperators sign with their full names.

**Witnesses**

Two credible witnesses who are **not** organizers of the cooperative must sign, attesting to the execution of the AOC. A credible witness must be personally known to the notary public, must personally know the cooperators, and must not be a party to the document.

**Acknowledgment (Notarization)**

A notary public fills in the acknowledgment section. Each cooperator must present **competent evidence of identity** -- at least one current government-issued ID bearing their photograph and signature.[^24] Acceptable IDs include: passport, driver's license, UMID, PhilHealth card, voter's ID, or senior citizen's ID. The AOC **must be notarized**. An un-notarized AOC will be rejected outright.

**Handling erasures and corrections.** If any page contains an erasure, strikeout, or superimposition, the authorized officer (Chairperson or Secretary) must initial the correction to validate it. Unsigned corrections are grounds for rejection. Better yet: reprint the page with the correction and sign fresh copies. Clean documents process faster.

## 4.3 Drafting the Bylaws

The AOC establishes your cooperative's identity. The **Bylaws** establish how it operates. If the AOC is the birth certificate, the bylaws are the operating manual. They govern everything from how members join to how the board meets to how surplus is distributed.

The bylaws must be filed at the same time as the AOC.[^25] They cannot contradict the AOC or the law. And they must cover specific topics required by BTA Bill No. 210.

### 4.3.1 Required Provisions Under Law

BTA Bill No. 210, Section 10 lists fifteen required provisions. Every set of bylaws you draft must address each one:[^26]

**1. Qualifications for admission to membership** and the payments or interest required as a condition for exercising membership rights.

Define who can join. Set the minimum share subscription and membership fee. Be specific about qualifications -- age, citizenship, common bond, and any additional requirements your cooperative imposes.

**2. Rights and privileges, duties and responsibilities, and liabilities of membership.**

Spell out what members can do (vote, inspect records, receive patronage refunds) and what they must do (pay subscriptions, patronize the cooperative, attend meetings, obey board decisions).

**3. Circumstances under which membership is acquired, maintained, and lost.**

State when membership begins (upon board approval and payment), how it continues (meeting minimum patronage, paying dues), and when it ends (resignation, expulsion, death, transfer).

**4. Procedure for termination of membership.**

Describe the process for involuntary termination -- grounds, notice requirements, hearing, and appeal. A member cannot be expelled without due process.

**5. Conditions for transfer of shares.**

Shares in a cooperative are personal. They can only be transferred under conditions your bylaws define -- typically to other members or back to the cooperative. No open-market trading.

**6. Rules and procedures for meetings.**

Cover the General Assembly (annual and special), Board of Directors meetings, and committee meetings. For each, specify:
- How meetings are called (notice period, method)
- Quorum requirements
- Voting procedures (one member, one vote for regular members)
- How agenda is set

**7. General conduct of affairs.**

Define the powers and duties of the General Assembly, Board of Directors, committees, and officers. Include qualifications and disqualifications for directors and officers.

**8. How capital may be raised and utilized.**

Describe share capital contributions, continuous capital build-up mechanisms, savings programs, and the purposes for which capital can be used.

**9. Mode of custody and investment of net surplus.**

Specify how net surplus is held before distribution and what investment vehicles are permitted. For Shari'ah-compliant cooperatives, this section must prohibit interest-bearing investments.

**10. Accounting and auditing systems.**

Identify the books of account to be maintained, the accounting standards to follow, and the audit requirements (internal and external).

**11. Manner of loaning and borrowing, including limitations.**

Set lending policies, borrowing limits, interest rates (or profit-sharing mechanisms for Shari'ah-compliant cooperatives), and loan approval procedures. BTA Bill No. 210 adds a critical clause: "Cooperatives that choose to operate under Islamic principles shall adhere to the Shari'ah compliance provisions of this Code."[^27]

**12. Method of distribution of net surplus.**

Describe how the cooperative allocates its net surplus among statutory funds and member distribution. Under RA 9520, Article 86, cooperatives must allocate to the **Reserve Fund** (at least 10%), **Education and Training Fund** (at least 10%), and **Community Development Fund** (at least 3%). The remainder goes to interest on share capital and patronage refund.[^28]

**13. Manner of adopting, amending, repealing, and abrogating bylaws.**

Amendments require a **two-thirds (2/3) vote** of all members with voting rights.[^29] Describe the process: board proposal or member petition, notice, General Assembly vote, filing with CSEA.

**14. Conciliation or mediation mechanism.**

Establish an internal dispute resolution system for amicable settlement of disputes among members, directors, officers, and committee members. This is mandatory -- not optional.[^30]

**15. Other matters incident to the purposes and activities of the cooperative.**

This catch-all provision allows you to include any additional rules your cooperative needs. Use it for operational policies, code of conduct, or governance mechanisms specific to your cooperative's type and context.

### 4.3.2 Shari'ah-Compliant Bylaws: What to Include

BTA Bill No. 210, Section 7 establishes **Islamic principles** that Bangsamoro cooperatives operating under the Shari'ah Governance Framework must follow.[^31] Even if your cooperative does not formally opt into the Shari'ah Governance Framework, these provisions reflect the values of many Bangsamoro communities and strengthen your cooperative's credibility.

**Key Shari'ah provisions to include in your bylaws:**

**1. Shari'ah Committee.** Establish a standing Shari'ah Committee (minimum three members) with advisory authority on whether the cooperative's transactions, products, and services comply with Islamic principles. The committee does not have veto power over the Board -- it provides guidance and issues opinions. Include their qualifications: knowledge of Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh al-muamalat*) or formal training in Islamic finance.

**2. Prohibition of riba (interest-based transactions).** If your cooperative provides financial services, your bylaws must replace interest-based lending with **Shari'ah-compliant financing modes**:

| Prohibited (Riba-based) | Shari'ah-Compliant Alternative | How It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Interest-bearing loans | **Murabaha** (cost-plus sale) | Cooperative purchases the item and sells it to the member at a disclosed markup |
| Fixed-interest savings | **Mudharabah** (profit-sharing) | Returns based on actual profits, not a guaranteed rate |
| Interest on deposits | **Wadiah** (safekeeping) | Deposits held in trust; any returns are voluntary gifts, not contractual |

**3. Halal business activities only.** Your bylaws should state that the cooperative will engage only in **halal** (permissible) transactions, products, and services.[^32] Prohibit involvement in gambling (*maysir*), speculation with extreme uncertainty (*gharar*), and *haram* products.

**4. Moral governance code.** BTA Bill No. 210, Section 5 requires all Bangsamoro cooperatives to conduct their affairs "in accordance with moral governance and the cultures, good values and experiences of the constituents of the region."[^33] Translate this into bylaws language: a code of ethical conduct for directors, officers, and members. Address transparency, accountability, and zero tolerance for graft and corruption.

**5. Fulfillment of maqasid al-Shari'ah.** Section 7(a) of BTA Bill No. 210 calls for cooperatives to fulfill the **objectives of Shari'ah**: preservation of faith, life, lineage, intellect, and wealth.[^34] Include a statement of purpose that connects your cooperative's activities to these objectives. For a marketing cooperative, this means preserving wealth through fair pricing and equitable trade.

**Sample bylaws provision:**

> **Article XII -- Shari'ah Compliance**
>
> Section 1. This Cooperative shall conduct all transactions, products, and services in accordance with the principles of Shari'ah as defined in BTA Bill No. 210, Section 7.
>
> Section 2. The Board of Directors shall appoint a Shari'ah Committee composed of at least three (3) members with knowledge of Islamic jurisprudence. The Committee shall review and advise on the Shari'ah compliance of all cooperative business activities.
>
> Section 3. The Cooperative shall not engage in or facilitate interest-based lending (riba), gambling (maysir), speculative transactions involving excessive uncertainty (gharar), or any transaction involving haram products or services.
>
> Section 4. All financial services offered by the Cooperative shall use Shari'ah-compliant financing modes, including but not limited to murabaha, mudharabah, musharakah, and wadiah.

### 4.4.2 Common Reasons for Rejection (And How to Avoid Them)

CSEA returns applications that fail compliance review. Here are the most common rejection reasons, drawn from actual registration practice:[^38]

**1. Inconsistent cooperative name across documents.**

The cooperative name on the AOC does not match the Name Reservation Slip, or the bylaws use a different variation. **Fix:** Before printing, search-and-compare the cooperative name across every document. It must be identical everywhere -- same spelling, same capitalization, same word order.

**2. Capital figures do not add up.**

The subscribed capital in the AOC does not match the Treasurer's Affidavit, or the paid-up amount falls below the legal minimum. **Fix:** Use the capital compliance check table from Section 4.2.2. Verify: (a) subscribed >= 25% of authorized, (b) paid-up >= 25% of subscribed, (c) paid-up >= PHP 15,000 (or PHP 100,000 for multipurpose). Cross-check the AOC subscriber table totals against the Treasurer's Affidavit figures.

**3. Common bond does not match the field of membership.**

The AOC declares a residential bond, but the bylaws describe an occupational field of membership. Or the area of operation is broader than the bond justifies. **Fix:** Align the AOC (Article III) with the bylaws (membership qualifications section). The common bond, field of membership, and area of operation must tell a consistent story.

**4. Principal office is outside the area of operation.**

The AOC states the area of operation as Municipality X, but the principal office address is in Municipality Y. **Fix:** The principal office must be physically located within the declared area of operation.[^39] If your office address is outside the area, either change the area of operation or relocate the office.

**5. Missing signatures on document margins.**

Organizers signed the last page of the AOC but forgot to sign the margins of every other page. **Fix:** Before notarization, physically check every page of all four copies. Every cooperator must sign every page margin. No exceptions.

**6. Photocopies submitted instead of originals.**

One or more of the four copies contains photocopied signatures. **Fix:** All four copies must bear original, wet-ink signatures. Print four sets. Sign four times. There is no shortcut.

**7. Wrong paper size or font.**

Documents are printed on A4 (8.27" x 11.69") instead of legal size (8.5" x 13"). Or the font is Times New Roman instead of Bookman Old Style. **Fix:** Check your printer settings before printing. Use Bookman Old Style 12 on 8.5" x 13" paper.

**8. Metallic fasteners used.**

Staple wires or paper clips hold the documents together. **Fix:** Use plastic fasteners, string ties, or document folders. CSEA will reject documents with metal fasteners.

**9. PRS certificates incomplete.**

Not all organizers have PRS certificates, or the certificates are expired. **Fix:** Every organizer listed in the AOC must have a valid PRS certificate.[^40] Certificates are valid for three years from the date of issuance. Verify each certificate's date before filing.

**10. Treasurer's Affidavit not notarized.**

The affidavit was signed but never taken before a notary public. **Fix:** The Treasurer's Affidavit is a sworn document. It must be notarized. Schedule notarization together with the AOC to save time and cost.

## 4.5 The Registration Filing Process

### 4.5.1 Filing with CSEA Provincial Offices

You have your complete application package. Here is how to file.

**Step 1: Reserve your cooperative name.**

Before filing the full application, submit a **Cooperative Name Reservation Report Form (CNRRF)** to CSEA. Pay the reservation fee:

| Reservation Period | Fee |
|---|---|
| 30 days | PHP 100 |
| 60 days | PHP 200 |
| 90 days | PHP 300 |

Choose the 60-day or 90-day option. The 30-day window is too tight if any document revisions are needed. Once CSEA approves your name, you receive a **Name Reservation Slip**. This slip is your authority to use the name on all registration documents.[^41]

**Name reservation rules to remember:**

- Your name must not be identical or confusingly similar to any existing registered cooperative. CSEA checks this against their registry.
- The name must not use prohibited terms: "incorporated," "corporation," "company," or "partnership." These terms belong to other business forms.
- Primary cooperatives cannot use "federation" or "union" in their name -- those terms are reserved for secondary and tertiary cooperatives.
- The words "development" and "integrated" are discouraged (not prohibited, but CSEA may flag them).
- Write the name in mixed case, not ALL CAPITALS, unless you are appending an acronym after the full name.[^41a]

If CSEA rejects your proposed name, they will tell you why. Choose an alternative and resubmit. The fee applies again for each new reservation.

**Step 2: Prepare your filing package.**

Organize four complete sets. Each set goes in a separate folder (no metallic fasteners). Each set contains:

1. Economic Survey (original signatures)
2. Articles of Cooperation (original signatures, notarized)
3. Bylaws (original signatures on adoption page)
4. Treasurer's Affidavit (original signatures, notarized)
5. Surety Bonds (originals)
6. PRS Certificates (originals or certified true copies)

Plus: the Name Reservation Slip and the registration fee.

**Step 3: Go to the CSEA Provincial Office.**

Bring all four sets, the Name Reservation Slip, all PRS certificates, the registration fee, and valid IDs for the person submitting. It is best practice to have the **Chairperson or Secretary** submit the application personally. They can answer any questions CSEA staff may have on the spot.

**Step 4: Submit and get your acknowledgment receipt.**

CSEA staff will conduct an initial check for completeness. If any document is missing or obviously non-compliant, they may flag it immediately and allow you to correct it. If the package appears complete, CSEA will accept the filing and issue an **acknowledgment receipt**. **Keep this receipt.** It marks the start of the 60-day processing period.

**What to bring -- quick checklist:**

- [ ] Four complete document sets in separate folders
- [ ] Name Reservation Slip (original)
- [ ] PRS Certificates for all organizers
- [ ] Registration fee (check with CSEA for the exact amount)
- [ ] Valid government-issued IDs for the person submitting
- [ ] Contact information (phone number, email) for follow-up

### 4.5.2 The 60-Day Processing Period and Your Rights

Once CSEA accepts your complete application, the clock starts. Here is what happens during the processing period:[^42]

| Stage | What CSEA Does | Typical Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Document Review** | Checks all documents for completeness and format compliance | 5-10 working days |
| **Substantive Evaluation** | Reviews AOC and bylaws for legal compliance; verifies capital structure; checks name against registry | 20-30 working days |
| **On-site Validation** | Visits the declared principal office; verifies common bond, voluntariness, and cooperative readiness | 5-10 working days |
| **Decision** | Approves or denies the application | Within 60 days total |

**What happens during on-site validation.** CSEA sends a field officer to your declared principal office. They verify three things:[^42a]

1. **Physical existence.** Does the principal office actually exist at the address stated in the AOC? Is it a real, functional space where the cooperative can conduct business? A vacant lot, a private residence with no cooperative signage, or a nonexistent address will fail validation.

2. **Common bond verification.** Do the organizers actually share the declared common bond? The field officer may interview several cooperators to confirm that the residential, occupational, institutional, or associational bond is genuine -- not fabricated to meet the legal requirement.

3. **Voluntariness and cooperative understanding.** Are the organizers joining voluntarily? Do they understand what a cooperative is, how it works, and what their obligations are? The PRS certificates establish formal compliance, but the field officer looks for genuine comprehension and willingness.

**Prepare for validation.** When CSEA schedules the visit, make sure key organizers are present at the principal office. Have copies of all filed documents available. Be ready to answer questions about your business plan, membership composition, and capital commitments. A smooth validation visit accelerates your registration.

**Your legal rights during the processing period:**

**Right 1: Action within 60 days.** BTA Bill No. 210, Section 11 requires CSEA to "finally dispose" of your application within sixty days from filing of complete requirements.[^43] This is not a suggestion. It is a statutory mandate.

**Right 2: Deemed approval.** If CSEA does not act within 60 days and the delay is not your fault, the application is **deemed approved**. This means your cooperative is considered registered by operation of law.[^44] In practice, you will still need to follow up with CSEA to obtain the physical Certificate of Registration.

**Right 3: Notice of deficiencies.** If CSEA finds deficiencies during document review, they should notify you in writing. The 60-day clock pauses while you correct the deficiencies. This is the "delay attributable to the applicant" exception. Respond promptly to any deficiency notices.

**What to do while waiting:**

- **Follow up every two weeks.** Call or visit the CSEA Provincial Office. Ask for the status of your application. Be polite but persistent. Document every follow-up (date, person spoken to, response given).
- **Prepare your post-registration action plan.** Do not wait for the COR to start planning your organizational meeting, bank account opening, and bookkeeping setup. Use this time productively.
- **Keep your members engaged.** Hold an informal update meeting. Remind members of their capital commitments. Begin discussing committee assignments and the first-year business plan.

### 4.5.3 What to Do If Your Application Is Denied

Denial is not the end. It is feedback.

**Common grounds for denial:**[^45]

1. Incomplete documentary requirements
2. Non-compliance with minimum capital requirements
3. Cooperative name identical or confusingly similar to an existing cooperative
4. False statements in the AOC or supporting documents
5. Purposes contrary to law, morals, or public policy

**Your appeal rights.** If CSEA denies your application, you have **ninety (90) days from receipt of the notice of denial** to file an appeal.[^46] Under RA 9520, the appeal lies with the **Office of the President**. Under BTA Bill No. 210, the appeal is filed with the **Authority** (CSEA itself, at a higher level). If the appellate authority fails to act on your appeal within ninety days, the application is **deemed approved**.

**Step-by-step appeal process:**

1. **Read the denial notice carefully.** Identify every specific ground for denial.
2. **Determine if the issues are curable.** Most denials involve fixable problems -- missing documents, capital shortfalls, naming conflicts. Only denials based on false statements or illegal purposes present fundamental obstacles.
3. **Fix and refile (fastest option).** If the problems are documentary, correct the specific issues and submit a new application. This is usually faster than a formal appeal. You will need to pay a new registration fee and reserve the name again if the reservation has expired.
4. **File a formal appeal (if you disagree with the denial).** Draft an appeal letter stating: (a) the date and reference number of the denial, (b) the specific grounds you are contesting, (c) your legal and factual basis for contesting each ground, and (d) supporting evidence. File within 90 days.
5. **Follow up on the appeal.** If the appellate authority does not act within 90 days, your application is deemed approved.

**Prevention is better than appeal.** The most effective strategy is to prevent denial altogether. Use the checklist in Section 4.4.1. Cross-check every document against the common rejection reasons in Section 4.4.2. Have a trusted person outside the organizing group review the entire package before submission. Fresh eyes catch errors that tired organizers miss.

## 4.6 After Registration: Your First 90 Days

Your COR has arrived. Your cooperative now has **juridical personality** -- it is a legal person, separate from any individual member.[^47] Congratulations. Now the real work begins.

The first 90 days after registration set the trajectory for your cooperative. Act with purpose. Here is what to do.

### 4.6.1 Conducting the Organizational Meeting

You must conduct an **organizational meeting within 30 days** of receiving your COR.[^48] This is a legal obligation, not a courtesy. At this meeting, the cooperative transitions from a founding group to an operating institution.

**Who attends:** All registered members (all organizers listed in the AOC, plus any new members who have been admitted per the bylaws).

**Required agenda items:**

1. **Call to order and opening prayer**
2. **Presentation of the Certificate of Registration** -- read the COR aloud. This is the cooperative's official "birth announcement."
3. **Ratification of the AOC and Bylaws** -- confirm that the registered documents represent the members' will
4. **Election of the first Board of Directors** -- if the AOC listed interim directors, hold a formal election to confirm or replace them. Follow the election procedures in your bylaws.
5. **Election of Committee Members** -- elect the five mandatory committees:

| Committee | Minimum Members | Primary Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Audit Committee** | 3 | Internal financial oversight |
| **Election Committee** | 3 | Manages all elections and voting |
| **Education and Training Committee** | 3 | Plans member education programs |
| **Mediation and Conciliation Committee** | 3 | Resolves internal disputes |
| **Ethics Committee** | 3 | Enforces code of conduct |

6. **Appointment of officers** -- the Board appoints the Treasurer, Secretary, and Manager (or General Manager)
7. **Approval of the first-year business plan and budget**
8. **Approval of banking arrangements** -- designate the bank and authorize signatories
9. **Approval of surety bond amounts** for accountable officers
10. **Setting the schedule for the first regular Board meeting**

**Document everything.** The Secretary must prepare minutes of the organizational meeting. These minutes become part of your cooperative's official records and will be needed for compliance filings.

### 4.6.2 Opening a Bank Account

Open a bank account **immediately after the organizational meeting**. Your cooperative cannot operate without one. All member capital contributions, business revenues, and grant funds must flow through the cooperative's bank account -- not through any individual's personal account.

**Documents typically required by banks:**

- [ ] Certificate of Registration (COR) -- certified true copy
- [ ] Articles of Cooperation -- certified true copy
- [ ] Bylaws -- certified true copy
- [ ] Board Resolution authorizing the opening of the bank account and designating authorized signatories
- [ ] Minutes of the organizational meeting
- [ ] List of elected officers
- [ ] Valid IDs of authorized signatories
- [ ] TIN (Tax Identification Number) of the cooperative -- apply with BIR first if you do not have one yet

**Which banks serve cooperatives in BARMM?** The major banks operating in BARMM provinces include **Land Bank of the Philippines (LBP)**, **Development Bank of the Philippines (DBP)**, and **Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank of the Philippines**. Al-Amanah is particularly relevant for cooperatives seeking Shari'ah-compliant banking services. Rural banks and cooperative banks in your area may also offer specialized accounts for cooperatives.

**Practical tip:** Visit the bank branch in advance -- before the organizational meeting -- to ask about their specific requirements for cooperative accounts. Each bank may have slightly different documentation requirements beyond the standard list. Some banks require a minimum maintaining balance. Confirm these details early so you can include the banking resolution in your organizational meeting agenda with the correct bank name and account details.

### 4.6.3 Setting Up Your Books

The law requires every cooperative to maintain proper books of account.[^49] This is not optional, and it is not something you can postpone. Set up your books within the first week after opening your bank account.

**Mandatory books of account:**

| Book | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **General Journal** | Records all financial transactions in chronological order |
| **General Ledger** | Summarizes all accounts (assets, liabilities, equity, income, expenses) |
| **Cash Receipts Book** | Records all cash and check receipts |
| **Cash Disbursements Book** | Records all cash and check payments |
| **Members' Subsidiary Ledger** | Tracks each member's share capital, savings, loans, and patronage |

**Hire a bookkeeper.** RA 9520, Article 11 requires that the cooperative's "structure and actual staffing pattern shall include a bookkeeper."[^50] This is a legal requirement, not a recommendation. Your bookkeeper does not need to be a CPA, but they must be competent in basic cooperative accounting. If you cannot afford a full-time bookkeeper, hire one on a part-time basis or contract with a bookkeeping service.

**Initial chart of accounts.** Your chart of accounts organizes every financial category your cooperative will use. Here is a simplified starting framework:

| Account Code | Account Name | Type |
|---|---|---|
| **100-199** | **Assets** | |
| 101 | Cash on Hand | Current Asset |
| 102 | Cash in Bank | Current Asset |
| 110 | Accounts Receivable -- Members | Current Asset |
| 120 | Inventory | Current Asset |
| 150 | Equipment | Fixed Asset |
| **200-299** | **Liabilities** | |
| 201 | Accounts Payable | Current Liability |
| 210 | Deposits from Members | Current Liability |
| **300-399** | **Equity** | |
| 301 | Share Capital -- Common | Equity |
| 310 | Membership Fees | Equity |
| 320 | Reserve Fund | Equity |
| 330 | Education and Training Fund | Equity |
| 340 | Community Development Fund | Equity |
| 350 | Undivided Net Surplus | Equity |
| **400-499** | **Income** | |
| 401 | Service Fee Income | Revenue |
| 402 | Marketing Commission Income | Revenue |
| 410 | Interest/Profit on Investments | Revenue |
| **500-599** | **Expenses** | |
| 501 | Cost of Goods Sold | Expense |
| 510 | Administrative Expenses | Expense |
| 520 | Salaries and Wages | Expense |
| 530 | Bonding Expenses | Expense |
| 540 | Education and Training Expenses | Expense |

Adapt this framework to your cooperative's type. A credit cooperative needs loan-related accounts. A consumer cooperative needs merchandise inventory accounts. A marketing cooperative (like our Sitio Marang example) needs commission income and cost-of-goods accounts.

**Register with the BIR.** Your cooperative must secure a **Tax Identification Number (TIN)** and register with the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) within 30 days of starting business operations. Cooperatives enjoy significant tax exemptions under RA 9520 and the Tax Code, but you still need to register, file returns, and claim your exemptions properly.[^51]

### 4.6.4 First Board Meeting Agenda

Hold the first Board of Directors meeting within **one week** of the organizational meeting. This is where the Board translates the cooperative's founding vision into operational decisions.

**Sample agenda for the first Board meeting:**

| Agenda Item | Purpose | Action Required |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Call to order** | Formally convene the Board | Chairperson opens; Secretary records |
| 2. **Adoption of Board meeting schedule** | Set regular meeting dates for the year | Resolve: "The Board shall meet every [2nd Saturday] of each month" |
| 3. **Appointment of Manager/Officer-in-Charge** | Designate the person who runs day-to-day operations | If no budget for a full-time manager, appoint an interim OIC from among the officers |
| 4. **Approval of bank signatories** | Formalize who can sign checks and authorize withdrawals | Standard: two of three signatories (Chairperson, Treasurer, Manager) |
| 5. **Approval of surety bond amounts** | Set the coverage amount for each accountable officer | Based on initial net worth (paid-up capital + membership fees + other assets) |
| 6. **Adoption of operational policies** | Establish key policies for the first year | Membership admission procedure, capital build-up schedule, loan policies (if applicable) |
| 7. **Approval of first-year budget** | Allocate resources for operations, education, and community development | Keep it lean. Prioritize the cooperative's primary business activity |
| 8. **BIR registration** | Assign responsibility for tax registration | Designate the Treasurer or Manager to process BIR registration within 30 days |
| 9. **Membership drive plan** | Expand membership beyond the founding group | Set target: 25-30 members within 6 months |
| 10. **Schedule of next Board meeting** | Maintain governance rhythm | Confirm the date, time, and venue |

**Key decisions to make immediately:**

- **Banking policy:** Require dual signatures for all disbursements above a threshold (e.g., PHP 5,000).
- **Membership fee:** Confirm the amount (set in the bylaws) and the process for collecting it from new members.
- **Capital build-up schedule:** Set monthly contributions for continuous capital build-up. The bylaws should already specify the mechanism -- the Board now sets the timeline for implementation.
- **Bonding:** All accountable officers must be bonded before the cooperative begins transacting.[^52] Do not delay this. An un-bonded officer who handles funds exposes the cooperative to legal risk.

## 4.7 Chapter Summary: Registration at a Glance

Here is the entire registration process compressed into a single reference:

**Before You File:**

- [ ] Reserve your cooperative name with CSEA (PHP 100-300)
- [ ] Complete the economic survey
- [ ] Draft and finalize the Articles of Cooperation
- [ ] Draft and finalize the Bylaws
- [ ] Ensure all organizers have valid PRS certificates
- [ ] Collect share capital subscriptions and payments from all members
- [ ] Execute and notarize the Treasurer's Affidavit
- [ ] Secure surety bonds for all accountable officers
- [ ] Notarize the Articles of Cooperation
- [ ] Prepare four original copies of all documents in separate folders (no metallic fasteners)

**Filing:**

- [ ] Submit to CSEA Provincial Office where your principal office is located
- [ ] Pay the registration fee
- [ ] Obtain and keep your acknowledgment receipt
- [ ] Note the date -- the 60-day processing period starts now

**After COR Issuance:**

- [ ] Conduct the organizational meeting within 30 days
- [ ] Elect the Board of Directors and committee members
- [ ] Appoint officers (Treasurer, Secretary, Manager)
- [ ] Open a cooperative bank account
- [ ] Register with the BIR for a TIN
- [ ] Set up your books of account
- [ ] Hire or contract a bookkeeper
- [ ] Hold the first Board meeting
- [ ] Adopt operational policies and the first-year budget
- [ ] Bond all accountable officers before beginning operations

Your cooperative is now registered, organized, and operational. The next chapter addresses how to build the governance structure that keeps it that way.
