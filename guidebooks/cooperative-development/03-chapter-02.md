# Chapter 2 — Understanding Cooperative Types and Choosing the Right One

The first decision you make about your cooperative is the most consequential. Choose the wrong type and you will spend years fighting a structure that does not match your community's needs. You will overpay for compliance. You will confuse your members. You will build governance for activities you never pursue while neglecting the ones that matter.

A women's savings group in Lanao del Sur registered as a multi-purpose cooperative because the organizer assumed bigger was better. The group wanted one thing: a **credit facility** for small loans among its thirty members. Instead, they inherited the governance burden of a multi-purpose structure — mandatory committees for lending, marketing, and production they never intended to operate, capital requirements nearly seven times higher than necessary, and reporting obligations that overwhelmed their part-time treasurer. Within two years, they lost their certificate of compliance. They joined the **88.34 percent** of BARMM cooperatives that cannot prove they meet minimum legal requirements.[^1]

That outcome was preventable. This chapter gives you the tools to prevent it.

## 2.1 Types of Cooperatives Under Philippine and Bangsamoro Law

Two legal frameworks define cooperative types in BARMM. **RA 9520** (the Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) applies today and governs all cooperatives registered with CSEA.[^2] **BTA Bill No. 210** (the proposed Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code) will replace many of these provisions once enacted.[^3] The good news: both frameworks recognize substantially the same cooperative types. The differences lie in the details — and in the addition of social enterprises under BTA Bill No. 210.

Know both frameworks. Build your cooperative under RA 9520 today. Prepare for BTA Bill No. 210 tomorrow.

### 2.1.1 The Twenty-One Types Recognized by Law

RA 9520 identifies the primary cooperative types under Article 23. BTA Bill No. 210, Section 20 expands the list to twenty-one explicit types — including several that RA 9520 recognized implicitly but did not name individually.[^4] Here is the complete list, drawn from both frameworks.

**1. Credit Cooperative**

Promotes and undertakes **savings and lending services** among members. Generates a common pool of funds to provide financial assistance for **productive and provident purposes**.[^5] This is the backbone of cooperative finance in BARMM.

*Example*: A group of market vendors in Cotabato City pools weekly savings and extends small loans to members for inventory restocking.

**2. Consumers Cooperative**

Procures and distributes **commodities** to members and non-members at fair prices.[^6] Think of it as a community-owned store.

*Example*: A barangay cooperative in Basilan bulk-purchases rice, canned goods, and school supplies, selling to members at lower prices than retail.

**3. Producers Cooperative**

Undertakes **joint production** — agricultural or industrial. Members produce raw materials or goods; the cooperative processes and sells them.[^7] This is the most common type in BARMM, accounting for **37.58 percent** of all registered cooperatives in the region.[^8]

*Example*: Cassava farmers in Maguindanao del Norte collectively process their harvest into cassava chips and flour for sale to distributors. BARMM produces **41.70 percent** of the country's cassava supply.[^9]

**4. Marketing Cooperative**

Supplies **production inputs** to members and **markets their products**.[^10] The critical distinction from a producers cooperative: a marketing cooperative does not process goods. It connects producers to buyers.

*Example*: A seaweed marketing cooperative in Tawi-Tawi aggregates dried seaweed from individual farmers and negotiates bulk contracts with processors in Zamboanga. BARMM produces **48.42 percent** of the Philippines' seaweed.[^11] Marketing cooperatives account for **27.54 percent** of all cooperatives in the region.[^12]

**5. Service Cooperative**

Engages in **medical and dental care, hospitalization, transportation, insurance, housing, labor, power and electricity, communication, professional services**, and other services.[^13] This is the broadest single-purpose type.

*Example*: A transport cooperative in Lanao del Sur operates multicabs along routes where no public utility vehicles run.

**6. Multi-Purpose Cooperative**

Combines **two or more** of the business activities of the other types.[^14] This is the most recognized type nationwide — and the most frequently misused. See Section 2.2.2 for why multi-purpose is not always the answer.

*Example*: A cooperative in Maguindanao del Sur that operates a lending window, a consumer store, and a rice trading business under one registration.

**7. Advocacy Cooperative**

Promotes and advocates **cooperativism** among members and the public through socially-oriented projects, education, training, research, and communication.[^15] This type does not engage in commercial business. Its mission is to strengthen the cooperative movement itself.

*Example*: A cooperative development advocacy group that conducts Pre-Registration Seminars and cooperative governance training in island municipalities of Tawi-Tawi.

**8. Agrarian Reform Cooperative**

Organized by **marginal farmers**, the majority of whom are agrarian reform beneficiaries, for developing appropriate systems of **land tenure, land development, land consolidation, or land management** in areas covered by agrarian reform.[^16] BARMM has **240 Agrarian Reform Beneficiary Organizations (ARBOs)** with 34,743 members across the region.[^17]

*Example*: Rice farmers in Lanao del Sur who received Certificates of Land Ownership Award (CLOAs) form a cooperative to consolidate their small plots for mechanized farming.

**9. Cooperative Bank**

Organized primarily to provide **a wide range of financial services to cooperatives and their members**.[^18] This is a secondary or tertiary cooperative — its members are other cooperatives, not individuals. Cooperative banks are regulated by the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) in addition to CSEA.

*Example*: Five credit cooperatives in Maguindanao pool resources to form a cooperative bank that can offer checking accounts, remittance services, and larger loan facilities.

**10. Dairy Cooperative**

Members are engaged in the **production of fresh milk**, which may be processed and marketed as dairy products.[^19]

*Example*: Carabao dairy farmers in a cluster of barangays organize to produce pasteurized milk and yogurt for sale in local markets.

**11. Education Cooperative**

Organized for the primary purpose of **owning and operating licensed educational institutions**.[^20] BTA Bill No. 210 explicitly references the Bangsamoro Education Code alongside RA 9155 (Governance of Basic Education Act).

*Example*: A community in Basilan establishes a cooperative that owns and operates a private elementary and high school, with tuition revenues reinvested into school facilities.

**12. Electric Cooperative**

Undertakes **power generation** (using renewable energy sources, including hybrid systems), acquisition, and **operation of sub-transmission or distribution** to household members.[^21]

*Example*: An off-grid island community in Tawi-Tawi forms an electric cooperative to install and manage solar panel systems for member households.

**13. Financial Service Cooperative**

Organized for **savings and credit services and other financial services** — broader than a credit cooperative but without the full banking license of a cooperative bank.[^22] Requires a minimum paid-up capital of **PHP 10,000,000**.[^23]

*Example*: A large cooperative in Cotabato City that accepts deposits, issues loans, and offers remittance services but does not operate as a bank.

**14. Fishermen Cooperative**

Organized by **marginalized fishermen** whose products are marketed as fresh or processed products.[^24] This type exists alongside producers cooperatives but targets the specific needs of fishing communities.

*Example*: Small-scale fishermen in island barangays of Basilan collectively own motorized bancas, share fuel costs, and market their daily catch through the cooperative.

**15. Health Services Cooperative**

Organized for **medical, dental, and other health services**.[^25]

*Example*: Health professionals and community members in Lanao del Sur form a cooperative to operate a community health clinic offering consultations, laboratory services, and a pharmacy.

**16. Housing Cooperative**

Organized to **assist or provide access to housing** for regular members who actively participate in the savings program for housing.[^26] Co-owned and controlled by members.

*Example*: A group of government employees in Cotabato City forms a housing cooperative to develop an affordable residential subdivision for its members.

**17. Insurance Cooperative**

Engaged in the **business of insuring life and property** of cooperatives and their members.[^27]

*Example*: A federation of cooperatives in BARMM establishes an insurance cooperative to offer affordable life and calamity insurance products to member-cooperatives.

**18. Transport Cooperative**

Includes **land and sea transportation**, limited to small vessels as defined under Philippine maritime laws.[^28]

*Example*: Motorela operators in a municipality of Lanao del Sur organize as a transport cooperative, pooling resources for vehicle maintenance and negotiating franchise terms collectively.

**19. Water Service Cooperative**

Organized to **own, operate, and manage water systems** for the provision and distribution of potable water to members and their households.[^29] Requires a minimum paid-up capital of **PHP 1,000,000**.[^30]

*Example*: Households in a rural barangay of Maguindanao del Sur form a water service cooperative to develop and maintain a spring-fed water distribution system.

**20. Workers Cooperative**

Organized by **workers, including the self-employed**, who are simultaneously the members and owners of the enterprise. The principal purpose is to **provide employment and business opportunities** to members.[^31]

*Example*: Weavers producing traditional Maranao malong organize a workers cooperative where each member is both an artisan and a co-owner of the weaving enterprise.

**21. Other Types**

Both RA 9520 and BTA Bill No. 210 include a catch-all provision: **other types of cooperatives as may be determined by the Authority**.[^32] This gives CSEA the flexibility to recognize new cooperative forms as the economy evolves.

### 2.1.2 Categories by Membership Size and Asset Level

Beyond type, every cooperative falls into a **category** that determines its regulatory obligations. Two classification systems apply: one based on **organizational level** and one based on **asset size**.

**Organizational Level**

| Category | Who Are the Members? | Minimum to Organize |
|----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| **Primary** | Natural persons (individuals) | 15 persons |
| **Secondary (Federation)** | Primary cooperatives | 3 cooperatives (BTA Bill 210) / 2 cooperatives (RA 9520) |
| **Tertiary (Union)** | Secondary cooperatives | 2 secondaries |

Most cooperatives you will encounter in BARMM are **primary cooperatives** — their members are individual people. When primary cooperatives band together, they form secondaries (federations). When federations organize at a higher level, they form tertiaries (unions). BTA Bill No. 210 requires a minimum of **three** registered cooperatives to form a federation.[^33] RA 9520 requires two.[^34]

**Asset Size Categories**

CDA classifies cooperatives by total asset size. This classification determines audit requirements, reporting standards, and eligibility for certain programs.[^35]

| Category | Total Assets | What This Means |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Micro** | Up to ₱3,000,000 | Internal audit by Audit Committee is sufficient |
| **Small** | ₱3,000,001 to ₱15,000,000 | CPA audit required (may be CDA-accredited) |
| **Medium** | ₱15,000,001 to ₱100,000,000 | CPA audit required; enhanced reporting |
| **Large** | Above ₱100,000,000 | CPA audit by CDA-accredited auditor; full compliance regime |

**Why does category matter?** Three reasons.

**First, audit requirements scale with category.** A micro cooperative with assets under ₱3 million can rely on its own Audit Committee for the annual audit.[^36] A large cooperative with assets exceeding ₱100 million must engage a CDA-accredited external CPA. The cost difference is significant for small communities.

**Second, reporting obligations increase with size.** Larger cooperatives face more granular reporting requirements in their Cooperative Annual Progress Report (CAPR) and Cooperative Audited Financial Statements (CAFS). If you register as a type that forces you into a higher asset category before you are ready, you inherit compliance burdens that can overwhelm your officers.

**Third, training mandates vary.** Officers of larger cooperatives face stricter training requirements. The CDA and CSEA mandate specific governance and management courses for officers based on cooperative category.

**Territorial Categories**

BTA Bill No. 210 also categorizes cooperatives by **territory** — their area of operations, which may or may not coincide with political subdivisions.[^37] A cooperative's area of operation is defined in its Articles of Cooperation. Start local. Expand only when your membership and capacity justify it.

## 2.2 Matching Your Community's Needs to a Cooperative Type

You now know the twenty-one types and the categories. The harder question is: **which type fits your community?**

Do not start with the type. Start with the need.

### 2.2.1 The Five Questions to Ask Before Choosing a Type

Before you select a cooperative type, gather your prospective members and answer five questions honestly. Write the answers down. They will shape every decision that follows.

**Question 1: What is the primary economic activity of your members?**

Identify the single activity that most members share. Are they farmers? Fishermen? Market vendors? Weavers? Transport operators? The answer usually points directly to a cooperative type.

If most members are seaweed farmers, you are looking at a **producers cooperative** or a **marketing cooperative** — not a multi-purpose cooperative. If most members are salaried workers who need savings and loan facilities, you are looking at a **credit cooperative**.

**Question 2: What do your members need most right now?**

Separate wants from needs. A community may want a cooperative store, a lending window, and a rice trading business. But what do they need *first*? If the answer is access to credit, start with credit. If the answer is fair prices for their produce, start with marketing.

The temptation is to say "we need everything." Resist that temptation. A cooperative that tries to do everything from day one does nothing well.

**Question 3: How much capital can your members realistically raise?**

Be honest about this. The minimum paid-up capital for a **primary single-purpose cooperative** is ₱15,000.[^38] For a **multi-purpose cooperative**, it is ₱100,000.[^39] For a **financial service cooperative**, it is ₱10,000,000.[^40] For a **cooperative bank**, the BSP sets additional capital requirements.

If your fifteen members can each contribute ₱1,000, you have ₱15,000 — enough for a primary single-purpose cooperative but far short of a multi-purpose structure. Do not overcommit your members. Start with a type your community can afford.

| Cooperative Type | Minimum Paid-Up Capital | If 15 Members Share Equally |
|------------------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| Primary (general single-purpose) | ₱15,000 | ₱1,000 per member |
| Multi-purpose | ₱100,000 | ₱6,667 per member |
| Credit | ₱100,000 | ₱6,667 per member |
| Water Service | ₱1,000,000 | ₱66,667 per member |
| Financial Service | ₱10,000,000 | ₱666,667 per member |
| Electric | ₱5,000,000 | ₱333,333 per member |

Look at the last column. If your members are marginalized fisherfolk in Tawi-Tawi earning subsistence-level incomes, a financial service cooperative is not realistic. A fishermen cooperative at ₱15,000 minimum paid-up is.

**Question 4: What regulatory burden can your officers manage?**

Every cooperative type carries compliance obligations: annual general assemblies, CAPR and CAFS submissions, audit requirements, committee formations, training mandates. Multi-purpose cooperatives carry the heaviest burden because they must comply with the rules for every business activity they operate.

Ask yourself: Do your prospective officers have the time, literacy, and bookkeeping skills to handle complex compliance? If your officers are volunteer farmers in a remote barangay, a simpler cooperative type with fewer reporting requirements may serve you better.

**Question 5: What infrastructure, organizations, and markets already exist in your community?**

Do not duplicate what already exists. If a strong credit cooperative already operates in your area, your community may benefit more from a marketing cooperative that helps members sell their products — and encourages them to join the existing credit cooperative for their savings and lending needs.

Map what exists. Find the gap. Fill that gap with the right cooperative type.

### 2.2.2 Common Mistakes: Why Multi-Purpose Is Not Always the Answer

Walk into any CSEA field office and ask which cooperative type people choose most often. The answer is **multi-purpose**. Ask which type has the highest failure rate. The answer is often the same.

Multi-purpose is the default because it sounds safe. If you register as multi-purpose, you can do credit, production, marketing, and consumer services all under one roof. Why limit yourself?

Here is why.

**Higher capital requirements.** The minimum paid-up capital for a multi-purpose cooperative is ₱100,000 — nearly **seven times higher** than the ₱15,000 required for a primary single-purpose cooperative.[^41] For a community of fifteen members with limited savings, that difference can mean the cooperative never forms at all, or forms with members overextending themselves financially.

**More complex governance.** A multi-purpose cooperative must form committees for each business activity. If you operate a lending window and a consumer store, you need a credit committee, an inventory committee, and the management capacity to oversee both. Each committee needs trained officers. Each business line needs its own bookkeeping. The governance overhead multiplies with every additional business activity.

**Heavier compliance burden.** Multi-purpose cooperatives must report on all their business activities in their CAPR and CAFS. Their audited financial statements must cover multiple revenue streams, cost centers, and fund allocations. For a micro cooperative with a part-time treasurer, this level of reporting is often the reason compliance lapses — and compliance lapses are the reason cooperatives lose their certificates.

**Diluted focus.** The most successful cooperatives in BARMM do one thing well before they expand. A seaweed marketing cooperative in Tawi-Tawi that builds a reliable supply chain for its one product creates more value than a multi-purpose cooperative in the same community that spreads its limited capital across lending, trading, and retail — doing all three poorly.

**When is multi-purpose the right choice?** When your cooperative has:

- Enough members (well beyond the minimum 15) to staff multiple committees
- Sufficient capital to fund multiple business activities without straining any one
- Officers with proven governance and bookkeeping experience
- Existing operations in two or more areas that genuinely complement each other
- The explicit demand from members for multiple services

If you cannot check all five, start single-purpose. You can always amend your Articles of Cooperation later to add business activities once your cooperative is stable and compliant.[^42]

## 2.3 Single-Purpose vs. Multi-Purpose: Trade-offs

The choice between single-purpose and multi-purpose is the most consequential structural decision you will make. Here are the trade-offs laid out clearly.

### 2.3.1 Regulatory Requirements by Type

| Requirement | Single-Purpose (e.g., Producers) | Multi-Purpose |
|-------------|----------------------------------|---------------|
| **Regulatory authority** | CSEA (BARMM) / CDA (national) | CSEA / CDA |
| **Minimum membership** | 15 natural persons | 15 natural persons |
| **Minimum paid-up capital** | ₱15,000 | ₱100,000 |
| **Mandatory committees** | Board of Directors, Audit Committee, Election Committee, Mediation/Conciliation Committee | All of the above PLUS committees for each business line (e.g., Credit Committee, Education and Training Committee) |
| **Annual reporting** | CAPR + CAFS for one business activity | CAPR + CAFS covering ALL business activities |
| **Audit requirement (Micro)** | Internal by Audit Committee | Internal by Audit Committee |
| **Audit requirement (Small+)** | External CPA | External CPA |
| **General Assembly** | Annual, within 90 days after fiscal year end | Same |
| **Training mandate** | Officers must complete mandatory training per CDA/CSEA rules | Same, plus training for each business-line committee |

### 2.3.2 Capital Requirements by Type

Use this table to determine how much capital your cooperative needs based on type and the capital structure rules under RA 9520 and CDA Memorandum Circular 2015-01 (as amended by MC 2016-02).[^43]

**Capital Structure Formula:**
- **Authorized Capital** = Total shares the cooperative can issue (set by organizers)
- **Subscribed Capital** = At least **25%** of Authorized Capital
- **Paid-Up Capital** = At least **25%** of Subscribed Capital (subject to minimum floor)

| Cooperative Type | Minimum Paid-Up Capital | Capital to Authorize (Working Backward) |
|------------------|------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Primary (single-purpose)** | ₱15,000 | ₱240,000 |
| **Multi-Purpose** | ₱100,000 | ₱1,600,000 |
| **Credit** | ₱100,000 | ₱1,600,000 |
| **Water Service** | ₱1,000,000 | ₱16,000,000 |
| **Electric** | ₱5,000,000 | ₱80,000,000 |
| **Financial Service** | ₱10,000,000 | ₱160,000,000 |
| **Cooperative Bank** | Per BSP regulations | Per BSP regulations |
| **Secondary (Federation)** | ₱100,000 | ₱1,600,000 |
| **Tertiary (Union)** | ₱100,000 | ₱1,600,000 |

**How to read the "Working Backward" column**: If the minimum paid-up is ₱15,000, and paid-up must be at least 25% of subscribed, then subscribed must be at least ₱60,000. And if subscribed must be at least 25% of authorized, then authorized must be at least ₱240,000. Organizers set the authorized capital in the Articles of Cooperation — so plan accordingly.

**Example for a Producers Cooperative:**

A cassava producers cooperative in Maguindanao del Norte has 20 members. They set the authorized capital at ₱300,000 (with a par value of ₱1,000 per share, 300 shares). Each member subscribes to 4 shares (₱4,000 each), for a total subscribed capital of ₱80,000 — this exceeds 25% of ₱300,000 (₱75,000). Each member pays 25% of their subscription upfront (₱1,000), for a total paid-up capital of ₱20,000 — this exceeds the ₱15,000 minimum floor.

The cooperative is now capitalized, compliant, and ready to register as a primary producers cooperative.

## 2.4 Federations, Unions, and Secondary Cooperatives

Individual cooperatives are powerful. Cooperatives working together are transformative. Philippine and Bangsamoro law recognizes higher-level cooperative structures designed for exactly this purpose.

### 2.4.1 When Your Cooperative Should Join a Federation

A **federation** is a secondary cooperative whose members are primary cooperatives of the **same type or related business activities**.[^44] Think of a federation as a cooperative of cooperatives.

**Minimum requirement under BTA Bill No. 210**: Three or more registered cooperatives in BARMM of the same type of services may form a federation.[^45] Under RA 9520, the minimum is two.[^46]

**What can a federation do?**

| Function | How It Helps Your Cooperative |
|----------|------------------------------|
| **Carry on complementary business** | Provides services that augment your cooperative's work without competing with it |
| **Education and advisory work** | Conducts trainings, seminars, and capability-building for member cooperatives |
| **Simplify operations** | Offers shared bookkeeping, accounting systems, and operational procedures |
| **Publish and communicate** | Produces newsletters, market information, and educational materials |
| **Coordinate activities** | Harmonizes schedules, standards, and market access across members |
| **Enter joint ventures** | Negotiates with national and international partners on behalf of all members |

**When should you consider forming or joining a federation?**

- When your cooperative **cannot negotiate good prices alone** because your volume is too small. Five seaweed marketing cooperatives in Tawi-Tawi negotiating as a federation have far more leverage with processors than any single cooperative.
- When your officers **need training** but cannot afford to bring in trainers individually. A federation can organize and subsidize trainings for all member cooperatives.
- When **shared services** (bookkeeping, audit preparation, compliance filing) would save money for every member cooperative.
- When you need to **advocate collectively** with CSEA, LGUs, or the BTA Parliament for policies that affect your sector.

**Capital requirement for federations**: Minimum paid-up capital of ₱100,000.[^47]

### 2.4.2 Cooperative Unions and Their Functions

A **cooperative union** is different from a federation. Where federations are organized by cooperatives of the **same type** and engage in **business activities**, unions represent the **interests and welfare of all types** of cooperatives at the provincial, city, regional, or national level.[^48]

Unions are primarily **advocacy and representation bodies**, not business enterprises. Under BTA Bill No. 210, cooperative unions may:[^49]

- **Represent** member organizations before government and other bodies
- **Acquire, analyze, and disseminate** economic and statistical information
- **Sponsor studies** in the economic, legal, financial, and social aspects of cooperation
- **Promote** cooperative principles and practices
- **Develop** the cooperative movement in their jurisdictions
- **Advise** authorities on all questions relating to cooperatives
- **Raise funds** through membership fees, dues, donations, and subsidies

**Key distinction**: A federation does business. A union does advocacy. A federation of fishermen cooperatives might market fish collectively. A cooperative union might lobby the BTA Parliament for tax incentives for all cooperatives.

**When should cooperatives form or join a union?**

- When cooperatives of **different types** in a province or city need a unified voice
- When the cooperative sector needs **representation** before CSEA, the BTA Parliament, or national agencies
- When **policy advocacy** — not shared business activity — is the primary goal

Cooperative unions may assist national, regional, and local governments in development activities within their jurisdictions.[^50] In a region where **most LGUs have no appointed Municipal Cooperative Officers**, a strong cooperative union can fill the gap between CSEA's regional office and the cooperatives in remote municipalities.[^51]

## 2.5 Social Enterprises: A Parallel Path

BTA Bill No. 210 does something that RA 9520 does not: it creates a **legal framework for social enterprises** alongside cooperatives.[^52] Book III of the proposed code introduces social enterprises as a distinct organizational form — and as a status that cooperatives themselves can adopt.

If you are organizing a community-based initiative in BARMM, you now have two paths, not just one. Understanding the difference — and the overlap — is critical.

### 2.5.1 What Makes a Social Enterprise Different from a Cooperative

A **social enterprise (SE)** under BTA Bill No. 210 is a business organization **primarily dedicated to addressing social, cultural, or environmental challenges**, with profit as a **secondary consideration**.[^53]

A **cooperative** is an autonomous association of persons with a common bond who voluntarily join together to achieve their **social, economic, and cultural needs** by making equitable contributions to capital and sharing risks and benefits.[^54]

The distinction is subtle but important:

| Feature | Cooperative | Social Enterprise |
|---------|------------|-------------------|
| **Primary purpose** | Meet members' shared needs | Address social, cultural, or environmental challenges |
| **Profit orientation** | Benefits distributed to members (patronage refunds, interest on capital) | Profit is secondary; revenues reinvested in social mission |
| **Governance** | Democratic member control (one member, one vote) | Democratic governance with stakeholder inclusion (not limited to member-owners) |
| **Who benefits** | Members primarily | The marginalized sector served or society at large |
| **Legal form** | Cooperative (registered with CSEA/CDA) | May be a corporation, partnership, sole proprietorship, or cooperative |
| **Regulatory body** | CSEA (BARMM) / CDA (national) | CSEA (BARMM) for registration and accreditation |

**The most important line in that table**: a social enterprise can take **any legal form** — including a cooperative.[^55] A cooperative can also be a social enterprise. The two are not mutually exclusive.

Social enterprises under BTA Bill No. 210 may operate in areas including:[^56]

- Social services
- Health care
- Education
- Livelihood development
- Environmental protection
- Peace and reconciliation
- Disaster risk reduction and management

### 2.5.2 When to Organize as a Social Enterprise Instead

Choose a **social enterprise** over a cooperative when:

**Your primary mission is to serve people who are not your members.** A cooperative exists to serve its members. A social enterprise exists to serve a target community or address a societal problem. If your initiative aims to provide free vocational training to out-of-school youth, or to rehabilitate mangrove ecosystems, or to offer subsidized health services to internally displaced persons — and the beneficiaries are not the same people who will own and govern the organization — a social enterprise structure may fit better.

**Your funding model relies on reinvestment rather than member returns.** Cooperatives distribute net surplus to members through patronage refunds and interest on capital. Social enterprises invest a **substantive part** of their net revenues back into their social mission.[^57] If your organizers want the profits to go back into the community rather than into member dividends, the SE model aligns better.

**Your governance includes stakeholders beyond member-owners.** Social enterprises must apply rules of **democratic governance where decision-making includes relevant stakeholders in addition to share owners or members**.[^58] If your organization wants beneficiaries, partner NGOs, or community leaders involved in governance alongside investor-members, the SE structure accommodates this.

**Can you be both?** Yes. BTA Bill No. 210 explicitly provides that cooperatives may be registered and accredited as social enterprises.[^59] A cooperative operating as a social enterprise enjoys the **tax treatment provided under the Cooperative Code** for its cooperative activities, plus additional benefits for its social enterprise status.[^60]

**Tax advantages for social enterprises under BTA Bill No. 210:**

| SE Category | Tax Benefit |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Micro SEs** | Exempt from tax on income from operations; LGUs may reduce or exempt from local taxes[^61] |
| **Other SEs** | Exempt from regional taxes, fees, and charges for the **first three years** of operation; LGUs may reduce or exempt from local taxes[^62] |
| **Cooperatives operating as SEs** | Covered by cooperative tax treatment under the Code[^63] |
| **All qualified SEs** | Preferential rights in government procurement processes[^64] |
| **SE reinvestments** | Reinvestments for SE initiatives within the area of operation are **tax deductible** from gross income[^65] |

**The dual-registration advantage.** A cooperative that also registers as a social enterprise under CSEA can access **both** cooperative support programs (PESO Project, STOCK Project) and social enterprise programs (RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development, Social Enterprise Capability Building and Sustainability Program).[^66] This is a strategic option for cooperatives whose work has a clear social mission beyond member benefit.

**Financing for social enterprises.** BTA Bill No. 210 mandates a **Shari'ah-compliant financing program** for startup and micro cooperatives and social enterprises, implemented by CSEA in collaboration with the Ministry of Finance and Budget and Management (MFBM), the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Aquatic Resources (MAFAR), and the Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT).[^67] This financing must be paired with **business-readiness and capacity development** interventions.[^68]

Local government units must allocate **five percent** of their funds toward financing social enterprise initiatives prioritized in local development plans.[^69] For communities in BARMM where access to conventional financing is limited, this provision opens a meaningful funding channel.

## Choosing Your Type: A Decision Summary

Use this flowchart as a final check before you commit to a cooperative type.

**Step 1: Identify the primary activity.**
Ask: What is the one thing your members do, need, or produce? Match it to one of the twenty-one types.

**Step 2: Check the capital requirement.**
Can your members realistically raise the minimum paid-up capital for that type? If not, consider a type with a lower threshold — or recruit more members to spread the cost.

**Step 3: Assess your governance capacity.**
Do you have enough capable, willing officers to staff the mandatory committees? If not, choose a simpler structure.

**Step 4: Evaluate multi-purpose honestly.**
Only register as multi-purpose if you genuinely need two or more business activities from day one AND you have the capital, members, and officers to manage them all.

**Step 5: Consider social enterprise status.**
If your mission extends beyond member benefit to community impact, explore dual registration as a cooperative and social enterprise under BTA Bill No. 210.

**Step 6: Plan to grow.**
Start simple. Register as a single-purpose cooperative. Build compliance discipline. Train your officers. Grow your membership and capital. When you are ready — and only when you are ready — amend your Articles of Cooperation to add new business activities or convert to multi-purpose.

The best cooperative for your community is not the biggest or the most versatile. It is the one your community can sustain.
