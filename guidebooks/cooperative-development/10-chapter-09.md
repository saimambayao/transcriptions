# Chapter 9 — Compliance: Reports, Audits, and Certificates

Of the 12,214 cooperatives registered in BARMM, only 11.66 percent are compliant. The rest are on borrowed time -- operating without current certificates, filing late reports, or not filing at all. Non-compliance does not just mean paperwork trouble. It means your cooperative cannot renew its tax exemption, cannot access government programs, and cannot prove to any bank or partner that it is legitimate. Every day you operate without a Certificate of Compliance, you accumulate PHP 100 in penalties for your accountable officers and drift closer to dissolution proceedings.

This chapter keeps your cooperative out of that majority. It covers every compliance obligation your cooperative faces -- what to file, when to file it, how to prepare it, and what happens if you fail. By the end, you will have a month-by-month calendar, a step-by-step guide for preparing your annual reports, and a clear understanding of the penalties that follow non-compliance.

Compliance is not bureaucracy. It is your cooperative's proof of life.

---

## 9.1 Why Compliance Matters (And What Happens When You Fail)

Think of compliance as your cooperative's vital signs. A hospital monitors a patient's pulse, blood pressure, and temperature. The Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA) monitors your reports, your audits, and your fund allocations. When those vital signs go silent -- when you stop filing, stop auditing, stop allocating -- the CSEA assumes your cooperative is in trouble. And it acts accordingly.

Compliance serves three purposes. **First**, it protects your members. Annual audits and financial statements reveal whether their money is being managed properly. **Second**, it protects your cooperative's legal standing. Without a current Certificate of Compliance (COC), your cooperative cannot secure a Certificate of Tax Exemption, cannot access government lending programs, and cannot be endorsed by the CSEA to any agency.[^1] **Third**, it protects your officers. When things go wrong in a non-compliant cooperative, the officers who failed to file bear personal liability -- including fines they must pay from their own pockets.[^2]

### 9.1.1 The Compliance Calendar: Annual Deadlines

Every cooperative registered in the Bangsamoro follows the same annual rhythm. The calendar below assumes your cooperative uses the **calendar year** (January 1 to December 31) as its fiscal year. If your bylaws specify a different fiscal year, adjust accordingly -- but your CAPR and CAFS submission deadline remains **120 calendar days after the close of the calendar year**, regardless of your fiscal year.[^3]

| Month | Compliance Activity | Responsible Party |
|---|---|---|
| **January** | Close your books for the preceding fiscal year. Begin preparing financial statements. | Treasurer, Bookkeeper |
| **February** | Engage your external auditor (if required by asset size). Begin compiling CAPR data. | Board of Directors, Audit Committee |
| **March** | Hold your **Annual General Assembly** within 90 days of fiscal year end. Present the audit report, approve net surplus distribution, elect officers (if election year). | Board of Directors, General Manager |
| **April 1-30** | **Submission deadline.** File your CAPR and CAFS through the CSEA reporting system within 120 days after the close of the calendar year. Pay the COC fee of **PHP 100.00**. | Chairperson, Treasurer |
| **May-June** | Respond to any **Notice of Deficiency** within 15 calendar days of receipt. Correct and resubmit. | Chairperson, Accountant |
| **July** | If you have not filed or corrected deficiencies by June 30, expect a **Show Cause Order (SCO)**. Respond within 15 days of receipt. | Board of Directors |
| **August-September** | Remit 50% of the Cooperative Education and Training Fund (CETF) to your chosen federation or union. File the semi-annual Mediation and Conciliation report. | Treasurer, Education Committee |
| **October-November** | Update board member list with the CSEA if officers changed after the GA. Renew surety bonds before expiry. | Secretary, Treasurer |
| **December** | Review compliance status. Confirm your COC is current (valid until April 30 of the succeeding year). Plan for next year's audit engagement. | Audit Committee, Board of Directors |

**The critical date is April 30.** Miss it, and the penalty clock starts at PHP 100 per day.[^4] Miss it by 60 days, and you have accumulated PHP 6,000 in fines for your accountable officers. Miss it entirely, and your cooperative cannot receive a COC for that year -- which means no tax exemption renewal, no government endorsements, and no proof of good standing.

### 9.1.2 Penalties for Non-Compliance

The law does not treat non-compliance as a minor inconvenience. It treats it as a failure of stewardship. The penalties escalate from fines to suspension to dissolution. Study the table below. Know where each violation falls.

| Violation | Penalty | Legal Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **Late submission** of CAPR/CAFS | **PHP 100.00 per day of delay**, imposed on accountable officers personally | MC 2025-02, Sec. 10[^5] |
| **Non-submission** of required reports | Notice of Non-Submission within 15 days of deadline; continued non-compliance triggers Show Cause Order (SCO) | MC 2025-02, Secs. 11, 13[^6] |
| **Reports with deficiency** (incomplete or inaccurate) | Notice of Deficiency; 15-day compliance window; failure treated as non-submission with PHP 100/day penalty | MC 2025-02, Sec. 12[^7] |
| **Failure to comply with SCO** | Revocation of authority to operate proceedings under Art. 53 of RA 9520 | MC 2025-02, Sec. 13[^8] |
| **Failure to submit mandatory documents** (board list, GA minutes) | Fine up to PHP 50,000 on the cooperative; ineligibility for COC or COR until penalty is settled | BTA Bill 210, Sec. 52[^9] |
| **False or misleading reports** | Imprisonment of 1-5 years or fine up to PHP 50,000, or both | BTA Bill 210, Book IV, Sec. 8(g)(ii)[^10] |
| **Hindering audit or examination** | Imprisonment of 1-5 years or fine up to PHP 50,000, or both | BTA Bill 210, Book IV, Sec. 8(g)(v)[^11] |
| **Failure to operate for 2 consecutive years** | Ground for dissolution | RA 9520, Art. 70[^12] |
| **Willful violation of the Code or bylaws** | Ground for dissolution | RA 9520, Art. 70[^13] |

**The personal liability rule.** The PHP 100/day penalty does not fall on the cooperative's funds. It falls on the **accountable officers personally** -- typically the Chairperson and Treasurer.[^14] If the cooperative's COC is not issued because of non-submission, those officers are also **barred from continuing as officers** of any cooperative, **disqualified from election or appointment** in the cooperative for the next two consecutive years, and required to shoulder any tax penalties the BIR or BARMM revenue authorities impose because the cooperative lost its tax-exempt status.[^15]

That last point deserves emphasis. When your cooperative loses its COC, it loses its pathway to a Certificate of Tax Exemption. When it loses its CTE, every transaction that was previously tax-free becomes taxable. The back taxes, interest, and penalties the BIR assesses do not fall on the cooperative's general fund. They fall on the officers who caused the non-compliance.

## 9.2 The Cooperative Annual Performance Report (CAPR)

The **Cooperative Annual Performance Report (CAPR)** is your cooperative's annual self-portrait. It tells the CSEA who you are, what you did during the year, how many members you have, who leads you, and what financial transactions you conducted. Every cooperative -- regardless of type, size, or category -- must file a CAPR.[^16]

### 9.2.1 What the CAPR Contains

The CAPR has six modules. Each module covers a different dimension of your cooperative's life. Here is what each contains and why the CSEA requires it.

**Module 1: General Information.** Your cooperative's name, registration number, date of registration, principal office address, contact details, type (primary, federation, union), category (credit, multi-purpose, marketing, service, producer, consumer, etc.), and area of operation. This module verifies that your cooperative's profile on record is current.

**Module 2: Business Activities.** The types of operations your cooperative conducted during the year. Revenue sources. Services offered to members and non-members. Transaction volumes. This module shows whether your cooperative is actually operating and generating economic activity for its members.

**Module 3: Membership.** Total number of members by type (regular and associate). New members admitted during the year. Members who resigned, were terminated, or died. The breakdown of active versus inactive members. Male versus female membership count. This module reveals your cooperative's growth trajectory and demographic composition.

**Module 4: Officers.** Current members of the Board of Directors, committee members (audit, election, mediation and conciliation, ethics), the General Manager or CEO, the Treasurer, and the Secretary. For each officer, the CAPR records whether they have completed their **mandatory training** within the first half of their term.[^17] Non-compliance with training requirements is a ground for COC non-issuance.

**Module 5: Transactions and Finances.** Deposit liabilities (for credit and multi-purpose cooperatives handling savings). Loans outstanding -- broken down by member loans and institutional borrowings. Taxes paid during the year. This module cross-references with your audited financial statements.

**Module 6: Other Information.** Type-specific data depending on your cooperative's classification. Community development activities conducted using your CDF. Awards or recognitions received. Socio-civic programs. This module captures what your cooperative is doing beyond its core business.

### 9.2.2 Step-by-Step Guide to Preparing Your CAPR

Do not wait until April to start preparing your CAPR. Start in January, immediately after your fiscal year closes. Here is the step-by-step process.

**Step 1: Assign a CAPR preparation team.** Designate the Treasurer, Secretary, and Bookkeeper (or Accountant) as the core team. The Audit Committee Chair should review the final submission before filing. Do not leave CAPR preparation to one person. It requires data from multiple sources.

**Step 2: Gather your raw data.**

| Data Needed | Source Document |
|---|---|
| Registration details | Certificate of Registration, Articles of Cooperation |
| Business activity data | Monthly/quarterly operations reports, sales records |
| Membership data | Membership register, admission and termination records |
| Officer data | Minutes of election GA, appointment records, training certificates |
| Financial data | General ledger, subsidiary ledgers, trial balance |
| Community activities | CDF utilization reports, project documentation |

**Step 3: Reconcile your membership register.** Before reporting membership numbers, reconcile your register. Cross-check admissions against application forms and board resolutions. Cross-check terminations against resignation letters or board termination resolutions. Count only members who are actually on your register as of December 31.

**Step 4: Verify officer training compliance.** For every director and committee member, confirm whether they completed the required training. Collect copies of training certificates from accredited providers. If any officer has not completed training, note this -- and arrange for training before filing the CAPR, if possible.

**Step 5: Complete each CAPR module.** Work through the six modules sequentially. Enter data directly into the CSEA's online reporting system. If you are a primary micro cooperative, use the prescribed Google Form on the CDA website.[^18] For all other cooperatives, use the **Cooperative Assessment Information System (CAIS)** at https://cs.cda.gov.ph/cais/ or the CSEA's successor reporting platform.[^19]

**Step 6: Attach required documents.** The CAPR is not complete without its attachments. You must include:

- **Audited Financial Statements** (eight components -- see Section 9.3)
- **List of Officers and Mandatory Training Undertaken/Completed**
- **Social Audit Report** with your program of activities for socio-civic goals
- **Performance Audit Report**
- **Semi-annual Report on Mediation and Conciliation**
- **Annual Tax Incentive Report (ATIR) and Annual Benefits Report (ABR)** -- only if your cooperative holds a Certificate of Tax Exemption[^20]
- **Sworn Statement** of officers affirming adherence to RA 9520 and applicable CDA/CSEA memorandum circulars[^21]

**Step 7: Validate and submit.** Review every field. Validate data entry for consistency -- membership numbers in Module 3 should match the totals implied by your financial statements. Submit electronically. Print the acknowledgment receipt and file it.

**Step 8: Submit hard copies.** Upload your Audited Financial Statements as a PDF through the reporting system. If the system requires hard copies, submit them to the nearest CSEA Extension Office. Keep your own copy with the acknowledgment stamp.

**Step 9: Pay the COC fee.** The fee is **PHP 100.00**.[^22] Pay it at the time of submission.

**Common CAPR mistakes to avoid:**

- **Leaving modules blank.** A blank module is treated as an incomplete submission. The CSEA will issue a Notice of Deficiency.
- **Mismatched membership numbers.** If your CAPR says 150 members but your financial statements show patronage refunds distributed to 180 members, the CSEA will flag the discrepancy.
- **Missing training certificates.** "Training in progress" is not the same as "training completed." If your directors have not finished their mandatory training, report it honestly. Falsifying training compliance is worse than reporting non-compliance.
- **Filing the CAPR without the CAFS.** Both must be submitted together. A CAPR without audited financial statements is incomplete.

## 9.3 The Cooperative Annual Financial Statement (CAFS)

Your CAFS is your cooperative's financial truth. It tells members, regulators, and creditors exactly where your money went during the year. The CAFS must be audited according to **generally accepted auditing standards for cooperatives** as jointly promulgated by the Philippine Institute of Certified Public Accountants (PICPA) and the CDA/CSEA.[^23]

### 9.3.1 Auditing Standards for Cooperatives

The type of audit your cooperative requires depends on your **total asset size**. The law sets a three-tier system.

| Asset Size | Audit Requirement | Who Conducts It |
|---|---|---|
| **Below PHP 3 million** | Internal audit acceptable | Audit Committee |
| **PHP 3 million to PHP 15 million** | CPA-signed financial statements | A Certified Public Accountant |
| **Above PHP 15 million** | External audit by CDA/CSEA-accredited auditor | An independent external auditor accredited by the CSEA |

**All cooperatives**, regardless of asset size, are subject to three types of audit every year:[^24]

1. **Financial audit.** Examines your books and financial statements for accuracy and conformity with generally accepted accounting practices. This is the audit that produces your CAFS.

2. **Performance audit.** Evaluates the efficiency and effectiveness of your cooperative's management, officers, and responsibility centers. It assesses whether your cooperative is achieving its operational objectives. It examines whether the required governance documents are maintained.[^25]

3. **Social audit.** Assesses your cooperative's social impact and ethical performance against its stated mission, vision, goals, and code of social responsibility. The social audit must be conducted by an independent social auditor accredited by the CSEA.[^26]

**Your CAFS must contain eight components.** These are not optional. A CAFS missing any component will trigger a Notice of Deficiency.

| No. | Component | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Statement of Management Responsibility** | Signed by the Chairperson and Treasurer. Affirms that management is responsible for the financial statements. |
| 2 | **Auditor's Report/Opinion** | The external auditor's professional opinion on whether the financial statements present a true and fair view. |
| 3 | **Statement of Representation** | Management represents that all information provided to the auditor is complete and accurate. |
| 4 | **Statement of Financial Condition** | Your cooperative's balance sheet -- assets, liabilities, and members' equity as of the fiscal year end. |
| 5 | **Statement of Operation** | Your income statement -- revenues, cost of services/goods, expenses, and net surplus (or deficit) for the year. |
| 6 | **Statement of Changes in Equity** | Tracks how members' equity moved during the year -- capital contributions, net surplus allocation, withdrawals. |
| 7 | **Statement of Cash Flows** | Shows cash inflows and outflows from operating, investing, and financing activities. |
| 8 | **Notes to Financial Statements** | Explains accounting policies, significant judgments, and additional disclosures required by auditing standards. |

**Special rule for unions of cooperatives.** If your cooperative is a union (not a primary or federation), replace the Statement of Operation with a **Statement of Receipts and Disbursements**, and replace the Statement of Changes in Equity with a **Statement of Changes in Fund Balance**.[^27]

**Special rule for newly registered cooperatives.** If your cooperative has been operating for less than one year from the date of registration as of December 31 of the preceding year, you submit a simplified CAFS: a **Statement of Financial Condition** and a **Statement of Operation**, signed by the Audit Committee and the Chairperson. No external audit is required for your first filing.[^28]

### 9.3.2 Working with Your External Auditor

If your cooperative's assets exceed PHP 15 million, you must engage an external auditor who is:

- **Independent** of your cooperative (no financial interest, no familial relationship with officers)[^29]
- **A member in good standing of PICPA**
- **Accredited by both the Board of Accountancy and the CSEA**[^30]

**Engage your auditor early.** Do not wait until March. Contact accredited auditors in December or January. In BARMM, CDA/CSEA-accredited auditors are limited in number. If you wait, you may not find one available before the April 30 deadline. Late engagement of an auditor is the single most common reason cooperatives file late.

**Prepare for the audit.** Before the auditor arrives, your bookkeeper or accountant should:

- Complete all journal entries for the year
- Prepare a trial balance
- Reconcile bank statements with your general ledger
- Prepare subsidiary schedules for loans receivable, savings deposits, and other major accounts
- Organize supporting documents (official receipts, vouchers, contracts, board resolutions authorizing major transactions)

**Review the audit report before submission.** The auditor submits the report to the Board of Directors and the Audit Committee first.[^31] The Board then presents the complete audit report to the General Assembly. Do not submit the CAFS to the CSEA until the General Assembly has received the report. If your GA is scheduled after the April 30 deadline, coordinate with the CSEA Extension Office on the proper sequencing.

**Keep the auditor's management letter.** Every external audit produces a management letter -- a list of findings, weaknesses, and recommendations the auditor identified during the audit. This letter is not submitted to the CSEA, but your board must act on it. Recurring management letter findings that remain unaddressed year after year will eventually appear in your performance audit and affect your COC issuance.

## 9.4 The Certificate of Compliance (COC)

The **Certificate of Compliance (COC)** is the CSEA's official certification that your cooperative has met all its statutory obligations for the year.[^32] Think of it as your cooperative's annual health certificate. Without it, you are operating in a regulatory gray zone.

### 9.4.1 Requirements for Issuance

To receive a COC, your cooperative must submit all of the following:[^33]

| No. | Requirement | Details |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **CAPR with all attachments** | Complete, accurate, and submitted electronically |
| 2 | **Audited Financial Statements** | Eight components, audited per asset-tier requirements |
| 3 | **ATIR and ABR** | Required only if your cooperative holds a Certificate of Tax Exemption |
| 4 | **Sworn Statement** | Officers affirm adherence to RA 9520 and applicable CDA/CSEA memorandum circulars |
| 5 | **COC fee of PHP 100.00** | Paid at the time of submission |
| 6 | **List of Officers and Mandatory Training** | Must show training completion status for all directors and committee members |
| 7 | **Social Audit Report** | With program of activities for socio-civic goals |
| 8 | **Performance Audit Report** | Governance and management audit |
| 9 | **Semi-annual Report on Mediation and Conciliation** | Covers dispute resolution activities |

**For newly registered cooperatives,** the initial COC is **automatically issued together with the Certificate of Registration**. It is valid until April 30 of the immediately succeeding year. For the following year, you submit a simplified package: CAPR with Financial Status Reports (Statement of Financial Condition and Statement of Operation), the Sworn Statement, and the PHP 100 fee.[^34]

**COC validity.** Your COC is valid **until April 30 of the succeeding year**, unless earlier suspended or revoked for cause.[^35] Starting in 2026, cooperatives will receive an email notification that their COC is available for printing as an **e-certificate of compliance**.[^36]

**What the COC unlocks.** Your COC is required for:

- Securing a **Certificate of Tax Exemption (CTE)** from the BIR
- Availing of incentives and privileges under Articles 60 and 61 of RA 9520[^37]
- Accessing **local tax exemption privileges** under the Local Government Code[^38]
- Accreditation as a **CDA/CSEA training provider**
- Proof of good standing in **bank loan applications**
- Participation in **government programs and partnerships**
- Any other legitimate purpose requiring proof of regulatory compliance

### 9.4.2 COC Submission Package

Submit these **six documents together** as a single package. A missing document makes the entire submission incomplete.[^68]

| Document | Prepared By | Regulatory Basis |
|---|---|---|
| **CAPR** (Cooperative Annual Progress Report) | Staff | MC 2025-02 |
| **Audited Financial Statements** | External CPA + Staff | RA 9520, Art. 80 |
| **Performance Audit Report (PAR)** | Staff + Officers | MC 2021-04 |
| **List of Officers and Training Completed** | Secretary/Staff | MC 2025-09 |
| **Social Audit Report** | Officers/Staff | MC 2025-02 |
| **Sworn Statement of Officers** | Board | MC 2025-02 |

**Assign a document owner for each item** before January. Do not compile at the last minute.

### 9.4.3 PAR Scoring Framework

Your **Performance Audit Report (PAR)** uses a two-component scoring system under MC 2021-04.[^69] The combined score determines whether your cooperative qualifies for COC issuance.

| Component | Weight | What It Measures |
|---|---|---|
| **GMR** (Governance Management Review) | **40%** of total PAR score | Presence and quality of required governance documents, board processes, and management systems |
| **STEPS** (Financial Performance) | **60%** of total PAR score | Five financial ratios: **S**tability, **T**urn-Over, **E**fficiency, **P**rofitability, **S**tructure of Assets |

**A low PAR score does not just affect your report — it blocks your COC.** Strengthen governance records and financial ratios throughout the year, not only at audit time.[^70]

### 9.4.4 What Happens If You Lose Your COC

Losing your COC means more than losing a piece of paper. It triggers a cascade of consequences for both the cooperative and its officers.

**Thirteen grounds for non-issuance.** The CSEA will refuse to issue your COC for any of the following reasons:[^39]

1. Non-submission of required reports
2. Significant discrepancies between online-encoded reports and uploaded PDF copies of your AFS
3. Non-payment of monetary penalties for late or non-submission (except micro cooperatives)
4. Non-settlement of monetary and non-monetary obligations to the CSEA (e.g., unliquidated grants or loans)
5. Willful failure to comply with mandatory officer training requirements
6. Two or more sets of Board of Directors claiming to be the legitimate officers (subject of a verified complaint)
7. The cooperative has been issued a Show Cause Order (SCO)
8. The cooperative is undergoing dissolution proceedings
9. The cooperative has been issued a Cease-and-Desist Order (CDO)
10. A previous COC was suspended or revoked and the grounds have not been addressed
11. Non-compliance with an order or directive to comply with examination or investigation findings
12. Failure to comply with statutory fund guidelines (Reserve Fund, CETF, CDF) as identified in an inspection
13. Officers found to have violated conflict-of-interest provisions under Articles 45, 48, and 49 of RA 9520 or CDA MC No. 2024-16

**Eight grounds for suspension.** Even after your COC is issued, it can be suspended if:[^40]

1. Two or more sets of BOD claim legitimacy (verified complaint filed with the CSEA and a court)
2. The cooperative is issued a Show Cause Order
3. The cooperative is issued an Order to Comply based on examination findings and fails to comply
4. The cooperative is issued a Cease-and-Desist Order
5. Non-compliance with an undertaking to settle obligations
6. Failure to address material findings in your AFS within the prescribed period
7. Failure to comply with CSEA issuances
8. Officers found to have violated conflict-of-interest provisions

**Automatic revocation.** Your COC is automatically revoked if the cooperative is issued an Order of Dissolution, its Certificate of Registration is revoked or cancelled, or a competent court orders its dissolution.[^41]

**Consequences for the cooperative:**

- No certification of any nature will be issued endorsing your cooperative for privileges, benefits, or programs from the CSEA or any government agency[^42]
- Your tax exemption privileges and CTE renewal will be **adversely affected**

**Consequences for accountable officers:**[^43]

| Consequence | Details |
|---|---|
| **Barred from continuing** as officers of any primary cooperative | Immediate effect |
| **Barred from continuing** as officers of any secondary or tertiary cooperative | Immediate effect |
| **Disqualified from election or appointment** in the cooperative | For the next **two consecutive years** |
| **Barred from continuing** as officers of Cooperative Development Councils, regional cooperative organizations, and other cooperative bodies | Immediate effect |
| **Personal liability** for PHP 100/day penalty | For non-issuance caused by non-submission |
| **Personal liability** for tax and other penalties | Imposed by BIR and other agencies due to loss of tax-exempt status |

**The reinstatement path.** If your COC was suspended, you have **10 calendar days** from receipt of the Order of Suspension to submit your response with proof of compliance. If the CSEA finds your response satisfactory, it issues an Order Lifting the Suspension. If not, it issues an Order of Revocation.[^44]

**Bangsamoro reality check.** In many BARMM communities, cooperative officers are volunteers -- farmers, fishers, teachers -- who juggle cooperative duties with their livelihoods. The compliance burden can feel overwhelming. But the alternative is worse. A cooperative without a COC is invisible to every government program designed to help cooperatives. The BARMM Government's livelihood programs, the MAFAR agricultural inputs, the MTIT market linkages, the MSSD social protection -- all of these prioritize compliant cooperatives. Non-compliance does not just risk penalties. It locks your members out of opportunities.

## 9.5 Statutory Fund Compliance

Every cooperative that earns a net surplus must allocate that surplus according to a specific order prescribed by law. You cannot distribute everything to members. The law requires you to set aside portions for three **mandatory funds** and permits one **optional fund** before any distribution to members.[^45]

### 9.5.1 Minimum Allocations to Reserve, Education, Community Development, and Optional Funds

Study this table. It governs every peso of your net surplus.

| Fund | Minimum Allocation | First 5 Years Rule | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reserve Fund** | At least **10%** of net surplus | At least **50%** of net surplus during the first 5 years after registration | Stability of the cooperative; cover net losses in operations |
| **Education and Training Fund (ETF/CETF)** | Up to **10%** of net surplus | Same rate applies | Training, development, and cooperative education activities |
| **Community Development Fund (CDF)** | At least **3%** of net surplus | Same rate applies | Projects and activities benefiting the community where the cooperative operates |
| **Optional Fund** (land and building, or any other necessary fund) | Up to **7%** of net surplus | Same rate applies | As defined by the bylaws |
| **Remaining net surplus** | After all fund allocations | After all fund allocations | Interest on share capital (not exceeding normal rate of return) and patronage refunds to members |

**The first-five-years trap.** During your cooperative's first five years after registration, the Reserve Fund must receive **not less than 50 percent** of net surplus.[^46] This is not optional. Many new cooperatives in BARMM distribute surplus to members prematurely, violating this rule. If the CSEA inspects your fund allocations and finds that you allocated only 10% to the Reserve Fund in year three, you have a compliance violation.

**Reserve Fund rules in detail:**[^47]

- **Allowed use:** Cover operating losses. Provide for stability.
- **Prohibited use:** Distribution to members. Ever. Even upon dissolution, the Reserve Fund does not go to members.[^48]
- **Investment restriction:** Cannot be invested except as allowed by the Code. Excess over share capital may be used for expansion projects -- but only upon General Assembly resolution.
- **Upon dissolution:** The GA may resolve to establish a usufructuary trust fund for the benefit of a federation or union, or to donate the amount to the community. If members cannot decide, the Reserve Fund goes to the federation or union the cooperative is affiliated with.

**Education and Training Fund (ETF/CETF) rules:**[^49]

- **50/50 split.** Half of the ETF must be spent by the cooperative itself on education and training. The other half may be remitted to a federation or union chosen by the cooperative.
- **The federation accountability.** Any federation or union receiving CETF contributions must submit to the CSEA and to contributing cooperatives: (a) a list of cooperatives that remitted CETF, (b) business consultancy assistance provided with nature and cost, and (c) other training activities with participants and costs.
- **Upon dissolution.** The unexpended balance goes to the CETF of the chosen federation or union.

**Community Development Fund (CDF) rules:**[^50]

- **Allowed use:** Community projects, social responsibility programs, local development initiatives.
- **Prohibited use:** Member-exclusive benefits. Political activities. The CDF is for the **community**, not for the membership roster.
- **Bangsamoro application.** A consumer cooperative in Marawi allocates 5% of net surplus to its CDF. It uses the fund to sponsor a community clean-up drive, donate school supplies to a public elementary school, and support a barangay health outreach. None of these activities require the beneficiaries to be cooperative members.

**Optional Fund rules:**[^51]

- The total of all optional funds (land and building, technology, calamity, or any other fund your bylaws create) shall not exceed **7 percent** of net surplus.
- The specific optional funds your cooperative creates must be defined in your bylaws and approved by the General Assembly.

### 9.5.2 Documentation and Reporting Requirements

The CSEA does not take your word for it. Inspectors verify your fund allocations annually. Here is what you must document and how.

**For the Reserve Fund:**

- Board resolution approving the net surplus allocation (specifying the exact peso amount and percentage allocated to each fund)
- General Assembly resolution approving the distribution of net surplus
- General ledger entries showing the allocation posted to the Reserve Fund account
- If any Reserve Fund amount was used to cover losses: GA resolution authorizing the charge, with the specific loss amount identified

**For the ETF/CETF:**

- Board resolution or GA resolution approving the ETF allocation
- Records of how the cooperative's 50% share was spent: training attendance records, training certificates, invoices from accredited providers, photos and documentation of educational activities
- Remittance records for the 50% share sent to the federation/union: official receipts, bank transfer confirmations
- The federation/union's accountability report showing how contributed CETF funds were used

**For the CDF:**

- Board resolution or GA resolution approving the CDF allocation and the specific community projects to be funded
- Project documentation: activity reports, photos, beneficiary lists, receipts, and community acknowledgments
- A clear paper trail showing that CDF funds went to **community** projects, not member-exclusive benefits

**For Optional Funds:**

- Bylaws provision creating the optional fund
- GA resolution approving the allocation
- Utilization records consistent with the fund's stated purpose

**The audit trail matters.** During annual audits, the external auditor (or audit committee, for smaller cooperatives) verifies that statutory fund allocations match the GA-approved distribution. During CSEA inspections, field officers check the same documents. Any mismatch -- such as a GA resolution allocating 10% to the Reserve Fund but the ledger showing only 7% posted -- will result in a finding and potential COC non-issuance.[^52]

## 9.6 Tax Treatment and Exemptions

One of the most powerful benefits of being a registered cooperative is **tax exemption**. But this benefit is not automatic, not permanent, and not unconditional. Your cooperative must earn and maintain its tax-exempt status through continuous compliance. Lose your COC, and you lose the gateway to tax exemption.

### 9.6.1 Tax Exemptions Under RA 9520 and BTA Bill 210

The tax treatment of cooperatives depends on two factors: **(1) whether your cooperative transacts with non-members**, and **(2) the size of your accumulated reserves and undivided net savings.**

**Category 1: Cooperatives that transact only with members (no non-member business).**[^53]

These cooperatives enjoy the broadest protection. Under both RA 9520 and BTA Bill 210, a duly registered cooperative that does not transact any business with non-members or the general public **shall not be subject to any taxes and fees** imposed under internal revenue laws and other tax laws. Full exemption. No conditions on reserve size.

**Category 2: Cooperatives that transact with both members and non-members.**[^54]

These cooperatives receive partial protection. Transactions with members remain tax-free -- including final taxes on members' deposits and documentary tax. For non-member transactions, the exemption depends on your reserve and net savings size.

| Reserve/Net Savings Level | Tax Treatment |
|---|---|
| **PHP 10 million or below** | Exempt from **all** national, city, provincial, municipal, and barangay taxes of whatever name and nature. Also exempt from customs duties on imported equipment not locally available (as certified by DTI or MTIT). |
| **Above PHP 10 million** | Must pay **income tax** on the amount allocated for interest on capitals. Must pay **VAT** on transactions with non-members (subject to exceptions). Must pay all other applicable taxes. |

**VAT exemption for cooperatives.** Even cooperatives with reserves above PHP 10 million may be exempt from VAT on non-member transactions if they are duly registered with the CSEA and at least 25% of net income is returned to members as interest and/or patronage refunds.[^55] The exemption covers cooperatives organized to produce, process, and market raw materials or finished products from members' production.

**Universal local tax exemption.** All cooperatives, regardless of reserve size, are exempt from **local taxes** and taxes on transactions with banks and insurance companies.[^56] This is a powerful provision. It means your cooperative does not pay business permits fees, mayor's permits, or other local government charges. However, sales or services to non-members may still be subject to applicable percentage taxes (except for producers, marketing, and service cooperatives).

**BTA Bill 210 enhancements.** The proposed Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code mirrors RA 9520's tax provisions but adds a Bangsamoro-specific dimension. It references both the national Internal Revenue Code and the **BARMM Revenue Code** when defining VAT obligations, signaling that cooperatives in the Bangsamoro will interface with both national and regional revenue frameworks.[^57]

**Additional exemptions and privileges:**[^58]

- **Free notarial services** in areas without available notaries public (for cooperative documents up to PHP 500,000)
- **Free registration of deeds** for cooperative loan instruments up to PHP 250,000
- **Exempt from court and sheriff's fees** for actions brought under the Cooperative Code
- **Exempt from appeal bonds** (CSEA certification of net assets accepted in lieu of bond)
- **Securities exemption** -- cooperative shares are exempt from the Securities Act (if non-speculative)

### 9.6.2 When Cooperatives Lose Tax-Exempt Status

Tax exemption is a privilege that you must actively maintain. Here are the ways cooperatives lose it.

**Loss of COC.** Without a current COC, you cannot secure or renew a Certificate of Tax Exemption. The CTE expires, and your cooperative becomes fully taxable on all transactions.[^59]

**Excessive non-member transactions.** If your cooperative's non-member transactions grow disproportionately large relative to member transactions, you risk being treated as a regular business entity for tax purposes. The BIR examines the ratio of member to non-member business. No hard statutory threshold exists, but a cooperative whose non-member revenue dwarfs its member revenue invites scrutiny.

**Tax-free imports sold within five years.** If your cooperative imported machinery or equipment duty-free and sells or transfers it within five years, both the cooperative and the transferee become **solidarily liable** to pay **twice the amount** of the original tax and duties.[^60] This is not a fine. It is double the original tax bill, paid jointly.

**Failure to return 25% of net income.** The VAT exemption for cooperatives with reserves above PHP 10 million requires that at least 25% of net income be returned to members as interest and/or patronage refunds.[^61] If your cooperative retains more than 75% of net income without distributing it, the VAT exemption may be challenged.

**False claims of tax exemption.** Any person who willfully attempts to evade or defeat tax by misusing the cooperative tax exemption provisions faces imprisonment of 2-4 years and a fine of PHP 30,000 to PHP 100,000.[^62] Government officials who deprive cooperatives of their legitimate tax exemptions face the same penalty range, plus disqualification from public office.[^63]

### 9.6.3 Filing Requirements with BIR and BARMM Revenue Authorities

Even if your cooperative is fully tax-exempt, you still have filing obligations. Tax exemption does not mean tax invisibility.

**Certificate of Tax Exemption (CTE).** You must apply for and secure a CTE from the BIR. The CTE is valid for **five years** from the date of issue.[^64] To apply, you need:

- Your current Certificate of Registration from the CSEA
- Your current Certificate of Compliance
- Your latest Audited Financial Statements
- Your Articles of Cooperation and Bylaws
- Board resolution authorizing the application

**Annual Tax Incentive Report (ATIR) and Annual Benefits Report (ABR).** If your cooperative holds a CTE, you must file these reports annually as part of your CAPR submission. The ATIR documents the tax incentives your cooperative claimed during the year. The ABR reports the benefits your cooperative provided to members and the community. Failure to file these reports is a ground for COC non-issuance.[^65]

**BIR registration and reporting.** Even tax-exempt cooperatives must:

- Register with the BIR and secure a Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN)
- File annual information returns
- Issue BIR-registered official receipts and invoices for transactions with non-members
- Withhold taxes on employee compensation and remit them to the BIR (your tax exemption does not cover employee withholding taxes)
- Allow BIR examination of books upon prior authorization by the CSEA[^66]

**BARMM Revenue Code considerations.** BTA Bill 210 explicitly references the BARMM Revenue Code alongside the national Internal Revenue Code when defining cooperative tax obligations.[^67] As the Bangsamoro revenue system matures, your cooperative will need to monitor both national BIR requirements and any regional revenue issuances from BARMM fiscal authorities. Keep in contact with your CSEA Extension Office for updates on regional tax compliance requirements.

**Practical guidance for BARMM cooperatives:**

1. **Apply for your CTE immediately after registration.** Do not wait. The CTE clock starts from the date of issue, not from registration.
2. **File your ATIR and ABR every year.** Even if you claimed no tax incentives, file a nil report. Non-filing is a ground for COC non-issuance.
3. **Keep your BIR registration current.** Update your registered address and authorized representatives whenever they change.
4. **Maintain a separate file for tax documents.** Official receipts issued, withholding tax remittances, BIR correspondence, CTE copy, annual returns -- all in one folder, organized by year.
5. **Consult a CPA for non-member transactions.** If your cooperative regularly transacts with non-members, engage a CPA to determine your exact tax obligations. The line between taxable and exempt transactions can be complicated, especially for multi-purpose cooperatives.

## Chapter Summary

Compliance is not a one-time event. It is a year-round discipline that protects your members, your officers, and your cooperative's legal standing. Here is what you must remember:

**File by April 30.** Your CAPR and CAFS are due within 120 days after the close of the calendar year. Late filing costs PHP 100 per day, personally charged to accountable officers.

**Prepare all eight CAFS components.** A financial statement missing the Statement of Management Responsibility or the Notes to Financial Statements is incomplete. The CSEA will issue a deficiency notice.

**Allocate your net surplus correctly.** Reserve Fund (10% minimum; 50% in the first five years), ETF (up to 10%), CDF (at least 3%), Optional Fund (up to 7%). Document every allocation with board and GA resolutions.

**Secure and maintain your COC.** It is valid until April 30 of the succeeding year. Without it, you cannot access tax exemptions, government programs, or bank financing.

**Protect your tax exemption.** Apply for a CTE, file your ATIR and ABR annually, and monitor both BIR and BARMM revenue requirements. Tax exemption is a privilege you must earn every year.

**Guard your officers.** Non-compliance penalties fall on officers personally -- fines, disqualification, and liability for back taxes. The best way to protect the people who volunteer their time to lead your cooperative is to keep the cooperative compliant.

The 88.34 percent of BARMM cooperatives that are non-compliant did not plan to be non-compliant. They just let one deadline slip, then another, until the gap became too wide to close. Do not join them. Start your compliance calendar in January. File early. File completely. And keep your cooperative's vital signs strong.
