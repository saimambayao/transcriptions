# Chapter 6 — Membership: Rights, Obligations, and Management

Your cooperative exists because of its members. Every right they hold and every obligation they fulfill shapes whether your cooperative thrives or stagnates. A cooperative with 200 members on paper but only 30 who patronize its services is weaker than one with 50 active, contributing members. Membership is not a formality. It is the engine of your cooperative's survival.

This chapter walks you through the full lifecycle of membership -- from the two kinds of members the law recognizes, through the application and admission process, to the rights every member holds and the obligations every member must fulfill. It ends where no one wants to begin: how membership terminates and what happens to a departing member's capital.

Get membership right, and you build a cooperative that grows stronger every year. Get it wrong, and you accumulate dead weight -- names on a roster who contribute nothing and drain resources your active members need.

---

## 6.1 Kinds of Membership

### 6.1.1 Regular Members vs. Associate Members

The law recognizes exactly two kinds of cooperative members: **regular members** and **associate members**.[^1] Your cooperative may choose to admit both, or only regular members. The distinction matters because it determines who votes, who runs for office, and who shapes the cooperative's direction.

**A regular member** has complied with all membership requirements and is entitled to all rights and privileges of membership.[^2] That means full voting rights, the right to be elected as a director or officer, the right to inspect records, and the right to share in the cooperative's net surplus. A regular member is a full participant in the cooperative's democratic life.

**An associate member** has limited standing. An associate member cannot vote and cannot be elected to office.[^3] Associate members receive only the rights and privileges that the cooperative's bylaws specifically grant them. Think of associate membership as a probationary status -- a pathway for people who want to join but have not yet met all regular membership requirements.

Here is the critical transition rule: **an associate member who meets the minimum requirements of regular membership, patronizes the cooperative for two consecutive years, and signifies intention to remain a member shall be considered a regular member.**[^4] This is not optional. The law mandates the upgrade once all three conditions are met.

| Feature | Regular Member | Associate Member |
|---|---|---|
| **Voting rights** | Full (one member, one vote) | None |
| **Right to be elected** | Yes (if qualified) | No |
| **Right to inspect records** | Yes | Yes (per bylaws) |
| **Right to patronize services** | Yes | Yes (per bylaws) |
| **Right to patronage refund** | Yes (proportional to patronage) | Per bylaws only |
| **Capital contribution required** | Full minimum subscription | As bylaws provide |
| **Pathway to upgrade** | Already regular | Automatic after 2 years + patronage + intent |
| **Duration** | Unlimited (until termination) | Limited (as bylaws provide) |

**Bangsamoro application.** A seaweed marketing cooperative in Bongao, Tawi-Tawi admits 25 regular members at formation. A year later, three recent graduates want to join but cannot yet afford the full share capital subscription. The cooperative admits them as associate members with a reduced initial subscription. They patronize the cooperative's consolidation and marketing services for two years. They pay their shares incrementally. At the end of the two-year period, they notify the board of their intention to remain. The board recognizes them as regular members. No general assembly vote is needed -- the law does it automatically.

**A common mistake to avoid.** Some cooperatives treat associate membership as a permanent second class. They never inform associate members about the upgrade path. They never track the two-year patronage requirement. This violates the spirit of the law and weakens your cooperative. Associate members who feel permanently excluded will stop patronizing your services. Track every associate member's admission date, patronage record, and capital payments. When the two-year mark approaches, notify them.

### 6.1.2 Membership Limitations (Age, Residence, Common Bond)

Not everyone can join your cooperative. The law imposes specific limitations that your board must enforce during every admission decision.

**Age requirement.** Members must be of legal age -- **18 years old** -- to join a regular cooperative.[^5] Minors may form **laboratory cooperatives**, but these must be affiliated with a registered cooperative and governed by special guidelines from the Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA).[^6]

**Citizenship.** Members of a primary cooperative must be **Filipino citizens**.[^7] This includes naturalized citizens. Foreign nationals residing in the Bangsamoro cannot be admitted as members of a primary cooperative.

**Common bond of interest.** Every cooperative is organized around a common bond -- geographic, occupational, institutional, or associational. Your bylaws define this bond. Every applicant must fall within it. A credit cooperative organized by BARMM ministry employees cannot admit a private businessperson who has no institutional connection to the ministry. A fishing cooperative in Lamitan cannot admit a farmer from Cotabato City who never fishes.

**The "only twice" rule under BTA Bill No. 210.** The proposed Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code introduces a significant limitation: **any person shall be eligible for membership in a cooperative in the region only twice.**[^8] An employee working in a government or private institution may join an employee's cooperative and a community-based cooperative simultaneously. But the ceiling is two. If this provision becomes law, your board must verify whether an applicant already holds membership in two other BARMM cooperatives before admitting them.

**Government officers and employees.** The law contains specific rules for government personnel:[^9]

| Category | Rule |
|---|---|
| **CSEA officers and employees** | Disqualified from elected or appointed positions in any cooperative (except a cooperative organized by CSEA employees themselves) |
| **All elective government officials** | Ineligible to become officers and directors of cooperatives |
| **Government employees as members** | May use official time for cooperative meetings, seminars, and training -- if the head of office approves and operations are not adversely affected |

**Practical tip.** If you are forming a cooperative among barangay officials or LGU employees in BARMM, be careful about who fills leadership roles. The barangay captain can be a member but cannot serve as a director or officer. The municipal councilor can attend meetings on official time but cannot chair the board. Build your leadership slate from non-elected members to avoid legal problems.

## 6.2 Becoming a Member

### 6.2.1 The Application Process

Membership does not happen by walking through the door. The law prescribes a formal process, and your bylaws should make it specific, transparent, and fair.

**Step 1: File a written application.** The applicant submits a duly accomplished membership application form to the board of directors.[^10] The form should collect personal data, confirm the applicant's common bond qualification, and include an undertaking to uphold the cooperative's bylaws, policies, and rules.[^11] Require the applicant to disclose any existing cooperative memberships -- this matters for the "only twice" limitation.

**Step 2: Complete the Pre-Membership Education Seminar (PMES).** Before admission, the applicant must complete the prescribed PMES.[^12] This seminar covers cooperative principles, the applicant's rights and obligations, and the cooperative's basic operations. Do not skip this requirement. A member who joins without understanding what a cooperative is will become a problem member.

**Step 3: Board review and decision.** The board of directors acts on the application within the period specified in your bylaws.[^13] The board verifies the applicant's qualifications -- age, citizenship, common bond, and share capital capacity. If the application is approved, the applicant becomes a member upon making the required payments.

**Step 4: Payment of membership fee and share capital.** The applicant pays the non-refundable membership fee and subscribes to the minimum number of shares required by the bylaws.[^14] The applicant must pay the value of the minimum shares upon approval. Only after this payment does the member exercise full membership rights.

**When the board denies an application.** The law protects applicants from arbitrary rejection. If the board refuses membership, the applicant may appeal to the general assembly, whose decision is final.[^15] The general assembly may create an **Appeal and Grievance Committee** to handle such appeals. The committee must decide within **30 days** of receiving the appeal. If the committee fails to decide within 30 days, the appeal is deemed approved in favor of the applicant.[^16]

This is a powerful safeguard. It means your board cannot quietly deny applications without consequences. If you reject an applicant, document the specific grounds. Common valid grounds for denial include:

- The applicant does not meet the common bond requirement
- The applicant is below legal age
- The applicant is not a Filipino citizen
- The applicant already holds membership in two BARMM cooperatives (under PB-210)
- The applicant has been previously expelled from the cooperative for cause

**Grounds that are never valid for denial:** gender, ethnicity, tribe, religious affiliation, political belief, or social status.[^17] Cooperatives are built on the principle of voluntary and open membership. Discrimination violates this principle and exposes your cooperative to legal action.

#### Sample Application Flow

```
Applicant submits form + membership fee
            |
            v
   Board verifies qualifications
   (common bond, age, citizenship)
            |
     +------+------+
     |             |
  APPROVED      DENIED

```

### 6.2.2 Share Capital Subscription and Payment

**Share capital is the price of membership.** Every member must subscribe to shares and pay the required minimum upon admission. This is not a donation. It is an investment that the member can recover upon leaving the cooperative, subject to the rules on refund.

Your bylaws set two numbers that every applicant needs to know:

1. **Minimum number of shares to subscribe.** This is the total number of shares the member commits to purchasing. The bylaws might require a regular member to subscribe to 10 shares at PHP 100 par value each (PHP 1,000 total subscription).

2. **Minimum number of shares to pay upon admission.** This is the amount the member pays upfront. The bylaws might require payment of at least 5 shares (PHP 500) upon approval, with the remaining 5 shares to be paid within a specified period.

**The 10% cap on individual share holdings.** No member of a primary cooperative may own or hold more than **10% of the total subscribed share capital** of the cooperative.[^18] This prevents any single member from accumulating disproportionate economic power. If your cooperative has a total subscribed share capital of PHP 500,000, no member can hold more than PHP 50,000 in shares.

**Par value of shares.** Under RA 9520, the par value of each share in a primary cooperative may be fixed at any figure **not exceeding PHP 1,000**.[^19] Your cooperative decides the par value through its articles of cooperation. A seaweed cooperative targeting low-income fisherfolk might set par value at PHP 100 per share. A credit cooperative serving government employees might set it at PHP 500 per share. Match the par value to your members' economic capacity.

| Share Capital Element | What Your Bylaws Must Specify | Example (Agriculture Cooperative) |
|---|---|---|
| **Par value per share** | PHP amount (max PHP 1,000) | PHP 100 per share |
| **Minimum subscription (regular)** | Number of shares | 10 shares (PHP 1,000 total) |
| **Minimum paid-up (regular)** | Number of shares or percentage | 5 shares (PHP 500) upon admission |
| **Minimum subscription (associate)** | Number of shares (if applicable) | 5 shares (PHP 500 total) |
| **Minimum paid-up (associate)** | Number of shares or percentage | 3 shares (PHP 300) upon admission |
| **Payment schedule for balance** | Timeline and installment amounts | Balance paid within 12 months |

**Bangsamoro example.** The Bangsamoro Farmers Multi-Purpose Cooperative in Datu Odin Sinsuat sets its par value at PHP 100 per share. Regular members subscribe to a minimum of 15 shares (PHP 1,500) and pay 10 shares (PHP 1,000) upon admission. The remaining 5 shares must be paid within one year through monthly installments of PHP 42. The cooperative deducts the installment from members' patronage refunds when available. This approach makes membership accessible to smallholder farmers while building the cooperative's capital base.

**What happens if a member falls behind on payments?** Your bylaws should address this directly. Common approaches include:

- **Grace period.** Allow 30-60 days past the due date before imposing consequences.
- **Fines.** RA 9520 permits cooperatives to prescribe fines on unpaid subscribed share capital, provided the fines are fair and reasonable.[^20]
- **Suspension of voting rights.** A member delinquent on share capital payments may lose voting privileges until the account is current.[^21]
- **Termination.** Persistent non-payment constitutes failure to comply with obligations, which is a ground for involuntary termination.[^22]

## 6.3 Rights of Members

Membership comes with legally protected rights. These rights are not favors from the board -- they are entitlements that the law guarantees. Your board's job is to protect and facilitate them, not to gatekeep them.

### 6.3.1 Voting Rights: One Member, One Vote

**The one-member, one-vote principle is the democratic foundation of every cooperative.**[^23] In a primary cooperative, every regular member has exactly one vote -- regardless of how many shares that member holds. A member who owns PHP 50,000 in shares has the same vote as a member who owns PHP 1,000. This is what separates a cooperative from a corporation, where voting power follows share ownership.

This principle applies to:

- **General assembly meetings** -- electing directors, approving budgets, amending bylaws, distributing net surplus
- **Special resolutions** -- mergers, consolidation, dissolution, delegation of powers

**Who can vote.** Not every member automatically has voting rights. Your bylaws should specify the conditions for becoming a **member entitled to vote**. The CDA Model Bylaws require all of the following:[^24]

1. Paid the membership fee and the value of the minimum shares required
2. Not delinquent in share capital subscriptions and other obligations
3. Completed the continuing education program prescribed by the board
4. Participated in cooperative affairs and patronized its services
5. Any additional conditions your bylaws specify

**Suspension of voting rights is not expulsion.** A member who temporarily fails one of these conditions -- say, falling behind on a capital payment -- loses voting rights until the condition is met. But the member remains a member. The board should notify the member of the suspension and specify what needs to happen to restore voting privileges.

**No proxy voting in primary cooperatives.** Members of primary cooperatives must vote in person.[^25] You cannot send someone to vote on your behalf. Proxy voting is allowed only in secondary and tertiary cooperatives, and only if the bylaws provide for it.

**Voting thresholds you need to know:**

| Decision | Required Vote |
|---|---|
| **Regular business** (approving reports, routine resolutions) | Majority of members present, quorum existing |
| **Election of directors and officers** | Majority of members present, quorum existing |
| **Amendments to Articles of Cooperation or Bylaws** | Two-thirds (2/3) of all members with voting rights |
| **Merger or consolidation** | Three-fourths (3/4) of all members with voting rights, present and constituting a quorum |
| **Dissolution** | Three-fourths (3/4) of all members |
| **Delegation of GA powers to smaller body** | Three-fourths (3/4) of all members with voting rights, present and constituting a quorum |

**Quorum.** Your general assembly needs at least **25% of all members entitled to vote** to constitute a quorum and transact business.[^26] If your cooperative has 100 voting members, at least 25 must be present. Without quorum, no valid decisions can be made.

**Bangsamoro application.** A multipurpose cooperative in Marawi City has 120 regular members. At the annual general assembly, only 28 members attend. That is 23% -- below the 25% quorum threshold. The assembly cannot proceed. The board must adjourn and reschedule, sending proper notice to all members. This is why member attendance matters. It is not just a duty -- it is a survival requirement for your cooperative's governance.

### 6.3.2 Right to Information and Inspection of Records

**Transparency is not optional in a cooperative.** Every member -- regular and associate -- has the right to inspect and examine the cooperative's records during reasonable office hours.[^27] This includes:

- Books of accounts
- Audited financial statements
- Minutes of general assembly, board, and committee meetings
- Share register (list of all members and their shares)
- Articles of cooperation and bylaws
- Any other documents prescribed by law or bylaws

Members also have the right to **secure copies** of cooperative records pertaining to their own account information.[^28] If a member wants to see her share capital balance, her patronage record, or her loan account status, the cooperative must provide it.

**What the cooperative must keep accessible.** RA 9520 requires every cooperative to maintain the following documents at its official address, ready for inspection by members and CSEA representatives:[^29]

1. A copy of the Cooperative Code and all related laws
2. A copy of CSEA regulations
3. The cooperative's articles of cooperation and bylaws
4. A register of members
5. Minutes of meetings (GA, board, committees)
6. Share books (where applicable)
7. Financial statements
8. Other documents prescribed by law or bylaws

**What your board cannot do.** Your board cannot deny a member access to records by claiming the information is "confidential" or "not yet ready." The law does not create exceptions for inconvenience. If a member requests to inspect the financial statements and your treasurer has not prepared them, the problem is your treasurer's, not the member's.

**Practical safeguard.** Designate a records officer -- this could be the secretary or a staff member -- who is responsible for producing documents upon request. Set a reasonable process: members submit a written request, the records officer makes the documents available within 3-5 business days, and inspection happens at the cooperative's office during business hours. This protects both the member's right and the cooperative's operational needs.

### 6.3.3 Right to Patronage Refunds and Interest on Share Capital

**Patronage refunds are the cooperative advantage.** When your cooperative earns a net surplus at the end of the fiscal year, a portion of that surplus goes back to members in proportion to their patronage.[^30] This is fundamentally different from a corporation, where profits go to shareholders based on how many shares they own. In a cooperative, the member who uses the cooperative's services the most receives the largest refund.

Here is how the law distributes the net surplus:[^31]

| Allocation | Minimum/Maximum | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Reserve fund** | At least 10% (50% in first 5 years) | Stability; covers net losses |
| **Education and training fund** | Up to 10% | Member and staff development |
| **Community development fund** | At least 3% | Community projects in area of operation |
| **Optional fund** | Up to 7% total | Land/building, other needs |
| **Remainder** | What is left after above | Interest on share capital + patronage refunds to members |

**Interest on share capital** is paid first from the remainder, at a rate not exceeding the normal rate of return on investments.[^32] After that, the balance is distributed as **patronage refunds** to all members in proportion to their individual patronage.

**How patronage refund works in practice.** Suppose your agricultural cooperative earned a net surplus of PHP 500,000 this year. After allocating the mandatory reserves (10%), education fund (10%), community fund (3%), and optional fund (7%), you have PHP 350,000 left. The board allocates PHP 50,000 for interest on share capital (say, 5% of total paid-up share capital) and the remaining PHP 300,000 for patronage refunds.

Member A sold PHP 200,000 worth of produce through the cooperative. Member B sold PHP 100,000. Member A gets twice the patronage refund of Member B -- because Member A patronized twice as much. This incentivizes active participation.

**Special rules for members with unpaid shares.** If a member has not fully paid their share capital subscription, their patronage refund is automatically credited to their account until the shares are fully paid.[^33] The member does not receive cash until the subscription is complete. This is a built-in mechanism for capital build-up.

## 6.4 Obligations of Members

Rights and obligations are two sides of the same coin. A cooperative where members exercise their rights but ignore their obligations will collapse. Your bylaws should spell out every obligation clearly -- and your board should enforce them consistently.

### 6.4.1 Capital Contributions and Build-Up Programs

**Every member must pay their share capital subscription.** This is the most basic obligation. When a member subscribes to shares upon admission, that subscription is a binding commitment.[^34] The member must pay installments as they fall due, according to the schedule in the bylaws.

Beyond the initial subscription, the law requires every cooperative to maintain a **capital build-up program**.[^35] This program allows the continuing growth of members' investment as their economic conditions improve. Your bylaws should define:

- **Regular capital contributions.** Monthly, quarterly, or annual contributions above the minimum subscription. A credit cooperative might require members to save PHP 200 per month into their share capital account.
- **Automatic deductions from patronage refunds.** As noted above, unpaid subscriptions are automatically offset by patronage refunds. But you can also design a program where a portion of patronage refunds is reinvested as additional shares.
- **Savings mobilization.** Encourage members to deposit savings with the cooperative (for credit and multipurpose cooperatives). While savings are not share capital, they build the cooperative's lending capacity.

**Why capital build-up matters.** Your cooperative cannot grow if it depends on external borrowing alone. Internal capital -- the money your members invest -- is the cheapest and most stable source of funds. Every peso your members contribute to share capital stays in the cooperative, earns interest, and supports the cooperative's operations. A cooperative with strong member capital can negotiate better terms with banks, invest in equipment, and weather economic downturns.

**Bangsamoro example.** A weaving cooperative in Parang, Maguindanao del Norte requires each member to subscribe to 20 shares at PHP 100 each (PHP 2,000) upon admission, paying 10 shares (PHP 1,000) upfront. The cooperative also operates a capital build-up program: each member contributes PHP 100 per month toward additional shares. After three years, each participating member holds PHP 5,600 in share capital. The cooperative's total member capital has grown from PHP 25,000 (25 founding members x PHP 1,000) to over PHP 140,000 -- enough to purchase a second industrial loom without borrowing.

### 6.4.2 Patronage Requirements

**A cooperative that members do not patronize is not a cooperative -- it is a social club.** Patronage means using the cooperative's products and services. If your cooperative is a marketing cooperative, patronage means selling your harvest through the cooperative. If it is a consumer cooperative, patronage means buying from the cooperative's store. If it is a credit cooperative, patronage means saving and borrowing through the cooperative.

**Your bylaws must define what patronage means for your cooperative.** Be specific. Do not write vague provisions like "members shall patronize the cooperative." Instead, specify:

- **Minimum transaction volume.** "Each member shall sell at least 50% of their seaweed harvest through the cooperative."
- **Minimum frequency.** "Each member shall purchase from the cooperative store at least once per month."
- **Minimum savings.** "Each member shall maintain a minimum savings balance of PHP 1,000."

**Why patronage requirements matter legally.** Non-patronage is a ground for involuntary termination. If a member has not patronized any service for an unreasonable period as determined by the board, the board may vote to terminate that member's membership.[^36] But "unreasonable period" is vague. Define it in your bylaws: six months, one year, whatever makes sense for your cooperative's business cycle.

**The patronage-surplus connection.** Members who do not patronize the cooperative receive no patronage refund. More importantly, they contribute nothing to the cooperative's revenue. A cooperative earns its surplus from the transactions of its members. Members who do not transact are free riders -- they hold shares and claim rights without contributing to the cooperative's economic activity.

### 6.4.3 Attendance and Participation

**Attendance at general assembly meetings is both a right and a duty.** Your bylaws should require members to attend and participate in all general assembly meetings.[^37] This obligation exists because quorum depends on attendance. If too many members skip the annual general assembly, the cooperative cannot conduct business, elect directors, approve budgets, or distribute surplus.

Beyond the general assembly, members have an obligation to:

- **Participate in cooperative education programs.** The board prescribes continuing education, and members must complete it.[^38] A member who refuses training may lose voting rights.
- **Observe and obey all lawful orders, decisions, rules, and regulations** adopted by the board and the general assembly.[^39]
- **Participate in the cooperative's activities and affairs.** This includes committee work, community projects, and volunteer activities that the cooperative undertakes.

**Enforcement mechanisms.** Your bylaws should attach consequences to non-attendance:

| Level of Non-Compliance | Consequence |
|---|---|
| **Missed 1 GA meeting** | Warning letter from the secretary |
| **Missed 2 consecutive GA meetings** | Suspension of voting rights until attendance resumes |
| **Missed 3 consecutive GA meetings** | Board review; possible referral to mediation committee |
| **Prolonged absence + non-patronage** | Ground for involuntary termination |

These are illustrative. Your cooperative should calibrate the consequences to its culture and context. In a small fishing cooperative in Sulu where members may be at sea during meeting dates, the board should schedule meetings around fishing seasons and offer alternative participation methods (written proxies are not allowed in primaries, but written comments on agenda items can be collected).

## 6.5 Termination of Membership

No one joins a cooperative expecting to leave. But departures happen -- by choice, by circumstance, or by board action. The law prescribes clear rules for each type of termination and protects both the departing member and the remaining cooperative.

### 6.5.1 Voluntary Withdrawal

**Any member may withdraw from the cooperative for any valid reason.** The process requires a **60-day written notice** to the board of directors.[^40] The member does not need the board's permission to withdraw. The notice is a courtesy that gives the cooperative time to prepare the refund and adjust its records.

During the 60-day notice period, the member remains a member with all rights and obligations. The member should continue to fulfill obligations (including capital payments) until the effective date of withdrawal.

**When members withdraw over policy disagreements.** The law specifically protects dissenting members in two situations:

1. **Amendments to articles or bylaws.** If the general assembly amends the articles of cooperation or bylaws by a 2/3 vote, dissenting members have the right to withdraw their membership.[^41]
2. **Merger or consolidation.** If the general assembly approves a merger by a 3/4 vote, dissenting members may exercise their right to withdraw.[^42]

In both cases, the standard 60-day notice and refund rules apply.

**Bangsamoro scenario.** A consumer cooperative in Cotabato City votes to merge with a larger regional cooperative. Twelve members disagree with the merger because they fear losing local control. They file 60-day withdrawal notices. The board processes their refunds after the notice period, and the merger proceeds with the remaining members. The departing members' share capital is returned according to the refund rules in Section 6.5.3 below.

### 6.5.2 Expulsion for Cause

**Membership termination by the board is a serious action that requires due process.** The board of directors may terminate a member's membership by a majority vote of all board members -- not just those present, but all members of the board.[^43] The grounds for expulsion are:

1. **Non-patronage.** The member has not patronized any service of the cooperative for an unreasonable period as previously determined by the board.
2. **Failure to comply with obligations.** The member has continuously failed to meet obligations -- share capital payments, attendance, or other duties specified in the bylaws.
3. **Violation of bylaws and rules.** The member has acted in violation of the cooperative's bylaws and internal regulations.
4. **Acts injurious to the cooperative.** The member has committed any act or omission injurious or prejudicial to the interest or welfare of the cooperative.[^44]

**Due process requirements.** The law mandates a specific procedure:[^45]

**Step 1: Written notice.** The board must inform the member in writing of the intended termination and the specific grounds.

**Step 2: Opportunity to be heard.** The member must be given the chance to present their side before the board makes its decision. This is not a formality. The member should be allowed to appear before the board, submit a written explanation, and present evidence.

**Step 3: Written decision.** The board's decision must be in writing and communicated to the member in person or by registered mail.

**Step 4: Right to appeal.** The member may appeal the board's decision to the general assembly within **30 days** from receipt of the written decision.[^46] The general assembly's decision is final. The GA may create an Appeal and Grievance Committee to hear such appeals. The committee has 30 days to decide. If the committee fails to decide within 30 days, the appeal is deemed approved in favor of the member.

**Pending appeal, membership remains in force.** This is a critical protection. Until the general assembly or the appeal committee issues a final decision, the expelled member remains a member with all rights intact.[^47] The board cannot cut off services or deny access to records during the appeal period.

**Practical warning.** Do not use expulsion as a weapon against dissenting members. A member who votes against a board proposal, criticizes management at the general assembly, or runs against an incumbent director is exercising legitimate rights. Expelling them for "acts injurious to the cooperative" would be an abuse of power. The grounds for expulsion must be genuine, documented, and defensible.

### 6.5.3 Refund of Share Capital

**A former member is entitled to a refund of share capital and all other interests in the cooperative.**[^48] This includes:

- The total paid-up share capital contribution
- Any accrued interest on share capital
- Any unpaid patronage refunds
- Any other amounts due under the bylaws

**The critical exception.** The cooperative may withhold the refund if paying it would reduce the cooperative's assets below the aggregate amount of its debts and liabilities (exclusive of the departing member's share capital).[^49] This protects the cooperative from insolvency caused by member withdrawals.

In plain terms: if the cooperative owes PHP 1 million and its assets are PHP 1.2 million, it cannot refund a member's PHP 300,000 share capital because doing so would leave assets (PHP 900,000) below liabilities (PHP 1 million). The member must wait until the cooperative's financial position improves.

**Refund timeline.** The law does not specify an exact number of days for processing refunds. Your bylaws should. Common practice is **60-90 days** from the effective date of termination. Some cooperatives set a longer period -- up to 180 days -- for large refunds to avoid liquidity strain.

**Refund through transfer.** All sums due to a former member shall be paid either by the cooperative or by an **approved transferee**.[^50] This means a departing member may, with the board's approval, transfer their shares to another person who qualifies for membership. The transferee pays the departing member directly. This option is useful when the cooperative has limited cash but the departing member needs immediate payment.

**Conditions for share transfer.** Under RA 9520, a member may not transfer shares unless:[^51]

1. The member has held the shares for **at least one year**
2. The transfer is made to the cooperative, to another member, or to a person who qualifies for membership
3. The board approves the transfer

| Termination Type | Notice Required | Refund Entitlement | Refund Exception |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Voluntary withdrawal** | 60-day written notice to board | Full paid-up capital + interests + patronage refunds | Refund withheld if cooperative assets would fall below liabilities |
| **Expulsion for cause** | None (board initiates) | Same entitlement as voluntary withdrawal | Same exception applies |
| **Death** | N/A (automatic) | Refund paid to legal heirs or next-of-kin | Same exception applies |
| **Insanity** | N/A (automatic) | Refund paid to legal guardian | Same exception applies |
| **Transfer of shares** | Subject to board approval | Paid by approved transferee | Transferee must qualify for membership; shares held at least 1 year |

**Death or insanity.** The death or insanity of a member in a primary cooperative is a ground for automatic termination.[^52] However, the law provides a special rule: **the next-of-kin may assume the duties and responsibilities of the original member.** This is particularly relevant in agrarian reform cooperatives but applies broadly. If a member of your fishing cooperative in Basilan passes away, the member's spouse or child may step into the membership -- assuming they meet the qualification requirements.

**Bangsamoro application.** A transport cooperative in Lamitan City has a member who dies in a fishing accident. The member held 30 shares (PHP 30,000 in paid-up capital) and had PHP 8,000 in unclaimed patronage refunds. The member's widow, who has been helping manage their motorboat, asks to assume the membership. The board verifies she is of legal age, a Filipino citizen, and within the common bond (she operates a transport vessel). They approve the transfer. She assumes her late husband's membership with his 30 shares and continuing obligations. No refund is processed because the membership continues through the next-of-kin.

## Quick Reference: Rights vs. Obligations at a Glance

| Rights (What Members Receive) | Obligations (What Members Owe) |
|---|---|
| Vote in general assembly (one member, one vote) | Attend and participate in general assembly meetings |
| Be elected as director or officer (if qualified) | Serve when elected (unless excused) |
| Inspect and examine cooperative records | Comply with bylaws, policies, and board decisions |
| Receive patronage refunds proportional to patronage | Patronize the cooperative's services and products |
| Earn interest on share capital | Pay share capital subscriptions on schedule |
| Access cooperative services and products | Participate in capital build-up programs |
| Appeal denied applications or expulsion decisions | Complete prescribed education and training |
| Withdraw membership with 60-day notice | Give proper notice before withdrawal |
| Receive refund of share capital upon termination | Settle all outstanding obligations before receiving refund |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Know your two kinds of members.** Regular members hold full rights. Associate members hold limited rights but can upgrade after two years of patronage plus intent to remain.

2. **Enforce the application process.** Written application, PMES completion, board approval, and share capital payment -- every step matters. Shortcuts create problem members.

3. **Protect voting rights fiercely.** One member, one vote. No proxies in primary cooperatives. Quorum is 25%. If your members do not show up, your cooperative cannot govern itself.

4. **Transparency builds trust.** Open your records to members. It is not a favor -- it is the law. A cooperative that hides its books from members is hiding problems.

5. **Patronage is the engine.** Members who do not use the cooperative's services contribute nothing to its surplus. Define patronage requirements. Enforce them.

6. **Due process in termination is non-negotiable.** Written notice, opportunity to be heard, written decision, right to appeal. Skip any step, and the termination is vulnerable to challenge.

7. **Refund share capital promptly.** Set a clear timeline in your bylaws. Former members who wait years for their refund will warn others against joining your cooperative.
