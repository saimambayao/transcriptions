# Chapter 7 — Financial Management and Capital Structure

Money is why cooperatives fail. Not lack of it -- mismanagement of it. A cooperative with P500,000 in share capital and sound financial practices will outlast one with P5 million and no bookkeeper. This chapter teaches you how to build your cooperative's capital base, invest it wisely, distribute your net surplus according to the law, and keep your financial records in order. Every section carries a legal requirement. Ignore it, and your cooperative loses its Certificate of Compliance. Follow it, and your members will trust you with their money -- which is the whole point.

---

## 7.1 Sources of Cooperative Capital

Your cooperative needs money to operate. The law specifies exactly where that money can come from. There are four lawful sources of cooperative capital under both RA 9520 and BTA Bill No. 210.[^1] Do not accept capital from any source that falls outside these categories without legal advice.

### 7.1.1 Share Capital (Member Subscriptions and Paid-Up Capital)

**Share capital** is the foundation of your cooperative's financial structure. It is the money your members contribute by subscribing to and paying for shares. Every member must subscribe to at least one share to join.[^2]

Two terms matter here:

- **Subscribed share capital** -- the total amount a member has committed to pay
- **Paid-up share capital** -- the amount a member has actually paid so far

A member who subscribes to 10 shares at P100 par value has subscribed P1,000. If she has paid P600, her paid-up capital is P600 and her unpaid balance is P400.

**The 10% ownership cap.** No single member may own or hold more than 10% of the cooperative's total share capital.[^3] This rule prevents any one member from dominating the cooperative financially. If your cooperative has P500,000 in total share capital, no member may hold more than P50,000.

**What happens when a member dies.** The heir can inherit the deceased member's shares -- but only if the heir qualifies for membership and the total shareholding stays within the 10% cap. Shares exceeding 10% revert to the cooperative, which pays the heir fair value.[^4]

**Transferring shares.** A member can assign shares only if three conditions are met: (1) the member has held the shares for at least one year, (2) the assignment goes to the cooperative, another member, or someone within the cooperative's field of membership, and (3) the board of directors approves the transfer.[^5]

Here is a practical example. Imagine the **Maimbung Seaweed Producers Cooperative** has 75 members. Each member subscribes to 5 shares at P500 par value. The total subscribed share capital is:

> 75 members x 5 shares x P500 = **P187,500**

If 60 members have fully paid and 15 have paid only P1,000 each, the paid-up capital is:

> (60 x P2,500) + (15 x P1,000) = P150,000 + P15,000 = **P165,000**

The unpaid balance of P22,500 is a receivable the cooperative must collect.

### 7.1.2 Deposits and Savings

Members may deposit additional funds beyond their share capital. These **deposits** are loans to the cooperative from members -- the cooperative uses the money and the member can withdraw it under terms set by the bylaws.

Deposits are liabilities, not equity. Record them separately from share capital. Your balance sheet must distinguish between what the cooperative owes to members (deposits) and what the members own in the cooperative (shares).

**For Shari'ah-compliant cooperatives:** Interest-bearing deposits violate the prohibition on **riba** (usury).[^6] Use the **wadiah** (safekeeping) model instead, where the cooperative holds the deposit as a trust. The cooperative may give a voluntary gift (**hibah**) to the depositor but cannot guarantee or pre-commit a fixed return. Section 7.6 explains this in detail.

### 7.1.3 Revolving Capital and Retained Earnings

**Revolving capital** is money retained from members' patronage refunds or interest on share capital. Instead of paying out these amounts in cash, the general assembly authorizes the board to hold them temporarily to strengthen the cooperative's capital structure.[^7]

The board must issue **revolving capital certificates** for each member's deferred amount. Each certificate must include:

- Serial number
- Member's name
- Amount deferred
- Rate of interest to be paid
- Retirement date (when the cooperative will return the money)

**Why this matters:** Revolving capital lets a young cooperative reinvest earnings without asking members for more cash. But you must honor the retirement dates. A cooperative that endlessly defers payouts will lose member trust.

### 7.1.4 Donated Capital, Subsidies, and Grants

Your cooperative may receive capital from **subsidies, donations, legacies, grants, and aid** -- from local or foreign sources, public or private.[^8] But this money comes with a permanent restriction:

**Donated capital can never be divided into individual shares.** It stays as collective cooperative property. Upon dissolution, donated capital is subject to **escheat** -- it reverts to the state.

Record donated capital in a separate account. Do not mix it with share capital in your books.

**Grants from government programs.** BARMM cooperatives can access capital from several government programs. The **PESO Project** (Profitability and Equity Support Oriented) under CSEA provides financial and technical assistance to improve cooperative profitability. The **Combatant Transitional Program** provides financial and material support to cooperatives organized by former combatants.[^52] MAFAR also channels support through Agrarian Reform Beneficiary Organizations (ARBOs) -- of which there are 240 across BARMM with 34,743 total members.[^53]

When you receive government grants, remember: the money comes with reporting obligations. Track every peso, document how it was spent, and submit liquidation reports on time. A cooperative that mismanages government funds will not receive a second grant -- and may face legal consequences.

| Capital Source | Nature | Can Be Withdrawn? | Appears on Balance Sheet As |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Share capital** | Member equity | Only upon withdrawal from membership | Members' equity |
| **Deposits** | Member loan to cooperative | Yes, per bylaws | Liability |
| **Revolving capital** | Deferred patronage/interest | Yes, upon certificate maturity | Liability (until returned) |
| **Donated capital** | Collective property | No -- subject to escheat | Donated capital fund |

## 7.2 Capital Build-Up Programs

### 7.2.1 Designing a Realistic Build-Up Program

The law requires every cooperative to include a **capital build-up program** in its bylaws.[^9] This program must be "reasonable and realistic" -- meaning it must match your members' actual economic capacity.

A seaweed farmer earning P8,000 per month cannot build capital at the same rate as a government employee earning P30,000. Design your program around who your members actually are.

**Three principles for a good capital build-up program:**

1. **Start small, scale gradually.** Set initial monthly contributions at a level every member can sustain. Increase the rate as the cooperative generates income and members see returns.

2. **Tie contributions to income cycles.** Seaweed farmers harvest every 45-60 days. Schedule capital contributions after harvest, not during planting when cash is tight.

3. **Reward consistency over size.** A member who contributes P200 every month for three years is more valuable than one who pays P5,000 once and disappears.

**Sample capital build-up schedule for a seaweed cooperative (75 members):**

| Year | Monthly Contribution | Shares Per Year | Annual Per Member | Total Annual Build-Up |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | P200 | 5 shares | P2,400 | P180,000 |
| Year 2 | P300 | 7 shares | P3,600 | P270,000 |
| Year 3 | P500 | 12 shares | P6,000 | P450,000 |

After three years, total cumulative share capital from contributions alone: **P900,000** -- before any reinvested surplus.

**Do not copy-paste another cooperative's build-up program.** Your members' income, harvest cycles, and obligations are unique. Survey them. Ask what they can realistically contribute. Build the program from their answers.

**What happens when members fall behind?** The bylaws may prescribe a **fine on unpaid subscribed share capital**, but the fine must be "fair and reasonable under the circumstances."[^39] A P50 fine on a farmer who missed one P200 payment because of typhoon damage is not fair. Build grace periods and hardship provisions into your bylaws. The goal is capital accumulation, not punishment.

**Consider a payroll deduction arrangement.** If your cooperative has members who are government employees, teachers, or factory workers, you can use the **salary deduction instrument** under the law.[^40] The member executes a written authorization allowing the employer to deduct cooperative contributions from their salary and remit directly to the cooperative. The employer must comply -- this obligation is mandatory for both government and private employers. The cooperative receives predictable monthly capital inflows, and the member never has to remember to pay.

**Track collection rates.** At every board meeting, review how much of the subscribed capital has been collected. A cooperative with P500,000 in subscriptions but only P200,000 collected has a 40% collection rate -- a warning sign. Set a target of at least 80% collection within the first year of each member's subscription.

### 7.2.2 Par Value, Shares, and Share Certificates

**Par value** is the face value assigned to each share of your cooperative. The law sets the maximum par value at **P1,000 per share**.[^10] You may set it lower. Many BARMM cooperatives use P100 or P500 per share to make ownership accessible to low-income members.

**How to choose par value:**

| Par Value | Best For | Why |
|---|---|---|
| P100 | Low-income members (farmers, fisherfolk) | Affordable; members can buy multiple shares gradually |
| P500 | Mixed-income cooperatives | Good balance between accessibility and capital growth |
| P1,000 | Professional or employee cooperatives | Maximum allowed; builds capital faster per share |

**Share certificates.** Your bylaws must prescribe the method for issuing share certificates.[^11] Each certificate should contain:

- Cooperative's name and registration number
- Member's name
- Number of shares
- Par value per share
- Date of issue
- Signatures of the chairperson and secretary
- Certificate serial number

Issue share certificates promptly. A member who has paid for shares but holds no certificate has no proof of ownership. This creates disputes during elections, dividend distribution, and withdrawal.

## 7.3 Investment of Cooperative Capital

### 7.3.1 Allowable Investments Under Law

Your cooperative cannot invest its capital anywhere it wants. The law limits investments to five categories.[^12]

| Investment Type | Description | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| Shares or securities of **other cooperatives** | Cross-investment in the cooperative movement | Low to moderate |
| **Bank deposits** in reputable local banks or cooperative banks | Savings, time deposits | Low |
| **Government securities** | Treasury bills, bonds issued or guaranteed by the government | Low |
| **Real estate** primarily for use by the cooperative or its members | Office, warehouse, drying area | Moderate |
| **Other investments authorized in the bylaws** | Must be specified and approved by the general assembly | Varies |

**What you must avoid:** Do not invest cooperative capital in speculative ventures, stocks of private corporations, cryptocurrency, or any instrument your general assembly has not explicitly authorized. If the investment is not in your bylaws, it is not lawful.

**Practical guidance for BARMM cooperatives:**

- Keep at least **60% of idle funds** in bank deposits or government securities for liquidity
- Invest in real estate only when the cooperative has a clear operational need (e.g., a drying facility, cold storage, or market stall)
- If your bylaws authorize investments beyond the four statutory categories, specify them precisely -- "investments in halal-certified enterprises within Bangsamoro" is better than "other authorized investments"

**The Reserve Fund investment restriction.** Your Reserve Fund has its own investment rules. It may not be used for investments other than those allowed by the Code.[^41] If your Reserve Fund exceeds your total share capital, the excess may be used for projects that expand the cooperative's operations -- but only upon a resolution of the general assembly. The general assembly decides, not the board alone.

**Real estate caution.** Land ownership in BARMM can involve overlapping claims -- ancestral domain, government land, and private titles sometimes conflict. Before investing cooperative capital in real estate, verify the title thoroughly. Engage a lawyer if the property is in an area with ancestral domain claims. A cooperative that sinks its capital into disputed land may lose everything.

**A note on the primary lien.** Your cooperative has a **primary lien** on a member's capital, deposits, or interest for any debt the member owes to the cooperative.[^42] This means if a member has P10,000 in share capital and owes the cooperative P8,000, the cooperative can offset the debt against the member's capital before releasing anything. This protects the cooperative, but use it transparently -- always inform members when you exercise the lien.

### 7.3.2 Shari'ah-Compliant Investment: Avoiding Riba, Gharar, and Maysir

BTA Bill No. 210 requires cooperatives operating under the **Shari'ah Governance Framework** to observe three prohibitions in all their financial activities:[^13]

| Prohibition | Arabic Term | What It Means | What It Forbids |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Usury** | Riba | Interest-based transactions | Charging or earning interest on loans, deposits, or investments |
| **Excessive uncertainty** | Gharar | Speculative transactions with unclear terms | Derivatives, futures, insurance products with ambiguous terms |
| **Gambling** | Maysir | Games of chance | Raffles, lotteries, speculative bets on price movements |

**What does this mean for your cooperative's investments?**

If your cooperative has declared Shari'ah compliance in its Articles of Cooperation, you must screen every investment for these three prohibitions. A conventional bank savings account that earns interest violates the riba prohibition. A raffle to raise funds violates the maysir prohibition.

**Permissible alternatives exist.** Section 7.6 covers Shari'ah-compliant finance instruments in detail. For now, understand these basic investment rules:

1. **Deposits:** Use **Islamic banking windows** that offer wadiah (safekeeping) or mudarabah (profit-sharing) accounts instead of interest-bearing savings accounts
2. **Government securities:** Invest in **sukuk** (Islamic bonds) where available, rather than conventional treasury bonds
3. **Real estate:** Permissible as long as the property is used for halal purposes
4. **Cross-investment in cooperatives:** Permissible if the partner cooperative also avoids riba, gharar, and maysir

**Your Shari'ah committee decides.** BTA Bill No. 210 requires cooperatives under the Shari'ah governance framework to establish a **Shari'ah committee** of at least three members with competence in Shari'ah.[^14] This committee reviews all financial transactions and investments for compliance. Do not make major investment decisions without their clearance.

## 7.4 Net Surplus and Distribution

### 7.4.1 How to Determine Net Surplus

**Net surplus is not profit.** The law makes this distinction deliberately.[^15] In a cooperative, net surplus is the excess of payments made by members -- the difference between what they paid for services and what those services actually cost. When you distribute net surplus, you are returning overpayments to the people who made them.

This is why cooperatives call it **patronage refund**, not **dividend**. The refund goes to members in proportion to how much business they did with the cooperative -- not in proportion to how much capital they contributed.

**How to calculate net surplus:**

| Line Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| **Gross income** (sales, service fees, interest income) | (A) |
| Less: **Cost of goods sold / Cost of services** | (B) |
| **Gross surplus** | A - B |
| Less: **Operating expenses** (salaries, utilities, rent, supplies) | (C) |
| **Net surplus before allocation** | A - B - C |

**Worked example -- Maimbung Seaweed Producers Cooperative, Year 1:**

| Line Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Revenue from seaweed trading (dried seaweed sold to exporters) | P2,400,000 |
| Service fees from drying facility rental | P180,000 |
| **Gross income** | **P2,580,000** |
| Less: Cost of raw seaweed purchased from members | (P1,800,000) |
| Less: Drying facility expenses | (P120,000) |
| **Gross surplus** | **P660,000** |
| Less: Operating expenses | |
| -- Manager salary | (P96,000) |
| -- Bookkeeper (part-time) | (P36,000) |
| -- Office rent | (P24,000) |
| -- Utilities and supplies | (P18,000) |
| -- Training expenses | (P12,000) |
| -- Transportation/logistics | (P30,000) |
| **Total operating expenses** | **(P216,000)** |
| **Net surplus for allocation and distribution** | **P444,000** |

Every cooperative must determine its net surplus **at the close of every fiscal year** and at any other time prescribed by the bylaws.[^16]

### 7.4.2 Statutory Fund Allocations (Reserve Fund, ETF, CDF, Optional Fund)

Before any peso goes back to members, the law requires you to set aside portions of the net surplus into **statutory funds**. These are not optional. Fail to allocate them, and your cooperative violates the Code.[^17]

**The four statutory allocations:**

| Fund | Minimum Allocation | First 5 Years | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reserve Fund (RF)** | At least 10% of net surplus | At least 50% of net surplus | Stability and loss coverage |
| **Education and Training Fund (ETF)** | Up to 10% of net surplus | Up to 10% of net surplus | Member and officer training |
| **Community Development Fund (CDF)** | At least 3% of net surplus | At least 3% of net surplus | Community projects |
| **Optional Fund (OF)** | Up to 7% of net surplus | Up to 7% of net surplus | Land, building, other needs |

**The first five years are different.** During the first five years after registration, your cooperative must allocate **at least 50%** of net surplus to the Reserve Fund.[^18] This rule exists because new cooperatives are fragile. The government wants you to build a financial cushion before distributing earnings to members.

**Worked example -- Statutory allocations for Year 1 (within first 5 years):**

| Fund | Rate | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| **Reserve Fund** | 50% (first 5 years minimum) | P222,000 |
| **Education and Training Fund** | 10% | P44,400 |
| **Community Development Fund** | 3% | P13,320 |
| **Optional Fund** | 7% | P31,080 |
| **Total statutory allocations** | **70%** | **P310,800** |
| **Remainder for members** | **30%** | **P133,200** |

**After Year 5, the same cooperative's allocation might look like this:**

| Fund | Rate | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| **Reserve Fund** | 10% (post-Year 5 minimum) | P44,400 |
| **Education and Training Fund** | 10% | P44,400 |
| **Community Development Fund** | 3% | P13,320 |
| **Optional Fund** | 7% | P31,080 |
| **Total statutory allocations** | **30%** | **P133,200** |
| **Remainder for members** | **70%** | **P310,800** |

Notice the difference. After five years, members receive more than double what they received in Year 1. This is by design -- early discipline creates long-term reward.

**The general assembly decides the exact percentages.** The law sets minimum and maximum boundaries, but your general assembly chooses the actual rates within those boundaries. A conservative board might propose allocating 15% to Reserve Fund (above the 10% minimum) and only 5% to ETF (below the 10% maximum). As long as the allocation stays within legal limits, the general assembly approves.

**Prepare a net surplus allocation resolution.** At every annual general assembly, the board presents a proposed allocation table. The general assembly votes on it. Record the resolution in the minutes. This resolution is what CSEA inspectors will ask for when they audit your statutory fund compliance.

**Rules for each fund:**

**Reserve Fund:**
- Use it only for stability and to cover net losses
- The general assembly may decrease the allocation once the Reserve Fund exceeds total share capital
- You cannot invest it in anything outside what the Code allows
- Upon dissolution, the Reserve Fund is **never distributed to members** -- it goes to the federation or union, or to the community[^19]

**Education and Training Fund:**
- Split it **50/50**: half stays with the cooperative for its own training programs; half goes to your federation or union[^20]
- The federation must report back to you how it spent the money -- list of contributors, consultancy costs, training activities
- Upon dissolution, the unexpended balance goes to your federation's education fund

**Community Development Fund:**
- Spend it on **projects that benefit the community** where the cooperative operates -- not on member-exclusive benefits[^21]
- Examples: barangay road repair, masjid renovation, school supplies for local students, clean water projects, mangrove planting
- Document every CDF expenditure. The CSEA inspectors will check.

**Optional Fund:**
- The total of optional funds, land and building fund, and any other necessary fund **must not exceed 7%** of net surplus[^22]
- The general assembly decides how to use these funds
- Common uses: building fund for a cooperative office, equipment fund for post-harvest facilities

### 7.4.3 Patronage Refunds and Interest on Capital

After setting aside statutory funds, the remaining net surplus goes to members in two forms: **interest on share capital** and **patronage refunds**.[^23]

**Interest on share capital** rewards members for investing in the cooperative. It is computed based on each member's **average share months** -- how much capital they had in the cooperative and for how long during the year.

**Patronage refunds** reward members for doing business with the cooperative. They are distributed in proportion to each member's **volume of patronage** -- how much they bought from, sold to, or used the cooperative's services.

**The law imposes two constraints:**

1. Interest on share capital must **not exceed the normal rate of return on investments** as determined by the registering authority[^24]
2. The amount allocated for patronage refunds must not be **less than 30%** of the net surplus remaining after statutory reserves[^25]

**Worked example -- distributing P133,200 to members (Year 1):**

The board recommends a 50/50 split between interest on share capital and patronage refunds. The general assembly approves.

| Distribution | Rate | Amount |
|---|---|---|
| Interest on share capital | 50% of remainder | P66,600 |
| Patronage refund | 50% of remainder | P66,600 |
| **Total to members** | | **P133,200** |

**Computing each member's interest on share capital:**

Use the **Total Average Share Month (TASM)** method:

1. Record each member's paid-up share capital balance at the end of each month
2. Add all 12 monthly balances to get the member's **Total Share Months (TSM)**
3. Divide TSM by 12 to get the **Average Share Month (ASM)**
4. Add all members' ASMs to get the **Total Average Share Month (TASM)**
5. Calculate the rate: **Rate = Amount for Interest / TASM**
6. Multiply each member's ASM by the rate

**Example for three members of the seaweed cooperative:**

| Member | Jan | Feb | Mar | Apr | May | Jun | Jul | Aug | Sep | Oct | Nov | Dec | TSM | ASM |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hadji Amir | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 5,000 | 5,000 | 5,000 | 5,000 | 5,000 | 5,000 | 44,500 | 3,708 |
| Sittie Fatima | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 30,000 | 2,500 |
| Musa | 0 | 0 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 2,500 | 25,000 | 2,083 |

If the cooperative's TASM across all 75 members is P250,000, and P66,600 is allocated for interest:

> Rate = P66,600 / P250,000 = **0.2664**

| Member | ASM | Rate | Interest Earned |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hadji Amir | 3,708 | 0.2664 | P987.65 |
| Sittie Fatima | 2,500 | 0.2664 | P666.00 |
| Musa | 2,083 | 0.2664 | P554.90 |

Hadji Amir earned more because he increased his capital mid-year. The system rewards members who build up their shares.

**Important: interest on share capital is non-cumulative.** If a member does not receive interest in a given year (because the cooperative had no surplus, or the general assembly allocated nothing to interest), that missed interest is gone. It does not carry over to the next year.[^43] This prevents interest from compounding and keeps the cooperative's obligations manageable.

**Computing patronage refunds:**

Patronage refunds are based on each member's **volume of business** with the cooperative.

For the seaweed cooperative, volume of business = total value of seaweed sold to the cooperative during the year.

| Member | Seaweed Sold to Cooperative | % of Total | Patronage Refund |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hadji Amir | P120,000 | 6.67% | P4,440 |
| Sittie Fatima | P84,000 | 4.67% | P3,111 |
| Musa | P48,000 | 2.67% | P1,778 |

**What happens to patronage refunds for members with unpaid shares?** The refund is **credited to the member's unpaid share capital** until the subscription is fully paid.[^26] The member does not receive cash -- the cooperative uses the refund to pay down the member's obligation. This is an elegant mechanism: the cooperative pays itself from the member's earnings.

**Non-member patrons.** If your cooperative does business with non-members (which many trading cooperatives do), the non-member's patronage refund is set aside in a general fund. It accumulates toward the minimum capital contribution for membership. When the accumulated amount equals the membership requirement, the non-member is invited to become a member.[^44] If the non-member refuses membership or fails to qualify within the period specified in your bylaws, the accumulated amount goes to the Reserve Fund or ETF.

**The full net surplus distribution flow looks like this:**

```
NET SURPLUS (P444,000)
    |
    |--- Reserve Fund (50% in Year 1) ------> P222,000
    |--- ETF (10%) -------------------------> P44,400
    |     |--- 50% Local Training -----------> P22,200
    |     |--- 50% Federation/Union ---------> P22,200
    |--- CDF (3%) --------------------------> P13,320
    |--- Optional Fund (7%) ----------------> P31,080
    |
    REMAINDER (P133,200)
    |
    |--- Interest on Share Capital (50%) ----> P66,600
    |--- Patronage Refund (50%) ------------> P66,600
         |--- Paid-up members: cash payment
         |--- Unpaid members: credit to share capital
         |--- Non-members: credit to membership fund
```

Post this diagram in your cooperative office. Every member should understand where the money goes. When members see exactly how their surplus is allocated -- how much goes to building the cooperative's stability, how much funds their training, how much supports their community, and how much returns to their pockets -- they stop suspecting the board of mismanagement. Transparency is not just a legal requirement. It is the cheapest form of trust-building available to your cooperative.

## 7.5 Bookkeeping and Financial Statements

### 7.5.1 Required Books of Account

Every cooperative must maintain records that show its **true and correct condition** at any time.[^27] The law is specific about what must be kept and accessible. Here are the minimum books of account your cooperative must maintain:

**Mandatory records:**

| Book/Record | Purpose | Who Maintains It |
|---|---|---|
| **General journal** | Records all transactions chronologically | Bookkeeper |
| **General ledger** | Summarizes all accounts (assets, liabilities, equity, income, expenses) | Bookkeeper |
| **Cash receipts journal** | Records all cash received | Treasurer/Bookkeeper |
| **Cash disbursements journal** | Records all cash paid out | Treasurer/Bookkeeper |
| **Members' subsidiary ledger** | Tracks each member's share capital, deposits, loans, and patronage | Bookkeeper |
| **Share book** | Records share ownership and transfers | Secretary |
| **Minutes books** | Records proceedings of GA, BOD, and committee meetings | Secretary |
| **Register of members** | Lists all members with dates of admission and cessation | Secretary |

Keep all of these at your **principal office**. They must be accessible to members and CSEA representatives during reasonable office hours.[^28]

**The register of members is evidence.** The law gives it **prima facie evidentiary value** -- meaning a court will accept it as proof of when someone became a member and when they stopped being one.[^29] Keep it accurate. A register with errors invites disputes during elections and surplus distribution.

**Retention period.** Keep all financial records for at least **five years**. You may destroy records older than five years -- but only if they are not subject to any pending civil, criminal, or administrative case. Before destroying records, the board secretary and audit committee chair must prepare a certified inventory, and the board must approve the destruction.[^30]

### 7.5.2 The Cooperative Annual Financial Statement (CAFS)

Your cooperative must prepare and submit **audited financial statements** every year. These are called the **Cooperative Audited Financial Statements (CAFS)**, and they are part of your annual compliance package.[^31]

**CAFS components:**

1. **Statement of Financial Position** (balance sheet) -- what the cooperative owns and owes
2. **Statement of Operations** (income statement) -- revenue, expenses, and net surplus
3. **Statement of Changes in Members' Equity** -- how equity changed during the year
4. **Statement of Cash Flows** -- where cash came from and where it went
5. **Notes to Financial Statements** -- explanations of accounting policies and significant items

**Who can audit your cooperative?**

| Cooperative Asset Size | Auditor Requirement |
|---|---|
| Assets below P3 million | Internal audit acceptable |
| Assets P3 million to P15 million | Must be signed by a CPA |
| Assets above P15 million | External audit by a CSEA-accredited auditor |

**Deadline:** Submit both the CAFS and the **Cooperative Annual Progress Report (CAPR)** within **120 days** after the end of your fiscal year.[^32] If your fiscal year ends December 31, your deadline is April 30. Miss it, and your cooperative faces fines and will not receive its **Certificate of Compliance (COC)**.

**The COC is your cooperative's lifeline.** Without it, you cannot transact with banks, apply for loans, do business with government agencies, or renew your registration. Every year, CSEA inspectors verify your statutory fund allocations, fund utilization, documentation, and accounting treatment.[^33]

**Publish your financial statements.** The law requires annual financial statements to be published and posted in a **conspicuous place** in your cooperative's principal office.[^34] Transparency builds trust. Members who can see the numbers are members who stay.

### 7.5.3 The PFRF: How Cooperative Financial Statements Differ

Your cooperative does not follow the same financial reporting rules as a regular business. The **Philippine Financial Reporting Framework for Cooperatives (PFRF)**, prescribed by **MC 2022-25**, is your authoritative reporting standard.[^56] This separate framework exists because RA 9520 creates accounts that have no equivalent in ordinary corporate accounting -- statutory funds, patronage refunds, CETF, and members' equity.

**Complete set of financial statements your cooperative must prepare annually:**[^57]

1. **Statement of Financial Condition** (as of reporting date)
2. **Statement of Operations** (for the reporting period)
3. **Statement of Changes in Equity, Statutory Reserves and Donations and Grants** (for the reporting period)
4. **Statement of Cash Flows** (for the reporting period)
5. **Notes to the Financial Statements** (accounting policies and disclosures)

Your Notes must explicitly state that the statements comply with the PFRF. Do not claim PFRS compliance -- your cooperative follows a different framework.

**How cooperative financial statements differ from regular business:**

| Element | Regular Business (PFRS) | Your Cooperative (PFRF, MC 2022-25) |
|---|---|---|
| **Equity section** | Shareholders' Equity | Members' Equity, Statutory Funds (Reserve Fund, CETF, Optional Fund, Community Development Fund), Donations and Grants |
| **Bottom line** | Net Income / Profit | **Net Surplus** (RA 9520, Art. 85) |
| **Surplus distribution** | Dividends per board discretion | Mandatory allocation: Reserve Fund (min 10%), CETF (max 10%), then Optional Fund, Interest on Share Capital, Patronage Refund (RA 9520, Art. 86) |
| **Unique payables** | -- | **Patronage Refund Payable**, **Interest on Share Capital Payable**, **Due to Unions and Federations** (CETF remittance) |
| **Unique income/expense** | -- | CETF Subsidy, Members' Benefit Expense, Social and Community Service Expense |
| **Reporting framework** | PFRS / PFRS for SMEs | PFRF for Cooperatives (MC 2022-25) |
| **Compliance statement** | States PFRS compliance | Must explicitly state **PFRF compliance** in the Notes |

**Why this matters for your officers.** When your board reviews the financial statements, they must understand what they are reading. The equity section of a cooperative balance sheet looks nothing like a corporate one. Your board members will see "Reserve Fund," "CETF," and "Patronage Refund Payable" -- terms that do not appear in any business accounting textbook. Train them on PFRF-specific line items before expecting them to approve the statements. A board that approves financial statements it does not understand is a board that will not catch errors or fraud.

**Net surplus allocation under the law** (RA 9520, Art. 86):[^58]

| # | Allocation | Percentage | Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Reserve Fund** | Min. 10% (min. 50% in first 5 years) | For stability and absorbing net losses; not distributable upon dissolution |
| 2 | **Education and Training Fund (CETF)** | Max. 10% | Half spent by cooperative; the other half remitted to federation/union |
| 3 | **Community Development Fund** | Min. 3% | For projects benefiting the community where the cooperative operates |
| 4 | **Optional Fund** | Max. 7% | As bylaws may provide; includes land/building fund |
| 5 | **Interest on Share Capital and Patronage Refund** | Remaining balance | Interest shall not exceed normal rate of return; patronage refund proportional to volume of patronage |

This allocation table is not discretionary. It is the law. See Section 7.4.2 for worked examples.

### 7.5.4 The STEPS Framework: Measuring Financial Health

Your cooperative's financial health is not measured by gut feeling. The CDA/CSEA uses the **STEPS framework** to evaluate every cooperative's financial performance through the **Performance Audit Report (PAR)**, prescribed by **MC 2021-04**.[^59] Understand this framework. It determines your cooperative's rating -- and your rating affects your **Certificate of Compliance**.

**STEPS stands for:**

| Indicator | What It Measures | Key Ratios | Source Statement |
|---|---|---|---|
| **S**tability | Capital adequacy and solvency | Equity / Total Assets; Loan Loss Provision / Total Loans | Statement of Financial Condition |
| **T**urn-Over | Asset utilization and efficiency | Net Revenue / Total Assets | Statement of Operations + Statement of Financial Condition |
| **E**fficiency | Operating cost control | Operating Expenses / Gross Revenue | Statement of Operations |
| **P**rofitability | Surplus generation capacity | Net Surplus / Total Assets (ROA); Net Surplus / Equity (ROE) | Statement of Operations + Statement of Financial Condition |
| **S**tructure of Assets | Portfolio quality and reserve adequacy | Past Due Loans / Total Loans; Earning Assets / Total Assets | Statement of Financial Condition |

**The PAR scoring breakdown.** Your cooperative's Performance Audit Report score has two components:[^60]

| Component | Weight | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| **Governance and Management Report (GMR)** | 40% | Board effectiveness, compliance with bylaws, meeting regularity, internal controls |
| **STEPS financial ratios** | 60% | The five financial indicators above |

Your financial performance carries **60% of your total score**. A cooperative with excellent governance but poor financial ratios will still receive a low PAR rating. Conversely, strong STEPS scores can compensate for minor governance gaps.

**How to use STEPS in practice.** Do not wait for the CSEA auditor to compute your ratios. Calculate them quarterly using your own financial statements. Here is what to watch:

- **Stability below 20% equity-to-assets ratio** -- your cooperative is overleveraged. Build up the Reserve Fund before taking on more liabilities.
- **Turn-Over declining year over year** -- your assets are not generating enough revenue. Review whether idle funds should be redeployed into productive activities.
- **Efficiency ratio above 80%** -- you are spending too much to generate each peso of revenue. Cut operating costs or increase revenue before both problems compound.
- **Profitability ratios negative** -- you are operating at a net loss. This erodes your Reserve Fund and threatens your viability. Identify the loss sources immediately.
- **Structure of Assets shows past-due loans above 10%** -- your lending portfolio is deteriorating. Tighten credit evaluation and collection procedures.

**Present your STEPS scores at every annual general assembly.** Members deserve to know how their cooperative is performing against the same benchmarks the regulator uses. When members see that the cooperative's Efficiency ratio improved from 75% to 65%, they understand that management is spending less to earn more. Numbers build confidence. Confidence builds participation.

### 7.5.5 Hiring and Working with Your Bookkeeper

Most primary cooperatives in BARMM cannot afford a full-time accountant. That is fine. What you cannot afford is having no financial records at all. The law holds your **bookkeeper or accountant responsible** for maintaining records in accordance with generally accepted accounting practices.[^35]

**Options for small cooperatives:**

| Arrangement | Cost Estimate | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Part-time bookkeeper** (2-3 days/month) | P3,000-5,000/month | Cooperatives with 50-100 members, simple operations |
| **Shared bookkeeper** (with other cooperatives) | P1,500-3,000/month per cooperative | Small cooperatives in the same municipality |
| **Volunteer treasurer** with bookkeeping training | Free (training cost only) | Micro cooperatives with very simple transactions |
| **CPA engagement** (monthly or quarterly) | P5,000-10,000/month | Cooperatives with assets above P3 million |

**What your bookkeeper must do each month:**

1. Record all transactions in the journals
2. Post entries to the general ledger
3. Update individual member subsidiary ledgers
4. Reconcile the cash balance with the bank statement
5. Prepare a monthly trial balance
6. File source documents (receipts, vouchers, checks) chronologically

**What you must give your bookkeeper:**

- All official receipts for money received
- All disbursement vouchers with supporting documents
- Bank statements and deposit slips
- Inventory records of products bought and sold
- A quiet, secure place to work with the books

**The audit committee's role.** Your audit committee is directly accountable to the general assembly -- not to the board.[^36] The audit committee continuously monitors the adequacy of your cooperative's management control system and audits performance. Think of the bookkeeper as the person who records the transactions, and the audit committee as the people who verify that the records are correct. Both are essential. Neither can replace the other.

### 7.5.6 Common Financial Mistakes That Kill Cooperatives

BARMM has over 12,000 registered cooperatives, but only 11.66% are compliant.[^45] Financial mismanagement is the leading reason. Here are the mistakes you must avoid:

**Mistake 1: Commingling personal and cooperative funds.** The treasurer deposits cooperative collections into a personal bank account "for convenience." This makes it impossible to audit the cooperative and exposes the treasurer to criminal liability. Open a dedicated bank account in the cooperative's name from day one.

**Mistake 2: No receipts, no vouchers.** Cash goes in and out without documentation. When the auditor arrives, there is no paper trail. Every single transaction -- every peso received and every peso spent -- must have a supporting document. Official receipts for income. Disbursement vouchers with supporting receipts for expenses. No exceptions.

**Mistake 3: Treating the Reserve Fund as a piggy bank.** The board dips into the Reserve Fund for operating expenses during a slow month. The Reserve Fund exists for stability and to cover net losses -- not for routine cash flow management.[^46] If you need working capital, borrow from a bank or raise capital from members. Do not raid the Reserve Fund.

**Mistake 4: Skipping the annual general assembly.** If you do not hold a general assembly, you cannot approve the net surplus allocation, elect officers, or present the audit report. All of these are legal requirements. A cooperative that skips its general assembly for two consecutive years faces dissolution.[^47]

**Mistake 5: Lending cooperative funds to officers.** A board member asks for a "loan" from cooperative funds. Unless your cooperative is a credit or multipurpose cooperative with a lending program, and the loan follows the same terms available to all members, this is a violation. Officers who acquire personal interests in conflict with their duties are **jointly and severally liable** for all resulting damages.[^48]

**Mistake 6: Ignoring the bonding requirement.** Every director, officer, and employee who handles cooperative funds must be covered by a **surety bond** from a registered insurance or bonding company.[^49] The bond protects the cooperative if the officer mishandles funds. Many BARMM cooperatives skip this requirement, leaving themselves exposed. The board determines the adequacy of bonds, and CSEA requires proof of bonding for your Certificate of Compliance.

**Mistake 7: Not separating ETF and CDF spending.** The treasurer uses education fund money to repair a community road (which is a CDF expense) or uses community development fund money to send officers to a training seminar (which is an ETF expense). Each fund has its own permitted uses. Mixing them creates audit findings and delays your COC.

**Mistake 8: Distributing surplus without a general assembly resolution.** The board decides to give patronage refunds without presenting the allocation to the general assembly for a vote. The net surplus allocation must be approved by the general assembly through a formal resolution.[^50] No resolution, no legal basis for the distribution.

## 7.6 Islamic Finance Instruments for Cooperatives

### 7.6.1 Mudarabah, Musharakah, and Murabaha Explained

BTA Bill No. 210 establishes Islamic finance principles as governing law for cooperatives under the Shari'ah governance framework.[^37] This is not advisory. If your cooperative has declared Shari'ah compliance, these principles are binding.

The conventional financial system runs on **interest** -- you borrow money, you pay interest; you deposit money, you earn interest. Islamic finance runs on **risk-sharing** and **asset-backed transactions**. The money must always be connected to a real economic activity. Here are the three most relevant instruments for BARMM cooperatives:

**Mudarabah (Profit-Sharing Partnership)**

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **What it is** | A partnership where one party provides capital (rabb al-mal) and the other provides labor and management (mudarib) |
| **How it works** | The cooperative (as rabb al-mal) provides funds to a member-entrepreneur (mudarib). Profits are shared at a pre-agreed ratio. Losses are borne by the capital provider unless the mudarib was negligent. |
| **Cooperative use** | Financing member livelihood projects -- seaweed drying, fish vending, small trading |
| **Key rule** | The profit-sharing ratio must be agreed before the transaction. It cannot be a fixed amount -- it must be a percentage of actual profit. |

**Example:** The cooperative provides P50,000 to a member who will buy and dry seaweed for resale. They agree on a 60/40 profit split -- 60% to the cooperative, 40% to the member. If the member earns P20,000 in profit, the cooperative receives P12,000 and the member keeps P8,000. If the venture loses money due to market conditions (not negligence), the cooperative absorbs the P50,000 loss.

**Musharakah (Joint Venture Partnership)**

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **What it is** | A partnership where both parties contribute capital and share profits and losses |
| **How it works** | The cooperative and the member both invest money in a joint project. Profits are shared according to a pre-agreed ratio. Losses are shared in proportion to capital contribution. |
| **Cooperative use** | Larger projects like buying a fishing boat, renting cold storage, or establishing a retail outlet |
| **Key rule** | Both parties share the risk. Neither party can guarantee the other's capital. |

**Example:** The cooperative invests P200,000 and a group of five members invest P100,000 collectively to purchase a motorized fishing boat. Ownership is 67% cooperative, 33% members. They agree to share profits 60/40 (favoring the members to incentivize effort). If the boat is damaged and the venture loses P30,000, the cooperative bears P20,000 (67%) and the members bear P10,000 (33%).

**Diminishing Musharakah** is a popular variant. The cooperative and the member co-own an asset. Over time, the member buys the cooperative's share through regular payments, eventually owning the asset outright. This works well for equipment financing.

**Murabaha (Cost-Plus Sale)**

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **What it is** | The cooperative purchases an asset and sells it to the member at a disclosed markup |
| **How it works** | The member identifies what they need (e.g., a seaweed drying rack). The cooperative buys it for P30,000 and sells it to the member for P33,000, payable in installments. The P3,000 markup is disclosed upfront. |
| **Cooperative use** | Equipment, production inputs, supplies needed by members |
| **Key rule** | The cooperative must take actual ownership of the asset before selling it. The cost and markup must both be disclosed. There is no penalty for late payment beyond what the contract states. |

**Why murabaha is not a disguised loan:** In a conventional loan, the cooperative gives money and charges interest regardless of what the borrower buys. In murabaha, the cooperative buys a specific asset and resells it. The transaction is asset-backed. The markup is a trading profit, not interest.

### 7.6.2 Practical Application in Bangsamoro Cooperatives

**Comparison: Conventional vs. Shari'ah-Compliant Cooperative Finance**

| Activity | Conventional Approach | Shari'ah-Compliant Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| **Member borrows P50,000 for livelihood** | Loan at 12% annual interest | Mudarabah: cooperative provides capital, shares profit 60/40 |
| **Cooperative finances equipment** | Loan for P100,000 at fixed interest | Murabaha: cooperative buys equipment, sells to member at disclosed cost-plus |
| **Members pool capital for a boat** | Members borrow from cooperative with interest | Musharakah: cooperative and members co-invest, share profit and loss |
| **Member deposits savings** | Savings account at 3% interest | Wadiah: cooperative holds deposit in trust, may give voluntary hibah |
| **Cooperative invests idle funds** | Time deposit at commercial bank | Mudarabah account at Islamic banking window; sukuk investment |
| **Insurance for cooperative assets** | Conventional insurance policy | Takaful: mutual guarantee arrangement among members |

**Steps to implement Shari'ah-compliant finance in your cooperative:**

1. **Establish your Shari'ah committee** -- at least three members with Shari'ah competence.[^38] They do not need formal degrees in Islamic law, but they must understand the basics of Islamic commercial transactions.

2. **Amend your bylaws** to specify which instruments you will use. Do not attempt to use all instruments at once. Start with **murabaha** for equipment financing -- it is the simplest to implement and closest to conventional practice.

3. **Train your officers and bookkeeper.** The accounting for mudarabah and musharakah differs from conventional loan accounting. Your bookkeeper must know how to record profit-sharing transactions, track ownership shares in diminishing musharakah, and account for murabaha receivables.

4. **Disclose everything.** Transparency is both an Islamic finance requirement and a cooperative principle. Every transaction must have clear terms: the cost, the markup or profit ratio, the timeline, and the conditions for loss-sharing.

5. **Document the Shari'ah committee's rulings.** Every financial product your cooperative offers should have a written Shari'ah opinion (fatwa) from your committee. Keep these on file for CSEA inspection.

**Common pitfalls in Shari'ah-compliant cooperative finance:**

- **Calling it Islamic but operating conventionally.** A cooperative that charges "service fees" calculated as a percentage of the loan amount over time is still charging interest -- it just changed the label. The Shari'ah committee must verify that the substance of each transaction, not just the name, complies with Islamic principles.

- **Ignoring the loss-sharing requirement.** In mudarabah and musharakah, the capital provider must absorb losses (unless the working partner was negligent). Some cooperatives insert clauses requiring the working partner to guarantee the capital -- this violates the risk-sharing principle and makes the contract impermissible.

- **Using murabaha as a universal substitute for loans.** Murabaha requires the cooperative to take actual ownership and possession of the asset before selling it to the member. A cooperative that transfers money directly to the member and calls it murabaha is offering a conventional loan with an Islamic label. The cooperative must purchase the item, hold it (even briefly), and then sell it to the member with a disclosed markup.

**Where to find Islamic banking services in BARMM:**

Several commercial banks operating in Bangsamoro have **Islamic banking windows** that offer Shari'ah-compliant deposit and investment products. Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank of the Philippines, based in Cotabato City, is the only Islamic bank in the country. Your cooperative should explore these options for depositing and investing its funds.

**A word of encouragement.** Shari'ah-compliant finance may seem complex, but it aligns naturally with cooperative principles. Both systems emphasize **risk-sharing over risk-transfer**, **real economic activity over speculation**, and **fairness in transactions**. A cooperative that practices genuine Islamic finance is simply a cooperative that takes the seventh cooperative principle -- Concern for Community -- to its logical conclusion.

The **maqasid al-Shari'ah** (objectives of Islamic law) -- preservation of faith, life, lineage, intellect, and wealth -- are served when a cooperative manages its finances honestly, shares risk equitably, and invests in the real economy of its community.[^51] This is what cooperatives were invented to do.

## Summary: Financial Management Checklist

Use this checklist to verify your cooperative's financial health at the end of every fiscal year.

**Capital Structure:**
- [ ] All member share capital subscriptions are recorded and monitored for collection
- [ ] No member holds more than 10% of total share capital
- [ ] Deposits are recorded as liabilities, separate from share capital
- [ ] Donated capital is held in a separate account and never divided among members
- [ ] Revolving capital certificates are issued with serial numbers and retirement dates

**Net Surplus Distribution:**
- [ ] Net surplus is calculated at fiscal year end
- [ ] Reserve Fund receives at least 10% (or 50% in first 5 years)
- [ ] ETF receives up to 10%, split 50/50 between local training and federation
- [ ] CDF receives at least 3%
- [ ] Optional Fund does not exceed 7%
- [ ] Patronage refunds are based on volume of business, not share capital
- [ ] Interest on share capital does not exceed the normal rate of return

**Bookkeeping and Compliance:**
- [ ] All eight required books/records are maintained at the principal office
- [ ] Monthly transactions are recorded, posted, and reconciled
- [ ] CAFS is prepared and audited according to the cooperative's asset size
- [ ] CAFS and CAPR are submitted within 120 days of fiscal year end
- [ ] Financial statements are posted in a conspicuous place at the office
- [ ] All source documents are filed and retained for at least 5 years

**Shari'ah Compliance (if applicable):**
- [ ] Shari'ah committee of at least 3 members is established
- [ ] All financial products have written Shari'ah opinions on file
- [ ] No transactions involve riba (interest), gharar (excessive uncertainty), or maysir (gambling)
- [ ] All financing is asset-backed or profit-sharing based

**Governance over Finance:**
- [ ] General assembly approved the net surplus allocation by formal resolution
- [ ] Board reviews collection rates on subscribed share capital at every meeting
- [ ] All officers handling funds are covered by surety bonds
- [ ] Audit committee reports directly to the general assembly, not the board
- [ ] No officer has a personal financial interest that conflicts with cooperative duties

**Tax Compliance:**
- [ ] If your cooperative transacts only with members, you are exempt from all taxes[^54]
- [ ] If your cooperative transacts with non-members, member transactions remain tax-exempt; non-member transactions are taxed per applicable rates
- [ ] Cooperatives with reserves and undivided net savings of P10 million or less enjoy full tax exemption on non-member transactions[^55]
- [ ] Tax records and payments are documented and filed with the appropriate authority
