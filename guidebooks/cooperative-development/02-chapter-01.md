# Chapter 1 -- Why Cooperatives Matter in the Bangsamoro

Out of every ten cooperatives registered in the Bangsamoro, fewer than two are functional. The rest exist on paper. They have certificates of registration, articles of cooperation, and bylaws gathering dust in filing cabinets. They held their organizing assembly, elected their first board, and then -- nothing. No annual reports. No general assemblies. No certificate of compliance. No business operations.

You already know this. You have seen it in your barangay, your municipality, your province. A cooperative was organized, received initial capital, and then dissolved into inactivity. The members lost their investment. The community lost an opportunity. The cycle repeated.

This chapter explains why that pattern persists and why cooperatives -- properly organized, properly governed, and properly supported -- remain your best available tool for building economic self-reliance in the Bangsamoro.

---

## 1.1 The Problem: Low Functionality and Unsustainable Profitability

The Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP) 2023-2028 names the core problem directly: **low functionality and unsustainable profitability of cooperatives**.[^1] This is not a minor administrative finding. It describes a systemic failure that affects hundreds of thousands of Bangsamoro families.

### 1.1.1 The 11.66 Percent Compliance Reality

Between 2010 and 2020, the Bangsamoro region had **12,214 registered cooperatives**.[^2] Of those, only **1,424 -- or 11.66 percent -- were able to secure their certificate of compliance** with the requirements set by law.[^3]

Think about what that number means. For every cooperative that met its basic legal obligations, roughly **eight did not**. They failed to submit annual reports. They failed to hold general assemblies. They failed to file their audited financial statements within the 120-day deadline.

The geographic concentration makes the picture clearer. **Maguindanao** accounts for 4,671 cooperatives (38.24 percent of the regional total), and **Lanao del Sur** accounts for 4,554 (37.29 percent).[^4] These two provinces hold more than 75 percent of all registered cooperatives in the region. If compliance fails here, it fails everywhere.

The breakdown by type reveals what kinds of cooperatives dominate -- and struggle:

| Cooperative Type | Number | Percent Share |
|---|---|---|
| **Producer cooperatives** | 4,590 | 37.58% |
| **Marketing cooperatives** | 3,364 | 27.54% |
| **Agricultural cooperatives** | 1,566 | 12.82% |
| Multi-purpose, credit, and others | 2,694 | 22.06% |

*Source: BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 7*

Producer and marketing cooperatives make up nearly two-thirds of the total. These are the cooperatives most directly tied to the livelihoods of farmers, fishers, and small-scale producers. When they fail, the people who can least afford failure are the ones who pay.

The BDP 2023-2028 sets an ambitious target: **raise the compliance rate from 11.66 percent to 50 percent** by the end of the plan period.[^5] That means moving from 1,424 compliant cooperatives to over 6,000. Every chapter in this guidebook is designed to help you reach that target.

### 1.1.2 What "Non-Functional" Actually Means for Communities

Statistics measure compliance. They do not measure consequences.

When a cooperative becomes non-functional, real people absorb the damage:

- **Members lose their capital contributions.** A fisher in Tawi-Tawi who invested PHP 5,000 in a seaweed cooperative that never operated has lost money the family needed. That member will not trust cooperatives again.
- **Communities lose access to collective bargaining.** A single cassava farmer in Lanao del Sur has no leverage with traders. A functioning cooperative with 50 members can negotiate prices, share transportation costs, and access markets that no individual farmer can reach alone.
- **Government programs lose their delivery channel.** Most interventions from development partners and government agencies -- especially MAFAR -- are channeled through cooperatives or organized groups.[^6] When cooperatives are non-functional, the assistance has nowhere to go.
- **Former combatants lose their transition pathway.** The BDP identifies cooperatives organized by former MILF and MNLF members as critical to the peace process. These cooperatives face particular compliance difficulties because the policies and guidelines were designed for the previous CDA structure.[^7]
- **The poverty cycle continues.** BARMM recorded a poverty incidence of **29.80 percent in 2021** -- down from 52.6 percent in 2018, a significant improvement, but still the highest among all regions.[^8] Approximately **408,951 Bangsamoro families live in poverty**.[^9] Functional cooperatives are one of the few proven mechanisms to aggregate resources, access markets, and distribute economic gains to the poor.

The 11.66 percent compliance rate is not just a regulatory statistic. It represents a broken bridge between government programs and the communities those programs are designed to serve.

### 1.1.3 Three Root Causes

The BDP 2023-2028 identifies three root causes behind cooperative failure in the Bangsamoro:[^10]

**1. Low compliance rate.** Only 11.66 percent of cooperatives secured their certificate of compliance. The requirements -- annual general assembly, audited financial statements, Cooperative Annual Performance Report (CAPR) submission -- are straightforward. The problem is not complexity. The problem is that cooperatives were registered without the capacity, governance structure, or business model needed to sustain operations beyond the organizing phase.

**2. Lack of an enacted cooperative and social enterprise code.** The Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code (BTA Bill No. 210) remains pending before the Bangsamoro Parliament.[^11] Without this legislation, the cooperative sector operates under **RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008)** -- a national law that does not account for the Bangsamoro's unique governance framework, Shari'ah compliance requirements, or moral governance standards. The absence of a Bangsamoro-specific code slows the full operationalization and institutionalization of cooperatives and social enterprises.

**3. Inadequate LGU participation.** Most LGUs in the Bangsamoro region have **no appointed Municipal Cooperative Officers (MCOs)**.[^12] Without MCOs, regional cooperative officers from CSEA find it difficult to coordinate at the LGU level. The cooperative sector's reach effectively ends at the provincial office. It never penetrates the municipalities and barangays where cooperatives actually operate.

Each of these root causes has a solution. This guidebook addresses all three -- through practical guidance on governance and compliance (Chapters 4-9), alignment with both RA 9520 and BTA Bill 210 (Chapter 14), and strategies for LGU engagement (Chapter 13).

## 1.2 Cooperatives as a Tool for Bangsamoro Development

Cooperatives are not an end in themselves. They are instruments. The Bangsamoro Development Plan positions cooperatives as tools for achieving a specific development goal -- and funds specific programs to make that goal real.

### 1.2.1 BDP 2023-2028: Goal 2, Objective 2.5 -- Functional Cooperatives and Social Enterprises Increased

The BDP 2023-2028 organizes its economic development agenda under **Goal 2: Equitable, competitive, and robust economy**.[^13] That goal envisions three things:

- An **equitable** economy where gains benefit all, with special consideration for the poor and marginalized
- A **competitive** economy with comparative advantage in selected commodities
- A **robust** economy with sustained growth and a diversified, stabilized base

Under Goal 2, the BDP establishes five sectoral objectives. Cooperatives fall under **Objective 2.5: Functional Cooperatives and Social Enterprises Increased**.[^14] This objective has two sub-sector targets:

| Sub-sector Objective | Target |
|---|---|
| **2.5.1** | Profitability of cooperatives increased |
| **2.5.2** | Established social enterprises increased |

Notice the language. The BDP does not merely call for more cooperatives. It calls for **functional** cooperatives with **increased profitability**. Registration alone achieves nothing. The measure of success is economic performance.

### 1.2.2 Strategy 21 and Strategy 22: The Government's Commitment

The BDP assigns two priority strategies to the cooperative sector:[^15]

**Strategy 21** -- Ensure profitability and functionality of cooperatives through:
- Sufficient **technical and financial assistance**
- Institutionalization of a **monitoring and evaluation system and mechanism**
- **Community promotion** of cooperatives
- **Capacitation of human resources**

**Strategy 22** -- Institutionalize enabling policies for the establishment of **social enterprises** in the BARMM.

These strategies are not aspirational statements. They are backed by specific programs with defined projects.

### 1.2.3 Four Programs You Should Know

The BDP funds four key programs under the cooperative and social enterprise sector:[^16]

**1. PESO Project (Profitability and Equity Support Oriented).** This project provides financial and technical assistance to improve cooperative profitability. If your cooperative needs business planning support, financial management training, or capital assistance, PESO is the channel.

**2. LAKAS SE Program (Lakbay Aral sa Social Enterprise).** This is a learning program for social enterprise development. It exposes participants to models, best practices, and operational frameworks for social enterprises in the Bangsamoro context.

**3. STOCK Project (Strengthening of Technical and Operational Knowledge).** Under the Combatant Transitional Program, STOCK provides capacity building specifically for cooperatives organized by former combatants. If your cooperative includes decommissioned members of the MILF or MNLF, this project targets your needs.

**4. Cooperative Financial and Material Support Project.** Also under the Combatant Transitional Program, this project provides direct financial and material assistance to former combatant cooperatives to support their business operations.

### 1.2.4 Why Cooperatives -- and Not Just Any Enterprise

BARMM is rich in natural resources. Consider the top commodities where the region leads nationally:[^17]

| Commodity | Percent Share of National Production | National Rank |
|---|---|---|
| **Seaweed** | 48.42% | 1st |
| **Cassava** | 41.70% | 1st |
| **Mangosteen** | 22.40% | 2nd |
| **Rubber** | 18.40% | 3rd |
| **Coffee** | 17.40% | 3rd |

BARMM produces nearly half the country's seaweed and more than 40 percent of its cassava. These are commodities where **collective action** creates value that individual production cannot. A seaweed farming cooperative in Tawi-Tawi can consolidate production, invest in post-harvest processing, negotiate directly with carrageenan exporters, and capture value that currently leaks to middlemen.

The same logic applies across every priority commodity. Consider the province-level priorities:[^55]

| Province | Priority Commodities |
|---|---|
| **Maguindanao** | Rubber, coffee, coconut, banana, goat |
| **Lanao del Sur** | Cassava, coconut, banana, abaca, coffee |
| **Basilan** | Rubber, coconut, seaweeds |
| **Sulu** | Coffee, cassava, abaca, seaweeds, coconut |

*Source: MAFAR, via BDP 2023-2028, Chapter 7*

Each province has commodities where cooperatives can aggregate supply, share post-harvest facilities, and negotiate better prices. A rubber cooperative in Basilan can invest in processing equipment that no individual smallholder can afford. A coconut cooperative in Lanao del Sur can build a shared copra dryer to reduce post-harvest losses. A coffee cooperative in Sulu can establish quality control standards that open access to specialty coffee markets.

The Bangsamoro's economic potential is enormous. BARMM has the largest land area in the country at 11.17 percent of total national land area.[^56] It has a young, dynamic population -- 42 percent fall within the 15-39 age bracket, providing a large pool of trainable workforce.[^57] The mechanism for unlocking that potential -- for aggregating small producers, pooling capital, sharing risk, and accessing markets -- is the cooperative.

The agrarian reform sector reinforces this point. As of October 2019, BARMM had **104 Agrarian Reform Communities (ARCs)** and **240 Agrarian Reform Beneficiary Organizations (ARBOs)** with a total of **34,743 members**.[^58] These organizations are the cooperative movement's natural allies. Many ARBOs operate as cooperatives or aspire to become one. Strengthening the cooperative sector strengthens the agrarian reform sector -- and both strengthen the Bangsamoro economy.

## 1.3 Islamic Foundations of Cooperative Enterprise

Cooperative principles did not originate in the Bangsamoro. But they are deeply compatible with Islamic values that the Bangsamoro has practiced for centuries. This section connects cooperative enterprise to its Islamic foundations -- not as an afterthought, but as the moral and spiritual framework that makes Bangsamoro cooperatives distinct.

### 1.3.1 Collective Action in Islamic Tradition

Three concepts from Islamic tradition directly underpin cooperative enterprise:

**Ta'awun (mutual assistance).** The Qur'an commands believers to cooperate in righteousness and piety. Ta'awun is not charity. It is the obligation to work together for mutual benefit and collective welfare. When cooperative members pool their resources and share risks, they practice ta'awun.

**Shirkah (partnership).** Islamic commercial law recognizes shirkah as a partnership in which two or more parties combine capital, labor, or both to share profits and losses. This principle maps directly onto the cooperative model where members contribute equitably to capital, patronize cooperative services, and accept a fair share of risks and benefits.

**Musharakah (joint venture).** A specific form of shirkah, musharakah involves all partners contributing capital and sharing in management. In a cooperative, every member is simultaneously an owner, a patron, and a participant in governance. Musharakah describes this relationship precisely.

These are not abstract theological concepts. They are operating principles. When BTA Bill 210 requires cooperatives to adhere to Islamic principles, it is codifying practices that Bangsamoro communities already understand.

### 1.3.2 How Cooperatives Fulfill Maqasid al-Shari'ah

**Maqasid al-Shari'ah** refers to the objectives of Islamic law. BTA Bill 210, Section 7 requires cooperatives under the Shari'ah Governance Framework to fulfill these objectives.[^18] There are five:

**1. Preservation of faith (hifz al-din).** A cooperative that operates according to Islamic principles -- halal transactions, no riba, no gambling -- enables its members to earn a livelihood without compromising their faith. The cooperative becomes a space where economic activity and religious obligation align.

**2. Preservation of life (hifz al-nafs).** Cooperatives that improve food security, provide access to healthcare through credit facilities, and create sustainable livelihoods directly serve the preservation of life. A cassava producers' cooperative that stabilizes household income in Lanao del Sur protects families from hunger.

**3. Preservation of lineage (hifz al-nasl).** Economic stability enables families to educate their children, maintain family bonds, and plan for the future. When cooperatives lift members out of poverty, they strengthen the family unit and ensure that future generations inherit opportunity rather than deprivation.

**4. Preservation of intellect (hifz al-aql).** Cooperative Principle 5 -- Education, Training, and Information -- requires cooperatives to invest in their members' knowledge. A cooperative that trains its members in financial literacy, agricultural techniques, or business management directly serves this maqasid.

**5. Preservation of wealth (hifz al-mal).** This is the most direct connection. Cooperatives exist to generate, protect, and grow the wealth of their members through lawful means. The prohibition against riba (usury), maysir (gambling), and gharar (excessive speculation) ensures that wealth generation follows Shari'ah-compliant pathways.[^19]

### 1.3.3 Shari'ah Compliance in Practice

BTA Bill 210, Section 7 establishes specific Islamic principles that cooperatives under the Shari'ah Governance Framework must follow:[^20]

| Principle | What It Means for Your Cooperative |
|---|---|
| **Halal transactions** | Only permissible business activities, products, and services |
| **Legitimate wealth** | Earnings come from real economic activity, not speculation |
| **Asset-based transactions** | Every transaction is backed by actual goods or services |
| **Risk sharing** | Risks are distributed equitably among members -- not dumped on borrowers |
| **No riba (usury)** | No interest-based lending. Use profit-sharing arrangements instead |
| **No maysir (gambling)** | No lotteries, raffles, or games of chance |
| **No gharar (excessive uncertainty)** | Contracts must have clear, transparent terms |
| **No haram products** | No trade in alcohol, pork products, or other forbidden goods |

Section 9 of BTA Bill 210 further requires the CSEA to adopt a Shari'ah governance framework. This includes establishing a **Shari'ah committee** in cooperatives -- composed of at least three members competent in Shari'ah -- to ensure compliance.[^21]

For cooperatives seeking Shari'ah-compliant alternatives to conventional financial instruments, the framework provides clear substitutions:

| Conventional Instrument | Shari'ah-Compliant Alternative |
|---|---|
| Interest-bearing loans | **Murabaha** (cost-plus financing) |
| Interest-bearing savings | **Wadiah** (safekeeping) |
| Conventional insurance | **Takaful** (mutual guarantee) |
| Standard joint venture | **Musharakah** (profit-and-loss sharing partnership) |
| Investor-funded enterprise | **Mudarabah** (profit-sharing with one party providing capital, the other providing labor) |

You do not need to be a Shari'ah scholar to apply these principles. You need to understand that Bangsamoro cooperatives operate within a moral framework that conventional cooperative law does not address. BTA Bill 210 fills that gap.

## 1.4 The ICA Seven Cooperative Principles in Bangsamoro Context

The **International Cooperative Alliance (ICA)** adopted the current Statement on the Cooperative Identity at its Centenary Congress in Manchester in 1995.[^22] These seven principles define what makes a cooperative a cooperative -- as distinct from a corporation, an NGO, or a government program. Both RA 9520 and BTA Bill 210 explicitly adopt these principles.[^23]

The cooperative movement traces its roots to 1844, when 28 weavers in Rochdale, England -- facing poverty wages and exploitative merchants -- pooled their resources and opened a small store that sold food at honest prices and distributed profits to members based on their purchases.[^59] Those **Rochdale Pioneers** established the principles that, over 180 years later, still govern cooperatives worldwide. The Bangsamoro communities forming cooperatives today face different circumstances. But the core challenge is the same: ordinary people pooling resources to build economic power they cannot achieve alone.

Principles on paper accomplish nothing. Each principle below is paired with a concrete Bangsamoro example so you can see what it looks like in practice.

### 1.4.1 Each Principle Explained with Local Examples

**Principle 1: Voluntary and Open Membership**

> Cooperatives are voluntary organizations, open to all persons able to use their services and willing to accept the responsibilities of membership, without gender, social, racial, cultural, political or religious discrimination.[^24]

**What this means:** Nobody can be forced to join a cooperative. And nobody who is willing to accept the responsibilities of membership can be turned away based on gender, clan, political affiliation, or religious practice.

**Bangsamoro example:** A women's credit cooperative in Cotabato City accepts members from Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, and Christian settler communities. Membership is based on willingness to save regularly and participate in cooperative governance -- not on ethnicity, clan, or political alignment. If your cooperative restricts membership to one clan or one political faction, you are violating this principle.

---

**Principle 2: Democratic Member Control**

> Cooperatives are democratic organizations controlled by their members, who actively participate in setting their policies and making decisions. Men and women serving as elected representatives, directors or officers are accountable to the membership. In primary cooperatives, members have equal voting rights of one-member, one-vote.[^25]

**What this means:** Every member gets one vote regardless of how much capital they contributed. The chairperson who invested PHP 50,000 has the same voting power as the member who invested PHP 1,000. Officers serve at the pleasure of the membership and must account for their decisions.

**Bangsamoro example:** A rubber producers' cooperative in Basilan holds its annual general assembly every March. Every member receives notice 15 days in advance. At the assembly, members review the financial statements, elect board members by secret ballot, and approve the annual budget. The outgoing board presents a full accounting of how funds were used. No single family controls the board. If your cooperative's general assembly is a rubber stamp where the chairperson announces decisions already made, you are violating this principle.

---

**Principle 3: Member Economic Participation**

> Members contribute equitably to, and democratically control, the capital of their cooperative. At least part of that capital is the common property of the cooperative.[^26]

**What this means:** Members must contribute capital. Surpluses are allocated to three purposes: (1) reserves to develop the cooperative, (2) patronage refunds distributed in proportion to each member's transactions with the cooperative, and (3) other activities approved by the membership. Members receive limited compensation on capital -- cooperatives are not investment vehicles for generating returns on equity.

**Bangsamoro example:** A multi-purpose cooperative in Lanao del Sur requires each member to contribute PHP 500 to the share capital and save at least PHP 100 per month. At year-end, the net surplus is allocated: 10 percent to a reserve fund, 3 percent to the community development fund, 7 percent to an education and training fund, and the remainder as patronage refund to members based on their volume of transactions. A member who sold PHP 50,000 worth of cassava through the cooperative receives a larger patronage refund than a member who sold PHP 10,000 -- not because they are more important, but because they patronized the cooperative more.

---

**Principle 4: Autonomy and Independence**

> Cooperatives are autonomous, self-help organizations controlled by their members. If they enter into agreements with other organizations, including government, or raise capital from external sources, they shall do so on terms that ensure democratic control of their members and maintain cooperative autonomy.[^27]

**What this means:** Government can support cooperatives. Development partners can fund cooperatives. But no external entity should control cooperative decisions. If a government program provides capital on the condition that the cooperative buy inputs from a specific supplier or hire a specific manager, that condition violates cooperative autonomy.

**Bangsamoro example:** A seaweed farming cooperative in Tawi-Tawi receives technical assistance and seedling support from MAFAR. The cooperative accepts the assistance gratefully -- but its board independently decides which varieties to cultivate, which buyers to sell to, and how to allocate the cooperative's profits. MAFAR provides support. The members make decisions. These are separate functions.

---

**Principle 5: Education, Training, and Information**

> Cooperatives shall provide education and training for members, elected and appointed representatives, managers, and employees, so that they can contribute effectively and efficiently to the development of their cooperatives.[^28]

**What this means:** Cooperatives must invest in their people. This is not optional. RA 9520 requires cooperatives to allocate a portion of net surplus to an Education and Training Fund. BTA Bill 210 reinforces this requirement. A cooperative that does not train its officers in governance, its managers in financial management, and its members in their rights and responsibilities is a cooperative heading toward non-compliance.

**Bangsamoro example:** A coffee producers' cooperative in Sulu allocates 7 percent of its annual net surplus to education and training. Each year, it sends two board members to CSEA's officer training program and conducts a one-day financial literacy seminar for all members. New members complete a Pre-Membership Education Seminar (PMES) before admission. The cooperative also invites extension workers from MAFAR to train members on improved coffee processing techniques. Education is not a cost. It is an investment that keeps the cooperative functional.

---

**Principle 6: Cooperation Among Cooperatives**

> Cooperatives serve their members most effectively and strengthen the cooperative movement by working together through local, regional, national and international structures.[^29]

**What this means:** Cooperatives should not operate in isolation. Three or more primary cooperatives doing the same line of business can form a federation -- a secondary cooperative that provides shared services, market access, and collective bargaining power that no primary cooperative can achieve alone.

**Bangsamoro example:** Five seaweed cooperatives in the municipalities of Sitangkai and Sibutu in Tawi-Tawi form a municipal federation. The federation negotiates directly with carrageenan exporters in Cebu, achieving prices 15 percent higher than what individual cooperatives could negotiate. The federation also establishes a shared drying facility, reducing post-harvest losses. Alone, each cooperative is small. Together, they are the largest seaweed supplier in the municipality.

---

**Principle 7: Concern for Community**

> Cooperatives work for the sustainable development of their communities through policies approved by their members.[^30]

**What this means:** Cooperatives are not just businesses for their members. They have an obligation to contribute to the broader community. RA 9520 requires cooperatives to allocate a Community Development Fund (CDF) from net surplus for community projects.

**Bangsamoro example:** A multi-purpose cooperative in Maguindanao allocates 3 percent of its annual net surplus to its Community Development Fund. The members vote to use the fund for three projects: a barangay water system improvement, a back-to-school supply distribution for 100 students, and a tree-planting activity along the riverbank. The cooperative did not build these programs because someone told it to. The members decided -- through the general assembly -- that these were the community's priorities.

### 1.4.2 Moral Governance as the Eighth Principle

The seven ICA principles are universal. They apply to cooperatives from Rochdale to Cotabato. But BTA Bill 210 adds a dimension that no other cooperative code in the Philippines includes: **moral governance**.[^31]

Section 8 of BTA Bill 210 defines moral governance as:

> "An approach in governance of cooperatives and social enterprises that is guided by moral principles of enjoining what is right and forbidding what is wrong."[^32]

The Bill enumerates thirteen moral governance principles that cooperatives must observe:

| # | Principle | What It Requires of Your Cooperative |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Servitude** | Leaders serve members, not the other way around |
| 2 | **Purity and sincerity of intentions** | Decisions are made for the cooperative's benefit, not personal gain |
| 3 | **Honesty** | Financial statements reflect reality. Inventories are accurate. Reports are truthful |
| 4 | **Integrity** | Officers have no hidden financial interests in cooperative transactions |
| 5 | **Accountability** | Every peso is accounted for. Every decision is documented |
| 6 | **Excellence** | Operations meet the highest achievable standard |
| 7 | **Stewardship** | Resources are managed as a trust, not personal property |
| 8 | **Balance** | Economic goals and moral obligations receive equal weight |
| 9 | **Harmony** | Internal conflicts are resolved through consultation, not power plays |
| 10 | **Consultation** | Major decisions involve member input before board action |
| 11 | **Justice** | Benefits and burdens are distributed fairly among members |
| 12 | **Trust** | Members, officers, and staff operate in an environment of mutual confidence |
| 13 | **Sincerity of deeds** | Actions match stated intentions |

Section 8 also requires cooperatives to:[^33]

- **Abide by the laws** of the land and regulations issued by the CSEA and other regulatory bodies
- **Adopt their own code of moral principles** and ethical standards
- **Adopt rules, processes, and practices** completely devoid of graft and corruption

The Bangsamoro government's **12 Pillars of Moral Governance** provide the broader framework:[^34]

1. Integrity
2. Accountability
3. Transparency and honesty
4. Sense of patriotism
5. Striving for excellence
6. Cultural understanding and tolerance
7. Unwavering loyalty to God
8. Justice
9. Inclusivity
10. Objectivity
11. Dialogue and meaningful engagement
12. Balance between practical life and moral side

These pillars are not decorative. They are the governance standard that the Bangsamoro government applies to all its institutions -- including cooperatives. When you organize a cooperative in the Bangsamoro, you are building an institution that must meet both the ICA's universal cooperative principles and the Bangsamoro's moral governance framework.

This dual standard is not a burden. It is an advantage. A cooperative governed by both the seven ICA principles and the moral governance framework earns deeper trust from its members, its community, and its regulators than a cooperative governed by commercial efficiency alone.

## 1.5 The Legal Framework: Three Laws You Need to Know

Three pieces of legislation define the legal landscape for cooperatives in the Bangsamoro. One is current national law. One is pending Bangsamoro legislation. One is the Bangsamoro Organic Law that gives the Bangsamoro government authority over cooperatives in the first place.

You need to understand all three.

### 1.5.1 RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) -- What Applies Now

**Republic Act No. 9520**, the **Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008**, was signed into law on February 17, 2009.[^35] It amended and strengthened RA 6938 (the Cooperative Code of 1990). It is the law that currently governs cooperative registration, organization, governance, operations, and dissolution across the Philippines -- including the Bangsamoro.

Here is what you need to know about RA 9520:

**Structure.** The law has **19 chapters and 146 articles**.[^36] It covers everything from general concepts (Chapter I) to specific types of cooperatives: agrarian reform cooperatives (Chapter XI), cooperative banks (Chapter XII), insurance cooperatives (Chapter XIII), credit cooperatives (Chapter XV), and electric cooperatives (Chapter XVII).

**Declaration of Policy.** Article 2 declares that the State shall foster the creation and growth of cooperatives as **practical vehicles for self-reliance** and shall harness people power toward **economic development and social justice**.[^37]

**Cooperative Definition.** Under RA 9520, a cooperative is:

> An autonomous and duly registered association of persons, with a common bond of interest, who have voluntarily joined together to achieve their social, economic, and cultural needs and aspirations by making equitable contributions to the capital required, patronizing their products and services and accepting a fair share of the risks and benefits of the undertaking in accordance with universally accepted cooperative principles.[^38]

**The Seven Principles.** Article 4 explicitly adopts the ICA's seven cooperative principles as the guiding framework for all Philippine cooperatives.[^39]

**Regulatory Body.** Under RA 9520, the **Cooperative Development Authority (CDA)** is the national regulatory body. In the Bangsamoro, the **Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA)** performs this function -- a transition from the CDA's former BARMM operations to a Bangsamoro-mandated agency.[^40]

**What RA 9520 does not cover.** The law does not address Shari'ah compliance. It does not incorporate moral governance. It does not recognize social enterprises. It does not account for the unique circumstances of former combatant cooperatives. These gaps are precisely what BTA Bill 210 is designed to fill.

Until BTA Bill 210 is enacted, RA 9520 remains the operative law. Your cooperative must comply with its requirements. The practical chapters of this guidebook (Chapters 4-9) are grounded in RA 9520's provisions while anticipating BTA Bill 210's additions.

### 1.5.2 BTA Bill 210 (Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code) -- What Is Coming

**BTA Bill No. 210** is the proposed **Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code (BCSEC) of 2023**.[^41] It was introduced by MP Diamila Disimban-Ramos, MP Ishak Mastura, MP Lanang Ali Jr., MP Rasul Mitmug Jr., MP Amilbahar Mawallil, MP Baintan Ampatuan, MP Eddie Alih, MP Michael Midtimbang, MP Ali Sangki, and MP Suharto Ambolodto, with thirteen co-authors.[^42]

**Status.** The bill is pending before the Bangsamoro Parliament. The BDP 2023-2028 identifies its passage as essential for the operationalization of the cooperative sector.[^43]

**Structure.** The bill spans 70 pages across four books:[^44]

| Book | Title | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| **Book I** | General Provisions | Definitions, principles, CSEA mandate |
| **Book II** | Cooperative Development | Types, registration, governance, operations |
| **Book III** | Social Enterprise Development | SE definition, requirements, support mechanisms |
| **Book IV** | Miscellaneous and Final Provisions | Transitional rules, penalties, effectivity |

**What BTA Bill 210 adds beyond RA 9520:**

**1. Shari'ah compliance framework.** Section 7 establishes Islamic principles for cooperatives. Section 9 mandates a Shari'ah governance framework and requires cooperatives to establish Shari'ah committees.[^45] RA 9520 contains no equivalent provisions.

**2. Moral governance.** Section 8 embeds moral governance -- with thirteen named principles -- into cooperative law.[^46] No other Philippine cooperative legislation does this.

**3. CSEA authority.** The bill establishes the Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority as the Bangsamoro government's regulatory body for cooperatives and social enterprises, replacing the CDA's jurisdiction within the region.

**4. Social enterprise recognition.** Book III creates a legal framework for social enterprises -- organizations whose primary purpose is addressing social, cultural, or environmental challenges, with profit as a secondary consideration.[^47] RA 9520 does not cover social enterprises. National legislation on social enterprises remains absent.[^48]

**5. Bangsamoro identity.** Section 5 requires cooperatives to conduct affairs in accordance with **moral governance and the cultures, good values and experiences of the constituents of the region** as well as universally accepted cooperative principles.[^49] This is a Bangsamoro-specific provision with no national equivalent.

**Purpose statement.** Section 2 of BTA Bill 210 frames the cooperative sector's role expansively. The Code aims to:[^50]

- **Reduce poverty**, address inequality, and build peace
- Establish a comprehensive legal framework promoting **equity, social justice, and economic development**
- Encourage **self-reliance, mutual assistance, and pooling of resources**
- Empower local communities while preserving the **environment and cultural heritage** of the Bangsamoro people
- Serve as an instrument for **peacebuilding and conflict resolution**, uniting diverse groups through shared economic interests

That final purpose -- peacebuilding through shared economic interests -- is distinctive. No other cooperative legislation in the Philippines frames cooperatives as peace instruments. In the Bangsamoro, where decades of armed conflict have shaped every institution, this framing reflects reality.

### 1.5.3 The Bangsamoro Organic Law (BOL)

**Republic Act No. 11054**, the **Bangsamoro Organic Law**, is the foundational legislation that created the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM). It is the source of the Bangsamoro government's authority over cooperative development.

Three BOL provisions matter for your cooperative work:

**BOL Article V, Section 2 -- Enumerated Powers.** This section lists the matters over which the Bangsamoro government exercises authority. Item (k) explicitly includes **"cooperatives and social entrepreneurship"** as an enumerated power of the Bangsamoro government.[^51]

This is the constitutional basis. The Bangsamoro government does not regulate cooperatives by delegation or agreement. It exercises this power by organic law, granted through the national plebiscite that ratified the BOL.

**BOL Article XIII, Section 24 -- Trade and Industry.** This section states:

> "The Bangsamoro Government recognizes the private sector as a mover of trade, commerce, and industry. To achieve equity, social justice, and economic development, it shall encourage and support the building up of entrepreneurial capability in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region and shall recognize, promote, protect, and support the development of cooperatives and other medium, small, and micro enterprises. **It shall adopt and implement cooperative development policies and programs through the enactment of a Bangsamoro cooperative code.**"[^52]

Read that last sentence carefully. The BOL does not merely permit a Bangsamoro cooperative code. It directs the Bangsamoro government to enact one. BTA Bill 210 is the legislative response to this mandate.

**BOL Article XIII, Section 27 -- Cooperatives and Social Entrepreneurship.** This section provides:

> "The Bangsamoro Government shall recognize, protect, and promote the establishment of cooperative enterprises. It shall likewise encourage and promote social entrepreneurship as a means to engage the private sector to assist in the development, funding, and implementation of solutions to social, cultural, or environmental issues."[^53]

The section further mandates:

> "The Parliament shall pass laws to strengthen the growth and development of social enterprises in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region to achieve social justice, social equity, and economic efficiency."[^54]

Together, these three provisions establish a clear chain of authority:

1. The **BOL** grants the Bangsamoro government power over cooperatives (Art. V, Sec. 2)
2. The **BOL** mandates the enactment of a Bangsamoro cooperative code (Art. XIII, Sec. 24)
3. The **BOL** mandates legislation to strengthen social enterprises (Art. XIII, Sec. 27)
4. **BTA Bill 210** fulfills mandates #2 and #3 -- once enacted
5. **RA 9520** provides the current operative framework until BTA Bill 210 is passed

Your cooperative exists within this legal architecture. Understanding it protects you from two common mistakes: assuming that only national law applies (it does not -- the Bangsamoro has autonomous authority), and assuming that BTA Bill 210 is already law (it is not -- it remains pending).
