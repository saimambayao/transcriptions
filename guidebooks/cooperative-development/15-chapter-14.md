# Chapter 14 — Looking Forward: BTA Bill 210 and the Future of Bangsamoro Cooperatives

The law that governs your cooperative is about to change. BTA Bill 210 does not just rename the regulatory authority. It rewrites the rules for how cooperatives form, govern themselves, and grow in the Bangsamoro region. The bill introduces **moral governance mandates**, **Shari'ah compliance frameworks**, **social enterprise pathways**, and a **dedicated Shari'ah and Legal Division** within CSEA. If your cooperative currently operates under RA 9520, you need to understand what changes and how to prepare. This chapter gives you that preparation.

---

## 14.1 What BTA Bill 210 Changes

BTA Bill 210 — formally titled the **Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code (BCSEC)** — is a comprehensive four-book legislative framework filed in the BTA Parliament.[^1] It does not merely localize RA 9520. It creates an entirely new legal architecture for cooperatives and social enterprises in BARMM. The differences are structural, philosophical, and operational.

### 14.1.1 Key Differences from RA 9520

The table below maps the most significant changes between the current national cooperative law and the proposed Bangsamoro code.

| Feature | RA 9520 (Current Law) | BTA Bill 210 (Proposed) |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulatory body** | Cooperative Development Authority (CDA) | Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA), attached to the Office of the Chief Minister[^2] |
| **Scope** | Cooperatives only | Cooperatives **and** social enterprises under one code[^3] |
| **Governing principles** | Universal cooperative principles (7 ICA principles) | Universal cooperative principles **plus** moral governance, Shari'ah compliance, and local cultural values[^4] |
| **Shari'ah governance** | No provision | Mandatory Shari'ah governance framework; each cooperative must establish a **Shari'ah committee** of at least three members competent in Shari'ah[^5] |
| **Moral governance** | No provision | Cooperatives must adopt their own **code of moral principles and ethical standards**, with rules "completely devoid of graft and corruption"[^6] |
| **Islamic finance principles** | Not addressed | Explicit prohibition of **riba** (usury), **maysir** (gambling), and **gharar** (excessive uncertainty); promotion of halal transactions and risk-sharing[^7] |
| **CSEA organizational structure** | CDA has regional offices | CSEA has four divisions: Administrative and Finance, Cooperative Development, Social Enterprise, and **Shari'ah and Legal Division**[^8] |
| **Provincial presence** | CDA field offices | Mandatory **Provincial Offices** in Maguindanao, Lanao del Sur, Basilan, Sulu, and Tawi-Tawi; Cotabato City and SGA under Maguindanao[^9] |
| **Cooperative types** | 14 types (Art. 23) | **21 types** including Advocacy, Dairy, Education, Fishermen, Health Services, Housing, Water Service, and Workers cooperatives[^10] |
| **Minimum members** | 15 natural persons (Art. 10) | 15 natural persons — same threshold, but adds **PRS completion** as an explicit pre-condition for membership[^11] |
| **Paid-up capital minimum** | PHP 15,000 (Art. 14) | PHP 15,000, with CSEA authorized to **increase it every five years** upon consultation with the sector and BPDA[^12] |
| **Social enterprise framework** | No provision | Full Book III dedicated to **social enterprise registration, accreditation, tax incentives, and the RISE Program**[^13] |
| **LGU cooperative officers** | No mandate | LGUs must appoint **Cooperative Development Officers** per RA 11535, with additional functions defined by CSEA[^14] |
| **Tax exemptions** | Art. 60-61 provisions | Expanded exemptions: cooperatives exempt from **all local taxes** regardless of reserves; micro social enterprises exempt from income tax[^15] |
| **Inactive cooperative treatment** | Dissolution after 2 years of non-operation | Authority intervenes after **6 months** of non-operation to provide support; official inactivity after **1 year**[^16] |
| **Dispute resolution** | CDA mediation | Mandatory conciliation/mediation, then **voluntary arbitration** as exclusive mode — decisions appealable to Office of the President[^17] |
| **Information systems** | Manual processes | Mandatory **electronic registration and annual reporting information systems**[^18] |
| **Peacebuilding role** | Not addressed | Cooperatives explicitly serve as **pathways to reintegrate former combatants, surrenderees, and disaster-affected communities**[^19] |

Study this table carefully. Every row represents a change your cooperative must prepare for.

### 14.1.2 New Provisions: Moral Governance, Shari'ah Compliance, Social Enterprises

Three provisions in BTA Bill 210 have no equivalent in RA 9520. They define what makes the Bangsamoro cooperative framework unique.

**Moral Governance (Section 8, Book I)**

BTA Bill 210 defines **moral governance** as "an approach in governance of cooperatives and social enterprises that is guided by moral principles of enjoining what is right and forbidding what is wrong."[^20] The bill lists specific principles: **servitude, purity and sincerity of intentions and deeds, honesty, integrity, accountability, excellence, stewardship, balance, harmony, consultation, justice, and trust**. These are not aspirational language. CSEA will issue implementing guidelines that translate these principles into measurable governance standards.

What does this mean for your cooperative? Three things:

1. **Adopt a code of moral principles.** Your bylaws must include your cooperative's own ethical standards. Draft these before the bill takes effect. Do not wait for CSEA to issue templates.
2. **Adopt anti-corruption rules.** The bill requires rules, processes, and practices "completely devoid of graft and corruption."[^21] Review your procurement, lending, and disbursement procedures now.
3. **Align your operations with the 12 Pillars of Moral Governance.** The BDP 2023-2028 identifies twelve pillars — integrity, accountability, transparency and honesty, sense of patriotism, striving for excellence, cultural understanding and tolerance, unwavering loyalty to God, justice, inclusivity, objectivity, dialogue and meaningful engagement, and balance between practical life and moral side.[^22] Use these as your checklist.

**Shari'ah Compliance (Section 9, Book I)**

BTA Bill 210 requires cooperatives to establish a **Shari'ah governance framework**. This means your cooperative must:

- Establish a **Shari'ah committee** composed of at least three members with competence in Shari'ah.[^23]
- Ensure all financial transactions comply with Islamic principles — **no riba, no maysir, no gharar**.[^24]
- Use Shari'ah-compliant alternatives for conventional financial instruments. Replace interest-bearing loans with **murabaha** (cost-plus financing). Replace conventional insurance with **takaful** (mutual guarantee). Replace speculative investments with **musharakah** (partnership) or **mudarabah** (profit-sharing).[^25]

The bill also creates a **Shari'ah and Legal Division** within CSEA that will conduct **Shari'ah risk assessments, compliance audits, and research** to ensure cooperatives adhere to Islamic principles.[^26] Expect CSEA to publish Shari'ah compliance guidelines within the first year of the code's effectivity.

**Social Enterprises (Book III)**

Book III of BTA Bill 210 creates an entirely new legal category: the **social enterprise**. A social enterprise is "any business organization whose primary purpose is addressing a social, cultural, or environmental challenge where creating profit for owners or shareholders is secondary."[^27] Social enterprises may be organized as stock corporations, non-stock corporations, partnerships, sole proprietorships, or cooperatives.[^28]

This matters because your cooperative can **add social enterprise status** to unlock additional benefits — tax exemptions, preferential government procurement, and access to the **Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Fund (BSEF)**.[^29] Section 14.3 of this chapter explains how.

## 14.2 Preparing Your Cooperative for the Transition

The transition from RA 9520 to the BCSEC will not happen overnight. But it will happen within a defined window. Prepare now.

### 14.2.1 The Transitory Provisions (BTA Bill 210, Book IV)

Section 12 of Book IV, Chapter III contains the **transitory provisions** that govern how existing cooperatives move into the new framework.[^30] Here is what the bill says, in plain language:

**Recognition of existing registration.** All cooperatives registered and confirmed with the CDA under RA 9520 and RA 11364 (Electric Cooperatives) are **deemed registered** under the new code.[^31] You do not need to re-organize. Your cooperative's legal personality carries over automatically.

**But you must act within one year.** The bill requires every existing cooperative to submit the following documents to the nearest CSEA office **within one year from the effectivity of the code**:

1. A copy of your **Certificate of Registration (COR)**
2. Your **Articles of Cooperation**
3. Your **Bylaws**
4. Your **latest audited financial statements**

**The consequence of inaction is severe.** If you fail to submit these documents within the one-year window, your registration is **deemed cancelled motu proprio** — meaning CSEA cancels it automatically, without a hearing.[^32] Your cooperative loses its legal personality.

**New Certificate of Registration.** After you submit your documents, CSEA will issue a **new COR** under the Bangsamoro code. This new COR replaces your CDA-issued certificate.

**New tax exemption certificate.** After receiving your new COR, secure a **new Certificate of Tax Exemption** from the nearest Bureau of Internal Revenue office. This exemption is valid for five years from the date of issue.[^33]

**Compromise on unpaid assessments.** If your cooperative has unpaid tax assessments from before the code, the bill provides for a **compromise settlement on terms favorable to the cooperative**. The BIR and CSEA will jointly issue regulations on this compromise within 90 days from the code's effectivity.[^34]

### 14.2.2 Re-Registration Requirements and the Compliance Window

Do not treat the one-year compliance window as a distant deadline. Treat it as a 12-month action plan. Here is your **Transition Preparation Checklist**:

**Months 1-3: Document Assembly**

- [ ] Locate your original **COR** issued by CDA. If lost, request a certified copy immediately.
- [ ] Gather your current **Articles of Cooperation**. Verify they reflect all amendments filed with CDA.
- [ ] Gather your current **Bylaws**. Check that they include conciliation and mediation mechanisms as required by the new code.
- [ ] Commission your **audited financial statements** for the most recent fiscal year. Engage your CPA early — demand will spike as all 12,214 registered cooperatives seek audits within the same period.

**Months 4-6: Gap Analysis**

- [ ] Compare your Bylaws against BTA Bill 210 requirements. Identify gaps in **moral governance provisions**, **Shari'ah compliance clauses**, and **cooperative officer training requirements**.
- [ ] Assess whether your financial products and services comply with **Islamic finance principles** — no riba, no maysir, no gharar.
- [ ] Evaluate your current **board composition** against the new code's qualifications and disqualifications for directors.
- [ ] Review your **membership provisions** to ensure alignment with the new code's limit of two cooperative memberships per person.[^35]

**Months 7-9: Amendments and Training**

- [ ] Draft amendments to your **Articles of Cooperation** and **Bylaws** to incorporate moral governance provisions, Shari'ah compliance clauses, and updated dispute resolution mechanisms.
- [ ] Call a **Special General Assembly** to approve the amendments by two-thirds vote of all voting members.[^36]
- [ ] Begin identifying and training potential **Shari'ah committee members**. You need at least three persons competent in Shari'ah.
- [ ] Send your board members and officers for **CSEA-accredited training** on the new code.

**Months 10-12: Submission and Certification**

- [ ] Submit your documents to the **nearest CSEA provincial office**: COR, Articles of Cooperation, Bylaws, and audited financial statements.
- [ ] Receive your **new COR** from CSEA.
- [ ] Secure your **new Certificate of Tax Exemption** from BIR.
- [ ] If your cooperative has unpaid assessments, file for **compromise settlement** under the transitory provisions.
- [ ] Update your records — bank accounts, government registrations, and partner contracts — to reflect your new COR number.

**Do not wait for the bill to pass.** Start the document assembly phase now. Your COR and Articles of Cooperation should already be in order under RA 9520. If they are not, fix them today. Every month you delay narrows the compliance window when the clock starts.

## 14.3 Social Enterprise as a Growth Path

BTA Bill 210 does not just regulate cooperatives. It opens a **second pathway** — social enterprise — that cooperatives can use to expand their mission and access new resources.

### 14.3.1 When to Add Social Enterprise Status

Under Book III of BTA Bill 210, your cooperative can be **qualified by CSEA as a social enterprise** if it meets the following conditions:[^37]

1. Your primary purpose is **addressing a social, cultural, or environmental challenge**. Profit is secondary.
2. You invest a **substantive part of your net revenues** to sustain the fulfillment of your social or environmental mission.
3. You provide **transformational services** for the well-being of the marginalized sector you serve or society at large.
4. Your governing documents include a **clear social purpose**.
5. You **re-invest a significant proportion of profits** back into the business for social, cultural, or environmental purposes.

The **social enterprise status is valid for three years**.[^38] During this period, you must submit **annual activity and social-impact reports** to CSEA demonstrating that you continue to meet SE criteria. Failure to renew at the end of the three-year period results in removal from the CSEA Registry.

**When should your cooperative pursue SE status?** Consider adding it when:

- Your cooperative already operates programs that serve marginalized communities — livelihood projects for former combatants, food security programs, environmental conservation activities, or educational services.
- Your cooperative wants access to the **Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Fund (BSEF)** for grants.[^39]
- Your cooperative wants **preferential treatment in government procurement** — qualified SEs have a preferential right in procurement processes across all BARMM government branches, agencies, and LGUs.[^40]
- Your cooperative seeks **tax incentives** — micro SEs are exempt from income tax, and other SEs enjoy a three-year regional tax holiday.[^41]
- Your cooperative wants to participate in the **RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program** for capability building, market access, and research support.[^42]

**The application process is straightforward.** CSEA registers and accredits social enterprises through guidelines it will issue under the new code.[^43] Prepare your social impact documentation now. Measure the communities you serve. Count the families whose incomes you have increased. Document the environmental outcomes of your programs. When CSEA opens the SE accreditation window, you want to be ready on day one.

### 14.3.2 The BCSED and RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program

Two institutions created by BTA Bill 210 will support social enterprise development in BARMM. Understand what they offer.

**The Bangsamoro Center for Social Enterprise Development (BCSED)**

The **BCSED** is a unit under CSEA that performs six core functions:[^44]

1. **Capability training and education** through the SE Capability Building and Sustainability Program
2. **Research and development** — equipping SEs with innovative approaches to improve access to basic social services for marginalized communities
3. **Marketing assistance** — managing a Social Enterprise Marketing Assistance Program (SMAP) to generate the highest possible income for SEs
4. **Project implementation** — managing capability-building projects approved by CSEA
5. **Project supervision** — overseeing capability-building projects implemented through contracted Social Enterprise Intermediaries
6. **International benchmarking** — researching policies and programs from other countries for best practices

If your cooperative obtains SE status, the BCSED becomes your primary support center. Use its training programs. Access its marketing networks. Tap its research for evidence-based business decisions.

**The RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program**

**RISE** stands for **Resilient and Inclusive Social Enterprises for Peace and Sustainable Development**.[^45] It is the comprehensive capacity-building program mandated by BTA Bill 210 to strengthen social enterprises across BARMM. The program has three components:

1. **SE Capability Building and Sustainability Program.** Covers training in social entrepreneurship, institutional strengthening, human resource skills, business planning, impact measurement, accounting systems, technology intervention, market studies, product development, trade fairs, and policy advocacy.[^46]

2. **Social Enterprise Research and Development System.** Developed jointly by CSEA, BPDA, the Department Academy of Bangsamoro, MOST, and MTIT. Provides studies on economic opportunities, equips SEs with appropriate technologies, and develops innovative approaches to basic social services.[^47]

3. **Social Enterprise Marketing Infrastructure Development.** Includes a **SE Marketing Assistance Program (SMAP)** that matches supply with demand in domestic and foreign markets, and a **SE Marketing Information Network (SMIN)** that provides business information, market intelligence, and trade leads.[^48]

**Who governs the RISE program?** The **Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Council (BSEC)** — an alliance of SE leaders composed of nine provincial and city SE representatives, two SE intermediaries, two impact investors, and representatives from nine BARMM ministries and commissions.[^49] The BSEC monitors RISE implementation, assesses SE progress, and provides strategic advice to the Bangsamoro government.

**How does your cooperative access RISE?** Through CSEA. Once qualified as a social enterprise, your cooperative is eligible for all RISE program components. Start by engaging your nearest CSEA provincial office.

## 14.4 Connecting to BDP 2023-2028 Goals

Your cooperative does not operate in isolation. It operates within the **Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028** — the roadmap that defines what the Bangsamoro government wants to achieve in six years.[^50] Understanding where cooperatives fit in the BDP helps you align your operations with government priorities, access development programs, and measure your social impact.

### 14.4.1 How Your Cooperative Contributes to Goal 2

The BDP 2023-2028 organizes development around six goals. Cooperatives fall under **Goal 2: Equitable, competitive, and robust economy**.[^51] Within Goal 2, the cooperative sector sits under **Sectoral Objective 2.5: Functional cooperatives and social enterprises increased**, with two sub-objectives:

- **2.5.1** — Profitability of cooperatives increased
- **2.5.2** — Established social enterprises increased[^52]

Two priority strategies drive the cooperative sub-sector:

**Strategy 21** — Ensure profitability and functionality of cooperatives through:
- Sufficient technical and financial assistance
- Institutionalization of monitoring and evaluation systems
- Community promotion of cooperatives
- Capacitation of human resources[^53]

**Strategy 22** — Institutionalize enabling policies for the establishment of social enterprises in BARMM[^54]

Two priority programs operationalize these strategies:

1. **Development of Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Program** — includes the **PESO Project** (Profitability and Equity Support Oriented) for financial and technical assistance, and the **LAKAS SE Program** (Lakbay Aral sa Social Enterprise) for SE learning.[^55]

2. **Combatant Transitional Program** — includes the **STOCK Project** (Strengthening of Technical and Operational Knowledge) for capacity building of former combatant cooperatives, and the **Cooperative Financial and Material Support Project** for direct financial and material assistance.[^56]

**What does this mean for your cooperative?** Align your annual plans with these strategies and programs. When you apply for CSEA technical assistance, frame your request in BDP language. When you report your progress, connect your outcomes to Objective 2.5. When you measure success, track the metrics that matter to the BDP — profitability, functionality, and compliance.

### 14.4.2 Measuring Social Impact and Reporting to CSEA

BTA Bill 210 introduces **social audit** as a formal process. The bill defines social audit as "a procedure wherein the cooperative assesses its social impact and ethical performance vis-a-vis its stated mission, vision, goals and code of social responsibility."[^57] CSEA will establish the standards for this process in consultation with the cooperative sector.

Start measuring your social impact now. You do not need to wait for CSEA guidelines. Track these indicators:

**Economic impact:**
- Number of members whose household income increased by at least 20% since joining
- Total value of loans disbursed to members annually
- Number of jobs created or sustained through cooperative operations
- Value of products marketed collectively versus individually

**Social impact:**
- Number of members who completed cooperative education programs
- Number of women in leadership positions (board, committees, management)
- Number of youth members (under 30) actively participating
- Number of former combatants successfully reintegrated through cooperative membership

**Community impact:**
- Value of community development fund allocations and projects completed
- Number of households in your barangay served by cooperative programs
- Number of partnerships with LGUs, schools, or other cooperatives
- Contribution to BDP targets — how your cooperative's outcomes map to Goal 2

**Shari'ah compliance:**
- Percentage of financial transactions reviewed by your Shari'ah committee
- Number of Shari'ah non-compliance findings and corrective actions taken
- Status of transition from conventional to Shari'ah-compliant financial products

**How to build your social impact reporting system:**

1. **Designate a social audit officer.** Assign one board member or committee member to oversee social impact data collection. This person coordinates with your treasurer, secretary, and committee chairs to gather the data above.
2. **Collect data quarterly.** Do not wait until year-end. Quarterly collection prevents data loss and gives your board timely information to adjust programs.
3. **Use a simple tracking sheet.** Create a spreadsheet with four tabs — economic, social, community, and Shari'ah compliance. Record raw numbers each quarter. Calculate year-over-year trends at fiscal year-end.
4. **Present social impact at your General Assembly.** Members deserve to see not only financial statements but also the social outcomes their cooperative produces. Include a one-page social impact summary in your annual report.
5. **Share your data with CSEA.** When CSEA issues its reporting templates, you will already have the data. Cooperatives that demonstrate measurable social impact will have a stronger case for CSEA support, RISE program access, and social enterprise qualification.

The BDP 2023-2028 also calls for **cooperation among cooperatives** as a strategy to strengthen the sector.[^53b] Connect with other cooperatives in your province. Share your social impact methods. Compare results. A cooperative movement is not built by one cooperative alone — it is built by cooperatives that learn from each other.

## 14.5 The Vision: A Vibrant Bangsamoro Cooperative Movement

The numbers tell a story of unrealized potential. The path forward is clear. Your cooperative is part of the solution.

### 14.5.1 From 11.66 Percent to 50 Percent Compliance

Here is the baseline. Out of **12,214 registered cooperatives** in BARMM from 2010 to 2020, only **1,424 — or 11.66 percent — were compliant**.[^58] That means 88.34 percent of registered cooperatives could not secure their certificate of compliance. The BDP 2023-2028 sets a target of **50 percent compliance**.[^59]

Why is the compliance rate so low? The BDP identifies three root causes:

1. **Policies aligned with the old CDA system.** The transition from CDA to CSEA left a regulatory gap. Cooperatives formed by former combatants — a priority constituency — found the old compliance requirements difficult to navigate.[^60]

2. **The pending cooperative code.** Without the BCSEC, CSEA operates with borrowed legal authority. Strategic planning and programming are constrained by the absence of a dedicated Bangsamoro legal framework for cooperatives.[^61]

3. **Inadequate LGU participation.** Most LGUs in BARMM have **no appointed Municipal Cooperative Officers**. Regional cooperative officers cannot effectively coordinate at the local level without LGU counterparts. CSEA's provincial extension offices need to be fully established.[^62]

BTA Bill 210 addresses all three root causes. The new code replaces CDA policies with a **Bangsamoro-specific framework**. It gives CSEA a **clear legal mandate** for strategic planning. It requires LGUs to appoint **Cooperative Development Officers** and allocate 5 percent of their funds for SE initiatives.[^63]

But legislation alone does not produce compliance. Your cooperative produces compliance.

Think about the math. The target is 50 percent of registered cooperatives compliant. That means roughly **6,100 cooperatives** must secure their Certificate of Compliance — an increase of approximately **4,676 cooperatives** from the 2020 baseline of 1,424. This is an ambitious target. It requires a four-fold increase in the number of compliant cooperatives within the BDP period.

Every time you submit your annual reports on time, you add to the numerator. Every time you secure your Certificate of Compliance, you move the region closer to the 50 percent target. Every time you help a neighboring cooperative understand the new requirements, you multiply the effect.

The cooperative sector also serves a **peacebuilding function** that no other economic institution can replicate. The BDP's Combatant Transitional Program channels former combatants into cooperative enterprises. BTA Bill 210 explicitly mandates CSEA to "promote and strengthen cooperatives and social enterprises as pathways to reintegrate former combatants, surrenderees, and disaster-affected communities."[^62b] When your cooperative welcomes former combatants as members, provides them livelihood, and trains them in democratic governance, you are not just building an enterprise. You are building peace.


### 14.5.2 Your Role in Building the Movement

This guidebook began with a simple premise: cooperatives work when members understand what they are building together. Thirteen chapters later, you have the knowledge. You understand how to form a cooperative, register it with CSEA, govern it through democratic processes, manage its finances, maintain compliance, train your members, grow your operations, resolve disputes, and connect to the CSEA ecosystem.

Now you have one more thing: a view of what comes next.

BTA Bill 210 will create a cooperative framework unlike any other in the Philippines. It is the only cooperative law that integrates **moral governance**, **Shari'ah compliance**, and **social enterprise** into a single code. It reflects the Bangsamoro people's aspiration for an economy grounded in justice, ethics, and community.

But a law on paper is just paper. The cooperative movement lives in what you do tomorrow.

**If you are a cooperative leader**, call a board meeting. Brief your directors on the coming changes. Start the gap analysis. Commission your audited financial statements. Identify your Shari'ah committee candidates. Do not wait for the bill to pass — prepare now so you can comply on day one.

**If you are a cooperative member**, attend your next General Assembly. Ask your board what preparations they are making for BTA Bill 210. Volunteer for committees. Complete your cooperative education requirements. Your participation is not optional — it is the foundation of democratic member control.

**If you are forming a new cooperative**, design it for the new code from the start. Build moral governance into your Articles of Cooperation. Include Shari'ah compliance in your Bylaws. Structure your business plan around the social enterprise pathway. Register with CSEA, not CDA. You have the advantage of building on the new foundation rather than retrofitting the old one.

**If you are a Municipal Cooperative Officer or LGU official**, champion the cooperative agenda in your municipality. Allocate the 5 percent of LGU funds mandated for SE initiatives. Coordinate with your CSEA provincial office. Compile the data that CSEA needs to track compliance progress. You are the bridge between regional policy and community practice.

The BDP 2023-2028 envisions an **empowered, cohesive, and progressive Bangsamoro**.[^64] Cooperatives are the vehicle that turns that vision into household-level reality. Every peso saved, every product marketed collectively, every member trained, every community project completed — these are the building blocks of an equitable, competitive, and robust Bangsamoro economy.

The Bangsamoro people have built something no other region in the Philippines has attempted: a cooperative framework that integrates universal cooperative principles with **moral governance**, **Islamic finance**, and **social enterprise** in a single code. This is not just legislation. It is an expression of identity — an economy built on the values of justice, consultation, stewardship, and trust that define the Bangsamoro.

The target is 50 percent compliance. The ambition is 100 percent functionality. The method is collective action rooted in moral governance and democratic principles.

You have read this guidebook. You have the tools. Now act.

Call the board meeting. Draft the amendments. Train the Shari'ah committee. File the audited financial statements. Submit your documents to CSEA. Secure your new Certificate of Registration. Then help the cooperative next door do the same.

Your cooperative is not waiting for the future. Your cooperative is building it.
