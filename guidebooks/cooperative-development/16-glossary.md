# Glossary of Cooperative and Social Enterprise Terms

This glossary defines key terms used throughout this guidebook. Terms defined in law cite their legal source. Terms are grouped by domain, then listed alphabetically within each group.

---

## Cooperative Terms

**Advocacy Cooperative** --- A primary cooperative that promotes and advocates cooperativism among its members and the public through socially-oriented projects, education and training, research and communication, and other similar activities to reach out to its intended beneficiaries.[^1]

**Agrarian Reform Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized by marginal farmers, the majority of whom are agrarian reform beneficiaries, for the purpose of developing an appropriate system of land tenure, land development, land consolidation, or land management in areas covered by agrarian reform.[^2]

**Annual Audit** --- The mandatory financial, performance, and social audit to which all registered cooperatives are subject each year. The financial audit must be conducted by an independent external auditor accredited by the Authority.[^3]

**Articles of Cooperation (AOC)** --- The founding document of a cooperative, equivalent to articles of incorporation for a corporation. Contains the cooperative's name, purpose, principal office, area of operation, membership requirements, and capital structure. Must be signed by all organizers and notarized before filing with the Authority.[^4]

**Associate Member** --- A cooperative member who has not yet met all requirements for regular membership. An associate member has no right to vote or be voted upon and is entitled only to such rights and privileges as the bylaws provide.[^5]

**Audit Committee** --- A mandatory committee elected by the general assembly, directly accountable to the general assembly. It has the power and duty to continuously monitor the adequacy and effectiveness of the cooperative's management control system and to audit performance.[^6]

**Board of Directors (BOD)** --- The body entrusted with the strategic planning, direction-setting, and policy-formulation activities of the cooperative, composed of not fewer than five and not more than fifteen members elected by the general assembly for a term of two years.[^7]

**Bond of Membership** --- The common interest that binds members of a cooperative together. May be geographic (residing or working in the same area), occupational (working in the same trade or profession), or associational (belonging to the same organization).[^8]

**Bylaws** --- A set of rules and procedures that govern the internal operations and management of a cooperative, including membership qualifications, rights and duties, meeting procedures, officer duties, share capital rules, and dispute mechanisms.[^9]

**Capital Build-Up Program** --- A reasonable and realistic program required in every cooperative's bylaws to allow the continuing growth of members' investment in their cooperative as their economic conditions improve.[^10]

**Certificate of Compliance (COC)** --- A certificate issued by the Authority to a registered cooperative after one year of operation and upon compliance with all requirements set forth by the Authority. Cooperatives that fail to operate for one year are denied COC issuance and rendered officially inactive.[^11]

**Certificate of Registration (COR)** --- The document issued by the Authority under its official seal that serves as conclusive evidence that the cooperative is duly registered and has acquired juridical personality, unless such registration has been cancelled.[^12]

**Chairperson** --- The presiding officer of the board of directors, elected from among the directors. Presides at general assembly and board meetings, executes authorized documents, and represents the cooperative in official functions.[^13]

**Committee** --- A group of members of a cooperative entrusted with specific functions and responsibilities under the bylaws or by resolution of the general assembly or the board of directors. Mandatory committees include audit, election, mediation and conciliation, and ethics.[^14]

**Common Bond of Interest** --- The shared characteristic that qualifies persons to join the same cooperative, such as residence or workplace in the same area, membership in the same organization, or employment in the same trade. Required of all organizers and members of a primary cooperative.[^15]

**Community Development Fund (CDF)** --- A mandatory fund representing not less than three percent of net surplus, used for projects or activities that benefit the community where the cooperative operates.[^16]

**Consolidation** --- The process by which two or more cooperatives form an entirely new cooperative, with the constituent cooperatives ceasing to exist. Requires approval by three-fourths vote of all members with voting rights in each constituent cooperative.[^17]

**Consumer Cooperative** --- A cooperative whose primary purpose is to procure and distribute commodities to members and non-members.[^18]

**Cooperative** --- An autonomous and duly registered association of persons, with a common bond of interest, who have voluntarily joined together to achieve their social, economic, and cultural needs and aspirations by making equitable contributions to the capital required, patronizing their products and services, and accepting a fair share of the risks and benefits of the undertaking in accordance with moral governance and universally accepted cooperative principles.[^19]

**Cooperative Bank** --- A cooperative organized for the primary purpose of providing a wide range of financial services to cooperatives and their members.[^20]

**Cooperative Development Authority (CDA)** --- The national government agency in charge of the registration, regulation, and development of cooperatives outside the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^21]

**Cooperative Development Fund** --- See Community Development Fund.

**Cooperative Development Officer (CDO)** --- A municipal, city, or provincial government official responsible for promoting, organizing, registering, and providing business advice, management guidance, and monitoring cooperatives in the local government unit's jurisdiction, pursuant to Republic Act No. 11535.[^22]

**Cooperative Education and Training Fund (CETF)** --- A mandatory fund of not more than ten percent of net surplus, allocated for training, development, and cooperative activities geared toward the growth of the cooperative movement. Half is spent by the cooperative; the other half may be remitted to a union or federation.[^23]

**Cooperative Union** --- An organization of registered cooperatives and federations at the provincial, city, regional, or national level, engaged primarily in non-business activities such as advocacy, representation, education, and technical assistance.[^24]

**Credit Cooperative** --- A cooperative that promotes and undertakes savings and lending services among its members, generating a common pool of funds to provide financial assistance and other related financial services for productive and provident purposes.[^25]

**Dairy Cooperative** --- A cooperative whose members are engaged in the production of fresh milk, which may be processed and marketed as dairy products.[^26]

**Democratic Member Control** --- The second internationally recognized cooperative principle, providing that cooperatives are democratic organizations controlled by their members, who actively participate in setting policies and making decisions. In primary cooperatives, each member has one vote regardless of capital contribution.[^27]

**Dissolution** --- The termination of a cooperative's existence, which may occur voluntarily (with or without creditors affected), involuntarily (by court order for law violation or insolvency), or by order of the Authority (for fraud, illegal purpose, or failure to operate).[^28]

**Donated Capital** --- Capital derived from subsidies, donations, legacies, grants, aids, and other assistance from local or foreign institutions. This capital cannot be divided into individual share capital holdings and, upon dissolution, is subject to escheat.[^29]

**Economic Survey** --- A mandatory document filed with the Authority by persons intending to form a cooperative, describing the proposed cooperative's structure, purposes, area of operation, size of membership, and other pertinent data including an economic feasibility study.[^30]

**Education Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized for the primary purpose of owning and operating licensed educational institutions.[^31]

**Education, Training, and Information** --- The fifth internationally recognized cooperative principle, requiring cooperatives to provide education and training for members, elected representatives, managers, and employees so they can contribute effectively to the cooperative's development.[^32]

**Election Committee** --- A mandatory committee elected by the general assembly, responsible for prescribing election rules, screening candidates, conducting elections, canvassing results, and resolving election protests.[^33]

**Electric Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized for the primary purpose of undertaking power generation (utilizing renewable energy sources including hybrid systems), acquisition, and operation of sub-transmission or distribution to its household members.[^34]

**Ethics Committee** --- A committee appointed by the board of directors, responsible for enforcing the cooperative's code of ethics and recommending sanctions for violations.[^35]

**Federation of Cooperatives** --- A secondary cooperative composed of three or more primary cooperatives of the same type of services, organized at the municipal, provincial, city, or economic zone level, registered with the Authority to undertake business activities in support of its member cooperatives.[^36]

**Financial Service Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized for the primary purpose of engaging in savings and credit services and other financial services, operating as a non-bank financial institution.[^37]

**Fishermen Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized by marginalized fishermen in localities whose products are marketed either as fresh or processed products.[^38]

**General Assembly (GA)** --- The full membership of the cooperative duly assembled for the purpose of exercising all rights and performing all obligations pertaining to cooperatives. It is the highest policy-making body of the cooperative. For cooperatives with numerous and dispersed membership, the GA may be composed of elected delegates.[^39]

**General Manager** --- The chief executive hired by the board of directors, responsible for the day-to-day operations and management of the cooperative. Cannot simultaneously serve as a member of the board of directors.[^40]

**Governance and Management Audit** --- The process of auditing the efficiency and effectiveness of cooperative performance by determining the presence of documents needed to be maintained in the operation and management of its business.[^41]

**Health Services Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized for the primary purpose of providing medical, dental, and other health services.[^42]

**Housing Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized to assist or provide access to housing for the benefit of its regular members who actively participate in the savings program for housing. It is co-owned and controlled by its members.[^43]

**Insolvency** --- The condition in which a cooperative fails or is unable to fulfill its obligations to creditors. An insolvent cooperative may apply for remedies under the Financial Rehabilitation and Insolvency Act (FRIA) of 2010.[^44]

**Insurance Cooperative** --- A cooperative engaged in the business of insuring the life and property of cooperatives and their members.[^45]

**Interest on Share Capital** --- A limited return paid to members on their paid-up share capital contributions, distinct from patronage refunds. The rate cannot exceed the normal rate of return on investments.[^46]

**Laboratory Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized by minors, which must be affiliated with a registered cooperative and is governed by special guidelines promulgated by the Authority.[^47]

**Limited Liability** --- The principle that a member's liability for the debts of the cooperative is limited to the extent of his or her share capital contribution. No personal liability exists beyond the subscription.[^48]

**Liquidation** --- The process of winding up a dissolved cooperative's affairs, including settling debts, disposing of assets, and distributing remaining property. A dissolved cooperative continues to exist for three years after dissolution for the purpose of liquidation.[^49]

**Marketing Cooperative** --- A cooperative that engages in the supply of production inputs to members and markets their products.[^50]

**Mediation and Conciliation Committee** --- A mandatory committee appointed by the board of directors to hear complaints and mediate disputes among members, officers, directors, and committee members within the cooperative.[^51]

**Member Economic Participation** --- The third internationally recognized cooperative principle, requiring that members contribute equitably to, and democratically control, the capital of their cooperative. Members receive limited compensation on capital subscribed as a condition of membership and allocate surpluses for reserves, patronage, and other approved activities.[^52]

**Members** --- Persons, either natural or juridical, adhering to the principles set forth in the Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code and in the articles of cooperation, who have been admitted by the cooperative as members.[^53]

**Merger** --- The process by which one cooperative absorbs another, with the surviving cooperative acquiring all assets and assuming all liabilities of the absorbed cooperative. Requires three-fourths vote of all members with voting rights in each constituent cooperative.[^54]

**Moral Governance** --- An approach to governance of cooperatives and social enterprises guided by moral principles of enjoining what is right and forbidding what is wrong, including servitude, purity of intentions, honesty, integrity, accountability, excellence, stewardship, balance, harmony, consultation, justice, and trust.[^55]

**Multipurpose Cooperative** --- A cooperative that combines two or more of the business activities of different cooperative types. A single-purpose cooperative may transform into a multipurpose only after at least two years of operations and with proof of economic viability.[^56]

**Net Surplus** --- The total income of a cooperative less all expenses for the fiscal year. Not construed as profit but as an excess of payments made by members, which is deemed returned to them when distributed as prescribed by law.[^57]

**Officers of the Cooperative** --- The chairperson, vice chairperson, secretary, treasurer, members of the board of directors, members of the different committees, general manager or chief executive officer, and members holding other positions as provided in the cooperative's bylaws.[^58]

**One Member, One Vote** --- The democratic voting rule in primary cooperatives whereby each member has exactly one vote, regardless of the amount of share capital contributed.[^59]

**Patronage Refund** --- A portion of net surplus distributed to members in proportion to their individual patronage of the cooperative's business during the fiscal year. Distinct from interest on share capital, which is based on capital contribution.[^60]

**Pre-Registration Seminar (PRS)** --- A mandatory educational seminar that all prospective organizers and members of a cooperative must complete before the cooperative may be registered. Conducted by the Authority or by accredited institutions.[^61]

**Primary Cooperative** --- A cooperative whose members are natural persons. Requires at least fifteen Filipino citizens of legal age with a common bond of interest to organize.[^62]

**Producers Cooperative** --- A cooperative formed and operated by its members to undertake the production and processing of raw materials or goods produced by its members into finished or processed products for sale by the cooperative.[^63]

**Quorum** --- The minimum number of members required to be present for a meeting to validly transact business. For general assembly meetings, at least twenty-five percent of all members entitled to vote. For board meetings, a majority of directors.[^64]

**Regular Member** --- A member who has complied with all membership requirements and is entitled to all rights and privileges of membership, including the right to vote and be voted upon.[^65]

**Representative Assembly** --- The full membership of a body of representatives elected by each sector, chapter, or district of the cooperative, duly assembled to exercise powers lawfully delegated by the general assembly in accordance with the bylaws.[^66]

**Reserve Fund** --- A mandatory fund of at least ten percent of net surplus (fifty percent during the first five years of operation), used for the stability of the cooperative and to meet net losses. Upon dissolution, the reserve fund is not distributed among members but is donated to the community or remitted to the cooperative's federation or union.[^67]

**Revolving Capital** --- Capital raised by the cooperative through deferred payment of patronage refunds or interest on share capital, or by authorized deduction from the proceeds of products sold or services rendered, evidenced by revolving capital certificates.[^68]

**Secondary Cooperative** --- A cooperative whose members are primary cooperatives. Includes federations of cooperatives.[^69]

**Service Cooperative** --- A cooperative that engages in medical and dental care, hospitalization, transportation, insurance, housing, labor, power and electricity, communication, professional, and other services.[^70]

**Share Capital** --- The money paid or required to be paid by members for the operations of the cooperative. The par value of each share may be fixed to any figure not more than one thousand pesos (PHP 1,000.00). No single member of a primary cooperative may own or hold more than ten percent of the total share capital.[^71]

**Single-Purpose Cooperative** --- A cooperative undertaking activities related to its main line of business or purpose. May also be referred to as a single-line cooperative.[^72]

**Social Audit** --- A procedure wherein the cooperative assesses its social impact and ethical performance against its stated mission, vision, goals, and code of social responsibility. It enables the cooperative to account for its social performance, evaluate its impact in the community, and be accountable for its decisions and actions to its regular members.[^73]

**Subsidiary Cooperative** --- An organization whose membership or shareholders come all or mostly from a cooperative, organized for a purpose different from the parent cooperative, and receiving technical, managerial, and financial assistance from it.[^74]

**Tertiary Cooperative** --- A cooperative whose members are secondary cooperatives (federations). Operates at the highest tier of the cooperative structure.[^75]

**Transport Cooperative** --- A cooperative engaged in land and sea transportation, limited to small vessels as defined under Philippine maritime laws.[^76]

**Treasurer's Affidavit** --- A sworn statement by the cooperative's elected treasurer certifying that at least twenty-five percent of the authorized share capital has been subscribed and at least twenty-five percent of the total subscription has been paid, with paid-up capital not less than fifteen thousand pesos (PHP 15,000.00).[^77]

**Voluntary and Open Membership** --- The first internationally recognized cooperative principle, providing that cooperatives are voluntary organizations open to all persons able to use their services and willing to accept the responsibilities of membership, without gender, social, racial, cultural, political, or religious discrimination.[^78]

**Water Service Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized to own, operate, and manage water systems for the provision and distribution of potable water for its members and their households.[^79]

**Workers Cooperative** --- A cooperative organized by workers, including the self-employed, who are at the same time the members and owners of the enterprise, with the principal purpose of providing employment and business opportunities to its members.[^80]

## Islamic Finance and Shari'ah Terms

**Al-Wadiah (Safekeeping)** --- An Islamic finance arrangement in which a bank or financial institution holds deposits in trust for customers, used as the Shari'ah-compliant alternative to conventional interest-bearing savings accounts.[^81]

**Gharar** --- Excessive uncertainty or ambiguity in a contract. Prohibited in Islamic finance because speculative business and financial transactions involving too much uncertainty or extreme risk can lead to disputes and exploitation.[^82]

**Hadith** --- The record of the sayings, actions, and silent approvals of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). Together with the Quran and the Sunnah, the Hadith forms one of the primary sources of Shari'ah law.[^83]

**Halal** --- Acts and deeds that are in accordance with Shari'ah law. Literally means "permissible" in Islam. In the cooperative context, all transactions, products, and services must be halal to comply with the Shari'ah governance framework.[^84]

**Haram** --- The opposite of halal; that which is prohibited under Shari'ah law. Cooperatives and social enterprises under the Shari'ah governance framework must avoid all haram products and services, including alcohol, pork products, and conventional interest-based transactions.[^85]

**Ijarah** --- An Islamic finance lease arrangement in which the lessor leases an asset to the lessee for a specified period at an agreed rental. The lessor retains ownership and bears maintenance costs, distinguishing it from conventional leasing with interest.[^86]

**Islamic Finance** --- Financial organizations and arrangements with risk-sharing as their core element, contributing to financial stability through the use of financial contracts and services founded on risk-sharing rather than speculation, in compliance with Shari'ah principles.[^87]

**Istisna** --- An Islamic finance contract for manufacturing or construction, in which one party commissions another to make or build something according to agreed specifications, price, and delivery schedule. Payment may be made in advance, in installments, or upon completion.[^88]

**Maqasid al-Shari'ah** --- The higher objectives of Shari'ah, which include the preservation of five essential values: faith (din), life (nafs), lineage (nasl), intellect (aql), and wealth (mal). Cooperatives and social enterprises under the Shari'ah governance framework must align their operations with these objectives.[^89]

**Maysir** --- Gambling and games of chance. Prohibited in Islamic finance alongside riba and gharar. Cooperative activities must not involve lotteries, raffles, or any form of gambling.[^90]

**Mudarabah (Profit-Sharing)** --- An Islamic finance partnership in which one party provides the capital (rab al-mal) and the other provides labor and expertise (mudarib). Profits are shared according to a pre-agreed ratio, while losses are borne by the capital provider unless caused by the manager's negligence.[^91]

**Murabaha (Cost-Plus Financing)** --- An Islamic finance transaction in which a financial institution purchases an asset and resells it to the client at a disclosed cost plus an agreed markup. The Shari'ah-compliant alternative to conventional interest-based lending.[^92]

**Musharakah (Joint Venture)** --- An Islamic finance partnership in which all parties contribute capital and share profits and losses in proportion to their investment. The Shari'ah-compliant equivalent of a conventional joint venture.[^93]

**Riba** --- A concept in Islam referring broadly to growth, increase, or excess, which forbids interest credited from loans or deposits. Translated as the pursuit of illegal, exploitative gains made in business or trade under Islamic law, akin to usury. Strictly prohibited in all cooperative and social enterprise financial transactions under the Shari'ah governance framework.[^94]

**Sadaqah** --- Charity given voluntarily. Describes any voluntary charitable act toward others, whether through generosity, love, compassion, or faith. Encouraged as a financing mechanism for social enterprises under the Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code.[^95]

**Salam** --- An Islamic finance contract of forward sale, in which the buyer pays the full price in advance for a specified commodity to be delivered on a future date. Commonly used in agricultural financing as an alternative to conventional futures contracts.[^96]

**Shari'ah** --- Islamic canonical law based on the teachings of the Quran and the traditions of the Prophet Muhammad (Hadith and Sunnah), prescribing both religious and secular duties. In the cooperative context, Shari'ah provides the framework for permissible financial transactions, products, and services.[^97]

**Shari'ah Committee** --- A committee composed of at least three members with competence in Shari'ah, required within cooperatives and social enterprises that choose to operate under Islamic principles and the Shari'ah governance framework.[^98]

**Shari'ah Compliance** --- The adherence of a cooperative's or social enterprise's operations, business activities, products, and services to Islamic principles and Shari'ah rules, as verified through regular review and audit by the Shari'ah and Legal Division of the Authority.[^99]

**Shari'ah Governance** --- Structures and processes adopted by an institution to ensure compliance with Islamic principles and Shari'ah rules in its business operations. The Authority adopts a Shari'ah governance framework and policies to guide cooperatives and social enterprises.[^100]

**Sunnah** --- The practices and traditions of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), as recorded in the Hadith. Together with the Quran, the Sunnah forms the foundational source of Shari'ah law and guides cooperative conduct under the Islamic principles framework.[^101]

**Takaful (Mutual Guarantee)** --- The Shari'ah-compliant alternative to conventional insurance. Participants contribute to a mutual fund that is used to compensate members who suffer losses. Based on the principles of mutual cooperation, shared responsibility, and joint indemnity.[^102]

**Waqf** --- Assets that are donated, bequeathed, or purchased for being held in perpetual trust for general or specific charitable causes that are socially beneficial. Similar to the Western concept of endowment. Encouraged as a strategic, empowering, and sustainable mechanism for financing social enterprises.[^103]

**Zakat** --- A form of obligatory charity in Islam, with the literal meaning of "to cleanse." Muslims believe that paying zakat purifies, increases, and blesses the remainder of their wealth. Identified in the Code as a strategic financing mechanism for social enterprises alongside sadaqah and waqf.[^104]

## Social Enterprise Terms

**Asset Lock** --- A governance requirement for social enterprises under the Code, mandating that in case of termination or dissolution, the enterprise's assets must be transferred to another social enterprise or a social enterprise network in the Bangsamoro region, rather than distributed to owners.[^105]

**Assets (Social Enterprise)** --- All kinds of properties, real or personal, owned by a social entrepreneur and used for the conduct of its business. For purposes of tax exemption and other incentives, the term includes all properties owned or used by the social enterprise directly related to its operations.[^106]

**Bangsamoro Center for Social Enterprise Development (BCSED)** --- An institution established under the Authority to provide capability training and education, manage the SE Marketing Assistance Program, and develop the research and development system for social enterprises.[^107]

**Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Council (BSEC)** --- An advisory body organized by the Authority, composed of an alliance of social enterprise leaders who leverage their networks, knowledge, and experience to build a strong and enabling environment for social enterprises. Monitors the implementation of the RISE Program.[^108]

**Bangsamoro Social Enterprise Fund (BSEF)** --- A fund established by the Bangsamoro Government through the Authority to give grants to social enterprises to improve their local communities. Managed by the Authority in partnership with the BCSED and the BSEC.[^109]

**Basic Sectors** --- Non-Moro indigenous peoples, farmer-peasants, artisanal fisherfolk, workers in the formal sector and migrant workers, workers in the informal sector, youth and students, and urban poor.[^110]

**Capability Building** --- The process of enhancing the viability and sustainability of social enterprises, including education, training, coaching, study tours, technical and consultancy services, organizational systems development, technology incubation, value chain development, trade fairs and missions, networking, and policy advocacy.[^111]

**Civil Society Organizations (CSOs)** --- Private voluntary organizations of citizens established to pursue a mission, usually involving the interest of the public, marginalized groups, or specific sectors of society. Includes non-government organizations, socio-civic groups, foundations, and people's organizations.[^112]

**Disadvantaged Sectors** --- Women, children, senior citizens, differently-abled persons, indigenous cultural communities, internally displaced persons (whether from natural or human-induced causes), widows and orphans, homeless persons, and victims of conflicts and atrocities that affect their well-being and participation in community affairs.[^113]

**Foundation** --- A non-profit organization duly registered that typically donates funds and support to other organizations or provides the source of funding for its own charitable purposes. Unlike a corporation, foundations have no shareholders, though they may have a board, an assembly, and voting members.[^114]

**Impact Investors** --- Individuals or institutions that choose to invest money in a business endeavor or activity not principally for financial profit but to fulfill a social mission, which may include poverty reduction, environmental protection, strong organizational governance, and a desire for a more economically just world.[^115]

**Micro Social Enterprise** --- A social enterprise at the smallest scale of operations, exempt from tax on income arising from the operations of the enterprise. Local government units may either reduce or exempt micro social enterprises from local taxes, fees, and charges.[^116]

**Microfinance** --- The provision of a broad range of financial services such as deposits, loans, payment services, money transfers, and insurance products to the poor and low-income households and their microenterprises and small businesses, to enable them to raise their income levels and improve their living standards.[^117]

**Minimum Basic Needs** --- The needs of a Filipino family pertaining to survival (food and nutrition, health, water and sanitation), security (shelter, peace and order, public safety, income and livelihood), enabling services (basic education and literacy, participation in community development, family and psycho-social care), and inclusion (assistive devices and technology, personal assistance, sign language interpretation, accessibility).[^118]

**Multiple Bottom-Lines** --- A social enterprise principle requiring social enterprises to work toward the achievement of measurable social and environmental impacts alongside financial return, rather than pursuing profit alone.[^119]

**Non-Government Organization (NGO)** --- A duly registered non-stock, non-profit organization focusing on the upliftment of the basic or disadvantaged sectors of society by providing advocacy, training, community organizing, research, access to resources, and other similar activities, organized and operated exclusively for scientific, research, educational, health, social welfare, cultural, or charitable purposes.[^120]

**Private Enterprise** --- An organization engaged in a business activity organized for the purpose of accumulating profit, whose primary stakeholders and beneficiaries are its owners. Distinguished from social enterprises, where profit is secondary to social mission.[^121]

**RISE for Peace and Sustainable Development Program** --- The Bangsamoro Resilient and Inclusive Social Enterprises for Peace and Sustainable Development Program --- a comprehensive and fully integrated capacity-building program formulated to strengthen the growth of social enterprises and promote inclusive innovation in BARMM. Includes SE capability building, research and development, and marketing infrastructure development.[^122]

**SE Marketing Assistance Program (SMAP)** --- A program under the Authority that assists social enterprises in matching supply with demand in both domestic and foreign markets, and promotes SE products and services through media, trade fairs, and trade missions.[^123]

**SE Marketing Information Network (SMIN)** --- A digital platform developed by the Authority with the Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism, providing business information, contacts, market intelligence, and trade leads to social enterprises.[^124]

**Social Audit (Social Enterprise)** --- The periodic reporting and assessment required of social enterprises to demonstrate how they meet the criteria for SE status, including annual activity and social-impact reports submitted to the Authority.[^125]

**Social Development** --- The continuing process of addressing the needs of society, beginning with the people's minimum basic needs, through systematic implementation of socioeconomic programs or packages.[^126]

**Social Enterprise (SE)** --- Any business organization whose primary purpose is addressing a social, cultural, or environmental challenge, where creating profit for owners or shareholders is secondary. It delivers positive social impact through its means and ends to achieve social justice, social equity, and economic efficiency, and must reinvest a significant proportion of its profits back into the business for social, cultural, or environmental purposes.[^127]

**Social Enterprise Intermediaries** --- Organizations that support social enterprises through training, investment brokering, creating platforms and networks between social enterprises and other ecosystem stakeholders. Government intermediaries are excluded from this category.[^128]

**Social Enterprise Status** --- A qualification granted by the Authority, valid for three years, during which the social enterprise must submit annual activity and social-impact reports. Failure to renew results in removal from the Authority's Registry.[^129]

**Social Entrepreneur** --- An innovative individual or institution that promotes the creation and operationalization of enterprises or livelihood endeavors for those in need, or which address social problems and improve societal well-being.[^130]

**Social Entrepreneurship** --- Innovations designed to explicitly improve societal well-being, housed within entrepreneurial organizations that initiate, guide, or contribute to change in society.[^131]

**Social Mission** --- A social enterprise principle requiring that social enterprises put primacy on objectives that improve human well-being, promote peace and social cohesion, reduce inequality, provide access to quality services, and ensure participation of the poor, low-income, underprivileged, disadvantaged, and marginalized sectors.[^132]

**Social Procurement** --- The practice of achieving economic empowerment by purchasing goods and services from social benefit suppliers and providing training and employment to socially and economically disadvantaged people. The Authority's Social Enterprise Division encourages social procurement within government agencies.[^133]

**Social Reform** --- The continuing process of addressing the basic inequities in society through a systematic, unified, and coordinated delivery of socioeconomic programs or packages.[^134]

**Social Value** --- The additional benefit to society of procuring a good or service, over and above the direct benefit and value of the good or service to the procuring entity. Includes support for poor communities, advancement of human rights, protection of the environment, and community development.[^135]

**Sustainable, Inclusive, and Equitable Development** --- A social enterprise principle requiring that social enterprises engage in business and entrepreneurial endeavors geared toward the upliftment of people's lives and the achievement of development that is environmentally sustainable, socially inclusive, and economically equitable.[^136]

## BARMM Governance Terms

**Bangsamoro Autonomy Act (BAA)** --- An act enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament. Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts are the primary legislative products of the BTA Parliament and carry the force of law within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^137]

**Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM)** --- The autonomous political entity in the southern Philippines established under the Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054), replacing the former Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (ARMM). Comprises the provinces of Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, Lanao del Sur, Basilan, Sulu, and Tawi-Tawi, plus Cotabato City and the Special Geographic Area (SGA).[^138] (Note: Following the Supreme Court ruling in *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea* (2024) and Executive Order No. 91 (2025), the status of Sulu within BARMM is subject to ongoing legal and administrative developments.)

**Bangsamoro Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code (BCSEC)** --- The proposed comprehensive legislation (BTA PB No. 210) to govern the registration, regulation, and development of cooperatives and social enterprises in BARMM. Also referred to as "the Code" throughout this guidebook.[^139]

**Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP) 2023-2028** --- The second comprehensive development plan for BARMM, setting out six development goals, eight strategies, and specific sector objectives. Goal 2 (equitable, competitive, and robust economy) directly governs cooperative and social enterprise development targets.[^140]

**Bangsamoro Organic Law (BOL)** --- Republic Act No. 11054, the foundational law that created the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao and defined its structure of government, powers, and area of coverage. Sections 24 and 27 of Article XIII mandate cooperative and social enterprise development policies.[^141]

**Bangsamoro Parliament** --- The legislative body of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, currently operating as the Bangsamoro Transition Authority (BTA) Parliament during the transition period. Enacts Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts and resolutions.[^142]

**Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA)** --- The Bangsamoro government agency responsible for overall development planning, including coordination of economic targets, poverty reduction strategies, and sectoral plans including cooperative development.[^143]

**Bangsamoro Transition Authority (BTA)** --- The interim government body administering the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region during the transition period from the former ARMM to the permanent BARMM government. Exercises both executive and legislative functions.[^144]

**Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA)** --- The Bangsamoro government agency that serves as the sole registering, regulating, and developing body for cooperatives and social enterprises in BARMM. Attached to the Office of the Chief Minister, it enjoys fiscal autonomy and operates through four divisions: Administrative and Finance, Cooperative Development, Social Enterprise, and Shari'ah and Legal.[^145]

**Cooperative Annual Performance Report (CAPR)** --- A mandatory annual report that cooperatives must submit to the Authority within one hundred twenty days after the close of the fiscal year, detailing the cooperative's activities, programs, performance, and statistics.[^146]

**Cooperative Audited Financial Statements (CAFS)** --- The mandatory audited financial statements that cooperatives must submit to the Authority within one hundred twenty days after the close of the fiscal year, prepared in accordance with generally accepted accounting standards for cooperatives.[^147]

**General Appropriations Act of the Bangsamoro (GAAB)** --- The annual budget law enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament, which appropriates funds for the implementation of government programs including cooperative and social enterprise development.[^148]

**Gross Regional Domestic Product (GRDP)** --- The total value of goods and services produced within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The BDP 2023-2028 targets annual GRDP growth of eight to nine percent.[^149]

**Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board** --- The body responsible for coordinating fiscal policies between the Bangsamoro Government and the National Government, including tax treatment for cooperatives and social enterprises in BARMM.[^150]

**LAKAS SE Program** --- Lakbay Aral sa Social Enterprise, a learning program for social enterprise development identified as a priority project under the BDP 2023-2028 cooperative and social enterprise sector.[^151]

**Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR)** --- The BARMM ministry responsible for agricultural development, fisheries, and agrarian reform, which coordinates with the Authority on agri-fishery cooperative development and the Shari'ah-compliant financing program for startup cooperatives.[^152]

**Ministry of Finance and Budget and Management (MFBM)** --- The BARMM ministry responsible for fiscal policy, budget management, and coordination of financial incentives for cooperatives and social enterprises.[^153]

**Ministry of Trade, Investments and Tourism (MTIT)** --- The BARMM ministry responsible for trade promotion, investment facilitation, and tourism development, which coordinates with the Authority on SE marketing, trade fairs, and the SE Marketing Information Network.[^154]

**Municipal Cooperative Officer (MCO)** --- A local government official at the municipal level responsible for promoting and assisting cooperative development. The BDP 2023-2028 identifies the absence of MCOs in most BARMM local government units as a key challenge.[^155]

**PESO Project** --- Profitability and Equity Support Oriented project, a priority initiative under the BDP 2023-2028 providing financial and technical assistance to improve cooperative profitability.[^156]

**Special Geographic Area (SGA)** --- The 63 barangays in the municipalities of Aleosan, Carmen, Kabacan, Midsayap, Pikit, and Pigcawayan in the Province of Cotabato that voted to join the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region through the 2019 plebiscite.[^157]

**STOCK Project** --- Strengthening of Technical and Operational Knowledge project, a capacity-building initiative under the BDP 2023-2028 for cooperatives organized by former combatants.[^158]
