# Chapter 10 — Education, Training, and Capacity Building

The fifth cooperative principle is not a suggestion. Education, training, and information are what separate a cooperative from a group of people who pooled money. Skip this principle and your cooperative will join the 88 percent that are non-compliant. Of the 12,214 cooperatives registered in BARMM, only 1,424 hold current compliance certificates.[^1] The pattern is consistent: cooperatives that invest in training comply. Cooperatives that skip training fail. The Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028 identifies **low functionality and unsustainable profitability** as the central problem in the cooperative sector -- and it prescribes **capacitation of human resources** as a primary remedy.[^2]

This chapter gives you the complete training roadmap. You will learn what training is required by law, what the four-tier curriculum looks like from pre-registration through advanced leadership, how to use your Education and Training Fund properly, and how to design, deliver, and document training in your cooperative. By the end, you will have a training needs assessment checklist, a course catalog organized by level, and a clear understanding of the sanctions that follow when officers skip their required training.

Education is not an expense. It is the operating system that makes everything else in this guidebook work.

## 10.1 The Fifth Cooperative Principle: Education, Training, and Information

### 10.1.1 Why This Principle Is Non-Negotiable

The International Cooperative Alliance lists seven principles that define what a cooperative is. The fifth principle -- **Education, Training, and Information** -- reads:

> "Cooperatives shall provide education and training for members, elected and appointed representatives, managers, and employees, so that they can contribute effectively and efficiently to the development of their cooperatives."[^3]

BTA Bill 210 adopts this principle verbatim in **Section 5(v)**.[^4] This is not aspirational language. It is a legal obligation. Every cooperative in the Bangsamoro must provide education and training to four groups: **members**, **elected representatives**, **managers**, and **employees**. Not one group. All four.

The law reinforces this obligation through three mechanisms:

1. **Mandatory statutory fund allocation.** Your cooperative must set aside up to 10 percent of net surplus for the **Education and Training Fund (ETF)**.[^5]
2. **Required officer training.** Every officer -- directors, secretary, treasurer, and all committee members -- must complete prescribed training within the first half of their term.[^6]
3. **Sanctions for non-compliance.** Willful failure to comply with training requirements is grounds for non-issuance of your Certificate of Compliance for the succeeding year. Officers who skip required training are disqualified from holding any elective or appointive position.[^7]

That third point stops many cooperatives cold. Your Chairperson, your Treasurer, your Audit Committee members -- if any of them have not completed the required training, they are technically **disqualified from office**. The CDA and CSEA can enforce this during inspection. Do not wait until inspection day to discover the problem.

**Why training matters for BARMM cooperatives specifically.** The Bangsamoro cooperative sector faces challenges that the rest of the Philippines does not. Many newly registered cooperatives are composed of **former combatants** who organized through the Combatant Transitional Program.[^8] These cooperatives often encounter difficulties complying with requirements because the policies were designed for established civilian cooperatives, not organizations in transition. Training bridges this gap. The **STOCK Project** (Strengthening of Technical and Operational Knowledge) exists specifically for this purpose -- building the operational capacity that former combatant cooperatives need to function and comply.[^9]

Beyond combatant transition, BARMM has the country's **lowest functional literacy rate at 71.6 percent** and a **poverty incidence of 29.80 percent**.[^10] Training programs in this region must account for lower baseline literacy, limited access to conventional training venues, and the need for content delivered in languages and cultural frameworks that resonate with Bangsamoro communities.

### 10.1.2 The Education and Training Fund: How to Use It

The **Education and Training Fund (ETF)** is one of four statutory funds your cooperative must maintain. Under BTA Bill 210, Section 62(b), you must allocate **up to ten percent (10%) of net surplus** to the ETF each year.[^11] Your bylaws may also direct certain fees or portions of fees to this fund.

**The 50-50 rule.** The law splits the ETF into two halves:

- **First half (50%).** Your cooperative spends this directly on education and training purposes -- PRS for incoming members, officer training, specialized courses, study tours, and cooperative awareness campaigns.
- **Second half (50%).** Your cooperative remits this to a **union or federation** of your choice, or one to which you belong. That union or federation pools the remittances and delivers training that individual cooperatives cannot afford on their own.[^12]

**What the receiving union or federation must report.** The law requires the union or federation to submit three schedules to both the CSEA and contributing cooperatives:

1. A **list of cooperatives** that remitted their Cooperative Education and Training Funds (CETF)
2. A record of **business consultancy assistance** provided, including nature and cost
3. A record of **other training activities** undertaken, specifying nature, participants, and cost[^13]

If your federation cannot produce these schedules upon request, your cooperative has grounds to redirect its CETF remittance to a different union or federation at the next General Assembly.

**What qualifies as a valid ETF expenditure.** The ETF covers:

| Allowed | Not Allowed |
|---|---|
| PRS for prospective members | Office supplies or equipment |
| Officer and director training courses | Building repairs or renovations |
| Committee-specific training (Audit, Election, Ethics, Mediation) | Staff salaries or allowances |
| Financial literacy programs for members | Loans to members |
| Study tours to model cooperatives | Entertainment or social events |
| Cooperative awareness campaigns in the community | Political activities |
| Subscription to cooperative publications | Investments or business capital |
| Training materials, handouts, and learning aids | General operating expenses |

**Track every peso.** Document each ETF expenditure with receipts, attendance sheets, training certificates, and post-training evaluation results. The CSEA will review your ETF utilization during inspection. Underspending the ETF is almost as common as overspending it on unauthorized items. If your cooperative earned a net surplus but spent nothing on training, the inspector will ask why -- and the answer may affect your compliance rating.

**Upon dissolution.** If your cooperative dissolves, the unexpended balance of the ETF goes to the cooperative education and training fund of your chosen union or federation.[^14] The ETF cannot be distributed to members. It belongs to the cooperative movement, not to individuals.

## 10.2 The Four-Tier Training Curriculum

Training for cooperatives follows a **four-tier structure** that moves from awareness to mastery. Each tier builds on the one before it. Skipping tiers creates knowledge gaps that surface as governance failures, compliance violations, and financial mismanagement.

The table below maps the complete curriculum. Use it as your cooperative's training roadmap.

| Level | Code | Target Audience | Focus Area | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Level 100** | PRS | Prospective members | Join decision, cooperative basics | 3 hours 45 minutes |
| **Level 100** | FCC | All members (within 1 year) | Fundamentals of Cooperatives Course | 16 hours |
| **Level 200** | ACC-Micro | Officers of micro cooperatives | Advanced Cooperative Course (Micro) | 8 hours per topic |
| **Level 200** | ACC-SML | Officers of small/medium/large cooperatives | Advanced Cooperative Course | 32 hours |
| **Level 200** | RFS | Officers by committee assignment | Role/Function-Specific Training | Minimum 4 hours |
| **Level 300** | FRM | Officers of savings and credit cooperatives (PHP 5M+ deposits) | Financial, Risk, and Credit Management | 16 hours |
| **Level 300** | BLT | Officers and managers | Business Line Training | 4-8 hours |
| **Level 300** | BARMM-1 | Officers of BARMM cooperatives | BDP Alignment and Moral Governance | 4-6 hours |
| **Level 400** | CLT | Chairpersons, senior officers, managers | Cooperative Leadership | 40+ hours |
| **Level 400** | STOCK | Former combatant cooperative members | Combatant Transitional Training | 24-40 hours |
| **Level 400** | LAKAS | Social enterprise organizers | Social Enterprise Development | 16-24 hours |
| **Level 400** | AGRI | Farmer organizations, agri-cooperatives | Agri-Cooperative Strengthening | 24-40 hours |

### 10.2.1 Level 100 (PRS and Basic): Foundation Knowledge

**Level 100 is where every cooperative member begins.** This tier has two components: the Pre-Registration Seminar (PRS) for prospective members, and the Fundamentals of Cooperatives Course (FCC) for all members.

**Pre-Registration Seminar (PRS).** Under CDA MC 2025-11, PRS is a **3-hour 45-minute seminar** conducted by the CDA Regional Extension Offices -- or, in BARMM, by the **Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA)**.[^15] PRS is mandatory before registration. Without a PRS Certificate, your cooperative cannot complete the registration process.

The PRS covers five modules:

| Module | Content | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Preliminaries** | Opening program, participant introductions | 10 minutes |
| **Objectives and Description of PRS** | Why PRS exists, what it covers | 20 minutes |
| **The CDA/CSEA and its Mandate** | Salient provisions of RA 11364, CSEA role in BARMM | 15 minutes |
| **Introduction to Cooperatives** | Cooperative identity, types and services, member rights and duties | 2 hours |
| **Requirements and Process of Registration** | E-CoopRIS, post-registration expectations | 1 hour |

Key PRS facts your cooperative needs to know:

- **Cost:** Free. No fees shall be collected from participants.[^16]
- **Mode:** Online or face-to-face.
- **Certificate validity:** Three years from date of issuance.[^17]
- **Certificate issuer:** In BARMM, the CSEA issues PRS certificates.
- **LGU role:** Local Government Units, through their Cooperative Development Officers (CDOs), assist prospective cooperatives with venue, logistics, and coordination with CSEA.[^18]

**Fundamentals of Cooperatives Course (FCC).** Under CDA MC 2025-09, all cooperatives must ensure that **every member** completes training on the fundamentals of cooperatives, financial literacy, and leadership and values formation **within one year of membership acceptance**.[^19] For existing members at the time the circular took effect, the cooperative must prepare an action plan for compliance within one year.

The FCC curriculum is prescribed by CDA MC 2025-09, Annex A:

| Subject Matter | Duration |
|---|---|
| **Fundamentals of Cooperatives** -- Origin and philosophy, statement of cooperative identity, cooperative difference, role in national and international development | 6 hours |
| **Cooperative Laws and Relevant Issuances** -- Salient provisions of RA 9520, RA 11364, RA 10744 | 6 hours |
| **Leadership and Values Formation** | 2 hours |
| **Financial Literacy** (including digital literacy) | 2 hours |
| **Total** | **16 hours** |

For BARMM cooperatives, add a **continuing education module** covering **BDP 2023-2028 alignment** (Goal 2, Objective 2.5), **Moral Governance principles**, **Shari'ah compliance basics**, and **CSEA compliance requirements**. Budget 4 additional hours for this module. Your Education and Training Committee should integrate this into your annual training plan.

### 10.2.2 Level 200 (Basic to Intermediate): Governance and Operations

**Level 200 is for officers.** Every elected and appointed officer must complete required training within the **first half of their term**.[^20] This is not optional. Officers who do not complete the training are **disqualified from holding office**.[^21]

The training requirements differ by cooperative category:

**For micro cooperatives** (assets below PHP 3 million):

| Subject Matter | Duration |
|---|---|
| **Cooperative Management and Governance** (includes applicable laws and policy formulation) | 8 hours |
| **Financial Management** (financial statement interpretation, financial ratios, analysis) | 8 hours |
| **Line-of-business training** (specific to your cooperative's business) | 8 hours |
| **Total** | **24 hours** |

**For small, medium, and large cooperatives:**

| Subject Matter | Duration |
|---|---|
| **Expanded Cooperative Management and Governance** (relevant laws, parliamentary procedure, policy formulation) | 16 hours |
| **Financial Management** (basic accounting for non-accountants, internal control) | 8 hours |
| **Line-of-business training** | 4 hours |
| **Role/function-specific training** (per committee assignment) | Minimum 4 hours |
| **Total** | **32 hours** |

**Authorized providers for Level 200 training:**

| Provider | Conditions |
|---|---|
| **CDA** | Free of charge (courses 1 and 2 only for micro cooperatives) |
| **Certified Cooperative Development Officers (CDOs)** and technical personnel of Local Cooperative Development Offices (LCDO-LGU) | Free of charge; must hold Certificate of Authority |
| **Accredited Cooperative Training Providers (CTPros)** | Fee-based; must hold CDA accreditation |
| **Unions and Federations** | For their affiliates |

**Micro cooperatives that graduate** to small, medium, or large category must take an **additional 8 hours** of training on expanded Cooperative Governance and Management, covering international concepts such as the ICA Second Decade Blueprint.[^22]

**Continuing education for officers.** The law mandates that all cooperatives adopt an **annual continuing education program for officers**, reflected in the cooperative's annual development plan.[^23] Do not treat the initial 24- or 32-hour training as a one-time requirement. Build a yearly training calendar that keeps your officers current on regulatory changes, financial management best practices, and governance standards.

### 10.2.3 Level 300 (Intermediate): Business Development and Financial Management

**Level 300 targets specialized knowledge** for cooperatives with significant financial operations. Two triggers bring you to this level.

**Trigger 1: Savings and credit operations with PHP 5 million or more in deposit liabilities.** If your cooperative is engaged in savings and credit and holds at least **PHP 5,000,000.00** in deposit liabilities based on the latest Audited Financial Statement, all officers must complete an additional **16 hours** of specialized training:[^24]

| Subject Matter | Duration |
|---|---|
| **Financial Management** (advanced) | Included in 16 hours |
| **Basic Accounting for Non-Accountants** | Included in 16 hours |
| **Internal Control** | Included in 16 hours |
| **Risk Management** | Included in 16 hours |
| **Credit Management** | Included in 16 hours |

This training is available free of charge from CDA and certified CDOs/LCDO-LGU technical personnel, or fee-based through accredited CTPros.[^25]

**Trigger 2: Credit Surety Fund (CSF) cooperatives.** Officers of registered CSF cooperatives must complete a **16-hour startup/refresher course** on RA 10744 and its IRR, plus role/function-specific training of at least 4 hours.[^26]

**BARMM-specific Level 300 content.** For cooperatives operating in the Bangsamoro, add these modules to your Level 300 training plan:

| Module | Content | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **BDP 2023-2028 Implementation** | Strategy 21 (cooperative profitability), Strategy 22 (SE institutionalization), M&E systems | 2 hours |
| **Profitability Enhancement** | PESO Project alignment, financial sustainability metrics, Objective 2.5.1 targets | 2 hours |
| **LGU Coordination** | Working with Municipal Cooperative Officers (MCOs), provincial extension offices, community promotion | 1 hour |
| **Shari'ah-Compliant Financial Products** | Murabaha, Musharakah, Mudarabah, Wadiah, Takaful | 2-4 hours |

**Shari'ah compliance in financial training.** Under BTA Bill 210, cooperatives in the Bangsamoro must adhere to Shari'ah objectives including **halal transactions**, **no riba (usury)**, **no maysir (gambling)**, **no gharar (excessive speculation)**, and **risk sharing principles**.[^27] If your cooperative offers credit services, your officers and credit committee must understand the Shari'ah-compliant alternatives:

| Conventional Product | Shari'ah Alternative | How It Works |
|---|---|---|
| Interest-bearing loan | **Murabaha** (cost-plus financing) | The cooperative buys the asset and resells it to the member at a disclosed markup |
| Interest-bearing savings | **Wadiah** (safekeeping) | The cooperative holds deposits as custodian; returns are discretionary gifts, not interest |
| Insurance | **Takaful** (mutual guarantee) | Members contribute to a common fund; claims are paid from the pool, surplus is shared |
| Joint venture (interest-based) | **Musharakah** (partnership) | Both parties contribute capital and share profits and losses proportionally |
| Investment (guaranteed return) | **Mudarabah** (profit sharing) | One party provides capital, the other provides labor/expertise; profits are shared by agreement |

### 10.2.4 Level 400 (Advanced): Strategic Growth and Federation Building

**Level 400 is for leaders who will shape the cooperative movement.** This tier covers strategic leadership, policy development, movement building, and specialized programs for unique BARMM contexts.

**Cooperative Leadership Course (CLT).** Target: Chairpersons, senior officers, and general managers. Duration: 40+ hours.

| Module | Content |
|---|---|
| **Leadership Styles** | Transformational, servant, ethical leadership; Moral Governance integration |
| **Strategic Management** | Planning, execution, evaluation; 5-year development planning |
| **Change Management** | Leading cooperative transformation; responding to regulatory change |
| **Stakeholder Relations** | Members, community, LGUs, CSEA, development partners |
| **Advocacy and Policy** | Influencing cooperative policy; engaging with BTA Bill 210 implementation |
| **Movement Building** | Federation formation, networking, solidarity purchasing, collective advocacy |
| **BDP 2023-2028 Leadership** | Implementing Strategy 21 and 22, community promotion, LGU coordination |

**Combatant Transitional Training -- STOCK Project.** Target: Former combatants organizing cooperatives. Duration: 24-40 hours.[^28]

| Module | Content |
|---|---|
| **Cooperative Fundamentals** | Basic concepts adapted for transitional context |
| **Business Legalization** | How to legally conduct business through cooperatives |
| **Compliance Orientation** | Understanding CDA/CSEA requirements |
| **Financial Literacy** | Basic bookkeeping, budgeting, fund management |
| **Market Access** | Connecting to markets and value chains |
| **Support Programs** | Cooperative Financial and Material Support Project access |

This is not a generic training program. It is designed for cooperatives whose members are transitioning from armed struggle to economic participation. The curriculum accounts for the reality that many participants have limited formal education, no prior experience with corporate governance structures, and urgent needs for livelihood support. Your cooperative can access this training through the CSEA's Combatant Transitional Program.[^29]

**Social Enterprise Development -- LAKAS SE Program.** Target: Social enterprise organizers and cooperative leaders exploring the SE model. Duration: 16-24 hours.[^30]

| Module | Content |
|---|---|
| **Social Enterprise Fundamentals** | Definition, characteristics, difference from cooperatives |
| **SE Legal Framework** | BTA Bill 210 Book III provisions, national SE legislation efforts |
| **Social Mission Development** | Identifying social, cultural, or environmental challenges |
| **Business Model Design** | Balancing social mission with financial sustainability |
| **Impact Measurement** | Measuring and reporting social impact |
| **Enabling Policies** | Strategy 22 alignment, institutionalization pathway |

**Agri-Cooperative Strengthening.** Target: Farmer organizations, agri-cooperatives, and Agrarian Reform Beneficiary Organizations (ARBOs). Duration: 24-40 hours.[^31]

| Module | Content |
|---|---|
| **Agricultural Value Chain** | From production to marketing; agro-entrepreneurship (Strategy 11) |
| **LGU Partnership** | Working with LGUs and development stakeholders |
| **Private Sector Collaboration** | Partnering with industry players |
| **Climate-Smart Agriculture** | Climate adaptation, disaster preparedness (Strategy 12) |
| **Young Farmers Program** | Professionalizing young farmers (Strategy 5) |
| **Market Access** | BARMM top commodities: seaweed (48.42% national production), cassava (41.70%), rubber, coffee |

BARMM is the country's **top seaweed producer** and **top cassava producer**.[^32] Agri-cooperatives in these value chains have a competitive advantage that training can sharpen into sustainable profitability.

## 10.3 Mandatory Training Requirements

Not all training is optional. The law mandates specific training for specific people at specific times. This section consolidates every mandatory requirement so you can build a compliance-ready training plan.

**MC 2025-09 Training Hours at a Glance.** Use this table as your quick reference. It shows every stakeholder category, the required courses, total hours, and what happens if your cooperative does not comply.

| Category | Required Courses | Total Hours | Sanction for Non-Compliance |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Members** (all categories) | Fundamentals of Cooperatives (6h) + Cooperative Laws (6h) + Leadership and Values Formation (2h) + Financial Literacy (2h) | **16 hours** | Member obligation; no direct sanction, but cooperative must show action plan during inspection |
| **Officers -- Micro** cooperatives | Cooperative Management and Governance (8h) + Financial Management (8h) + Line of Business (8h) | **24 hours** | **COC non-issuance** for the cooperative; **officer disqualification** from any elective or appointive position[^46] |
| **Officers -- Small, Medium, Large** cooperatives | Expanded Governance (16h) + Financial Management (8h) + Line of Business (4h) + Role/Function-Specific (4h minimum) | **32 hours** minimum | **COC non-issuance** for the cooperative; **officer disqualification** from any elective or appointive position[^47] |
| **Officers -- Savings and Credit** (PHP 5M+ deposits) | Additional Financial, Risk, and Credit Management | **+16 hours** | Same as above; additional non-compliance finding during examination |
| **Officers -- CSF** cooperatives | RA 10744 Startup/Refresher (16h) + Role-Specific (4h+) + Compliance Orientation (4h) | **+20 hours** minimum | Same as above |
| **Staff** (all cooperatives) | G1-G10 encouraged topics (see Section 10.3.4) | **No prescribed hours** | **No sanctions** -- encouraged, not mandatory[^48] |

**Read this table carefully.** The consequences column is not hypothetical. The CSEA can deny your Certificate of Compliance and disqualify your officers. Build your training plan around these numbers.

### 10.3.1 PRS for All Members (Before Registration)

**Who must attend:** Every prospective member of a primary cooperative must complete a Pre-Registration Seminar before joining.[^33]

**When:** Before your cooperative files for registration with CSEA. Existing cooperatives must ensure new members attend a PRS or equivalent orientation before the General Assembly approves their membership.

**Duration:** 3 hours 45 minutes.

**Conducted by:** CSEA (in BARMM). CDA Regional Extension Offices (outside BARMM).[^34]

**Certificate validity:** 3 years.[^35]

### 10.3.2 Basic Cooperative Training for Officers and Directors

**Who must complete this:** Every officer of the cooperative -- Board of Directors, Secretary, Treasurer, Election Committee, Audit Committee, Ethics Committee, Mediation and Conciliation Committee, other GA-created committees, and the General Manager or CEO.[^36]

**When:** Within the **first half of their term**. If your cooperative's officers serve two-year terms beginning in April, all required training must be completed by October.

**What happens if they do not complete it:**

- The officer is **disqualified from holding any elective or appointive position** in the cooperative.[^37]
- The cooperative faces **non-issuance of its Certificate of Compliance** for the succeeding year if it willfully fails to comply with training requirements despite due notice.[^38]

**Summary of required hours by cooperative category:**

| Category | Required Training | Total Hours |
|---|---|---|
| **Micro** cooperative | Advanced Cooperative Course (Governance + Finance + Line of Business) | 24 hours (8 per topic) |
| **Small, Medium, Large** cooperative | Expanded Governance (16) + Financial Management (8) + Line of Business (4) + Role-Specific (4 minimum) | 32 hours minimum |
| **Savings and credit** cooperative (PHP 5M+ deposits) | Additional Financial, Risk, and Credit Management | 16 additional hours |
| **CSF** cooperative | RA 10744 Startup/Refresher + Role-Specific | 20 hours minimum |

### 10.3.3 Specialized Training by Committee Assignment

Each committee in your cooperative has distinct functions that require distinct knowledge. The table below maps committee assignments to training content.

| Committee | Required Training Content | Minimum Duration |
|---|---|---|
| **Audit Committee** | Internal control systems, audit procedures, financial analysis, report writing, GA accountability | 8-16 hours |
| **Election Committee** | Election rules and procedures, candidate validation, conduct of elections, results certification, protest resolution | 4-8 hours |
| **Mediation and Conciliation Committee** | Conflict resolution, mediation techniques, hearing procedures, decision writing, confidentiality | 8-16 hours |
| **Ethics Committee** | Code of ethics development, ethics case analysis, investigation procedures, sanction recommendations, Moral Governance integration | 8-16 hours |
| **Education and Training Committee** | Training needs assessment, program planning, ETF management, provider coordination, impact evaluation, BDP 2023-2028 training alignment | 8-16 hours |
| **Credit Committee** (if applicable) | Credit policies, loan evaluation, collection procedures, delinquency management, Shari'ah-compliant lending alternatives | 16-24 hours |

**The Education and Training Committee deserves special attention.** This is the committee responsible for making everything in this chapter happen. Its members must know how to assess training needs, plan annual programs, manage ETF budgets, coordinate with accredited providers, and evaluate training impact. If your Education and Training Committee members have not been trained themselves, they cannot train anyone else.

### 10.3.4 Staff Training: The G1-G10 Track

MC 2025-09, Section VI.3 **encourages** -- but does not require -- cooperatives to provide onboarding and continuous training for hired staff.[^49] No hours are prescribed. No sanctions apply. But cooperatives that neglect staff development pay for it in turnover, errors, and weak member service.

The MC lists ten encouraged topics. Adapt them to your staff's actual roles.

| Code | Topic | What It Covers |
|---|---|---|
| **G1** | Basic Cooperative Orientation | Cooperative principles, values, organizational structure, and the staff role within the cooperative |
| **G2** | Sales | Product and service promotion, member engagement, market awareness |
| **G3** | Fraud Detection and Prevention | Recognizing fraud indicators, internal controls, reporting procedures |
| **G4** | Records Management | Filing systems, document retention, member records, regulatory documentation |
| **G5** | Office Management | Workplace organization, supplies management, scheduling, administrative procedures |
| **G6** | Store Management | Inventory control, merchandising, point-of-sale operations (for consumer and multi-purpose cooperatives) |
| **G7** | Customer Service Management | Member relations, complaint handling, service quality, communication skills |
| **G8** | Bookkeeping | Basic accounting, transaction recording, CDA Standard Chart of Accounts, financial record-keeping |
| **G9** | Basic Computer Literacy | Computer operations, office software, basic data management, digital tools for cooperatives |
| **G10** | Data Privacy | Data Protection Act (RA 10173) compliance, member data handling, privacy policies and procedures |

**The G1-G10 list is illustrative.** MC 2025-09 uses the phrase "may include but not limited to." Add topics your staff actually needs. A cashier needs **G8 (Bookkeeping)** and **G10 (Data Privacy)**. A field coordinator needs **G2 (Sales)** and **G7 (Customer Service)**. Do not force every staff member through all ten.

**Authorized providers for staff training:** CDA (free for Bookkeeping), accredited CTPros, certified CDOs (free of charge), and federations for their affiliates.[^50]

**Key distinction from officer training.** Officers face **disqualification** for skipping required training. Staff face no regulatory sanction. But a cooperative with untrained staff will underperform -- and the Board will bear the consequences when inspection findings cite weak internal controls, poor records, or member complaints.

## 10.4 Designing and Delivering Training in Your Cooperative

Knowing what training is required is only half the job. The other half is making it happen -- assessing needs, finding providers, scheduling sessions, and documenting results. This section gives you the practical tools.

### 10.4.1 Assessing Training Needs

Before you spend a single peso from the ETF, assess what your cooperative actually needs. A training needs assessment identifies the gap between what your members and officers know now and what they need to know to perform their roles effectively.

**Training Needs Assessment Checklist.** Use this checklist at the start of each fiscal year. The Education and Training Committee should lead the process with input from the Board of Directors and the General Manager.

**Part A: Compliance Requirements**

- [ ] Have all current officers completed the required Advanced Cooperative Course (24 or 32 hours)?
- [ ] Have officers of savings and credit cooperatives (PHP 5M+ deposits) completed the 16-hour specialized training?
- [ ] Have all committee members completed role/function-specific training (minimum 4 hours)?
- [ ] Have all members admitted in the past 12 months completed the Fundamentals of Cooperatives Course (16 hours)?
- [ ] Is the cooperative's PRS certificate current (valid for 3 years)?
- [ ] Has the cooperative adopted an annual continuing education program for members and officers?

**Part B: Knowledge and Skills Gaps**

- [ ] Can your Treasurer read and interpret the cooperative's financial statements?
- [ ] Can your Audit Committee conduct an internal audit without external assistance?
- [ ] Do your Board members understand the difference between policy-making and management?
- [ ] Can your General Manager prepare a budget proposal with cash flow projections?
- [ ] Do your members understand their rights and obligations under the bylaws?
- [ ] Can your Credit Committee evaluate loan applications using documented criteria?
- [ ] Do your officers understand Shari'ah compliance requirements for cooperative transactions?
- [ ] Can your cooperative prepare and submit the CAPR and CAFS without errors?

**Part C: Strategic Development Needs**

- [ ] Does your cooperative have a 5-year strategic plan?
- [ ] Are officers trained in BDP 2023-2028 alignment (Goal 2, Objective 2.5)?
- [ ] Does your cooperative know how to access PESO Project support?
- [ ] Are members aware of market opportunities in BARMM's top commodities?
- [ ] Has your cooperative explored Shari'ah-compliant financial products?
- [ ] Do officers know how to coordinate with the Municipal Cooperative Officer (MCO)?

**Part D: Training Infrastructure**

- [ ] Does your cooperative have a designated Education and Training Committee?
- [ ] Has the committee prepared a training calendar for the current fiscal year?
- [ ] Does the cooperative have a list of accredited training providers in the region?
- [ ] Is the ETF balance sufficient to cover planned training activities?
- [ ] Does the cooperative maintain a training records file?

**Scoring.** Count the unchecked boxes. Each unchecked box represents a training gap. Prioritize as follows:

| Unchecked Boxes | Priority Level | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Part A gaps** | **Critical** -- address within 30 days | Schedule mandatory training immediately; non-compliance risks COC denial and officer disqualification |
| **Part B gaps** | **High** -- address within 90 days | These gaps cause operational errors, audit findings, and governance failures |
| **Part C gaps** | **Medium** -- address within 180 days | These gaps limit growth and miss strategic opportunities |
| **Part D gaps** | **Foundational** -- address within 60 days | Without training infrastructure, no other training can happen |

### 10.4.2 Designing Training for BARMM Learners

BARMM has the **lowest functional literacy rate in the Philippines at 71.6 percent**.[^51] Tawi-Tawi is at **33.2 percent** -- meaning two out of three adults cannot functionally read and write.[^52] Standard CDA training materials assume Metro Manila literacy levels. They will not work here without adaptation.

**The five BARMM learner personas.** The CSEA Training Package identifies five composite learner profiles that represent the actual range of cooperative members, officers, and staff in the Bangsamoro.[^53] Use them to anchor every training design decision.

| Persona | Profile | Role | Education | Max Passive Segment |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hadji Musa** | 62, male, Maguindanaon farmer, Datu Odin Sinsuat | Board of Directors member (Micro coop) | Grade 6 completed | **12 minutes** |
| **Bai Norhata** | 45, female, Maranao sari-sari store operator, Marawi City | Treasurer (Small coop) | High school graduate | **12 minutes** |

**What "maximum passive segment" means.** This is the longest stretch a trainer should talk without an activity, question, or group exercise. For **low-literacy learners** (Hadji Musa, Bai Norhata, Hadji Abdulnasser), never exceed **12 minutes** of passive instruction. For **advanced learners** (Bai Sittie Aminah), you can stretch to **20 minutes**. For **young digital-literate staff** (Akmad), keep it to **15 minutes**. Exceed these limits and your audience checks out.

**Five design rules for BARMM training.**

1. **Use oral and visual instruction as the primary mode** for Personas 1-3. Text-heavy slides and handouts fail when a third of the audience has limited reading ability.
2. **Deliver in the learner's strongest language.** Use Maguindanaon, Maranao, Tausug, Sama, or Iranun -- not English -- as the primary language of instruction for community-level training.
3. **Open every topic with a real scenario** the learner recognizes. Fishing expenses for Tausug cooperatives. Market trading for Maranao cooperatives. Rice farming for Maguindanaon cooperatives.
4. **Schedule around prayer times and Ramadan.** Adjust session length, break timing, and energy-level expectations during fasting months.
5. **Design for GIDA constraints.** Island municipalities have no reliable internet, limited electricity, and boat-dependent schedules. Your module must work offline with printed and visual materials only.

**The BARMM Calibration Checklist.** Before delivering any training module in the Bangsamoro, run it through the **21-item BARMM Calibration Checklist** (see **Appendix [X]**). The checklist covers five domains: **Linguistic Adaptation** (5 items), **Cultural Responsiveness** (5 items), **Economic Contextualization** (4 items), **Infrastructure Considerations** (4 items), and **Transition Context** (3 items). A module scoring below 15 out of 21 has significant gaps and should be revised before delivery.[^54]

### 10.4.3 Partnering with CSEA, LGU-CDOs, and Accredited Trainers

Your cooperative does not have to deliver all training in-house. In fact, for mandatory training, you **cannot** deliver it in-house -- only authorized providers may conduct required training.[^39]

**CSEA (Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority).** In BARMM, CSEA serves as the primary regulatory and development agency for cooperatives. CSEA conducts:

- **Pre-Registration Seminars** (free)
- **Technical assistance** visits (free)
- **Compliance-related orientations** (free)
- **Specialized programs** through the PESO Project, STOCK Project, and LAKAS SE Program

Contact CSEA early. Slots for PRS sessions fill quickly, especially during cooperative registration season (January-March). Ask CSEA about their quarterly training calendar.

**Cooperative Development Officers (CDOs) at LGUs.** Under RA 11535, every municipal, city, and provincial government must have a Cooperative Development Officer.[^40] In BARMM, most LGUs have not yet appointed MCOs -- which is one of the key challenges the BDP 2023-2028 identifies.[^41] But where CDOs exist, they are authorized to conduct certain required trainings free of charge, provided they hold a **Certificate of Authority** from the CDA and have completed the required training-of-trainers orientation.

CDOs can assist your cooperative by:

- Providing venue and logistical support for PRS sessions
- Conducting the Advanced Cooperative Course (for micro cooperatives)
- Coordinating with CSEA on your cooperative's behalf
- Facilitating access to LGU cooperative development programs

**Accredited Cooperative Training Providers (CTPros).** Only CDA-accredited CTPros may conduct fee-based required training. Before engaging a CTPro, verify their accreditation. Ask for:

- CDA accreditation certificate (must be current)
- At least **2 years of organizational existence**
- At least **5 qualified trainers** on staff
- Adoption of the **standard training curricula** prescribed in CDA MC 2025-09, Annex A[^42]

**Federations and unions.** Your cooperative's federation can conduct required training for its affiliates. This is often the most cost-effective option because the federation pools CETF remittances from multiple cooperatives and delivers training at scale. Check whether your federation offers an annual training calendar. If it does, align your cooperative's training plan with the federation schedule to reduce costs.

### 10.4.4 Recording and Reporting Training Activities

Every training activity must be documented. This is not optional paperwork -- it is a compliance requirement and your cooperative's proof that officers and members have met their training obligations.

**For every training session, maintain:**

| Document | Purpose | Retention |
|---|---|---|
| **Attendance sheet** | Proves who attended; names, organization, address, gender | Permanent |
| **Certificates of completion** | Proves successful completion; includes provider name and accreditation | Permanent |
| **Training materials** | Documents what was taught; presentation slides, handouts, manuals | 5 years minimum |
| **Pre-test and post-test results** | Measures learning gain; baseline vs. post-training knowledge | 5 years minimum |
| **Evaluation forms** | Measures participant satisfaction and training effectiveness | 5 years minimum |
| **Provider accreditation proof** | Proves the provider was authorized to conduct the training | Permanent |
| **ETF expenditure receipts** | Proves funds were used for authorized purposes | Subject to audit retention rules |

**Reporting requirements.** Under CDA MC 2025-09, all cooperatives must submit a **list of trainings undertaken by officers** to the Regional Office (or CSEA in BARMM) in accordance with the form prescribed by the Supervision and Examination Division.[^43] Records of trainings attended by **members and staff** must be kept by the cooperative and made available during inspection.

**Training records management tip.** Create a **Training Records File** with three sections:

1. **Officers** -- sorted by name, showing all training completed, dates, providers, and certificates
2. **Members** -- sorted alphabetically, showing FCC completion date and any additional training
3. **ETF Utilization** -- chronological record of all ETF expenditures with receipts and matching attendance sheets

Keep this file where your Secretary and Education and Training Committee Chair can access it at any time. When the CSEA inspector arrives, this file should be the first thing you hand over.

## 10.5 Cross-Skill Development

The first four sections of this chapter covered compliance-driven training -- the training the law requires. This section covers something different: training that gives your cooperative a competitive edge. Three methodologies from the business and development world can transform how your cooperative identifies problems, designs solutions, and measures impact.

### 10.5.1 Design Thinking for Cooperative Product Development

**Design thinking** is a structured method for solving problems by starting with the people who experience the problem. It has five phases: **Empathize**, **Define**, **Ideate**, **Prototype**, and **Test**. For cooperatives, this methodology is powerful because it forces you to design products and services around what your members and customers actually need -- not what your Board assumes they need.

**How to apply design thinking in your cooperative:**

**Phase 1 -- Empathize.** Go to your members. Do not send a survey. Visit their farms, their homes, their workplaces. Ask what problems they face. Ask what they wish the cooperative could do. Listen for the problems they do not articulate -- the ones you see in their daily struggles.

*Example: A seaweed cooperative in Tawi-Tawi conducted home visits and discovered that members' biggest problem was not the price of seaweed -- it was the 3-day delay getting their harvest to the buyer in Zamboanga. The members never mentioned logistics because they assumed it was unsolvable. The cooperative did not assume. It investigated.*

**Phase 2 -- Define.** Turn your observations into a clear problem statement. "Our members lose 15-20 percent of their seaweed harvest value because of a 3-day logistics gap between harvest and buyer delivery."

**Phase 3 -- Ideate.** Generate multiple solutions. Do not commit to the first idea. Options might include: a cooperative-owned boat, a refrigerated storage facility, a partnership with a logistics company, or a direct buyer arrangement that eliminates the middleman.

**Phase 4 -- Prototype.** Test the simplest version of your best idea. Before investing millions in a refrigerated facility, rent a cold storage unit for one harvest cycle. Measure whether the 3-day delay decreases and whether member income increases.

**Phase 5 -- Test.** Evaluate the results. Did member income increase? By how much? Was the cost sustainable? Refine and repeat.

**Training your Education and Training Committee in design thinking** takes 8-16 hours. The return is a cooperative that solves real problems instead of guessing at solutions.

### 10.5.2 Theory of Change for Cooperative Impact Measurement

A **Theory of Change (ToC)** is a roadmap that connects your cooperative's activities to the outcomes you want to achieve. It answers the question: "If we do X, then Y will happen, because of Z."

Most cooperatives operate without a Theory of Change. They conduct activities -- training, lending, marketing -- without a clear logic connecting those activities to measurable results. When the CSEA or a funding partner asks, "What impact has your cooperative achieved?" they cannot answer with data. They answer with stories.

Stories matter. But data wins funding.

**A simple Theory of Change for a BARMM agricultural cooperative:**

| Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Problem** | Members earn below PHP 15,000/month from cassava farming due to low yields and no value-added processing |
| **Activities** | (1) Train 50 members in improved cassava cultivation, (2) Establish a cooperative-owned cassava chip processing facility, (3) Secure market contracts with 3 regional distributors |
| **Outputs** | 50 members trained, 1 processing facility operational, 3 market contracts signed |
| **Outcomes** | Member cassava yields increase by 30%, value-added products generate 50% higher revenue per kilogram, members reach PHP 20,000/month average income |
| **Impact** | Cooperative members lifted above the poverty line; BARMM poverty incidence in the community reduced |
| **Assumptions** | Members will adopt improved techniques; market demand for cassava chips is sufficient; cooperative can secure startup capital for the facility |

**Align your ToC with BDP 2023-2028 targets.** Your Theory of Change becomes more powerful when it connects to the Bangsamoro Development Plan. If your cooperative's ToC targets poverty reduction, link it to the BDP's target of reducing poverty incidence from **29.80% to 20-25%**.[^44] If it targets cooperative profitability, link it to **Objective 2.5.1** (profitability of cooperatives increased).[^45] Funding partners and government agencies want to see alignment.

### 10.5.3 Lean Canvas for Cooperative Business Modeling

The **Lean Canvas** is a one-page business model that forces you to answer nine questions about your cooperative's business on a single sheet of paper. It was designed for startups, but it works equally well for cooperatives launching a new product, service, or business line.

**The nine Lean Canvas boxes, adapted for cooperatives:**

| Box | Question | Cooperative Application |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Problem** | What are the top 3 problems your members or customers face? | Low income, limited market access, no value-added processing |
| **2. Customer Segments** | Who are your members and customers? | 200 cassava farmers in Maguindanao del Norte, regional food distributors |
| **3. Unique Value Proposition** | What makes your cooperative's offering different? | "Farm-to-shelf cassava chips -- cooperative-grown, community-processed, halal-certified" |
| **4. Solution** | How does your cooperative solve each problem? | Processing facility, direct market contracts, member training |
| **5. Channels** | How do you reach your customers? | Direct delivery to distributors, cooperative store, trade fairs |
| **6. Revenue Streams** | How does the cooperative earn? | Cassava chip sales, member patronage, processing fees |
| **7. Cost Structure** | What are the major costs? | Equipment, raw materials, labor, transport, packaging |
| **8. Key Metrics** | What numbers tell you the business is working? | Monthly production volume, member income change, customer retention |
| **9. Unfair Advantage** | What can your cooperative do that competitors cannot easily copy? | Member loyalty, cooperative land access, BARMM seaweed/cassava production dominance |

**When to use Lean Canvas in your cooperative:**

- Before launching a new product line
- Before applying for PESO Project support
- Before requesting a loan from a cooperative bank
- When preparing your cooperative's annual business plan
- When evaluating whether to expand into a new market

**Training your officers in Lean Canvas** takes 4-8 hours and pays for itself the first time your cooperative avoids investing in a business idea that sounded good in a Board meeting but collapses under scrutiny on a one-page canvas.

## 10.6 Recommended and Voluntary Training Programs (H1-H15)

MC 2025-09, Section VII lists **fifteen recommended training topics** for officers, members, and staff.[^55] These are voluntary. No hours are prescribed. No sanctions apply. But cooperatives that pursue them build capabilities their competitors lack.

| Code | Recommended Training Topic |
|---|---|
| **H1** | Organizational Development and Management |
| **H2** | Project Development and Management |
| **H3** | Basic Computer Literacy |
| **H4** | Digitalization |
| **H5** | Succession Planning |
| **H6** | Franchising |
| **H7** | Cooperative Business Continuity and Resiliency |
| **H8** | Conflict Management |
| **H9** | Strategic Planning |
| **H10** | Audit Management |
| **H11** | Records Management |
| **H12** | Entrepreneurial and Business Management |
| **H13** | Cooperative Performance Standards |
| **H14** | Risk Management and Business Continuity Management |
| **H15** | Other trainings (e.g., Intellectual Property Rights) |

**The H1-H15 list is illustrative.** MC 2025-09 uses the phrase "including but not limited to." Your cooperative can add any topic that serves its development needs.

**How to prioritize.** Start with your Training Needs Assessment (Section 10.4.1). Match unchecked boxes in **Part C (Strategic Development Needs)** to H1-H15 topics. A cooperative that lacks a strategic plan needs **H9 (Strategic Planning)** before it needs **H6 (Franchising)**. A cooperative with weak records needs **H11 (Records Management)** before **H4 (Digitalization)**.

**For BARMM cooperatives, four topics deserve early attention:**

1. **H7 (Business Continuity and Resiliency)** -- natural disasters, conflict disruptions, and pandemic-era lessons make this essential for Bangsamoro cooperatives.
2. **H8 (Conflict Management)** -- cooperatives in post-conflict communities face internal disputes rooted in clan dynamics, political affiliations, and transitional tensions.
3. **H9 (Strategic Planning)** -- align your 5-year plan with BDP 2023-2028 targets to unlock CSEA and development partner support.
4. **H14 (Risk Management)** -- cooperatives handling savings and credit in GIDA areas face unique risks that standard risk frameworks do not address.

**Fund these from your ETF.** Recommended training is a valid ETF expenditure. Document attendance, provider credentials, and costs the same way you document required training.

## Chapter Summary: What You Must Do Now

This chapter covered a lot of ground. Here is what matters most, in order of urgency.

**Immediate (within 30 days):**

1. **Check whether all current officers have completed required training.** If not, schedule the training immediately. Officers without training are disqualified from office.
2. **Verify your Education and Training Committee is constituted and trained.** Without this committee, nothing in this chapter can happen.
3. **Review your ETF balance.** Know how much you have and how much you have spent.

**Short-term (within 90 days):**

4. **Complete the Training Needs Assessment Checklist** in Section 10.4.1. Identify every gap.
5. **Contact CSEA and your federation** to schedule training for officers and members who need it.
6. **Create a training records file** with three sections: Officers, Members, ETF Utilization.

**Medium-term (within 180 days):**

7. **Develop your annual training plan.** Map every required training to a specific date, provider, and budget.
8. **Ensure all members admitted in the past year** have completed the 16-hour Fundamentals of Cooperatives Course.
9. **Introduce cross-skill development** -- start with one Design Thinking workshop or one Lean Canvas session for your Board.

**Ongoing:**

10. **Adopt a continuing education program** for both officers and members, reflected in your annual development plan.
11. **Track training outcomes** against BDP 2023-2028 targets. Report impact, not just attendance.
12. **Remit 50% of CETF** to your federation or union annually. Demand the three required schedules in return.

Education, training, and information are not the fifth principle by accident. They are the principle that makes the other six possible. Voluntary membership means nothing if members do not understand what they joined. Democratic control is hollow if officers do not know how to govern. Economic participation fails if the Treasurer cannot read a financial statement. Invest in your people. Everything else follows.
