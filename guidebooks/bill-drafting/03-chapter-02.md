# Chapter 2 — From Legislative Directive to Bill: The Drafting Process

## 2.1 Overview: Seven Stages from Legislative Directive to Filing

An MP hands you a legislative directive. The topic is complex — maybe a new regulatory framework, maybe a budget measure with a tight deadline. The research has not started, the stakeholders have not been consulted, and the Bills and Index Division expects a properly formatted bill. What do you do first? What do you do second? This chapter gives you the answer. Between the moment you receive that directive and the moment the bill is filed, there are seven distinct stages of work, and each one exists to prevent a specific kind of failure.

The seven stages are:

1. **Legislative Directive** — You receive, document, and confirm the MP's policy direction.
2. **Legislative Research** — You gather the legal, policy, and factual foundation for the bill.
3. **Preparing the First Draft** — You build a working outline, write the bill text, and map it against existing laws.
4. **Consultation** — You test the draft against the knowledge of experts, implementing agencies, and affected communities.
5. **Review and Revision** — You absorb what you learned from consultations and fix the draft.
6. **Presentation and Finalization with the MP** — You present the revised draft to the MP, incorporate final comments, and write the Explanatory Note.
7. **Co-Authorship and Filing** — You identify co-authors, secure their approval, and file the bill.

These stages are sequential in theory, but in practice you will sometimes loop back. Research may reveal that the original directive needs refining. Consultations may expose a flaw that sends you back to research. A co-author may request changes that require a new round of internal review. That is normal. The stages give you a clear path forward while allowing the flexibility that real legislative work demands.

A note about timing: the seven stages do not all take equal effort. Stage 2 (Research) and Stage 4 (Consultation) are usually the most time-intensive. Stage 1 might take a single meeting. Stage 7 depends on how many co-authors you pursue and how responsive they are. As you gain experience, you will develop a sense of how long each stage takes for different types of bills — a narrow amendatory bill moves faster than a bill creating a new ministry from scratch.

One thing should be clear from the start: this process is designed to produce bills that survive scrutiny. A bill drafted without proper research gets torn apart in committee. A bill drafted without consultation gets opposed by the very agencies meant to implement it. A bill filed without co-authors may never get calendared for debate. Each stage exists for a reason, and skipping any of them weakens the final product.

The rest of this chapter treats each stage in detail — what you do, what you produce, and what decisions you need to make before moving on.

## 2.2 Stage 1 — Legislative Directive

This is where the process begins. The MP has a policy direction — sometimes clear and specific, sometimes broad and aspirational. Your job is to receive that direction, document it, check it against the MP's broader legislative agenda, and get formal approval to proceed.

### 2.2.1 Receiving the Legislative Direction

A legislative directive can arrive in many forms. The MP might give you a detailed written brief. More often, the directive comes as a conversation — a passing remark during a staff meeting, a response to a constituent's complaint, a reaction to a news report, or an instruction relayed through a chief of staff.

However it arrives, your first task is to listen carefully and ask the right questions. You need to understand:

- **What problem is the MP trying to solve?** Not the solution — the problem. If the MP says "we need a bill creating a Bangsamoro scholarship program," the problem might be that qualified students from the region cannot afford higher education. Understanding the problem, not just the proposed solution, gives you room to draft a bill that actually works.
- **Who is affected?** Which communities, sectors, or populations does this bill target? Is it region-wide or limited to specific areas?
- **What is the urgency?** Is there a political window — an upcoming committee hearing, a plenary session, a public event — that creates a deadline?
- **Has this been attempted before?** The MP may or may not know whether similar bills have been filed in previous sessions of the BTA Parliament or in the national Congress. You will verify this during research, but the MP's awareness shapes the starting point.
- **What is the MP's non-negotiable position?** Every MP has aspects of the bill they will not compromise on. Identify these early so you do not waste time drafting provisions the MP will reject.

Do not filter or second-guess the directive at this stage. Your role is to capture the MP's intent as accurately as possible. Legal and policy analysis comes later.

**Practical tip:** If the directive comes verbally, take detailed notes during the conversation and send a written summary to the MP (or the MP's chief of staff) within 24 hours. Do not rely on memory. A one-paragraph summary with the key points — problem, target population, urgency, non-negotiables — prevents misunderstandings that surface weeks later when you are deep into drafting.

**Common pitfall:** Some directives are solutions looking for a problem. An MP might say "file a bill creating a Bangsamoro Sports Commission" without articulating the problem — maybe the existing sports programs under the ministry are already working fine, or maybe the real issue is funding, not institutional structure. Your job is not to push back at this stage, but to probe gently. Ask "what problem would the Sports Commission solve that the current setup cannot?" The answer shapes everything that follows.

### 2.2.2 Documenting the Initial Legislative Template

Once you have a clear understanding of the directive, you create an **Initial Legislative Template** — a short document (usually one to two pages) that captures the bill's essential parameters before research begins.

The template should include:

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Working Title** | A descriptive title for the proposed bill. This will change during drafting, but you need a handle to refer to. |
| **Policy Problem** | A clear statement of the problem the bill addresses. Two to three sentences. |
| **Proposed Solution** | The MP's preferred approach, stated in plain terms. |
| **Target Beneficiaries / Affected Population** | Who benefits and who is affected. |
| **Scope** | Region-wide, province-specific, sector-specific, or institutional. |
| **Lead Author** | The sponsoring MP. |
| **Potential Co-Authors** | Other MPs who might support the bill — based on their committee assignments, advocacy areas, or past statements. |
| **Target Filing Date** | When the MP wants the bill filed. |
| **Related Legislation** | Any existing laws, pending bills, or previous legislative attempts the MP is aware of. You will expand this list during research. |
| **Notes / Special Instructions** | Anything else — the MP's preferences on structure, provisions to include or exclude, political sensitivities. |

This template is a living document. You will update it as research progresses and the bill takes shape. But at Stage 1, its purpose is simple: create a written record of what the MP wants so that everyone — the MP, the chief of staff, you, and any other staff involved — is working from the same starting point.

**Example:** Suppose the MP's directive is about protecting internally displaced persons (IDPs) in the Bangsamoro region. Your Initial Legislative Template might read:

- **Working Title:** Bangsamoro Internally Displaced Persons Protection and Assistance Act
- **Policy Problem:** Thousands of families displaced by armed conflict and natural disasters in the BARMM lack access to consistent protection, return assistance, and livelihood support. Existing national frameworks are not tailored to the Bangsamoro context.
- **Proposed Solution:** Establish a regional IDP protection framework with clear government obligations for shelter, health, education, and livelihood support during displacement and return.
- **Target Beneficiaries:** All internally displaced persons and communities within the BARMM.
- **Scope:** Region-wide.

Even this short example shows how the template forces you to translate a general directive ("do something about IDPs") into specific, actionable parameters.

### 2.2.3 Aligning with the MP's Legislative Agenda

Most MPs maintain a **legislative agenda** — a list of bills they plan to file during a session or across their full term. Some agendas are formal documents shared publicly. Others exist only as informal lists discussed in staff meetings. Either way, you need to know where this new bill fits.

Ask yourself:

- **Is this bill already on the agenda?** If so, it has an expected priority level and timeline. Align your work accordingly.
- **Does it conflict with other bills on the agenda?** Two bills from the same MP should not contradict each other. If the MP is pushing a bill to strengthen provincial government autonomy and a separate bill that centralizes a function at the regional level, you need to flag the tension.
- **Does it complement other bills?** Sometimes a new directive fits naturally as a companion to an existing agenda item. A bill on agricultural support, for example, might pair well with a pending bill on rural infrastructure. Identifying these connections early can help with co-authorship strategy later.
- **What is the priority?** If the MP's agenda already has ten pending bills and the legislative staff is stretched thin, you need a realistic conversation about timelines.

If the MP does not have a written legislative agenda, this is a good opportunity to help build one. Even a simple spreadsheet tracking bill titles, status, target dates, and assigned staff brings order to the office's legislative work.

A useful agenda tracker includes these columns:

| Bill No. | Working Title | Status | Priority | Target Filing | Assigned Staff | Notes |
|----------|--------------|--------|----------|---------------|----------------|-------|
| — | IDP Protection Act | Stage 2 (Research) | High | May 2025 | [Name] | Linked to BDP Chapter 11 |
| — | Bangsamoro ECCD Act | Stage 4 (Consultation) | Medium | August 2025 | [Name] | Coordinating with MBHTE |

This tracker gives the MP and the entire staff a real-time picture of the office's legislative pipeline — what is moving, what is stalled, and where resources need to shift.

### 2.2.4 Securing MP Approval of the Initial Legislative Template

Before you invest days or weeks in research and drafting, get the MP's explicit approval to proceed. This approval is based on the Initial Legislative Template — the MP reviews it, confirms that it captures the directive accurately, and authorizes the next stage.

This might seem overly formal for a small legislative staff, but it serves two purposes. First, it prevents scope creep. Without a documented starting point, the bill's direction can drift as different staff members add their own interpretations. Second, it creates accountability. If the bill eventually diverges from the original intent — for good reasons discovered during research — you can point back to the template and explain why.

The approval does not need to be elaborate. A signed copy of the template, an email confirmation, or even a note in the office records will do. The point is to have a clear "go" signal before moving to Stage 2.

## 2.3 Stage 2 — Legislative Research

Research is the foundation of a strong bill. You may be tempted to jump straight into drafting — especially if the MP is impatient or the timeline is tight — but a bill written without thorough research is a bill that will be picked apart in committee, challenged on constitutional grounds, or rendered unimplementable by conflicting provisions in existing law.

This stage has four parts: gathering legislative requirements, working with the Parliament's research arm, conducting your own policy and legal research, and preparing a Legislative Research Briefer that captures everything you found.

### 2.3.1 Gathering Legislative Requirements

Before diving into databases and statute books, step back and define what you need to know. Based on the Initial Legislative Template, identify your research questions:

- **Constitutional and BOL questions:**[^1] Does the Bangsamoro Government have the power to legislate on this subject matter? Is the subject within the exclusive powers listed in the BOL, or is it a shared or concurrent power with the national government? Are there constitutional limits?
- **Existing law questions:** What national laws already cover this area? What Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts (BAAs) are already in force? Are there implementing rules and regulations (IRRs) that add detail to those laws? Do any local government ordinances within the BARMM touch on this subject?
- **Policy questions:** What do experts, practitioners, and affected communities say about this problem? Are there research studies, policy papers, or international models relevant to the proposal?
- **Budget and implementation questions:** How much will this cost? Which agencies will implement it? Do those agencies have the capacity?
- **Shari'ah and gender questions:** Does the bill intersect with Islamic law? Does it affect men and women differently?

Write these questions down. They become your research checklist. Each question should be answerable — "Does the Bangsamoro Parliament have exclusive power to legislate on IDP protection?" is answerable. "What is the best approach to IDP protection?" is too broad. Break broad questions into smaller, specific ones that your research can actually resolve.

The checklist also helps you recognize when you have enough information to stop researching and start drafting. Research can become a form of procrastination — there is always one more study to read, one more law to check. Your research questions define the finish line.

### 2.3.2 Working with the Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS)

The **Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS)** is the Parliament's institutional research arm. It exists to support all MP offices with legislative research, legal analysis, and policy review. You do not have to do everything yourself — and you should not try to.

Here is how to work with the PRLS:

**What the PRLS can do for you:**
- Research existing laws and pending bills related to your proposal
- Provide legal opinions on constitutional and BOL compliance
- Analyze policy options and their likely effects
- Prepare background papers on specific legislative subjects
- Review draft bills for legal consistency and technical correctness

**How to request PRLS support:**
- Submit a written research request. Be specific about what you need. "Please research all national laws related to early childhood education in autonomous regions" is far more useful than "please look into education."
- Include a copy of your Initial Legislative Template so the PRLS understands the bill's context.
- Set a realistic deadline. PRLS serves the entire Parliament, and complex research requests take time.

**How to use PRLS output:**
- PRLS research supplements your own work — it does not replace it. You still need to verify findings, apply them to your specific bill, and exercise judgment about what matters and what does not.
- If the PRLS provides a legal opinion that conflicts with your own analysis, do not ignore it. Resolve the disagreement before moving forward. Bring it to the MP if necessary.
- Credit the PRLS in your Legislative Research Briefer when you use their findings.

**Practical tip:** Build a working relationship with the PRLS staff early. Introduce yourself, explain the MP's legislative agenda, and give them a heads-up about upcoming research requests. A five-minute courtesy visit saves days of turnaround time later.

### 2.3.3 Conducting Policy and Legal Research

While the PRLS handles its part, you conduct your own research in parallel. Your research follows the hierarchy of laws established in Chapter 1 — starting from the top and working down.

**Step 1: The 1987 Philippine Constitution**

Start here. Check whether the subject matter falls within the national government's reserved powers or the Bangsamoro Government's exclusive and concurrent powers. Key provisions include:

- Article X (Local Government),[^2] which provides the constitutional basis for autonomous regions
- Article II (Declaration of Principles and State Policies), which may contain relevant policy mandates
- The Bill of Rights (Article III), if the proposal affects individual rights
- Any other provisions specific to the bill's subject — education, health, taxation, environment, etc.

**Step 2: Republic Act No. 11054 — The Bangsamoro Organic Law (BOL)**

The BOL is your primary reference for determining what the Bangsamoro Parliament can and cannot do. Check:

- **Article V (Powers of Government)**[^3] — Is the subject in the list of exclusive powers (Section 2)? Does the general welfare clause (Section 3) apply?
- **Article VI (Intergovernmental Relations)**[^4] — Does the bill touch an area of shared jurisdiction between the Bangsamoro Government and the National Government?
- **Article VII (The Bangsamoro Government)** and **Article VIII (Wali)** — Are there structural requirements or limitations that affect the bill?
- **Article IX (Basic Rights)**[^5] — Does the bill affect any of the rights guaranteed under the BOL?
- **Article X (Bangsamoro Justice System)**[^6] — If the bill touches on Shari'ah matters, what are the boundaries?
- **Article XII (Fiscal Autonomy)**[^7] — If the bill has financial implications, what does the BOL say about taxation, revenue sharing, and the block grant?
- **Article XIII (Regional Economy and Patrimony)**[^8] — Does the bill affect economic zones, natural resources, or regional patrimony?

Do not skim the BOL. Read the relevant articles carefully. Many drafting errors come from overlooking a single provision buried in an article the drafter assumed was irrelevant.

**Example:** A drafter working on a revenue bill might focus on Article XII (Fiscal Autonomy) and miss a provision in Article XII, Section 9 (Limitations on the Taxing Powers)[^9] or an enumerated power boundary in Article V (Powers of Government) that limits the types of taxes the Bangsamoro Government may impose. The result — a bill that the Supreme Court could strike down — would have been avoided by reading the BOL systematically, not selectively.

**Step 3: National Laws and IRRs**

Search for all national laws that cover the same subject matter. Use the following sources:

- The Official Gazette (officialgazette.gov.ph)
- Chan Robles Virtual Law Library or LAWPHiL Project for statute texts
- The relevant national agency's website for IRRs

For each law you find, note:

- What it requires, prohibits, or authorizes
- Whether it applies to the Bangsamoro region specifically or to the Philippines generally
- Whether the BOL grants the Bangsamoro Parliament the power to amend, supplement, or replace its provisions within the region
- Whether an IRR adds requirements not in the statute itself

**Step 4: Existing Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts (BAAs)**

Search the Parliament's records for any BAAs already passed on the same or related subjects. If a BAA exists, your bill may need to amend it rather than create a new, parallel law — or you may need to include a repealing clause to avoid conflicts.

Check the index of enacted BAAs maintained by the Bills and Index Division and the Office of the Secretary General.

**Step 5: Local Government Ordinances**

If the bill affects local government units (LGUs) within the BARMM — provinces, municipalities, cities, barangays — check whether any local ordinances cover the same ground. A bill that contradicts existing local ordinances without explicitly addressing the conflict creates implementation problems.

**Step 6: Policy Research and International Models**

Beyond the law, look for:

- **Academic studies** on the problem the bill addresses. Google Scholar, university research repositories, and think tank publications are good starting points.
- **International models.** How have other countries — especially those with autonomous regions, federal systems, or Islamic governance traditions — handled similar issues? The experience of Malaysia's Sabah and Sarawak states, Indonesia's Aceh province, or the Kurdistan Region of Iraq may offer useful parallels.
- **Philippine national agencies' data** — statistics, reports, and assessments that quantify the problem.
- **NGO and civil society reports** — organizations working in the BARMM often have on-the-ground data that government sources lack.

Not every bill needs all of this. A straightforward bill renaming a government office requires less research than a bill overhauling the region's education system. Use judgment about how deep to go, but always cover Steps 1 through 4.

**A note on organizing your research files:** Create a dedicated folder — digital or physical — for each bill. Store all research materials in it: downloaded statutes, printouts of BOL articles, PRLS reports, policy papers, interview notes. Label files clearly. Six months from now, when the bill is in committee and a member asks "where did you get this figure?" you need to find the source in minutes, not days. Good file hygiene during research prevents scrambles later.

#### The 5-Step Legal Research Process

The six steps above are your research tasks. But how do you organize the process itself — especially when the legal landscape is complex, with multiple tiers of authority that may conflict? The following methodology provides a structured approach.

**Step 1: Issue Analysis.** Before searching any law, frame the legal question precisely. Identify:

- What legal question does this bill raise? (e.g., "Does Parliament have the authority to regulate digital commerce within BARMM?")
- Which jurisdiction applies — BARMM exclusive powers, concurrent with national, or national reserved?
- What search terms will you use? (Include synonyms: "digital commerce" / "e-commerce" / "electronic transactions" / "online business")
- Which tiers of the legal hierarchy are most likely to be relevant?

A precise question produces precise research. A vague question produces scattered results.

**Step 2: Landscape Review.** Before diving into specific provisions, understand the legal landscape:

- Review the 7-tier legal hierarchy (Chapter 1, Section 1.2) to orient yourself on where authority sits for this subject
- Consult the BDP 2023-2028 for development priorities related to the bill's subject
- Check institutional context — which ministry or agency currently handles this area? What existing programs exist?
- If the bill aligns with the government's strategic agenda — currently the Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028, OCM Executive Order No. 2, s. 2026) — note the relevant pillar

This step prevents tunnel vision. Understanding the landscape first means you know what to look for when you start searching specific provisions.

**Step 3: Systematic Source Search.** Now search each tier methodically (this is Steps 1-5 of Section 2.3.3 above). For each authority found, record:

- Full citation (Philippine Manual of Legal Citations format)
- Tier number in the legal hierarchy
- Verbatim provision text (or a precise summary)
- What mandate or authority it grants or imposes
- Whether it supports, constrains, or conflicts with the proposed bill

**Step 4: Provision Chain Trace.** After finding initial provisions, trace what they reference. Legal provisions do not exist in isolation — they reference other provisions, define terms used elsewhere, and create chains of authority.

For each key provision found in Step 3:

- Extract traceable concepts: if a provision references "rights," trace which rights are enumerated elsewhere in the BOL; if it references "powers," trace the specific enumerated powers in Article V, Section 2
- Read the full relevant source document — not just keyword matches. Many critical provisions are missed because the drafter searched only for specific terms rather than reading the relevant article in full
- Filter for relevance: not every traced provision is relevant to the specific research question
- Follow chains up to three levels deep (provision A references provision B, which defines concept C)

This step is critical for BOL and BAA No. 13 provisions, where authority chains can be complex.

**Step 5: Verification ("Good Law" Check).** Confirm that every authority you plan to cite is still in force:

- Has this BAA been amended or repealed by a later BAA? The BTA Parliament has enacted over 89 BAAs since 2019 — laws change frequently.
- Has the national law been modified?
- Has an executive issuance been superseded by a later one?
- Has the Supreme Court issued any ruling that narrows or expands the provision's scope?

Mark each authority's status: "In force," "Amended by [law]," "Superseded by [law]," or "Under review."

A bill that cites a repealed or superseded provision is a bill that will fail at committee — or worse, be enacted with a defective legal basis.

**Output: The Research Memorandum.** Compile your research findings into a Research Memorandum — a structured document that organizes all applicable authorities by tier. This memorandum becomes the foundation for your Legislative Research Briefer (Section 2.3.4) and the legal basis section of your bill's Explanatory Note. Its format:

| # | Tier | Source | Provision | Authority/Mandate | Status |
|---|------|--------|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| 1 | 1 | 1987 Constitution | Art. X, Sec. 15 | Constitutional basis for autonomous regions | In force |
| 2 | 2 | BOL (RA 11054) | Art. V, Sec. 2(d) | Exclusive power: creation of sources of revenues | In force |
| 3 | 4 | BAA No. 46 | Sec. 3 | Revenue Code provisions on local taxation | In force |

Include: key provisions with verbatim text, conflict analysis with hierarchy resolution, gaps identified (authority implied but not explicit), and your search record (for audit and reproducibility).

This memorandum is a working document — it evolves as the bill moves through drafting and committee review. Update it when you discover new authorities or when existing ones are amended.

### 2.3.4 Preparing the Legislative Research Briefer

Everything you found during research goes into a single document: the **Legislative Research Briefer**. This document serves two audiences — the MP, who needs to understand the legal and policy terrain before reviewing the first draft, and you, who will use it as a reference throughout the drafting process.

The briefer follows a standard structure:

#### 2.3.4.1 Brief Description of the Proposed Measure

Open with a plain-language summary of the proposed bill — what it does, who it affects, and why it is needed. This should be understandable to someone with no background in the subject. Three to five paragraphs.

Include:
- The policy problem, restated from the Initial Legislative Template but refined by your research
- The MP's proposed approach
- A brief explanation of why legislation is needed (as opposed to an executive order, administrative regulation, or no action at all)

#### 2.3.4.2 Related Laws and Issuances

List every law — constitutional provisions, national statutes, BOL provisions, BAAs, IRRs, and local ordinances — that you found during research. For each law, provide:

- Full title and citation (e.g., Republic Act No. 10931, the Universal Access to Quality Tertiary Education Act)
- Date enacted
- A one-to-three sentence summary of its relevant provisions
- Whether it applies to the BARMM
- Whether the proposed bill conflicts with, supplements, or replaces any of its provisions

Organize this section by the hierarchy of laws: Constitution first, then the BOL, then national laws, then BAAs, then local ordinances. This organization makes it easy for the MP and for you to see where the bill sits within the existing legal structure.

#### 2.3.4.3 Policy and Legal Analysis

This is the analytical core of the briefer. Here you move beyond listing laws and into interpreting them. Address:

- **Constitutionality and BOL compliance.** Is the bill within the Bangsamoro Parliament's legislative power? If the subject involves concurrent powers, how should the bill be structured to avoid conflict with national law?
- **Consistency with existing BAAs.** Does the bill conflict with any enacted Bangsamoro law? If so, which provisions need a repealing or amendatory clause?
- **Policy options.** You may have found more than one way to address the problem. Present the options with their advantages and disadvantages. The MP makes the final decision, but you supply the analysis.
- **Legal risks.** Could this bill be challenged before the Supreme Court? Is there a legal theory that would support a challenge? What can be done in the drafting to reduce that risk?

Write this section in plain language. Avoid the temptation to impress with legal jargon. The MP — who may or may not be a lawyer — needs to understand the analysis, not marvel at your vocabulary.

#### 2.3.4.4 Impact Assessment

Estimate the bill's likely effects on:

- **Budget.** How much will implementation cost? Where will the money come from — the annual appropriations act, the block grant, special funds, or revenue generated by the bill itself?
- **Institutional capacity.** Does the implementing agency exist? If so, does it have the staff, expertise, and infrastructure to carry out the bill's mandates? If the bill creates a new body, what are the setup costs and timeline?
- **Economic effects.** Will the bill create jobs, attract investment, increase costs for businesses, or change market dynamics in the region?
- **Social effects.** How will the bill affect communities — particularly vulnerable populations like internally displaced persons, indigenous peoples, women, and youth?

Not every bill requires a full impact assessment. A bill creating an awards program for outstanding teachers has different analytical needs than a bill restructuring the region's tax system. Scale the analysis to the bill.

#### 2.3.4.5 Shari'ah and Gender Considerations

The Bangsamoro region operates under a distinct legal order that includes the Shari'ah justice system and a population whose cultural and religious values shape how laws are received and implemented. This section addresses two questions:

**Shari'ah considerations:**
- Does the bill affect matters governed by Shari'ah law — personal status, family law, property succession, commercial transactions among Muslims?
- If so, does the bill respect the boundaries established by the BOL between the Shari'ah system and the regular legal system?
- Would the bill benefit from review by scholars with expertise in Islamic jurisprudence?

**Gender considerations:**
- Does the bill affect men and women differently — in access to services, economic opportunities, legal protections, or political participation?
- Does it advance gender equity or, unintentionally, reinforce existing barriers?
- Are there provisions that should be added to address gender-specific impacts?

These are not box-checking exercises. A bill on land titling that ignores Islamic inheritance rules will create enforcement problems. A bill on livelihood programs that assumes all beneficiaries can travel freely ignores the reality that women in some communities face mobility restrictions. Surface these issues during research so they can be addressed in drafting.

#### 2.3.4.6 Recommendations

Close the briefer with your recommendations to the MP. Based on everything you found, advise on:

- Whether to proceed with drafting (and if not, why)
- Which policy option to pursue
- Key provisions the bill should include
- Potential political challenges and how to address them
- Suggested co-authors and allies
- A realistic timeline for drafting, consultation, and filing

Keep the recommendations concise and actionable. The MP does not need a summary of the entire briefer — they just read it. Give them a clear "here is what I recommend and here is what I need from you to move forward."

**Practical tip:** Present the briefer to the MP in person or over a call, not just as an email attachment. Walking the MP through the analysis lets you answer questions in real time, gauge their reactions, and clarify points before drafting begins. If the MP is too busy for a full presentation, prepare a one-page executive summary and offer to discuss any section in detail.

## 2.4 Stage 3 — Preparing the First Draft

With the research briefer approved and the MP's go-ahead, you begin drafting. This stage is where the bill starts to look like a bill — with a title, a structure, and actual provisions. But do not start writing bill text immediately. Build the scaffold first.

### 2.4.1 Preparing the Working Outline

Before writing any provision, create a working outline of the bill's structure. The outline organizes your thinking and prevents the common problem of a bill that wanders from topic to topic without logical flow.

A working outline typically follows this structure:

```
Title
Declaration of Policy (Section 1)
Definition of Terms (Section 2)

ARTICLE I — [FIRST MAJOR TOPIC]
  Section 3 — [First provision]
  Section 4 — [Second provision]
  ...

ARTICLE II — [SECOND MAJOR TOPIC]
  Section X — [First provision]
  Section X+1 — [Second provision]
  ...

ARTICLE III — [INSTITUTIONAL PROVISIONS]
  Section X — [Creation of body, if applicable]
  Section X — [Composition]
  Section X — [Powers and functions]
  ...

ARTICLE IV — [FUNDING AND APPROPRIATIONS]
  Section X — [Appropriations]
  Section X — [Source of funds]
  ...

ARTICLE V — [FINAL PROVISIONS]
  Section X — [Implementing Rules and Regulations]
  Section X — [Separability Clause]
  Section X — [Repealing Clause]
  Section X — [Effectivity]
```

Under each section heading, write a one-to-two sentence note describing what the section will contain. This turns the outline into a roadmap you can share with the MP for early feedback before investing time in full drafting.

Not every bill needs all of these elements. A short bill amending a single provision of an existing BAA may have only a handful of sections. A major bill creating a new institution might have dozens. The outline should reflect the bill's actual scope.

**Practical tip:** If the bill addresses a subject already covered by a national law that the BARMM is adapting for regional implementation, use that national law's structure as a starting point for your outline. This makes the Comparative Matrix (discussed next in Section 2.4.3) easier to build and helps committee members see how the regional bill relates to the national framework.

### 2.4.2 Drafting the Bill

With the outline approved (or at least reviewed) by the MP, begin writing the bill text. The technical requirements of bill drafting — format, structure, language, and standard provisions — are covered in Chapter 3. Here, the focus is on the drafting process itself.

**Start with the provisions you understand best.** You do not have to draft in order from Section 1 to the last section. If the institutional provisions are clearest in your mind, start there. If the definition of terms depends on how you draft the substantive provisions, leave it for later. Build momentum by writing what you can write well, then fill in the gaps.

**Keep the research briefer open beside you.** Every provision you write should be traceable to a policy objective identified in the research. If you find yourself drafting a section that does not connect to any finding in the briefer, ask whether it belongs in the bill at all.

**Write in plain, direct language.** Legislation must be precise, but precision is not the same as complexity. A provision that says "Any person who violates Section 5 of this Act shall be subject to a fine of not less than fifty thousand pesos" is both precise and clear. A provision buried in three levels of subordinate clauses, cross-references, and qualifications may be precise, but nobody will understand it — including the judge who has to interpret it.

**Flag uncertainties as you go.** You will encounter questions during drafting that your research did not answer. Mark these with a visible tag — [TO CONFIRM], [RESEARCH NEEDED], [CHECK WITH MP] — so you do not forget them. A bill should not go to the MP for review with unresolved questions hidden inside it.

**Draft with implementation in mind.** For every provision, ask: Who enforces this? How? With what resources? If you cannot answer these questions, the provision may be aspirational language rather than enforceable law. Aspirational language belongs in the Declaration of Policy section. Substantive provisions need teeth.

**Practical tip:** Keep a running "issues list" in a separate document. Every time you encounter a policy question, a legal uncertainty, or a structural choice that could go either way, note it down. This list becomes your agenda for the MP review session after the first draft is complete.

### 2.4.3 Preparing the Comparative Matrix

The Comparative Matrix is one of your most important tools. It is a table that places your draft bill's provisions side by side with the provisions of related existing laws — showing where the bill aligns, where it conflicts, and where it fills gaps.

A basic Comparative Matrix looks like this:

| Subject / Provision | Proposed Bill | National Law (e.g., RA 10931)[^10] | Existing BAA (e.g., BAA No. 18)[^11] | Notes / Analysis |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Coverage / Scope | "All qualified Bangsamoro residents..." (Sec. 3) | "All Filipino citizens..." (Sec. 4) | Not addressed | Proposed bill narrows scope to BARMM — within BOL authority |
| Implementing Agency | MBHTE (Sec. 12) | CHED nationally (Sec. 8) | MBHTE (Sec. 6) | Consistent with existing BAA |
| Funding Source | Annual GAA + special fund (Sec. 18) | National GAA (Sec. 15) | Block grant allocation (Sec. 10) | Check if dual-source funding creates accounting issues |
| Penalty for Violations | Fine of P50,000 (Sec. 22) | None | Fine of P20,000 (Sec. 14) | Proposed penalty is higher — deliberate policy choice or oversight? |

**Why the matrix matters:**

- **It catches conflicts.** If your bill says one thing and an existing BAA says the opposite, the matrix makes the conflict visible. You can then decide whether to include a repealing clause, an amendatory provision, or a reconciliation mechanism.
- **It reveals gaps.** If a national law covers a topic that your bill does not address, the matrix shows the gap. You can then decide whether the gap is intentional (because the national law already applies) or an oversight.
- **It supports committee deliberations.** When the bill reaches committee, members will ask how it relates to existing law. The Comparative Matrix gives you — and the MP — a ready-made answer.
- **It demonstrates due diligence.** A bill accompanied by a Comparative Matrix signals that the drafter did the work. This builds credibility with committee chairs, legal staff, and fellow MPs.

**How to build the matrix:**

1. List your bill's major provisions in the first column. Group them by subject (coverage, definitions, institutional setup, funding, penalties, etc.), not necessarily by section number.
2. For each provision, find the corresponding provision (if any) in each related law. If the related law does not address the subject, write "Not addressed" or "Silent."
3. In the Notes/Analysis column, flag conflicts, explain alignments, and note any questions that need resolution.
4. Add as many comparison columns as needed. If five existing laws are relevant, the matrix has five comparison columns. Do not limit yourself to one or two.

**Practical tip:** Build the matrix as you draft, not after. When you write a provision, immediately check it against the related laws and add it to the matrix. This real-time comparison catches problems early, before they are buried in 30 pages of bill text.

### 2.4.4 Review of the First Draft

Before the first draft goes to anyone outside the legislative staff, conduct an internal review. This is your chance to catch errors, fill gaps, and tighten the language before the MP or consultants see it.

Your internal review checklist:

- **Structural completeness.** Does the bill have all required parts — title, declaration of policy, definition of terms, substantive provisions, institutional setup (if applicable), funding, final provisions?
- **Internal consistency.** Do the defined terms match how they are used throughout the bill? Does Section 12 contradict Section 7? Are cross-references accurate?
- **Legal compliance.** Does every provision stay within the Parliament's legislative authority under the BOL? Is the bill consistent with the Constitution?
- **Comparative Matrix alignment.** Does the matrix reflect the current state of the draft? Have you addressed all conflicts flagged in the matrix?
- **Drafting standards.** Does the bill follow the format and language conventions expected by the Parliament? (See Chapter 3 for these standards.)
- **Issues list.** Review your running issues list. Have all flagged items been resolved? If any remain open, note them for the MP's attention.

If your office has other legislative staff, ask a colleague to read the draft cold — without the benefit of your research and thinking. Fresh eyes catch things the drafter misses.

Only after this internal review should the first draft go to the MP for initial feedback. Present it together with the working outline, the Comparative Matrix, and the issues list. This gives the MP everything needed to evaluate the draft in context.

**What the first draft is — and what it is not:** The first draft is a working document, not a finished product. It will change after consultations. It will change again after the MP's review. Resist the urge to polish every sentence at this stage. Focus on getting the substance right — the right provisions in the right order, saying the right things. Language refinement comes later. A perfectly worded bill with wrong policy choices is worthless; a rough draft with sound policy choices can be polished into a strong law.

## 2.5 Stage 4 — Consultation

Drafting in isolation produces bills that look good on paper but fail in practice. The consultation stage takes your first draft out of the office and exposes it to people who know things you do not — subject-matter experts, implementing agencies, and affected communities.

Consultation is not a formality. It is where you find out whether the bill will actually work.

### 2.5.1 Interviews and RTDs with Experts

**Interviews** are one-on-one or small-group conversations with people who have deep knowledge of the bill's subject matter — academics, practitioners, retired government officials, technical specialists, and civil society leaders.

**Round Table Discussions (RTDs)** bring several experts together in a structured conversation about the bill. The advantage of an RTD over individual interviews is that experts challenge each other's assumptions, which produces insights you would not get from separate conversations.

**How to plan and conduct expert consultations:**

1. **Identify the right experts.** Start with the people cited in your research — authors of studies, leaders of relevant organizations, former officials who implemented similar programs. Ask the PRLS and the MP's network for recommendations.

2. **Prepare a consultation brief.** Send participants a short document (3-5 pages) before the meeting. Include the policy problem, the bill's proposed approach, and specific questions you want to discuss. Do not send the full draft text unless you want a line-by-line review — at this stage, you want feedback on the policy approach, not the comma placement.

3. **Ask focused questions.** Do not open with "what do you think?" Open with specific questions:
   - "The bill proposes X to address Y. Based on your experience, will X work? What are the risks?"
   - "We are choosing between approach A and approach B. What are the trade-offs?"
   - "What implementation challenges should we anticipate?"
   - "Is there anything we are missing?"

4. **Listen more than you talk.** Your job in consultation is to gather information, not defend the draft. If an expert criticizes the approach, dig into why. The criticism might be wrong — but it also might save the bill from a fatal flaw.

5. **Document everything.** Take detailed notes. Record the meeting if participants consent. You will need to report these findings back to the MP and use them in the revision stage.

### 2.5.2 Consultation with Implementing Agencies

Any bill that creates a program, establishes an office, mandates reporting, or requires enforcement needs the buy-in — or at least the informed input — of the agencies responsible for implementation.

For Bangsamoro bills, the relevant agencies are typically:

- **Bangsamoro ministries** (Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education; Ministry of Health; Ministry of Social Services and Development; etc.)
- **Attached agencies and offices** of the Bangsamoro Government
- **National government agencies** with offices or operations in the BARMM, if the bill involves concurrent or shared powers
- **Local government units** (provincial, municipal, city, barangay) if the bill affects their functions

**What to ask implementing agencies:**

- "Do you currently have the staff, budget, and infrastructure to carry out the bill's mandates?"
- "What would you need that you do not currently have?"
- "Are there provisions in the bill that conflict with your existing operations or standing orders?"
- "What implementation timeline is realistic?"
- "How would you draft the IRR for this bill?"

The last question is particularly revealing. If the agency cannot explain how they would implement a provision, the provision may need to be rewritten.

**Practical tip:** Go to the agency, not the other way around. Visiting the ministry or office that will implement the bill shows respect for their work and gives you a chance to observe their operations firsthand. A drafter who has seen the inside of the implementing agency writes more realistic provisions than one who has only read about it.

**Warning:** Implementing agencies may resist provisions that expand their responsibilities without increasing their budget or staffing. This resistance is legitimate and should be taken seriously. It does not mean you drop the provision — it means you address the resource gap in the bill or in the accompanying budget request.

### 2.5.3 Consultations with Sectors and Constituencies

Bills affect real people. The consultation process should include the voices of those who will live with the law.

**Types of constituency consultations:**

- **Community consultations.** Open meetings in areas affected by the bill — provinces, municipalities, conflict-affected communities. These are especially important for bills affecting land, natural resources, livelihoods, and indigenous peoples' rights.
- **Sectoral consultations.** Meetings with organized groups — fisher folk, farmers, women's groups, youth organizations, business associations, Bangsamoro diaspora communities. These groups often have articulated positions and concrete recommendations.
- **Written submissions.** Invite interested parties to submit written comments on the bill's general framework. Not everyone can attend a meeting, and some organizations prefer to respond in writing.

**How to conduct effective constituency consultations:**

1. **Use the local language.** In the Bangsamoro region, this may mean Meranaw, Maguindanaon, Tausug, Sama, or other languages depending on the area. Prepare materials in the appropriate languages and have interpreters available.

2. **Explain the bill in accessible terms.** Do not hand a 40-page draft to community members and ask for comments. Present the bill's purpose, key provisions, and expected effects in plain language. Use examples relevant to the audience.

3. **Create space for honest feedback.** People may be reluctant to criticize a bill associated with a powerful MP. Use small-group breakout sessions, anonymous written feedback forms, and facilitators who are not from the MP's office.

4. **Do not promise what you cannot deliver.** Community consultations sometimes raise expectations. Be clear about what the bill can and cannot do, and what the consultation process is — input that will be considered, not a guarantee that every suggestion will be adopted.

5. **Report back.** After the consultation, send a summary of what was discussed and how the input will be used. People who feel heard are more likely to support the bill. People who feel ignored become opponents.

**Document all consultations** — date, venue, participants (or number of participants, for large public meetings), key points raised, and your preliminary assessment of which suggestions to incorporate. This documentation becomes part of the bill's record and can be referenced in the Explanatory Note.

**Managing the consultation budget and logistics:** Consultations cost money — venue rental, transportation, meals, printing of materials, facilitator fees. Plan the budget early and get the MP's approval. For community consultations outside Cotabato City, factor in travel time and overnight stays. Coordinate with the MP's office manager or administrative staff on logistics so that you can focus on the substance.

A typical consultation package for each session includes:
- A brief (3-5 pages) explaining the bill's purpose and key provisions
- A feedback form with specific questions
- An attendance sheet
- The presentation materials (slides or a printed handout)
- Recording equipment (with consent forms if recording)

Prepare these materials in advance. Nothing undermines a consultation faster than arriving unprepared.

## 2.6 Stage 5 — Review and Revision

You have conducted consultations and returned with notebooks full of feedback, objections, suggestions, and questions. Now you have to make sense of it all and decide what changes to make.

### 2.6.1 Reviewing Findings

Start by organizing your consultation findings. Group them by source and by topic:

**By source:**
- Expert recommendations
- Implementing agency feedback
- Constituency and sector input

**By topic:**
- Comments on the bill's scope and coverage
- Comments on specific provisions
- Implementation concerns
- Budget and resource concerns
- Legal and constitutional issues
- Political and cultural concerns

This cross-referencing reveals patterns. If three different experts and two implementing agencies all flag the same provision as problematic, that provision needs attention. If a concern was raised by only one person and contradicts the evidence from your research, it may warrant less weight — though you should still note it.

### 2.6.2 Assessing Impact on the First Draft

For each finding, make a judgment call:

- **Incorporate.** The feedback is sound, and the provision should be changed or added. Example: the implementing agency pointed out that the bill's reporting requirement assumes a database system that does not exist yet. You add a transition period and a provision for system development.
- **Note but do not incorporate.** The feedback raises a valid concern, but changing the bill would undermine the MP's policy objective. You note the concern in your revision report and explain why the provision remains unchanged. The MP makes the final call.
- **Reject.** The feedback is incorrect, irrelevant, or reflects a political position rather than a technical concern. You note it for completeness but do not act on it.
- **Escalate to the MP.** The feedback raises a fundamental question about the bill's approach — not just a technical fix. You cannot resolve it on your own. Flag it for the MP's decision.

### 2.6.3 Identifying and Addressing Gaps

Consultations often reveal things that your research missed. An expert might point out a law you did not find. An implementing agency might describe operational realities that are not documented anywhere. A community member might describe an effect of the bill that you did not anticipate.

Go back to your Comparative Matrix and your research briefer. Update both with the new information. If the gaps are significant — a major related law you missed, a constitutional issue you overlooked — you may need to conduct additional research before revising the draft. Do not patch the bill with guesswork. Go back and do the work.

### 2.6.4 Revising the First Draft

With your findings organized and your judgments made, revise the draft. Track your changes carefully — the MP will want to see what changed and why.

Prepare a **Revision Report** that accompanies the revised draft. For each significant change, explain:

- What was changed
- Why (citing the consultation feedback or new research)
- What the provision said before
- What it says now

The Revision Report does not need to document every comma fix. Focus on substantive changes — provisions added, removed, or significantly rewritten.

After completing the revision, repeat the internal review from Stage 3 (Section 2.4.4). The revised draft must still pass all the same checks: structural completeness, internal consistency, legal compliance, Comparative Matrix alignment, and drafting standards. Revision sometimes introduces new errors — a changed definition that is not updated everywhere it appears, a new section that creates a numbering gap, a cross-reference that now points to the wrong provision. Do not skip this step.

**Version control matters.** By this point, you may have three or more versions of the bill — the first draft, the post-consultation revision, and any interim versions created during the revision process. Label each version clearly with dates. Keep all versions on file. You may need to compare versions later, and the MP may ask "what did the original draft say about this?" Being able to answer that question instantly builds trust in your process.

## 2.7 Stage 6 — Presentation and Finalization with the MP

The revised draft is ready to go back to the MP. This is the stage where the MP takes ownership of the bill — reviewing every provision, making final policy decisions, and approving the text that will be filed.

### 2.7.1 Presenting the Revised Draft

Schedule a dedicated meeting with the MP for this presentation. Do not try to squeeze it into five minutes at the end of a busy day. The MP needs time to read, question, and decide.

Prepare your presentation package:

1. **The revised draft** — clean copy, properly formatted
2. **The Revision Report** — what changed and why
3. **The updated Comparative Matrix** — showing the revised bill against existing laws
4. **A summary of outstanding issues** — decisions you could not make without the MP's input
5. **The consultation documentation** — summaries of expert, agency, and constituency feedback

Walk the MP through the bill section by section. For each section, explain:
- What it does
- How it connects to the research findings
- What the consultations revealed about it
- Whether and how you changed it from the first draft
- Any remaining issues or options that need the MP's decision

Allow the MP to challenge any provision. If the MP questions something, explain your reasoning. If the MP disagrees, note the MP's preferred approach. The MP is the principal author — your job is to give the best advice you can, but the final call is the MP's.

### 2.7.2 Incorporating MP Comments

After the presentation, you may have a new round of changes. The MP might accept most of the revised draft but want three provisions rewritten. Or the MP might make a significant policy shift that requires rethinking an entire article.

Handle these revisions promptly. If the changes are minor, you can often turn them around in a day or two. If they require new research or structural changes to the bill, communicate a realistic timeline.

For each round of MP comments, keep a log:
- Date of the meeting or communication
- What the MP requested
- How you implemented it
- Any follow-up questions

This log keeps the revision process organized, especially when there are multiple rounds of back-and-forth.

### 2.7.3 Finalizing the Draft

Finalization means the bill is ready for filing. The MP has reviewed and approved every provision. All outstanding issues have been resolved. The draft has been proofread and formatted to the Parliament's standards.

As part of finalization, you write the **Explanatory Note**. This is the document that accompanies the bill when it is filed and that the MP delivers as a speech during plenary sponsorship. It is written in the first person — it is the MP's statement, not a research paper.

**The Explanatory Note should include:**

- **The policy problem.** Why is this bill needed? What situation is the MP responding to? Use concrete facts — statistics, documented incidents, identified gaps in existing law. Draw from your research briefer.
- **The proposed solution.** What does the bill do? Summarize the bill's key provisions in plain language. Do not reproduce the entire bill text — highlight the most important features.
- **Legal and policy basis.** What constitutional provisions, BOL articles, and existing laws support this bill? Why is legislation (rather than executive action or administrative regulation) the right tool?
- **Expected impact.** What will change if this bill becomes law? Who will benefit and how?
- **Consultation findings.** Briefly note that consultations were conducted and what they confirmed or revealed. This demonstrates that the bill is grounded in real-world input, not drafted in a vacuum.
- **Closing appeal.** A short paragraph urging fellow Members of Parliament to support the bill.

Write the Explanatory Note after the bill is finalized, not before. The note must accurately describe what is in the bill, and last-minute changes to the bill would make an earlier note inaccurate.

**Practical tip:** The Explanatory Note is often the only part of the filing that other MPs read before deciding whether to co-author or support the bill. Make it compelling. Lead with the problem, not the legal framework. Use real examples and human stories where appropriate. A note that reads like a bureaucratic report will not inspire co-authorship.

**Length and tone:** There is no fixed length requirement for the Explanatory Note, but most effective notes run between three and seven pages. Shorter is better if the bill is straightforward. Longer is acceptable for complex bills that need more context. The tone should be persuasive but factual — the MP is making a case for the bill, not delivering a campaign speech. Ground every claim in evidence from your research.

Present the final draft and the Explanatory Note to the MP together. Once the MP approves both, you are ready for the last stage.

## 2.8 Stage 7 — Co-Authorship and Filing

A bill filed by a single MP has a harder path than a bill filed by five. Co-authorship signals broad support, increases the bill's chances of being calendared for committee hearing, and distributes the political work of shepherding the bill through the legislative process.

This stage is as much about relationships and persuasion as it is about paperwork.

### 2.8.1 Identifying and Inviting Potential Co-Authors

Go back to the Initial Legislative Template — you listed potential co-authors there. Now, with the research and consultation behind you, refine that list.

**Criteria for identifying co-authors:**

- **Committee assignment.** Which MPs sit on the committee that will handle the bill? A co-author on the relevant committee is a major advantage — they can advocate for the bill from inside the committee.
- **Legislative agenda alignment.** Which MPs have publicly expressed interest in the bill's subject matter? Check their speeches, resolutions filed, and committee participation records.
- **Constituency interest.** Which MPs represent areas or sectors most affected by the bill? An MP whose constituents would directly benefit has a natural reason to co-author.
- **Political relationships.** Which MPs have a good working relationship with the lead author? Co-authorship often follows existing alliances and collegial ties.
- **Diversity.** A bill with co-authors from different provinces, sectors, and political backgrounds sends a stronger signal of broad support than a bill co-authored only by close allies.

**How to invite co-authors:**

Do not simply send the bill by email with a "please co-author" note. Co-authorship is a commitment, and MPs take it seriously. Follow a more deliberate process:

1. The lead author MP (or the chief of staff on the MP's behalf) sends a personal invitation — a letter, a call, or a face-to-face request.
2. The invitation includes a brief summary of the bill (the Explanatory Note works well for this) and an offer to present the full draft.
3. Respect the invited MP's process. Some will want to review the full text. Others will trust the lead author and agree quickly. Some will consult their own staff before deciding.

### 2.8.2 Presenting the Draft

For MPs who want to review the bill before committing, prepare a presentation similar to what you did for the lead author — but shorter and focused on the highlights.

Cover:
- The policy problem and why it matters
- The bill's key provisions (not every section — focus on the most important ones)
- How the bill was developed (research, consultations, revisions)
- Why this MP's co-authorship would strengthen the bill

Tailor the presentation to each MP. If the invited MP represents a coastal province, emphasize the bill's relevance to coastal communities. If the invited MP sits on the finance committee, address the budget provisions in detail.

### 2.8.3 Incorporating Comments

Co-authors may request changes before signing on. These requests range from minor — adding a provision they care about, adjusting language in the Declaration of Policy — to major — restructuring an entire article.

Handle co-author comments the same way you handle any revision:

- Evaluate each request on its merits
- Consult with the lead author before making significant changes
- Communicate clearly about what was incorporated and what was not, and why

The lead author has the final say. A co-author who insists on changes the lead author rejects may choose not to co-author — and that is their right.

**Practical tip:** Set a deadline for co-author feedback. Without a deadline, the back-and-forth can drag on indefinitely. Two weeks is usually reasonable for a standard bill.

### 2.8.4 Securing Approval

Once comments are incorporated and the draft is finalized again, circulate the final version to all co-authors for formal approval. Each co-author confirms — in writing — that they approve the bill and authorize their name to appear as co-author.

The lead author signs the bill's cover page. Co-authors may sign the same page or provide separate authorization letters, depending on the Parliament's requirements.

### 2.8.5 Filing with the Bills and Index Division

The bill is filed with the **Bills and Index Division** of the Office of the Secretary General. Filing requirements include:

- The signed bill text — original and the required number of copies
- The Explanatory Note — signed by the lead author
- The list of co-authors
- Any supporting documents required by the Parliament's rules (this may vary by session — check current requirements before filing)

Upon filing, the Bills and Index Division assigns the bill a **number** (the bill number that identifies it throughout the legislative process) and refers it to the appropriate committee.

**What to expect at filing:** The Bills and Index Division will check that the filing is complete — signed bill text, Explanatory Note, co-author list. They may ask for additional copies or point out formatting issues. This is an administrative check, not a substantive review. Fix any issues on the spot if possible, or return the same day with corrected documents.

**Practical tip:** File early in the session. Bills filed later in the session have less time for committee deliberation and plenary debate. If the bill is ready, do not wait. Also, keep track of the committee referral — knowing which committee received the bill determines your next steps in supporting the MP during the committee stage.

### 2.8.6 Circulating to Co-Authors

After filing, send each co-author:

- A copy of the filed bill with its assigned bill number
- The committee referral (which committee will handle the bill)
- A note of thanks for their co-authorship

This is a courtesy that maintains the relationship and keeps co-authors informed. It also signals the start of the next phase — committee deliberations — where the drafter's work continues in a different form (supporting the MP during committee hearings, preparing amendments, and coordinating with co-authors).

**Reading the flowchart:**

- Each stage box shows the main activities in sequence.
- Decision diamonds (shown as bracketed questions) indicate points where the process may loop back.
- Outputs are listed at the bottom of each stage box.
- The arrows between stages show the primary forward flow. Backward loops (Stage 5 back to Stage 2, for example) indicate that the process is iterative when circumstances require it.

**Key decision points:**

| Stage | Decision | If No |
|-------|----------|-------|
| 1 | MP approves template? | Revise and re-present |
| 2 | MP approves briefer and direction? | Conduct additional research |
| 5 | New research needed? | Return to Stage 2 for targeted research |
| 6 | MP approves revised draft? | Incorporate comments and re-present |
| 7 | Co-authors request changes? | Incorporate with lead author approval |

The entire process — from directive to filing — can take anywhere from a few weeks for a simple bill to several months for a complex one. The timeline depends on the bill's scope, the depth of research needed, the number of consultations required, and how quickly the MP and co-authors respond to review requests. Build a realistic timeline at Stage 1 and adjust it as work progresses. Speed matters, but a rushed bill costs more time in committee than a well-prepared one saves in drafting.
