# Bill Drafting Guidebook — LGB Source Integration

## What This Document Is

A revision guide for incorporating *The MP's Little Green Book* (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024) as a normal cited source in the *Bill Drafting Guidebook for the Bangsamoro Parliament* (Mambayao, 2026). The LGB is treated the same way the guidebook already treats Thornton, the Oregon Manual, and Segal — cited where its content informs the text, not given special treatment.

---

## Bibliographic Entry

Add to the guidebook's references/bibliography:

> Adil-Ampatuan, Baintan & Violeta T. Veloso. *The MP's Little Green Book: A Personal Guide for Members of the Bangsamoro Parliament.* 1st ed. Cotabato City: Institute for Autonomy and Governance, 2024.

Short citation form: **Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024** (or **LGB** in repeated references after first use).

### 1. The Capacity-Building Challenge (Introduction / Ch. 1)

**Current text** (ver01 §1.3):

> "'Language and tone guidance' was the most frequently cited need, mentioned nine times across interviews"

**Source:** These findings originate from the MP interviews and survey conducted by Adil-Ampatuan, documented in both the LGB (Our Humble Goal, pp. 8-9; Introduction, pp. 10-11) and the training PPT (Part 1). The LGB notes: "It takes eighteen months to two years of sustained practice for a legislative staff member to develop genuine expertise in bill drafting" — the same figure used in the guidebook.

## Part B: New Content from the LGB

These are places where the guidebook can be strengthened by incorporating LGB content that isn't currently used. Each entry provides the specific text to add.

### 5. Completed Staff Work as a Drafting Principle (Ch. 2 — The Drafting Process)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 24 (pp. 200-202) provides the most detailed treatment of CSW in the Bangsamoro legislative context, including a definition from the Department of Budget and Management and a practical anecdote about a bill where "40-50% of the text has already been revised by the committee" due to inadequate staff work.

**Where to add:** After the guidebook's discussion of the pre-drafting checklist or first-draft review stage.

**Suggested text:**
> The principle of completed staff work — *tapos na ang trabaho* — is not merely administrative. In the context of the Bangsamoro Parliament, completed staff work (CSW) means that a bill presented to an MP for signature has already been researched, analyzed, consulted upon, and vetted, so that the MP "does not need to do anything further than reviewing the submitted document and indicating approval or disapproval" (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 200). A bill that reaches the committee level without adequate CSW invites wholesale revision — a signal to other Members that the drafting office was not thorough (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 202).

### 6. The Budget Cycle in Detail (Ch. 4 — Special Bill Types / Ch. 9 — Budget Briefer)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 18 (pp. 150-178) is the most comprehensive publicly available description of the BARMM budget cycle. It includes:
- The 4-phase cycle: preparation, legislation, execution, accountability (p. 151)
- The Bangsamoro Budget Memorandum and its annexes (p. 154)
- The calendar of the FY 2023 budget process with 13 specific activity dates (Table 6, p. 154)
- The GAAB structure: PS, MOOE, CO breakdown; organizational outcomes; special provisions (pp. 161-177)
- Budget scrutiny methodology: burn rates, performance indicator review, document requirements (pp. 168-171)
- The 45% PS cap from the block grant (referenced in LGB Ch. 11, p. 107)

**Where to add:** Enrich Ch. 4 §4.1 (Appropriation Bills) and Ch. 9 (Budget Briefer) with specifics.

**Suggested additions to Ch. 4 §4.1:**

> The budget preparation process follows a defined calendar. The MFBM issues a Bangsamoro Budget Memorandum that contains Tier 1 and Tier 2 budget guidelines, standard forms, and submission deadlines. Ministries submit proposals, defend them in technical budget hearings before an MFBM oversight panel, and revise based on the panel's recommendations. The consolidated Bangsamoro Expenditure Program (BEP) is then presented to the Chief Minister and Cabinet before submission to Parliament (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, pp. 154-157).

> At the Parliament, the Committee on Finance, Budget and Management (CFBM) divides the budget among subcommittees that conduct simultaneous hearings on assigned ministries. Each subcommittee presents its findings to the CFBM, which may accept, increase, or decrease the proposed allocations. The Parliament's own budget is handled separately by the Committee on Accounts (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, pp. 158-159).

**Suggested additions to Ch. 9:**

> When preparing a budget briefer, request the following documents from BMOAs in advance of the hearing: (a) annual accomplishment report, (b) 3-year comparative budget with physical and financial accomplishments, (c) 3-year budget utilization rate, (d) detailed engineering design and program of works for proposed new buildings, (e) COA Audit Observation Memorandum, and (f) explanation of budget increase or decrease (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 170).

> Pay attention to budget utilization rates — what practitioners call the "burn rate." A ministry requesting a budget increase while showing low utilization in previous years must justify the gap. Conversely, high utilization paired with strong performance indicators may support an increase. Compare utilization rates across BMOAs and across fiscal years to identify patterns (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, pp. 168-169).

### 7. Types of Bills and Resolutions (Ch. 5 — Resolutions / Ch. 7 — Legislative Process)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 10 (pp. 94-104) provides the AR 268 definitions of government bills, private member bills, and popular initiatives, along with the three types of resolutions (simple, concurrent, joint committee). These are cited to specific rule sections. The LGB also includes annotated samples:
- Figure 10 (p. 95): Sample private member bill (Bangsamoro Permanent Seat Act)
- Figure 11 (p. 99): Sample simple resolution (Wali reaffirmation)
- Figure 12 (p. 102): Sample concurrent resolution (HRep calendar amendment)

**Where to add:** The guidebook's chapters on resolutions and the legislative process.

**Suggested text for Ch. 5 (Resolutions):**

> A practical illustration of the simple resolution format appears in BTA Resolution No. 92, which reaffirmed the consensus on the appointment of the Wali. The resolution uses a standard WHEREAS-RESOLVED structure with five recital clauses grounding the action in the BOL and the extension law before stating the operative clause (see Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 99).

### 8. The Interpellation as Cross-Examination (Ch. 8 — Legislative Briefer / Ch. 11 — Quality Review)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 12 (pp. 108-115) provides practical guidance on interpellation that no drafting manual can invent from first principles:
- "Try to anticipate possible questions on any section of your bill" (p. 113)
- "Ask the Majority Floor Leader for the names of the MPs who have signified their intentions to interpellate" (p. 114)
- "Reach out to them if you can, either personally or at staff level" (p. 114)
- "Legislative policymaking does not have to be adversarial: try to keep an open mind" (p. 114)
- The "Why Dissent is Healthy in a Parliament" note (p. 124): when there are no interpellations, "an adequate explanation cannot be gleaned from the record, a scenario that will likely engender a conflict in the interpretation of the law"

**Where to add:** Ch. 8 §8.4 (Preparing for Interpellation) and Ch. 11 §11.2 (The Interpellation Test).

**Suggested addition to Ch. 8 §8.4:**

> Interpellation in the Bangsamoro Parliament is substantive, not ceremonial. Members ask detailed questions about specific provisions, demand justifications for policy choices, and probe for weaknesses. As Adil-Ampatuan and Veloso observe, the absence of interpellation on a measure is itself a problem: "when there are no interpellations during the consideration of a measure, there is a good probability that an adequate explanation cannot be gleaned from the record" — leaving courts without guidance on legislative intent (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 124).

> The practical implication for the drafter: every section of your bill must be defensible under questioning. Before the bill reaches the floor, identify the provisions most likely to draw interpellation and prepare written answers for the sponsoring MP. Where possible, reach out to likely interpellators through the Majority Floor Leader to surface concerns early — a practice that experienced MPs use to "find common ground, smoothen the plenary process, and ideally help strengthen the final output" (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 114).

### 9. The 45% Personnel Services Cap (Ch. 4 — Special Bill Types)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 11 (p. 107) states: "the BOL provides that not more than 45 percent (45%) of the block grant may be allocated for personnel services (PS). It is best to determine first how much is required to support the PS, maintenance and other operating expenses (MOOE), and capital outlay (CO) requirements of a proposed measure."

**Where to add:** Ch. 4 §4.1 or §4.3 (Bills Creating Government Bodies — Funding).

**Suggested text:**

> Every bill with personnel implications must account for the BOL's personnel services cap: no more than forty-five percent (45%) of the block grant may be allocated to PS. A bill that creates a new ministry with a large staffing complement may push the government closer to this ceiling, crowding out PS allocations for existing ministries. The drafter should verify the current PS-to-total ratio with the MFBM before finalizing staffing provisions (see Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 107; BOL, Art. XII).

### 10. Gender and Development — Institutional Detail (Ch. 11 — Quality Review)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 15 (pp. 132-136) provides the institutional GAD framework:
- BOL Art. IX §4 (non-discrimination), §12 (CEDAW), Art. XIII §5 (women in governance)
- BAA No. 8 creating the Bangsamoro Women Commission
- The 5% GAD budget requirement (Bangsamoro Administrative Code)
- The Regional Action Plan on Women, Peace and Security (RAP-WPS) structured around UNSCR 1325
- The WEF Global Gender Gap Index ranking: Philippines at 16th (79.1% parity)

**Where to add:** Ch. 11 §11.4 (Gender Sensitivity Review), after the 8-question checklist.

**Suggested text:**

> The Bangsamoro has a well-developed GAD institutional framework. The Bangsamoro Women Commission (BAA No. 8) is the primary policy-making, coordinating, and monitoring body for gender concerns in the BARMM. The Bangsamoro Administrative Code requires at least five percent (5%) of each ministry's budget to be earmarked for gender-responsive programs. The Bangsamoro Regional Action Plan on Women, Peace and Security (RAP-WPS, 2023-2028), structured around the four pillars of UNSCR 1325 — protection, participation, prevention, and relief and recovery — provides a regional planning framework that bills may need to align with (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, pp. 132-135).
>
> When reviewing appropriation bills, verify that the 5% GAD allocation is explicitly included in the GAAB. When reviewing bills that create new government bodies, verify that the body's composition includes provisions for women's representation, consistent with the BOL's mandate that the Bangsamoro Government "shall guarantee full and direct participation of women in governance and development" (BOL, Art. XIII, §5; see also Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 134).

### 11. Coalition-Building and Co-Authorship (Ch. 2 — The Drafting Process, Stage 7)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 11 (pp. 106-107) and Chapter 7.7 (p. 80) provide practical guidance on the co-authorship process:
- "If you are the principal author of a measure, you are obliged to defend it before the committee"
- "as an author, you have the obligation to understand the full implications of the measure"
- "as co-author, you cannot oppose any of the provisions of the measure when it is deliberated upon by the Parliament in plenary session"
- The distinction between principal author, co-principal author, and co-author

**Where to add:** Ch. 2 §2.8 (Co-Authorship and Filing).

**Suggested text:**

> The distinction between principal author, co-principal author, and co-author carries procedural weight. A principal author is obligated to defend the bill before the committee; in their absence, a co-principal author or co-author may do so. Critically, an MP who signs on as co-author accepts a constraint: "you cannot oppose any of the provisions of the measure when it is deliberated upon by the Parliament in plenary session" (Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, p. 106). The drafter should ensure that the MP understands this constraint before the co-authorship form is circulated.

### 12. The Parliament's Organizational Support (Ch. 2 or Ch. 8)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 14.1.1 (pp. 120-129) maps the Parliament's internal support structure relevant to drafters:
- Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS) for research and legal matters
- Legislative Technical Affairs and Information Services (LTAIS) for media
- Bills and Index Division (BID) for filing and numbering
- Committee Support Service for committee affairs
- Plenary Support Service for plenary sessions

**Where to add:** Ch. 2 §2.3.2 (Working with the PRLS) or as an introductory note.

**Suggested text:**

> The Parliament's Secretariat provides several services that support the drafting process. The Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS) can assist with legal research and comparative analysis. The Bills and Index Division (BID) assigns bill numbers and maintains the official legislative record. The Committee Support Service coordinates committee scheduling, invitations, and documentation. Drafters should establish working relationships with these offices early — they are resources, not gatekeepers (see Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, pp. 120, 128).

### 13. Development Agenda as Legislative Framework (Ch. 2 — Stage 1)

**What the LGB contributes:** Chapter 9 and Figures 8-9 (pp. 88-92) show how an MP's legislative agenda is structured — from a 10-point development agenda to specific bills per agenda item. Annex 3 (p. 250) provides a 7-row table mapping development goals to filed bills.

**Where to add:** Ch. 2 §2.2.3 (Aligning with the MP's Legislative Agenda).

**Suggested text:**

> A well-organized MP maintains a development agenda that maps policy goals to specific legislative measures. One approach structures the agenda around priority themes — organizational strengthening, social services, fiscal management, environmental protection, local government functionality — with each theme linked to one or more bills or resolutions (see Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso, 2024, pp. 88-92, Annex 3). When you receive a drafting directive, ask where it fits in the MP's agenda. This alignment ensures that the bill's Declaration of Policy and objectives connect to the MP's broader legislative program, which strengthens the bill during committee deliberation.

## Part C: The LGB as a Source — Summary

After these revisions, the LGB would be cited approximately 15-20 times across the guidebook — comparable in frequency to Thornton (cited in Chapters 6 and throughout) and the Oregon Manual (cited in the style chapter). The LGB fills a specific niche that no other source covers: **first-person institutional knowledge of the Bangsamoro Parliament's actual practice**, written by a sitting MP and a veteran legislative executive.

| Source | What it provides | Guidebook use |
|---|---|---|
| BOL (RA 11054) | Legal authority | Quoted verbatim in blockquotes |
| AR 268 | Procedural rules | Cited by rule and section number |
| Enacted BAAs | Legislative precedent | Cited by BAA number as examples |
| Thornton | Drafting theory (5 stages) | Cited in Ch. 6 as methodological framework |
| Oregon Manual | ABC principles, style rules | Cited in Ch. 6 and Ch. 11 |
| Segal | Legislative drafting technique | Cited in Ch. 11 |
| **Adil-Ampatuan & Veloso (LGB)** | **Bangsamoro parliamentary practice, MP survey data, budget cycle, interpellation dynamics, CSW methodology, institutional support structure, GAD framework** | **Cited in Intro, Ch. 2, Ch. 4, Ch. 5, Ch. 8, Ch. 9, Ch. 11** |
