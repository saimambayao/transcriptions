# Chapter 9 — Budget Legislation and Preparing a Budget Briefer

The Bangsamoro Expenditure Program is the single most important legislative measure the Parliament considers each year. It determines how much money every ministry, office, and agency receives. It decides which programs continue, which expand, and which get cut. It sets the ceiling on what the Bangsamoro government can do for the next twelve months.

For all the attention given to policy bills — and rightly so — the annual budget is where policy becomes real. A law establishing a new education program is a statement of intent. The budget line that funds it is what puts teachers in classrooms. When the Parliament deliberates on the budget, it is not reviewing an accounting spreadsheet. It is making the most consequential policy decisions of the year, one line item at a time.

This chapter covers the Parliament's budget oversight function and teaches you how to prepare a budget briefer. Your job is to help the MP scrutinize agency budgets, ask the right questions in committee hearings and plenary deliberation, and make informed decisions about how public money is spent.

**The Legal Framework for Bangsamoro Budgeting**

The budget process in the Bangsamoro is governed by two key instruments: the **Bangsamoro Budget System Act** (BAA No. 84)[^1] and the annual **Bangsamoro Budget Memorandum** (BBM) issued by the Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management (MFBM).[^2]

BAA No. 84 establishes the comprehensive legal framework for budget preparation, authorization, and execution in the Bangsamoro. It creates the Bangsamoro Budget Coordinating Committee (BBCC) and mandates four budget philosophies: programmatic, transparent, performance-based, and phased. It prescribes the planning-investment-programming-budgeting continuum that connects the BDP to annual agency budgets. It also sets the rules for budget authorization in Parliament — including the prohibition against increasing the total appropriations proposed by the Chief Minister (Section 31),[^8] the schedule for budget consideration (Section 26),[^9] and the rules governing re-enacted budgets when the Parliament fails to enact the General Appropriations Bill on time (Section 33).[^10]

The annual BBM translates BAA No. 84's framework into specific guidelines for each fiscal year. It prescribes the **Two-Tier Budgeting Approach (2TBA)**, sets budget ceilings, defines evaluation criteria for agency proposals, and establishes the calendar and forms for budget submission. The BBM is the primary implementing instrument that agencies follow when preparing their proposals and that the Parliament's staff should reference when scrutinizing those proposals.

For the budget briefer, these instruments provide the legal benchmarks against which agency budgets should be measured. Key statutory thresholds include:

- **Personnel Services cap.** The proposed PS budget for the entire Bangsamoro Government shall not exceed forty-five percent (45%) of the total revenue source.[^3]
- **Development spending floor.** At least twenty percent (20%) of total annual appropriation shall be for development projects.[^4]
- **DRRM allocation.** At least five percent (5%) of total annual appropriations shall be for disaster risk reduction and management, split seventy percent (70%) into agency P/A/Ps and thirty percent (30%) into Quick Response Funds.[^5]
- **GAD allocation.** At least five percent (5%) of the total budget appropriation of each M/O/A and BGOCC shall be set aside for gender-responsive programs following a gender and development plan.[^6]
- **Education, health, and social services priority.** The highest budgetary priority shall be given to education, health, and social services.[^7]

These thresholds are powerful scrutiny tools. When an agency's PS exceeds the 45 percent cap, or when development spending falls below the 20 percent floor, or when DRRM allocation is missing entirely, the MP has a concrete, legally grounded basis for questioning the proposal.

## 9.1 Purpose and Use of the Budget Briefer

The budget briefer serves the same function as the legislative briefer described in Chapter 8, but applied to the budget process. It is a Completed Staff Work document that gives the MP everything needed to evaluate an agency's budget proposal: the agency's mandate, its performance record, the specific amounts it is requesting, how those amounts compare to previous years, and the questions the MP should ask to determine whether the request is justified.

Budget deliberation in the Bangsamoro Parliament follows a structured process. The Bangsamoro Expenditure Program — the annual budget document — is submitted by the Chief Minister to the Parliament. It is referred to the Committee on Finance, which conducts agency-by-agency hearings. After committee review, the budget goes to plenary for deliberation and voting. At each stage, the MP needs a briefer tailored to the specific agency or agencies under consideration.

The budget briefer differs from the legislative briefer in one important respect: it is built around numbers. While a legislative briefer analyzes legal provisions and policy implications, the budget briefer analyzes financial data — allocations, expenditures, variances, cost projections. This does not mean the budget briefer is a pure accounting exercise. The numbers tell a story about the agency's priorities, its capacity, and its performance. Your job is to read that story clearly and present it to the MP.

A second difference: the budget briefer is inherently comparative. You are always comparing this year's request to last year's allocation and expenditure. Budgets make sense only in context, and the context is the trend — are allocations going up, going down, or staying flat? Are they moving in a direction that aligns with the Parliament's priorities? The briefer must provide this context for every number it presents.

**Sources of Budget Data**

The primary source is the Bangsamoro Expenditure Program itself — the document submitted by the Chief Minister to the Parliament. This document contains the agency-by-agency budget proposals, including the breakdown by expenditure category (PS, MOOE, CO) and by program/activity/project.

Supplement the BEP with:

- **Previous years' General Appropriations Acts (or equivalent BEP enactments).** These show what was actually authorized in prior years.
- **Budget execution reports from the Ministry of Finance, Budget, and Management (MFBM).** These show actual spending against authorized amounts — the difference between what was budgeted and what was actually disbursed.
- **Agency performance reports.** Annual reports, BEDC sectoral committee reports, and RPMECom monitoring reports provide context on what the money produced.
- **Audit findings.** If the Commission on Audit has issued findings related to the agency, those findings are relevant to the budget review. Audit observations about underspending, misuse of funds, or procurement irregularities should inform the MP's scrutiny questions.
- **BDP results matrix.** The BDP's results framework provides the targets against which agency performance can be measured.

Gathering this data takes time. Start early. The day the BEP is filed, your office should begin collecting the background materials for every agency in your MP's portfolio. Waiting until the week before the committee hearing is too late for serious analysis.

## 9.2 When to Prepare a Budget Briefer

Understanding the budget timeline helps you plan when to prepare briefers. BAA No. 84 prescribes the following sequence: in April, the Bangsamoro Budget Coordinating Committee (BBCC) presents the Budget Priorities Framework to the Chief Minister and the Cabinet. The MFBM then issues the annual Bangsamoro Budget Memorandum — the Budget Call. This document sets agency ceilings, defines Tier 1 and Tier 2 parameters, and prescribes the forms and deadlines for agency submissions. Agencies prepare and submit their Bangsamoro Budget Preparation (BBP) Forms to the MFBM. The Chief Minister submits the Proposed Bangsamoro Budget to the Parliament, as far as practicable, not later than the thirtieth (30th) of September.[^11]

Once the budget is submitted, the General Appropriations Bill of the Bangsamoro (GABB) must be filed and calendared for first reading within five calendar days. After first reading, the Parliament refers it to committee, which has sixty calendar days to submit its report. Plenary debate and amendments must be completed within thirty calendar days from the committee report. Third reading follows three days after the conclusion of second reading.[^12]

**Constraints on Parliament's Budget Power**

Two rules from BAA No. 84 are particularly important for budget scrutiny. First, the Parliament shall in no case increase the total budget appropriations proposed by the Chief Minister. Any reduction must be transferred to the Contingent Fund or to another line item (Section 31). This means the Parliament's power is to reallocate and reduce, not to add. Second, if the Parliament fails to enact the GABB by the end of the fiscal year, the previous year's GAAB is deemed re-enacted, with significant limitations on what can be funded under a re-enacted budget (Section 33). These constraints define the boundaries within which budget amendments operate.

The Proposed Bangsamoro Budget itself consists of five documents: the Chief Minister's Budget Message, the Bangsamoro Expenditure Program (BEP), the Budget of Expenditures and Sources of Financing (BESF), the Budget Priorities Framework, and the Annual Investment Strategy (BAA No. 84, Section 24). Your office should obtain and review all five documents — not just the BEP — when preparing budget briefers. The BESF in particular contains macroeconomic parameters, revenue projections, and financing details that provide the fiscal context for agency-level budget analysis.

### 9.2.1 For the Annual Budget Deliberation

The primary trigger is the annual budget cycle. When the Bangsamoro Expenditure Program is submitted to the Parliament, your office needs to prepare budget briefers for every agency that falls within the MP's committee assignments or policy interests.

For MPs who sit on the Committee on Finance, this means briefers for every agency whose budget they will review in committee hearings. That can be a substantial number. Prioritize based on budget size, policy relevance to the MP, and the likelihood of controversy. But even low-priority agencies deserve at least a short-form briefer — surprises in the budget tend to come from the agencies nobody is watching.

For MPs who do not sit on the Committee on Finance, prepare briefers for agencies that fall within their other committee assignments and for agencies that serve their constituency or legislative priorities. When the budget reaches plenary, any MP can participate in the deliberation. The briefer ensures your MP is ready.

Start preparing budget briefers as soon as the Bangsamoro Expenditure Program is available. The committee hearing schedule moves quickly, and the difference between a prepared office and an unprepared one is usually a matter of days.

The annual budget cycle also presents an opportunity for proactive legislative work. Budget deliberation is when the MP has the most direct influence over government spending priorities. A well-prepared MP can use the budget process to advance their legislative agenda — by pushing for increased funding for programs they champion, by questioning agencies whose performance has been weak, and by proposing budget amendments that redirect resources toward underfunded priorities. The budget briefer is the tool that makes all of this possible.

### 9.2.2 For Committee on Finance Hearings

The Committee on Finance conducts hearings where agency heads and budget officers present and defend their budget proposals. These hearings are where the real scrutiny happens. Prepare a full budget briefer for each agency hearing your MP will attend.

The briefer for a committee hearing should include not only the analysis described in the sections below but also a set of specific questions the MP can ask the agency head. Budget hearings are the MP's opportunity to probe — to ask why personnel costs increased by 40 percent, why a capital project was not completed, why a new program is being proposed without a baseline study. Your questions should be specific, grounded in the data, and sequenced strategically.

### 9.2.3 For Plenary Budget Deliberation

When the budget reaches plenary, the debate is broader but no less important. The Committee on Finance presents its report, including any recommended amendments. MPs may propose floor amendments — additions, deletions, or reallocations.

The plenary briefer should build on the committee hearing briefer but focus on the big picture: total budget allocation by sector, major policy shifts reflected in the numbers, items the Committee on Finance flagged, and amendments the MP may wish to propose. The plenary is not the place for line-item questioning — that was the committee's job — but it is the place for structural concerns about the budget's alignment with the Bangsamoro Development Plan and the Parliament's stated priorities.

If the MP plans to propose budget amendments during plenary, the briefer should include fully drafted amendments with justifications, following the same format described in Chapter 8 for legislative amendments. Budget amendments proposed in plenary must be specific — stating the exact line item, the current amount, and the proposed change — and the MP must be prepared to defend the amendment on the floor. The briefer provides the foundation for that defense.

## 9.3 Agency Profile

Every budget briefer begins with a profile of the agency whose budget is under review. This gives the MP the essential context before diving into the numbers.

### 9.3.1 Ministry, Agency, or Office

Identify the agency precisely. State its full name, its abbreviation, and its place in the Bangsamoro government structure. Is it a ministry, an office under the Office of the Chief Minister, a parliamentary office, a government-owned or controlled corporation, or a constitutionally mandated body?

Note who heads the agency and, if relevant, when the current head was appointed. If there has been recent leadership turnover, note that — new leadership can affect budget execution and program direction.

Also note the agency's organizational structure in brief. How many divisions or bureaus does it have? Does it have field offices? How many provinces does it operate in? This organizational sketch gives the MP a sense of the agency's scale and reach. An agency with headquarters staff only is a different creature from one with field offices across all five provinces and Cotabato City.

### 9.3.2 Mandate and Legal Basis

State the agency's mandate and the law that created it. For ministries, this is typically the Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13) or the specific BAA that established the ministry's functions. For offices created by executive action, cite the relevant executive order.

Describe the agency's core functions in two to three sentences. What is the agency supposed to do? This may seem obvious for well-known ministries, but it becomes important when you compare the agency's stated mandate to the programs it is actually spending money on. If an agency's budget is heavily weighted toward activities outside its core mandate, that is a finding worth flagging.

Also note whether the agency's mandate has changed since the previous budget cycle. New legislation — a recently enacted BAA — may have expanded or altered the agency's functions. If so, the budget should reflect the new mandate. An agency with an expanded mandate and a flat budget has an unfunded obligation. An agency whose mandate was narrowed but whose budget remains the same may be spending on functions that now belong to another office. Either scenario is a finding for the briefer.

### 9.3.3 Overall Development Goals

Connect the agency to the larger development framework. The 2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028 identifies six development goals:

1. **Stable, just, and accountable government** — strengthening governance mechanisms, institutional development, and bureaucratic capacity.
2. **Equitable, competitive, and robust economy** — economic growth, investment, job creation, and equitable distribution of economic gains.
3. **Peaceful, safe, and resilient Bangsamoro communities** — lasting peace, security, and community resilience to natural and human-caused disasters.
4. **Inclusive, responsive, and quality social services** — education, health, social welfare, and human capital development for all Bangsamoro people regardless of background.
5. **Rich and diverse Bangsamoro culture and identity preserved and recognized** — promotion and protection of cultural heritage, traditions, and identity.
6. **Strategic, adequate, and climate-resilient infrastructure** — infrastructure that supports inclusive growth and withstands the effects of climate change.

Which of these goals does the agency primarily serve? Most agencies will align with one or two goals. A ministry of education aligns with Goal 4. A ministry of public works aligns with Goal 6. Some agencies — the Ministry of the Interior and Local Government, for instance — may touch on several goals.

Stating this alignment is not decorative. It provides the framework for evaluating whether the agency's budget request actually advances the development priorities the Parliament has endorsed. An agency that claims alignment with Goal 2 (equitable, competitive, and robust economy) but spends 80 percent of its budget on personnel and 5 percent on economic programs has an alignment problem. The briefer should surface that.

Also note which of the BDP's eight development strategies the agency is expected to implement. These strategies — from "improve and strengthen governance mechanisms" to "scale-up functional, strategic, climate-resilient infrastructure" — provide a second layer of alignment testing. An agency's programs should connect visibly to at least one strategy.

The BDP's overall goal — an empowered, cohesive, and progressive Bangsamoro — provides the ultimate benchmark. "Empowered" means improved community capacity through better services and economic opportunities. "Cohesive" means reduced conflict and stronger partnerships across stakeholders. "Progressive" means sustained ability to optimize resources for development. When you review an agency's budget, ask whether the proposed spending moves the region closer to that overall goal or merely maintains the status quo.

A useful exercise: for each agency, prepare a one-paragraph "alignment statement" that describes how the agency's budget proposal connects to the BDP framework. If you struggle to write that paragraph — if the connection is forced or tenuous — that struggle itself is a finding. It means the agency's budget may not be aligned with the development priorities the Parliament has adopted.

## 9.4 Organizational Outcomes and Performance Indicators

Before examining the budget numbers, establish what the agency is supposed to achieve. The budget request makes sense only in the context of outcomes — what results is the money supposed to produce?

### 9.4.1 Listing and Defining KPIs

Identify the agency's key performance indicators. These should come from the agency's own planning documents, from the BDP's results matrix, or from the BEDC sectoral committee reports.

For each KPI, provide:

- **The indicator name.** (e.g., "Number of classrooms constructed," "Percentage of barangays with functional health stations," "Hectares of farmland under irrigation.")
- **The definition.** What exactly does this indicator measure? This matters because agencies sometimes redefine indicators to make their performance look better. If "functional health station" meant something different last year than it does this year, note the change.
- **The unit of measurement.** Numbers, percentages, ratios — be precise.

If the agency does not have clearly defined KPIs — and some agencies in the early stages of institutional development may not — flag that as a concern in the briefer. An agency requesting a budget increase without defined performance indicators is asking the Parliament to approve spending on faith.

### 9.4.2 Alignment with Bangsamoro Development Goals

Map each KPI to the relevant BDP development goal and strategy. This creates a direct line from the agency's performance targets to the Parliament's development priorities.

When KPIs do not align with any BDP goal, ask why the agency is measuring something that does not connect to the development plan the Parliament adopted. When BDP goals have no corresponding KPI in the agency's framework, ask what the agency is doing about that gap.

This alignment check is one of the most powerful tools in budget scrutiny. It shifts the conversation from "how much money does the agency need?" to "what results is the money producing, and are those results what the Parliament decided to prioritize?"

### 9.4.3 Baseline Data and Targets

For each KPI, present:

- **The baseline.** What was the starting value? When was it measured?
- **Previous year's target.** What did the agency commit to achieving last year?
- **Previous year's actual.** What did the agency actually achieve?
- **Current year's target.** What is the agency proposing to achieve this year?

A simple table works best:

| KPI | Baseline | FY ____ Target | FY ____ Actual | FY ____ Target |
|-----|----------|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| Classrooms constructed | 1,200 (2022) | 300 | 185 | 350 |
| Teachers trained | 4,500 (2022) | 1,000 | 1,120 | 1,200 |

This table immediately reveals performance gaps. In the example above, the agency achieved only 185 of its 300-classroom target but is now requesting a higher target of 350. That is a legitimate basis for scrutiny. The MP should ask what went wrong last year and what has changed to make a higher target achievable this year.

Where baseline data is missing, note it. The absence of baseline data is itself a finding. If the agency cannot say where it started, it cannot credibly claim progress.

**Distinguishing Outputs from Outcomes**

A common problem in agency reporting is the confusion between outputs and outcomes. Outputs are what the agency produces — classrooms built, teachers trained, road kilometers constructed. Outcomes are the changes those outputs create — improved literacy rates, better student-teacher ratios, reduced travel time between municipalities. Outputs measure effort. Outcomes measure impact.

Many agencies report only outputs because outputs are easier to count. But the Parliament should care about outcomes, because outcomes tell you whether the money is actually making a difference. The briefer should push past output reporting and identify whatever outcome data is available. If outcome data does not exist, flag it as a gap — and the MP should ask the agency what it plans to do about measuring actual impact.

The BDP's results framework, maintained by the BEDC, is the authoritative reference for outcome indicators at the regional level. When preparing the budget briefer, cross-reference the agency's KPIs against the BDP results matrix to check for consistency. An agency that defines its KPIs differently from the BDP framework creates confusion about what "success" means — and that confusion makes it harder for the Parliament to evaluate performance.

## 9.5 Budget Comparison and Variance Analysis

This is the quantitative core of the budget briefer. You are comparing the agency's current budget request to its previous allocations and expenditures, broken down by the three standard budget categories.

### 9.5.1 Personnel Services (PS)

Personnel Services covers salaries, wages, allowances, bonuses, and benefits of government employees. In most Bangsamoro agencies, PS is the largest single budget item — often consuming 50 to 70 percent of total allocation.

Present the following data:

- **Previous year allocation.** How much was allocated for PS last year?
- **Previous year actual expenditure.** How much was actually spent?
- **Current year request.** How much is the agency requesting for PS this year?
- **Variance.** What is the change in absolute terms (pesos) and relative terms (percentage)?

Then analyze the variance. Budget increases in PS are typically driven by:

- **New positions.** The agency is requesting additional staff. How many positions? What are the position titles and salary grades? Are these positions necessary to deliver the agency's programs, or are they administrative overhead?
- **Salary adjustments.** Government-wide salary standardization or step increments. These are usually non-discretionary.
- **Reclassification.** Existing positions are being upgraded to higher salary grades. Why? Is the reclassification justified by expanded responsibilities?
- **Conversion of contractual to permanent positions.** This is common in agencies that hired contract workers during the transition period and are now regularizing them.

A significant PS increase without a corresponding increase in program delivery is a red flag. If the agency is adding fifty positions but its program targets are flat, the MP should ask what those fifty people will do.

Also check the agency's fill rate — the percentage of authorized positions that are actually occupied. A low fill rate combined with a request for new positions is contradictory. Why create new positions when you have not filled the existing ones?

**The 45 Percent PS Cap**

The Bangsamoro Budget Memorandum prescribes that the proposed PS budget for the entire BARMM shall not exceed forty-five percent (45%) of total revenue. While this is a government-wide cap rather than an agency-specific one, the briefer should note the agency's PS as a percentage of its total budget and flag agencies where PS exceeds 70 percent. The higher the agency's PS share, the less fiscal space it has for program delivery and capital investment. The MP should ask whether the agency's PS ratio is sustainable and whether it leaves enough room for the services the agency is supposed to deliver.

Under the Two-Tier Budgeting Approach, PS has distinct Tier 1 and Tier 2 components. Tier 1 PS covers salary and allowances of all authorized positions at current rates, standard benefits, and entitlements. Tier 2 PS covers adjustments due to budget policy decisions — such as new positions created for new programs, abolition or expansion of existing P/A/Ps, major organizational restructuring, or transfer of functions between agencies. When reviewing PS increases, the briefer should identify which increases are Tier 1 (automatic, baseline) and which are Tier 2 (policy-driven, discretionary). Tier 2 PS increases are the ones that warrant deeper scrutiny because they reflect deliberate spending choices.

One additional consideration specific to the Bangsamoro context: the transition from ARMM to BARMM created a unique staffing situation. Many agencies absorbed positions from the defunct ARMM regional departments, and the process of rationalizing those positions — aligning old ARMM structures with the new BARMM organizational framework — is ongoing. PS increases that are driven by this rationalization are qualitatively different from PS increases driven by new hiring. The briefer should distinguish between the two. Rationalization costs are transitional and should eventually stabilize. Expansionary hiring is a policy choice that creates permanent recurring obligations.

### 9.5.2 Maintenance and Other Operating Expenses (MOOE)

MOOE covers the day-to-day costs of running the agency: office supplies, travel, utilities, training, professional services, repairs, and similar operational expenses. MOOE is where program delivery actually happens — the training workshops, the field monitoring visits, the procurement of materials.

Present the same data: previous allocation, actual expenditure, current request, and variance.

Analyze the variance by looking at:

- **Program-related MOOE.** How much of the MOOE goes to actual program delivery (training, service delivery, monitoring) versus administrative operations (office rental, utilities, supplies)? A high administrative-to-program ratio suggests the agency is spending more to maintain itself than to serve its beneficiaries.
- **Travel expenses.** In the Bangsamoro context, where the region spans multiple islands and remote areas, travel is a legitimate operational cost. But excessive travel budgets — or travel budgets that increase faster than program budgets — warrant scrutiny.
- **Professional and consultant fees.** Is the agency outsourcing work that its regular staff should be doing? Consultant fees that grow year over year may indicate a structural capacity problem the agency is not addressing through regular hiring.
- **Training expenses.** Training is valuable, but it should be linked to measurable outcomes. An agency that spends heavily on training but shows no improvement in staff performance indicators has a training effectiveness problem, not a budget problem.

### 9.5.3 Capital Outlay (CO)

Capital Outlay covers long-term investments: construction of buildings and facilities, procurement of vehicles and heavy equipment, information technology infrastructure, and land acquisition.

CO is where the biggest individual expenditures occur, and it is the category most prone to implementation delays. Present the data and analyze:

- **Project completion rates.** For capital projects that were funded in previous years, what percentage are complete? What percentage are ongoing? What percentage have not started? Unspent CO from previous years is a strong indicator of implementation capacity problems.
- **New capital projects.** What new projects is the agency proposing? Are they supported by feasibility studies? Is the cost estimate based on actual market prices or on rough estimates?
- **Multi-year projects.** For projects that span multiple fiscal years, is the total project cost stated? What is the cumulative amount already spent? What amount is needed to complete the project? Is the project on schedule?
- **Alignment with infrastructure priorities.** Does the capital project align with the BDP's infrastructure strategy? Is it in the Bangsamoro Development Investment Program (BDIP)?

Capital projects that are funded year after year without completion deserve aggressive questioning. The MP should ask for a project-by-project status report with specific completion dates and explanations for delays.

### 9.5.4 Identifying and Explaining Variances

After analyzing each category individually, step back and look at the overall picture. Present a summary comparison:

| Budget Category | FY ____ Allocation | FY ____ Actual | FY ____ Request | Variance (Allocation vs. Request) |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------|-----------------|-----------------------------------|
| PS | | | | |
| MOOE | | | | |
| CO | | | | |
| **Total** | | | | |

Then address these questions in narrative form:

- **Is the overall budget increasing or decreasing?** By how much? Is the direction consistent with the agency's expanding or contracting role?
- **Is the budget composition shifting?** If PS is growing as a share of the total while MOOE and CO are shrinking, the agency is spending proportionally more on people and less on programs and infrastructure. That may be justified during a staffing build-up phase, but it warrants examination.
- **How does actual expenditure compare to allocation?** An agency that consistently spends less than its allocation has an absorption capacity problem. Requesting a budget increase when you have not spent last year's allocation requires a compelling explanation.
- **Are there unexplained variances?** Any line item that changes by more than 20 percent from the previous year deserves an explanation. If the agency's MOOE increased by 45 percent, why?

Present these findings in plain terms. The MP does not need accounting jargon. "The agency spent only 62 percent of its capital outlay last year, but is requesting a 30 percent increase this year" communicates the issue clearly.

**Three-Year Trend Analysis**

Where data permits, present a three-year trend rather than a single year-over-year comparison. A three-year view reveals patterns that a single comparison misses. An agency whose CO utilization has declined for three consecutive years has a systemic problem, not a one-time hiccup. An agency whose PS share has grown from 50 percent to 60 percent to 70 percent over three years is on a trajectory that will eventually crowd out program spending entirely.

The three-year trend is also useful for identifying deliberate budget strategy. Some agencies deliberately request more than they need in one category, expecting the Committee on Finance to reduce it — a negotiating tactic that creates slack in the approved budget. A multi-year view makes this pattern visible.

If three years of comparable data are available, add a trend table:

### 9.5.5 Gender and Development (GAD) Budget Review

Under Republic Act No. 9710 (the Magna Carta of Women) and BAA No. 84 (Section 17), every M/O/A is required to allocate at least five percent (5%) of its total budget to gender-responsive programs following a gender and development plan. The Bangsamoro Women Commission (BWC) issues separate guidelines on GAD budget preparation, and the BWC's endorsement is required for all gender and development-related budget proposals.

Review the agency's GAD allocation:

- **Does the budget include a GAD allocation?** What percentage of the total budget is it?
- **Is it at least 5 percent?** If not, the agency is non-compliant.
- **What specific programs and activities does the GAD allocation fund?** Are these genuine gender-responsive programs, or has the agency simply relabeled existing activities as GAD-related to meet the 5 percent threshold?
- **Are GAD targets included in the agency's KPIs?** If the agency claims to mainstream gender, that should be reflected in measurable indicators.

GAD budget review is often treated as a checkbox exercise. Do not let it be one. Meaningful GAD budgeting requires that gender considerations actually shape program design, not just budget labels. If the agency's GAD plan consists entirely of "gender sensitivity training for staff," the MP should ask whether the agency's actual service delivery — its health programs, its education programs, its livelihood programs — has been designed with gender analysis.

In the Bangsamoro context, GAD budgeting carries additional weight. The region has some of the lowest gender parity indicators in the country — in education access, maternal health, economic participation, and political representation. Agencies whose mandates directly affect these gaps — the Ministry of Social Services and Development, the Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education, the Ministry of Health — should have substantive GAD programs that go beyond training workshops to address structural barriers. The budget briefer should evaluate whether the GAD allocation is proportional to the scale of gender gaps in the agency's sector.

## 9.6 Existing Programs, Projects, Activities, and Services (Tier 1)

Under the Two-Tier Budgeting Approach (2TBA), Tier 1 represents the agency's baseline budget — the estimated cash requirements for Personnel Services of authorized positions, administrative operating expenses sensitive to inflation and fixed expenses, and recurring programs and activities based on the agency's minimum cash requirement level from the previous year. These are the bedrock of the agency's operations: the costs of keeping the lights on, the staff paid, and the existing programs running.

The MFBM issues each agency a prescribed Tier 1 budget ceiling, which the agency is expected to allocate across its administrative and operational priorities. The Tier 1 ceiling may be increased from fiscal space to fund major expansion and new development P/A/Ps under Tier 2.

### 9.6.1 Categorizing Existing PPAS

List and categorize the agency's existing PPAS. A useful categorization:

- **Core mandate programs.** These are the programs that directly implement the agency's legal mandate. For the Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education, core programs include school operations, teacher deployment, and curriculum delivery.
- **Support programs.** These are programs that support the agency's core mandate but are not direct service delivery — capacity building, monitoring and evaluation, planning and coordination.
- **Administrative programs.** General administration, financial management, human resource management, and similar internal functions.
- **Cross-cutting programs.** Programs that serve multiple sectors or involve interagency coordination — disaster response, GAD implementation, anti-corruption measures.

This categorization reveals how the agency distributes its resources across functions. An agency that spends 60 percent of its budget on administration and 40 percent on core mandate programs has its priorities inverted. The briefer should surface that distribution.

The Bangsamoro budget framework uses a parallel classification system under the Program Expenditure Classification (PREXC) approach. Under PREXC, P/A/Ps are classified by cost structure into three categories: **General Administration and Support (GAS)** — activities that provide overall administrative management support to the M/O/A; **Support to Operations (STO)** — activities that provide technical and substantive support to operations, such as planning, policy formulation, monitoring and evaluation, and information systems; and **Operations** — activities directed at fulfilling the M/O/A's mandate, including regulatory services, production of goods, and delivery of services. When agency budget data is presented using the PREXC structure, the briefer should map these categories to the practical framework above — GAS corresponds to administrative programs, STO to support programs, and Operations to core mandate programs. The PREXC classification, combined with the expenditure class breakdown (PS, MOOE, CO), gives the MP a two-dimensional view of where the money goes: what type of activity it funds and what type of expense it covers.

A note on how to get this information: the BEP typically presents budget data at the program level, but the level of detail varies by agency. Some agencies provide granular program-level breakdowns. Others aggregate multiple activities under broad headings. If the BEP data is not detailed enough for your analysis, you may need to request supplementary documents from the agency or from the MFBM. The Committee on Finance can also request detailed program budgets as part of the hearing process — your MP can formally request this information through the committee chair.

### 9.6.2 Budget Allocation per PPAS

For each continuing program, present:

- **The program name and brief description.**
- **Previous year allocation.**
- **Previous year actual expenditure.**
- **Current year request.**
- **Variance.**

Use a table:

| PPAS | Description | FY ____ Alloc | FY ____ Actual | FY ____ Request | Variance |
|------|-------------|--------------|----------------|-----------------|----------|
| Program A | Direct service delivery | | | | |
| Program B | Capacity building | | | | |
| Program C | General administration | | | | |

For programs with large budgets, break down the allocation by sub-component if the data is available. A program listed as "Livelihood Development" with a PhP 500 million allocation is not informative. What kind of livelihood development? Where? For whom? The breakdown should show whether the money goes to training, to capital grants, to equipment procurement, or to some combination. Without this detail, neither the MP nor the Parliament can make informed decisions about the allocation.

When the BEP data does not provide sub-component breakdowns, note that as a limitation in the briefer and recommend that the MP request the detailed breakdown during the committee hearing.

### 9.6.3 Performance Evaluation of Existing PPAS

This is the critical section. For each major continuing program, evaluate performance:

- **Physical targets vs. actual accomplishment.** Did the program meet its targets? If the target was to construct 300 classrooms and only 185 were built, what happened?
- **Financial targets vs. actual expenditure.** Did the program spend its allocation? Underspending can mean implementation delays, procurement problems, or overestimated costs. Overspending can mean poor planning.
- **Outcome indicators.** Beyond outputs (things built, people trained), what outcomes can be observed? If the agency trained 1,000 teachers, did student performance improve? If the agency built 185 classrooms, did enrollment increase in those areas?
- **Issues and challenges.** What problems did the program encounter? Are those problems resolved, or will they recur in the current fiscal year?

Performance evaluation of continuing programs is the foundation of budget scrutiny. An agency that failed to deliver on last year's targets should not automatically receive the same or higher allocation this year. The MP should demand an explanation of what went wrong and a credible plan for doing better.

At the same time, an agency that exceeded its targets and can demonstrate measurable outcomes has earned the credibility to request an increase. The briefer should present both scenarios fairly.

**The Absorption Capacity Test**

One of the most useful analytical tools in budget scrutiny is the absorption capacity test — comparing the amount an agency was allocated against the amount it actually spent. An agency with a low absorption rate (spending significantly less than its allocation) has a capacity problem. It may lack the procurement expertise to execute projects, the staffing to deliver programs, or the administrative systems to process disbursements.

When an agency with low absorption requests a budget increase, the burden of proof shifts. The MP should ask: "You did not spend what we gave you last year. Why should we give you more this year?" The agency may have legitimate answers — a procurement delay caused by factors outside its control, a natural disaster that disrupted operations, or a policy change that required program redesign. But the question must be asked, and the answer must be credible.

Present absorption rates for each major Tier 1 program. A table works well:

| PPAS | FY ____ Allocation | FY ____ Actual Expenditure | Absorption Rate |
|------|-------------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| Program A | PhP 100M | PhP 82M | 82% |
| Program B | PhP 50M | PhP 31M | 62% |
| Program C | PhP 200M | PhP 120M | 60% |

Programs with absorption rates below 80 percent warrant scrutiny. Programs below 60 percent warrant serious questions about whether the funding is being wasted — not through corruption, necessarily, but through inefficiency, poor planning, or insufficient capacity.

## 9.7 New and Expanded Programs, Projects, Activities, and Services (Tier 2)

Tier 2 covers new and expanded spending — programs the agency is proposing for the first time, or existing programs proposed for significant expansion in scope, geography, or beneficiary coverage. Under the Two-Tier Budgeting Approach (2TBA), Tier 2 represents everything above the agency's baseline (Tier 1) budget ceiling, funded from the fiscal space between projected revenues and Tier 1 requirements.

New and expanded programs deserve heavier scrutiny than continuing ones. Continuing programs have a performance record you can evaluate. New programs have only projections. The burden of proof is on the agency to show that the new program is necessary, feasible, and affordable.

This is not a presumption against new programs. The Bangsamoro region is in a developmental stage where new initiatives are essential — new programs address gaps in service delivery, respond to emerging needs, and implement newly enacted legislation. But the Parliament's job is to ensure that new spending is justified, not to rubber-stamp every proposal an agency submits. The budget briefer provides the analytical foundation for that judgment.

### 9.7.1 Categorizing New and Expanded PPAS

List and categorize the Tier 2 proposals using the same framework as Tier 1:

- Core mandate programs
- Support programs
- Administrative programs
- Cross-cutting programs

For each new program, note whether it is:

- **A genuinely new initiative** — something the agency has never done before.
- **An expansion of an existing program** — a current program being expanded in scope, geography, or beneficiary coverage.
- **A pilot program** — a small-scale trial intended to test an approach before scaling up.
- **A one-time project** — a time-bound initiative with a defined completion date (a building, a study, a system installation).

The distinction matters. A genuinely new initiative requires more scrutiny than an expansion of a proven program. A pilot program with a built-in evaluation mechanism is more defensible than an agency's request to launch a full-scale program without prior evidence.

### 9.7.2 Rationale and Scope

For each new program, the briefer should answer:

- **Why is this program needed?** What problem does it address? Is the problem documented with data, or is it based on anecdotal evidence?
- **What is the legal or policy basis?** Does a law, an executive order, or the BDP require this program? Or is it the agency's own initiative?
- **What is the scope?** Who are the beneficiaries? How many? Which geographic areas?
- **What is the timeline?** When does the program start? When is it expected to produce results? Is there a completion date, or is it intended to be permanent?
- **Has a feasibility study been conducted?** For capital-intensive programs, is there engineering or economic analysis supporting the proposal?
- **Has the program been endorsed by the relevant BEDC sectoral committee?** Programs that align with the BDP and have BEDC endorsement have stronger standing than programs proposed unilaterally by the agency.

If the agency's Tier 2 proposal lacks clear answers to these questions, the MP has grounds to question the proposal in the committee hearing.

### 9.7.3 Budget Allocation and Priority Ranking

For each new program, present:

- **The total proposed budget.**
- **The breakdown by PS, MOOE, and CO.**
- **The proportion of the agency's total budget increase attributable to this new program.**

Then rank the new programs by priority. This ranking should reflect:

- **Alignment with BDP goals and strategies.** Programs that directly advance a BDP development goal rank higher.
- **Alignment with the MP's legislative agenda.** Programs that support bills the MP has filed or advocacies the MP has championed rank higher for the MP's purposes.
- **Cost-effectiveness.** Programs that promise large impact relative to cost rank higher.
- **Implementation readiness.** Programs that have completed planning, feasibility studies, and stakeholder consultation rank higher than programs still at the concept stage.
- **Budget Utilization Rate (BUR).** The agency's track record of spending its allocation. A low BUR indicates capacity constraints that cast doubt on the agency's ability to execute new programs.
- **Indicative Annual Procurement Plan (APP).** Whether the agency has prepared a credible procurement plan for the new program. Tier 2 proposals without an APP lack the operational detail needed for implementation within the fiscal year.
- **Absorptive capacity.** The agency's demonstrated ability to convert budget allocation into delivered goods and services. This goes beyond BUR to include the quality and timeliness of delivery, not just the amount disbursed.
- **Endorsement by the relevant authority.** Under the Bangsamoro Budget Memorandum, specific proposals require endorsement from designated authorities: the Ministry of Public Works for buildings and infrastructure, the Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources and Energy for environment-related proposals, the Information and Communications Office for ICT proposals, and the Bangsamoro Women Commission for gender and development proposals. Proposals lacking the required endorsement should be flagged — the MP can ask why the endorsement was not obtained.
- **BPDA certification.** The Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority is required to certify that all proposed P/A/Ps are aligned with the BDP's six development goals and eight development strategies. Proposals without BPDA certification may not have been vetted for development alignment.

These criteria track closely with the formal budget evaluation criteria established by BAA No. 84 (Section 21), which requires proposed P/A/Ps to be reviewed based on: relationship with the Chief Minister's priority agenda; past performance vis-a-vis target spending; implementation readiness including analysis of development plans and M/O/A capability; program convergence with other agencies' P/A/Ps; and all available sources of funds. The budget briefer should evaluate Tier 2 proposals against both the BAA No. 84 criteria and the BBM's practical evaluation considerations.

If the agency's total Tier 2 request exceeds what the budget can accommodate, the priority ranking tells the MP which new programs to support and which to defer to the next fiscal year.

**The "What If" Question**

For every new program, run a simple thought experiment: what happens if this program is not funded? If the answer is "nothing changes" — the agency continues operating as before, services continue at current levels, no mandate goes unfulfilled — then the program is discretionary, not essential. Discretionary programs are not necessarily bad, but they should be evaluated differently from programs that fulfill a legal mandate or address an urgent need.

Conversely, if the answer is "a legal obligation goes unmet" or "a critical service gap persists," the program has a stronger claim on resources. The briefer should present this analysis so the MP can weigh the trade-offs.

## 9.8 Budget Scrutiny Questions

This section provides the MP with specific questions to ask during budget hearings and plenary deliberation. Unlike the legislative briefer's interpellation section, budget questions are often more direct — they call for numbers, dates, and explanations rather than policy arguments.

Organize the questions by section, corresponding to the structure of the briefer.

**Agency Profile and Mandate**

- What is the agency's current authorized staffing complement? How many positions are filled? What is the fill rate?
- Have there been any changes to the agency's mandate since the last budget cycle? If so, what are they and what is their fiscal impact?
- What major institutional developments occurred during the previous fiscal year?

**Performance and KPIs**

- For each KPI that fell short of target last year: What caused the shortfall? What corrective measures have been taken? Why should the Parliament expect a different result this year?
- Has the agency's results matrix been reviewed by the BEDC sectoral committee? What were the committee's findings?
- What outcome data — not just output data — can the agency present? For example, if the agency trained 1,000 farmers in new techniques, what was the measurable impact on crop yields or farm income?

**Personnel Services**

- How many new positions are being requested? What are the position titles, salary grades, and justifications?
- What is the ratio of administrative positions to program delivery positions? Is that ratio improving?
- Are there positions that have been vacant for more than twelve months? If so, why have they not been filled?
- What is the agency's overtime and allowance expenditure as a proportion of base salaries? Is it within normal parameters?

**MOOE**

- What percentage of MOOE goes to direct program delivery versus administrative operations?
- Has the agency conducted a cost analysis of its recurring operational expenses? Where are the areas of potential savings?
- What is the agency's travel budget as a percentage of total MOOE? How does it compare to the previous year?
- For training and capacity-building expenses: What specific training programs are planned? What are the expected outcomes? How will training effectiveness be measured?

**Capital Outlay**

- For capital projects funded in the previous year: What is the completion status of each project? What is the total amount of unspent CO from the previous year?
- For new capital projects: Has a detailed engineering design or feasibility study been completed? What is the total project cost, including multi-year projections?
- Are any capital projects subject to procurement delays? If so, what is the agency doing to resolve them?
- What is the agency's CO utilization rate over the past three fiscal years?

**GAD Budget**

- What percentage of the total budget is allocated to GAD? Does it meet the 5 percent minimum?
- What specific programs and activities does the GAD allocation fund?
- What measurable outcomes has the agency achieved from its GAD budget in the previous fiscal year?
- Has the agency conducted a gender analysis of its core programs? If so, what did it reveal, and how has the budget responded?

**New and Expanded Programs (Tier 2)**

- What evidence supports the need for this new program? Has a needs assessment been conducted?
- What are the specific, measurable targets for the program's first year?
- What happens if the program does not achieve its targets? Is there an exit strategy or evaluation trigger?
- Has the program been endorsed by the relevant BEDC sectoral committee?
- If the program requires multi-year funding, what is the total projected cost over its lifecycle?
- Has the agency prepared an Indicative Annual Procurement Plan for this program?
- Has the program received BPDA certification for alignment with the BDP?
- Has the program received endorsement from the relevant Budget Endorsing Authority (MPW for infrastructure, MENRE for environment, ICO for ICT, BWC for GAD)?
- If this is an expansion of an existing program, what evidence shows the existing program is performing well enough to justify scaling up?

**Overall Budget**

- What is the total budget request compared to the previous year's allocation? What is the percentage change?
- What proportion of the budget increase is driven by PS versus MOOE versus CO?
- If the Parliament approves a reduced allocation — say, 90 percent of the request — which programs would the agency cut or defer?
- What are the agency's three highest-priority budget items? What are the three lowest?

These questions are starting points. Tailor them based on the specific agency's budget data. The most effective budget scrutiny questions are not generic — they reference specific line items, specific programs, and specific numbers from the agency's own submission.

**Sequencing Your Questions**

The order of questions matters. In a budget hearing, you typically have limited time. Agency heads and budget officers are skilled at consuming time with long answers to open-ended questions. Structure your questions to get the most important information first.

Start with performance questions — did the agency deliver what it promised? Then move to variance questions — why did the numbers change? Then move to new program questions — why should the Parliament fund something new? End with accountability questions — what are the agency's specific commitments for the coming fiscal year?

This sequence builds a narrative. Performance questions establish the baseline. Variance questions identify the gaps. New program questions test the agency's ambitions against its track record. Accountability questions lock in commitments that can be monitored in the next budget cycle.

A practical tip: draft the questions on individual cards or separate pages. During the hearing, the MP can shuffle the order based on how the proceeding unfolds. If another MP asks one of your prepared questions, the MP can skip it and move to the next. If the agency's answer to one question opens a new line of inquiry, the MP can insert a follow-up without losing the overall structure.

## 9.9 Recommendations for the MP

The budget briefer concludes with your recommendations. As with the legislative briefer, the staff prepares the analysis and recommendations; the MP makes the decision.

Structure your recommendations clearly:

**Recommended Position on the Overall Budget**

State whether you recommend that the MP support, conditionally support, or oppose the agency's overall budget request. If conditional, specify the conditions — adoption of certain amendments, receipt of additional documentation, or resolution of specific concerns.

**Recommended Amendments to the Budget**

If your analysis reveals problems — unjustified increases, unfunded mandates, non-performing programs, or misaligned priorities — recommend specific budget amendments. These can take the form of:

- **Reduction.** Reduce a specific line item by a stated amount. (e.g., "Reduce the CO allocation for Project X by PhP 50 million, pending completion of the feasibility study.")
- **Reallocation.** Transfer funds from one line item to another within the same agency. (e.g., "Reallocate PhP 20 million from administrative MOOE to the scholarship program under Program B.")
- **Conditional release.** Recommend that a specific allocation be approved but released only upon compliance with stated conditions. (e.g., "Approve the Tier 2 allocation for Program D, subject to submission of a detailed implementation plan within 90 days.")
- **Deletion.** Recommend the removal of a line item entirely. This is rare and should be reserved for proposals that are clearly unjustified.

For each recommended amendment, provide a brief justification — the same one-paragraph structure used in the legislative briefer's amendment section.

A note on budget amendments: unlike legislative amendments, which change the text of a bill, budget amendments change numbers. The amendment form is different. Instead of "delete the words '___' and insert '___,'" a budget amendment states: "Reduce the allocation for [agency] under [program/expenditure item] from PhP ___ to PhP ___." Or: "Insert a new line item under [program] for [purpose] in the amount of PhP ___." When reducing one item and increasing another, the MP should present both sides of the reallocation together so the body can see that the total budget remains balanced.

The rules on budget amendments vary — some Parliaments require that any increase in one line item be offset by a decrease in another (revenue-neutral amendments). Others allow amendments that increase total spending. Check the Parliament's Rules of Procedure and the Committee on Finance's guidelines for the specific rules applicable in the current budget cycle.

**Recommended Questions for the Hearing**

From the full list of scrutiny questions in Section 9.8, select the five to ten most important questions for this agency. Rank them by priority. If the hearing runs short, the MP should ask these questions first.

**Items Requiring Follow-Up**

List any items that cannot be resolved during the budget hearing and require follow-up — missing data, documents the agency promised to submit, issues deferred to plenary deliberation. These items should be tracked in the action log.

**Action Log and Approval**

Use the same action log and approval block format as the legislative briefer:

| Date | Action | Status |
|------|--------|--------|
| [date] | BEP submitted to Parliament | Complete |
| [date] | Agency budget data compiled | Complete |
| [date] | Budget briefer prepared | Complete |
| [date] | Briefer submitted to MP for review | Pending |
| [date] | Committee on Finance hearing | Upcoming — [date] |
| [date] | Plenary budget deliberation | TBD |

The approval block:

- **Prepared by:** [Staff member name and designation]
- **Date submitted:** [Date]
- **Recommended position:** [Support / Support with amendments / Oppose / Reserve position]

For the MP:

- **Approved as recommended:** [ ]
- **Approved with modifications:** [ ]
- **Disapproved / Return for further study:** [ ]
- **MP's signature:** _______________
- **Date:** _______________

## The Budget as a Policy Document

Budget work is sometimes treated as the dull sibling of legislative drafting. It lacks the drama of interpellation, the intellectual satisfaction of crafting a new law, the visibility of authoring a landmark bill. But budget scrutiny is where the Parliament's oversight function is most consequential.

Every peso in the Bangsamoro Expenditure Program is a decision. The decision to fund a road in one province is simultaneously a decision not to fund a road in another. The decision to create fifty new positions in one ministry is a decision to leave fifty positions unfunded elsewhere. Budget scrutiny forces those trade-offs into the open.

The budget briefer is the instrument that enables your MP to make those trade-offs intelligently. It converts raw financial data into actionable analysis. It connects spending to outcomes. It identifies waste, misalignment, and missed opportunities. And it ensures that the MP walks into the budget deliberation with the same level of preparation they would bring to a major policy bill — because the budget is a major policy document. It is arguably the most important one the Parliament passes each year.

The six development goals of the BDP 2023-2028 are not just aspirational statements. They represent the Bangsamoro people's priorities as adopted by the Parliament. Goal 1 demands accountable government. Goal 2 demands an equitable economy. Goal 3 demands peaceful communities. Goal 4 demands quality social services. Goal 5 demands cultural preservation. Goal 6 demands resilient infrastructure. Every budget line item should advance at least one of these goals. When a line item does not, the MP should ask why.

That is the work. It requires patience, numerical literacy, and a willingness to dig into the details. But it is some of the most important work a legislative office can do.

The fiscal sources that fund the BEP — the annual block grant from the national government, the Special Development Fund, locally generated revenues, and other sources enumerated in the BOL — are finite. Every peso allocated to one purpose is a peso unavailable for another. The budget briefer makes those trade-offs visible. It puts numbers on choices. It connects spending to performance. It links appropriations to development goals.

Prepare the briefer. Do the analysis. Give the MP everything they need.

That is Completed Staff Work applied to the budget, and it is the standard your office should meet every fiscal year.
