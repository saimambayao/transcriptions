# Chapter 8 — Legislative Strategy: Standalone vs. Mainstreaming

This is the chapter that addresses the question the Bangsamoro Parliament must answer: **How should Shari'ah be legislated in BARMM?**

The BOL gives Parliament the power. PD 1083 covers personal law. The court system is in place. The primary sources are accessible. But Parliament has not yet developed a coherent strategy for *how* to exercise its Shari'ah legislative power. Should it enact standalone Shari'ah BAAs? Should it mainstream Shari'ah provisions into every relevant piece of legislation? Or should it pursue a hybrid approach?

This chapter analyzes the three options and recommends a path forward.

---

## 8.1 The Strategic Question

BOL Art. X, Sec. 4 grants Parliament power to legislate on personal, family, and property law; commercial and civil actions not provided for under PD 1083; and *ta'zir* criminal offenses.[^1] This is a broad mandate. But a mandate without a strategy produces fragmented legislation.

Consider the current situation: of the 89 BAAs enacted through February 2026, none is a dedicated Shari'ah law.[^2] Shari'ah provisions appear *within* other legislation — the BDI is established within BAA 13 (the Administrative Code), halal certification is referenced in BAA 13's provisions on the Halal Board, and the Hijri calendar is mandated by BAA 20. But no standalone "Bangsamoro Shari'ah Code," "Bangsamoro Waqf Act," or "Bangsamoro Zakat Act" exists.

Is this a problem? Or is it a design choice? The answer depends on which legislative strategy Parliament adopts.

---

## 8.2 Standalone Approach — Dedicated Shari'ah BAAs

The standalone approach enacts dedicated legislation for each Shari'ah subject area: a Waqf Act, a Zakat Act, an Islamic Finance Act, a Halal Regulatory Act, and potentially a comprehensive Bangsamoro Shari'ah Code.

**Advantages:**

- **Clarity.** Each subject gets a complete, self-contained law. Practitioners know exactly where to find the rules for waqf, zakat, or Islamic finance.
- **Institutional framework.** A standalone BAA can create dedicated institutions — a Waqf Board, a Zakat Authority, an Islamic Finance Commission — with defined mandates, budgets, and accountability structures.
- **Comprehensive treatment.** A dedicated act can address a subject in full depth — definitions, procedures, administration, penalties, implementing rules — without competing for space within a broader law.
- **Symbolic weight.** Standalone Shari'ah legislation signals that Parliament takes its Art. X mandate seriously and is actively exercising the legislative power the BOL grants.

**Risks:**

- **Slow pace.** Drafting comprehensive standalone legislation takes time — research, consultation with *'ulama*, committee deliberations, BDI review. With the BTA transition period pressing, slow legislation means missed deadlines.
- **Resource-intensive.** Each standalone BAA requires its own drafting team, committee hearings, and implementing rules. Parliament's technical capacity is finite.
- **Potential isolation.** If Shari'ah legislation exists only in standalone BAAs, mainstream legislation (budget, education, health, environment) may ignore Shari'ah dimensions entirely — creating a two-track system where Islamic principles apply only in "Islamic" laws.

**Models:** Malaysia has enacted standalone Islamic finance legislation (Islamic Financial Services Act 2013), a separate Zakat administration framework, and dedicated Waqf laws at the state level. Indonesia enacted a national Zakat Law (Law No. 23 of 2011) as standalone legislation.

---

## 8.3 Mainstreaming Approach — Shari'ah Provisions in Every BAA

The mainstreaming approach embeds Shari'ah provisions within every relevant piece of legislation rather than creating separate Shari'ah laws.

**How it works:** Every bill that passes through Parliament would undergo a Shari'ah checkpoint (Chapter 9). If the bill has a Shari'ah dimension, the drafters add provisions that address it — halal compliance in a food safety BAA, Shari'ah-compliant options in a cooperative code, *waqf* provisions in a property law, zakat considerations in a fiscal policy.

**Advantages:**

- **Immediate impact.** Shari'ah provisions take effect as soon as each mainstreamed BAA is enacted — no waiting for a separate dedicated law.
- **Integration.** Shari'ah becomes part of the fabric of all governance, not a separate silo. Budget law considers zakat. Education law considers Islamic education. Health law considers halal.
- **Faster enactment.** Adding Shari'ah provisions to bills that are already being drafted is faster than drafting entirely new standalone legislation.

**Risks:**

- **Fragmentation.** Shari'ah provisions scattered across dozens of BAAs are harder to find, harder to keep consistent, and harder to amend.
- **Inconsistency.** Different drafters may interpret Shari'ah requirements differently across bills, leading to contradictions.
- **Shallow treatment.** A few sections within a broader BAA cannot provide the same depth as a dedicated standalone law. Complex subjects like waqf administration or Islamic finance need comprehensive treatment.

**Models:** BARMM already uses this approach informally — the BDI in BAA 13, Hijri calendar in BAA 20. Indonesia mainstreams Shari'ah compliance into its banking regulations through Bank Indonesia circulars rather than standalone Islamic banking laws.

---

## 8.4 Hybrid Approach — Foundational Code + Mainstreaming Checklist

The hybrid approach combines both strategies:

1. **Enact a foundational "Bangsamoro Shari'ah Code"** as a standalone BAA that establishes the general principles, institutional framework, and cross-cutting provisions for Shari'ah in BARMM governance.

2. **Mainstream Shari'ah provisions** into all subsequent legislation through a mandatory Shari'ah checkpoint in the bill-drafting process (Chapter 9).

3. **Enact standalone BAAs** for subjects that require comprehensive treatment (waqf, zakat, Islamic finance) — but anchor them in the foundational code.

**The foundational code would cover:**
- Declaration of principles: how Shari'ah relates to BARMM governance
- Institutional framework: BDI's role in legislative review (building on BAA 13, Secs. 52-56)
- Shari'ah checkpoint mandate: requirement that every bill undergo Shari'ah review
- General provisions on Islamic finance, waqf, and zakat — with authorization for detailed standalone BAAs
- Citation protocol for scriptural sources in legislation
- Relationship between PD 1083 and BARMM Shari'ah legislation

**The mainstreaming checklist would require:**
- Every bill to be reviewed against the Shari'ah checkpoint (Chapter 9)
- Shari'ah dimensions to be addressed in the bill text where identified
- BDI consultation for bills with significant Shari'ah implications
- A Shari'ah analysis section in every legislative briefer (Section VII of the CSW format)

## 8.5 Analysis: Which Approach Fits BARMM's Legislative Reality?

**Parliament's track record** tells us something important. In 89 enacted BAAs, Parliament has prioritized administrative codes (BAA 13), education (BAA 18), civil service (BAA 17), electoral law (BAA 35), local governance (BAA 49), budget legislation, and municipality creation. Shari'ah has been addressed incidentally — within BAA 13 — not as a primary legislative agenda item.

This is not neglect. It reflects a sequencing choice: build the governance infrastructure first, then legislate on more specialized matters. The administrative code had to come before a Shari'ah code because the BDI needed an institutional home.

**BOL Art. X, Sec. 4 scope** is specific: personal/family/property law, commercial matters not in PD 1083, and *ta'zir*. This is not unlimited. Parliament cannot legislate on criminal matters beyond *arresto menor*, cannot override PD 1083, and cannot extend Shari'ah to non-Muslims without voluntary submission.

**PD 1083 as baseline** means that personal law is already covered. Parliament's opportunity lies in the gaps: Islamic finance, waqf administration, zakat regulation, halal certification, and commercial matters. These are all subjects that benefit from standalone legislation.

**The BDI's capacity** is a practical constraint. A comprehensive Shari'ah legislative review function requires institutional readiness — qualified staff, established procedures, and a track record of issuing *fatawa*. The BDI, established under BAA 13, is still building this capacity.

---

## 8.6 Recommendation: A Phased Hybrid Strategy

Based on the analysis, the recommended strategy is a **phased hybrid** approach:

**Phase 1 (Immediate): Institutionalize the Shari'ah Checkpoint**
- Formalize the Shari'ah checkpoint as a mandatory step in Parliament's bill-drafting process
- Train committee staff on how to conduct Shari'ah analysis using this guidebook
- Establish a working relationship between committee staff and the BDI for legislative consultation

**Phase 2 (Near-term): Enact Priority Standalone BAAs**
- **Bangsamoro Waqf Act** — establish a waqf registry, administration framework, and regulatory body
- **Bangsamoro Zakat Act** — formalize zakat collection, distribution, and administration
- **Bangsamoro Islamic Finance Framework Act** — establish the legal basis for Shari'ah-compliant financial instruments
- **Bangsamoro Halal Act** — comprehensive halal certification and regulatory framework

**Phase 3 (Medium-term): Foundational Shari'ah Code**
- Consolidate the principles, institutional framework, and cross-cutting provisions from Phase 1-2 into a comprehensive "Bangsamoro Shari'ah Code"
- This code becomes the anchor for all existing and future Shari'ah legislation

**Why this sequence?** The checkpoint (Phase 1) can be implemented immediately with no new legislation — it is a procedural reform within Parliament. The standalone BAAs (Phase 2) address the most pressing legislative gaps. The foundational code (Phase 3) comes last because it requires the experience of Phases 1-2 to get right.

> - Enactment of Zakat Act and Islamic Finance Framework Act

## 8.8 Common Pitfalls

- **Waiting for the perfect comprehensive code.** A Shari'ah Code that covers everything will take years to draft. Standalone BAAs on priority subjects can be enacted faster.
- **Mainstreaming without a checkpoint.** If there is no formal process for identifying Shari'ah dimensions, mainstreaming becomes ad hoc and inconsistent.
- **Ignoring BDI capacity.** Any strategy that relies on BDI legislative review must account for the BDI's current staffing, expertise, and procedural readiness.
- **Treating Shari'ah legislation as a separate track.** The goal is integration — Shari'ah principles embedded in governance, not isolated in "Islamic" laws that mainstream agencies ignore.
- **Legislating beyond Art. X, Sec. 4 scope.** Parliament's Shari'ah power has specific boundaries. Legislation that exceeds them risks constitutional challenge.
