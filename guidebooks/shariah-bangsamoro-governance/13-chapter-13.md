# Chapter 13 — Economy, Finance, and Natural Resources

Two BOL articles govern BARMM's economic powers. Article XII establishes fiscal autonomy — the block grant, revenue sources, borrowing authority. Article XIII covers the regional economy: Islamic banking, cooperatives, halal development, agriculture, fisheries, environment, mining, trade, tourism, and economic zones. Together, they define how BARMM generates and manages wealth.

The Shari'ah dimension here is not abstract. Art. XIII, Sec. 31 directs the Bangsamoro Government to encourage banks established "in accordance with the principles of the Islamic banking system." Art. XIII, Sec. 30 expressly mandates halal development legislation. Art. XIII, Sec. 32 directs Parliament to enact laws promoting Islamic finance, including a Shari'ah Supervisory Board and tax neutrality. These are constitutional directives that remain largely unfulfilled.

This chapter covers the full economic landscape: from Islamic banking and halal economy to agriculture, natural resources, trade, and environmental stewardship.

---

## 13.1 Art. XII-XIII and the Economic Shari'ah Dimension

**The governing maqsad:** *Hifz al-mal* — preservation of wealth and property. Every economic power in this chapter serves this objective. But *hifz al-mal* in Islamic jurisprudence is not unlimited accumulation — it means wealth acquired through permissible means, used productively, and distributed justly.[^1]

**Three prohibitions** shape all Islamic commercial law:
1. ***Riba*** (interest/usury) — no profit from lending money itself; profit must come from trade, services, or shared risk[^2]
2. ***Gharar*** (excessive uncertainty) — contracts must have clear terms; no speculative or ambiguous transactions
3. ***Maysir*** (gambling/speculation) — no zero-sum games where one party's gain is another's loss without productive activity

**The recurring principle:** *Maslahah* (public interest) appears in 14 of the 15 economic powers covered in this chapter. *Amanah* (trust/stewardship) appears in 12. These two principles justify government regulation of commerce — the state acts as trustee of the public good.

## 13.2 Islamic Banking and Finance

**BOL basis:** Art. XIII, Sec. 31 (Islamic banking system); Sec. 32 (Shari'ah-compliant institutions, Shari'ah Supervisory Board, tax neutrality); Sec. 34 (Board functions).[^3]

This is the most Shari'ah-intensive economic power. Islamic banking *is* Shari'ah-based finance — without Shari'ah compliance, there is no Islamic banking. The BOL devotes **four consecutive sections** to this power — more than any other single economic subject.

### What the BOL mandates

Art. XIII, Secs. 32 and 34 contemplate three things that do not yet exist:
1. **Shari'ah-compliant financial institutions** — not just Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank (RA 6848) but a broader framework
2. **Bangsamoro Shari'ah Supervisory Board** — to monitor compliance, certify products, and issue *fatawa* on Islamic finance
3. **Tax neutrality** — Islamic finance instruments must not bear higher tax burdens than conventional equivalents (e.g., *murabaha* involves two property transfers, each potentially taxable; conventional mortgage involves one)[^4]

### Permissible instruments

| Instrument | Arabic | Structure | Risk Sharing |
|-----------|--------|-----------|-------------|
| *Mudarabah* | مضاربة | One provides capital, other provides labor; profits shared by ratio | Loss borne by capital provider |
| *Musharakah* | مشاركة | All contribute capital and management; profits per agreement | Loss per capital ratio |
| *Murabaha* | مرابحة | Seller discloses cost + agreed markup; sale, not loan | Buyer bears post-sale risk |
| *Ijarah* | إجارة | Lease with option to purchase at end of term | Owner bears maintenance risk |
| *Istisnaa* | استصناع | Manufacturing/construction contract; payment in stages | Shared per contract terms |
| *Sukuk* | صكوك | Asset-backed certificates; returns from underlying asset | Shared with certificate holders |
| *Takaful* | تكافل | Mutual risk-sharing (Islamic insurance); surplus returned | Pooled risk |

**Critical distinction:** These are *fiqh* concepts, not enacted BARMM law. No BAA codifies any of these instruments. Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to legislate — but the legislation has not been enacted. Never present these instruments as positive law in a bill's legal basis section.[^5]

### How to legislate

A **Bangsamoro Islamic Finance Framework Act** should:
1. Establish the Shari'ah Supervisory Board (Art. XIII, Sec. 34 mandate)
2. Define Shari'ah-compliant financial products recognized in BARMM
3. Create tax neutrality provisions (Art. XIII, Sec. 32 mandate) — the double-transfer tax problem for *murabaha* and *ijarah* must be resolved
4. Coordinate with BSP supervision (Islamic banks remain under BSP, not BTA)
5. Regulate non-bank Islamic financial institutions (cooperatives, microfinance, *takaful*)

## 13.3 Islamic Finance for Cooperatives and Social Enterprises

**BOL basis:** Art. XIII, Sec. 24 (cooperative development — named legislative mandate, not a priority code); Art. X, Sec. 4.[^6]

Cooperatives are where Islamic finance meets grassroots economic development. A fishing cooperative in Tawi-Tawi, an agricultural cooperative in Maguindanao del Sur, a women's livelihood cooperative in Lanao del Sur — these are the institutions where *mudarabah* and *musharakah* stop being textbook concepts and become working financial structures.

### The dual compliance challenge

A Shari'ah-compliant cooperative in BARMM must satisfy two frameworks simultaneously:

| RA 9520 Requirement | Shari'ah Alternative |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Interest on share capital (Sec. 23) | *Mudarabah* profit distribution (profit from trade, not interest on money) |
| Savings/deposit operations (Sec. 60) | *Wadiah* (safekeeping) or *mudarabah* (profit-sharing) accounts |
| Loan/credit services (Sec. 61) | *Murabaha* (cost-plus sale) or *qard hasan* (benevolent loan) |
| Insurance (Sec. 34) | *Takaful* (mutual risk-sharing) |
| Investment of surplus (Sec. 86) | Shari'ah-compliant investment (no *riba*, *gharar*, *maysir*) |

This is a **dual-track** model — cooperatives choose at registration whether to operate as conventional or Shari'ah-compliant. The choice is structural, not cosmetic. A Shari'ah-compliant cooperative cannot simply relabel interest as "profit" — the underlying transaction must be different.[^7]

### Shari'ah-compliant cooperative bylaws

Five requirements for a cooperative to be Shari'ah-compliant:
1. **No interest-based transactions** — all earnings from trade, shared investment, or service fees
2. **Transparent profit-sharing ratios** — defined in bylaws, not post-hoc
3. **Shari'ah-compliant investment policy** — no investment in prohibited sectors (alcohol, gambling, pork)
4. **Zakat provision** — cooperative may facilitate *zakat* collection on member profits (optional)
5. **Shari'ah advisory committee** — at minimum, one member with Islamic finance qualification to review transactions

### *Waqf* and *zakat* as cooperative funding mechanisms

***Waqf*** (perpetual endowment) can fund cooperative infrastructure. A donor endows land or capital; the cooperative uses the income in perpetuity. The endowment itself cannot be sold or distributed — only its returns.[^8]

***Zakat*** (obligatory alms) can be channeled through cooperatives to reach the eight recipient categories specified in Qur'an 9:60 — the poor, the needy, *zakat* administrators, those whose hearts are to be reconciled, freeing captives, debtors, in the cause of Allah, and wayfarers.[^9]

Neither *waqf* nor *zakat* has dedicated BARMM legislation. A Bangsamoro Waqf Act and a Bangsamoro Zakat Act are among the highest-priority legislative gaps.

### Worked example: Structuring a Shari'ah-compliant fishing cooperative

**Scenario:** Thirty fishermen in Maguindanao del Sur want to form a cooperative. They want Shari'ah-compliant operations.

1. **Capital structure** — *Musharakah*: each member contributes capital (cash, equipment, boats). Profits from collective fishing and processing are distributed according to capital contribution ratios. No interest.
2. **Equipment acquisition** — *Murabaha*: the cooperative buys a motorized boat from a supplier for P500,000, sells it to itself (as a legal entity) for P575,000 payable in 12 monthly installments. Cost and markup are disclosed. This is a sale, not a loan.
3. **Insurance** — *Takaful*: members contribute to a mutual fund. If a boat is damaged, the fund covers repair. Surplus is returned to members annually.
4. **Savings** — *Wadiah* (safekeeping) for liquid reserves. No interest earned on safekeeping.
5. **Bylaws** — register as Shari'ah-compliant with CDA/CSEA; include Shari'ah advisory committee; prohibit investment in non-compliant sectors.

## 13.4 Halal Economy and Regulatory Framework

**BOL basis:** Art. XIII, Sec. 30 — expressly mandates Parliament to "enact laws to further strengthen its policy and programs on halal development."[^10] This is one of the few Art. XIII sections that explicitly uses the word "halal."

### The Bangsamoro Halal Board

BAA 13 establishes the Bangsamoro Halal Board under MTIT (Book VI, Title XIV, Chapter 7).[^11] Key features:

- **Chair:** MTIT Minister
- **Vice Chair:** MAFAR Minister
- **Members:** MOST, MOH, MOLE, MOTC, BDI Mufti, MSU representative, 2 Bangsamoro professionals[^12]
- **Functions:** Formulate halal standards, conduct certification, regulate halal establishments, coordinate with national Halal Board (RA 10817)

**Critical distinction:** The Bangsamoro Halal Board is a *policy-making board*, not a full regulatory agency. A comprehensive Bangsamoro Halal Act would need to establish: detailed certification standards, enforcement mechanisms, penalties for false halal claims, laboratory testing requirements, and coordination protocols with the Philippine Halal Board.[^13]

### Halal beyond food

Halal extends to every product and service that Muslims consume:
- **Pharmaceuticals** — gelatin capsules, alcohol-based solvents, animal-derived ingredients
- **Cosmetics** — animal fats, alcohol content, testing methods
- **Tourism** — halal-friendly hotels (prayer facilities, halal kitchen, no alcohol in minibar), halal tour packages
- **Financial services** — Islamic banking products (covered in Sec. 13.2)
- **Logistics** — halal supply chain integrity (segregation of halal and non-halal goods in transport)

### BARMM's unique position

BARMM is the only region in the Philippines with:
- A majority-Muslim population AND its own halal regulatory body
- A BOL mandate for halal development (Art. XIII, Sec. 30)
- Access to Islamic development finance (IsDB, OIC)
- A domestic market and export potential to the global halal market (estimated at over $2 trillion)

**How to legislate:** A comprehensive Bangsamoro Halal Development Act should cover: standards (food, non-food, services), certification (Halal Board authority, accredited certifiers), enforcement (penalties for false claims, inspection powers), institutional coordination (Bangsamoro Halal Board and Philippine Halal Board under RA 10817), and market development (halal industrial zones within BEZA economic zones, halal tourism certification).[^14]

## 13.5 Agriculture, Fishery, and Marine Resources

**BOL basis:** Art. V, Sec. 2(c) (agriculture); Art. XIII, Sec. 23 (fishery, consistency with RA 8550); Art. XIII, Sec. 30 (halal program).[^15]

### Agriculture: Three Shari'ah dimensions

**1. Islamic land tenure.** PD 1083 governs Muslim property relations, including inheritance (*mirath*) of agricultural land and *waqf* endowments. *Waqf* agricultural land is **inalienable** under Islamic law — it cannot be sold, gifted, or redistributed. This creates a direct tension with CARP (Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Program), which provides for compulsory acquisition and redistribution. A Bangsamoro Agrarian Reform Act must address this: either exempt *waqf* land entirely or create a special category that preserves the endowment while ensuring tenant protections.[^16]

**2. Agricultural finance.** Conventional agricultural credit charges interest. Shari'ah alternatives include *salam* (advance payment for future harvest delivery) and *murabaha* (cost-plus sale for inputs). A Bangsamoro Agricultural Credit Act should offer both conventional and Shari'ah-compliant financing options.[^17]

**3. Halal production chain.** From farm to market: livestock slaughter standards, food processing, packaging, transport, and retail. The Halal Board (BAA 13) coordinates with MAFAR on agricultural halal standards.

### Fishery: Stewardship and halal

Fishery intersects with Shari'ah through:
- ***Amanah*** (stewardship of marine resources) — the sea is a trust, not a commodity
- **Prohibition of *israf*** (waste) — overfishing, destructive fishing methods, excessive bycatch
- **Halal compliance** — post-harvest processing, storage, and transport must maintain halal integrity
- ***Maslahah*** (food security) — fishing regulation serves the public good

A Bangsamoro Fisheries Code should incorporate stewardship language and halal post-harvest certification through the Halal Board.

## 13.6 Environment and the Khalifah Stewardship Framework

**BOL basis:** Art. XIII, Sec. 8 (protected areas — Parliament shall legislate); Sec. 9 (management transfer from DENR); Sec. 3 (comprehensive sustainable development framework).[^18]

Environmental stewardship is among the most extensively addressed subjects in Islamic law and theology. The Qur'an establishes humans as *khalifah* (stewards/vicegerents) of the earth — a sacred trust (*amanah*), not ownership.[^19]

### Five Islamic environmental principles

| Principle | Arabic | Application |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| ***Khalifah*** | خليفة | Humans are stewards, not owners, of natural resources |
| ***La darar wa la dirar*** | لا ضرر ولا ضرار | No harm and no reciprocal harm — one of five universal legal maxims |
| ***Hima*** | حمى | Protected reserves — the oldest documented protected area system (14 centuries) |
| ***Harim*** | حريم | Inviolable buffer zones around water sources, wells, and settlements |
| ***Ihya al-mawat*** | إحياء الموات | Reviving dead land — reforestation, land reclamation |

### How to legislate

**Model: Integration, not separate track.** Both secular environmental law and Shari'ah mandate protection. The chapter drafts should:

1. **Formally recognize *hima*** as a protected area category alongside NIPAS categories — providing a culturally resonant framework for community-managed conservation
2. **Incorporate *harim*** into watershed and buffer zone regulations — the Islamic precedent for protecting water sources predates modern environmental regulation by centuries
3. **Use *khalifah* stewardship language** in the preamble of any environmental BAA — this gives the law moral authority rooted in the region's dominant faith
4. **Establish community-managed protected areas** modeled on classical *hima* with *ulama* participation in management boards

A **Bangsamoro Protected Areas Act** is expressly mandated by Art. XIII, Sec. 8 but has not been enacted. This is a critical gap.[^20]

## 13.7 Ancestral Domain and Land Administration

**BOL basis:** Art. V, Sec. 2(d) (ancestral domain); Art. XIII, Sec. 8-16 (natural resources); Art. IX, Sec. 3 (*fusaka inged* native titles).[^21]

### The Shari'ah dimension

Islamic property law intersects with ancestral domain through:
- ***Ihya al-mawat*** (revival of dead land) — long-term cultivation can establish land rights, providing an Islamic basis for ancestral domain claims
- ***Fusaka inged*** (native titles) — recognized in BOL Art. IX, Sec. 3(a) and BAA 64; the concept of inherent land rights predating colonial titles has parallels in Islamic property doctrine
- **PD 1083 property provisions** — Muslim inheritance (*mirath*) and *waqf* provisions affect how land transfers between generations

### Water resources

Inland waters (Art. V, Sec. 2(gg)) and inland waterways (Sec. 2(hh)) have Shari'ah dimensions through:
- ***Harim*** — buffer zone protections for rivers and water sources
- **Prohibition of *darar*** — no pollution or degradation of communal water
- **Prohibition of *israf*** — no wasteful water extraction
- ***Maslahah*** — water as a public good, not a private commodity

### Land survey and classification

Cadastral land survey (Sec. 2(h)) and public land classification (Sec. 2(j)) have indirect Shari'ah dimensions:
- **Mandatory native title screening** — before any cadastral survey, check for *fusaka inged* and Islamic customary tenure claims
- **Community consultation** — land classification should recognize *hima* areas and respect customary allocation
- **PD 1083 inheritance** — Muslim inheritance rules affect cadastral records; survey procedures must account for multiple heirs under Islamic succession law

## 13.8 Trade, Tourism, and Economic Zones

**BOL basis:** Art. V, Sec. 2(zz) (trade/industry); Sec. 2(yy) (tourism); Sec. 2(e) (barter trade); Sec. 2(u) (economic zones); Art. XIII, Sec. 26 (barter trade); Art. XIII, Sec. 28 (economic zones); Art. XIII, Sec. 35 (tourism).[^22]

### Trade and industry

Islamic commercial law (*fiqh al-mu'amalat*) provides the framework for ethical trade. The classical concept of *hisbah* — market supervision to prevent fraud, ensure fair pricing, and enforce quality standards — is the Islamic equivalent of modern consumer protection and trade regulation.[^23]

**How to legislate:** Principled integration. A Bangsamoro Trade and Industry Act should include: consumer protection grounded in *maslahah*, halal product standards (coordinated with the Halal Board), Shari'ah-compliant trade financing options, and *hisbah*-informed market supervision.

### Tourism

Halal tourism is a growth sector. A halal-friendly tourism certification should cover:
- **Accommodation** — prayer facilities, halal kitchen, no alcohol, separate swimming facilities
- **Food services** — halal-certified restaurants, no pork
- **Tour packages** — heritage tourism featuring Islamic historical sites (Sultanate ruins, old mosques, *kirim* manuscript collections)
- **Pilgrimage tourism** — Hajj/Umrah travel services (coordinated with BPA)

**Model: Voluntary.** Halal tourism certification is a market differentiator, not a regulatory mandate. Establishments opt in.[^24]

### Barter trade

Art. XIII, Sec. 26 authorizes BARMM to regulate barter trade with ASEAN neighbors. The Islamic framework for barter is *bay' al-muqayadah* (exchange of goods for goods without money). The classic prohibition applies: no *gharar* (excessive uncertainty) and no exchange of identical commodities in unequal quantities (*riba al-fadl*).[^25]

### Economic zones

Art. XIII, Sec. 28 mandates BEZA (Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority). Economic zones can include halal industrial parks — designated areas where all tenants must comply with halal production standards. This creates a concentrated halal manufacturing hub with export potential.[^26]

## 13.9 Worked Example: Integrating Khalifah Principles into an Environmental Protection BAA

**Scenario:** The Committee on Environment asks you to review a proposed Bangsamoro Protected Areas Act. You need to assess Shari'ah integration opportunities.

**Step 1: Shari'ah Checkpoint (Chapter 9)**
- Does the subject involve property law? **Yes** — protected areas restrict property use.
- Does it involve natural resources? **Yes** — *khalifah* stewardship applies.
- Does the bill affect non-Muslims? **Yes** — universal application. Shari'ah language should complement, not replace, secular environmental justifications.

**Step 2: BOL basis**
- Art. XIII, Sec. 8 — Parliament shall establish protected areas
- Art. X, Sec. 4 — property law authority

**Step 3: PD 1083 check**
- Does PD 1083 cover environmental protection? **No.** No conflict possible.

**Step 4: Shari'ah provisions to recommend**
- **Preamble:** Add *khalifah* stewardship language: "Recognizing that the Bangsamoro people are stewards (*khalifah*) of the natural resources entrusted to them..."
- **Protected area categories:** Add *hima* as a culturally-recognized protected area category alongside NIPAS designations
- **Buffer zones:** Reference *harim* principles for watershed protection areas
- **Management boards:** Include *ulama* representative on community-managed protected area boards (following the BAA 60/61 precedent)
- **Reforestation programs:** Frame as *ihya al-mawat* (reviving dead land)

**Step 5: What NOT to do**
- Do not make Shari'ah compliance mandatory for environmental regulation — the *khalifah* framework enriches the law, it does not replace technical environmental standards
- Do not restrict non-Muslim access to protected areas based on Islamic rationale
- Do not present *hima* as a legal requirement — present it as a recognized category alongside existing frameworks

## 13.10 Common Pitfalls

- **Presenting Islamic finance instruments as enacted BARMM law.** *Mudarabah*, *musharakah*, *murabaha*, *sukuk* — none are codified in any BAA. They are *fiqh* concepts that Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes Parliament to enact. Until enacted, they are principles, not law.

- **Claiming the BOL does not mention halal.** It does. Art. XIII, Sec. 30 expressly mandates halal development legislation. This is one of the most common errors in policy documents.

- **Confusing the Bangsamoro Halal Board with the Philippine Halal Board.** The Bangsamoro Board (BAA 13) is a policy-making body. The Philippine Board (RA 10817) is the national regulatory framework. They must coordinate, not compete.

- **Treating the cooperative code as a priority code.** Art. XIII, Sec. 24 is a named legislative mandate, but it is NOT one of the 7 priority codes in Art. XVI, Sec. 4(a).

- **Ignoring the *waqf*-CARP conflict.** *Waqf* agricultural land is inalienable under Islamic law. CARP provides for compulsory redistribution. Any agrarian reform legislation must address this tension explicitly — either through exemption or a special category.

- **Applying *khalifah* stewardship as a regulatory mandate.** Environmental stewardship enriches legislation. It does not replace technical standards (EIA requirements, emission limits, conservation biology). Use it in preamble and policy declarations, not in operative provisions.
