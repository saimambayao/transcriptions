# Chapter 9 — The Shari'ah Checkpoint for Bill Drafting

Chapter 8 gave you the strategy. This chapter gives you the **process** — a step-by-step checkpoint that every bill should pass through to determine whether it has a Shari'ah dimension and, if so, how to address it.

The checkpoint is not a separate Shari'ah review process layered on top of the existing legislative workflow. It is a screening step *within* the workflow — the same way a constitutional review or a gender analysis is built into the bill-drafting process, not bolted on after.

---

## 9.1 When a Bill Has a Shari'ah Dimension

A bill has a Shari'ah dimension when any of the following conditions are met:

| Condition | Examples |
|-----------|---------|
| The subject involves **personal law** for Muslims | Marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody, guardianship, legitimacy, paternity |
| The subject involves **property relations** between Muslims | Land disputes, communal property, matrimonial property |
| The subject involves **minor criminal offenses** under Shari'ah court jurisdiction | *Ta'zir* offenses punishable by *arresto menor* or equivalent |
| The subject intersects with **Islamic finance** | Cooperative bylaws, banking, investment, insurance, bonds |
| The subject intersects with **halal certification** or regulation | Food safety, product standards, trade, tourism |
| The subject involves **waqf** (endowment) or **zakat** (alms) | Property administration, social welfare, fiscal policy |
| The subject involves **Islamic education** or *madaris* | Curriculum, accreditation, teacher qualifications |
| The subject involves the **BDI** or religious authorities | Institutional mandates, *fatwa* procedures, religious affairs |
| A provision in the bill **references Shari'ah**, "Islamic law," or "Muslim personal law" | Any bill text that invokes these terms |

If none of these conditions are met, the bill has no Shari'ah dimension. Note this in the committee report and proceed with the standard legislative process.

If one or more conditions are met, proceed to the compliance review.[^1]

---

## 9.2 The Shari'ah Compliance Review Process

The compliance review has four steps:

### Step 1: Identify the Shari'ah Provisions

Read the entire bill. For each section, ask: does this provision touch any of the conditions in the table above? Mark every section that does.

### Step 2: Map to Enacted Law

For each marked section, determine:
- **Does PD 1083 cover this subject?** If yes, verify that the bill does not conflict with PD 1083. The bill can supplement PD 1083 but not override it.[^2]
- **Does BOL Art. X authorize legislation on this subject?** Confirm the subject falls within Sec. 4's scope: personal/family/property law, commercial matters not in PD 1083, or *ta'zir*.
- **Does any existing BAA address this subject?** Check for overlap or conflict with enacted BAAs.

### Step 3: Check Constitutional Constraints

Apply the three constitutional constraints from Chapter 2:

1. **"Exclusively Muslim" boundary** — Does the bill apply Shari'ah obligations to non-Muslims? If so, does it include a voluntary submission provision?[^3]
2. **Bill of Rights** — Does the bill impose penalties (*ta'zir*)? If so, does it satisfy due process (notice, hearing, proportionality)?
3. **Supreme Court supervision** — Does the bill affect Shari'ah court jurisdiction? If so, does it preserve SC supervisory authority?[^4]

### Step 4: Determine BDI Consultation Need

Does the bill require the BDI's input? Consult the BDI when:
- The bill codifies a *fiqh* position where schools disagree
- The bill creates obligations grounded in religious practice
- The bill's Shari'ah basis needs authoritative confirmation

If BDI consultation is needed, frame the referral using the protocol in Chapter 7, Section 7.4.

## 9.3 Drafting Shari'ah-Consistent Legislation

When you have identified and mapped the Shari'ah dimensions, apply these drafting principles:

**Principle 1: Use general legislative language in the bill text.** Express Islamic principles in neutral, legal terms. Place the specific Qur'anic or Hadith source in a footnote — not in the operative text of the bill. This follows the citation protocol in Chapter 7, Section 7.1.

**Principle 2: State the PD 1083 relationship explicitly.** If the bill supplements PD 1083, say so: "This Act shall apply in addition to the provisions of Presidential Decree No. 1083, as amended." If the bill addresses a matter not covered by PD 1083, state that too: "This Act governs [subject], a matter not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083."

**Principle 3: Include a voluntary submission provision if non-Muslims may be affected.** Standard clause: "Where a party is a non-Muslim, the provisions of this Act shall apply only if the non-Muslim voluntarily submits to the jurisdiction established herein, in accordance with Section 1, Article X of Republic Act No. 11054."

**Principle 4: Identify the implementing agency.** If the bill creates new Shari'ah-related functions, assign them to an appropriate agency. For matters requiring religious expertise, the BDI is the natural implementing agency (BAA 13, Secs. 52-56). For regulatory matters, the relevant Ministry (e.g., MTIT for halal, MFBM for Islamic finance).[^5]

**Principle 5: Include a Shari'ah governance provision for complex bills.** For bills on Islamic finance, waqf, or zakat, include a section establishing a Shari'ah advisory board or requiring BDI certification of compliance. This provides ongoing Shari'ah oversight, not just one-time review.

## 9.4 The Role of the BDI in Legislative Review

The Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta, established under BAA 13, Secs. 52-56, is mandated to "promulgate *fatwa* (religious edicts) pertaining to the whole breadth of Islamic jurisprudence."[^6] This mandate extends to legislative review — the BDI can provide authoritative guidance on whether a proposed bill aligns with Shari'ah principles.

**Current practice:** The BDI's legislative review role is not yet formalized in Parliament's rules of procedure. The Shari'ah checkpoint proposed in this chapter would formalize it.

**Recommended process:**
1. Committee staff conducts the checkpoint (Secs. 9.1-9.2)
2. If BDI consultation is needed, the committee chair transmits the bill to the BDI with specific questions
3. The BDI reviews and issues a written advisory — not a *fatwa* on the bill, but an advisory opinion on the Shari'ah dimensions identified
4. The committee incorporates the BDI advisory into the committee report
5. Parliament considers the committee report, including the Shari'ah analysis, during deliberations

**What this is NOT:** This is not a veto power. The BDI advises; Parliament legislates. The BDI's role is advisory — it provides the Shari'ah expertise that committee staff may lack. The legislative decision remains with Parliament.

## 9.5 Worked Example: Reviewing a Draft BAA

> **Scenario:** The Committee on Trade and Industry receives a proposed "Bangsamoro Halal Industry Development Act." You are asked to conduct the Shari'ah checkpoint.
>
> **Step 1: Identify Shari'ah dimensions.**
> - Sec. 3 defines "halal" using Islamic dietary standards — Shari'ah dimension: **halal certification**
> - Sec. 8 requires all food manufacturers in BARMM to obtain halal certification — Shari'ah dimension: **may affect non-Muslims**
> - Sec. 12 establishes a Halal Standards Board with BDI representation — Shari'ah dimension: **BDI institutional role**
> - Sec. 15 imposes penalties for false halal labeling — Shari'ah dimension: **regulatory enforcement**
>
> **Step 2: Map to enacted law.**
> - PD 1083 does not cover halal regulation — this is a commercial matter "not provided for" under PD 1083
> - BOL Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes legislation on commercial matters — Parliament has jurisdiction
> - BAA 13 already establishes the Bangsamoro Halal Board under MTIT — check for overlap
>
> **Step 3: Constitutional check.**
> - Sec. 8 (mandatory certification for all manufacturers) could apply Shari'ah standards to non-Muslim businesses — **constitutional issue**. Fix: limit mandatory certification to products marketed as halal; make it voluntary for others.
> - Sec. 15 (penalties) must satisfy due process — include notice, hearing, and proportional sanctions.
>
> **Step 4: BDI consultation.**
> - The definition of "halal" in Sec. 3 needs BDI validation — halal standards are grounded in Islamic dietary law
> - The composition of the Halal Standards Board (Sec. 12) should include BDI as a mandatory member
>
> **Checkpoint result:** The bill has a Shari'ah dimension, falls within Parliament's jurisdiction, and needs two fixes (Sec. 8 scope, Sec. 15 due process) plus BDI consultation on the halal definition.

## 9.6 Common Pitfalls

- **Skipping the checkpoint for "non-Islamic" bills.** A budget bill, an education bill, or a health bill may have Shari'ah dimensions even if the word "Shari'ah" does not appear in the title. Run the checkpoint on every bill.
- **Treating the BDI advisory as a veto.** The BDI advises; Parliament decides. If Parliament disagrees with the BDI advisory, it can proceed — but should explain its reasoning in the committee report.
- **Forgetting PD 1083 check.** Before drafting any Shari'ah-related provision, always check whether PD 1083 already covers the subject.
- **Applying Shari'ah obligations to non-Muslims without voluntary submission.** This is the most common constitutional error. Always include the voluntary submission clause when non-Muslims may be affected.
- **Leaving the implementing agency unclear.** Every Shari'ah-related provision needs a designated implementing agency. Do not assume the BDI handles everything — it has a specific mandate.

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting a Shari'ah checkpoint report:

- [ ] Have you screened the bill against all conditions in the Sec. 9.1 table?
- [ ] Have you checked PD 1083 for overlap?
- [ ] Have you confirmed BOL Art. X, Sec. 4 jurisdiction?
- [ ] Have you applied the three constitutional constraints?
- [ ] Have you determined whether BDI consultation is needed?
- [ ] If non-Muslims may be affected, does the bill include voluntary submission?
- [ ] Have you identified the implementing agency for each Shari'ah-related provision?
