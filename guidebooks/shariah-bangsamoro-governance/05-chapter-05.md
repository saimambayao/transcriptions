# Chapter 5 — Maqasid al-Shari'ah as a Policy Framework

Chapters 2-4 gave you the legal architecture: the Constitution, the BOL, PD 1083, and the court system. This chapter gives you the **philosophical lens** through which Muslim scholars have understood the purpose of Shari'ah itself.

The Maqasid al-Shari'ah — the objectives or higher purposes of Islamic law — is a framework developed by classical scholars to evaluate whether laws and policies serve the fundamental values that Shari'ah aims to protect. It is not enacted law. No BAA codifies it. But it is the most widely used analytical tool in Islamic governance for evaluating legislation, development programs, and public policy.

This chapter teaches you how to use it — and how to know when you are outside your depth and need the BDI.

---

## 5.1 The Five Objectives

Classical Islamic scholars, most notably Imam Abu Hamid al-Ghazali (d. 1111 CE) and Imam Abu Ishaq al-Shatibi (d. 1388 CE), identified five fundamental objectives that Shari'ah seeks to protect:

| # | Arabic | English | What It Protects |
|---|--------|---------|-----------------|
| 1 | *Hifz al-Din* | Protection of Faith | Freedom of religion, religious practice, Islamic institutions |
| 2 | *Hifz al-Nafs* | Protection of Life | Right to life, health, physical security, dignity |
| 3 | *Hifz al-'Aql* | Protection of Intellect | Education, mental health, prohibition of intoxicants |
| 4 | *Hifz al-Nasl* | Protection of Lineage | Family integrity, marriage, child welfare, reproductive rights |
| 5 | *Hifz al-Mal* | Protection of Property | Economic rights, fair trade, prohibition of exploitation |

These five objectives operate at three levels of necessity:

1. ***Daruriyyat*** (necessities) — what is essential for survival and social order
2. ***Hajiyyat*** (needs) — what removes hardship and facilitates life
3. ***Tahsiniyyat*** (enhancements) — what improves quality of life and embellishes society

A policy that protects life at the necessity level (emergency healthcare) takes priority over one that enhances property at the improvement level (tourism promotion).[^1]

## 5.2 Maqasid as a Legislative Lens

Maqasid is not a substitute for enacted law. It is a lens for evaluating whether proposed legislation aligns with the broader purposes of Shari'ah. Here is how practitioners can use it:

**When drafting a bill:** Ask whether the proposed legislation serves one or more of the five objectives. A Bangsamoro Waqf Act serves *hifz al-mal* (property protection) and *hifz al-din* (faith — through endowment for religious purposes). A Bangsamoro Education Code serves *hifz al-'aql* (intellect). Making this connection explicit in the bill's explanatory note strengthens its Shari'ah foundation.

**When analyzing a policy:** Evaluate whether the policy protects or threatens any of the five objectives. A health program that improves maternal care serves *hifz al-nafs* (life) and *hifz al-nasl* (lineage). A budget cut that defunds Islamic schools threatens *hifz al-din* (faith) and *hifz al-'aql* (intellect).

**When resolving competing interests:** Use the hierarchy. Necessities take priority over needs, which take priority over enhancements. Protection of life (*nafs*) generally takes priority over protection of property (*mal*).

**What Maqasid cannot do:** It cannot tell you the specific legal ruling on a contested question. If scholars disagree on whether a particular financial instrument is Shari'ah-compliant, Maqasid analysis cannot resolve the disagreement — that requires *fiqh* expertise and, in the BARMM context, a BDI consultation.

## 5.3 How Maqasid Aligns with the BDP and Mas Matatag Agenda

The Maqasid framework maps naturally onto BARMM's development goals. This alignment is not accidental — Islamic governance and development planning share common aspirations.

| Maqasid | BDP Goal | Mas Matatag Pillar |
|---------|---------|-------------------|
| *Hifz al-Din* (Faith) | Goal 5: Culture and identity preserved | Mas Matatag na Paniniwala (Stronger Faith) |
| *Hifz al-Nafs* (Life) | Goal 4: Inclusive social services | Mas Matatag na Pamayanan |
| *Hifz al-'Aql* (Intellect) | Goal 4: Inclusive social services (education) | Mas Matatag na Pamayanan |
| *Hifz al-Nasl* (Lineage) | Goal 3: Peaceful communities | Mas Matatag na Seguridad |
| *Hifz al-Mal* (Property) | Goal 2: Equitable economy | Mas Matatag na Kabuhayan (Stronger Livelihood) |
| Cross-cutting | Goal 1: Stable governance | Mas Matatag na Gobyerno |

**Practical use:** When writing a policy recommendation or legislative briefer, mapping the proposed policy to both the BDP goal AND the corresponding Maqasid objective gives the document a dual foundation — one in the secular development framework, one in the Islamic governance tradition. This dual grounding is particularly effective for the Bangsamoro context, where governance operates at the intersection of both traditions.[^2]

## 5.4 Application in Program Design and Policy Analysis

**For development practitioners:** When designing a program for BARMM, consider which Maqasid objectives it serves. This is especially relevant for programs that will operate in communities where Islamic governance principles are expected.

**Example — Cooperative development program:**
- *Hifz al-mal* (property): Builds economic capacity through collective enterprise
- *Hifz al-nasl* (lineage): Strengthens family livelihoods
- *Hifz al-'aql* (intellect): Provides financial literacy and skills training

**Example — Halal industry development:**
- *Hifz al-din* (faith): Ensures food compliance with Islamic dietary requirements
- *Hifz al-mal* (property): Creates economic opportunities in halal markets
- *Hifz al-nafs* (life): Promotes food safety standards

**For policy analysts:** When evaluating a proposed BAA or resolution, assess its Maqasid impact as part of the Shari'ah analysis section (Section VII of a legislative briefer). This goes beyond checking whether the bill mentions Shari'ah — it evaluates whether the bill's substance aligns with Islamic governance principles.

## 5.5 When Maqasid Analysis Needs BDI Consultation

Maqasid is a useful policy lens. But it has limits.

**You can apply Maqasid analysis yourself when:**
- You are mapping a proposed policy to the five objectives (descriptive analysis)
- You are comparing competing policy options using the necessity hierarchy
- You are writing an explanatory note that articulates a bill's Islamic governance rationale
- The connection between the policy and the Maqasid objective is straightforward and uncontested

**You should recommend BDI consultation when:**
- Two Maqasid objectives conflict and the resolution requires *fiqh* reasoning (e.g., protecting faith vs. protecting life in a public health context)
- The policy involves a novel application of Maqasid to a modern issue with no classical precedent
- The analysis will be used to argue that a specific legislative provision is "Shari'ah-compliant" — this is a religious compliance claim that requires scholarly authority
- Stakeholders disagree on which Maqasid objective should take priority

**The boundary:** You are an analyst, not a *mufti*. You can identify the Maqasid dimensions of a policy. You can map the connections. You can present the framework. But when the question shifts from "which objectives does this policy serve?" to "is this policy Shari'ah-compliant?" — that is a BDI question.

## 5.6 Worked Example

> **Scenario:** The Ministry of Health is proposing a mandatory vaccination program for children in BARMM. Some community leaders raise concerns that the program may conflict with Islamic principles. You are asked to prepare a Maqasid analysis.
>
> **Analysis:**
>
> 1. **Maqasid mapping:**
>    - *Hifz al-nafs* (protection of life): Vaccination directly protects children's lives and health — this is a *daruriyyat* (necessity) level concern
>    - *Hifz al-nasl* (protection of lineage): Protecting children's health preserves future generations
>    - *Hifz al-din* (protection of faith): Some community leaders question whether vaccine ingredients comply with Islamic dietary standards — this is a legitimate *hajiyyat* (need) level concern
>
> 2. **Priority analysis:** Protection of life at the necessity level takes priority over dietary compliance at the need level. The stronger Maqasid argument supports vaccination.
>
> 3. **Practical recommendation:** The program should proceed, but with accommodations: (a) verify vaccine ingredients against halal standards where possible, (b) engage the BDI to issue guidance on the permissibility of vaccines, (c) communicate the Maqasid analysis to community leaders.
>
> 4. **BDI referral:** The specific question of whether vaccines with non-halal-certified ingredients are permissible under Islamic law requires a *fatwa* from the BDI. The analyst can present the Maqasid framework; the BDI issues the ruling.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Treating Maqasid as enacted law.** It is a scholarly framework, not legislation. No BAA codifies it. Use it as an analytical lens, not a legal authority.
- **Using Maqasid to bypass enacted law.** If PD 1083 or a BAA addresses a subject, the enacted law governs — regardless of what a Maqasid analysis might suggest.
- **Oversimplifying the five objectives.** Each objective has depth and nuance. "Protection of property" does not mean all economic activity is Shari'ah-compliant — it means the Shari'ah framework prioritizes fair, non-exploitative economic relations.
- **Claiming Shari'ah compliance based on Maqasid alone.** Maqasid analysis can show that a policy aligns with Islamic values. It cannot certify that the policy is Shari'ah-compliant — that requires *fiqh* analysis and, for binding determinations, BDI authority.
- **Ignoring the three levels of necessity.** Not all Maqasid applications are equal. A policy that serves a *tahsiniyyat* (enhancement) should not be prioritized over one that serves a *daruriyyat* (necessity).

---

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting any work product that uses Maqasid analysis:

- [ ] Have you identified which of the five objectives the policy serves?
- [ ] Have you specified the necessity level (*daruriyyat*, *hajiyyat*, *tahsiniyyat*)?
- [ ] Have you mapped the policy to both BDP goals AND Maqasid objectives?
- [ ] Have you labeled the analysis as "Maqasid framework analysis" — not as a compliance determination?
- [ ] Have you recommended BDI consultation where the question shifts from analysis to compliance?
- [ ] Have you avoided presenting Maqasid as enacted law?
