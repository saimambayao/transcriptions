# Chapter 10 — The Current Legislative Landscape

Chapters 8-9 gave you the strategy and the process. This chapter maps the **current state** — what Parliament has enacted, what is pending, and where the legislative gaps are. Use it as a baseline for any Shari'ah legislative agenda.

---

## 10.1 Foundational Codes with Embedded Islamic Principles

Three foundational BAAs embed Islamic principles within their broader governance frameworks:

**BAA 13 — Bangsamoro Administrative Code**
The most important. Establishes the BDI (Secs. 52-56), the Bangsamoro Halal Board (under MTIT), and references Islamic institutions throughout the government structure. This is the institutional home for Shari'ah governance in BARMM.[^1]

**BAA 17 — Bangsamoro Civil Service Code**
Establishes the civil service framework. While not explicitly Shari'ah legislation, it governs the personnel system that staffs the BDI, Shari'ah courts, and other Islamic institutions.

**BAA 18 — Bangsamoro Education Code**
Establishes the integrated education system including Islamic education and *madaris*. Directly addresses the intersection of secular and Islamic education within BARMM.[^2]

**BAA 20 — Dual Hijri-Gregorian Calendar**
Mandates dual calendar use across all BARMM ministries, agencies, and LGUs. Implemented through the BDI. A small but symbolic exercise of Parliament's authority to integrate Islamic practice into governance.[^3]

## 10.3 Bills with Implicit Shari'ah Alignment

Some bills do not explicitly reference Shari'ah but align with its principles:

- **Cooperative development legislation** — aligns with Islamic finance principles (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*) even when not explicitly framed as such
- **Social welfare legislation** — may align with *zakat* distribution principles
- **Environmental legislation** — aligns with *hifz al-mal* (protection of property) and stewardship (*khalifah*) principles
- **Health legislation** — aligns with *hifz al-nafs* (protection of life) and may involve halal pharmaceutical standards

These implicit alignments are opportunities for mainstreaming. When such bills pass through the Shari'ah checkpoint (Chapter 9), the implicit alignment can be made explicit through appropriate provisions.

## 10.4 Legislative Gaps: Where Parliament Has Not Yet Acted

BOL Art. X, Sec. 4 authorizes legislation in areas where no BAA yet exists. The major gaps:

| Subject | BOL Basis | PD 1083 Status | Gap |
|---------|----------|---------------|-----|
| **Waqf (endowment)** | Art. X, Sec. 4 (property law) | Basic property provisions only | No comprehensive waqf registry, administration, or regulatory framework |
| **Zakat (alms)** | Art. X, Sec. 4 (personal/family law) | Not covered | No formal zakat collection, distribution, or administration system |
| **Islamic finance** | Art. X, Sec. 4 (commercial matters) | Not covered | No legal framework for *mudarabah*, *musharakah*, *murabaha*, *sukuk*, or *takaful* |
| **Halal regulation** | Art. X, Sec. 4 (commercial matters) | Not covered | BAA 13 creates Halal Board but no comprehensive halal regulatory act |
| **Islamic banking** | Art. X, Sec. 4 (commercial matters) | Not covered | No BARMM-specific Islamic banking framework |
| **Shari'ah legal profession** | Art. X, Sec. 16 | Not covered | Parliament authorized to create Shari'ah public assistance office, prosecution service, academy, and Office of the Jurisconsult — none yet created |

Each of these gaps represents a legislative opportunity. Chapter 8's phased hybrid strategy recommends prioritizing waqf, zakat, Islamic finance, and halal in the near term.[^5]

## 10.5 Comparative: How Other Autonomous Muslim Regions Legislate Shari'ah

BARMM is not the first autonomous Muslim region to face these legislative questions. Comparative models offer lessons.

**Malaysia** operates a dual legal system where each state enacts its own Islamic law. Key features:
- State-level Shari'ah courts with jurisdiction over Muslims
- Federal Islamic Financial Services Act 2013 for Islamic banking and finance
- State-level *waqf* and *zakat* administration
- **Lesson for BARMM:** Standalone Islamic finance legislation at the national level, combined with state-level personal law, provides comprehensive coverage

**Aceh, Indonesia** has the broadest Shari'ah authority among Indonesian provinces. Key features:
- *Qanun* (regional regulations) on Islamic criminal law, including *ta'zir*
- Zakat management through a dedicated government agency (Baitul Mal)
- *Waqf* regulation under national law with local implementation
- **Lesson for BARMM:** Dedicated zakat administration agency is effective; criminal law beyond *ta'zir* requires careful constitutional navigation

**The former ARMM (1989-2019)** operated under RA 6734 and RA 9054. Key features:
- Limited legislative output on Shari'ah matters
- PD 1083 continued as the primary Islamic law framework
- No standalone Shari'ah legislation enacted
- **Lesson for BARMM:** The BOL gives Parliament significantly broader Shari'ah legislative power than ARMM had — but this power must be actively exercised, or it will remain dormant as it did under ARMM

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming PBs are enacted.** A pending bill is not law. Verify the status of any bill before citing it as authority.
- **Ignoring the ARMM precedent.** ARMM's failure to exercise its Shari'ah legislative authority is a cautionary tale for BARMM. Authority that is not exercised atrophies.
- **Copying foreign models without adaptation.** Malaysia and Aceh operate within different constitutional frameworks. Their models inform but cannot be directly transplanted.
- **Overlooking Art. X, Sec. 16 opportunities.** Parliament can create a Shari'ah public assistance office, prosecution service, academy, and Office of the Jurisconsult. None has been created yet — these are low-hanging legislative opportunities.
