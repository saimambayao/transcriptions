# Chapter 11 — Islamic Finance for Cooperatives and Social Enterprises

This chapter bridges Shari'ah law and economic development. If you organize cooperatives, manage social enterprises, or design economic programs in BARMM, you need to understand how Islamic finance instruments work — and how they intersect with Philippine cooperative law.

---

## 11.1 The Legal Framework

Two legal frameworks govern cooperatives in BARMM simultaneously:

**Republic Act No. 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008)** governs the registration, organization, and operation of all cooperatives in the Philippines, including BARMM. Every cooperative must comply with RA 9520 regardless of its financing structure.[^1]

**BOL Art. X, Sec. 4** grants Parliament power to legislate on commercial matters not provided for under PD 1083. Islamic finance instruments fall squarely within this mandate — PD 1083 covers personal law, not commercial finance.[^2]

**The dual compliance challenge:** A Shari'ah-compliant cooperative must satisfy both RA 9520's requirements (registration, governance, reporting) AND Islamic finance principles (prohibition of *riba*, requirement of asset-backing, profit-and-loss sharing). No BARMM BAA yet codifies this dual framework — it is a legislative gap identified in Chapter 10.

---

## 11.2 Key Instruments: Mudarabah, Musharakah, Murabaha

Three Islamic finance instruments are most relevant to cooperative and social enterprise development in BARMM:

### Mudarabah (Profit-Sharing Partnership)

**How it works:** One party provides capital (*rabb al-mal*), the other provides labor and management (*mudarib*). Profits are shared according to an agreed ratio. Losses are borne by the capital provider — the labor provider loses only their effort.

**Cooperative application:** A cooperative pools member capital (the *rabb al-mal* function) and deploys it through a management team (the *mudarib* function). Profits are distributed to members based on the agreed ratio.

**Current legal status in BARMM:** *Fiqh* concept — no BAA codifies it. Parliament can legislate under Art. X, Sec. 4.[^3]

### Musharakah (Joint Venture)

**How it works:** All parties contribute capital AND share in management. Profits are shared per agreement; losses are shared in proportion to capital contribution.

**Cooperative application:** A multi-stakeholder cooperative where members are both investors and workers. Each member contributes capital and labor, sharing profits and losses proportionally.

**Current legal status in BARMM:** *Fiqh* concept — no BAA codifies it.[^4]

### Murabaha (Cost-Plus Financing)

**How it works:** The seller discloses the cost of a good and adds an agreed markup. The buyer knows exactly what the cost is and what the profit margin is. This is permissible because it is a sale transaction, not a loan — there is no interest (*riba*).

**Cooperative application:** A cooperative purchases equipment or inventory at cost and sells to members at a disclosed markup. The markup replaces interest-based financing.

**Current legal status in BARMM:** *Fiqh* concept — no BAA codifies it.[^5]

### The Critical Distinction: Fiqh vs. Enacted Law

**None of these instruments are codified in BARMM law.** The BOL authorizes Parliament to legislate on them, but no BAA has been enacted. When you use these terms in a program design or proposal, label them as *fiqh* concepts — not as enacted BARMM law. Do not write "BARMM law requires *mudarabah* financing." Write "Islamic finance principles, which Parliament is authorized to codify under BOL Art. X, Sec. 4, include *mudarabah*."

## 11.3 Waqf and Zakat

### Waqf (Islamic Endowment)

*Waqf* is the dedication of property for charitable or religious purposes in perpetuity. The property is held in trust — it cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited. Income generated from the *waqf* property is distributed according to the donor's terms.

**BOL basis:** Art. X, Sec. 4 (property law jurisdiction).[^6]

**PD 1083 coverage:** PD 1083 includes basic property provisions but does not comprehensively address *waqf* as an institution. This is a legislative gap.

**What BARMM needs:** A Bangsamoro Waqf Act establishing a waqf registry, administration framework, regulatory body, and dispute resolution mechanism. See Chapter 8, Sec. 8.6 (Phase 2 priorities).

### Zakat (Obligatory Alms)

*Zakat* is one of the Five Pillars of Islam — an obligatory charitable contribution calculated as a percentage of qualifying wealth. It is distinct from voluntary charity (*sadaqah*).

**BOL basis:** Art. X, Sec. 4 (personal law jurisdiction — zakat is a personal obligation).[^7]

**PD 1083 coverage:** Not covered.

**What BARMM needs:** A Bangsamoro Zakat Act establishing collection mechanisms, distribution categories (the eight categories specified in the Qur'an, Surah At-Tawbah 9:60), and an administration framework.

## 11.4 Shari'ah-Compliant Cooperative Bylaws

When organizing a cooperative that will use Islamic finance principles, the bylaws must address:

1. **Prohibition of *riba* (interest).** The bylaws should state that the cooperative does not engage in interest-based lending or borrowing. Alternative provisions: profit-sharing (*mudarabah*), joint venture (*musharakah*), or cost-plus sale (*murabaha*).

2. **Profit-and-loss sharing.** The bylaws should specify the profit-sharing ratio and the loss allocation mechanism.

3. **Asset-backing requirement.** Islamic finance requires that transactions be backed by real assets or services — not purely monetary speculation. The bylaws should require that all financial transactions have an underlying asset or service.

4. **Shari'ah advisory.** For larger cooperatives, the bylaws may establish a Shari'ah advisory committee or require periodic BDI review of financial operations.

5. **RA 9520 compliance.** The bylaws must satisfy all standard cooperative requirements: minimum membership, governance structure, financial reporting, audit requirements. Shari'ah compliance is additional — it does not replace statutory requirements.

---

## 11.5 The Dual Compliance Challenge

The challenge is real: RA 9520 assumes interest-based financing as the norm. Cooperative lending, savings deposits, and credit operations are all framed in terms of interest rates, loan terms, and debt obligations.

A Shari'ah-compliant cooperative must restructure these operations:

| RA 9520 Concept | Shari'ah-Compliant Alternative |
|----------------|-------------------------------|
| Savings deposits earning interest | Investment accounts earning profit share (*mudarabah*) |
| Loans to members at interest | Cost-plus sale (*murabaha*) or profit-sharing venture (*musharakah*) |
| Credit operations | Trade-based financing or asset-backed transactions |
| Fixed interest returns | Variable profit returns based on actual performance |

**The legal question:** Does RA 9520 prohibit non-interest-based financing structures? No — RA 9520 requires financial transparency and member protection, but does not mandate interest-based operations. A cooperative that uses profit-sharing instead of interest can comply with RA 9520 by disclosing the profit-sharing mechanism clearly in its bylaws and financial reports.

## 11.6 Worked Example: Structuring a Shari'ah-Compliant Cooperative

> **Scenario:** A group of 30 farmers in Maguindanao del Sur wants to form a cooperative that provides Shari'ah-compliant agricultural financing.
>
> **Structure:**
>
> 1. **Registration:** Register under RA 9520 as a multipurpose cooperative with the Cooperative Development Authority (or its BARMM counterpart).
>
> 2. **Capital:** Members contribute share capital (minimum as required by RA 9520). This forms the cooperative's investment pool.
>
> 3. **Financing model:** Instead of lending to members at interest, the cooperative purchases agricultural inputs (seeds, fertilizer, equipment) at cost and sells to members at a disclosed markup (*murabaha*). The markup replaces interest.
>
> 4. **Revenue sharing:** The cooperative's net surplus from operations is distributed to members based on patronage (RA 9520 requirement) and capital contribution.
>
> 5. **Bylaws:** Include Shari'ah compliance provisions: prohibition of *riba*, asset-backing requirement, and a Shari'ah advisory clause (periodic review by BDI or qualified *'ulama*).
>
> 6. **Legal basis:** RA 9520 (cooperative structure) + BOL Art. X, Sec. 4 (Islamic finance authorization, pending legislation).

## 11.7 Common Pitfalls

- **Presenting Islamic finance instruments as enacted law.** *Mudarabah*, *musharakah*, and *murabaha* are *fiqh* concepts. No BAA codifies them. Always label them correctly.
- **Ignoring RA 9520.** A Shari'ah-compliant cooperative must still comply with Philippine cooperative law. Islamic principles supplement — they do not replace — statutory requirements.
- **Assuming all interest is *riba*.** The definition of *riba* in Islamic jurisprudence is specific and debated among scholars. Consult the BDI before declaring a specific financial practice as *riba*.
- **Creating bylaws without legal review.** Shari'ah-compliant bylaws must satisfy both Islamic principles and RA 9520. Have them reviewed by both a cooperative development specialist and a qualified Islamic finance advisor.
