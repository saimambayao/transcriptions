# Chapter 11 — Social Justice and Human Development

Every bill that touches health, education, labor, social welfare, or housing in BARMM has a Shari'ah dimension. Not because someone decided to add one — but because the BOL's social justice provisions operate in a region where three of the five *maqasid al-Shari'ah* directly govern how these services must be delivered: preservation of life (*hifz al-nafs*), preservation of intellect (*hifz al-'aql*), and preservation of lineage (*hifz al-nasl*).

This chapter walks through each Art. IX power, shows you where Shari'ah is already legislated, where the gaps are, and how to draft legislation that integrates Shari'ah principles without overreach.

---

## 11.1 Art. IX and the Shari'ah Imperative

BOL Article IX (Basic Rights) establishes BARMM's social development mandate through its social justice provisions. It covers health, education, labor, social services, housing, and human rights — the powers that directly shape daily life.[^1]

What makes Art. IX different from the rest of the BOL is this: every power here serves at least one *maqsad* (objective) of Shari'ah. Health serves *hifz al-nafs*. Education serves *hifz al-'aql*. Social welfare serves *hifz al-nasl*. This is not an abstract theological point — it means that a health bill without halal compliance provisions, or an education bill that ignores the Madaris system, is not just incomplete governance. It is incomplete Shari'ah compliance.

**Three integration models** apply across these powers:

1. **Universal standard** — Shari'ah-informed protections apply to all workers, all facilities, all beneficiaries. No opt-out. BAA 82 (Labor Code) uses this model.
2. **Dual-track** — Both conventional and Shari'ah-compliant options exist side by side. The beneficiary chooses. Housing finance should use this model.
3. **Principled integration** — Shari'ah values (*maslahah*, *'adl*, *amanah*) guide policy design, but no specific Shari'ah compliance mechanism is mandated. Human rights legislation uses this model.

The right model depends on the power. This chapter tells you which one to use and why.

## 11.2 Health: Legislating Hifz al-Nafs

**BOL basis:** Art. IX, Sec. 22 (health mandate). Art. X, Sec. 4 (Shari'ah legislative authority).[^2]

**BAA 13 mandate:** Sec. 3(j) — the Ministry of Health shall formulate "policies, guidelines, regulations as well as operating procedures congruent to Islamic teachings on health and sanitation."[^3] This is the **most explicit Shari'ah integration mandate** for any ministry in the entire Administrative Code. No other ministry has language this direct.

### What exists

Parliament has signaled intent but not yet enacted comprehensive legislation:

- **BTA Resolution 201** — urges ibadah-friendly hospital standards[^4]
- **BTA Resolution 371** — calls for Muslim Filipino nutritional guidelines[^5]
- **Proposed Bill 302** — ibadah-friendly hospital accreditation (pending)[^6]
- **BAA 13 Sec. 3(j)** — the standing mandate for the MOH

### What is missing

No comprehensive Bangsamoro Health Act exists. No halal pharmaceutical standards. No Shari'ah-compliant bioethics code for reproductive health, organ donation, or end-of-life care. No Darul-Ifta' *fatawa* on health facility standards.

### How to legislate

**Model: Dual-track** — baseline mandatory + optional enhanced standard.

**Baseline (all government health facilities):**
- Halal food service — no pork, no non-halal meat in hospital meals
- Prayer space — at minimum, a designated clean area with *qiblah* direction
- Modesty accommodation — female health workers available for female patients on request
- Halal medicine screening — pharmacy reviews ingredient lists for non-halal components (gelatin capsules, alcohol-based solvents)

**Enhanced (ibadah-friendly accreditation):**
- Dedicated *wudu'* facilities
- Friday prayer arrangements for long-stay patients
- Islamic end-of-life care protocol (*talqin*, body preparation)
- Shari'ah-compliant family planning counseling alongside conventional options
- On-call Islamic chaplaincy service

**BDI referral triggers:** Bioethical questions where *madhhab* positions diverge — assisted reproduction, organ transplantation from non-Muslim donors, brain death criteria. These require a *fatwa*, not staff discretion.

**The bill structure:** A health BAA should include a Shari'ah compliance chapter (similar to BAA 82's Book III for labor). This chapter sets baseline standards for all government facilities, creates the ibadah-friendly accreditation program, and establishes the BDI consultation mechanism for bioethical questions.

## 11.3 Education and the Madaris System

**BOL basis:** Art. IX, Sec. 18 — mandates the Madaris educational system and integration of Islamic and Arabic studies for Muslim pupils.[^7] This is not optional — it is a constitutional-level requirement.

**Existing legislation:** BAA 18 (Bangsamoro Education Code) governs the integrated education system. BAA 13 establishes the MBHTE.[^8]

### The Shari'ah dimension

Education is one of the few areas where Shari'ah and the BOL give the same instruction: education is an obligation. The Qur'anic tradition of *talab al-'ilm* (seeking knowledge as a religious duty) mirrors Art. IX, Sec. 16's designation of education as BARMM's "top priority."[^9]

The preservation of intellect (*hifz al-'aql*) — one of the five *maqasid* — makes education not just a government service but an Islamic obligation. A BARMM education system that fails to provide Islamic and Arabic studies is constitutionally deficient under the BOL *and* religiously deficient under Shari'ah.

### What is missing

- **No standalone Madaris Development Act** with dedicated funding formula
- **No codified *waqf*-based education funding** — the traditional Islamic mechanism for sustaining educational institutions
- **Madaris teacher qualification standards** — the boundary between CSC and MBHTE jurisdiction needs legislative clarity
- **No comprehensive curriculum standards law** for Islamic studies in public schools

### How to legislate

**Model: Direct mandate** — the Madaris system is constitutionally required, not optional.

A Madaris Development Act should:
1. Establish dedicated funding through a combination of general appropriations and *waqf* endowment income
2. Set teacher qualifications that recognize both secular education credentials and *'alim* (Islamic scholarship) credentials
3. Define curriculum standards for Islamic and Arabic studies integrated into the K-12 system
4. Create an accreditation pathway for private Madaris to access government support
5. Include a Darul-Ifta' consultation clause for Islamic curriculum content questions

**The *waqf* funding mechanism:** A separate provision (or a standalone Bangsamoro Waqf Act) should codify *waqf* property — land, buildings, cash — as a perpetual endowment for Madaris. This mirrors the traditional Islamic model where wealthy patrons funded educational institutions through irrevocable charitable endowments. The *waqf* cannot be sold, gifted, or inherited — its income funds the institution in perpetuity.[^10]

## 11.4 Labor: BAA 82 as the Mainstreaming Model

**BOL basis:** Art. IX, Sec. 10 (labor power). Art. X, Sec. 4 (Shari'ah legislative authority).[^11]

**Existing legislation:** BAA 82 (Bangsamoro Labor and Employment Code), Book III, Secs. 106-113 — the first Philippine labor statute to codify Islamic workplace practices.[^12]

### What BAA 82 got right

BAA 82 is the best existing example of how to mainstream Shari'ah into sectoral legislation. Book III does not create a separate "Islamic labor track." It establishes universal standards that apply to **all employers and all workers** in BARMM — Muslim and non-Muslim. The protections are:

| Section | Provision | What It Requires |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| Sec. 106 | Religious clothing | Employers must respect Islamic dress for Muslim and non-Muslim workers |
| Sec. 107 | Prayer space | Establishments with 20+ employees must endeavor to provide a prayer area |
| Sec. 108 | Holy day prayer break | 60 minutes compensable time for congregational prayer |
| Sec. 109 | Call for prayer | Minimize workplace disturbances during *adhan* |
| Sec. 110 | Pilgrimage leave | Up to 30 days unpaid leave for Hajj/Umrah, once per tenure, after 2 years of service |
| Sec. 111 | Dietary accommodation | No worker may be compelled to handle food or substances against their beliefs |
| Sec. 113 | Ramadhan observance | Employers must accommodate *Iftar* and *Suhoor* meal breaks |

### Why this model works for other sectors

BAA 82's approach succeeds because it:
1. **Does not create a parallel system** — one labor code for everyone
2. **Accommodates without imposing** — prayer space is provided, but prayer is not mandated
3. **Uses plain statutory language** — no Arabic terms in operative provisions; *fiqh* concepts are translated into enforceable standards
4. **Applies to all employers** — public and private, Muslim and non-Muslim
5. **Includes enforcement** — MOLE has authority to inspect and penalize

**When you draft a Shari'ah-informed bill in any sector, ask:** *Can I use the BAA 82 model?* If the answer is yes — universal standard, no parallel track, accommodation without imposition — you have a tested template.

### What is still needed

- **Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR)** for Book III — MOLE has not yet issued these
- **Enforcement mechanism** — how inspections will verify prayer space compliance, dietary accommodation
- **Clarity on dual-registered establishments** — businesses registered under both BARMM and national systems

## 11.5 Social Welfare, Housing, and Humanitarian Services

### Social Welfare

**BOL basis:** Art. V, Sec. 2(vv) (social services). Art. X, Sec. 4 (Shari'ah jurisdiction on family/property matters).[^13]

**PD 1083 intersection:** Art. 70-72 — guardianship (*walaya*) of Muslim minors falls under Shari'ah court jurisdiction. This means any social welfare legislation on child placement, foster care, or guardianship **must not conflict** with PD 1083's guardianship provisions.[^14]

**Key gap: *Kafala* legislation.** Islamic fostering (*kafala*) is distinct from Western adoption. Under *kafala*, the child retains their biological family name and inheritance rights. The foster family provides care but does not sever the child's legal ties to their biological parents. No BARMM legislation codifies *kafala*. A Bangsamoro Social Welfare Act should include a *kafala* chapter alongside conventional foster care — the dual-track model.[^15]

**Key gap: *Zakat* as welfare funding.** The eight *zakat* recipient categories (*asnaf*) specified in the Qur'an overlap significantly with social welfare beneficiaries — the poor (*fuqara'*), the needy (*masakin*), debtors (*gharimin*), and wayfarers (*ibn al-sabil*). No Bangsamoro Zakat Act exists to formalize this as a supplementary welfare funding stream.[^16]

### Housing

**BOL basis:** Art. V, Sec. 2(cc) (housing). Art. X, Sec. 4 (commercial matters not in PD 1083).[^17]

**The central problem:** Conventional mortgages charge interest (*riba*). Interest is prohibited under Shari'ah. A government housing program that offers **only** interest-bearing finance excludes observant Muslims — or forces them to choose between housing and religious compliance. This is not a theoretical concern. Shelter is a necessity (*daruriyyat*) under the *maqasid*.[^18]

**How to legislate:** Dual-track housing finance.

| Track | Instrument | How It Works |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| Conventional | Mortgage | Bank lends money, borrower repays with interest |
| Shari'ah | *Murabaha* | Bank buys the property, sells it to the beneficiary at cost + disclosed markup (installment sale, no interest) |
| Shari'ah | *Ijarah muntahia bi al-tamlik* | Lease-to-own: beneficiary rents the property, ownership transfers at end of lease term |
| Shari'ah | *Musharakah mutanaqisah* | Diminishing partnership: bank and beneficiary co-own the property; beneficiary gradually buys out the bank's share |

A Bangsamoro Housing Act should mandate that all government housing programs offer at least one Shari'ah-compliant financing option alongside conventional options. The beneficiary chooses. Darul-Ifta' certifies which financing instruments qualify as Shari'ah-compliant.

### Humanitarian Services

**BOL basis:** Art. V, Sec. 2(dd) (humanitarian services). BAA 62 (IDP Protection Act).[^19]

**The precedent:** BAA 62 is the only enacted BAA that expressly incorporates the *maqasid al-Shari'ah* as a binding legal framework. Section 2 states that the law is "guided by the principles and objectives of Shari'ah, including the preservation of life, intellect, faith, posterity, and property."[^20] This makes BAA 62 a template for how to integrate Shari'ah into any legislation — cite the *maqasid* in the Declaration of Policy, then let specific provisions implement each objective.

**What is needed:** A Bangsamoro Disaster Risk Reduction and Management (DRRM) enabling law that follows the BAA 62 model — *maqasid* in the policy declaration, practical accommodations in service delivery (halal relief food, gender-appropriate evacuation centers, prayer space in shelters), and *zakat*/*sadaqah* as supplementary emergency funding.

## 11.6 Human Rights through an Islamic Lens

**BOL basis:** Art. IX, Secs. 6-7 (rights guarantees). BAA 4 (Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission). BAA 62 (IDP Act — *maqasid* integration precedent). BAA 89 (Transitional Justice and Reconciliation).[^21]

**Model: Principled integration** — human rights are universal. The Shari'ah dimension enriches, not restricts.

The five *maqasid al-Shari'ah* map directly onto international human rights:

| Maqsad | Human Right Equivalent |
|--------|----------------------|
| *Hifz al-nafs* (life) | Right to life, right to health |
| *Hifz al-'aql* (intellect) | Right to education, freedom of thought |
| *Hifz al-din* (faith) | Freedom of religion |
| *Hifz al-nasl* (lineage) | Family rights, children's rights |
| *Hifz al-mal* (property) | Property rights, economic rights |

**Why this matters for legislation:** When drafting human rights legislation, citing the *maqasid* alongside international standards does two things. First, it gives Shari'ah-grounded legitimacy to the law in a majority-Muslim region. Second, it shows that human rights protections are not "Western imports" but are rooted in Islamic jurisprudence.

BAA 62 demonstrated this approach. BAA 89 (Transitional Justice) builds on it by using Islamic reconciliation concepts (*sulh* and *islah*) alongside transitional justice mechanisms.[^22]

**What is needed:** No standalone BARMM anti-discrimination act exists. The BHRC-CHR operational relationship needs MOA formalization. Future human rights legislation should follow the BAA 62 *maqasid* model.

## 11.7 Worked Example: Drafting an Ibadah-Friendly Hospital Accreditation BAA

**Scenario:** A Member of Parliament asks you to prepare a draft bill establishing ibadah-friendly hospital accreditation standards for BARMM government hospitals.

**Step 1: Shari'ah Checkpoint (Chapter 9)**
- Does the subject involve personal law for Muslims? No.
- Does it involve health services? **Yes** — *hifz al-nafs* applies.
- Does it involve halal certification or regulation? **Yes** — halal food, halal medicine.
- Does the bill affect non-Muslims? **Yes** — hospital staff and some patients may be non-Muslim. Include voluntary compliance for private facilities.

**Step 2: BOL basis**
- Art. IX, Sec. 22 — health provision mandate
- Art. X, Sec. 4 — Shari'ah legislative authority
- BAA 13 Sec. 3(j) — MOH's Islamic teaching mandate

**Step 3: PD 1083 check**
- Does PD 1083 cover hospital standards? **No.** PD 1083 covers personal status, not health regulation. No conflict possible.

**Step 4: Draft key provisions**
- **Sec. 1 (Declaration of Policy):** The Bangsamoro Government recognizes that access to health care is a fundamental right consistent with the preservation of life (*hifz al-nafs*), a core objective of Islamic law and the Bangsamoro Organic Law.
- **Sec. 5 (Baseline Standards):** All BARMM government hospitals shall provide: halal food service, prayer space with *qiblah* direction, modesty accommodation, and halal medicine screening.
- **Sec. 8 (Enhanced Accreditation):** Hospitals meeting additional standards — *wudu'* facilities, Islamic chaplaincy, end-of-life care protocol — may apply for ibadah-friendly accreditation.
- **Sec. 12 (BDI Consultation):** The MOH shall consult the Darul-Ifta' on bioethical questions arising from the implementation of this Act.

**Step 5: Shari'ah Compliance Flags**
- &#9745; Shari'ah dimension identified (health, *hifz al-nafs*)
- &#9745; BOL basis confirmed (Art. IX Sec. 22, Art. X Sec. 4)
- &#9745; PD 1083 conflict check: No conflict
- &#9745; BDI consultation clause included (Sec. 12)
- &#9745; Non-Muslim participation: Voluntary for private facilities

## 11.8 Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming all Art. IX powers need the same integration model.** Health needs dual-track (baseline + enhanced). Labor needs universal standard. Human rights needs principled integration. One size does not fit all.

- **Ignoring BAA 13 Sec. 3(j).** This is the strongest existing Shari'ah mandate for any ministry. A health bill that does not reference it misses the legal foundation already laid by Parliament.

- **Drafting *kafala* provisions that conflict with PD 1083 guardianship.** PD 1083 Art. 70-72 governs guardianship of Muslim minors. Any *kafala* legislation must supplement, not contradict, these provisions.

- **Presenting Shari'ah-compliant housing finance as the only option.** The dual-track model means beneficiaries choose. Mandating only Shari'ah-compliant finance would exclude non-Muslim residents and violate the BOL's equal protection guarantees.

- **Forgetting that BAA 82 is already enacted.** When citing Islamic workplace practices, cite BAA 82 Book III — not *fiqh* concepts. The *fiqh* concepts have been codified into positive law. Cite the law.

- **Treating human rights as a "Western" framework incompatible with Shari'ah.** The five *maqasid* predate and align with international human rights standards. BAA 62 proved that Shari'ah integration *strengthens* human rights legislation, not weakens it.

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting any work product involving Art. IX social justice powers:

- &#9744; Have you identified which *maqasid* objectives apply to your subject?
- &#9744; Have you checked BAA 13 Sec. 3(j) for the health Shari'ah mandate?
- &#9744; Have you verified whether PD 1083 covers any aspect of your subject?
- &#9744; Have you chosen the right integration model — universal, dual-track, or principled?
- &#9744; Have you included a BDI consultation clause for questions requiring *fatwa*?
- &#9744; If drafting housing finance provisions, have you offered a Shari'ah-compliant alternative?
- &#9744; If drafting child welfare provisions, have you checked PD 1083 Art. 70-72 on guardianship?
- &#9744; Have you cited BAA 82 Book III (not just *fiqh* concepts) when referencing Islamic workplace practices?
- &#9744; Have you followed the BAA 62 *maqasid* integration model for policy declarations?
