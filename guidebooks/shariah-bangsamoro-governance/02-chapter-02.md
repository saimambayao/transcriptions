# Chapter 2 — The Constitutional and Legal Framework

Every Shari'ah provision in BARMM exists within a legal architecture. The 1987 Constitution authorizes autonomous regions. The BOL creates BARMM within the framework of that Constitution. BOL Article X establishes the Shari'ah justice system within the framework of the BOL. And PD 1083 continues to govern Muslim personal law unless Parliament legislates on matters "not provided for."[^1]

You cannot work with Shari'ah in BARMM without understanding this architecture. This chapter walks you through it — from the Constitution down to the specific sections that define what Parliament can and cannot do.

---

## 2.1 The 1987 Constitution and Autonomous Regions

The constitutional basis for BARMM is Article X of the 1987 Constitution, titled "Local Government." Sections 15 through 21 specifically address autonomous regions.

Three provisions matter most for Shari'ah:

**Section 15** authorizes the creation of autonomous regions "in Muslim Mindanao and in the Cordilleras" through organic acts that define "the basic structure of government for the region consisting of the executive department and legislative assembly."[^2]

**Section 18** establishes the critical constraint: autonomous regions are created "within the framework of this Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines."[^3] This means BARMM legislation — including Shari'ah-based legislation — must comply with the Constitution.

**Section 20** provides that the organic act defines the legislative powers of the autonomous region "subject to the provisions of this Constitution and national laws."[^4]

These three sections establish what practitioners call **nested sovereignty**: BARMM governs itself, but within the Philippine constitutional framework. This is not a limitation to resent — it is the legal foundation that makes Shari'ah legislation possible within a unitary republic.

---

## 2.2 The BOL as Social Compact — Nested Sovereignty

Republic Act No. 11054, the Bangsamoro Organic Law, was signed on July 27, 2018 and ratified by plebiscite in January-February 2019.[^5] Its preamble frames the relationship:

> Imploring the aid of Almighty God, in recognition of the aspirations of the Bangsamoro people and other inhabitants in the autonomous region in Muslim Mindanao to establish an enduring peace on the basis of justice, balanced society and asserting their right to conserve and develop their patrimony, reflective of their system of life as prescribed by their faith, in harmony with their customary laws, cultures and traditions, **within the framework of the Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines**...[^6]

The bolded phrase appears twice in the BOL — once in the preamble and once in Art. I, Sec. 3 (Purpose). It is the constitutional anchor for everything that follows, including Shari'ah legislation.

The BOL has **18 Articles**. Article X ("Bangsamoro Justice System") is dedicated entirely to Shari'ah courts, traditional justice, and Parliament's power to legislate on Islamic law.[^7]

---

## 2.3 BOL Article X: The 20 Sections

Article X contains 20 sections. Every practitioner working with Shari'ah should know what each section covers. Here is the complete map:

| Section | Title | What It Covers |
|---------|-------|---------------|
| 1 | Justice System in the Bangsamoro | Scope: Shari'ah applies "exclusively to cases involving Muslims"; voluntary submission for non-Muslims |
| 2 | Shari'ah Courts | Courts are part of the Philippine judicial system under Supreme Court supervision |
| 3 | Sources of Shari'ah | Principal: Qur'an, Sunnah. Secondary: Ijma' (Consensus), Qiyas (Analogy) |
| 4 | Power of Parliament | Personal, family, property law; commercial/civil not in PD 1083; criminal (*ta'zir*/*arresto menor*) |
| 5 | Shari'ah Circuit Courts | Exclusive original jurisdiction — PD 1083 offenses, marriage/divorce/dower, *ta'zir*, civil (real ≤ P400K, other ≤ P200K) |
| 6 | Shari'ah District Courts | Custody/guardianship, estate settlement, Muslim Registry, contracts, civil (real > P400K, other > P200K), appellate over Circuit |
| 7 | Shari'ah High Court | Annulment of District judgments, auxiliary writs, appellate over District; decisions final except questions of law to SC |
| 8 | Qualifications of Judges | Requirements for Circuit, District, and High Court judges |
| 9 | Composition of High Court | Five Justices including the Presiding Justice |
| 10 | Compensation and Benefits | Same rank and privileges as Court of Appeals Justices |
| 11 | Shari'ah Rules of Court | Existing rules continue until SC promulgates new ones |
| 12 | Special Bar Examinations | SC administers Shari'ah bar exam; successful examinees become counselors-at-law |
| 13 | Practice of Law | Who may practice before Shari'ah courts |
| 14 | Court Personnel | SC appoints and disciplines Shari'ah court personnel |
| 15 | Integrated Bar | SC may integrate the Shari'ah Bar |
| 16 | Creation of New Offices | Parliament may create: Shari'ah public assistance office, prosecution service, academy, Office of the Jurisconsult |
| 17 | Traditional Justice | Parliament shall promote traditional/tribal justice for indigenous peoples |
| 18 | Office for Tribal Justice | Created to oversee study, preservation, and development of tribal justice |
| 19 | Regular Courts | Continue to function under national law |
| 20 | Alternative Dispute Resolution | Parliament shall legislate ADR mechanisms; Shari'ah courts may use ADR |

**Key observation:** Sections 1-16 deal with Shari'ah. Sections 17-18 deal with traditional/tribal justice. Sections 19-20 deal with regular courts and ADR. The three justice systems coexist within the same constitutional framework.[^8]

## 2.4 Sources of Shari'ah Recognized by the BOL

Section 3 is brief but foundational. It recognizes four sources of Shari'ah, divided into two tiers:

**Principal Sources:**
- **Al-Qur'an** — the holy book of Islam, containing divine revelation
- **Al-Sunnah** — the traditions of Prophet Muhammad (SAW), preserved in Hadith collections[^9]

**Secondary Sources:**
- **Al-Ijma'** — scholarly consensus on a point of law
- **Al-Qiyas** — analogical reasoning applied to new situations[^10]

For practitioners, this hierarchy matters. When you research a Shari'ah topic, you search the sources in this order. A Qur'anic verse carries higher authority than a Hadith narration. A well-established *ijma'* carries higher authority than a novel *qiyas*. And enacted BARMM law (which codifies these sources into legislation) takes precedence for legal purposes — because it is the BOL's own framework that authorizes the legislation.

---

## 2.5 The "Exclusively Muslim" Scope and Voluntary Submission

Section 1 establishes the most important boundary in BARMM Shari'ah law:

> *Shari'ah* shall apply exclusively to cases involving Muslims. Where a case involves a non-Muslim, *Shari'ah* law may apply only if the non-Muslim voluntarily submits to the jurisdiction of the *Shari'ah* court.[^11]

This is both a **scope rule** and a **constitutional safeguard**.

As a scope rule, it tells you: before doing any Shari'ah analysis, confirm that the parties are Muslim. If they are not, Shari'ah does not apply — unless the non-Muslim voluntarily submits.

As a constitutional safeguard, it preserves freedom of religion. The Constitution guarantees that no one shall be compelled to follow a religion they do not profess. The voluntary submission mechanism ensures that non-Muslims are never subjected to Shari'ah without consent.

**Practical implication:** If you are drafting a bill that applies Shari'ah obligations, check whether it could affect non-Muslims. If it could, the bill must include a voluntary submission provision — or risk unconstitutionality.

Section 1 also adds two important protections:

> The traditional or tribal laws shall be applicable to disputes of indigenous peoples within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.[^12]

> The provisions of this Article shall not prejudice the rights of non-Muslims and nonindigenous peoples.[^13]

## 2.6 Constitutional Constraints on Shari'ah Legislation

Shari'ah legislation operates within three constitutional constraints. Understanding them prevents drafting bills that will be struck down.

### Constraint 1: The Bill of Rights (Art. III)

All BARMM legislation must comply with the Bill of Rights. Key provisions that intersect with Shari'ah:

| Right | Implication for Shari'ah Legislation |
|-------|-------------------------------------|
| **Due process** (Sec. 1) | A BAA imposing *ta'zir* penalties must provide notice, hearing, and proportionality |
| **Equal protection** (Sec. 1) | Shari'ah provisions treating Muslims differently from non-Muslims are permissible because the BOL explicitly authorizes them and the "exclusively Muslim" boundary ensures non-Muslims are not subjected without consent |
| **Freedom of religion** (Sec. 5) | Non-Muslim submission to Shari'ah must be genuinely voluntary — not coerced, not presumed |
| **Right against unreasonable searches** (Sec. 2) | Shari'ah enforcement mechanisms (e.g., halal compliance inspection) must satisfy constitutional search and seizure standards |

### Constraint 2: Autonomous Region Limits (Constitution Art. X)

| Provision | What It Means |
|-----------|---------------|
| Sec. 18 | BARMM is created "within the framework of this Constitution" — not outside it |
| Sec. 20 | The organic act defines legislative powers "subject to the provisions of this Constitution and national laws" |

### Constraint 3: Supreme Court Supervision

Section 2 of BOL Article X provides:

> *Shari'ah* courts within the Bangsamoro territorial jurisdiction shall form part of the Philippine judicial system subject to the supervision of the Supreme Court.[^14]

This means the Supreme Court has final authority over questions of law arising from Shari'ah court decisions. The Shari'ah High Court's decisions are "final and executory except on questions of law which may be raised before the Supreme Court."[^15]

**Practical implication:** When drafting Shari'ah-related legislation, always verify that the bill does not diminish Supreme Court supervisory authority over Shari'ah courts. Any provision that attempts to make Shari'ah courts independent of the SC would be unconstitutional.

## 2.7 Worked Example

> **Scenario:** You are reviewing a draft bill titled "Bangsamoro Halal Regulatory Act." Section 12 of the draft provides that "All food establishments in BARMM shall obtain halal certification from the Bangsamoro Halal Board."
>
> **Constitutional analysis using this chapter:**
>
> 1. **Scope check (Sec. 2.5):** Does this bill apply Shari'ah to non-Muslims? Yes — "all food establishments" includes non-Muslim-owned businesses. This triggers the constitutional question.
>
> 2. **Bill of Rights check (Sec. 2.6, Constraint 1):** Requiring non-Muslim food establishments to obtain halal certification could violate freedom of religion (Art. III, Sec. 5) if it compels compliance with Islamic dietary standards without voluntary submission.
>
> 3. **Fix:** The bill should either (a) limit the mandatory certification requirement to establishments that choose to market products as halal, or (b) create a voluntary certification program for all establishments with mandatory certification only for those marketing to Muslim consumers.
>
> 4. **BOL basis:** Parliament has the power to legislate on this under Art. X, Sec. 4 (commercial matters) — the question is not whether Parliament *can* legislate, but whether this specific provision respects constitutional constraints.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming Shari'ah operates outside the Constitution.** It does not. BOL Article X exists within the constitutional framework — not parallel to it.
- **Forgetting the voluntary submission requirement.** Every bill that could affect non-Muslims must include a voluntary submission mechanism or limit its scope to Muslims only.
- **Conflating Parliament's power with unlimited authority.** Section 4 grants specific powers — personal, family, property, commercial (not in PD 1083), and *ta'zir*. Parliament cannot legislate beyond these categories under Article X.
- **Ignoring Supreme Court supervision.** Shari'ah courts are part of the Philippine judicial system. The SC has the final word on questions of law.
- **Citing "Art. X" without specifying the section.** Article X has 20 sections covering very different subjects. Always cite the specific section.
