# Chapter 1 — Why Shari'ah Matters in Bangsamoro Governance

The Bangsamoro Organic Law does something no previous Philippine law has done at this scale: it recognizes Shari'ah as part of the formal justice system of an autonomous region, defines its sources, creates a three-tiered Shari'ah court structure, and grants Parliament the power to legislate on Islamic law matters.[^1]

This is not symbolic. It is an operational mandate. Every bill that touches personal law, family relations, property among Muslims, Islamic finance, halal certification, or criminal offenses under *ta'zir* has a Shari'ah dimension that practitioners must identify, research, and address.

Yet most governance practitioners in BARMM — legislative staff, legal officers, planners, cooperative organizers — have not been trained to work with Shari'ah as a legal system. They can navigate the Civil Code, the Revised Penal Code, and the Local Government Code. They cannot navigate PD 1083 or BOL Article X with the same confidence.

This guidebook exists to close that gap.

---

## 1.1 What This Guidebook Is — and What It Is Not

This guidebook is a **practitioner's manual** for working with Shari'ah in BARMM governance. It teaches you three things:

1. **How to research** — find relevant Qur'anic verses, Hadith narrations, enacted law, and *fiqh* positions on any Shari'ah topic
2. **How to legislate** — determine whether Parliament should enact standalone Shari'ah BAAs, mainstream Shari'ah into existing legislation, or pursue a hybrid approach
3. **How to apply** — integrate Islamic finance principles into cooperatives, navigate halal economy regulation, and use Maqasid al-Shari'ah as a policy framework

It is **not** a *fiqh* treatise. It does not teach Islamic theology, jurisprudential theory, or the internal debates of the classical schools. Those belong to the *'ulama* and the scholarly tradition. What it teaches is how to **use** the outputs of that tradition — enacted law, scriptural sources, and established scholarly positions — in governance work.

## 1.2 The Bangsamoro Identity and Islamic Governance

The Bangsamoro people's relationship with Islamic governance predates the Philippine state by centuries. The Sultanate of Sulu was established in 1457 — the oldest Islamic institution in the Philippines. The Sultanate of Maguindanao followed, controlling much of mainland Mindanao. Both administered justice according to Shari'ah and customary law long before Spanish colonization.[^2]

The BOL recognizes this heritage. Its preamble affirms the Bangsamoro people's "right to conserve and develop their patrimony, reflective of their system of life as prescribed by their faith" and their right to "genuine and meaningful self-governance."[^3] Article II defines the Bangsamoro people as those who "at the advent of the Spanish colonization, were considered natives or original inhabitants of Mindanao and the Sulu archipelago and its adjacent islands."[^3b]

This identity shapes governance. The 12 Pillars of Moral Governance adopted by the BTA government include "Unwavering Loyalty to God" and "Justice" — principles rooted in Islamic values.[^4] The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda includes "Mas Matatag na Paniniwala" (Stronger Faith) as one of its five pillars.

Shari'ah is not an add-on to Bangsamoro governance. It is foundational.

## 1.3 Who This Guidebook Is For

**Legislative staff** — You draft bills, prepare committee reports, and analyze proposed legislation. When a bill touches marriage, inheritance, zakat, halal, or any subject under BOL Article X, you need to know what PD 1083 already covers, what Parliament can legislate on, and how to cite scriptural sources in footnotes.

**MOA legal officers** — You review contracts, draft memoranda, and advise your Minister on legal questions. When a contract involves Shari'ah-compliant financing or a program serves Muslim communities, you need to identify the Shari'ah dimensions and know when to recommend BDI consultation.

**Development practitioners** — You design programs, manage cooperatives, and write project proposals. When your cooperative needs Shari'ah-compliant bylaws or your project operates in a community that expects Islamic governance principles, you need the legal framework and practical tools.

**Policy analysts** — You produce briefers, policy notes, and recommendations. When your analysis involves health, education, social welfare, or any sector that intersects with Muslim personal law or Islamic institutions, you need the Shari'ah checkpoint.

**Governance consultants** — You advise BARMM entities on institutional development. When your client is the BDI, a Shari'ah court, or any agency with Islamic mandate, you need the full picture.

## 1.4 The Institutional Boundary: Legal Analysis vs. Binding Religious Rulings

This guidebook teaches you to produce **Shari'ah legal analysis** — research, opinions, compliance reviews, and legislative recommendations. This is the same kind of analytical work you do for secular law. You read the sources, identify the relevant provisions, analyze how they apply, and present your findings.

What you produce is **legal analysis**. It is not a *fatwa*.

A *fatwa* is a binding religious ruling issued by a qualified Islamic legal scholar (*mufti*) in response to a specific question. The Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta (BDI), established under BAA 13, Secs. 52-56, is the institutional authority for issuing *fatawa* in BARMM.[^5] The BDI is headed by the Bangsamoro Mufti, assisted by Islamic jurists and a Secretariat headed by an Executive Director.

The distinction is the same one that applies to all legal work: a legal researcher can analyze constitutional provisions without being a Supreme Court justice. They read, analyze, and advise — but the authoritative ruling comes from the court.

**When to refer to the BDI:**

- The legal question turns on a disputed point of *fiqh* with no enacted law resolution
- The matter involves novel circumstances (*nawazil*) not addressed by classical scholarship
- The output will create binding policy affecting Muslim religious practice
- Multiple *madhhab* positions lead to materially different legal outcomes

**When you can proceed with your own analysis:**

- The question is answered by enacted law (BOL, PD 1083, BAAs)
- The *fiqh* position is well-established and uncontested
- You are identifying Shari'ah dimensions, not issuing rulings
- You are presenting evidence for decision-makers, not making the decision

**Read Part I first.** Chapters 1-5 give you the constitutional framework, the court system, and the Maqasid policy lens. Everything else builds on these foundations.

**Use Part II when you need to research.** Chapter 6 teaches you how to build your own searchable Islamic source library and find relevant verses and narrations. Chapter 7 teaches you how to turn that research into a legal opinion using IRAC methodology.

**Use Part III when you need to legislate.** Chapter 8 addresses the strategic question of how Parliament should legislate Shari'ah. Chapter 9 gives you the practical checkpoint for bill drafting. Chapter 10 maps the current legislative landscape — what exists, what's pending, and what's missing.

**Use Part IV when you need to apply.** Chapters 11-16 cover how Shari'ah should be legislated and implemented across all BOL powers — from social services and health (Chapter 13) to environment and natural resources (Chapter 15) to governance and administration (Chapter 16). Chapter 17 provides a complete sector directory mapping all 55 powers.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Treating Shari'ah as optional.** If a bill affects Muslim personal law, property, or family relations, the Shari'ah dimension is not optional — it is legally mandated by BOL Article X.
- **Confusing PD 1083 scope with Parliament's power.** PD 1083 covers personal and family law. Parliament can legislate on matters "not provided for" under PD 1083 — but if PD 1083 already covers it, PD 1083 governs.[^6]
- **Citing *fiqh* as enacted law.** Arabic legal terms (mudarabah, waqf, sulh) are *fiqh* concepts from Islamic jurisprudence. The BOL authorizes Parliament to legislate on them, but most have not been codified by any BAA. Do not present them as enacted BARMM law.
- **Using "Shari'ah Appellate Court."** The correct name is **Shari'ah High Court** (BOL Art. X, Sec. 7). This is a common error to watch for — always use the BOL's terminology.
- **Ignoring the "exclusively Muslim" boundary.** Shari'ah applies only to cases involving Muslims. Non-Muslims must voluntarily submit to Shari'ah court jurisdiction.[^7] This is a hard constitutional limit.

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting any work product with a Shari'ah dimension:

- [ ] Have you identified whether the subject falls under BOL Article X?
- [ ] Have you checked whether PD 1083 already covers the subject?
- [ ] Have you verified all BAA numbers against the enacted BAA index?
- [ ] Have you cited scriptural sources from authoritative texts with exact references?
- [ ] Have you distinguished between enacted law and *fiqh* concepts not yet codified?
- [ ] Have you noted which *madhhab* position you are presenting?
- [ ] Have you flagged any matter that requires BDI consultation?
- [ ] Have you used "Shari'ah High Court" — not "Shari'ah Appellate Court"?
