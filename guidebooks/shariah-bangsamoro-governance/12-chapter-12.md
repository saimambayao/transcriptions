# Chapter 12 — Shari'ah Courts, Worship, and Identity

BOL Article X exists because the Bangsamoro justice system is not just a court system — it is a constitutional commitment to administer justice "in consonance with Shari'ah."[^1] No other article in the BOL carries this weight. Art. X creates the Shari'ah courts, defines Parliament's power to enact Islamic law, recognizes traditional justice, and authorizes alternative dispute resolution — all within the framework of the Philippine Constitution and the Supreme Court's supervisory authority.

This chapter covers the powers that are *directly and inherently Islamic* — the ones where Shari'ah is not an optional overlay but the defining feature. If Chapter 11 showed you how to mainstream Shari'ah into social services, this chapter shows you the powers where Shari'ah *is* the service.

---

## 12.1 Art. X: The Shari'ah-Dedicated Article

Article X has 20 sections. Every practitioner working with Shari'ah in BARMM should know what each section covers. Chapter 2 mapped the full article; Chapter 4 detailed the court system. This chapter focuses on what remains to be *legislated* — the unfulfilled mandates.

**Five major legislative gaps in Art. X:**

1. **Traditional Justice Act** — Art. X, Sec. 17 directs Parliament to enact laws promoting traditional/tribal justice for indigenous peoples. No legislation exists.[^2]
2. **ADR Legislation** — Art. X, Sec. 20 authorizes Parliament to legislate conciliation and mediation mechanisms for Shari'ah courts. No legislation exists.[^3]
3. **Shari'ah Prosecution Service** — Art. X, Sec. 16 authorizes creation of a public assistance office, prosecution service, and academy. None have been created.[^4]
4. **Shari'ah Academy** — Same section. No academy exists for training Shari'ah court personnel and practitioners.[^5]
5. **Shari'ah Commercial and Civil Code** — Art. X, Sec. 4 gives Parliament power to legislate on commercial matters, civil actions not covered by PD 1083, and *ta'zir* offenses. No comprehensive legislation has been enacted under this power.[^6]

These are not hypothetical — they are constitutional mandates waiting for legislation. Bills have been filed for some (PB 170, 173, 174 for barangay justice; PB 304 for Shari'ah scholarship; PB 363 for a Shari'ah academy), but none have been enacted.

### How to legislate Art. X mandates

**Model: Mandatory Shari'ah compliance.** These are inherently Islamic functions. A Shari'ah prosecution service that does not comply with Shari'ah procedural standards is a contradiction. A traditional justice framework that contradicts established *fiqh* on the matters it adjudicates fails its constitutional purpose.

For each unfulfilled mandate:
- Cite the specific Art. X section as the constitutional basis
- Include a Darul-Ifta' consultation clause for interpretive questions
- Ensure Supreme Court supervisory authority is preserved (Art. X, Sec. 2)
- Define the relationship with PD 1083 (supplement, not conflict)

## 12.2 Hajj and Umrah: Legislating a Religious Obligation

**BOL basis:** Art. V, Sec. 2(aa) (enumerated power); Art. VII, Sec. 42 (BPA creation).[^7]

Hajj is the only enumerated power in Art. V, Sec. 2 that is *directly and exclusively a religious obligation*. It is the Fifth Pillar of Islam — obligatory once in a lifetime for every Muslim who meets the condition of *istita'ah* (financial and physical capacity).[^8]

This makes Hajj fundamentally different from every other BOL power. Health, education, labor — these are government services that have Shari'ah dimensions. Hajj *is* Shari'ah. The government does not create the obligation; it facilitates its fulfillment.

### What exists

- **BAA 13, Secs. 43-45** — establishes the Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority (BPA) with mandate to coordinate with NCMF and manage Saudi engagement (with Chief Minister approval)[^9]
- **BAA 85, Ch. XXV** — allocates P200M for the Hajj Financing Program[^10]
- **PB 282** — proposes 30-day paid Hajj leave (pending)

### What is missing

- **No comprehensive Pilgrimage Act** — the BPA operates under BAA 13 mandate only; no standalone law defines eligibility criteria, pilgrim rights, or operator accreditation
- **No Shari'ah-compliance framework for Hajj financing** — the P200M program operates without statutory requirements that financing must be *riba*-free
- **No Pilgrim Bill of Rights** — consumer protection for pilgrims (refund rules, service standards, complaint mechanisms)
- **No *awqaf* management act** — endowment properties that could fund pilgrimage assistance lack a legislative framework

### How to legislate

**Model: Mandatory Shari'ah compliance** — because Hajj is a purely Islamic obligation.

A Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Act should include:

1. **Shari'ah-compliant financing** — All government Hajj financing must use *qard hasan* (interest-free loan) or outright grants. Interest-bearing loans for Hajj violate the purpose of the pilgrimage.[^11]
2. **Sheikh accreditation** — Travel companions and religious guides must be certified by the Darul-Ifta' as qualified to lead rituals
3. **BPA regulation review** — All BPA administrative orders subject to Shari'ah review by the Darul-Ifta' before promulgation
4. **Pilgrim protection** — Service standards, complaint mechanisms, refund rules for cancelled or substandard pilgrimages
5. ***Awqaf* integration** — Endowment income dedicated to subsidizing pilgrimage for those who meet *istita'ah* financially but need partial assistance

**BDI referral triggers:** Disputed *istita'ah* criteria (does disability waive the physical requirement?), group pilgrimage financing structures, *awqaf* investment policy for pilgrimage funds.

## 12.3 Culture, Language, and Islamic Heritage

**BOL basis:** Art. IX, Secs. 24-26 (BCPCH mandate); Art. V, Sec. 2(p) (culture and language power).[^12]

Bangsamoro cultural heritage is inseparable from Islamic heritage. The Sultanates of Sulu and Maguindanao were Islamic polities. The *okir* decorative tradition has Islamic geometric roots. Arabic-script manuscripts (*kirim*) document centuries of Islamic scholarship. Mosque architecture defines the built heritage of the region.

### The Shari'ah dimension

Three *maqasid* apply:
- ***Hifz al-din*** (preservation of faith) — mosques, religious manuscripts, Sultanate records that document Islamic governance are religious heritage, not just cultural artifacts
- ***Hifz al-nasl*** (preservation of lineage) — Sultanate genealogies (*tarsila*) and ethno-linguistic identity preservation connect to the Islamic concept of preserving lineage
- **Arabic as liturgical language** — language preservation supports Qur'anic literacy and religious practice

### What exists

- **BAA 20** — dual Hijri-Gregorian calendar (a symbolic but significant Shari'ah-informed cultural law)[^13]
- **BAA 39** — Bangsamoro regional holidays (reflects Islamic cultural dimension)[^14]
- **BAA 60 and BAA 61** — site-specific heritage declarations that include the Darul-Ifta' as a member of heritage management boards (a precedent for Shari'ah institutional involvement in cultural governance)[^15]
- **BAA 13, Secs. 46-51** — BCPCH mandate and functions[^16]

### What is missing

- **No comprehensive cultural heritage code** — PB 204 is pending
- **No language preservation act** — Arabic and regional languages lack legislative protection
- **No *waqf*-based cultural endowment** — the traditional Islamic mechanism for funding cultural institutions
- **Art. IX, Sec. 26 site transfer** — the mechanism for transferring national cultural sites to Bangsamoro control is uncodified

### How to legislate

**Model: Principled integration** — Islamic heritage preservation guided by Shari'ah values without mandating specific Shari'ah compliance for all cultural activities.

A Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code should:
1. Create heritage categories that distinguish Islamic heritage (mosques, *kirim* manuscripts, Sultanate sites) from indigenous and colonial-era heritage — each with appropriate conservation standards
2. Establish a *waqf*-based cultural endowment fund as an optional financing mechanism alongside government appropriations
3. Include Darul-Ifta' consultation for sacred sites and Islamic heritage artifacts (following the BAA 60/61 precedent)
4. Require culturally appropriate conservation standards — for example, *halal* considerations in heritage site food services, gender-appropriate access to sacred spaces

## 12.4 Customary Laws: The Shari'ah-Adat Interface

**BOL basis:** Art. X, Sec. 1 (justice in consonance with Shari'ah, traditional/tribal laws); Art. X, Secs. 17-18 (traditional justice); Art. IX, Sec. 4 (customary law adoption).[^17]

This is the most complex normative intersection in BARMM. Three legal systems coexist: statutory law (BAAs, national law), Shari'ah (PD 1083, Art. X), and customary law (*adat*). The BOL recognizes all three — but does not provide a comprehensive framework for when they conflict.

### The Shari'ah-adat relationship

Islamic jurisprudence recognizes custom (*'urf*) as a subsidiary source of law under the maxim *"al-'adah muhakkamah"* (custom is authoritative). But this recognition is conditional — custom is valid **only if it does not contradict established Shari'ah**.[^18]

**PD 1083, Art. 3** sets the precedent: "customary law shall supplement the provisions of this Code." This means customary law fills gaps in PD 1083 but cannot override it.[^19]

### The critical question

What happens when *adat* and Shari'ah diverge? Three scenarios:

| Scenario | Example | Resolution |
|----------|---------|-----------|
| *Adat* consistent with Shari'ah | Elder-mediated dispute settlement (*sulh*) | *Adat* has full force — recognized as valid *'urf* |
| *Adat* neutral (Shari'ah silent) | Land use customs, fishing rights traditions | *Adat* applies — Shari'ah does not prohibit what it does not address |
| *Adat* contradicts Shari'ah | Customary inheritance rules that override Qur'anic shares | Shari'ah prevails for Muslim parties; Darul-Ifta' or Shari'ah court resolves |

### What is missing

- **No comprehensive customary law codification** — Bangsamoro Muslim *adat* has no dedicated legislative framework
- **Art. X, Secs. 17-18 mandate unfulfilled** — no traditional justice system legislation enacted
- **No systematic documentation** of unwritten customs
- **No reconciliation mechanism** for when *adat* and Shari'ah diverge

### How to legislate

**Two-track approach:**

1. **Bangsamoro Muslim *adat*** — customs undergo Shari'ah compatibility review. Compatible customs receive formal recognition. Incompatible customs are either reformed or given limited application (e.g., only when all parties consent and no Shari'ah right is prejudiced).
2. **Non-Moro IP customary law** — governed by IPRA (RA 8371) standards, not Shari'ah. The BOL's non-diminution clause (Art. IX, Sec. 3) protects IP rights. Shari'ah compliance is **not applicable** to non-Muslim IP communities.[^20]

A Traditional Justice Act should:
- Codify the *'urf* recognition principle (valid if not contradicting Shari'ah)
- Establish a reconciliation panel for Shari'ah-*adat* conflicts (composed of *ulama* and *adat* leaders)
- Document customs through a systematic program (oral history collection, community validation)
- Integrate with the barangay justice system (PB 170, 173, 174 address this)

## 12.5 Indigenous Peoples' Rights and Shari'ah

**BOL basis:** Art. V, Sec. 2(ff); Art. X, Sec. 17-18 (traditional justice for IPs); BAA 64 (MIPA).[^21]

Most indigenous cultural communities (ICCs/IPs) in BARMM are non-Muslim — Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, B'laan, Higaonon. Shari'ah does not directly govern their matters. The BOL treats IP justice as a **separate track** from Shari'ah justice.[^22]

### The Shari'ah dimension

**Indirect but important.** In a majority-Muslim autonomous region, non-Muslim IPs need assurance that their rights are protected. Islamic principles provide that assurance:

- ***'Adl*** (justice) — extends to all regardless of religion. Protecting non-Muslim IP rights in a Muslim-majority region is an exercise of *'adl*.[^23]
- ***Amanah*** (trust) — IP communal land stewardship mirrors the Islamic *khalifah* concept
- ***Maslahah*** (public interest) — IP welfare serves the common good of the entire region

### What exists

- **BAA 64** — creates MIPA with FIDO, IPs Council, and Regional Adjudicators Bureau. Does **not** mandate Shari'ah compliance for any IP institution or process.[^24]
- **RA 8371 (IPRA)** — provides the constitutional floor per Art. IX, Sec. 3 non-diminution clause

### What is needed

- NCIP-MIPA jurisdictional boundary definition
- Tribal University Charter (Art. IX, Sec. 19 unfulfilled — PB 274 pending)
- Revenue-sharing mechanisms (BAA 64, Sec. 77 IRR) operationalization

### How to legislate

**Model: Principled** — legislation guided by Islamic values (*'adl*, *maslahah*, *amanah*) without requiring IPs to comply with Islamic law. Where Moro and IP communities share territory, mediation of overlapping land claims should draw on both customary law and Shari'ah property principles — but the IP community is never subject to Shari'ah jurisdiction unless they voluntarily submit (Art. X, Sec. 1).

## 12.6 Worked Example: Drafting a Cultural Heritage Preservation BAA

**Scenario:** The Committee on Culture and Heritage asks you to prepare a committee report on PB 204 (Bangsamoro Cultural Heritage Code). You need to assess the bill's Shari'ah dimension.

**Step 1: Shari'ah Checkpoint (Chapter 9)**
- Does the subject involve Islamic heritage? **Yes** — mosques, *kirim* manuscripts, Sultanate sites
- Does the subject involve the BDI or religious authorities? **Yes** — Darul-Ifta' should review sacred site designations (BAA 60/61 precedent)
- Does the bill affect non-Muslims? **Yes** — heritage code covers all Bangsamoro heritage, including non-Moro IP sites

**Step 2: BOL basis**
- Art. IX, Secs. 24-26 — BCPCH mandate
- Art. X, Sec. 4 — Shari'ah legislative authority (property, including *waqf* properties)

**Step 3: PD 1083 check**
- Does PD 1083 cover cultural heritage? **No.** PD 1083 covers personal status, not heritage preservation. *Waqf* intersects at Art. 110 et seq. (personal property only — not institutional).

**Step 4: Shari'ah provisions to recommend**
- **Heritage classification** — add "Islamic heritage" as a distinct category alongside "indigenous heritage" and "colonial-era heritage"
- **Sacred site protections** — mosques and religious schools should have enhanced conservation standards, including Darul-Ifta' consultation on any modification
- ***Waqf* endowment fund** — optional mechanism for cultural funding (perpetuity principle)
- **Curatorial ethics** — Islamic artifacts require culturally appropriate handling (e.g., Qur'anic manuscripts should not be displayed with non-Islamic religious texts in ways that could be seen as disrespectful)

**Step 5: Shari'ah Compliance Flags**
- &#9745; Shari'ah dimension identified (Islamic heritage, *waqf*)
- &#9745; BOL basis confirmed (Art. IX Secs. 24-26, Art. X Sec. 4)
- &#9745; PD 1083 conflict check: No conflict
- &#9745; BDI consultation: Recommended for sacred sites (BAA 60/61 precedent)
- &#9745; Non-Muslim inclusion: IP heritage preserved separately, no Shari'ah requirements for IP sites

## 12.7 Common Pitfalls

- **Treating Art. X unfulfilled mandates as optional.** They are constitutional requirements. A Traditional Justice Act, an ADR framework, a prosecution service — these are not policy choices. The BOL requires them.

- **Confusing Hajj administration with Hajj itself.** The government does not perform or validate the pilgrimage. It facilitates travel, protects pilgrims, and ensures Shari'ah-compliant financing. The religious obligation belongs to the individual, not the state.

- **Applying Shari'ah to non-Moro IP customary law.** The BOL creates separate tracks. Art. X, Secs. 17-18 address traditional justice for IPs under their own customary systems — not under Shari'ah. BAA 64 confirms this separation.

- **Ignoring the *'urf* compatibility test for Bangsamoro *adat*.** Not all customs are automatically valid under Shari'ah. The maxim *"al-'adah muhakkamah"* (custom is authoritative) is conditional — only customs that do not contradict established Shari'ah receive recognition.

- **Presenting *fiqh* principles as enacted law.** *Hifz al-din*, *ta'aruf*, and *ummah* are Islamic jurisprudential concepts. They inform legislative purpose but are not codified in any BARMM law. Always distinguish between *fiqh* concepts and enacted law.

- **Forgetting the Supreme Court's supervisory authority.** Art. X, Sec. 2 places Shari'ah courts within the Philippine judicial system under SC supervision. No legislation can diminish this authority.

## Quality Checklist

Before submitting any work product involving Art. X powers:

- &#9744; Have you identified the specific Art. X section that authorizes the legislation?
- &#9744; Have you confirmed Supreme Court supervisory authority is preserved?
- &#9744; If legislating Hajj matters, is all financing *riba*-free (*qard hasan* or grant)?
- &#9744; If codifying customary laws, have you applied the *'urf* compatibility test?
- &#9744; If affecting IPs, have you confirmed that Shari'ah compliance is NOT required for non-Muslim communities?
- &#9744; Have you distinguished between *fiqh* concepts and enacted BARMM law?
- &#9744; Have you included a Darul-Ifta' consultation clause for interpretive questions?
- &#9744; Have you checked whether PD 1083 already covers the subject?
- &#9744; Have you used "Shari'ah High Court" — not "Shari'ah Appellate Court"?
