# Chapter 12 — Halal Economy and Regulatory Framework

The halal economy is where Shari'ah meets market development. BARMM has a constitutional mandate, an institutional framework, and a growing market — but no comprehensive halal regulatory act. This chapter maps what exists, what is missing, and where the opportunities are.

---

## 12.1 Halal Certification in BARMM

"Halal" means permissible under Islamic law. In the context of products and services, halal certification verifies that an item complies with Islamic dietary and ethical standards — from ingredients and processing to handling and packaging.

The institutional framework already exists. BAA 13 (Bangsamoro Administrative Code) establishes the **Bangsamoro Halal Board** under the Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT).[^1] The Board's mandate includes:

- Granting or denying halal accreditation certificates
- Formulating halal development and promotion programs
- Coordinating with government regulatory agencies on halal standard-setting
- Investigating complaints and disputes on halal standards enforcement[^2]

The Board is chaired by the Minister of MTIT and includes the Ministers of MAFAR (Vice Chair), MOST, MOH, MOLE, and MOTC, the BDI Mufti, a representative from Mindanao State University, and two Bangsamoro professionals with halal industry experience.[^3]

**What exists:** The institutional framework — a policy-making board with defined membership and mandate.

**What is missing:** A comprehensive Bangsamoro Halal Act that establishes detailed standards, certification procedures, enforcement mechanisms, penalties, and a regulatory regime. The Halal Board provisions in BAA 13 are foundational but not comprehensive.

---

## 12.2 The Mas Matatag Agenda and Halal Economy

The Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda (2026-2028) includes **Mas Matatag na Kabuhayan** (Stronger Livelihood) as one of its five pillars. Halal industry development falls squarely within this pillar — it creates economic opportunities in BARMM's food processing, agriculture, and trade sectors.

The 2nd BDP 2023-2028 also supports halal economy development under Goal 2 (Equitable, competitive, and robust economy). BDP Chapter 7 covers economy including agriculture, fisheries, trade, and industry — all sectors with halal potential.[^4]

For development practitioners, the connection is direct: halal economy programs serve both Shari'ah principles (*hifz al-din* — protection of faith through compliant food; *hifz al-mal* — protection of property through economic opportunity) and the secular development agenda (BDP Goal 2, Mas Matatag na Kabuhayan).

## 12.3 BOL Provisions for Halal Regulation

The BOL does not mention "halal" explicitly. But the legislative authority exists through two provisions:

**Art. X, Sec. 4** — Parliament's power to legislate on commercial matters not provided for under PD 1083. Halal certification and regulation are commercial matters — PD 1083 does not cover them.[^5]

**Art. XIII (Regional Economy and Patrimony)** — The BOL grants BARMM authority over economic development, trade, and industry within its territory. Halal regulation is an exercise of this economic authority.

**BAA 13's halal provisions** demonstrate that Parliament has already exercised this authority at the institutional level. A comprehensive Halal Act would extend it to the regulatory level.

---

## 12.4 National Halal Framework and BARMM's Role

The Philippines has a national halal framework. Key national legislation:

- **RA 10817 (Philippine Halal Export Development and Promotion Act of 2016)** — establishes the Philippine Halal Export Development and Promotion Board, sets national halal standards, and requires accreditation of halal certifying bodies
- **Department of Trade and Industry** administrative orders on halal product certification

**BARMM's unique position:** BARMM is the only region in the Philippines with a significant Muslim-majority population AND its own halal regulatory body (the Bangsamoro Halal Board under BAA 13). This creates both an opportunity and a coordination challenge:

- **Opportunity:** BARMM can develop the most advanced halal regulatory framework in the Philippines, driven by local expertise and market demand
- **Challenge:** The Bangsamoro Halal Board must coordinate with the national Philippine Halal Board to avoid conflicting standards and ensure national and international market access

## 12.5 Opportunities for Development Practitioners

**For cooperative organizers:** Halal certification opens market access for BARMM agricultural cooperatives. Products with halal certification can access both domestic Muslim consumers and the global halal market (estimated at over $2 trillion globally).

**For program managers:** Halal industry development programs — processing facilities, certification training, market linkages — are eligible for BARMM development funding under the BDP and Mas Matatag frameworks.

**For policy analysts:** The legislative gap (no comprehensive Halal Act) is an opportunity. A well-drafted Bangsamoro Halal Act could:
- Establish detailed halal standards for food, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, and services
- Create a halal certification and accreditation system
- Define enforcement mechanisms and penalties for false halal claims
- Coordinate BARMM's halal framework with the national framework under RA 10817
- Position BARMM as the halal hub of the Philippines

**For legislative staff:** Use the Shari'ah checkpoint (Chapter 9) when reviewing any trade, agriculture, food safety, or tourism bill — these sectors intersect with halal regulation.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming the Halal Board is a regulatory body.** BAA 13 creates a policy-making board, not a full regulatory agency. A comprehensive Halal Act would need to establish the regulatory powers, procedures, and enforcement mechanisms.
- **Conflating BARMM halal standards with national standards.** The Bangsamoro Halal Board and the Philippine Halal Board are separate bodies. Their standards must be coordinated to avoid contradictions.
- **Ignoring non-food halal.** Halal extends beyond food to pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, personal care products, tourism services, and logistics. A comprehensive framework should cover all sectors.
- **Presenting halal certification as a Shari'ah obligation for non-Muslims.** Halal certification is a market standard, not a religious obligation. Non-Muslim food establishments are not required to obtain halal certification unless they choose to market their products as halal. See Chapter 2, Sec. 2.5 (voluntary submission) and Chapter 9's worked example.
