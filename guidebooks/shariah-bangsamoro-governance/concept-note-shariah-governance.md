# Concept Note

## Shari'ah and Bangsamoro Governance Guidebook

## I. Background

Republic Act No. 11054 (the Bangsamoro Organic Law) grants the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region a direct mandate to legislate and apply Shari'ah for Muslims within its jurisdiction. Article X dedicates twenty sections to the Shari'ah justice system — covering the three-tier Shari'ah Court structure, sources of Shari'ah, and jurisdictional authority over personal, family, property, and commercial law. Beyond Article X, Shari'ah-related provisions appear throughout the BOL: Hajj administration and Islamic banking under Article V, halal regulation under Article XIII, and the Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority under Article VII. Article V, Section 2 assigns the Bangsamoro Government fifty-five powers spanning health, education, finance, environment, labor, infrastructure, and trade — each of which has Shari'ah dimensions that the BOL does not elaborate.

No unified reference exists to guide legislators, policy-makers, or ministry and agency personnel in applying Shari'ah across these powers. Parliament drafts Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts without a standard Shari'ah review process. Ministries design programs, issue administrative orders, and allocate budgets without a framework for integrating Shari'ah considerations into their sector-specific work. Practitioners rely on fragments: isolated provisions of PD 1083, individual fatawa from the BDI, scattered judicial decisions from the Shari'ah Courts, and personal study of Islamic sources. The result is inconsistency across both legislation and executive policy.

This concept note proposes the formal development, expert validation, and institutional publication of a guidebook that addresses this gap.

## II. The Guidebook

The proposed guidebook, titled **"Shari'ah and Bangsamoro Governance: How to Research, Legislate, and Apply Shari'ah in BARMM,"** is structured across 16 chapters organized in five parts, with a glossary, bibliography, and appendices. A preliminary working manuscript has been developed using responsible and regulated AI tools to demonstrate the methodology, structure, and scope of the guidebook. It is not a theological treatise. It is designed as a practitioner's manual — but one that requires expert validation, institutional review, and collaborative refinement before publication.

**Part I (Foundations)** establishes the legal architecture. Chapter 1 defines the line between Shari'ah legal analysis — which any competent legal researcher can perform — and the issuance of binding religious rulings (fatawa), which belongs to the BDI. Chapters 2 through 4 cover the 1987 Constitution's provisions on autonomous regions, the BOL's twenty sections on Shari'ah, PD 1083's five books, and the three-tier Shari'ah Court system. Chapter 5 introduces the Maqasid al-Shari'ah as a practical policy analysis framework, showing how it aligns with the Bangsamoro Development Plan and the Mas Matatag na Bangsamoro Agenda.

**Part II (Researching Shari'ah)** provides the research methodology. Chapter 6 teaches practitioners how to locate, cite, and cross-verify sources from the Qur'an and the Kutub al-Sittah (the six canonical Hadith collections). Chapter 7 covers the production of Shari'ah legal opinions using the IRAC methodology adapted for Islamic sources, including when to refer a question to the BDI.

**Part III (Legislating and Policy-Making)** addresses both legislative strategy and executive policy. Chapter 8 presents three approaches — standalone Shari'ah BAAs, mainstreaming Shari'ah into every BAA, or a phased hybrid — and recommends the hybrid model based on BARMM's current legislative capacity. Chapter 9 introduces a Shari'ah Checkpoint for bill drafting. Chapter 10 surveys the current legislative landscape, identifying enacted BAAs with embedded Shari'ah provisions and the gaps where Parliament has not yet acted.

**Part IV (Applying Shari'ah Across All BOL Powers)** moves from legislation to policy and program design. Chapters 11 through 14 show how Shari'ah applies to the day-to-day work of ministries and agencies across four domains: social justice and human development (Art. IX), Shari'ah courts, Hajj administration, and cultural identity (Art. X), economy, finance, and natural resources (Art. XII-XIII), and governance, infrastructure, and administration (Art. V). Chapter 15 provides a complete directory mapping all 55 BOL powers against the Maqasid framework, with a priority matrix for both legislative agenda-setting and executive program planning.

**Part V (Practical Tools)** equips practitioners with ready-to-use instruments: a Shari'ah Checkpoint Checklist, a Jurisdictional Determination Worksheet, a Shari'ah Compliance Flags Template, citation format references for the Qur'an and Hadith, and guidance on when a particular question requires a fatwa from the BDI rather than a legal opinion from a researcher.

## III. Three Problems This Guidebook Solves

**There is no Shari'ah manual for Bangsamoro practitioners.** Bill drafters in Parliament, legal researchers in MP offices, and policy analysts in the ministries have no common reference document for applying Shari'ah to their work. When a legislator wants to know whether a proposed BAA on cooperatives should include Islamic finance provisions, or when a ministry director needs to align a health program with Maqasid al-Shari'ah, there is no standardized source to consult. Each practitioner improvises — relying on personal study, informal consultations, or incomplete readings of PD 1083. The guidebook replaces this with a shared, documented methodology that serves both legislative and executive functions.

**The line between legal analysis and the issuance of religious rulings is unclear.** BAA No. 13 established the BDI as the consultative body for policy-making through Islamic jurisprudence. But when a bill drafter encounters a question about whether a provision aligns with Shari'ah, there is no standard protocol for determining whether the question can be resolved through legal research or whether it requires a fatwa from the BDI. The guidebook draws this line explicitly: Chapters 1 and 7 define where legal analysis ends and where referral to the BDI begins.

**Shari'ah integration across sectors is ad hoc.** Some BAAs incorporate Shari'ah provisions deliberately — BAA 82 on labor, for instance, mainstreams Shari'ah into its framework. Others miss the Shari'ah dimension entirely, despite governing sectors where Shari'ah has direct application. On the executive side, ministries design programs and issue administrative orders without a systematic way to assess Shari'ah relevance. The guidebook's sector-by-sector mapping across all 55 BOL powers ensures no sector is overlooked — whether the entry point is a bill in Parliament or a program in a ministry.

## IV. Partnerships

A guidebook of this nature cannot be developed in isolation. It must be shaped by the institutions and practitioners who will ultimately use it. The proponent welcomes partnerships across five areas.

**Collaborators.** Shari'ah experts, legal scholars, and government practitioners who deal with Islamic law in their daily work can participate in the development of this guidebook through co-authorship, expert review, consultation sessions, or written feedback on specific chapters. Collaborators may include legal researchers from the BTA Parliament, practicing lawyers with Shari'ah expertise, faculty from universities in the region, and scholars from the Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta'. Their involvement ensures the guidebook reflects operational realities rather than academic theory alone.

**Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' (BDI) and Shari'ah Courts.** The BDI serves as the consultative body for Islamic jurisprudence in the BARMM under BAA No. 13, Sections 52-56, with the power to issue religious opinions and fatawa. Any guidebook that provides a methodology for citing religious texts and defining the bounds of Islamic legal analysis benefits from scholarly review by this body. Similarly, the three-tier Shari'ah Court system applies Islamic law daily in matters of personal status, family law, and related civil disputes — judges and court administrators are well-positioned to verify whether the guidebook's jurisdictional references align with actual practice. Partnership with these institutions can take various forms: technical review of relevant chapters, consultation sessions, or written feedback on specific provisions.

**BTA Parliament.** The Parliament is a primary end-user of this guidebook. Parliamentary committees whose jurisdiction intersects with Shari'ah — from the Bangsamoro Justice System to Health, Labor and Employment, Agriculture, Trade, and others — stand to benefit from a standard reference for legislative drafting. Parliamentary engagement can range from staff participation in consultations to technical review of the legislative chapters. Individual Members of Parliament with portfolios touching Shari'ah, social welfare, or economic development may also serve as legislative champions for this initiative.

**Ministries, Offices, and Agencies (MOAs).** The guidebook's application chapters (Part IV) map Shari'ah integration across all 55 BOL powers — covering health, education, labor, environment, finance, trade, and infrastructure. The ministries and agencies that administer these sectors can contribute by reviewing the chapters relevant to their mandate, providing feedback on whether the sector-specific guidance reflects current programs and operational realities, or designating technical staff to participate in focused consultations. MOA involvement ensures the guidebook is grounded in how agencies actually operate, not only in how the BOL describes their powers.

**Development Partners.** International organizations and development agencies with programs in Bangsamoro governance, Islamic finance, rule of law, or institutional capacity-building can support this project through financial support, technical assistance, or participation in the validation and dissemination process. Areas where support would be particularly valuable include expert consultation activities, publication and printing, a Training-of-Trainers program for parliamentary staff and ministry focal persons, and regional dissemination across the five provinces and the Special Geographic Area. The guidebook aligns with the Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028 (Goal 1: Stable, Just, and Accountable Government; the Pillars of Moral Governance), the OIC 2025 Programme of Action on good governance in Muslim-majority contexts, and SDG 16 (Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions). A detailed budget and activity breakdown is available upon request.

## V. Timeline

The project runs for three months. Planning and preparation activities primarily occur during the first three weeks. After that, consultations and drafting happen concurrently and iteratively — not in sequence.

**Planning and Preparation (Weeks 1 to 3).** Identify and confirm collaborators. Reach out to the BDI, Shari'ah Courts, and relevant parliamentary committees. Set the consultation schedule. Circulate the working manuscript to all confirmed partners for initial reading and feedback.

**Consultations and Drafting (Months 1-3, continuous).** These activities run in parallel throughout the project. Consultation sessions with BDI scholars, Shari'ah Judges, and parliamentary staff produce written feedback on specific chapters. That feedback is incorporated into the manuscript immediately — not stockpiled for a later revision phase. As new chapters are revised, they go back to partners for verification. The quality audit (checking every scriptural citation, legal reference, and factual claim) runs alongside the drafting, not after it. The Memorandum of Understanding formalizing institutional endorsement is prepared and signed as the manuscript reaches its final form. The goal is a publication-ready guidebook in both print and digital formats at the end of the three-month period.

## VI. Call to Action

The BOL established the legal mandate for Shari'ah legislation and policy in the Bangsamoro. Translating that mandate into daily practice requires standardized tools and methodologies. This guidebook bridges that gap. It provides practitioners in Parliament and the ministries with a documented, peer-reviewed framework for researching Islamic sources, drafting Shari'ah-consistent legislation, and designing policies that reflect Shari'ah considerations across every sector. We are inviting collaborators, institutional partners, and development agencies to participate in this three-month development process.
