# Chapter 15: Career Growth and Development

*Government is one of the few employers in the Philippines where your promotion path is legally defined, your salary increases are guaranteed by law, and your training is a right -- not a privilege. But you have to know the system to use it.*

---

Your first day is behind you. Your probation is complete. You are producing quality work and your supervisor trusts you with real assignments. Now the question changes from "Can I do this job?" to "Where does this job take me?"

This chapter answers that question. It covers how promotion works, what you will earn at each level, the competency frameworks that shape your development, the training available to you, how to move laterally across offices, and the path from staff member to supervisor to director.

---

## 15.1 How Promotion Works -- The Mechanics

Promotion in BARMM government is **not automatic**. You do not get promoted just because you have been in your position for a certain number of years. Promotion requires three things: a **vacancy**, you meeting the **Qualification Standards (QS)**, and you competing successfully through the **HRMPSB process**.

### The Promotion Process (Step by Step)

**Step 1: A vacancy opens.** Someone resigns, retires, gets promoted, transfers, or a new position is created through the plantilla. Without a vacancy, there is no promotion -- regardless of how qualified you are.

**Step 2: The vacancy is posted.** The HR office posts the position for a minimum of **10 calendar days** (CSC rule),[^3] both internally and externally. This means external applicants can also compete for the position -- including people from other offices and from outside government.

**Step 3: "Next-in-rank" gets priority.** Employees one level below the vacant position receive **priority consideration** in the evaluation.[^4] But "next-in-rank" is a preference, not a guarantee. The HRMPSB evaluates ALL qualified applicants -- internal and external.

**Step 4: Comparative Assessment.** The same 5 criteria used in hiring apply to promotion:

| Criteria | Weight | What Is Evaluated |
|---|---|---|
| **Education** | 25% | Degree relevance, graduate units, honors |
| **Experience** | 25% | Years and relevance of work experience |
| **Training** | 15% | Relevant seminars, workshops, certifications |
| **Eligibility** | 10% | Type and level of CS eligibility |
| **Interview** | 25% | Competency-Based Interview performance |

**Step 5: HRMPSB recommendation.** The board ranks candidates and submits the top 3-5 to the appointing authority (Minister, Secretary General, Director).

**Step 6: Appointment.** The appointing authority selects from the shortlist. The selected candidate receives a new appointment letter with the higher salary grade.

### Realistic Promotion Timelines

| From | To | Typical Timeline | What You Need | Common Blocker |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SG 11 | SG 13 | 2-3 years | SPMS rating of VS or higher, basic training hours | No vacancy in your office |
| SG 13 | SG 15 | 2-3 years | Accumulated experience, intermediate training | CSC eligibility (if you do not have it yet) |
| SG 15 | SG 18 | 3-5 years | Specialized training, demonstrated CSW proficiency | Competition from lateral entrants |
| SG 18 | SG 22 | 4-6 years | Supervisory training, leadership competencies | Fewer positions -- the pyramid narrows |
| SG 22 | SG 24 | 3-5 years | Division Chief readiness, management experience | Very competitive -- may require transfer |
| SG 24 | SG 27 | 5-10 years | Director-level competencies, strategic track record | Often requires transfer to a different MOA |

### The Reality Check

Promotion speed depends on two factors you cannot fully control:

1. **Whether vacancies exist in your office.** In a small office with 10 staff and no turnover, you might wait 5 years for a promotion. In a rapidly growing ministry with many new positions being created, you might get promoted in 18 months.
2. **Whether you have competitors.** A next-in-rank position with no other qualified applicants is almost certain. The same position with 5 strong applicants is a genuine competition.

**Choose your office strategically** (see Chapter 6). An office that is growing, has frequent turnover, or is creating new positions offers faster promotion than a stable office with low turnover.

## 15.2 Salary Grade Progression

Your salary is determined by two factors: your **Salary Grade (SG)** set by the Salary Standardization Law (SSL)[^1] and your **Step** within that grade. Each SG has 8 steps. You advance to the next step every **3 years** of continuous satisfactory service.[^2]

### Salary Grade Table

| SG | Position Level | Monthly Base (Step 1, approx.) | Monthly Base (Step 8, approx.) | Annual Base (Step 1, approx.) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 8 | Entry clerk/aide | PHP 20,000 | PHP 22,500 | PHP 240,000 |
| 11 | Entry professional (fresh graduate) | PHP 27,000 | PHP 31,000 | PHP 324,000 |
| 13 | Junior professional | PHP 30,500 | PHP 35,000 | PHP 366,000 |
| 15 | Mid-junior professional | PHP 38,000 | PHP 43,000 | PHP 456,000 |
| 18 | Senior professional / Section Chief | PHP 46,000 | PHP 53,000 | PHP 552,000 |
| 20 | Senior specialist | PHP 55,000 | PHP 63,000 | PHP 660,000 |
| 22 | Supervising / Department Head (LGU) | PHP 65,000 | PHP 75,000 | PHP 780,000 |
| 24 | Division Chief | PHP 85,000 | PHP 97,000 | PHP 1,020,000 |
| 25 | Assistant Director | PHP 95,000 | PHP 109,000 | PHP 1,140,000 |
| 27 | Director III | PHP 130,000 | PHP 149,000 | PHP 1,560,000 |
| 28 | Director IV | PHP 140,000 | PHP 160,000 | PHP 1,680,000 |
| 30 | Undersecretary | PHP 145,000 | PHP 170,000 | PHP 1,740,000 |
| 31 | Minister / Secretary General | PHP 155,000 | PHP 180,000 | PHP 1,860,000 |

**Note**: These are approximate figures based on recent SSL tranches.[^5] Actual amounts change with each tranche update.

### Additional Compensation

On top of your base salary, you receive:

| Allowance/Bonus | Amount | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| **PERA** (Personnel Economic Relief Allowance) | PHP 2,000/month | Monthly |
| **ACA** (Additional Compensation Allowance) | Varies | Monthly |
| **COLA** (Cost of Living Allowance) | Integrated into base pay in recent tranches | -- |
| **Year-end bonus** (13th month) | 1 month salary | December |
| **Cash gift** | PHP 5,000 | December |
| **PBB** (Performance-Based Bonus) | PHP 10,000-35,000 (varies by tier) | Annual |
| **Step increment** | Automatic salary increase within your SG | Every 3 years |
| **Loyalty award** | Cash award | Every 5 years (10th, 15th, 20th year, etc.) |

### Fresh Graduate Salary Reality

At **SG 11 (Step 1)**, you earn approximately **PHP 27,000/month base** plus allowances. After deductions for GSIS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG, your take-home is approximately **PHP 25,000-28,000/month**.

This is lower than many private sector entry positions. But government provides:

- **Job security** -- plantilla positions are permanent. You cannot be fired without cause and due process.[^6]
- **Comprehensive health insurance** -- PhilHealth plus GSIS sickness benefits
- **Retirement pension** -- GSIS provides a monthly pension for life after retirement (private sector 401k/SSS does not match this)
- **Housing loan access** -- Pag-IBIG housing loan at below-market interest rates
- **Leave benefits** -- 30 days annually (VL + SL), not counting special leaves
- **PBB and 13th month** -- guaranteed annual bonuses
- **Career path** -- legally defined promotion structure from SG 11 to SG 31

**The total compensation package** -- salary plus benefits plus pension -- becomes increasingly competitive with private sector as you advance beyond SG 18.

## 15.3 The Three Competency Frameworks

Your career development is guided by **competency frameworks** -- structured descriptions of the knowledge, skills, and attitudes required at each level. BARMM uses three interconnected frameworks. Knowing which one applies to you, and where you stand on each competency, is essential for career planning.

### Framework 1: Parliament Staff Competencies (25 Competencies)[^7]

**Who it applies to**: All staff working in an MP's office or the Parliament Secretariat.

**Structure**: Three tiers with 25 total competencies:
- **Core competencies** (5) -- for ALL Parliament staff: Critical/Creative/Strategic Thinking, Effective Communication, Synergistic Collaboration, Complete Staff Work, Public Service and Cultural Competence
- **Leadership competencies** (5) -- for supervisors and senior staff
- **Functional competencies** (15) -- 5 per role type:
  - **PAO** (Political Affairs Officer): Stakeholder engagement and constituency building, representation, advocacy and lobbying, project and events management, monitoring and evaluation
  - **LSO** (Legislative Staff Officer): Investigation and research, policy making and analysis, legal/legislative competence, attention to details and quality, oversight skills
  - **PIMAO** (Public Information and Media Affairs Officer): Strategic communications, documentation, social media management, print and digital media, media handling

**Assessment scale**: 4 levels -- Needs Improvement, Developing, Proficient, Exemplary

**Your target as entry staff**: Reach "Developing" in all Core competencies within Year 1. Reach "Proficient" by Year 3.

### Framework 2: ACSCCRaPATFEE (12 Competencies)[^8]

**Who it applies to**: Any BARMM government worker who produces Complete Staff Work -- which means everyone.

**The 12 competencies**:

| # | Competency | What It Means in Practice |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Analytical and Critical Thinking** | Examining issues from multiple angles before recommending action |
| 2 | **Creativity and Innovation** | Generating new approaches to persistent problems |
| 3 | **Strategic Thinking** | Connecting your daily work to BDP goals and the broader mission |
| 4 | **Communication** | Writing clear memos, briefers, and reports; presenting ideas effectively |
| 5 | **Collaboration and Teamwork** | Working across divisions and ministries to produce coordinated outputs |
| 6 | **Research and Analysis** | Gathering data, reviewing literature, validating sources |
| 7 | **Problem-Solving and Decision Making** | Identifying the root cause and recommending actionable solutions |
| 8 | **Attention to Details and Quality** | Producing error-free work that meets professional standards |
| 9 | **Time and Attention Management** | Meeting deadlines while managing competing priorities |
| 10 | **Flexibility and Adaptability** | Adjusting to new assignments, changing priorities, and ambiguity |
| 11 | **Emotional Intelligence and Accountability** | Managing your reactions, taking responsibility for your work |
| 12 | **Ethics and Integrity** | Practicing amanah, adl, and the 12 Pillars in every decision |

**Assessment scale**: 5 levels -- Limited, Developing, Proficient, Advanced, Expert

**Your target as entry staff**: Reach "Developing" across all 12 within Year 1. Reach "Proficient" in at least 6 by Year 3.

**How it connects to CSW**: Each competency maps to specific ADDRESS IT steps. Analytical Thinking maps to Step 1 (Analyze Context) and Step 3 (Research). Communication maps to Step 5 (Submit). Problem-Solving maps to Step 4 (Explore Options).

### Framework 3: 12 Supervisory Competencies[^9]

**Who it applies to**: Supervisors and Division Chiefs. You will encounter this framework when you reach SG 18-22 and take on direct reports.

**The 12 competencies**:

1. **Foundations of Supervision** -- Understanding your role as a supervisor (you are no longer the doer; you are the enabler)
2. **Assessing Performance, Potential, and Readiness** -- Using the PPR framework to evaluate staff
3. **Giving Effective Feedback** -- Using the SBI model (Situation-Behavior-Impact)
4. **Coaching for Performance** -- Developing staff through the 5-element coaching cycle
5. **Setting Goals and Expectations** -- Collaborative goal-setting using the 5-step protocol
6. **Conducting Effective Check-Ins** -- Monthly structured conversations with direct reports
7. **Managing Underperformance** -- Addressing performance gaps with progressive interventions
8. **Developing Talent** -- Identifying high-potential staff and creating stretch assignments
9. **Leading Teams** -- Building cohesive, high-performing teams
10. **Managing Change** -- Guiding staff through organizational transitions
11. **Delegation and Empowerment** -- Assigning work with authority, not just tasks
12. **Building Accountability Culture** -- Creating an environment where people own their results

**When you need it**: When you are promoted to a position with direct reports (typically SG 18+). A mandatory **Supervisory Development Program** must be completed before or within 6 months of your first supervisory appointment.[^10]

## 15.4 Training Catalog -- What Is Available and How to Access It

Training is both a right and a career investment. Every training hour is recorded and counts toward the **Training** component (15%) of the Comparative Assessment when you compete for promotion.

### Available Training Programs

| Training | Mandatory? | Provider | How to Request | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Government Orientation** | Yes (new employees) | CSC / HR office | Automatic upon appointment | Once |
| **Values Orientation Workshop** | Yes (all staff) | CSC | Through HR office | Once |
| **CSW Training (ADDRESS IT)** | Recommended for all | Internal trainers / DAB | Request through supervisor | Annually |
| **SPMS Orientation** | Yes (all staff) | HR office | Automatic | Annual |
| **Records Management** | Recommended (admin staff) | CSC / National Archives | Through HR office | As scheduled |
| **Basic Supervisory Course** | Yes (new supervisors) | CSC / DAB | Through HR office | Before or within 6 months of appointment |
| **Supervisory Development Program** | Recommended (supervisors) | Internal trainers / DAB | Request through supervisor | As scheduled |
| **Financial Management** | Recommended (budget/finance) | MFBM / COA | Through HR office | Quarterly |
| **Procurement Training** | Required (BAC members) | GPPB / procurement office | Through BAC chair | As needed |
| **Legislative Process Training** | Recommended (Parliament staff) | Parliament Secretariat | Through committee director | Semi-annually |
| **IT Skills** | Optional (all staff) | BICTO / DAB | Request through supervisor | Quarterly |
| **Leadership and Management** | Optional (SG 18+) | CSC / DAB / external providers | Through HR office | Annually |
| **Graduate Studies** | Optional (scholarship/leave) | Universities / CHED / DAB | Apply through HR with supervisor endorsement | Annual cycle |

### How to Request Training

1. **Check your IPCR.** Training should align with your performance targets and development needs. A training request that connects to your IPCR is more likely to be approved.
2. **Discuss with your supervisor during a check-in.** Get their endorsement before submitting any request.
3. **Submit a request memo to HR through your supervisor.** Specify: the training name, dates, provider, cost (if any), and how it connects to your work and IPCR targets.
4. **HR evaluates** against the office's Training and Development Plan and budget availability.
5. **If approved**, you receive a **training order** -- the official authorization to attend.

### Building Your Training Hours

In your first year, take **every training offered to you**. Here is why:

- Each training hour is recorded in your personal file
- Training = 15% of the Comparative Assessment for promotion
- Free government webinars and CSC online courses count
- Certificates of completion are accepted as evidence

**Sources of free training hours:**
- CSC Learning and Development Portal (online courses)
- BARMM ministry webinars and orientations
- DAB (Development Academy of the Bangsamoro) programs
- Development partner workshops (UNDP, JICA, AusAID)
- Professional association events

Build your training hours as aggressively as you build your work experience. Both matter for promotion.

## 15.5 Scholarship and Graduate Study Programs

A **master's degree** is not required for entry-level positions. But as you advance, especially toward Division Chief (SG 22-24), the QS increasingly requires "master's degree" or "completion of academic requirements for a master's degree." Plan for this early.

### Available Programs

| Program | Who Qualifies | What It Covers | Return Service |
|---|---|---|---|
| **CSC Scholarship** | Permanent employees, 2+ years service, VS+ rating | Tuition and allowance for graduate studies in public admin, development management | 2 years per year of scholarship |
| **CHED Scholarship** | BARMM residents, may include employees | Tuition for graduate and post-graduate programs | Varies |
| **DAB Programs** | BARMM government employees | Short courses, executive development, specialized training | None (in-service) |
| **Foreign Scholarships** (JICA, AusAID, KOICA) | Government employees nominated by ministry | Full scholarship for master's abroad | 2-3 years per year of scholarship |
| **Study Leave** (CSC) | Permanent employees, 7+ years service | Leave with pay for up to 6 months for bar/board review or graduate studies | Complete studies within approved period |[^12]
| **No-Cost Study** (self-funded) | Any permanent employee | Permission to attend classes outside work hours -- no financial support | None |

### How to Position Yourself for Scholarships

- **Maintain consistent SPMS ratings** of Very Satisfactory (4) or Outstanding (5). Scholarship programs require performance evidence.
- **Choose a graduate program aligned with your office's mandate and the BDP.** A master's in public administration, development management, or your technical field will serve you best.
- **Build a relationship with your supervisor.** They must endorse your application. A supervisor who trusts your work and sees your potential will write a strong endorsement.
- **Apply early.** Deadlines are strict and slots are limited. Missing a deadline by one day means waiting another year.
- **Understand the return service obligation.** If you receive a government scholarship and resign before completing your return service, you must reimburse the full cost. Calculate this before accepting.

## 15.6 Lateral Movement, Detailing, and Secondment

Not all career growth is upward. Sometimes the best move is **sideways**.

### Types of Lateral Movement

| Movement | What It Is | Duration | Your Salary | When to Use It |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Transfer** | Permanent move to another office | Permanent | Adjusts to new position's SG | When you want to change tracks or offices permanently |
| **Detailing** | Temporary assignment to another office | 30-90 days (extendable) | Stays with original office | When you want to explore a different office before committing |
| **Secondment** | Extended assignment to another entity | 6-12 months | Paid by original or receiving office | For inter-agency projects or professional development |
| **Reassignment** | Movement within the same office to a different unit | Indefinite | Same | When your office restructures or you need a different assignment |[^11]

### Why Lateral Moves Are Underrated

A lateral move is an investment, not a detour. Consider:

- A 2-year detailing to **BPDA** teaches you planning and M&E skills that make you a stronger candidate for Division Chief when you return to your ministry.
- A secondment to the **Parliament** teaches you the legislative process in a way that no training course can match. When you return to your ministry, you understand how laws are made -- and you can shape them.
- A transfer from an **LGU** to a **ministry** after 3 years of frontline experience gives you both the community perspective and the institutional platform to advance.

### Common Lateral Movement Patterns

| From | To | What You Gain |
|---|---|---|
| LGU (3 years frontline) | Ministry (same sector) | Institutional platform, broader scope, higher SG ceiling |
| Ministry (technical) | Parliament (committee) | Legislative process knowledge, policy analysis skills |
| Office/Agency (planning) | Ministry (operations) | Implementation experience, sector depth |
| Parliament (legislation) | BPDA or commission | Strategic perspective, cross-sector coordination |
| Any track | Foreign scholarship (1-2 years) | International exposure, advanced degree, expanded network |

### How to Arrange a Lateral Move

1. **Identify the target office and position.** Check whether a vacancy exists or is expected.
2. **Discuss with your current supervisor.** Getting their support -- or at least their non-objection -- is critical.
3. **Contact the receiving office's HR.** Express interest and inquire about openings.
4. **For transfer**: Apply through the regular HRMPSB process at the receiving office.
5. **For detailing/secondment**: Your current head of office and the receiving head must both approve. Formalize through a memorandum of agreement.

## 15.7 The Path from Staff to Supervisor to Director

The career journey in government has distinct **transitions** -- moments when the nature of your work fundamentally changes. Understanding these transitions in advance helps you prepare for them.

### Career Level Progression

| Level | SG Range | Your Role | Competency Focus | The Key Transition |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Staff** | 11-15 | Individual contributor. You produce CSW, memos, reports, data. | ACSCCRaPATFEE at Developing-Proficient. Technical knowledge of your sector. | Learning to produce quality work **independently**. |
| **Senior Staff** | 18-20 | Advanced individual contributor. You handle complex assignments and begin mentoring junior staff. | ACSCCRaPATFEE at Proficient-Advanced. Beginning to coach others. | Shift from "doing the work" to "ensuring quality of work." |
| **Supervisor** | 22-24 | Division Chief or Section Head. You manage a team of 3-15 staff. | 12 Supervisory Competencies activated. | The hardest transition: from "best doer" to "best enabler." You are now accountable for OTHER people's output, not just your own. |
| **Director** | 25-28 | Bureau, Department, or Service Head. You manage multiple divisions and shape organizational direction. | Strategic thinking, stakeholder management, resource allocation, policy influence. | From managing people to **managing systems**. You think in years, not weeks. |
| **Executive** | 29-31 | Minister, Secretary General, Undersecretary. You set organizational direction and represent the entity externally. | Political acumen, intergovernmental relations, public accountability, vision-setting. | From managing systems to **shaping the institution**. You are accountable to the Chief Minister, the Parliament, and the Bangsamoro people. |

### What Each Transition Feels Like

**Staff to Senior Staff**: "I used to ask for permission. Now people ask me for guidance."

You have mastered the technical work. Junior staff come to you with questions. Your supervisor assigns you the complex cases because you are reliable. The danger at this stage is becoming comfortable -- doing excellent work at SG 18 forever without pushing toward leadership.

**Senior Staff to Supervisor**: "I used to be the best writer in my team. Now I review other people's writing -- and it is harder than writing myself."

This is the most difficult transition in government. As a supervisor, you **stop doing most of the technical work** and start enabling others to do it. Your value is no longer in the quality of your memos. It is in the quality of your team's memos. You must learn to give feedback (SBI model), coach underperformers, set goals collaboratively, and run effective check-ins. Many excellent individual contributors struggle with this transition because they cannot stop doing the work themselves.

**Supervisor to Director**: "I used to manage a team. Now I manage teams of teams, and I see problems I never noticed when I was closer to the work."

At the Director level, you manage multiple divisions, each led by a Division Chief. You allocate resources, set strategy, negotiate with other offices, and represent your bureau in senior meetings. You no longer read every memo -- your Division Chiefs do that. You see the big picture and make decisions that affect dozens of staff and thousands of beneficiaries.

### Preparing for Each Transition

| Current Level | What to Do Now to Prepare for the Next Level |
|---|---|
| **Staff** (SG 11-15) | Master CSW. Build competencies. Accumulate training hours. Maintain VS+ SPMS ratings. |
| **Senior Staff** (SG 18-20) | Start mentoring junior staff. Volunteer to coordinate team tasks. Complete supervisory training. |
| **Supervisor** (SG 22-24) | Develop strategic thinking. Build cross-office relationships. Seek Director-level assignments. Study resource allocation and policy influence. |
| **Director** (SG 25-28) | Build intergovernmental relations. Develop public communication skills. Understand political dynamics. Position yourself for executive leadership. |
