# Chapter 14: The Bangsamoro Government Employee Journey

*Your career is a journey with defined stages. Know what is coming at each one.*

---

You have applied, interviewed, and received your appointment letter. Now what?

This chapter maps the **complete employee lifecycle** from application to retirement, based on BAA 17 (Bangsamoro Civil Service Code).[^1] Think of it as a roadmap -- you are at the starting point, and this chapter shows you every stage ahead so you can prepare, not just react.

Every Bangsamoro government employee -- whether you work in the Parliament, a ministry, an office, or an LGU -- follows this journey. The milestones are the same. The timeline varies based on your performance, the availability of vacancies, and the choices you make along the way.

---

## 14.1 The Journey Map

Here is your career from beginning to end, in one view:

```
APPLY --> HIRE --> ONBOARD --> PROBATION --> PERFORM --> DEVELOP --> PROMOTE --> LEAD --> TRANSITION
```

| Stage | Duration | What Happens | Key Milestone |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Apply** | 1-3 months | Job search, PDS submission, interview, Comparative Assessment | Appointment letter |
| **Onboard** | Days 1-30 | Oath of office, ID processing, orientation, office tour, first assignments | Completed orientation checklist |
| **Probation** | Months 1-6 | Learning the job, first CSW assignments, supervisor check-ins | Probationary evaluation PASSED |
| **Perform** | Years 1-3 | IPCR targets, regular CSW output, SPMS ratings, training | First "Very Satisfactory" or higher rating |
| **Develop** | Years 2-5 | Functional training, CSW mastery, lateral assignments, graduate studies | Competency proficiency across core areas |
| **Promote** | Year 3+ | Next-in-rank competition, competitive selection, salary grade advancement | First promotion |
| **Lead** | Year 5+ | Supervisory role, team management, system design, mentoring | First supervisory appointment |
| **Transition** | Varies | Transfer, secondment, retirement, resignation, or new career | Graceful exit with knowledge transfer |

Not everyone follows this timeline exactly. Some people reach the "Lead" stage in 5 years. Some take 15. The stages are the same -- the speed depends on you, on vacancies, and on opportunity.

What matters is that you **know where you are** at every stage, and you know **what comes next**.

## 14.2 Onboarding: Your First 30 Days

Your first 30 days set the tone for your entire career. This is when your supervisor, your colleagues, and your office leadership form their first impression of you. Make it count.

### Day 1: The Administrative Foundation

Your first day is mostly paperwork -- but every step matters.

- **Report to HR.** You will process your appointment documents, take your oath of office, and receive your government ID. Bring all original documents (diploma, TOR, CSC eligibility certificate, birth certificate, NBI clearance). HR will need certified true copies.
- **Oath of office.** This is administered by the appointing authority or an authorized officer. It is a brief ceremony, but it is legally significant. From the moment you take the oath, you are bound by RA 6713 (Code of Conduct),[^2] BAA 17 (Bangsamoro Civil Service Code),[^3] and all applicable laws governing public officials and employees.
- **Government ID and system access.** You will be enrolled in biometric attendance (or receive a Daily Time Record form), issued your government email, and given access to office systems. Some offices use Google Workspace. Others use Microsoft. Learn whichever your office uses.
- **Benefits enrollment.** HR will process your enrollment in GSIS (Government Service Insurance System),[^4] PhilHealth,[^5] and Pag-IBIG Fund.[^6] These are mandatory deductions from your salary -- but they provide retirement benefits, health insurance, and housing loan access.

### Days 1-3: Know Your Office

- **Meet your immediate supervisor.** This is the most important relationship in your first year. Ask about their expectations, communication preferences, and how they want you to report progress. Some supervisors want daily email updates. Others prefer weekly face-to-face check-ins. Ask -- do not guess.
- **Receive your position description.** This document defines your official duties and responsibilities. Read it carefully. If anything is unclear, ask your supervisor to explain. Your **IPCR** (Individual Performance Commitment and Review) will be based on this.[^7]
- **Get your office tour.** Learn where the conference room is, where the prayer room is, where the filing room is, where the comfort room is. Small details, but they matter when you need to find a document at 4:30 PM.
- **Meet your team.** Introduce yourself to every colleague in your division. Learn their names, their positions, and what they do. In Bangsamoro culture, relationships are foundational -- take time to build them.

### Week 1: Understand the System

- **Read the Manual of Procedures (MOP)** -- if your office has one. Not all offices do. If there is no MOP, ask your supervisor how key processes work: How does a memo get approved? How are documents filed? What is the routing for outgoing correspondence?
- **Study the organizational chart.** Know where your division sits in the office structure. Know who your Division Chief is, who the Director is, who the Minister or head of office is. Understand the chain of command.
- **Learn the filing system.** Every office has one -- some digital, some paper-based, some both. Learn it immediately. You will be filing and retrieving documents from Day 1.
- **Attend your first staff meeting.** Listen more than you speak. Take notes. Observe how people interact, who leads the discussion, and what the current priorities are. You will learn more about your office from one staff meeting than from reading 10 documents.

### Week 1-2: Your First Supervisor Check-In

Your supervisor will schedule a check-in -- or you should request one. This is your opportunity to:

- **Clarify expectations.** What does your supervisor expect you to produce in your first month? What does "good work" look like in this office?
- **Receive your first assignments.** These will likely be small: drafting a memo, organizing a file, preparing data for a report. Treat them as seriously as you would a major project. Your supervisor is testing your reliability and attention to detail.
- **Ask about communication preferences.** How should you submit work -- email, printed copy, shared drive? When should you escalate problems versus solving them yourself? How does your supervisor prefer to be addressed?

### Week 2-4: Your First CSW Assignment

Within your first month, you should complete at least one substantive **Complete Staff Work** assignment using the ADDRESS IT methodology (see Chapter 10). This is your proof of concept -- your demonstration that you can produce decision-ready work.

Your supervisor will evaluate not just the content but the process:

- Did you understand the assignment correctly? (Step 1: Analyze Context)
- Did you research adequately? (Step 3: Research)
- Did you present options, not just information? (Step 4: Explore)
- Is the output complete -- no loose ends, no missing attachments? (Step 5: Submit)

### By Day 30: Your Checklist

By the end of your first month, you should be able to answer "yes" to all of these:

- [ ] I know my office's mandate under BAA 13[^8]
- [ ] I have read my position description and understand my duties
- [ ] I know my supervisor's expectations and communication preferences
- [ ] I know the approval workflow for documents in my office
- [ ] I have submitted at least one completed staff work assignment
- [ ] I am enrolled in GSIS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG
- [ ] I have my government ID and system access
- [ ] I know my colleagues' names and what they do
- [ ] I know where the MOP is (or I know there is none)
- [ ] I have attended at least one staff meeting

If you cannot check all of these, you are behind. Ask your supervisor for help catching up.

## 14.3 Probation: Months 1-6

The first six months of your appointment are a **probationary period**. This is when your supervisor formally evaluates whether you can do the job.

### What Is Being Evaluated

Your probationary evaluation measures two things:

1. **Results** -- Are you completing assignments on time? Is your work accurate? Does your CSW meet the standard? Are you meeting the targets in your position description?
2. **Behaviors** -- Are you reliable? Do you follow the chain of command? Do you accept feedback constructively? Do you work well with your team? Do you observe the 12 Pillars of Moral Governance[^9] in your daily conduct?

### The Probationary Evaluation

At the end of your probationary period, your supervisor submits a formal evaluation to HR. The evaluation states whether you have performed **satisfactorily** or **unsatisfactorily**.

- **Satisfactory**: You pass probation. Your appointment is confirmed. You are now a regular employee with full security of tenure (if you hold a permanent appointment).
- **Unsatisfactory**: Your appointment may be terminated. BAA 17[^10] and CSC rules allow for termination of probationary employees who fail to meet performance standards. This is rare -- but it happens.

### How to Succeed During Probation

**Ask questions early.** In your first month, asking questions shows initiative. By month three, not knowing basic processes signals that you are not learning. Front-load your questions.

**Seek feedback proactively.** Do not wait for your supervisor to tell you what you did wrong. After completing an assignment, ask: "Is this the level of detail you expect?" or "How can I improve this for next time?"

**Document your work.** Keep a log of every assignment you complete, every training you attend, and every positive feedback you receive. This becomes evidence during your evaluation.

**Produce quality CSW from Day 1.** Your supervisor is looking for the answer to one question: "Can this person produce work that I do not need to redo?" Every memo, every spreadsheet, every briefer you submit is your answer.

**Be on time. Every day.** Attendance matters. Chronic tardiness during probation is one of the most common reasons for a negative evaluation. Government tracks attendance through biometrics or DTR -- there is no hiding it.

### The Most Common Reasons Probation Fails

| Reason | What It Looks Like | How to Avoid It |
|---|---|---|
| Poor attendance | Frequent absences, chronic tardiness | Set an alarm. Arrive 15 minutes early. File leave properly. |
| Incomplete work | Memos with missing attachments, data with errors | Use a checklist before submitting anything. Double-check numbers. |
| Inability to follow instructions | Producing different output than what was assigned | Repeat back the assignment in your own words before starting. |
| Poor attitude toward feedback | Becoming defensive or argumentative when corrected | Thank your supervisor for feedback. Fix the issue. Move on. |
| Not learning office systems | Still not knowing the filing system in month 3 | Take notes. Create your own reference sheet. Ask colleagues. |

## 14.4 Civil Service Systems You Must Know

Every BARMM civil servant interacts with these systems. Learn them in your first month -- they will govern your career for as long as you serve.

### SPMS: Strategic Performance Management System

The **SPMS** is the official CSC performance management framework used across all government agencies, including BARMM.[^11] It connects your individual work to your office's targets and to the Bangsamoro Development Plan.

**IPCR (Individual Performance Commitment and Review)** -- This is YOUR performance contract. At the beginning of each rating period, you and your supervisor agree on specific targets. At the end, you are evaluated against those targets.

**OPCR (Office Performance Commitment and Review)** -- This is your office's performance contract. Your office commits to specific outputs and outcomes for the year. Your IPCR targets should support the OPCR.

**The annual cycle:**

| Month | Activity | Your Role |
|---|---|---|
| January | Target-setting | Meet with your supervisor. Set IPCR targets for the year. Align with OPCR. |
| July | Mid-year review | Submit mid-year accomplishment report. Discuss progress with your supervisor. Adjust targets if needed. |
| December | Year-end evaluation | Submit accomplishment report. Your supervisor rates your performance. |

**The 5-point rating scale:**

| Rating | Score | What It Means | Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Outstanding** | 5 | Exceeded all targets significantly | Priority for promotion, training, awards |
| **Very Satisfactory** | 4 | Met or exceeded most targets | Eligible for PBB, strong promotion candidate |
| **Satisfactory** | 3 | Met basic targets | Eligible for PBB, meets minimum requirement |
| **Unsatisfactory** | 2 | Failed to meet some targets | Not eligible for PBB. Performance improvement plan. |
| **Poor** | 1 | Failed to meet most targets | Grounds for non-renewal, demotion, or dismissal |

**Your SPMS rating determines:**
- **PBB eligibility** -- you need at least "Satisfactory" AND your office must meet its OPCR
- **Promotion ranking** -- higher ratings give you an edge in the Comparative Assessment
- **Training priority** -- Outstanding performers get first pick of training opportunities
- **Step increment** -- satisfactory performance is required for your periodic salary increase

### SALN: Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth

**SALN** is a constitutional requirement.[^12] Every government official and employee must file one annually. The deadline is **April 30**.

- **Your first SALN**: Filed within 30 days of your appointment
- **Annual SALN**: Filed every year covering assets as of December 31 of the previous year
- **Last SALN**: Filed within 30 days of separation from service

What to declare: all real property, personal property over PHP 50,000, liabilities, business interests, financial connections, and relatives in government within the 4th degree of consanguinity or affinity. See Chapter 16 for a complete SALN walkthrough.

**Failure to file is grounds for dismissal.** This is not optional.

### Leave Management

Permanent employees earn:
- **15 days vacation leave** per year (earned monthly at 1.25 days)[^13]
- **15 days sick leave** per year (earned monthly at 1.25 days)[^14]
- **Maternity leave**: 105 days (RA 11210, Expanded Maternity Leave Act)[^15]
- **Paternity leave**: 7 days
- **Solo parent leave**: 7 days
- **VAWC leave**: 10 days (for victims of violence against women and children)
- **Special privilege leave**: 3 days per year
- **Study leave**: up to 6 months after 7 years of service (with supervisor endorsement)
- **Force leave**: 5 days of your VL are mandatory (you must use them)

**How to file leave:**
1. Fill out the CSC Leave Application Form (CS Form 6)
2. Indicate the type of leave, dates, and reason
3. Submit to your supervisor for recommendation
4. HR processes the approval
5. For VL: file at least 5 days in advance. For SL: file within 5 days of return with medical certificate if applicable

**COS and JO employees do NOT receive leave benefits.** This is one of the strongest reasons to pursue a permanent appointment.

### PDS: Personal Data Sheet

Keep your **CS Form 212** updated. You will need it for every promotion, transfer, and appointment. When you complete a training, earn a degree, or change your personal information, update your PDS and submit the revised version to HR.

### PRAISE and PBB

**PRAISE** (Program on Awards and Incentives for Service Excellence) is the government's recognition framework.[^16] Your office should have a PRAISE committee that identifies and recognizes outstanding performance monthly, quarterly, and annually.

**PBB** (Performance-Based Bonus) is an annual cash bonus. To qualify:
- Your office must meet its OPCR targets (at least "Satisfactory")
- You must individually meet your IPCR targets (at least "Satisfactory")
- Amount varies by performance tier: typically PHP 10,000-35,000

If your office fails its OPCR, no one in the office receives PBB -- even if your individual performance was Outstanding. This creates shared accountability.

### Step Increment

Your salary increases within your current Salary Grade through **step increments**. Each SG has 8 steps. You move to the next step every **3 years** of continuous satisfactory service.[^17] This is an automatic salary increase -- no competition required -- as long as your SPMS ratings are satisfactory.

### Government Benefits

Upon appointment, you are automatically enrolled in:

| System | What It Provides | Your Contribution |
|---|---|---|
| **GSIS** | Retirement pension, life insurance, disability benefits, salary loans | Monthly premium deducted from salary |
| **PhilHealth** | Health insurance coverage | Monthly premium deducted from salary |
| **Pag-IBIG** | Housing loan, savings program, calamity loan | Monthly contribution deducted from salary |

These deductions reduce your take-home pay but provide long-term security that private sector employment rarely matches.

## 14.5 Cultural Navigation

Working in the Bangsamoro government is not just about mastering systems and procedures. It is about navigating a **cultural environment** that may be different from what you experienced in school or in other workplaces.

### Prayer Times and Prayer Rooms

Every BARMM office provides a **prayer space**. The five daily prayers (Fajr, Dhuhr, Asr, Maghrib, and Isha) are observed. During work hours, Dhuhr and Asr are the most relevant. Staff will briefly leave their desks to pray. This is normal, expected, and protected.

**If you are Muslim**: Coordinate with your supervisor so work continuity is maintained during prayer times. Most offices have informal rotating arrangements.

**If you are not Muslim**: Maintain quiet and respect during prayer times. Do not schedule urgent meetings during these periods. You will not be required to pray -- but you are expected to create space for those who do.

### Ramadan

Ramadan transforms the workplace. For approximately 30 days each year:

- **Adjusted work hours.** Many BARMM offices shift to earlier schedules (e.g., 7:30 AM - 3:30 PM) to accommodate fasting employees.
- **Energy levels change.** Fasting colleagues may have less physical energy, especially in the afternoon. Be considerate in scheduling demanding tasks.
- **Iftar (breaking of fast).** Some offices organize collective iftar gatherings. Participating -- even if you are not fasting -- is a meaningful gesture of solidarity.
- **Heightened spiritual observance.** The office atmosphere becomes more reflective. Respect this tone.

**For non-fasting staff**: Avoid eating conspicuously in front of fasting colleagues. Use the pantry or cafeteria. This is not a rule -- it is respect.

### Eid al-Fitr and Eid al-Adha

These are the two major Islamic holidays. BARMM offices close on these days. Learn the appropriate greeting: **"Eid Mubarak"** (Blessed Eid). For non-Muslim staff, saying "Eid Mubarak" to your colleagues is appreciated and appropriate.

### Bangsamoro Hospitality and Workplace Culture

- **Food sharing** is common. When a colleague shares food, accepting is a sign of respect.
- **Respect for elders** is deeply valued. Address senior colleagues with appropriate honorifics. "Sir" and "Ma'am" are baseline; "Ama" (father/elder) and "Ina" (mother/elder) are used in some groups.
- **Clan and family networks** are real. Your colleague's cousin may work in another ministry. Treat every professional interaction with integrity because reputation travels through networks.
- **Pakikisama** (getting along) and **hiya** (social propriety) operate here as they do elsewhere in the Philippines, with the added dimension of Islamic and Bangsamoro cultural values.

### Working Across Ethnolinguistic Groups

BARMM has **13 ethnolinguistic groups**: Meranaw, Maguindanaon, Tausug, Sama, Yakan, Iranun, Kalibugan (from Zamboanga Peninsula), Palawanon, Molbog, Jama Mapun, Badjao (Sama Dilaut), Sangil, and Teduray. Plus settler communities (Visayan, Ilocano, Tagalog) and indigenous peoples.

Your office may include colleagues from several of these groups. Here is how to navigate:

- **Do not assume all Bangsamoro are the same.** A Tausug colleague and a Meranaw colleague may have very different customs, languages, and perspectives.
- **Learn basic greetings** in your colleagues' languages. Even "Salamat" (thank you) in Meranaw or Maguindanaon signals respect.
- **Ask about customs you do not understand.** Genuine curiosity is always appreciated. Assumptions are not.
- **Language in meetings**: Official business is conducted in English and Filipino. But informal conversations often happen in local languages. If you feel excluded, politely ask colleagues to include you. Most will.

### "I Am Not Muslim" -- Your Place in a Muslim-Majority Workplace

If you are not Muslim and you work in BARMM government, here is what you need to know:

- **You are welcome.** The BOL guarantees the rights of all communities in the Bangsamoro, including settler communities (Article IX, Section 15)[^18] and indigenous peoples (Article IX, Section 3).[^19] The 12 Pillars of Moral Governance include **inclusivity** as a core value.
- **You will not be required to practice Islam.** No one will ask you to pray, fast, or wear Islamic clothing.
- **You will be expected to respect Islamic governance values.** Amanah (trustworthiness), adl (justice), shura (consultation), rahmah (compassion), and sidq (truthfulness) are universal values. Respecting them does not require changing your faith.
- **The greeting "Assalamu Alaikum"** means "peace be upon you." It is appropriate for anyone to use. The response is "Wa Alaikum Assalam" (and upon you, peace). Using it signals respect and cultural awareness.
- **Non-Muslim staff serve in every part of BARMM government** -- including the Parliament Secretariat, ministries, commissions, and LGUs. Your competence, not your faith, determines your career.

## 14.6 Ten Mistakes New Staff Make (And How to Avoid Them)

Every mistake on this list has been made by real government employees in their first year. Learn from their experience.

### Mistake 1: Not Reading the BOL

You work for the only autonomous region in the Philippines -- a government created by Republic Act 11054 (the Bangsamoro Organic Law).[^20] If you have not read at least the key provisions of the BOL, you do not fully understand the institution you serve. Read Articles IV-V (powers), Article VII (the Parliament), Article IX (basic rights), Article X (justice system), and Article XII (fiscal autonomy).

### Mistake 2: Assuming National Government Rules Apply

BARMM has its own **Civil Service Code** (BAA 17),[^21] its own **Administrative Code** (BAA 13),[^22] its own **Budget System Act** (BAA 84),[^23] its own **Education Code** (BAA 18),[^24] and its own **Local Governance Code** (BAA 49).[^25] These may differ from national rules. When in doubt, check the BAA -- not the national law.

### Mistake 3: Not Learning the Approval Workflow

Every document in government follows a routing chain. A memo does not go directly from your desk to the Minister. It goes through your supervisor, your Division Chief, possibly a Director, and then to the head of office. Learn this chain on Day 1. Skipping it -- even once -- erodes trust.

### Mistake 4: Waiting to Be Told What to Do

Government rewards initiative. If you see a problem, propose a solution. If you finish your assignment early, ask for the next one. If you notice a process that could be improved, write a brief memo suggesting improvements. Your supervisor will notice the difference between a staff member who waits and one who acts.

### Mistake 5: Submitting Incomplete CSW

The "Completed" in Complete Staff Work means **completed**. No missing attachments. No placeholder data. No sections marked "TBD." No forwarding a problem without a recommendation. Before you submit any work, ask yourself: "If my supervisor signs this right now, will there be any unanswered question?" If the answer is yes, the work is not complete.

### Mistake 6: Not Knowing Your Supervisor's Communication Style

Some supervisors want detailed daily updates by email. Others want a weekly verbal report. Some prefer printed documents. Others work entirely through shared drives. Some like formal "Sir/Ma'am" address. Others prefer first names. **Ask your supervisor directly** in your first week. Getting this wrong creates friction that has nothing to do with the quality of your work.

### Mistake 7: Avoiding the Cultural Context

You are in the Bangsamoro. Learn the greetings. Learn the prayer times. Learn the cultural calendar. Attend the iftar. Say "Eid Mubarak" on Eid. If you treat the cultural context as something to endure rather than something to embrace, you will always be an outsider -- regardless of how technically competent you are.

### Mistake 8: Skipping Training Opportunities

Every training you attend is recorded and counts toward your competency profile for promotion. Training accounts for **15% of the Comparative Assessment** score when you compete for your next position.[^26] Accept every training offered in your first year. Attend free CSC webinars. Complete online courses. Build your training hours aggressively.

### Mistake 9: Not Connecting Your Work to BDP Goals

Your IPCR should reference the **Bangsamoro Development Plan**. When you write a project proposal, cite which BDP goal it supports. When you draft a briefer, note the relevant BDP chapter. When you present to your team, connect your division's output to the BDP framework. This demonstrates strategic awareness -- a competency that separates staff who get promoted from staff who get passed over.

### Mistake 10: Thinking This Is Just a Job

You are not working for a regular bureaucracy. You are implementing the **Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro** -- a peace agreement that ended decades of armed conflict. Every program you run, every policy you draft, every barangay you serve is a **peace dividend**. The transition period is when credibility is built or lost. If the government delivers, trust deepens. If it fails, the peace process is undermined. Your daily work is the frontline of this test.

Treat it accordingly.
