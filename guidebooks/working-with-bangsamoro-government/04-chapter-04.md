# Your Employment Framework

BAA 17 governs your hiring, your evaluation, your promotion, and your dismissal. It is the Bangsamoro Civil Service Code — the employment framework for every government worker in BARMM. If the BOL is the constitution and BAA 13 is the organizational chart, BAA 17 is the employee handbook.

This chapter explains what BAA 17 covers, how it affects you from day one, and what systems you will encounter throughout your career. Read it before you apply. Read it again after you are hired.

---

## 4.1 What BAA 17 Covers

The **Bangsamoro Civil Service Code (BAA 17)**, enacted in February 2021, establishes the complete employment framework for BARMM government workers.[^1] It covers:

- **Recruitment and appointment** — how positions are filled
- **Qualification standards** — what you need to qualify
- **Types of appointments** — permanent, temporary, coterminous, contractual, casual
- **Performance management** — how your work is measured
- **Rewards and recognition** — how good performance is acknowledged
- **Discipline** — what happens when rules are broken
- **Leave benefits** — your time-off entitlements
- **Code of conduct** — the ethical standards you must follow
- **Separation from service** — how employment ends

BAA 17 does not exist in isolation. It works alongside **national Civil Service Commission (CSC) rules**, which still apply to BARMM in areas not specifically covered by BAA 17. The relationship is straightforward: where BAA 17 provides its own rules, those rules apply. Where BAA 17 is silent, national CSC rules fill the gap.

## 4.2 The Merit and Fitness Principle

The foundation of BAA 17 — and of the Philippine civil service generally — is the **merit and fitness principle**. This means:

- Positions are filled based on **qualifications, competence, and competitive selection** — not on political connections, family ties, or personal loyalty
- All applicants who meet the qualification standards have an equal opportunity to be considered
- Selection is conducted by a **Human Resource Merit Promotion and Selection Board (HRMPSB)** — a multi-member panel that evaluates candidates

> "Appointments in the civil service shall be made only according to merit and fitness, to be determined as far as practicable by competitive examination."
> — Philippine Constitution, Article IX-B, Section 2(2)[^2]

**What this means in practice:** When a position opens, it is posted publicly. Applicants submit credentials. The HRMPSB evaluates all qualified applicants against the same criteria. The most qualified applicant is recommended for appointment. The appointing authority (Minister, Secretary General, or other authorized official) makes the final appointment based on the HRMPSB recommendation.

Does this system work perfectly? No. Patronage pressures exist in any government, and BARMM is no exception. But the legal framework is designed to resist patronage. Understanding the merit-based process protects you — both as an applicant (you know what you are entitled to) and as a future government worker (you know what standards to uphold).

## 4.3 Types of Appointments

Not all government jobs are the same. The type of appointment determines your job security, benefits, and career trajectory. Understanding these distinctions is critical before you accept any position.

### Permanent (Plantilla)

**What it is:** A permanent appointment to a position that is included in the approved staffing pattern (the "plantilla") of the office. This is the gold standard of government employment.

**What you get:**
- **Security of tenure** — you cannot be removed except for cause and with due process
- **Full benefits** — GSIS, PhilHealth, Pag-IBIG, leave credits, retirement benefits
- **Promotion eligibility** — you can be promoted to higher positions through the merit-based process
- **Step increments** — automatic salary increases based on length of service and performance

**What you need:**
- Meet all **qualification standards** for the position (education, experience, training, eligibility)
- **Civil Service eligibility** — either Professional or Sub-Professional, depending on the position level
- Pass the HRMPSB evaluation process

**How it works:** Permanent positions are the ones listed on the government's plantilla — the authorized staffing pattern approved by the Department of Budget and Management (DBM) or MFBM. Each position has a **salary grade (SG)**, a **position title**, and a set of **qualification standards** (QS).

### Temporary

**What it is:** An appointment to a position for a specified period, usually when the appointing authority cannot find a qualified applicant who meets all requirements — particularly CSC eligibility.

**What you get:**
- Benefits similar to permanent — but **no security of tenure**
- The appointment expires after the specified period (typically 12 months) and may or may not be renewed
- You can be replaced at any time if a fully qualified applicant becomes available

**When it happens:** A ministry has a vacant SG 15 position. No eligible applicant applies. An applicant with the right degree and experience but no CSC eligibility is available. The ministry can issue a temporary appointment. If that person later passes the CSC exam, the appointment can be converted to permanent.

**Key warning:** Temporary appointments do not carry security of tenure. If you are offered a temporary appointment, prioritize passing the **Civil Service exam** so your appointment can be converted.

### Coterminous

**What it is:** An appointment that is tied to the appointing authority's term. When the appointing authority leaves office, your appointment ends.

**What you get:**
- Benefits during the term of appointment
- **No security of tenure** — your appointment automatically expires when the appointing authority's term ends

**Who gets it:** Staff directly serving officials with fixed terms — confidential staff, executive assistants, and special project staff. In BARMM, this is especially relevant during the **BTA transition**. If your appointment is coterminous with a BTA member's term, your position ends when the BTA's mandate expires or is reconstituted.

**Key warning:** If you are offered a coterminous position, understand that your employment is tied to someone else's tenure. When the BTA transitions to an elected government, coterminous positions may not survive. Ask explicitly: "Is this position coterminous or plantilla?"

### Contractual (Contract of Service / Job Order)

**What it is:** Employment under a Contract of Service (COS) or Job Order (JO). These are not regular government appointments — they are service contracts.

**Contract of Service (COS):**
- A contract for a specific deliverable or project
- Duration: usually 6 months to 1 year, renewable
- You receive compensation but are **not considered a government employee** in the traditional sense
- Limited benefits — no GSIS, no security of tenure, no leave credits (though some offices provide these voluntarily)

**Job Order (JO):**
- The most basic form of government engagement
- Task-based, short-term (usually less than 6 months)
- Minimal benefits
- No employer-employee relationship in the legal sense

**The reality:** Many fresh graduates enter government through COS or JO engagements. These are not ideal — the benefits are limited and the security is low. But they offer a way to gain **government experience**, build your network, and position yourself for a permanent appointment when one opens. Think of COS/JO as the entry point, not the destination.

### Casual

**What it is:** Employment for seasonal or intermittent work. Less common in BARMM's central offices, more common in LGUs and project-based programs.

### Summary Table: Appointment Types

| Type | Security of Tenure | Full Benefits | Promotion Eligible | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Permanent** | Yes | Yes | Yes | Until retirement or separation |
| **Temporary** | No | Partial | Limited | Up to 12 months, renewable |
| **Coterminous** | No | Partial | Limited | Tied to appointing authority's term |
| **COS** | No | Minimal | No | Contract period (6-12 months) |
| **Job Order** | No | Minimal | No | Task-based (< 6 months) |
| **Casual** | No | Partial | No | Seasonal/intermittent |

**The bottom line:** Aim for a **permanent appointment**. If you start as COS or temporary, make it your goal to convert. The two things you need: meet the **qualification standards** and obtain **CSC eligibility**.

## 4.4 Qualification Standards

Every position in the government has a set of **qualification standards (QS)** — the minimum requirements you must meet to be considered. QS have four components:

### Education

The degree or level of education required. Examples:
- **SG 6-9 (clerical/sub-professional):** High school diploma or vocational course
- **SG 11-15 (professional/entry):** Bachelor's degree (sometimes in a specific field — e.g., BS Agriculture for MAFAR technical positions)
- **SG 18-22 (supervisory/specialist):** Bachelor's degree + graduate study, or a master's degree
- **SG 24+ (managerial):** Master's degree or higher

### Experience

The number of years of relevant work experience required. Entry-level professional positions (SG 11) typically require **none**. Mid-level positions (SG 18) may require **2-4 years of relevant experience**. Senior positions (SG 24+) may require **4-8 years**.

**What counts as "relevant":** Experience in the function area of the position. If you are applying for a Planning Officer III position, relevant experience means work in planning, research, data analysis, or M&E — whether in government, NGOs, or the private sector.

### Training

Hours of relevant training completed. Expressed in hours:
- Entry-level (SG 11-15): Often **none required** or 4 hours
- Mid-level (SG 18-22): **16-40 hours** of relevant training
- Senior (SG 24+): **40-120 hours** of relevant training

Training includes seminars, workshops, short courses, and certificate programs. Keep records of all training you attend — you will need them for every promotion throughout your career.

### Eligibility

**Civil Service eligibility** — the gateway requirement. The CSC administers exams that grant eligibility:

- **Career Service Professional (CSP)** — required for SG 11+ professional positions. Obtained by passing the Career Service Professional exam administered by the CSC, or through equivalent eligibility (RA 1080 for licensed professionals, PD 907 for college honor graduates, etc.)[^3]
- **Career Service Sub-Professional (CSSP)** — for SG 1-10 sub-professional positions

**Key point:** If you are a licensed professional (CPA, nurse, engineer, teacher, lawyer), your professional license may serve as your CS eligibility. This is called **RA 1080 eligibility** — bar and board exam passers are automatically granted civil service eligibility.[^4] College honor graduates (*cum laude* and above) receive eligibility through **PD 907**.[^5] Check with the CSC which licenses and honors qualify.

**If you are not yet eligible:** You can still apply for temporary appointments (the office can hire you temporarily while you prepare for the CS exam). But your career advancement will be blocked until you obtain eligibility. Take the exam seriously.

### How BARMM QS Relate to National CSC Standards

BAA 17 allows BARMM to create its own qualification standards within the CSC framework. This means:
- **CSC eligibility remains the national standard** — you still need to pass the CSC exam
- **BARMM can add requirements** relevant to the autonomous region (e.g., knowledge of Bangsamoro governance, cultural competency, language skills for specific positions)
- **BARMM cannot lower standards below CSC minimums** — the floor is national, but the ceiling can be higher

## 4.5 Performance Management

Once you are hired, your work is measured through the **Strategic Performance Management System (SPMS)**.[^6] This system connects individual performance to organizational goals, all the way up to the BDP.

### The SPMS Cascade

The system works top-down:

1. **BDP goals and targets** — the government's overall objectives
2. **Office Performance Commitment and Review (OPCR)** — each office sets annual targets aligned with BDP goals
3. **Individual Performance Commitment and Review (IPCR)** — each employee sets personal targets aligned with the OPCR

Your IPCR is your performance contract. At the beginning of each rating period, you and your supervisor agree on:
- **Performance targets** — specific, measurable outputs you will deliver
- **Success indicators** — how achievement is measured
- **Timeline** — when deliverables are due

### The Rating Cycle

| Phase | When | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| **Target-setting** | January (or start of rating period) | You and your supervisor set IPCR targets |
| **Mid-year review** | June-July | Progress check — are you on track? Adjust if needed |
| **Year-end evaluation** | December-January | Final assessment of performance against targets |
| **Rating** | After year-end evaluation | You receive a numerical rating (1-5 scale) |

### Performance Ratings

| Rating | Description | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **5 — Outstanding** | Exceeded all targets with exceptional quality | Eligible for Performance-Based Bonus (PBB) and awards |
| **4 — Very Satisfactory** | Met all targets, some exceeded | Eligible for PBB |
| **3 — Satisfactory** | Met most targets at acceptable quality | Standard — meets expectations |
| **2 — Unsatisfactory** | Failed to meet several targets | Performance improvement plan required |
| **1 — Poor** | Failed to meet most or all targets | Subject to administrative action |

**What you need to know as a new employee:** Your first rating period matters. It establishes your reputation and sets the baseline for your career. Take target-setting seriously — make sure your IPCR targets are **specific, measurable, and achievable**. Do not agree to vague targets ("improve office operations") that cannot be measured. Push for clear deliverables ("process 50 applications per quarter with 95% accuracy").

### BDP Alignment in Your IPCR

Every IPCR should reference how your work contributes to BDP goals. This is not bureaucratic box-checking — it is how the government connects individual effort to regional development. When you set your targets, ask: "Which BDP goal does this support?" Document the link.

For example:
- A budget analyst at MFBM → BDP Goal 1 (Stable, Just, and Accountable Government)
- A teacher at MBHTE → BDP Goal 4 (Inclusive, Responsive, and Quality Social Services)
- A project officer at MPW → BDP Goal 6 (Strategic, Adequate, and Climate-Resilient Infrastructure)

## 4.6 Rewards and Recognition

Good performance is recognized through several mechanisms:

### Performance-Based Bonus (PBB)

The PBB is an annual bonus given to government employees and offices that meet their performance targets. Eligibility depends on both your individual IPCR rating (at least Satisfactory) and your office's OPCR rating. If your office performs poorly as a whole, even individual high performers may not receive the PBB.

### Step Increment

A **step increment** is a salary increase within your salary grade. Each SG has 8 steps. You advance one step every **3 years of satisfactory service** (length of service increment) or for **outstanding performance** (merit increment).[^7] Step increments are automatic — but you must have at least a Satisfactory rating.

### Loyalty Award

After **10 years of continuous government service**, you are eligible for a Loyalty Award. Subsequent awards come at 5-year intervals (15, 20, 25 years, etc.). The award includes a cash bonus.[^8]

### PRAISE

The **Program on Awards and Incentives for Service Excellence (PRAISE)** is the government's formal recognition program. Each office nominates outstanding employees for awards — Employee of the Month, Employee of the Quarter, Employee of the Year, and special citations.

## 4.7 Discipline

BAA 17 and the national **2017 Rules on Administrative Cases in the Civil Service (RACCS)** govern disciplinary matters.[^9] As a government employee, you are subject to administrative discipline for violations of the Code of Conduct and other rules.

### Grounds for Disciplinary Action

Grounds for administrative cases include, among others:
- **Dishonesty** — lying, falsifying documents, misrepresenting facts
- **Neglect of duty** — failing to perform assigned tasks
- **Misconduct** — improper behavior in office
- **Habitual absenteeism or tardiness** — chronic attendance problems
- **Inefficiency and incompetence** — persistent inability to perform duties
- **Conduct prejudicial to the best interest of the service** — behavior that damages the government's reputation

### Due Process

No government employee can be disciplined without due process. This means:
- **Written notice** — you receive a formal complaint specifying the charges
- **Opportunity to respond** — you have the right to answer the charges in writing
- **Investigation** — a fact-finding process determines what happened
- **Decision** — a written decision based on evidence, with explanation
- **Appeal** — you can appeal the decision to a higher authority or the CSC

### Penalties

Penalties are graduated based on the offense and whether it is a first, second, or third occurrence:

| Severity | Examples | Penalty Range |
|---|---|---|
| **Light offenses** | Discourtesy, improper attire, minor delay in reports | Reprimand → 1-30 days suspension |
| **Less grave offenses** | Simple neglect, simple misconduct | 1-6 months suspension → dismissal (on 2nd offense) |
| **Grave offenses** | Dishonesty, grave misconduct, falsification | 6 months suspension → dismissal (even on 1st offense) |

**Key advice:** The most common disciplinary issues for new employees are **attendance-related** — tardiness, unauthorized absences, failure to follow timekeeping rules. These seem minor but accumulate quickly. Follow the attendance rules from day one.

## 4.8 Leave Benefits

Government employment comes with leave benefits that are generally better than private sector standards:

| Leave Type | Annual Entitlement | Key Rules |
|---|---|---|
| **Vacation Leave (VL)** | 15 days per year | Earned monthly (1.25 days/month). Can be accumulated. Requires advance filing. |
| **Sick Leave (SL)** | 15 days per year | Earned monthly (1.25 days/month). Can be accumulated. Requires medical certificate for 5+ consecutive days. |
| **Maternity Leave** | 105 days (RA 11210)[^10] | For female employees who give birth. Paid. |
| **Paternity Leave** | 7 days (RA 8187)[^11] | For married male employees whose legitimate spouse gives birth. |
| **Solo Parent Leave** | 7 days (RA 8972)[^12] | For qualified solo parents. |
| **Study Leave** | Up to 6 months | For employees pursuing studies relevant to their work. With pay, if approved. |
| **Special Leave for Women** | 2 months (RA 9710)[^13] | For gynecological surgery. Paid. |
| **Forced/Mandatory Leave** | 5 days per year | You must take at least 5 days of VL annually. Non-negotiable. |

**What you need to know:** Leave credits accumulate. Unused VL and SL carry over to the next year. When you retire or separate from service, your accumulated leave credits are **monetized** — paid out as terminal leave pay. This can amount to a significant sum after decades of service. Start accumulating from your first month.

## 4.9 Government Systems and Compliance

From your first week, you will encounter several systems and requirements. None of these are optional.

### SPMS — Strategic Performance Management System

Already covered in Section 4.5. Your IPCR is the central document — it defines your targets, measures your output, and determines your rating.

### SALN — Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth

Every government employee must file a **SALN** annually. This is a **constitutional requirement** (Article XI, Section 17 of the Philippine Constitution).[^14] You declare all your assets (property, bank accounts, investments), liabilities (loans, debts), and net worth.

The SALN is not a tax return — it is a transparency tool. It allows the government and the public to monitor whether your wealth is proportional to your legitimate income. Failure to file or filing a false SALN is a ground for administrative and criminal liability.

**First-time tip:** File your SALN honestly and on time. The biggest mistake new employees make is forgetting to file or treating it as unimportant. It is one of the most legally consequential documents you will sign.

### Attendance and Timekeeping

Government offices follow prescribed working hours. Timekeeping is typically done through biometric systems or daily time records (DTRs). The standard work schedule is **8:00 AM to 5:00 PM, Monday to Friday**, though some offices have flexible work arrangements.

**Tardiness and absences** are tracked and can result in disciplinary action. The rules are strict: habitual tardiness (reporting late 10 or more times in a month) and habitual absenteeism (unauthorized absences for 3 or more days in a month or at least 5 consecutive days) are grounds for administrative cases.

### ARTA Compliance — Citizen's Charter

Under the **Anti-Red Tape Act (ARTA)**, originally enacted as RA 9485 (2007) and strengthened by RA 11032 (2018),[^15] every government office must maintain a **Citizen's Charter** — a document listing all services the office provides, the steps to access each service, the processing time, and the responsible person. If your office delivers services to the public, you may be involved in maintaining and implementing this charter.

### Government Benefits Enrollment

Upon appointment, you will be enrolled in three mandatory benefit systems:

- **GSIS (Government Service Insurance System)** — your retirement, insurance, and loan provider
- **PhilHealth** — health insurance
- **Pag-IBIG (HDMF)** — housing and savings fund

Monthly contributions are deducted from your salary. These are not optional — they are mandatory for all government employees. In exchange, you get retirement benefits, loan access, health coverage, and housing fund accumulation.

## 4.10 The Code of Conduct

Two layers of ethical standards govern your behavior as a BARMM government worker:

### RA 6713 — Code of Conduct and Ethical Standards for Public Officials and Employees[^16]

This is the national standard. It requires all government employees to:
- Act with professionalism, justness, and sincerity
- Be accountable to the people
- Respond to public needs promptly and adequately
- Disclose conflicts of interest
- Avoid using office resources for personal gain
- Maintain a simple and modest lifestyle

### Moral Governance Overlay

On top of RA 6713, BARMM applies the **12 Pillars of Moral Governance** (covered in Chapter 1). This adds a faith-informed, culturally grounded ethical dimension that is unique to the Bangsamoro government. In practice, this means:

- **Integrity and amanah (trust)** — you are a trustee of public resources, not an owner
- **Accountability and shura (consultation)** — decisions are made through consultation, not unilateral authority
- **Justice and adl** — fairness in all dealings, regardless of personal relationships

You do not need to be Muslim to serve in BARMM. But you do need to understand and respect these governance principles. They will come up in orientation, in performance discussions, and in the day-to-day culture of the workplace.

## 4.11 The CSC and BARMM

The relationship between the **Civil Service Commission (CSC)** and the Bangsamoro government can be confusing. Here is the short version:

**The CSC is a national constitutional body.** It has jurisdiction over all civil service matters in the Philippines, including BARMM.[^21]

**BAA 17 creates a Bangsamoro civil service system** within the CSC framework. This means:
- **CSC eligibility exams** are still the standard — you take the same exam as any other government applicant in the Philippines
- **CSC rules on appointments, promotions, and discipline** apply unless BAA 17 provides otherwise
- **BARMM has its own HRMPSB** — the merit selection board that evaluates applicants within BARMM
- **BARMM can set additional qualification standards** beyond the CSC minimums, tailored to the needs of the autonomous region

**The practical takeaway:** Take the CSC exam. Without it, your career options are limited. The exam is offered multiple times per year in testing centers across Mindanao. Study for it the way you would study for a board exam — it covers vocabulary, grammar, mathematics, and general information about Philippine government and the Constitution.

## 4.12 Other Key Civil Service Issuances

Several CSC issuances affect your daily work. You do not need to memorize them, but you should know they exist:

| Issuance | Subject | What It Means for You |
|---|---|---|
| **CSC MC No. 14, s. 2018**[^17] | Omnibus Rules on Appointments | The detailed rules governing how appointments are made — posting, evaluation, attestation |
| **2017 RACCS (CSC Res. No. 1701077)**[^18] | Rules on Administrative Cases in the Civil Service | The disciplinary framework — complaints, investigation, penalties, appeals |
| **OP MC No. 72, s. 2019 and OP MC No. 2, s. 2022**[^19] | Complete Staff Work (CSW) | The standard for professional staff work — your memos, briefers, and recommendations must be complete, accurate, and actionable |
| **Various EOs** | Government working hours, flexible work | Rules on work schedules, telecommuting, and flexible work arrangements |

**Complete Staff Work (CSW)** deserves special attention. CSW is the principle that every document you submit to a decision-maker must be so thorough and well-prepared that the decision-maker only needs to approve or disapprove — no further research, no missing data, no incomplete analysis. CSW is the professional standard that separates competent government workers from mediocre ones. Chapters 11-14 of this guidebook will teach you how to do it.

## 4.13 Salary and Compensation

Government salaries follow the **Salary Standardization Law (SSL)**, which assigns salary amounts to each Salary Grade (SG 1-33).[^20] All government employees at the same SG receive the same base pay, regardless of where they work.

### Entry-Level Salary Ranges (SSL Step 1, approximate)

| Salary Grade | Monthly Base Pay (approx.) | Typical Entry Positions |
|---|---|---|
| **SG 6** | ~PHP 18,000 | Administrative Aide VI (clerical) |
| **SG 9** | ~PHP 22,000 | Administrative Assistant III |
| **SG 11** | ~PHP 27,000 | Professional entry — LSO I, Admin Officer I, Project Dev't Officer I |
| **SG 13** | ~PHP 30,000 | LSO II, Admin Officer III, DMO II |
| **SG 15** | ~PHP 38,000 | LSO III, Senior Staff positions |

### Beyond Base Pay

Your actual take-home pay is higher than the base salary. Additional compensation includes:
- **COLA (Cost of Living Allowance)** — PHP 2,000/month
- **PERA (Personnel Economic Relief Allowance)** — PHP 2,000/month
- **Clothing/Uniform Allowance** — PHP 6,000/year
- **Mid-Year Bonus** — equivalent to 1 month salary (May)
- **Year-End Bonus** — equivalent to 1 month salary (November)
- **Cash Gift** — PHP 5,000 (November)
- **Performance-Based Bonus** — variable, based on office and individual performance rating

**An honest comparison:** A fresh graduate at SG 11 earns approximately PHP 27,000/month base pay. With allowances and bonuses, the annualized compensation is higher. But in the private sector — especially in Manila — a comparable graduate might earn PHP 25,000-40,000/month with faster salary progression. The trade-off is job security, benefits (GSIS retirement), and the non-monetary value of public service. Make this trade-off consciously.

## 4.14 Your First 90 Days: What to Expect

Here is what typically happens during your first three months as a new government employee in BARMM:

**Week 1: Orientation and Onboarding**
- Report to your office. Meet your supervisor and colleagues.
- Complete enrollment forms for GSIS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG.
- Receive your office's organizational chart, Citizen's Charter, and any orientation materials.
- Get access to your workspace, email, and any office systems.
- Some offices conduct formal orientation programs. Others simply assign you a desk and a task. Be ready for either.

**Week 2-4: Learning the Systems**
- Begin learning office processes, filing systems, and reporting requirements.
- Draft your first IPCR targets with your supervisor's guidance.
- Start building relationships with colleagues — especially those who have been in the office longest.
- Observe how memos, letters, and reports are formatted. Every office has unwritten format standards.
- File your SALN if required upon assumption of duty.

**Month 2: Building Competence**
- You should be handling routine tasks independently by now — processing documents, drafting correspondence, maintaining records.
- Attend any available training — orientation on SPMS, CSW, ARTA compliance, or technical skills relevant to your office.
- Ask questions. Lots of them. There is no penalty for asking; there is a penalty for guessing wrong.

**Month 3: Contributing Productively**
- You should be producing work that contributes to your IPCR targets.
- Your supervisor should have a clear picture of your strengths and development areas.
- Start thinking about your career trajectory — what skills do you need to develop? What training should you pursue?

**The unwritten rule:** In BARMM government offices, the culture tends to be relationship-oriented. Making an effort to get to know your colleagues — their names, their roles, their working styles — is not socializing. It is essential to getting your work done. You will need their cooperation, their institutional knowledge, and their willingness to help you navigate a young bureaucracy that does not always have written procedures for everything.
