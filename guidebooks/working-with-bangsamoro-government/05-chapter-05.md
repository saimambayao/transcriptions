# Chapter 5: The Bangsamoro Development Plan — What You're Working Toward

Every memo you write, every briefer you draft, every program you implement serves one plan. Know it.

---

If you have ever wondered why the Bangsamoro government exists, the answer is in the **2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP) 2023-2028**.[^1] This document is the government's roadmap. It tells every ministry, every office, every staff member what BARMM is trying to achieve and how it plans to get there. When you join the government, your daily work connects to this plan. Your performance targets trace back to it. Your budget proposals must justify spending against it. The BDP is not a shelf document. It is the operating system.

This chapter gives you the essentials. You do not need to memorize all 14 chapters. But you do need to understand the goals, the strategies, the targets, and the ethical framework. When an interviewer asks "What do you know about the BDP?" you should be able to answer with substance.

---

## 5.1 What the BDP Is

The **2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan** covers the period **2023 to 2028**.[^2] It is the successor to the 1st BDP (2020-2022), which guided BARMM through its initial years of institution-building. The **Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA)** led the formulation of the plan in coordination with all 15 ministries, attached agencies, and development partners.[^3]

The BDP is organized into **14 chapters** covering governance, economy, peace and security, social services, culture, infrastructure, and cross-cutting concerns.[^4] It is publicly available through the BPDA website and the BARMM Official Gazette.

**Why the BDP matters to you personally:**

- Every **ministry and office** aligns its Annual Work and Financial Plan to the BDP
- Every **Individual Performance Commitment and Review (IPCR)** should reference BDP alignment
- Every **policy recommendation** you write should cite the relevant BDP chapter
- Every **budget proposal** must justify spending against BDP targets
- During **job interviews**, knowledge of the BDP demonstrates strategic awareness that most applicants lack

---

## 5.2 The Overall Goal

The BDP's overall goal is:

> **An empowered, cohesive, and progressive Bangsamoro.**[^5]

Each word carries specific meaning:[^6]

| Term | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Empowered** | Improved capacity of communities through enhanced access to quality basic social services and widened economic opportunities via institution building and service delivery |
| **Cohesive** | Improved relationships between stakeholders evidenced in reduced violent conflict and forged partnerships in governance and development |
| **Progressive** | Sustained ability to optimize existing and potential resources addressing community development needs and realizing economic and human potential |

When someone says "empowered Bangsamoro," they are not using a slogan. They are referencing a measurable outcome: communities that have access to quality services and economic opportunity because the government built the institutions to deliver them.

## 5.3 The Vision and Mission

### The Vision

> "A Bangsamoro that is united, enlightened, self-governing, peaceful, just, morally upright, and progressive."[^7]

This vision is long-term. It extends beyond the 2028 plan period. Key elements like peace, unity, and justice remain relevant as desired states the Bangsamoro people continue to work toward.

### The Mission

> "Guided by moral governance and in pursuit of genuine and meaningful autonomy, the Bangsamoro government ensures the necessary conditions for enduring peace and sustained socioeconomic development suitable to the systems of life, needs, and aspirations of its people by providing services to communities, ensuring multi-stakeholder participation, and facilitating appropriate partnership."[^8]

Two phrases in the mission statement deserve your attention:

- **"Systems of life"** -- this means development that fits the Bangsamoro people's way of living, not development imposed from outside
- **"Multi-stakeholder participation"** -- the government does not work alone. It partners with communities, civil society, the private sector, and development partners

## 5.4 The Six Development Goals

These six goals define what BARMM is working toward. Every office in the government contributes to at least one of them.[^9]

### Goal 1: Stable, Just, and Accountable Government

**What it means:**

- **Stable** -- the government continues to function during emergencies, elections, or changes in power. Ministries deliver services even during shocks.
- **Just** -- the government is the primary agent of economic and social justice. Leaders exercise power to advance the public interest, not personal gain.
- **Accountable** -- institutions, leaders, and staff are held responsible for how they use government resources and powers. Accountability frameworks and systems are in place.

**Which offices work on this:** All ministries (governance is everyone's job), plus MFBM (fiscal accountability), the Bangsamoro Internal Auditing Body (BIAB),[^24] and the Office of the Chief Minister (OCM).

**What your work might look like:** Preparing compliance reports, developing standard operating procedures, processing financial documents with proper documentation, supporting performance evaluations.

### Goal 2: Equitable, Competitive, and Robust Economy

**What it means:**

- **Equitable** -- economic gains benefit all, with special consideration for the poor and marginalized. Key inequality indicators (Gini coefficient, poverty gap) improve.
- **Competitive** -- BARMM enjoys comparative advantage in selected commodities (seaweed, cassava, rubber, palm oil) with optimized local and international market linkages.[^25]
- **Robust** -- sustained growth over six years with a diversified, stabilized economy that creates quality jobs.

**Which offices work on this:** MTIT (trade and tourism), MAFAR (agriculture and fisheries), MOLE (labor and employment), BBOI (investment promotion), MOST (science and technology).

**What your work might look like:** Analyzing trade data, supporting cooperative registration, drafting investment incentive policies, coordinating livelihood programs, processing business permits.

### Goal 3: Peaceful, Safe, and Resilient Bangsamoro Communities

**What it means:**

- Achieve lasting peace through ensuring security and intensifying peace-building efforts
- Create safe communities by protecting Bangsamoro people
- Build resilient communities that can withstand natural calamities and human-induced disasters

**Which offices work on this:** MPOS (public order and safety), MILG (interior and local government), the Bangsamoro Regional Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Office (BRDRRMO), OCM peace and normalization units.

**What your work might look like:** Supporting peace and order councils, preparing disaster preparedness plans, coordinating with local governments on community safety, processing normalization program documents.

### Goal 4: Inclusive, Responsive, and Quality Social Services

**What it means:**

- Social services are **inclusive** -- available to all sectors regardless of tribe, religion, or political affiliation
- Services are **responsive** -- they address the actual needs of communities, not just what the government decides to provide
- Services are **quality** -- they meet standards that improve lives

**Which offices work on this:** MBHTE (education), MOH (health), MSSD (social services), MHSD (human settlements), MOLE (employment services).

**What your work might look like:** Tracking school enrollment and dropout rates, processing beneficiary lists for social protection programs, coordinating health service delivery in municipalities, supporting scholarship program administration.

### Goal 5: Rich and Diverse Bangsamoro Culture and Identity Preserved and Recognized

**What it means:**

- The Bangsamoro exercises its powers to maintain and promote its rich, diverse culture and identity
- Mandated under Section 24, Article IX of the BOL[^10] -- cultural preservation is not optional. It is a constitutional responsibility.
- The BOL recognizes that Bangsamoro identity is the primary cause of the Bangsamoro struggle. Preserving it is inseparable from the peace process.

**Which offices work on this:** BCPCH (cultural heritage preservation), MIPA (indigenous peoples' affairs), MBHTE (cultural education), the Office of the Wali (moral and cultural authority).

**What your work might look like:** Documenting cultural heritage sites, supporting indigenous peoples' rights programs, developing culturally responsive curricula, coordinating cultural events and festivals.

### Goal 6: Strategic, Adequate, and Climate-Resilient Infrastructure

**What it means:**

- **Strategic** -- infrastructure that has broad-based, inclusive effects on industries and communities (not bridges to nowhere)
- **Adequate** -- infrastructure that meets the demands of communities and industries (not token projects)
- **Climate-resilient** -- infrastructure that withstands calamities induced by climate change

**Which offices work on this:** MPW (public works), MOTC (transportation and communications), MENRE (environment and natural resources), MHSD (human settlements).

**What your work might look like:** Processing infrastructure project proposals, monitoring construction progress, preparing environmental compliance documents, coordinating road network plans with LGUs.

## 5.5 The Eight Development Strategies

The six goals tell you **what** BARMM wants to achieve. The eight strategies tell you **how**.[^11]

1. **Improve and strengthen governance mechanisms** for a stable, just, and accountable Bangsamoro government
2. **Create an enabling environment** towards an equitable, competitive, and robust economy
3. **Harness technology and innovations** to increase socioeconomic opportunities and improve government services
4. **Improve ecological integrity and resilience** of communities and ensure a healthy and clean environment
5. **Enhance and strengthen peace, public order, safety, and security** and uphold human rights for inclusive, peaceful, progressive, and sustainable development
6. **Ensure inclusive and equitable access** to quality services for social justice and human capital development
7. **Mainstream Bangsamoro cultural diversity**, beliefs, heritage, and identity
8. **Scale up functional, strategic, climate-resilient, and quality infrastructure** to support sustainable socioeconomic development

Notice how the strategies map to the goals:

| Strategy | Primary Goal Served |
|---|---|
| Strategy 1 (Governance) | Goal 1 (Stable government) |
| Strategy 2 (Economy) | Goal 2 (Robust economy) |
| Strategy 3 (Technology) | Goals 1, 2, 4 (cross-cutting) |
| Strategy 4 (Environment) | Goal 3 (Resilient communities) |
| Strategy 5 (Peace and security) | Goal 3 (Peaceful communities) |
| Strategy 6 (Social services) | Goal 4 (Quality services) |
| Strategy 7 (Culture) | Goal 5 (Culture preserved) |
| Strategy 8 (Infrastructure) | Goal 6 (Climate-resilient infrastructure) |

Strategy 3 -- **harnessing technology and innovations** -- is cross-cutting. It applies to governance (digital government services), economy (technology-enabled agriculture and trade), and social services (e-learning, telemedicine). This means every office, not just MOST, has a role in technology adoption.

## 5.6 The Twelve Pillars of Moral Governance

The BDP is not only about economic targets and infrastructure. It is grounded in an ethical framework called the **Twelve Pillars of Moral Governance**.[^12] These pillars are the centerpiece of BARMM's leadership and governance for 2023-2028. They are cited in performance evaluations, referenced in legislation, and expected in daily conduct.

1. **Integrity** -- Avoid conflicts of interest in executing official duties. Those in positions of public trust should not bind themselves in any manner, whether financially or otherwise.

2. **Accountability** -- Be open and transparent about decisions and actions. Document decisions and limit information only when the larger public interest clearly requires it.

3. **Transparency and Honesty** -- Declare private interests related to public duties. Resolve conflicts in a way that protects the public interest. All public transactions should be made public.

4. **Sense of Patriotism** -- Invest in the reputation and integrity of the Bangsamoro. Protect the community's achievements, history, and way of life.

5. **Striving for Excellence** -- Uphold the legitimacy of all actions. Deliver the highest quality in everything, prioritizing process over results for the good of all.

6. **Cultural Understanding and Tolerance** -- Gain better understanding of socioeconomic differences between societies. Appreciate the variety within the Bangsamoro.

7. **Unwavering Loyalty to God** -- Strict adherence to rules and regulations as a result of devotion to God. This pillar reflects the Islamic foundation of Bangsamoro governance.

8. **Justice** -- Serve the public without prejudice. Equity should triumph without regard for oneself, family, companions, or political alliances.

9. **Inclusivity** -- Foster welcoming environments free from discrimination based on religion, ethnicity, or culture. Appreciate the region's identity-building and cultural characteristics.

10. **Objectivity** -- Make merit-based decisions in public business, including appointments, contracts, and awards. Decide solely in the public interest.

11. **Dialogue and Meaningful Engagement** -- Consult the community. Engage in meaningful dialogue to identify needs and achieve positive change.

12. **Balance Between Practical Life and Moral Side** -- Balance practical and ethical values in carrying out government duties. Never deviate from the moral aspect of public service.

**What the pillars mean for you as a new employee:**

These are not decorative values. When your supervisor evaluates your performance, they may reference these pillars. When the HRMPSB interviews you, they may ask what moral governance means to you. When you face an ethical dilemma at work -- a colleague asks you to backdate a document, a politician pressures you to approve an unqualified applicant, a contractor offers you a "gift" -- these pillars are your guide.

The most practical pillar for daily work is **Pillar 10: Objectivity**. It says decisions must be merit-based. This applies to everything from hiring (the HRMPSB process) to procurement (competitive bidding) to budget allocation (BDP alignment). When in doubt, ask: "Is this decision based on merit and public interest?"

## 5.7 Macroeconomic Targets

The BDP sets measurable targets for the Bangsamoro economy. These numbers appear in budget proposals, program evaluations, and policy briefs. You should know the key ones.[^13]

| Indicator | Baseline | End-of-Plan Target (2028) |
|---|---|---|
| **GRDP Annual Average Growth Rate** | 7.5% (2020-2021) | 8-9% for six years |
| **GRDP Per Capita Growth Rate** | 6.4% (2020-2021) | 6-9% |
| **Industry/Services Real Growth** | Industry 7.9%, Services 6.6% | 10-12% (double-digit) |
| **Gross Capital Formation (% of GRDP)** | 16.91% (2021) | 18-20% by 2028 |
| **Inflation Rate** | 2.40% (2021) | 2-4% |
| **Unemployment Rate** | 3.80% (2021) | 3-5% |
| **Underemployment Rate** | 9.0% (2020) | 4-7% |
| **Poverty Incidence** | 29.80% (2021) | 20-25% by 2028 |

Source: 2nd BDP, Chapter 4, Table 4.1 (PSA Reports)[^14]

**Key assumptions behind these targets:**

- No sustained large-scale armed conflict in the plan period
- Significant progress in peace and normalization
- Continued recovery from the pandemic
- Government expenditure as an economic stimulus ("pump-priming")
- Passage of priority codes (Revenue Code, Investment Code) to create a predictable investment climate

**What these numbers mean for your work:**

If you work in **MAFAR**, your programs contribute to GRDP growth through agricultural productivity. If you work in **MOLE**, your employment programs help maintain the unemployment rate within target. If you work in **MSSD**, your social protection programs reduce poverty incidence. If you work in **BPDA**, you monitor all these indicators and report progress. Every ministry's work connects to at least one macroeconomic target.

## 5.8 What the 1st BDP Achieved

The 1st BDP (2020-2022) set six macroeconomic targets. Five were achieved:[^15]

| Target | Result |
|---|---|
| GRDP Growth Rate | 7.5% -- **Achieved** |
| Population Growth | 3.26% -- **Achieved** |
| Labor Force Participation | 60% -- **Exceeded** (target was 46-50%) |
| Unemployment | 7.60% -- **Not Met** (target was 3%) |
| Inflation | 2.40% -- **Achieved** |
| Poverty Incidence (families) | 29.80% -- **Exceeded** (target was 49.6%) |

The poverty reduction from 54.20% (2018) to 29.80% (2021) was the most significant achievement -- a **24.4 percentage point reduction** in three years.[^16] However, unemployment remained above target, and the COVID-19 pandemic disrupted economic activity.

The 2nd BDP builds on these gains while addressing persistent challenges: underemployment, infrastructure gaps, education quality, and health service access.

## 5.9 Vertical Alignment -- How the BDP Connects to National and International Plans

The BDP does not exist in isolation. It aligns vertically with higher-level plans:

- **National**: Philippine Development Plan 2023-2028 and Ambisyon Natin 2040[^17]
- **Regional**: Mindanao Development Agenda[^26]
- **International**: United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)[^27]
- **BARMM-specific**: Enhanced 12-Point Priority Agenda[^18]

This alignment matters because BARMM receives an **Annual Block Grant** from the national government (equal to 5% of net national internal revenue)[^19] and a **Special Development Fund** (PHP 5 billion per year).[^20] The national government expects BARMM's spending to align with national priorities. Development partners (UNDP, World Bank, JICA, USAID) align their support with the SDGs. When you write a project proposal, showing alignment with both the BDP and the SDGs strengthens your case.

## 5.10 The Enhanced 12-Point Priority Agenda

The **Enhanced 12-Point Priority Agenda** is the Chief Minister's implementation framework within the BDP.[^21] It translates the BDP's broad goals into specific priorities for the transition period.

| Priority | Focus | Key Offices |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Bureaucracy | Strengthen the regional bureaucracy | All MOAs |
| 2. Priority Codes | Enact the 7 priority codes | Parliament, Legal offices |
| 3. Revenue Generation | Increase revenue and economic advantages | MFBM, BBOI, MTIT |
| 4. Social Services | Education, health, social welfare | MBHTE, MOH, MSSD |
| 5. Infrastructure | Strategic infrastructure development | MPW, MOTC |
| 6. Economic Development | Inclusive economic growth | MTIT, MAFAR, MOLE |
| 7. Environment | Natural resources protection | MENRE |
| 8. Disaster Resilience | Community resilience | MILG, BRDRRMO |
| 9. Peace and Normalization | Peace agreement provisions | OCM, MPOS |
| 10. Marawi Rehabilitation | Marawi City recovery | Task Force Bangon Marawi, MHSD |
| 11. Peace and Security | Sustain peace, justice, security | MPOS, BHRC |
| 12. Cultural Heritage | Preserve Bangsamoro culture | BCPCH, MIPA |

When you research a prospective office, identify which of the 12 priorities it supports. This gives you a concrete answer when an interviewer asks: "How does this office's work connect to the Chief Minister's agenda?"

## 5.11 How to Connect YOUR Work to the BDP

Here is the chain of connection:

```
BDP Goal → Development Strategy → Ministry/Office Mandate → Office Work Plan → Your IPCR
```

**Example 1 -- Education Staff at MBHTE:**
- **BDP Goal 4**: Inclusive, responsive, and quality social services
- **Strategy 6**: Ensure inclusive and equitable access to quality services
- **MBHTE Mandate** (BAA 13):[^22] Comprehensive education system
- **Office Work Plan**: Increase school enrollment in underserved barangays
- **Your IPCR Target**: Process 200 AHME scholarship applications per quarter

**Example 2 -- Legislative Staff at Parliament:**
- **BDP Goal 1**: Stable, just, and accountable government
- **Strategy 1**: Improve and strengthen governance mechanisms
- **Parliament Mandate** (BOL Art. VII):[^23] Legislate for the Bangsamoro
- **Committee Work Plan**: Draft and deliberate priority codes
- **Your IPCR Target**: Prepare 4 legislative briefers per quarter on referred bills

**Example 3 -- Planning Staff at BPDA:**
- **BDP Goal 1**: Stable, just, and accountable government (cross-cutting)
- **Strategy 1**: Improve and strengthen governance mechanisms
- **BPDA Mandate**: Coordinate BDP implementation, monitor progress
- **Office Work Plan**: Conduct mid-term review of BDP performance
- **Your IPCR Target**: Collect and consolidate progress reports from 4 assigned ministries per quarter

**How to use BDP alignment in your application:**

In your cover letter, write one sentence connecting your target office's work to a specific BDP goal. In your interview, when asked "Why this office?" reference the BDP. This demonstrates strategic awareness that separates you from applicants who have not read the plan.

## Key Takeaways

- The **2nd BDP (2023-2028)** is the government's roadmap. All offices align to it.
- The overall goal is an **empowered, cohesive, and progressive Bangsamoro**.
- Know the **6 development goals** -- they define what your work contributes to.
- Know the **8 development strategies** -- they define how goals become action.
- The **12 Pillars of Moral Governance** are not aspirational. They are the ethical standard you will be held to.
- The **macroeconomic targets** (GRDP 8-9%, poverty 20-25%, unemployment 3-5%) are the numbers your work is measured against.
- Connect your work to the BDP in your cover letter and interview. It separates informed applicants from uninformed ones.
