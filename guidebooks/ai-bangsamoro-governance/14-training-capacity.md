# Chapter 14 — Training, Capacity Building, and Sustaining AI Skills

You can buy the best AI tools on the market. You can write a comprehensive AI use policy. You can assign an AI Champion and build a knowledge base. None of it matters if your staff cannot use AI effectively — and keep using it as the technology evolves.

Training is where AI adoption either takes root or withers. Not a single workshop. Not a one-time orientation. A sustained, structured investment in human capacity that treats AI literacy the same way government treats financial management or records management: as a **core competency** that every staff member must develop and maintain.

This chapter covers how to build that capacity. It starts with why AI literacy belongs in every government employee's competency framework. It provides a training curriculum you can adapt. It defines the supervisor's specific role in sustaining AI adoption. It shows you how to build communities of practice that outlast any single champion. And it gives you metrics to prove that your investment in AI capacity is producing results.

---

## 14.1 AI Literacy as a Core Government Competency

### The Competency Gap

BARMM government offices invest in training. Every ministry has a training budget. The Development Academy of the Bangsamoro (DAB) exists specifically to build civil service capacity.[^1] Annual training plans include leadership development, technical skills, financial management, and public administration.

But AI literacy does not appear in any of these training plans. It is not listed in the competency standards for any plantilla position. No civil service eligibility examination tests it. No performance evaluation measures it. The result is a workforce that is trained in every dimension of government work except the one that is changing most rapidly.

**This gap is not unique to BARMM.** It exists in almost every government worldwide. But BARMM has a particular urgency. The transition period demands maximum productivity from a still-growing workforce. Every unfilled plantilla position increases the workload on existing staff. Every BAA the Parliament enacts creates new implementation requirements. Every new ministry directive generates cascading documentation demands. AI does not eliminate these pressures. But AI-literate staff handle them more effectively than AI-illiterate staff.[^2]

### What AI Literacy Means for Government

**AI literacy is not programming.** It is not data science. It is not computer engineering. For a government employee, AI literacy means the ability to:

1. **Identify tasks that AI can assist with.** Knowing which parts of your work involve information processing that AI handles well (summarization, comparison, drafting, analysis) and which parts require human judgment that AI cannot replace (negotiation, stakeholder relationships, political sensitivity, ethical decisions).
2. **Write effective prompts.** Providing clear instructions with sufficient context, in the format the AI tool needs, to produce useful output. This is the skill covered in Chapters 2 and 3.
3. **Evaluate AI outputs critically.** Recognizing when AI output is accurate and useful, when it is plausible but wrong, and when it is fabricated. This skill is not taught by the AI tool. It is taught by subject-matter expertise combined with a healthy skepticism.
4. **Work within ethical and policy boundaries.** Knowing what data you can share with AI tools, what outputs require human review, and how to disclose AI assistance appropriately. This is the skill covered in Chapter 12.
5. **Adapt as tools evolve.** AI tools change rapidly. A staff member who learned Claude in January will encounter a different Claude by December. AI literacy includes the ability to learn new tools and adjust workflows without starting from zero.

### Placing AI Literacy in the Competency Framework

Every BARMM position has a set of competency requirements — technical competencies specific to the role and core competencies required across all positions. AI literacy should be added to the **core competency set** — the competencies expected of every government employee regardless of position.

**Table 14.1: AI Literacy Competency Levels**

| Level | Title | Description | Target Positions |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Basic** | AI-Aware | Can use AI for simple tasks (drafting, summarizing). Understands basic prompting. Knows the office AI policy. | All staff, SG 1-10 |
| **Intermediate** | AI-Proficient | Can use AI for complex tasks (analysis, cross-referencing, research synthesis). Maintains prompts and knowledge bases. Can train peers. | Technical staff, SG 11-18 |
| **Advanced** | AI-Strategic | Can design AI-integrated workflows. Can evaluate AI tools for institutional adoption. Can develop AI use policies and training programs. | Division chiefs, directors, SG 19+ |

This three-level framework maps to the three maturity levels in Chapter 13. Basic competency supports Level 1 (individual use). Intermediate competency supports Level 2 (team integration). Advanced competency supports Level 3 (institutional adoption).

## 14.2 Designing AI Training Programs for Government Staff

### Principles for Government AI Training

Government staff are not university students. They are working professionals with full task loads, competing priorities, and limited patience for training that does not connect to their daily work. Design your training accordingly.

**Seven principles for effective government AI training:**

1. **Use real tasks, not hypothetical exercises.** Every training activity should involve a document, process, or task that participants actually encounter in their work. A legislative researcher should practice summarizing an actual committee hearing transcript. A budget analyst should practice analyzing actual budget allocation data. Hypothetical scenarios produce hypothetical skills.
2. **Start with quick wins.** The first training session must produce a tangible result that participants can use immediately. If a staff member walks out of a two-hour session and uses AI successfully on a real task that afternoon, they will come back for more. If they walk out with abstract knowledge and no practical application, they will not.
3. **Separate orientation from skill building.** Orientation answers "what is AI and why does it matter?" Skill building answers "how do I use AI for my specific work?" Do not combine them. Orientation is a one-time event. Skill building is ongoing.
4. **Match training to role.** Do not put legislative researchers and administrative officers in the same advanced session. Their tasks differ. Their prompts differ. Their knowledge bases differ. Shared orientation is fine. Role-specific skill building is better.
5. **Include ethical and cultural components.** Every training session should include time for discussing the Moral Governance pillars and Islamic principles from Chapter 12. AI literacy without ethical literacy is incomplete.
6. **Build in practice time.** A training session that is 80% lecture and 20% practice produces 20% competency. Flip the ratio. Aim for 30% instruction, 70% guided practice.
7. **Plan for follow-up.** A single workshop without follow-up produces a spike of interest that decays within two weeks. Schedule follow-up sessions at 2 weeks, 4 weeks, and 8 weeks after the initial training.

### The Three-Day AI Training Curriculum

The curriculum below is designed for a ministry, attached agency, or parliamentary secretariat running its first formal AI training. It covers three days, each with a distinct focus. Adjust the timing to your office's constraints — the content can be compressed into two full days or expanded to four half-days.

**Table 14.2: Three-Day AI Training Curriculum**

| Day | Theme | Sessions | Duration | Key Activities |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Day 1** | **AI Literacy and First Prompts** | Session 1: What AI Is and What It Is Not | 1.5 hours | Lecture + Q&A. Dispel misconceptions (Chapter 2, Section 2.6). Show live AI demo with BARMM example. |
| | | Session 2: The Four-Step AI Workflow | 1.5 hours | Teach Prepare-Prompt-Review-Refine (Chapter 3). Participants write their first prompt using a real task from their desk. |
| | | Session 3: Hands-On Practice | 2 hours | Each participant runs three prompts on tasks from their own workload. Facilitator circulates and coaches. Group shares results. |
| | | Session 4: Ethics and Responsible AI | 1 hour | Moral Governance pillars applied to AI (Chapter 12). Data classification exercise. Discussion: when must AI NOT be used? |
| **Day 2** | **AI for Your Governance Function** | Session 5: Role-Specific AI Applications | 2 hours | Breakout groups by function (legislative, budget, planning, admin, M&E). Each group works through prompts specific to their role. Uses prompt starters from Table 13.2. |
| | | Session 6: Building and Using a Knowledge Base | 1.5 hours | Live setup: create a Claude Project or NotebookLM notebook. Participants upload their team's core documents. Test prompts with and without the knowledge base. Compare outputs. |
| | | Session 7: Quality Assurance and Verification | 1.5 hours | Exercise: identify errors in AI-generated outputs. Participants receive an AI-drafted briefer with deliberate errors (wrong BAA number, misquoted provision, fabricated statistic). They must find and correct every error. |
| | | Session 8: Prompt Engineering for Complex Tasks | 1 hour | Advanced prompting: chain-of-thought, multi-step analysis, cross-referencing documents. Participants attempt one complex prompt from Appendix A. |

| | | Session 11: The 90-Day Plan | 1.5 hours | Each team adapts the 90-Day AI Adoption Plan (Chapter 13, Table 13.3) to their office. Identify pilot tasks, assign AI Champion, set review dates. |
| | | Session 12: Closing and Commitments | 30 minutes | Each participant states one commitment: "Within two weeks, I will use AI to [specific task]." Written commitments collected. Follow-up scheduled. |

### Training Logistics for BARMM Contexts

**Venue considerations.** AI training requires internet access and personal devices (laptops or smartphones). If your office lacks reliable internet, conduct the training at a venue that has it. Cotabato City hotels and the DAB training facility are common options. For island province offices, check whether the provincial capitol or a BARMM satellite office has adequate connectivity.

**Device requirements.** Participants need their own devices. AI tools work on smartphones, tablets, and laptops. If some participants lack personal devices, pair them with a colleague during hands-on sessions. Do not let device access become a barrier to training.

**Facilitator qualifications.** The facilitator must have at least intermediate AI competency (as defined in Table 14.1). They must also have BARMM government experience. A technically skilled facilitator who does not understand government workflows will produce generic training. A government-savvy facilitator who is new to AI will lack credibility during troubleshooting. Prioritize facilitators who have both.

**Language.** Conduct training in the language your participants work in — typically Filipino or English, sometimes both. The AI tools work in English, Filipino, and can accept prompts in either. Do not force participants to prompt in English if they think and work in Filipino. Show them that AI responds to Filipino prompts effectively.

## 14.3 The Supervisor's Role in AI Adoption

### Why Supervisors Are the Bottleneck

The Supervision and Supervisory Development Guidebook establishes that supervisors are the bridge between policy and practice.[^3] When the ministry issues a new directive, the division chief translates it into team action. When a training program ends, the section head determines whether new skills are applied or forgotten.

AI adoption follows the same pattern. **The supervisor determines whether AI training becomes AI practice.** A supportive supervisor creates space for staff to experiment, asks about AI use in weekly check-ins, and celebrates early wins. An indifferent supervisor allows AI skills to atrophy. A hostile supervisor actively discourages adoption.

The Supervision Guidebook's Performance Management Cycle — Plan, Monitor, Evaluate, Improve — applies directly to AI adoption.[^4] The supervisor does not need to be the team's best AI user. They need to manage the adoption process the same way they manage any performance improvement initiative.

### The Supervisor's AI Adoption Checklist

Use this checklist during the first 90 days of your team's AI adoption. Each item is a specific action, not a vague aspiration.

**Table 14.3: Supervisor's AI Adoption Checklist**

| Week | Action | How to Do It | Done? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Week 1** | Communicate support publicly | In a team meeting, state: "I want our team to explore AI tools for our work. I support experimentation. Mistakes during learning are expected and acceptable." | [ ] |
| **Week 2** | Identify the AI Champion | Select a staff member who meets the criteria in Chapter 13, Section 13.6. Discuss the role with them. Agree on protected time (4-6 hours/week). | [ ] |
| **Week 3** | Approve pilot task selection | Review the three pilot tasks chosen by the team. Confirm they are appropriate: recurring, document-heavy, low-risk. Remove any that are too politically sensitive for a first attempt. | [ ] |
| **Week 4** | Check in on pilot progress | Ask each pilot task owner: "What did you try? What worked? What did not?" Listen. Do not evaluate. This is a learning phase. | [ ] |
| **Week 5** | Attend the pilot review session | Be present for the team's pilot results review. Ask questions. Show interest. If results are mixed, emphasize what was learned, not what failed. | [ ] |
| **Week 6-8** | Review SOPs and prompt library | Read the team's draft AI SOP. Provide feedback. Ensure quality assurance steps are realistic and enforceable. Approve the SOP for team use. | [ ] |
| **Week 9** | Attend the training workshop | Be present for at least the opening and closing sessions. Participate in one exercise yourself. Your visible participation signals that this matters. | [ ] |
| **Week 10-11** | Ask about AI in regular check-ins | Add one question to weekly individual check-ins: "Did you use AI on any task this week? How did it go?" This signals that AI use is expected, not optional. | [ ] |
| **Week 12** | Review 90-day data | Read the AI Champion's 90-day report before it is presented. Verify the numbers. Prepare your own assessment: was this worth the investment? | [ ] |
| **Week 13** | Present results to your supervisor | Bring the 90-day report to the office head. Request budget for Year 2 if results justify it. Request training for yourself if you identify knowledge gaps. | [ ] |

### Coaching AI Adoption: The Four Quadrants

The Supervision Guidebook's PPR Framework — Performance, Potential, Readiness — provides a useful lens for managing AI adoption across your team.[^5] Your staff will fall into four categories:

1. **High performance + high AI adoption.** These are your champions. Give them harder AI tasks. Ask them to teach others. Recognize their contribution.
2. **High performance + low AI adoption.** These staff members produce excellent work without AI. They may see no reason to change. Do not force adoption. Instead, show them a specific task where AI would make their already-good work even better. The Supervision Guidebook calls this the "stretch" approach.[^6]
3. **Low performance + high AI adoption.** Be careful here. AI can mask weak analytical skills. A staff member who prompts AI fluently but cannot evaluate the output critically is a risk. Focus coaching on output quality and verification, not just prompting speed.
4. **Low performance + low AI adoption.** Address the performance issue first. AI is not a fix for fundamental competency gaps. If a staff member cannot write a clear memo manually, teaching them to prompt AI will produce AI-generated memos that they cannot evaluate. Build the foundation before adding the tool.

## 14.4 Building Communities of Practice

### What a Community of Practice Is

A **community of practice** (CoP) is a group of people who share a professional interest, meet regularly, and learn from each other. It is not a committee. It has no formal authority. It does not produce reports for a director. It exists to share knowledge, solve problems, and raise the collective skill level of its members.[^7]

For AI adoption in BARMM, a community of practice is the structure that prevents knowledge from staying trapped inside one team. When the MFBM budget division discovers a prompt that cuts variance analysis time in half, a community of practice is how that discovery reaches MAFAR's budget unit, MILG's finance section, and MBHTE's planning division.

### Starting a BARMM AI Community of Practice

**Membership.** The community includes AI Champions from different offices, plus any interested staff. Keep it voluntary. Mandatory participation kills communities of practice. Target 10-25 active members across multiple agencies.

**Meeting cadence.** Meet monthly. One hour. No more. Busy government staff will not attend two-hour meetings. Keep it focused.

**Format.** Every meeting follows the same structure:

1. **Show and Tell (20 minutes).** Two members present a prompt, workflow, or AI application they developed. They show the actual output. The group asks questions.
2. **Problem Solving (20 minutes).** One member brings a challenge: a task they tried to AI-enable and could not, a prompt that produces inconsistent results, a quality concern they cannot resolve. The group brainstorms solutions.
3. **Tool Updates (10 minutes).** One member reports on recent AI tool updates, new features, or emerging best practices relevant to government work.
4. **Action Items (10 minutes).** Each member commits to trying one new technique before the next meeting.

**Facilitation.** Rotate the facilitator role monthly. This prevents the community from depending on one person and gives every member practice in organizing and leading.

**Communication.** Create a Viber or Signal group for between-meeting communication. Members share tips, ask quick questions, and post links to useful resources. Keep the channel active but low-pressure.

### Cross-Office Learning Opportunities

The 15 line ministries, 10 attached agencies, 4 commissions, and the Parliament each face different governance tasks. But their AI challenges overlap significantly. Consider these cross-office learning pairings:

| Office A | Office B | Shared AI Challenge |
|---|---|---|
| **MFBM** Budget Division | **BPDA** Planning Division | Budget-to-plan alignment analysis |
| **Parliament** Committee Secretariats | **MILG** LGU Coordination | Cross-referencing BAAs with LGU compliance |
| **MBHTE** Education Planning | **MOH** Health Service Planning | Data-driven service delivery targeting |
| **MAFAR** Program M&E | **MSSD** Social Services M&E | Quarterly accomplishment report generation |
| **MENRE** Environmental Data | **MOST** Science and Technology | AI-assisted environmental research synthesis |
| **BWC** Gender Analysis | **BYC** Youth Development | Population-segmented policy analysis |
| **DAB** Training Design | Any Ministry Training Unit | AI-assisted training material development |

These pairings are suggestions. The community of practice will identify its own most productive connections as it matures.

### Sustaining the Community

Communities of practice die when they lose momentum. Three practices keep them alive:

1. **Celebrate wins publicly.** When a community member's AI innovation saves their office significant time or improves output quality, share the story. Write a one-paragraph case study. Post it in the community's Viber group. Mention it in the next meeting. Recognition fuels participation.
2. **Rotate leadership.** If the same person facilitates every meeting, the community becomes their personal project. When they leave or burn out, the community dies. Rotate facilitation. Rotate who presents. Distribute ownership.
3. **Connect to institutional incentives.** If the community produces real value, advocate for its members to receive recognition in their performance evaluations. Participation in a cross-office AI community of practice demonstrates initiative, collaboration, and continuous learning — all competencies that merit recognition.

## 14.5 Keeping Up with AI Evolution

### The Pace of Change

AI tools change faster than any technology that government has previously adopted. Word processing reached maturity and then stabilized. Spreadsheets added features gradually over decades. Email changed slowly. Government staff learned these tools once and used them for years.

AI is different. The capabilities of Claude, GPT, Gemini, and other tools shift every few months. A technique that produced excellent results in January may be unnecessary by June because the tool improved. A limitation you worked around in March may be eliminated by September.

**This is not a reason to wait.** It is a reason to build adaptability into your training and workflow design.

### Three Strategies for Staying Current

**Strategy 1: Designate a "tool watcher" in every office.**

One staff member — ideally the AI Champion — spends 30 minutes per week scanning for updates to the AI tools the office uses. They check the tool's official blog, release notes, or announcement page. When a relevant update occurs, they summarize it in one paragraph and share it with the team.

This is not a full-time job. It is a 30-minute weekly habit that prevents the entire office from being surprised by changes.

**Strategy 2: Test prompts quarterly.**

Every quarter, the AI Champion or community of practice tests the office's top 10 prompts against the current version of each tool. The test confirms whether the prompts still produce the expected output quality. When prompts degrade, they are updated. When tools improve, prompts are simplified.

Build this testing into the office's regular workflow. Schedule it on the calendar. Treat it like the quarterly fire drill of AI adoption — routine, brief, and non-negotiable.

**Strategy 3: Learn from outside BARMM.**

Other Philippine government agencies are adopting AI. National government agencies, regional offices, and LGUs are experimenting with the same tools. The Civil Service Commission, the Department of Budget and Management, and DICT have published or are developing AI-related policies and training programs.[^8]

Additionally, international organizations working in BARMM — UNDP, World Bank, JICA, ADB — are increasingly using AI in their development programs. Their experiences, training materials, and best practices are resources you can draw on.

Join relevant mailing lists, follow relevant organizations on social media, and attend webinars when they are offered. The investment is minimal. The return — staying current without having to discover everything independently — is significant.

### When to Adopt New Tools

Not every new AI tool deserves your attention. Government offices cannot chase every shiny technology. Use this three-question filter before investing time in any new AI tool:

1. **Does it solve a problem we actually have?** If the tool's primary use case does not match a task your office performs, skip it.
2. **Does it work with our existing workflow?** A tool that requires a complete workflow redesign is a high-risk adoption. Prioritize tools that integrate with what you already do.
3. **Does it meet our data security requirements?** A tool that requires uploading confidential government data to servers outside the Philippines may be technically excellent and still be prohibited by your office's AI policy. Check before you test.

If a new tool passes all three questions, run a small pilot. One person. One task. One week. Evaluate before expanding.

## 14.6 Measuring AI Impact

### Why Measurement Matters

You cannot sustain what you cannot measure. AI adoption requires budget. Budget requires justification. Justification requires data. Without measurement, AI adoption depends on enthusiasm alone — and enthusiasm fades when the next organizational priority arrives.

**Measure from Day 1.** Do not wait until the 90-day plan is complete to start collecting data. Build measurement into every phase.

### The Four Dimensions of AI Impact

AI impact in government is not a single number. It has four dimensions, each measured differently.

**Dimension 1: Efficiency (Time Saved)**

This is the most visible and easiest to measure. For each AI-enabled task, record:

- **Baseline time** — how long the task took before AI
- **AI-assisted time** — how long the task takes with AI (including prompting, review, and revision)
- **Net time saved** — the difference

Aggregate these across tasks and across staff to produce a total hours-saved figure per month or quarter.

**Dimension 2: Quality (Output Improvement)**

Harder to measure than efficiency, but equally important. Quality improvements include:

- **Comprehensiveness** — AI-assisted briefers cover more issues, cite more references, and address more stakeholder perspectives than manually produced briefers
- **Consistency** — AI-assisted outputs follow standardized formats because the prompt enforces the format
- **Accuracy** — AI-assisted cross-referencing catches conflicts and redundancies that manual review misses (note: this assumes proper verification, per Chapter 3)
- **Timeliness** — tasks completed on time or ahead of schedule that previously required deadline extensions

Measure quality through **supervisor assessment**. After 90 days, ask each supervisor: "Has the quality of your team's outputs changed since AI adoption? In what way?" Collect structured responses, not just impressions.

**Dimension 3: Capacity (Workload Absorption)**

AI-augmented staff can handle more tasks with the same headcount. This is particularly valuable in BARMM offices with unfilled positions. Measure:

- **Additional tasks completed** — tasks the team could not handle before AI that they can now handle
- **Backlog reduction** — overdue reports, pending analyses, or delayed correspondence that has been cleared
- **Scope expansion** — new types of analysis or documentation the team now produces (e.g., comparative budget analysis that was previously too time-consuming)

**Dimension 4: Staff Development (Competency Growth)**

AI adoption changes how staff think about their work. Measure:

- **AI competency levels** — how many staff have moved from Basic to Intermediate or Intermediate to Advanced (per Table 14.1)
- **Self-reported confidence** — pre- and post-training surveys measuring staff confidence in using AI for their work
- **Knowledge sharing** — how many staff have contributed prompts to the shared library or presented at the community of practice

### Table 14.4: AI Impact KPIs

| KPI | How to Measure | Frequency | Target (Year 1) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Total hours saved per month** | Sum of (baseline time - AI time) across all AI-enabled tasks | Monthly | 40-80 hours per team of 10 |
| **Tasks AI-enabled** | Count of distinct task types using AI regularly | Quarterly | 8-12 by end of Year 1 |
| **Staff at Intermediate AI competency** | Count of staff who pass an intermediate-level skills assessment | Semi-annually | 50% of team |
| **Prompt library size** | Count of tested, maintained prompts in the shared library | Quarterly | 20-30 by end of Year 1 |
| **Knowledge base currency** | Percentage of knowledge base documents updated within the last 6 months | Quarterly | 80% or higher |
| **Supervisor satisfaction with output quality** | Structured survey (1-5 scale) on comprehensiveness, consistency, accuracy, timeliness | Quarterly | Average 3.5 or higher |
| **Staff AI confidence score** | Pre/post survey (1-5 scale) on confidence in AI-assisted task completion | Post-training; then semi-annually | Average increase of 1.5 points |
| **Community of practice participation** | Number of active members attending at least 50% of meetings | Quarterly | 15+ across offices |
| **Budget ROI** | Total estimated value of time saved / Total AI-related expenditure | Annually | 3:1 or higher |
| **Backlog reduction** | Percentage decrease in overdue tasks compared to pre-AI baseline | Quarterly | 30% reduction by end of Year 1 |

### Reporting AI Impact

**To your team:** Share monthly data in the team's regular meeting. Keep it simple. Three numbers: hours saved, tasks enabled, notable wins. Celebrate progress. Identify areas for improvement.

**To your office head:** Produce a quarterly one-page AI impact brief. Include the KPIs from Table 14.4, one or two specific examples of AI-assisted outputs, and any resource requests. The office head does not need a detailed report. They need evidence that the investment is producing results and a clear request for any additional support.

**To DAB and BPDA:** If BARMM develops a cross-ministry AI adoption initiative, the DAB (as the civil service development body) and the BPDA (as the planning authority) will need aggregate data. Standardize your reporting format now so it can be compiled later.[^9]

**To the community of practice:** Share your metrics openly. Comparison across offices is not competition. It is learning. If MFBM's budget division saves 80 hours per month and MAFAR's planning division saves 20, the question is not "who is better?" The question is "what is MFBM doing differently that MAFAR can learn from?"

### The Annual AI Capacity Review

At the end of each fiscal year, conduct a comprehensive review of your office's AI adoption. The review covers:

1. **Competency progress.** How many staff moved up in AI competency levels? Who is still at Basic? What training do they need?
2. **Tool effectiveness.** Are the current tools meeting the office's needs? Should subscriptions be renewed, upgraded, or replaced?
3. **Knowledge base health.** Is the knowledge base current, organized, and actively used? Or has it been neglected?
4. **Policy compliance.** Is the AI use policy being followed? Have there been any data handling incidents, quality failures, or ethical concerns?
5. **Budget adequacy.** Were the AI-related budget allocations sufficient? What adjustments are needed for next year?
6. **Next-year priorities.** Based on the review, what are the top three AI capacity priorities for the next fiscal year?

This annual review feeds directly into the office's annual work and financial plan. AI capacity building becomes a permanent agenda item, not a one-time project.
