# Glossary of AI and Digital Governance Terms

This glossary defines terms used throughout this guidebook. Definitions are written in plain language for government staff who may be encountering these concepts for the first time. Terms are organized alphabetically.

---

**ADDRESS IT** — A seven-step methodology for Complete Staff Work (CSW) used across BARMM offices. The acronym stands for: Analyze context, Define/Deliberate the problem, Develop options, Research, Explore options, Submit, Sustain implementation, and Iterate. See Appendix E for the AI-enhanced version.

**Adl (Justice)** — An Islamic principle requiring fairness and equity. In the AI context, Adl demands that AI-assisted decisions do not create or reinforce bias against any group. See Chapter 12, Section 12.2.2.

**AI (Artificial Intelligence)** — Computer systems that perform tasks normally requiring human intelligence. In government work, AI typically means tools that read, write, summarize, translate, and analyze text. This guidebook focuses on generative AI tools that produce text outputs from text inputs.

**AI Agent** — An AI system that can perform multi-step tasks autonomously, breaking down complex requests into subtasks, using tools, and making intermediate decisions. More capable than a single-prompt interaction. Government applications include automated document routing and legislative research assistants.

**AI Ethics Framework** — A set of principles and rules governing how an organization uses AI. Chapter 12 proposes a Bangsamoro-specific framework grounded in moral governance and Islamic values. See also: Amanah, Adl, Maslahah, Shura.

**AI Hallucination** — When an AI tool generates text that sounds correct but is factually wrong. Examples include citing a BAA number that does not exist, attributing a provision to the wrong article, or inventing a statistic. Chapter 3's "Trust but Verify" protocol addresses this risk.

**AI Literacy** — The ability to understand what AI can and cannot do, write effective prompts, evaluate AI outputs critically, and integrate AI tools into professional workflows. Chapter 2 provides the foundation. Chapter 14 covers training programs.

**AI Strategy** — A formal government document that assigns institutional ownership, training mandates, ethics standards, procurement policies, and performance indicators for AI adoption. Section 15.4 calls for a Bangsamoro AI Strategy.

**Algorithm** — A set of step-by-step instructions that a computer follows to complete a task. AI tools use algorithms to process text, recognize patterns, and generate outputs.

**Amanah (Trustworthiness)** — An Islamic principle requiring honesty and reliability. In the AI context, Amanah demands that AI-assisted outputs are verified for accuracy before submission. See Chapter 12, Section 12.2.1.

**API (Application Programming Interface)** — A set of rules that allows one software application to communicate with another. APIs enable AI tools to connect with government platforms like e-Bangsamoro.

**Automation** — Using technology to perform tasks without continuous human intervention. In government, automation ranges from auto-routing citizen complaints to generating standard reports. AI adds intelligence to automation by handling unstructured tasks like summarization and analysis.

**BAA (Bangsamoro Autonomy Act)** — Legislation enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament. As of March 2026, the BTA Parliament has enacted 89 BAAs covering administrative structure, civil service, education, public finance, and other governance domains.

**BARMM (Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao)** — The autonomous political entity in the southern Philippines created by the Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054). BARMM replaced the Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (ARMM) in 2019.

**BDP (Bangsamoro Development Plan)** — The medium-term development framework for BARMM. The 2nd BDP covers 2023-2028 and contains six goals, eight strategies, and quantified socioeconomic targets. Referenced throughout this guidebook, especially Chapter 5.

**Bias** — Systematic errors in AI outputs that favor or disfavor certain groups, perspectives, or outcomes. Bias can enter through training data, prompt design, or the absence of local context. Chapter 12 addresses bias in the context of Islamic values and moral governance.

**BOL (Bangsamoro Organic Law)** — Republic Act No. 11054, the foundational law that created BARMM and established its governance structure. The BOL defines Parliament's powers, the Chief Minister's authority, the fiscal framework, and the rights of the Bangsamoro people.

**BPDA (Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority)** — The BARMM ministry responsible for development planning, coordination, and monitoring. BPDA manages the BDP and coordinates with national planning agencies.

**BTA (Bangsamoro Transition Authority)** — The transitional government established under BOL Article XVI to govern BARMM until the first regular elections. The BTA exercises both legislative and executive powers.

**Budget Portal** — One of e-Bangsamoro's four portals. Handles budget preparation, authorization tracking, execution monitoring, and COA audit integration. See Section 15.2.

**Chain of Thought** — A prompting technique where you ask the AI to show its reasoning step by step before giving a final answer. Useful for complex analytical tasks like legal analysis or budget comparison, because you can see where the reasoning goes wrong and correct it.

**Chatbot** — An AI application designed to simulate conversation with users. Government chatbots handle citizen inquiries, route service requests, and provide information. Singapore's Ask Jamie is an example.

**Citizen Portal** — One of e-Bangsamoro's four portals. Provides public access to government services, transparency dashboards, document repositories, and feedback mechanisms. See Section 15.2.

**Claude** — An AI assistant developed by Anthropic. Claude is one of the primary AI tools referenced in this guidebook for text generation, document analysis, research synthesis, and workflow automation.

**Cloud Computing** — Delivering computing services (storage, processing, software) over the internet rather than from local hardware. Most AI tools run on cloud infrastructure. Government use requires awareness of where data is stored and processed.

**COA (Commission on Audit)** — The constitutional body that audits all government agencies in the Philippines, including BARMM. COA annual audit reports are the primary source material for parliamentary oversight. See Chapter 10.

**Codification** — The process of organizing, harmonizing, and consolidating enacted legislation into a systematic legal code. Chapter 11 covers AI-assisted codification of the 89 BAAs.

**Complete Staff Work (CSW)** — A methodology requiring that any document submitted to a decision-maker is complete, accurate, and ready for signature. BARMM uses the ADDRESS IT framework for CSW. See Appendix E.

**Context Window** — The maximum amount of text an AI tool can process in a single conversation. Larger context windows allow you to upload longer documents. As of 2026, leading AI tools support context windows ranging from 128,000 to 1,000,000 tokens.

**Cross-Referencing** — Comparing provisions across multiple laws, policies, or documents to identify overlaps, conflicts, or gaps. AI excels at cross-referencing large document sets. See Chapter 7, Section 7.2.3.

**Dashboard** — A visual display that presents key data and performance indicators in a single view. AI-assisted dashboards automate data aggregation and visualization. See Chapter 9.

**Data Classification** — The process of categorizing government data by sensitivity level (public, internal, confidential, restricted). Determines which documents can be uploaded to AI tools and which require additional safeguards. See Chapter 4.

**Data Privacy** — The protection of personal and sensitive information from unauthorized access, use, or disclosure. Chapter 4 covers data classification and privacy safeguards for AI use in government. The Data Privacy Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10173) governs data protection in the Philippines.

**Digital Governance** — The use of digital technologies to improve government operations, service delivery, transparency, and citizen engagement. This guidebook treats AI as one component of a broader digital governance strategy.

**Digital Literacy** — The ability to use digital tools and technologies effectively. AI literacy (defined separately) is a subset of digital literacy focused specifically on AI tools and workflows.

**Digital Transformation** — The process of fundamentally changing how an organization operates by integrating digital technology into all areas of work. Goes beyond digitizing paper forms to redesigning workflows around technology capabilities.

**e-Bangsamoro** — A four-portal e-governance platform under development for the Bangsamoro government. Portals: Parliamentary, Ministerial, Budget, and Citizen. See Section 15.2.

**e-Governance** — The use of information and communication technology (ICT) to deliver government services, exchange information, and enable transactions between government and citizens, businesses, and other government agencies.

**Embedding** — A mathematical representation of text that captures its meaning as numbers. AI systems use embeddings to understand how words and documents relate to each other. Enables semantic search — finding documents by meaning rather than exact keyword matches.

**Ethics** — The moral principles governing the use of AI in government. Chapter 12 grounds AI ethics in both secular governance principles (moral governance, transparency, accountability) and Islamic values (Amanah, Adl, Shura, Maslahah).

**Explainability** — The ability of an AI system to describe how it reached a particular output or recommendation. In government, explainability matters because decisions must be justifiable to oversight bodies and the public. If you cannot explain why the AI produced a result, you should not rely on that result for official action.

**Few-Shot Prompting** — A prompting technique where you include two or three examples of the desired output format in your prompt. The AI uses these examples as a template. Effective for standardizing output format across different tasks.

**Fine-Tuning** — The process of training an existing AI model on a specific dataset to improve its performance for a particular domain. Fine-tuning a model on BARMM legislation would make it more accurate on Bangsamoro legal questions. Requires technical expertise and computing resources.

**GAA (General Appropriations Act)** — The annual law that authorizes government spending for a fiscal year. In BARMM, the GAA is enacted by Parliament based on the Chief Minister's proposed budget. See Chapter 6.

**Gemini** — An AI model developed by Google. Available through Google's AI tools and integrated into NotebookLM. One of the AI platforms referenced in this guidebook.

**Generative AI** — AI systems that create new content — text, images, code, audio — based on patterns learned from training data. This guidebook focuses on generative AI for text, which is the primary modality for government document work.

**GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer)** — A type of AI architecture that processes text by predicting the next word in a sequence. The "Transformer" refers to the underlying neural network design. GPT models power several commercial AI tools.

**Guardrails** — Rules and limitations built into AI systems or organizational policies to prevent harmful, inaccurate, or inappropriate outputs. Chapter 12 discusses guardrails for BARMM AI use.

**Human-in-the-Loop** — A design principle where AI systems require human review and approval before their outputs are acted upon. Every AI workflow in this guidebook follows this principle. The AI drafts; the human decides. See Chapter 3's Four-Step Workflow.

**ICT (Information and Communications Technology)** — The broader category of digital tools including hardware, software, networks, and telecommunications. AI is a subset of ICT capabilities.

**Interoperability** — The ability of different systems, platforms, or databases to exchange and use data. Interoperability between e-Bangsamoro's portals, BangsamoroHR, and other BARMM platforms is what enables AI to connect data across government functions. Estonia's X-Road is a model for interoperability.

**IRR (Implementing Rules and Regulations)** — The detailed rules issued by the executive branch to operationalize an enacted law. BAAs require IRRs to specify implementation procedures. Chapter 11 covers AI-assisted IRR development.

**Institutional Memory** — The collective knowledge, experiences, precedents, and informal practices accumulated within an organization over time. Chapter 10 describes how AI-searchable knowledge bases preserve institutional memory across leadership transitions.

**Knowledge Base** — A structured collection of documents, data, and reference materials that AI tools can search and reference. Chapter 4 covers building an institutional knowledge base for BARMM. NotebookLM (Appendix B) is one tool for creating knowledge bases.

**Large Language Model (LLM)** — An AI system trained on vast amounts of text data that can understand and generate human language. Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini are LLMs. The "large" refers to billions of parameters (numerical values) that the model uses to process text.

**Machine Learning (ML)** — A subset of AI where systems improve their performance by learning from data without being explicitly programmed for every scenario. LLMs are one application of machine learning.

**Maslahah (Public Interest)** — An Islamic jurisprudential principle that evaluates actions based on whether they serve the public welfare. In the AI context, Maslahah is the test for whether an AI application should be adopted: does it genuinely serve the Bangsamoro people's interests? See Chapter 12, Section 12.2.4.

**Maturity Model** — A framework that describes levels of organizational capability. Chapter 13 defines three AI maturity levels for BARMM: Level 1 (Individual Use), Level 2 (Team Integration), Level 3 (Institutional Adoption).

**Ministerial Portal** — One of e-Bangsamoro's four portals. Supports program management, implementation tracking, reporting, and procurement documentation. See Section 15.2.

**MOA (Memorandum of Agreement)** — A formal document between two or more parties establishing a cooperative arrangement. In BARMM, MOAs govern intergovernmental relationships, partnership programs, and resource-sharing arrangements.

**Model** — In AI terminology, the trained software system that processes inputs and generates outputs. When this guidebook says "the AI model," it refers to the underlying system (Claude, GPT, Gemini) that powers the tool you use.

**MOP (Manual of Operations)** — A document that defines the standard operating procedures, organizational structure, and workflow processes for a government office. AI can assist in drafting and maintaining MOPs.

**Moral Governance** — A BARMM governance principle emphasizing ethical conduct, transparency, accountability, and values-based leadership. The BTA's 12 Pillars of Moral Governance inform the AI ethics framework in Chapter 12.

**MOST (Ministry of Science and Technology)** — The BARMM ministry responsible for science, technology, and innovation policy. A potential institutional owner for BARMM's AI strategy (see Section 15.1).

**Multimodal AI** — AI systems that process multiple types of input — text, images, audio, video — rather than text alone. Multimodal capabilities allow you to upload a scanned document image and have the AI extract its text, or provide a chart and have the AI describe its contents.

**Natural Language Processing (NLP)** — The branch of AI that deals with the interaction between computers and human language. NLP enables AI tools to understand prompts, extract meaning from documents, translate between languages, and generate text.

**NotebookLM** — A Google AI tool that allows users to upload documents and have AI-powered conversations with them. Useful for building topic-specific knowledge bases. See Appendix B.

**Obsidian** — A knowledge management application that stores notes as local markdown files with bidirectional linking. Used in this guidebook's author's toolkit as a persistent knowledge base. See Appendix B.

**OCR (Optical Character Recognition)** — Technology that converts scanned images of text (such as PDF scans of printed documents) into machine-readable text. Essential for making older BARMM documents accessible to AI analysis.

**Open Data** — Government data that is freely available to the public in machine-readable formats. Open data enables transparency, accountability, and citizen participation. See Chapter 10, Section 10.3.

**Open Source** — Software whose source code is publicly available and can be freely used, modified, and distributed. Several AI tools and frameworks are open source.

**Output** — The text, table, analysis, or other content that an AI tool generates in response to a prompt. All AI outputs require human review before use in official government documents.

**Parameters** — The numerical values inside an AI model that determine how it processes text. More parameters generally means more capability. Current LLMs have billions to trillions of parameters. You do not need to understand parameters to use AI effectively.

**Parliamentary Portal** — One of e-Bangsamoro's four portals. Supports legislation tracking, bill drafting workflows, committee management, and plenary session records. See Section 15.2.

**PPA (Programs, Projects, and Activities)** — The standard classification for government interventions in Philippine development planning. PPAs are the building blocks of the BDP and annual work plans. AI helps align PPAs with strategic goals and track their implementation.

**Prompt** — The text instruction you give to an AI tool. A prompt tells the AI what role to play, what context to consider, what task to perform, and what format to use for the output. Chapters 2 and 3 teach structured prompting for government work.

**Prompt Engineering** — The skill of writing effective prompts that produce accurate, useful AI outputs. Includes techniques like role assignment, context supply, format specification, and iterative refinement. Chapter 3 covers this as the "Four-Step AI Workflow."

**Prompt Library** — A maintained collection of tested, reusable prompts for common tasks. Appendix A provides a prompt library organized by governance function. Teams at Level 2 maturity share prompt libraries to ensure consistency.

**RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)** — A technique where AI retrieves relevant information from a specific document set before generating its response. RAG reduces hallucination by grounding AI outputs in actual source material. NotebookLM uses a form of RAG.

**Resolution** — A formal expression of the opinion, will, or decision of the Bangsamoro Parliament. Unlike BAAs, resolutions do not have the force and effect of law. The BTA has filed over 570 resolutions.

**Role Prompting** — A technique where you tell the AI to assume a specific professional role (e.g., "You are a parliamentary oversight analyst for the Bangsamoro Parliament"). Role prompting improves output relevance and tone. Used extensively throughout this guidebook.

**Rule of Signature** — A principle stated in this guidebook: if you would not sign a document without reading it, do not submit an AI-generated document without reviewing it. The person who signs bears accountability, regardless of how the document was produced.

**Semantic Search** — Searching for documents or information based on meaning rather than exact keyword matches. AI-powered semantic search finds relevant documents even when they use different terminology than your query.

**Shari'ah** — Islamic law derived from the Quran, Sunnah, and scholarly consensus. The BOL requires that legislation and governance in BARMM be consistent with Shari'ah principles. Chapter 12 addresses Shari'ah considerations for AI adoption.

**Shura (Consultation)** — An Islamic principle requiring collective deliberation before making significant decisions. In the AI context, Shura demands stakeholder consultation before deploying AI in government processes that affect communities. See Chapter 12, Section 12.2.3.

**SOP (Standard Operating Procedure)** — A documented set of step-by-step instructions for carrying out routine operations. At Level 2 and Level 3 maturity, SOPs incorporate AI-assisted steps with defined human review checkpoints.

**Structured Output** — AI output that follows a specific format — tables, numbered lists, labeled sections, JSON — rather than free-form text. Requesting structured output makes AI responses more consistent and easier to integrate into government documents.

**System Prompt** — A set of instructions given to an AI tool that governs its behavior throughout a conversation. System prompts can establish the AI's role, define constraints, and specify output formats. Advanced AI tools allow custom system prompts.

**Template** — A pre-structured document format that standardizes outputs. This guidebook provides templates for legislative briefers (Appendix F), budget briefers (Appendix G), and the 90-Day AI Adoption Plan (Appendix H).

**Token** — The basic unit of text that an AI model processes. A token is roughly three-quarters of a word in English. Token limits determine how much text you can input and how long an output the AI can generate. When this guidebook refers to "context window," it means the total number of tokens available.

**Training Data** — The text corpus that an AI model learned from during its development. Training data determines what the model "knows." AI tools trained primarily on English-language text may perform less well on Filipino or Arabic content.

**Transformer** — The neural network architecture underlying modern LLMs. Transformers process text by calculating relationships between all words in a passage simultaneously, which enables them to understand context and generate coherent text.

**Transparency** — In AI governance, the principle that government should disclose when AI tools are used in producing official documents, analysis, or decisions. Chapter 12 proposes a BARMM transparency standard.

**Trust but Verify** — The core protocol for using AI outputs in government work, introduced in Chapter 3. Accept AI outputs as useful starting points but verify every factual claim, citation, number, and legal reference against authoritative sources before submitting.

**Vendor Lock-In** — A situation where an organization becomes dependent on a single AI provider and cannot switch without significant cost or disruption. Chapter 12's integrity pillar warns against vendor lock-in that compromises government independence.

**Verification** — The process of checking AI outputs against authoritative sources to confirm accuracy. Verification is the non-negotiable step in every AI workflow described in this guidebook. Without verification, AI outputs are drafts, not documents.

**Wali** — The ceremonial head of the Bangsamoro government, representing the moral authority of the Bangsamoro people. The office is established under BOL Article VIII, Section 2. Referenced in Chapter 12's discussion of moral governance.

**Workflow** — A defined sequence of steps for completing a task. This guidebook provides AI-augmented workflows for planning (Ch 5), budgeting (Ch 6), legislation (Ch 7), implementation (Ch 8), M&E (Ch 9), oversight (Ch 10), and codification (Ch 11).

**X-Road** — Estonia's data exchange platform that connects all government databases. Referenced in Section 15.1 as a model for BARMM's digital governance infrastructure.

**Zero-Shot Prompting** — A prompting technique where you ask the AI to perform a task without providing examples. The AI relies entirely on its training data and your instructions. Simpler than few-shot prompting but may produce less consistent formatting.
