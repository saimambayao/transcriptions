# How to Use AI in Governance, Legislation, and Development Work in the Bangsamoro

**Professional Standards and Practical Guidance for AI-Augmented Governance, Legislation, and Development Work**

*Saidamen R. Mambayao*

---

## Why These Standards Exist

Every guidebook I have authored was produced with AI assistance. The Legal Research Guidebook, the Bill Drafting Guidebook, the Strategic Planning Guidebook, the Cooperative Development Guidebook, and this AI and Bangsamoro Governance Guidebook — all were written using AI tools as part of a deliberate, disciplined workflow that combines deep domain expertise with the speed and thoroughness that AI enables.

This document is my professional commitment to transparency about how that works. It describes the standards I follow, the boundaries I enforce, and the verification practices I apply when using AI to produce governance publications. It is not a government issuance. It is not institutional policy. It is the working methodology of an independent practitioner who has spent years in Bangsamoro governance — as a legislative staff member, a policy consultant, a bill drafter, a program officer, and a technology builder — and who uses AI as a tool within that experience, not as a substitute for it.

These standards draw from multiple sources: the Supreme Court's Governance Framework on Human-Centered Augmented Intelligence (A.M. No. 25-11-28-SC, February 18, 2026), international AI ethics frameworks, Islamic principles of governance, and — most importantly — hard lessons learned from producing over a dozen governance guidebooks where AI-generated errors could have damaged institutional credibility if not caught.

I share these standards for two reasons. First, because anyone reading my guidebooks deserves to know how they were made. Second, because the practitioners who use these guidebooks — legal researchers, bill drafters, planners, budget analysts — may want to adopt similar practices in their own work. The standards are offered as a professional model, not a compliance requirement.

## 1. The Authorial Position

I am an independent governance practitioner and author. I write guidebooks on Bangsamoro governance based on my experience working inside BARMM institutions — the Bangsamoro Parliament (BTA), the Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE), the Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG), and the Office of the Chief Minister (OCM) — as well as my work in the private sector through MoroTech and MoroAcademy.

These guidebooks are independently authored publications. They are not official government issuances. They draw on enacted law, official government documents, and direct professional experience, but the analysis, methodology, and recommendations are mine. When a guidebook recommends a specific approach — such as the 5-step legal research methodology or the ADDRESS IT framework for Complete Staff Work — that recommendation carries the authority of professional expertise and sourced evidence, not the force of law.

The same applies to these AI standards. They reflect my professional judgment about responsible AI use, informed by the best available standards and my own experience. They are not binding on any government office. They are the standards I hold myself to, and they are available for others to adopt, adapt, or reject as they see fit.

## 2. How AI Is Used in These Guidebooks

### What AI Does

AI tools assist with the following tasks in guidebook production:

**Governance and Legislation:**

| Task | AI Role | Human Role |
|------|---------|------------|
| **Legal research** | Searches the legislative archive (11,866 RAs, 89 BAAs, 38,857 SC decisions), extracts provisions, traces provision chains, builds comparative matrices | Formulates research questions, applies the National Law Relationship Test, verifies every provision against source text, forms legal conclusions |
| **Bill drafting** | Produces structural drafts, checks cross-references against existing BAAs, flags inconsistencies with the BOL | Determines legislative intent, makes policy choices, owns every provision |
| **Policy analysis** | Scans BDP chapters, compares policy options, drafts recommendation frameworks | Weighs political realities, assesses institutional capacity, makes recommendations |
| **Budget and fiscal analysis** | Extracts data from BAA 84/85 provisions, compares appropriation tables, drafts budget briefers | Interprets fiscal implications, assesses COA compliance, forms conclusions |

**Development Work:**

| Task | AI Role | Human Role |
|------|---------|------------|
| **Cooperative development** | Researches RA 9520 requirements, drafts Articles of Cooperation templates, analyzes registration processes, compares cooperative types | Assesses community readiness, understands local economic conditions, knows which cooperative type fits which community |
| **Business planning** | Assists with Lean Canvas drafting, market analysis research, financial projection templates, value chain mapping | Validates assumptions against field knowledge, understands Bangsamoro market realities, makes strategic decisions |
| **Islamic finance research** | Compiles mudarabah/musharakah/murabaha frameworks, cross-references PD 1083 and BOL Art. X provisions | Verifies fiqh concepts with scholarly sources, understands practical application in Bangsamoro cooperatives |
| **Community needs assessment** | Processes survey data, identifies patterns across demographics, drafts situational analyses | Designs assessment methodology, interprets community dynamics, weighs qualitative factors AI cannot capture |
| **Training and capacity building** | Drafts training modules, develops session guides, creates assessment tools | Designs curriculum based on practitioner experience, understands adult learning in the Bangsamoro context, adapts to audience |
| **Proposal and program design** | Researches funding requirements, drafts program frameworks, builds logical frameworks and theories of change | Shapes program strategy, assesses feasibility based on field experience, builds institutional relationships |

**Cross-Cutting:**

| Task | AI Role | Human Role |
|------|---------|------------|
| **Drafting** | Produces first drafts of chapters, sections, and worked examples based on detailed outlines | Reviews every sentence, rewrites for voice and accuracy, ensures domain expertise is reflected |
| **Cross-referencing** | Checks consistency across chapters, identifies contradictions between guidebooks, flags citation gaps | Makes judgment calls about conflicts, decides which source controls, resolves ambiguities |
| **Fact-checking** | Scans for known fabrication patterns (wrong BOL articles, invented BAA numbers, misattributed mandates) | Confirms every flagged error against source documents, makes final determination |
| **Transcription** | Extracts text from PDFs using OCR, structures raw text into formatted markdown | Verifies transcription accuracy against source documents |
| **Translation assistance** | Assists with English-Filipino-Arabic terminology | Verifies Islamic legal terms with domain expertise, confirms Bangsamoro-specific usage |

### What AI Does Not Do

- **AI does not determine methodology.** The 5-step legal research process, the 7-tier BARMM legal hierarchy, the ADDRESS IT framework, the provision chain trace technique, the Lean Canvas adaptation for cooperatives — these methodologies come from professional experience and domain knowledge. AI did not design them.
- **AI does not make editorial decisions.** What to include, what to emphasize, what to omit, how to frame a worked example — these are authorial choices that require judgment about the audience, the institutional context, and the purpose of the guidebook.
- **AI does not interpret Shari'ah.** AI tools lack the jurisprudential expertise (*fiqh*) needed to interpret Islamic law. Any Shari'ah content in the guidebooks — whether in legislation, cooperative bylaws, or Islamic finance instruments — is informed by BOL Art. X provisions, PD 1083, and consultation with the relevant scholarly tradition — not by AI-generated interpretations.
- **AI does not substitute for institutional knowledge.** Knowing how a bill actually moves through the BTA Parliament, how a Ministry prepares for a COA audit, how a planning officer navigates the BDP alignment process — this knowledge comes from years inside these institutions. AI can process documents, but it cannot replicate the practitioner's understanding of how government actually works.
- **AI does not understand community dynamics.** Whether a community is ready for a cooperative, which cooperative type fits its economic reality, how post-conflict conditions in Marawi shape what programs are feasible, why a federation model works in mainland BARMM but not in island provinces — these judgments require field experience and relationships that AI cannot replicate.
- **AI does not replace entrepreneurial judgment.** Market viability, financial sustainability, partnership decisions, pricing strategy for Bangsamoro social enterprises — these require business experience and an understanding of local economic conditions that no AI model possesses.

## 3. Ethical Principles

These principles guide my use of AI in professional work. They draw on several traditions:

### 3.1 Human Judgment First

AI is a drafting tool, not a thinking tool. It produces text. I produce judgment. Every guidebook chapter begins with my outline, my methodology, and my understanding of the domain. AI accelerates the drafting. It does not direct the thinking.

This aligns with what the Supreme Court calls the "human-centered" principle — the recognition that AI should "complement and empower human cognitive skills, rather than completely supplanting or overriding human judgment."[^1] It also aligns with the Islamic principle of *Khalifah* — stewardship. The tool serves the human. The human answers for the work.

### 3.2 Transparency

Every guidebook produced with AI assistance says so. This standards document is part of that transparency. Readers deserve to know:

- That AI assisted in the production
- What AI was used for (and what it was not used for)
- That every claim was verified by the author against source documents
- That the author takes full responsibility for the final product

The Supreme Court's Framework mandates a 6-point disclosure for AI-assisted work in the Judiciary.[^2] I adopt an adapted version of that disclosure as my professional standard — not because the SC requires it of me (it does not — the Framework applies to the Judiciary), but because the principle of transparency is sound regardless of who issues it.

### 3.3 Verification Before Publication

AI tools fabricate. This is not a theoretical risk. In the course of producing these guidebooks, I have caught AI generating:

**In governance and legislation work:**
- Wrong BOL article numbers (citing Art. IX when the correct reference is Art. X — 13+ documented occurrences)
- Invented BAA numbers (e.g., "BAA 25" for the Education Code when the actual law is BAA 18)
- Plausible-sounding provision text that does not match any enacted law
- National ministry abbreviations (DBM) instead of BARMM equivalents (MFBM)
- Fiqh concepts presented as enacted BARMM law when no BAA codifies them

**In development work:**
- Wrong cooperative type classifications under RA 9520 (confusing multi-purpose with multipurpose-with-savings, or misclassifying a service cooperative as a producers cooperative)
- Fabricated CDA memorandum circular numbers and registration requirements
- Islamic finance terms (*mudarabah*, *musharakah*) applied to structures that do not actually comply with the fiqh requirements behind them
- Wrong BDP chapter and strategy references for development program alignment (e.g., citing BDP Goal 3 when the relevant goal is Goal 2, Objective 2.5)
- Fabricated cooperative statistics (membership numbers, compliance rates) that sound authoritative but have no source

Every factual claim in every guidebook is verified using a three-tag system:

| Tag | Meaning | What I Do |
|-----|---------|-----------|
| **EXTRACTED** | Directly stated in enacted law | Read the source file, confirm the provision exists, copy the verbatim text |
| **INFERRED** | Reasoned from a provision or doctrine | Confirm the source provision exists, document the reasoning chain |
| **UNVERIFIED** | Cannot be traced to any source | Mark it prominently — do not present as fact |

A wrong citation is three times worse than writing [UNVERIFIED]. This is a lesson learned from experience, not a rule imported from a framework.

### 3.4 Accountability

My name is on every guidebook. If a citation is wrong, if a provision is misquoted, if a mandate is misattributed — that is my error, not the AI's. The Supreme Court puts this well: the use of AI "shall neither excuse the responsibility of the user for the consequences of such use, nor become a ground to mitigate or justify any liability."[^3] I adopt this principle not as a legal obligation but as a professional one. Authorship means accountability.

### 3.5 Fairness and Context

AI tools are trained on data that may not represent the Bangsamoro context. The diversity between mainland and island provinces, the distinction between Islamized and non-Moro indigenous communities, the institutional differences between BTA-era governance and ARMM-era structures — these nuances require human expertise to navigate. I review every AI output for contextual accuracy, cultural sensitivity, and representational fairness.

### 3.6 Privacy

I do not input personally identifiable information, classified documents, or confidential data into AI tools without authorization. When working with documents that contain sensitive information, I anonymize or redact before processing. This practice is consistent with RA 10173 (Data Privacy Act) and with the Islamic principle of *Sitr* — the obligation to protect what is entrusted to you.

### 3.7 Respect for Islamic Values

AI tools have limited understanding of Islamic governance principles. When the guidebooks address topics with a Shari'ah dimension — and several do, including the Legal Research Guidebook's Chapter 9 and this guidebook's Chapter 12 — I apply additional verification:

- AI cannot issue fatwas or interpret divine commands
- Arabic legal terms (*mudarabah*, *sulh*, *ta'zir*, *waqf*, *zakat*) require expert verification
- The Islamic principles applied in the guidebooks (Amanah, Adl, Shura, Maslahah) are drawn from scholarly tradition, not generated by AI
- BOL Art. X provisions and PD 1083 are the enacted legal sources; fiqh concepts are labeled as scholarly rather than statutory

## 4. The Oversight Model

I use a **Human-in-the-Loop (HITL)** model for all substantive work. This means:

- AI produces drafts, research compilations, and comparative analyses
- I review every output before it enters a guidebook
- I verify every legal provision against the source document
- I form all analytical conclusions independently
- I make all editorial and methodological decisions

The Supreme Court's Framework defines three oversight levels — HITL, Human-on-the-Loop (HOTL), and Human-in-Command (HIC).[^4] For guidebook production, only two are relevant:

| Level | My Application |
|-------|---------------|
| **HITL** | All legal research, chapter drafting, fact-checking, cross-referencing, Shari'ah content. I review every AI output before use. |
| **HOTL** | PDF transcription, OCR, document formatting. I review outputs periodically and spot-check for accuracy. |

No guidebook content is published without human review. Period.

## 5. Risk Awareness

The SC Framework classifies AI systems into four risk tiers: Prohibited, High-Risk, Limited-Risk, and Minimal-Risk.[^5] While this classification was designed for the Judiciary, the thinking behind it is useful for any practitioner.

My AI-augmented guidebook work falls squarely in the **limited-risk** category:

- The AI assists with research, drafting, and analysis — not with decisions that directly affect individual rights
- All outputs are reviewed by a human author before publication
- The risk of harm from an undetected error is reputational and institutional (a wrong citation in a guidebook), not adjudicatory (a wrong citation in a court decision)

That said, limited-risk does not mean no-risk. A guidebook that misquotes the BOL, misattributes a mandate, or presents fiqh as enacted law could mislead practitioners who rely on it. A cooperative development guidebook that recommends the wrong cooperative type could lead a community to register incorrectly and waste months of organizing effort. A training module with fabricated financial projections could mislead cooperative officers into unsustainable decisions. A proposal with wrong BDP alignment could fail government review. The verification standards in Section 3.3 exist precisely because the risk — whether in legislation or development — is real.

## 6. Disclosure Practice

### The Standard Disclosure

Every guidebook I publish includes a disclosure statement, typically in the About This Guidebook section. The disclosure states:

1. **That AI was used** — identifying the tool (Claude Code / Anthropic Claude)
2. **What AI was used for** — research compilation, drafting assistance, cross-referencing, fact-checking
3. **What AI was NOT used for** — methodology design, editorial decisions, Shari'ah interpretation, professional judgment
4. **That every claim was verified** — against enacted law, official documents, and source texts
5. **That the author bears full responsibility** — for the accuracy and completeness of the published work

### Recommended Disclosure for Practitioners

For practitioners who use AI in their own legal research, bill drafting, policy analysis, development planning, or community capacity building, I recommend a similar disclosure practice. The template below is adapted from the SC Framework's 6-point disclosure requirement and tailored for professional governance, legislation, and development work:

```
---

**AI Assistance Disclosure**

This [document type] was prepared with AI assistance.

1. **Tool:** [name and version — e.g., Claude Code (Anthropic Claude Opus 4.6)]
2. **Purpose:** [what the AI assisted with — e.g., legal research compilation,
   provision extraction, comparative analysis]
3. **Oversight:** [Human-in-the-Loop / Human-on-the-Loop]. All AI-generated
   content was verified by the author against source documents.
4. **Preservation:** AI outputs used in preparing this document have been
   preserved and are available upon request.
5. **Responsibility:** The undersigned bears full responsibility for the
   accuracy and completeness of this document.

Prepared by: ___________________________
Date: __________________________________

---
```

This disclosure is not required by any regulation for independent authors. I use it as a professional practice and recommend it as a best practice.

## 9. Quality Commitment

Before any guidebook chapter is published, I verify:

### Content Verification
- [ ] Every BOL article number checked against the 18-article map
- [ ] Every BAA number checked against the enacted BAA index
- [ ] Every verbatim quotation verified against the source text
- [ ] Every provision confirmed as good law (not repealed, amended, or struck down)
- [ ] No BARMM guidebook cited as a primary source for legal claims — all factual claims traced back to enacted law

### AI-Specific Verification
- [ ] All AI-generated findings tagged (EXTRACTED / INFERRED / UNVERIFIED)
- [ ] No AI output accepted without human review
- [ ] Known fabrication patterns checked (BOL articles, BAA numbers, ministry abbreviations, Shari'ah terms)
- [ ] No confidential or personal data processed through AI without authorization
- [ ] Shari'ah content reviewed against scholarly and enacted sources (not accepted from AI alone)

### Disclosure
- [ ] AI assistance disclosed in the guidebook's About section
- [ ] The nature and extent of AI use described honestly
- [ ] The author's full responsibility for the published content affirmed
