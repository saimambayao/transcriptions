# Chapter 12 — Ethics, Islamic Values, and Responsible AI

Every tool is morally neutral until a human picks it up. A spreadsheet can track disaster relief distributions or hide stolen funds. A policy brief can clarify public debate or manipulate it. A budget proposal can allocate resources justly or divert them to political allies. The tool does not decide. You do.

AI is no different. It can draft legislation that serves the public interest or generate plausible-sounding text that obscures accountability. It can analyze budget data to reveal inequities or produce analyses that confirm whatever conclusion you wanted. It can accelerate government services to underserved communities or widen the gap between connected insiders and everyone else.

The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao has something that most AI governance frameworks worldwide lack: **a moral foundation that predates the technology.** The Twelve Pillars of Moral Governance, the Islamic principles embedded in the Bangsamoro Organic Law, and the Enhanced 12-Point Priority Agenda provide an ethical infrastructure that is not borrowed from Silicon Valley or imported from a UN working paper. It grows from the identity and values of the Bangsamoro people themselves.[^1]

This chapter connects that moral foundation to the practical reality of AI use in government. It does not offer abstract philosophy. It gives you specific principles, clear boundaries, and a Bangsamoro-grounded framework for deciding when AI serves public interest — and when it does not.

## 12.1 Moral Governance and AI

The BARMM government anchors its operations in Moral Governance — a leadership framework built on twelve pillars that guide all public servants in the Bangsamoro region.[^2] These pillars are not aspirational slogans. They are operational expectations. Every ministry, office, and agency is expected to reflect them in daily decisions.

When you introduce AI into government work, you do not suspend these pillars. You extend them to cover a new tool. The question is not whether AI aligns with Moral Governance. The question is **how you use AI** so that it aligns.

### 12.1.1 The 12 Pillars Applied to AI Adoption

Each pillar creates specific obligations for anyone using AI in government work. Table 12.1 maps all twelve to concrete AI governance applications.

**Table 12.1: The 12 Pillars of Moral Governance Applied to AI**

| # | Pillar | Core Principle | AI Governance Application |
|---|--------|---------------|---------------------------|
| 1 | **Integrity** | Public office holders must not bind themselves in any manner that could influence their duties | Do not use AI tools that require surrendering government data to private companies without data protection agreements. Do not allow vendor lock-in that compromises independent judgment. |
| 2 | **Accountability** | Public office holders must be open and transparent; must answer to authority | Every AI-assisted document must identify that AI was used in its preparation. The signing official takes full responsibility for the output. Never attribute a decision to "the AI said so." |
| 3 | **Transparency and Honesty** | Declare private interests; make all public transactions public | Disclose AI assistance in policy recommendations, legislative briefers, and budget analyses. If a ministry uses AI to draft a bill, the committee reviewing it has a right to know. |
| 4 | **Sense of Patriotism** | Invest in the Bangsamoro way of life and heritage | Use AI to strengthen — not replace — Bangsamoro cultural knowledge. Train AI with local context and Bangsamoro source materials, not generic global templates. |
| 5 | **Striving for Excellence** | Deliver the highest quality, prioritizing process over results | AI enables faster output. Do not use speed as an excuse to skip review, verification, and quality assurance. A fast but inaccurate briefer is worse than a slow but correct one. |
| 6 | **Cultural Understanding and Tolerance** | Appreciate socioeconomic differences and provincial diversity | Test AI outputs for cultural bias. Check whether AI-generated policy language accounts for differences between mainland and island provinces, between Islamized and non-Moro indigenous communities, and between urban and rural populations. |
| 7 | **Unwavering Loyalty to God** | Public officials are guided by devotion to God | AI is a tool, not an authority. It does not issue fatwas, interpret divine commands, or replace the moral compass that faith provides. Use it within the boundaries of your faith, not as a substitute for it. |
| 8 | **Justice** | Serve without prejudice; equity triumphs over personal allegiance | Audit AI outputs for discriminatory patterns. If an AI-assisted analysis systematically disadvantages a particular province, ethnic group, or gender, reject it. Justice requires that AI serves everyone equitably. |
| 9 | **Inclusivity** | Foster environments free from discrimination | Ensure AI adoption does not create a two-tier bureaucracy where tech-literate staff advance while others are left behind. Design training that reaches all staff, including those in island and remote provinces. |
| 10 | **Objectivity** | Make only merit-based decisions in the public interest | Use AI to reduce bias in analysis, not introduce it. When AI summarizes stakeholder input, verify that it represents all voices — not just the loudest or most digitally accessible ones. |
| 11 | **Dialogue and Meaningful Engagement** | Seek community opinion through dialogue | Consult staff, communities, and stakeholders before deploying AI in services that affect them. Do not automate processes that communities expect to involve human interaction without first discussing the change. |
| 12 | **Balance Between Practical Life and Moral Side** | Balance practical duties with moral/ethical values | AI maximizes efficiency. But efficiency without ethics produces harm. Before automating any process, ask: does this serve the community's well-being, or just our workload? |

The table is not decorative. **Print it.** Post it in your office. When you are about to use AI for a government task, scan the twelve pillars and ask yourself: does this use honor every one? If any pillar is compromised, stop and redesign the approach.

### 12.1.2 Transparency and Honesty in AI-Assisted Outputs

Pillar 3 — Transparency and Honesty — deserves its own section because it is the pillar most frequently violated in AI-assisted government work. The violation is rarely deliberate. It happens by omission.

**The pattern works like this.** A policy analyst uses AI to draft a legislative briefer. The briefer is well-structured, properly cited, and convincingly written. The analyst submits it to the committee chairperson. The chairperson reads it, approves it, and presents its findings in a committee hearing. At no point does anyone disclose that AI assisted in the drafting. The committee members, the ministry officials responding to the briefer's findings, and the public watching the hearing all assume the analysis is entirely the product of the parliamentary staff's expertise.

Is the briefer wrong? Not necessarily. But is the process honest? No.

**The Transparency Rule for AI-Assisted Government Outputs:**

1. **Label AI-assisted documents.** Include a note — "This document was prepared with AI assistance. All content has been reviewed and verified by [name and designation]." Place it in the document header or at the end, before the signature block.
2. **Identify what AI contributed.** Was it the initial draft? The research synthesis? The data analysis? The formatting? Be specific.
3. **Confirm human verification.** State who reviewed the AI output, what verification steps were taken, and what sources were checked.
4. **Do not present AI speed as human productivity.** If your team produced a 30-page analysis in two days that would normally take two weeks, acknowledge that AI was part of the process. Claiming the output as purely manual work misrepresents your team's capacity and sets unrealistic expectations for future work.

This transparency rule applies to all outputs covered in this guidebook: legislative briefers (Chapter 7), budget analyses (Chapter 6), M&E reports (Chapter 9), oversight analyses (Chapter 10), and policy recommendations.

## 12.2 Islamic Principles for AI Use

The Bangsamoro Organic Law recognizes Islamic values as integral to governance in the region.[^3] Article X establishes Shari'ah jurisdiction, with the Qur'an and Sunnah as its principal sources.[^4] Article IX, Section 24 mandates the preservation and promotion of Bangsamoro culture and identity.[^5] The Wali serves as the ceremonial head representing the moral authority of the Bangsamoro people.[^6]

These provisions are not ceremonial. They establish that governance in BARMM operates within an Islamic moral framework. When a new tool enters that framework, it must be evaluated against the principles that govern it.

Four Islamic principles are directly relevant to AI adoption in government. Each creates a specific obligation.

### 12.2.1 Amanah — Trustworthiness

**Amanah** means trust — the trust placed in you by God, by your institution, and by the people you serve. In the supervision guidebook, amanah is defined as the ethical foundation of the supervisory role: "You hold a trust. Your staff trust you to be fair. Your superiors trust you to deliver results. The public trusts you to use resources wisely."[^7]

Applied to AI, amanah creates three obligations:

**First, protect data entrusted to you.** When you upload government documents to an AI tool, you transfer information that the public entrusted to your office. Ask: Does this AI tool store the data? Who has access? Is the data processed on servers outside the Philippines? If a citizen's personal information appears in the document, does the AI tool comply with the Data Privacy Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10173)?[^8]

**Practical test:** Before uploading any document to an AI tool, answer these questions:

- Does the document contain personally identifiable information?
- Does the document contain classified or restricted information?
- Does the AI tool's privacy policy allow data retention and model training on uploaded content?
- Would you be comfortable if this document appeared publicly because of a data breach at the AI provider?

If any answer gives you pause, do not upload the document. Use the AI tool with sanitized or anonymized content instead.

**Second, verify before you submit.** Amanah requires that what you present is truthful. AI generates plausible text — but plausible is not the same as accurate. If you submit an AI-drafted briefer containing a fabricated BAA number, an incorrect provision reference, or an invented statistic, you have breached amanah. The AI did not lie. **You failed to verify.**

**Third, do not overstate AI's contribution as your own — or understate it.** Both directions violate amanah. Claiming AI work as purely your own is dishonest. Dismissing your own analytical judgment as "just AI" understates the human expertise that shaped the prompt, selected the sources, and verified the output. Accurate representation is the standard.

> "Every one of you is a shepherd, and every one of you will be asked about his flock." — Hadith (Bukhari and Muslim)[^9]

The hadith applies directly. The document you sign is part of your flock. The data you handle is part of your flock. The AI tool you select and the outputs you submit — all of it is your responsibility.

### 12.2.2 Adl — Justice

**Adl** means justice and fairness. In Islamic governance, justice is not optional — it is obligatory. The Qur'an commands: "O you who have believed, be persistently standing firm in justice, witnesses for Allah, even if it be against yourselves or parents and relatives" (An-Nisa 4:135).[^10]

Applied to AI, adl requires you to ask: **Does this AI application treat everyone fairly?**

**Bias in AI is real.** Large language models are trained on internet text. That text reflects the biases of its authors — and most of that text was written in English by people in North America, Europe, and East Asia. When you use AI to analyze data about BARMM communities, the AI has almost no training data about Maguindanaon governance traditions, Tausug maritime culture, or Teduray highland communities.[^11] It will default to generalizations. Those generalizations may be wrong, and they may systematically disadvantage communities that are already underrepresented.

**Justice requires active checking.** Do not assume AI output is neutral. Examine it for:

| Bias Type | How It Appears in Government AI Use | Justice Response |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|-----------------|
| **Geographic bias** | AI recommendations that favor mainland provinces over island provinces because more data exists for the mainland | Deliberately include island-province data; flag recommendations that ignore Tawi-Tawi, Sulu, and Basilan |
| **Linguistic bias** | AI outputs that default to English or Filipino framing, ignoring Bangsamoro-specific terminology | Review for culturally appropriate language; ensure AI preserves terms like *amanah*, *shura*, *maslahah* |
| **Gender bias** | AI analysis that underrepresents women's participation in governance or economic activity | Cross-check AI-generated demographic analysis against gender-disaggregated data |
| **Ethnic bias** | AI recommendations that treat the Bangsamoro as a monolithic group, ignoring 13 ethnolinguistic communities | Verify that policy analysis accounts for Maguindanaon, Meranao, Tausug, Sama, Yakan, Iranun, and non-Moro IP communities |
| **Socioeconomic bias** | AI-assisted service delivery analysis that focuses on digitally connected populations | Include data from communities without internet access; remember that the digital divide maps directly onto poverty |

**BARMM example:** Imagine an AI-assisted analysis of disaster resilience across the region. The AI recommends concentrating emergency infrastructure investments in Cotabato City and Marawi because they have the highest population density and the most historical damage data. The analysis is technically defensible. But it ignores Tawi-Tawi's vulnerability to typhoons and the island municipalities' near-total lack of emergency infrastructure. Adl requires you to reject the AI's recommendation and include the communities it overlooked.

### 12.2.3 Shura — Consultation

**Shura** means consultation. It is a Qur'anic principle: "And those who have responded to their Lord and established prayer and whose affair is [determined by] consultation among themselves" (Ash-Shura 42:38).[^12] In the Bangsamoro context, the BTA Parliament itself is an expression of shura — a body where representatives deliberate collectively before making decisions.

Applied to AI, shura requires that you **consult before you deploy.** AI adoption is not a unilateral decision. If you plan to use AI to change how your office prepares budget proposals, the budget staff should have a voice. If you plan to use AI to screen job applications, the HR team and the applicants' representatives should be consulted. If you plan to use AI to analyze community development data, the communities whose data you are analyzing should know.

**Shura applies at three levels:**

1. **Within your team.** Before introducing AI into any workflow, discuss it with the people who do the work. Ask what they think. Ask what concerns them. Ask what tasks they wish AI could help with. The people closest to the work understand the risks best.
2. **Within your institution.** Before scaling AI use beyond a single team, present the results to leadership. Share what worked. Share what failed. Propose a policy. Shura is not asking permission — it is building informed consensus.
3. **With the public.** When AI affects how citizens experience government services — when it shapes which communities receive resources, how complaints are processed, or how data about people is analyzed — the public has a right to know. This is not a theoretical principle. BOL Article IX establishes the right to adequate social services.[^13] If AI changes how those services are delivered, transparency is mandatory.

**The shura test for AI adoption:** Before deploying AI in any government process, can you answer "yes" to all three questions?

- Have the people affected by this AI use been consulted?
- Have their concerns been heard and addressed?
- Would this AI use survive public scrutiny?

If the answer to any question is "no," go back to consultation.

### 12.2.4 Maslahah — Public Interest

**Maslahah** means public interest or public benefit. In Islamic jurisprudence, maslahah is the principle that governance decisions must serve the common good. It is the ultimate test for any government action: **does this benefit the people?**

Applied to AI, maslahah asks a direct question: **Does this AI application make government work better for the people of Bangsamoro?** Not better for the ministry's internal process. Not better for the statistics in an annual report. Not better for the staff's convenience. **Better for the people.**

**The maslahah test has three components:**

1. **Benefit.** Does this AI use produce a tangible benefit for the public? Faster service delivery? More accurate budget analysis? Better-informed legislation? If the benefit is purely internal efficiency with no public-facing improvement, reconsider.
2. **Harm avoidance.** Does this AI use create risks for any community? Could it lead to biased resource allocation? Could it expose personal data? Could it reduce human contact in services where human judgment is essential? Maslahah requires that you prevent harm before pursuing benefit.
3. **Proportionality.** Is the benefit proportional to the cost and risk? If deploying an AI tool requires a significant technology investment but serves only one office's convenience, maslahah may not be satisfied. If the same investment improves service delivery across five provinces, the calculus changes.

**BARMM application:** A ministry proposes using AI to automate the processing of social welfare applications. The maslahah analysis looks like this:

- **Benefit:** Processing time drops from 30 days to 7 days. More families receive assistance faster. Maslahah is served.
- **Harm risk:** The AI screening algorithm may reject applications from families who lack documentation — precisely the most vulnerable families. Maslahah is violated.
- **Resolution:** Use AI to accelerate processing but keep human reviewers for rejected applications. Require that every denial receives a manual second review within 48 hours. Maslahah is restored.

**Table 12.2: Islamic Principles as AI Guardrails**

| Principle | Arabic | Core Requirement | AI Guardrail | Violation Example |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Amanah** | Trust | Protect what is entrusted to you | Protect data; verify outputs; disclose AI use | Uploading confidential documents to an unsecured AI tool |
| **Adl** | Justice | Treat all people fairly | Audit AI outputs for bias against any community | AI-generated budget analysis that consistently underfunds island provinces |
| **Shura** | Consultation | Consult before deciding | Involve affected parties before deploying AI | Implementing AI-based service screening without consulting the communities being screened |
| **Maslahah** | Public Interest | Serve the common good | Evaluate every AI use against public benefit | Using AI to optimize internal workflows while citizens still wait months for services |

The table reveals a consistent pattern. **Every priority agenda item benefits from AI assistance — and every one requires human judgment to prevent AI from violating the very values the agenda is meant to serve.** There is no priority area where AI can operate without guardrails.

## 12.4 Risks and Safeguards

AI adoption in BARMM governance creates five categories of risk. Each one is real, each one is already present in some form, and each one requires specific safeguards.

### Risk 1: Bias

**What it is.** AI systems reflect the biases in their training data. Large language models are trained predominantly on English-language internet text written by people in the Global North. This means AI has limited understanding of Bangsamoro governance, culture, and context. When it generates analysis about BARMM, it fills gaps with generalizations that may be wrong.

**How it appears.** An AI-generated policy analysis recommends governance reforms based on best practices from countries with established democracies. The recommendations ignore that BARMM is in a transition period, operates under a parliamentary system within a presidential framework, and has a unique intergovernmental relationship with the national government through the BOL.[^16] The recommendations sound sophisticated but are irrelevant.

**Safeguard.** Always provide BARMM-specific context in your prompts. Supply the relevant BAA text, BDP chapter, or institutional data. Do not rely on the AI's general knowledge of Philippine governance — it is incomplete. Review every output for assumptions that do not match BARMM reality.

### Risk 2: Data Sovereignty

**What it is.** When you upload government documents to a commercial AI tool, the data leaves your control. Most AI providers process data on servers outside the Philippines. Some retain uploaded data for model training. This creates a sovereignty risk: government data about Bangsamoro citizens, budgets, and operations may be stored on foreign servers with no guarantee of privacy.

**How it appears.** A ministry uploads five years of provincial budget data to an AI tool for analysis. The AI provider's terms of service allow data retention for "service improvement." The budget data — including granular allocations by municipality — now sits on servers outside the Philippines, accessible to an unknown number of the provider's employees.

**Safeguard.** Establish a data classification system. Define which documents can be uploaded to external AI tools and which must stay within government-controlled systems. As a baseline:

- **Public documents** (published BAAs, resolutions, public reports): May be uploaded.
- **Internal working documents** (draft policies, internal memos, staff records): Upload only with anonymization.
- **Confidential documents** (security data, personnel records with PII, financial audit details): Do not upload to external AI tools under any circumstances.

### Risk 3: Over-Dependency

**What it is.** When AI produces good outputs consistently, staff may stop developing the skills that make those outputs possible. If the AI drafts every briefer, staff may lose the ability to draft one from scratch. If the AI summarizes every audit report, staff may lose the ability to read one critically.

**How it appears.** A new legislative analyst joins the team. She learns to use AI from Day 1. Two years later, the AI tool's subscription lapses. She cannot draft a legislative briefer without it — because she never learned the underlying analytical process. The tool replaced the skill instead of augmenting it.

**Safeguard.** Use the **"apprentice model"** described in the Supervision Guidebook. AI is the journeyman assistant, not the master. Staff must learn the craft first and use AI to amplify what they know.[^17] Periodically require manual work to ensure skills remain sharp. Include "AI-off" exercises in training programs.

### Risk 4: Digital Divide

**What it is.** AI tools require internet access, modern devices, and digital literacy. Not all BARMM staff have all three. Island provinces face connectivity challenges.[^18] Older staff may have limited experience with digital tools. Staff in remote field offices may lack the hardware to run AI applications.

**How it appears.** A ministry's central office in Cotabato City adopts AI tools and sees productivity improve dramatically. The provincial offices in Tawi-Tawi and Basilan do not have reliable internet. A two-tier bureaucracy emerges: fast, AI-augmented decision-making in the center; slow, manual processing in the periphery. The periphery — which serves the most underserved communities — falls further behind.

**Safeguard.** Design AI adoption with the periphery in mind. If AI tools require internet connectivity, pair them with offline alternatives. If training is available only in the central office, create recorded sessions for field staff. Track AI adoption rates by geographic location and address gaps proactively. The 12-Point Priority Agenda's commitment to inclusivity (agenda item 12) demands this.

### Risk 5: Inequality

**What it is.** AI adoption may create power asymmetries within government. Offices that adopt AI early gain advantages in document production, analysis speed, and institutional knowledge management. Offices that do not adopt AI fall behind. Over time, this creates a competitive imbalance that concentrates capability — and influence — in a few units.

**How it appears.** The Office of the Chief Minister's policy staff uses AI extensively. They produce comprehensive analyses faster than any ministry. Over time, ministries begin to defer to OCM analysis rather than developing their own. Institutional capacity concentrates at the top while ministries — the entities closest to service delivery — weaken.

**Safeguard.** Distribute AI training and resources equitably. Ensure that every ministry, not just central offices, has access to AI tools and the skills to use them. The Chapter 13 implementation roadmap addresses this through a phased adoption plan that prioritizes breadth over depth.

## 12.5 A Bangsamoro AI Ethics Framework

No existing AI ethics framework was designed for BARMM. Global frameworks from the EU, UNESCO, and OECD address AI governance in general terms but do not account for the Islamic governance tradition, the BOL's unique federal-asymmetric structure, or the specific challenges of a region in political transition.[^19]

BARMM needs its own framework. What follows is a proposed set of principles — grounded in the moral foundations already discussed in this chapter — that can guide AI adoption across the Bangsamoro government.

### The Seven Principles of Bangsamoro AI Ethics

**Principle 1: Human Authority Over AI (Khalifah)**

AI advises. Humans decide. No AI output replaces the judgment of an accountable public servant. The concept of *khalifah* (stewardship/vicegerency) in Islam means that humans are entrusted with authority on earth. AI is a tool within that trust — not a co-decision-maker.

**Practical application:** Every AI-assisted government process must have a designated human decision-maker whose name and designation appear on the final output.

**Principle 2: Truthfulness in AI-Assisted Outputs (Sidq)**

All AI-assisted documents must be verified for accuracy before submission. Sidq (truthfulness) is a non-negotiable Islamic value. Presenting AI-generated content as verified fact without actually verifying it violates sidq.

**Practical application:** Implement the verification protocol from Chapter 3. Check every citation, every data point, every attributed quote. If the AI output contains information you cannot verify, remove it or flag it as unverified.

**Principle 3: Justice in AI Application (Adl)**

AI must serve all communities equitably. No AI application should systematically advantage one ethnic group, province, gender, or socioeconomic class over another. If bias is detected, the AI application must be corrected or withdrawn.

**Practical application:** After any AI-assisted analysis that involves resource allocation, service delivery, or policy targeting, conduct a bias check. Ask: Which communities are included in this analysis? Which are missing? Would a Teduray community in the Maguindanao highlands experience this policy recommendation the same way a Meranao community in Marawi City would?

**Principle 4: Protection of Trust (Amanah)**

Government data is a public trust. It must be protected with the same diligence that applies to public funds. Data classification, access controls, and privacy protections apply to all AI-processed government information.

**Practical application:** Classify all government documents before processing them with AI tools. Maintain an AI usage log that records what documents were processed, which AI tool was used, and who authorized the processing.

**Principle 5: Consultation Before Deployment (Shura)**

No AI system should be deployed in a government process without consulting the people affected. This applies to government staff whose workflows will change, to communities whose data will be analyzed, and to Parliament when AI affects how laws are implemented.

**Practical application:** Before any ministry adopts AI for a new process, convene a consultation meeting with the relevant division staff. Document the consultation. Record concerns and how they were addressed.

**Principle 6: Public Benefit as the Standard (Maslahah)**

Every AI application in BARMM governance must demonstrate a public benefit. Internal efficiency that does not translate to better public service does not satisfy this principle. The benefit must be measurable and must reach the communities the government serves.

**Practical application:** For each AI adoption proposal, require a one-page maslahah assessment: What is the public benefit? Who benefits? Who might be harmed? How will harm be prevented? This assessment accompanies the adoption decision.

**Principle 7: Transparency and Accountability (Hisab)**

Hisab means accountability — the awareness that all actions will be accounted for. In AI governance, this means full transparency about how AI is used, what decisions it informs, and who is responsible for the outcomes.

**Practical application:** Publish an annual AI transparency report for each ministry or office that uses AI tools. The report lists: which AI tools are used, for what purposes, how many documents were AI-assisted, what safeguards are in place, and what issues were identified and corrected.

### Summary Table: The Seven Principles

| # | Principle | Arabic Root | One-Sentence Rule |
|---|-----------|------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Human Authority | Khalifah | AI advises; humans decide and sign. |
| 2 | Truthfulness | Sidq | Verify every AI output before submission. |
| 3 | Justice | Adl | Check every AI output for bias against any community. |
| 4 | Protection of Trust | Amanah | Classify data and protect it before AI processes it. |
| 5 | Consultation | Shura | Consult affected people before deploying AI. |
| 6 | Public Benefit | Maslahah | Every AI use must serve the public, not just the office. |
| 7 | Accountability | Hisab | Log, disclose, and report all government AI use. |

## 12.6 When AI Must Not Decide

Throughout this guidebook, the principle has been consistent: **AI prepares; humans decide.** But some decisions carry such weight — moral, legal, cultural, or political — that AI should not even prepare the ground without extraordinary caution. In some cases, AI must be excluded entirely.

This section draws a clear boundary.

### Category 1: Shari'ah Rulings

**AI must never issue, simulate, or recommend Shari'ah rulings.** The Bangsamoro justice system includes Shari'ah courts with jurisdiction over personal law — marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody — and property relations among Muslims.[^20] These courts draw their authority from the Qur'an and Sunnah. Their rulings require qualified Shari'ah judges trained in Islamic jurisprudence.

AI does not have Islamic jurisprudential training. It cannot weigh competing interpretations of hadith. It cannot consider the specific circumstances of a family dispute with the pastoral sensitivity that Islamic law requires. It cannot issue a ruling that carries religious and legal authority.

**What AI may do:** Organize case records for Shari'ah court clerks. Compile relevant precedents from prior court decisions. Format pleadings and legal documents. These are clerical functions.

**What AI must not do:** Recommend a ruling. Suggest how Islamic law should apply to a specific case. Generate a fatwa or anything resembling one. Draft a Shari'ah court decision.

### Category 2: Political Negotiations

**AI must not participate in political negotiations.** The Bangsamoro peace process — from the Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (2014) through the BOL's ratification (2019) and the ongoing normalization process — was built on human trust between parties who fought each other for decades.[^21] Political negotiations within the BTA — coalition-building, committee assignments, legislative bargaining — depend on relationships, cultural norms, and political judgment that no AI possesses.

**What AI may do:** Prepare background research for negotiators. Summarize historical agreements. Compare provisions across different drafts. These are research functions.

**What AI must not do:** Recommend negotiation positions. Predict political outcomes. Draft compromise language without explicit human direction on the terms of the compromise.

### Category 3: Cultural Determinations

**AI must not make cultural judgments.** The Bangsamoro region includes 13 ethnolinguistic groups with distinct traditions, customary laws, and cultural heritage.[^22] Determining whether a cultural practice should be preserved, how a tradition should be represented, or whether a heritage site qualifies for protection requires cultural expertise that AI does not have.

**What AI may do:** Assist in cultural documentation — transcribing oral histories, organizing artifact catalogs, mapping heritage sites.

**What AI must not do:** Determine the cultural significance of a practice. Recommend which traditions merit preservation. Represent any ethnolinguistic group's cultural values or positions.

### Category 4: Disciplinary Actions

**AI must not recommend disciplinary actions against government personnel.** The Bangsamoro Civil Service Code establishes due process requirements for administrative proceedings.[^23] Disciplinary actions affect livelihoods, reputations, and careers. They require the procedural safeguards that only human judgment can provide.

**What AI may do:** Compile attendance records, performance data, and prior corrective actions to support a supervisor's review.

**What AI must not do:** Recommend termination, suspension, or demotion. Assess whether an employee's behavior constitutes a violation. Draft the finding of fact in an administrative case.

### Category 5: Decisions Affecting Indigenous Peoples' Rights

**AI must not make decisions affecting the rights of non-Moro indigenous peoples.** BOL Article IX recognizes indigenous peoples' rights.[^24] The Teduray, Lambangian, Dulangan Manobo, and B'laan communities within BARMM have distinct governance traditions and customary laws that predate both Philippine national law and Islamic governance structures.[^25]

**What AI may do:** Assist in documenting IP customary laws and governance practices. Compile data for IP community development plans.

**What AI must not do:** Determine land use boundaries in ancestral domains. Recommend policies that affect IP communities without those communities' direct participation. Generate analysis that treats non-Moro IPs as a footnote to Bangsamoro governance.

### Category 6: Life-and-Death Decisions

**AI must not participate in decisions that directly affect human life and safety.** Disaster response triage, security threat assessments, and public health emergency decisions must remain fully human.

**What AI may do:** Compile weather data, analyze historical disaster patterns, and organize evacuation logistics information.

**What AI must not do:** Determine which communities receive emergency aid first. Assess which security threats are credible. Decide which patients receive scarce medical resources.

### The Boundaries List

For quick reference, post this list in your office alongside Table 12.1:

**AI MUST NOT:**

1. Issue, simulate, or recommend Shari'ah rulings
2. Recommend political negotiation positions
3. Make cultural judgments about Bangsamoro heritage
4. Recommend disciplinary actions against personnel
5. Determine policies affecting indigenous peoples' rights without IP participation
6. Make decisions that directly affect human life and safety
7. Replace the signature authority of any accountable public servant
8. Operate without a designated human decision-maker
9. Process classified security or peace-process documents on external systems
10. Deny government services to citizens without human review

**If you are unsure whether AI should be involved in a decision, apply this test:** Would you be comfortable if a newspaper reported that this decision was made with AI assistance? If the answer is no — or even "maybe" — keep AI out of it.

## Chapter Summary

This chapter established the ethical foundation for AI adoption in BARMM governance. The foundation is not imported. It is Bangsamoro.

**The 12 Pillars of Moral Governance** apply to AI just as they apply to every other government tool. Table 12.1 maps each pillar to a specific AI governance obligation. **Transparency and Honesty** — Pillar 3 — requires that every AI-assisted document be labeled, that AI's contribution be identified, and that human verification be confirmed.

**Four Islamic principles** — amanah, adl, shura, and maslahah — create guardrails that predate AI by over a millennium. Amanah protects data. Adl prevents bias. Shura requires consultation. Maslahah demands public benefit.

**The Enhanced 12-Point Priority Agenda** benefits from AI in every priority area — and requires ethical boundaries in every priority area. Table 12.3 maps the opportunities and the limits.

**Five categories of risk** — bias, data sovereignty, over-dependency, digital divide, and inequality — require specific safeguards. None of these risks is hypothetical. All are present today.

**Seven principles** constitute a proposed Bangsamoro AI Ethics Framework, rooted in Islamic governance and Moral Governance traditions. Each principle is actionable. Each one names a specific obligation.

**Six categories of decisions** must exclude AI entirely or restrict it to clerical support: Shari'ah rulings, political negotiations, cultural determinations, disciplinary actions, IP rights decisions, and life-and-death decisions.

The principle that runs through this entire guidebook reaches its sharpest expression in this chapter: **AI is a tool. You are the authority. The ethics are yours.**
