# Chapter 9 — Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Every speechwriter makes mistakes. The difference between a good speechwriter and a great one is how quickly you learn from them. This chapter catalogs the most common mistakes in Bangsamoro parliamentary speech writing and shows you how to prevent each one. Read this chapter early. Revisit it after every speech that does not land well.

## 9.1 Factual Errors

Factual errors are the most damaging mistakes a speechwriter can make. One wrong name, one incorrect statistic, one misquoted legal provision can overshadow every other element of the speech.

### 9.1.1 Wrong Names, Titles, and Designations

**The mistake:** Writing "Speaker Macacua" when the Speaker is **Mohammad S. Yacob** and Macacua is the Chief Minister. Calling an MP "Congressman." Using "Governor" instead of "Chief Minister."

**Why it happens:** Speechwriters working from memory, using outdated reference materials, or confusing BARMM titles with national government equivalents.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Keep the BARMM officials reference updated and accessible at your desk.
2. Verify every name and title against current official records before finalizing a speech.
3. Use the correct BARMM titles: Speaker (not Senate President), Chief Minister (not Governor), Member of Parliament (not Congressman/Congresswoman), Minister (not Secretary).
4. Check for recent changes. The Bangsamoro Parliament's leadership changed in October 2025. Use current titles.

**BARMM vs. National Title Equivalents:**

| Incorrect (National) | Correct (BARMM) |
|---|---|
| Governor | Chief Minister |
| Vice Governor | Deputy Chief Minister |
| Congressman/Congresswoman | Member of Parliament / MP |
| Senate President | Speaker |
| Department Secretary | Minister |

### 9.1.2 Incorrect Legal Citations (BOL, BAA, RA)

**The mistake:** Citing "Article VII, Section 12" when the provision is in Article IX. Calling a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act a "Republic Act." Citing a repealed provision.

**Why it happens:** Working from memory, copying citations from secondary sources, or confusing the BOL's article structure.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Always verify citations against the actual text of the law.
2. Use the correct terminology: **Republic Act 11054** is the BOL. **BAA** is a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act passed by the Parliament. **RA** is a Republic Act passed by the Philippine Congress.
3. Check whether cited provisions have been amended by subsequent BAAs.
4. Keep digital copies of the BOL and key BAAs on your device for quick reference.

### 9.1.3 Outdated Statistics and Data

**The mistake:** Citing 2020 data in a 2026 speech. Using pre-BARMM statistics that do not reflect current conditions. Quoting a target that has already been achieved or revised.

**Why it happens:** Reusing data from old speeches. Relying on news articles instead of primary sources.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Check the date on every statistic. If the data is more than two years old, find a more recent source.
2. Use primary sources: Philippine Statistics Authority, BARMM ministry reports, BDP baseline data.
3. Note the data source and date in the speech text: "According to the PSA's 2024 survey..." This forces you to verify currency.
4. Update your data file regularly. Keep a running document of frequently cited statistics with their sources and dates.

## 9.2 Protocol Violations

### 9.2.1 Incorrect Order of Recognition

**The mistake:** Greeting the Deputy Speaker before the Speaker. Recognizing a Minister before the Chief Minister. Listing dignitaries out of protocol order.

**Why it happens:** Not knowing the protocol hierarchy. Copying greetings from a previous speech without adjusting for the current event.

**How to prevent it:**

Follow this recognition order for parliamentary speeches:

1. The Speaker
2. Deputy Speakers (if present)
3. The Chief Minister (if present and not the speaker)
4. Ministers (in order of precedence)
5. Fellow Members of Parliament
6. External dignitaries (by rank)
7. Audience/public

For external events, verify the protocol order with the organizing office. Each event may have its own hierarchy depending on who is present.

### 9.2.2 Missing or Improper Islamic Greetings

**The mistake:** Skipping the Basmala. Using a truncated Salam ("Assalamu Alaikum" without "Warahmatullahi Wabarakatuh" in a formal setting). Placing the Islamic greeting after the recognition of dignitaries instead of before.

**Why it happens:** Unfamiliarity with Islamic protocols. Treating the greeting as optional.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Open every formal speech with the Basmala and full Salam. No exceptions.
2. Place the Islamic greeting first, before recognizing dignitaries.
3. Match the greeting level to the occasion (see Chapter 6, Section 6.1.4).
4. Include the closing Islamic formula: "Wassalamu Alaikum Warahmatullahi Wabarakatuh."

### 9.2.3 Wrong Honorifics

**The mistake:** Addressing a Sultan as "Datu." Calling an Aleem "Ustadz." Using "Attorney" for someone who is not a member of the Philippine Bar.

**Why it happens:** Not verifying the correct title. Assuming all traditional leaders use the same title.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Research each individual's correct title. Ask their office if you are unsure.
2. Use the title the person uses for themselves. Do not impose a title.
3. When in doubt, "Honorable" is safe for elected and appointed officials.
4. For religious titles, verify with someone who knows the individual's standing in the Islamic scholarly community.

## 9.3 Structural Problems

### 9.3.1 Speeches That Are Too Long

**The mistake:** Writing a 30-minute speech for a 10-minute slot. Adding section after section without cutting anything. Including interesting but irrelevant information.

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter tries to include everything. The MP keeps adding requests. No one enforces the time limit during drafting.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Calculate your target word count before drafting. Average speaking pace: 130-150 words per minute. A 10-minute speech is 1,300-1,500 words.
2. Time your read-aloud test. If it runs long, cut content. Do not speed up.
3. Apply the **"so what?" test** to every paragraph. If cutting it does not weaken the key message, cut it.
4. Give your MP the time limit in writing. "Mr. Speaker, this speech is drafted for 15 minutes. Adding the section on [topic] will extend it to 22 minutes."

**Speaking pace reference:**

| Minutes | Words (at 140 wpm) |
|---|---|
| 1 minute | 140 words |
| 5 minutes | 700 words |
| 10 minutes | 1,400 words |
| 15 minutes | 2,100 words |
| 20 minutes | 2,800 words |
| 30 minutes | 4,200 words |

### 9.3.2 Missing the Key Message

**The mistake:** The speech covers five topics but the audience cannot identify the main point. The speech starts with the key message but buries it under secondary arguments. The closing does not reinforce the opening.

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter did not identify the key message before drafting. The MP changed direction during revision without refocusing the speech.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Write the key message in one sentence before you start the outline.
2. State the key message in the opening (first 2 minutes).
3. Support the key message in the body (every section should serve it).
4. Restate the key message in the closing (last 2 minutes).
5. After drafting, ask a colleague to read the speech and tell you the main point. If they cannot, revise.

### 9.3.3 Weak Openings and Closings

**The mistake:** Opening with "Good morning, I am honored to be here today." Closing with "Thank you, and may God bless us all." Both are forgettable.

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter saves their best material for the body and treats the opening and closing as formalities.

**How to prevent it:**

Write the opening and closing first. They are the two most important parts of the speech. The opening determines whether the audience listens. The closing determines what they remember.

**Strong openings:**
- A striking fact: "40% of Bangsamoro youth are not in school."
- A question: "How many schools have we built since 2019? The answer may surprise you."
- A story: "Last month, a teacher in Talayan told me..."
- A challenge: "We pass laws. But are our laws reaching the communities that need them?"

**Strong closings:**
- A callback to the opening: "That teacher in Talayan is watching us today."
- A direct call to action: "Vote yes. Our students cannot afford to wait."
- A memorable phrase: "Peace is not a destination. It is the road we build while walking it."

## 9.4 Language and Style Errors

### 9.4.1 Inconsistent Language (Mixing Registers Without Purpose)

**The mistake:** Switching from formal English to casual Taglish mid-paragraph without rhetorical purpose. Using academic language in one section and street language in the next.

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter does not have a clear language strategy. Different sections were drafted at different times or by different writers.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Choose the dominant language register before drafting (formal English, formal Filipino, Taglish, etc.).
2. Code-switch deliberately for effect, not accidentally. Each language switch should serve a purpose: emphasis, connection, or cultural signaling.
3. Review the full speech for consistency. If one section sounds like a different speaker, revise it.

### 9.4.2 AI-Generated Slop and Generic Phrasing

**The mistake:** Speeches that sound like they were generated by an AI chatbot — filled with phrases like "leveraging synergies," "fostering inclusive growth," and "at the end of the day."

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter uses AI tools without substantial editing, or they default to generic governance language instead of writing with specificity.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Cut every sentence that could appear in any speech from any legislature anywhere. If the sentence does not mention something specific to the Bangsamoro, it is probably too generic.
2. Replace abstract language with concrete examples. Not "fostering inclusive growth" but "opening 15 livelihood centers in the poorest municipalities."
3. If you use AI tools for research or drafting, rewrite every line in the MP's voice. AI-generated text is a starting point, not a final product.
4. Read the speech aloud. AI-generated text sounds robotic when spoken. Human-written text flows naturally.

**Prohibited phrases (these signal lazy writing):**
- "Leveraging synergies"
- "Moving forward together"
- "At the end of the day"
- "Each and every one of us"
- "We stand at a crossroads"
- "A paradigm shift"
- "Stakeholder engagement"
- "Holistic approach"

Replace each with a specific claim, a concrete action, or a vivid description.

### 9.4.3 Dense Paragraphs Instead of Speakable Text

**The mistake:** Writing paragraphs of 8-10 sentences with complex subordinate clauses. The text reads well on paper but is impossible to deliver.

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter writes essays, not speeches. They forget that the audience hears the speech once — they cannot re-read a confusing sentence.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Maximum 5 sentences per paragraph.
2. Average 15-20 words per sentence. Maximum 30.
3. One idea per sentence.
4. Read aloud. If you stumble, simplify.
5. Use the formatting techniques from Chapter 5: pause marks, emphasis marks, and section breaks.

## 9.5 Political and Cultural Missteps

### 9.5.1 Inadvertent Partisanship

**The mistake:** Using language that implicitly favors one political faction over another. Praising the MILF's contribution to the peace process while ignoring the MNLF's. Using a phrasing that echoes a political rival's talking points.

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter shares the MP's political alignment and does not notice the bias.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Have someone outside your MP's political circle review sensitive speeches.
2. When referencing the peace process, acknowledge both the MILF and MNLF.
3. When citing governance achievements, attribute them to the Parliament or the BARMM government, not to a faction.
4. Avoid language that echoes specific political campaigns.

### 9.5.2 Cultural Insensitivity

**The mistake:** Referencing only Meranaw or Maguindanaon examples in a speech about the entire Bangsamoro. Using a term that is respectful in one community but offensive in another. Ignoring non-Moro communities in the BARMM.

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter defaults to the communities they know best.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Include examples from multiple ethnolinguistic groups when speaking about the Bangsamoro as a whole.
2. Verify culturally specific terms with members of the relevant community.
3. Acknowledge indigenous peoples and settler communities in region-wide speeches.
4. When unsure about cultural sensitivity, consult a colleague from the community in question.

### 9.5.3 Overpromising and Unsubstantiated Claims

**The mistake:** "This bill will end poverty in the Bangsamoro." "We have achieved 100% school enrollment." Claims that are easily disproven or impossible to verify.

**Why it happens:** The speechwriter wants the speech to sound impressive. The MP wants to make a strong case.

**How to prevent it:**

1. Verify every claim. If you cannot find a source, do not make the claim.
2. Use qualified language: "This bill aims to..." rather than "This bill will..."
3. For targets, cite the source: "The BDP targets a 30% reduction in poverty by 2028."
4. For achievements, cite the data: "As of December 2025, the AHME program has supported 8,000 scholars."

## 9.6 Learning from Mistakes: A Self-Correction Framework

When a speech goes wrong, conduct a post-mortem. Use this framework:

1. **Identify the error.** What specifically went wrong? Be precise.
2. **Determine the cause.** Was it a research failure, a drafting error, a review gap, or a last-minute change?
3. **Assess the impact.** How did the error affect the speech's reception? Was it noticed publicly?
4. **Implement a fix.** What process change prevents this error from recurring?
5. **Document the lesson.** Add the lesson to your personal quality reference file.

| Error Category | Common Cause | Process Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Wrong name/title | Outdated reference file | Update the officials reference monthly |
| Wrong citation | Working from memory | Always verify against the actual law text |
| Too long | No word count target | Set word count limit before drafting |
| Missed key message | No outline approval | Always get outline approved before drafting |
| Protocol violation | Unfamiliarity with protocol | Keep the protocol checklist at your desk |
| Cultural misstep | Limited perspective | Have a diverse reviewer check sensitive speeches |

---

**Key Takeaways:**

- Factual errors are the most damaging. Verify every name, title, statistic, and legal citation.
- Protocol violations undermine your MP's credibility. Learn the Islamic greeting protocols and recognition order.
- Long speeches lose their audience. Set word count targets and enforce them.
- Generic, AI-generated language signals lazy writing. Write with specificity.
- Conduct post-mortems after every speech that does not land well. Document the lesson.
