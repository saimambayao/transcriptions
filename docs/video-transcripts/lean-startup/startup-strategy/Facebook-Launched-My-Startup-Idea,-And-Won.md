**The Timing Trifecta Ingredient #1: Inflections**

**04:03**
Ingredient number one is inflections. Every successful idea is baked in one or more external events that signal that the new idea's time has come. These events break the old way and enable a path for the new way.

**04:18**
The most common and obvious type of inflection is a technology inflection. Things like the internet, cloud computing, and now AI are all examples of external changes that challenge the status quo solutions and signal a change towards something new and better. These new products get the same job done but differently and, the key part here is, significantly better.

**04:41**
Another example of an inflection is a behavioral inflection. COVID, for instance, broke the old way of working and paved the way for remote work. Now remote work is not a new concept; I've been working remotely myself since 2004, but this one external event triggered a massive switch globally.

**04:59**
There are at least half a dozen other types of inflections that break the old way and enable the new way. Identifying them for your idea is the first key ingredient in stress-testing the timing of your idea.

**The Timing Trifecta Ingredient #2: Impact**

**05:13**
Ingredient number two is impact. An inflection without measurable stakes that force behavior change doesn't create forward motion because of customer inertia. Because people are set in their old habits, they ignore inflections until there are real stakes at play.

**05:30**
Climate change is the perfect illustration of this. For change to then happen:
1. The old way must hurt enough to drive switching behavior.
2. The new way must deliver enough value to justify the switching cost.

**The Timing Trifecta Ingredient #3: Insight**

**05:45**
And the final and most important ingredient in the trifecta is ingredient number three: insight. An insight is often a contrarian perspective that founders have about why now is the right time for their idea, and this contrarian insight is often something that other people don't yet see.

**06:04**
These insights could stem from:
1. A market insight: What's changing about customer behavior that creates this opening?
2. A technology insight: What capability is now possible that wasn't before?
3. Or even a business model insight: What new economic model that wasn't viable before, now becomes viable now?

**06:22**
As Peter Thiel says in Zero to One, these insights are like secrets—obvious in hindsight, but the hard part is requiring the foresight to see them. One way of getting to insights, which is often the more common way you find in practice, is to simply guess at them. But guessing is largely unpredictable and suboptimal.

**06:42**
The better way is to systematically study existing customer behavior to uncover these insights. And I find that running carefully scripted customer interviews is the fastest way to do that. There's a lot of content on this channel that you can find on how to do that. If you're interested, you should check out this video over here.

**Applying the Trifecta: 6Degrees vs. Facebook**

**07:00**
Coming back to timing though, when all three ingredients align—external change, a compelling reason to switch, and one or more unique insights—you get Facebook timing, not 6Degrees timing.

**07:12**
Let me show you why my product, 6Degrees, failed the Timing Trifecta test.
On inflections, it gets a partial pass. The internet was mature, broadband was spreading, but not yet widespread. That was the first bet I was making that didn't pan out as quickly as I needed it to. And the reason this was important is that my peer-to-peer solution relied on reliable high-speed internet, and while I'd expected everyone would have it within a year, that was a flawed assumption.

**07:41**
On impact, my idea failed. The problem I was solving, private professional networking, wasn't painful or popular enough. People had email and phone calls and networking events. The status quo worked just fine enough.

**07:55**
But where my idea really failed the test was on insight. My key insight was that people wanted private social networks, and everything I built hinged on this key differentiator. But time proved that this belief ended up not being an insight but rather a blindside. People actually wanted to share publicly and connect with strangers.

**08:15**
Facebook, on the other hand, launched three years later, and this is how they would have scored. Let's start with inflections. First being cloud-based, they didn't face the same technical challenges I had with my peer-to-peer infrastructure. Next, at the time, we were going through a digital camera inflection where online photo sharing was becoming normal and most college students were already online.

**08:37**
On impact, Facebook leaned in on social isolation on campus, the need for digital identity, and FOMO from missing connection. If you got tagged by a friend, for instance, you just had to see it. This is what drove massive switching behavior on college campuses.

**08:53**
And their real genius though was baked in a simple insight: that social networking should be about real identity and college communities, not anonymous networking that was prevalent on the internet at the time. People already belonged to offline social networks; Facebook first transferred them online and then helped people extend them.

**09:14**
This is how the same kernel of an idea, like connecting people online, can result in very different business models and how timing plays the key deciding factor between success and failure.
