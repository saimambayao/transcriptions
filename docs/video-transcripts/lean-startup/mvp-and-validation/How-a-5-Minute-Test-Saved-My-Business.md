**The 5-Minute Test That Saved My Business: CloudFire Case Study**

**Introduction: The Speed Myth**

**00:00**
You have probably heard that speed is a startup's only unfair advantage: move fast, break things, get outside the building, go talk to customers, and start building an MVP.

**00:09**
But here is the thing: simply moving fast is not enough. In fact, it can be a recipe for just getting lost faster. I learned this lesson the hard way with a product I built called CloudFire, where I seemingly did everything by the book.

**00:23**
But it wasn't until I learned about a five-minute test that I realized I was heading down a dead end. This five-minute test saved my business, and I'm going to share that story with you today.

**00:34**
Hi there, if you're new to this channel, my name is Ash Maurya. **CloudFire: The "Scratch Your Own Itch" Product**

**00:47**
Okay, so CloudFire was a classic "scratch your own itch" product. I had recently become a parent and watched my wife struggle with sharing photos and videos with family and friends.

**00:57**
On the one hand, she was taking a lot more photos and videos of the new baby than ever before, which she wanted to share regularly with friends and family. But on the other hand, neither of us were hardly getting any sleep and had no time to be uploading, organizing, and sharing photos.

**01:13**
To put this in context, this was around the 2009 timeframe when sharing digital photos was still pretty clunky. And so I had the idea of building a desktop product that would automatically sync photos and videos on your computer to the cloud and make sharing with friends and family really simple.

**Applying the "Sell Before You Build" Approach**

**01:32**
Now, I already had one failed product under my belt by this point, and I didn't want to rush into building another product that nobody wanted. So this time around, I decided to do things by the book.

**01:42**
I reached out to a few friends who had also become new dads like myself to see if they too were struggling with this problem. They quickly pointed me to the moms, who were doing most of the sharing.

**01:54**
With the help of my wife's network, referrals from other friends, and a few other channels, I conducted almost 40 problem interviews over the next six weeks.

**02:03**
What I found was that almost everyone I spoke with related to the struggle of sharing lots of photos and videos. Their typical workaround was hand-picking a couple of pictures every week and then emailing them as an attachment to family.

**02:16**
This only reinforced my problem assumptions, and my confidence grew. Over the next two weeks, I stitched together a quick demo using a few screenshots from my product, which I hadn't fully built yet, to highlight how easily one could share a folder full of photos and videos in just minutes versus hours.

**Testing Demand with Demo-Sell-Build**

**02:34**
Then I spent the next four weeks pitching this concept. Now, those of you familiar with this channel will recognize this as the demo-sell-build, or sell before you build approach, where the goal is to test demand for a new product by selling a demo before building the product.

**02:50**
In other words, if you can't sell the demo, why build the product? In my case, I managed to get over 70% of my prospects to agree to test drive my product, which I had packaged as a 30-day trial followed by a $49 a year software product.

**Friction: Separating Users from Customers**

**03:05**
Now, I didn't want to just rely on verbal commitments at this point because a lot of people politely lie to you at the stage where they say they are interested and then don't follow up to buy your product when the time comes.

**03:18**
So to secure a more tangible commitment, I had them fill out an early access sign-up form where I collected their credit card information. And sure enough, half of my prospects who told me they loved my demo decided not to move forward.

**03:31**
Just a little bit of sign-up friction goes a long way to separating users from customers. Now, I still ended up with several dozen people ready to pay for my product, and so I was off to the races.

**MVP Development and Initial Results**

**03:43**
I spent the next two months getting the first version of my product, or MVP, out the door and into the hands of the first batch of my customers.

**03:52**
Now, during this time, I ran many in-person sessions with parents, showing them how to use the product, recording quick-start videos, and answering tons of support questions.

**04:02**
Then came the moment of truth: would people keep paying past the 30 days? Now, no funnel converts at 100%, and I ended up with a 20% conversion rate. Not all that great, but still encouraging to keep moving forward.

**The Scalability Wake-Up Call**

**04:15**
And so over the next several months, I continually refined my product, signed up more customers, and grew my paying customer base. Fast-forward nine months, I had 500 paying customers, which seemed like great progress.

**04:28**
And that's when I had my wake-up call. I knew I'd eventually need to bring on a co-founder, but even with 500 paying customers, the product just wasn't making enough revenue to justify that yet. 500 customers times $49 a year equals $24,500 a year.

**Consulting an Expert**

**04:40**
Now, maybe it was time to raise a seed funding round, and so I reached out to a longtime friend who was also an angel investor for advice. I gave him a quick update and asked for his advice on fundraising at this point. And here's how that conversation went.

**04:54**
He told me that while I had made a lot of progress, I might need to show better metrics to really get an investor's attention.

**05:01**
I said, "Well, I know the revenue number isn't that impressive yet, but doesn't having 500 customers help de-risk the product for an early-stage investor? After all, the market for parents is massive; there's a ton of growth potential."

**05:13**
He said, "Yes, that's a great start. But you still need to show a clear roadmap—how you're going to meet an investor's expectations around product-market fit."

**The Five-Minute Viability Calculation**

**05:22**
Right now, his instincts were telling him something else. And that's when he pulled out his notebook and walked me through a simple five-minute back-of-the-envelope calculation that changed everything.

**05:36**
Then he drew a simple traction roadmap and told me that to be able to pitch my idea to an investor, I'd ideally need to increase the number of customers I had by fourfold, from 500 to 2,000, in the next three months.

**05:50**
And then in the next year, I needed to grow that number to 20,000 paying customers, and then to 200,000 customers by the following year. Now, I was totally shocked because these numbers were a lot bigger than I thought.

**06:03**
Even factoring a 10% conversion rate, which is already quite high for a product like this one, I'd need a funnel with millions of leads to pull that off. And that's when a lightbulb went off for me.

**The Pivot: Professional Customers and Pricing**

**06:14**
I said to him, "I noticed one of the key metrics you looked at was my pricing model—which is also why the customer numbers are so high. If I could charge more for the product, I'd need fewer customers, right?"

**06:27**
And he said, "Yeah, pricing is one of the key inputs for business model viability. But can you really charge parents much more than $50 a year? Their current solution is email, and that's free."

**06:39**
I told him I wasn't really thinking about parents anymore. Just the other day, a wedding photographer—who's also a parent—emailed me. He had signed up for CloudFire and loved it. He actually asked if I'd consider creating a Pro version for photographers to use with their clients.

**06:58**
He said he's taking thousands of wedding photos every week and struggling to manage everything with his current tools. My friend then asked me if I needed to build a completely new product for him.

**07:10**
Not really. I'd just need to add some integrations with tools like Photoshop and Lightroom. It's not that complicated. And since it'd be more of a B2B offering, I was thinking I could probably charge three or four times more.

**07:27**
And that's when my friend's eyes lit up and he told me to go bigger. Charge 12 times more. Instead of $49 a year, start at $49 a month. You could probably raise it higher over time. He told me to set up the call with the wedding photographer immediately.

**The Lesson: Business Viability Before Building**

**07:44**
On the ride home from my meeting, while I was deflated about my busy parents business model, I was excited about the potential pivot to a more promising customer segment and was glad I had reached out to my friend when I did.

**07:57**
But then it hit me: nothing we did in that back-of-the-envelope calculation required a working product. I could have done that same exact math nine months ago and would have reached the exact same conclusion!

**08:09**
In my exuberance for getting outside the building, I had rushed out too quickly. I seemingly did everything by the book: I had sketched a one-page Lean Canvas, ran problem interviews with potential customers, pitched an offer using demo-sell-build, built a quick MVP, and even charged my customers after a 30-day trial, and I had a growing customer base.

**08:30**
All this sounds exactly like what you should do, but I realized that the real danger is getting stuck even after all that with a sub-optimal business model six or nine months down the road that either doesn't match your ambition or isn't scalable and you get stuck in a zombie startup that is neither dead nor alive. You're just stuck.
