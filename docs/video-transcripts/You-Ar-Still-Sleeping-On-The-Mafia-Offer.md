**Storytelling That Makes Saying No Irrational**

**01:37**
And here's the key part: it's not about being the cheapest or even the best, it's about telling a story that makes saying no feel irrational. So here's the 4-act structure.

**Act 1: The Undeniable Triggering Event**

**01:47**
Act 1: Set context with an undeniable triggering event. Your goal here is showing them how their world has changed and make them feel the ground shifting beneath their feet.

**Highlighting Market Shifts and Progress Gaps**

**01:58**
You do this by highlighting an undeniable external market shift, independent of your product, that's creating winners and losers in the marketplace. When done well, they should be able to visualize a progress gap between where they are now and where they could be.

**Act 2: Breaking the Status Quo**

**02:14**
Act 2: Break the status quo. This is where you show them how their current solution or existing alternative is incapable of bridging the progress gap that you identified in Act 1.

**Exposing Frustrations with Current Solutions**

**02:26**
You do this by clearly naming the existing alternative they already use and then go on to describe specific, familiar, and compelling pet peeves, struggles, and workarounds that they are currently living with. When done well, they should start seeing you as the expert, which creates the opening you need for Act 3.

**Act 3: Demonstrating the "New Way"**

**02:46**
And this is where you demonstrate your different and better New Way. The goal here is showing the transformation, not the breadth of your product's features. This is exactly where I was going off course before my conversation with my mentor friend.

**Focusing on Transformation over Features**

**03:01**
I was speaking fast during the demo and trying to cram a dozen features in 30 minutes, which was not convincing my customers but rather scaring them off. Since then, I've learned that the art of a good demo is showing the smallest thing possible to make the sale.

**The Art of the Minimal Demo**

**03:16**
You do this by showing just what's needed to solve the problems you identified in Act 2 and nothing more. When done well, your customers will either ask you to see more, which is your cue to then show them more features, or better yet, they'll ask how they can try your product now, which is the natural segue to the final act.

**Act 4: The Strategic Call to Action**

**03:36**
Act 4: Call to Action. Like a lot of founders, this is another area I used to fumble before, especially when it came to pricing. If you have run through Acts 1 through 3, talking about pricing shouldn't be an emotional experience but rather a natural extension to your story.

**Summarizing the Story Journey**

**03:54**
You've already set context, identified what they currently use, why it's broken, and what are the stakes and costs of doing nothing. And you've also shown them a different and better New Way.

**Anchoring Price and Clarifying Next Steps**

**04:08**
And if they are here in Act 4, all that is left to do is anchoring your product's price to the price of their existing alternatives, demonstrate how you arrived at your pricing model, and present them with clarity on next steps to get started.

**The Goal: Making Action Inevitable**

**04:19**
When done well, you should end up with a fair pricing model that they can understand and a binary choice: buy or don't buy. Most people pitch features and beg for the sale, but a true Mafia Offer tells a story that makes action inevitable.
