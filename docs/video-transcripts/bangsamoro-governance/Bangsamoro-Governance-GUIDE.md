# Bangsamoro Governance

The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao is building a government from scratch -- not merely administering inherited structures, but legislating, staffing, and institutionalizing an entirely new political entity within a constitutional deadline. The stakes are existential: if the transition fails to produce durable systems before the first regular elections, the autonomy that decades of armed struggle secured could collapse into dysfunction.

This guide synthesizes the governance patterns that emerged from the 1st Bangsamoro Government Executive-Legislative Forum 2026 -- the first time the BTA Parliament, Cabinet, governors, mayors, and LGU leaders convened in a single room to align priorities. The forum revealed both the ambition and the friction points of autonomous governance: a legislative body that has enacted thirteen laws and six fundamental codes, an executive branch operating under a five-pillar framework, and a constellation of local governments struggling with data conflicts, program duplication, and infrastructure gaps that no single institution can solve alone.

---

## Unity as Governance Architecture, Not Rhetoric

The most consequential message from the forum came not from a policy presentation but from the Wali of the Bangsamoro, Sheikh Muslim M. Guiamaden, who reframed unity as an institutional design principle. His argument -- that managing diversity within a framework of unity is the source of strength, not uniformity -- provides the philosophical foundation for how autonomous regions should structure decision-making across ethnic, geographic, and institutional lines. [^1]

> **Key insight**: The greatest danger to autonomous governance is not disagreement but the transformation of disagreement into institutional division that weakens the collective capacity to govern. [^1]

This principle has immediate structural implications. A parliamentary system with 80 members representing five provinces, one city, and special geographic areas must accommodate competing development priorities without fragmenting into factional gridlock. The Wali's framework suggests that alignment mechanisms -- shared forums, joint planning sessions, unified data systems -- are not administrative conveniences but existential requirements for autonomy to function.

The Chief Minister reinforced this by noting that "the people do not distinguish between executive and legislative, between regional and provincial authority. They see only one government -- and they expect that government to function clearly, efficiently, and responsibly." [^1] When citizens cannot tell which level of government is responsible for a pothole or a hospital, institutional fragmentation becomes a legitimacy crisis.

**The takeaway**: Autonomous governance survives not by enforcing agreement but by building institutions that convert disagreement into coordinated action.

## The Five-Pillar Framework: From Campaign Slogan to Operating System

Chief Minister Abdulraof A. Macacua's "Mas Matatag" (Stronger) framework organizes governance around five pillars: Stronger Government (institutions, merit, digital governance), Stronger Livelihood (MSMEs, agriculture, Islamic finance), Stronger Communities (barangay-level development), Stronger Security (peace through systems, not personalities), and Stronger Faith (Madrasah education, Sharia, interfaith harmony). [^1]

> **Key insight**: "We must institutionalize peace so it does not depend on personality, but on system." [^1]

What elevates this beyond a typical governance agenda is the Chief Minister's explicit insistence on systems over personalities -- a rare acknowledgment in Philippine politics that institutional continuity matters more than individual leadership. With the first regular BARMM elections expected in September or October 2026, every policy, program, and institutional structure built during the transition must be designed to survive a change in leadership. [^2]

The framework also exposes the coordination problem at the heart of autonomous governance. As the Chief Minister stated: "If we pass budget without coordination, we delay progress. If we implement without alignment, we waste resources. We cannot afford duplication. We cannot afford contradiction. We cannot afford institutional competition." [^1] This is not a theoretical concern -- the forum surfaced concrete examples of ministries producing conflicting data, programs overlapping across agencies, and LGUs unable to determine which regional office to coordinate with. [^2][^4]

**The takeaway**: A governance framework only works if every level of government can see itself in the structure and knows which pillar its work serves.

## Legislative Productivity Under a Constitutional Clock

The BTA Parliament's legislative output reveals both ambition and urgency. Since October 2025, seven new laws have been enacted, including the Bangsamoro Labor Code, Budget System Act, Parliamentary District Act, and the Transitional Justice and Reconciliation Program. Six fundamental codes -- Administrative, Civil Service, Electoral, Education, Indigenous Peoples, and Local Governance -- now provide the statutory infrastructure for LGU operations. Seven BOL-mandated priority bills remain in various stages of committee deliberation. [^1][^3]

> **Key insight**: "We pass laws, you bring them to life. We define policies, you translate them into services." [^1]

Speaker Mohammad S. Yacob's framing of Parliament's relationship with LGUs as co-leadership -- not a top-down mandate -- reflects the reality that legislation without local implementation capacity is meaningless. The Revenue Code, the last remaining priority code mandated by the BOL, is nearly complete with only one MP reservation outstanding. [^1] The Gender and Development Code has reached its amendment period after Darul Ifta resolved contentious provisions. [^1][^2]

Parliament has also invested in transparency mechanisms: livestreaming plenary sessions and committee hearings, publishing resolutions and session summaries online, and appointing an official spokesperson. These measures address a legitimacy gap that autonomous institutions face -- the need to demonstrate to both constituents and the national government that legislative processes are open and accountable. [^1]

**The takeaway**: Legislative output under a transition deadline must balance speed with institutional quality -- passing laws fast enough to meet the BOL mandate while building them well enough to survive judicial scrutiny and leadership turnover.

## The Executive-Legislative Forum as a Governance Innovation

The forum itself represents a structural innovation worth studying. By bringing together Parliament, Cabinet, governors, mayors, commission heads, and provincial board members in a single session with real-time Q&A via Slido, the BTA created a feedback mechanism that most Philippine regional governments lack. [^1][^2]

The morning session covered parliamentary reports and the executive agenda; the afternoon session became a rapid-fire policy exchange on topics ranging from budget constitutionality to barter trade to the education crisis. [^1][^2] Provincial representatives delivered formal impressions, surfacing issues -- conflicting agency data, customs jurisdiction gaps, infrastructure bottlenecks -- that would never reach the Chief Minister through normal bureaucratic channels. [^2][^4]

- **Fuel crisis response** -- an emergency task force was activated under OCM with short-term subsidies and a long-term study on direct fuel importation from Malaysia or Brunei [^1][^3]
- **Cotabato City budget shortfall** -- a PHP 1.6 billion NTA gap caused by a DBM error was escalated directly to the Chief Minister and MFBM Minister [^1][^2][^3]
- **National roads jurisdiction** -- the Chief Minister proposed using BARMM funds for national highways within the region, citing the Palawan precedent of advancing costs for DPWH reimbursement [^2]
- **Education crisis** -- BARMM provinces rank lowest in national reading performance; only three LGUs collect the Special Education Fund; Synergeia Foundation partnerships were championed but MBHTE remains resistant to NGO coordination [^2][^4]
- **Barter trade** -- operational guidelines adopted in September 2025 are now recognized by national Customs, but the counter-trade challenge remains: BARMM must produce goods to export, not just import [^2][^3]

The Chief Minister's closing directive to make this a regular quarterly forum with province-by-province reviews converts a one-time event into an institutional mechanism -- a recurring accountability cycle that connects legislation to implementation. [^2][^3]

**The takeaway**: Governance forums that combine formal reporting with real-time public Q&A create accountability loops that traditional bureaucratic channels cannot replicate.

## Intergovernmental Coordination: The Hardest Problem in Autonomy

The forum's most persistent theme was coordination failure between levels of government. The Lanao del Sur Provincial Development Officer reported conflicting data between agencies, duplicity of programs, overlapping functions, and waste of resources. She proposed creating a single unified program per province and a regular tactical platform for technical coordination. [^2][^4]

The Maguindanao province split illustrates the problem concretely: despite the division into Maguindanao del Norte and del Sur, commissioners in regional bodies still represent both provinces. The solution requires no legislation -- the Chief Minister has continuing power to restructure via executive order under the Administrative Code -- but the delay reveals how institutional inertia can outlast political decisions. [^1][^3]

The national roads problem adds another layer: DPWH retains jurisdiction over national highways within BARMM, but constituents blame the regional government for their poor condition. The BOL provides a mechanism -- BARMM can identify critical highways and submit proposals to DPWH -- but no systematic inventory or submission process exists yet. [^2][^3]

- **Vertical coordination gap** -- regional-to-LGU alignment on data, programs, and budget remains fragmented [^4]
- **Horizontal coordination gap** -- ministries miss interagency meetings; Cabinet Secretary Pendatun acknowledged that formal accountability mechanisms are needed [^2]
- **National-regional coordination gap** -- competences are legally defined but operationally unclear to both agencies and citizens [^2][^3]

**The takeaway**: Autonomous governance requires solving coordination problems at three levels simultaneously -- vertical (regional-LGU), horizontal (ministry-ministry), and diagonal (regional-national) -- and none can be solved without shared data infrastructure.

## Transition Safeguarding: Building for the Government After You

With the first regular BARMM elections expected in late 2026, the transition government faces a unique challenge: ensuring that the institutions, policies, and programs built over seven years of transition survive a change in leadership. Cabinet Secretary Pendatun confirmed that a final transition report -- now in its second draft -- is being prepared as required by the BOL and will be submitted to Parliament, Congress, and the Office of the President. [^2][^3]

> **Key insight**: The value of transition gains depends not on the outgoing government's achievements but on the incoming government's ability to continue them. [^2]

Deputy Speaker Jose I. Lorena addressed the Supreme Court petition questioning the constitutionality of the BARMM budget with a legal argument that budget discretion granted to a legislative committee cannot be substituted by judicial discretion without constituting overreach. [^2] His confidence notwithstanding, the mere existence of the petition underscores that the legal foundations of autonomous governance remain contested.

The transition challenge extends beyond documentation. The Bangsamoro Women's Commission presented three instruments -- a Joint Memorandum Circular localizing the Magna Carta of Women, the GAD Code, and an Executive Order on Women, Peace, and Security -- each designed to institutionalize gender-responsive governance as a permanent structural requirement, not a discretionary program that a new administration could deprioritize. [^2][^4]

**The takeaway**: Transition-proofing requires encoding governance gains into laws, codes, executive orders, and institutional structures that constrain future discretion -- making regression harder than continuity.
