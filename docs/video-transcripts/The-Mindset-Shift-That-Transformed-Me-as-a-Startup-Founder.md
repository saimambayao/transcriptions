**Three Pillars of Traditional Failure**

**02:53**
Now here’s why traditional approaches fail. 

**02:54**
Problem number one: people don’t know their real problems. When you ask directly, customers give you obvious surface-level issues they are consciously aware of, but breakthrough products solve problems customers didn’t even know they had. 

**03:07**
Problem number two: you bias their answers. The moment you mention a problem, you put a spotlight on it and exaggerate its importance in their mind. This is called the spotlight effect. 

**03:18**
Problem number three: you get opinions and not real customer behavior. Customers are terrible at predicting their future behavior. They tell you what they think they should want, but not what they actually do in the future.

**The "Study Behavior" Philosophy**

**03:31**
The Running Lean approach flips this completely. Instead of asking about problems, you study their current behavior and let the problems reveal themselves. 

**03:39**
Here's the exact 30-minute interview structure from Running Lean that cuts through customer lies and gets to real customer forces. **Phase 1: Welcome and Setup (2 Minutes)**

**04:00**
You start off with just two minutes for welcoming and setting up your interview. And here's what you say: 

"Thanks for your time. I'm building something for people just like you, and I want to understand how you currently handle X..." 

**04:12**
This is where you introduce the high-level job or bigger context that includes the space you're operating in. Think of the bigger context as a circle that includes both your solution as well as the other existing alternatives in your space that your customers are actually using.

**Phase 2: Anchoring in Recent Usage (5 Minutes)**

**04:28**
Once the stage is set, then you ask them to walk you through their last experience using their existing alternative of choice. Notice what I'm not asking. I'm not asking about problems, I'm not asking what they want. I'm asking them to walk me through something specific they actually did. 

**04:45**
And then you spend five minutes anchoring them in recent usage. And this step is absolutely critical. Don't let them speak hypothetically. Instead, ask them:

"Tell me about the last time you used [current solution]. What triggered that need?"

**05:02**
The key here is specificity. You want them thinking about Tuesday afternoon at 3:00 p.m. when they actually open the app, not some general impression of what they usually do.

**Phase 3: Exploring Triggers (5 Minutes)**

**05:13**
The next five minutes then explores triggers. You're digging deeper. 

1. What prompted you to look for a solution that day? 
2. Had anything changed? 
3. What was the situation you found yourself in? 

**05:24**
You're looking for the moment something shifted in their world that triggered the need for that job.

**Phase 4: Understanding the Selection Process (5 Minutes)**

**05:30**
The next five minutes are about understanding their selection process. 

1. Can you walk me through how you chose this? 
2. What else did you consider? 
3. Where did you hear about [their chosen solution]? 

**05:39**
These questions reveal their decision-making patterns or solution hiring criteria and what influences them.

**Phase 5: Unpacking Usage Patterns (10 Minutes)**

**05:46**
The longest section in the script is the next 10 minutes where you unpack specific usage scenarios. 

1. Walk me through how you got started? 
2. What did you do next? 
3. What happened after that? 

**05:58**
Notice that here too I am not asking at any point in time for problems. I am instead simply getting the story out, and every story that a customer has has drama. When I hear excitement or disappointment in their voice, I chase that thread further. 

**06:16**
The art here is to not expect a big problem banner in the interview, but rather to listen for small frustrations, pet peeves and workarounds. Things they've just accepted as the way it is. That's where you're going to find real interesting problems worth solving.

**Final Minutes: The Wrap-Up**

**06:31**
And the final minutes of the interview are for wrap-up. And this is also an important step. I end each interview with three critical things: the hook, the ask, and the referral. 

**06:42**
The hook is not a place to pitch or design your solution. There's no time for that. You need to first learn before you pitch and should defer talking about your solution to the next conversation. 

**06:53**
What I do instead in the hook is to summarize their story in 30 seconds, highlighting a few key problems that may be worth solving for them. If you can describe those problems better than they do, that's the best tease for your solution. 

**07:07**
I then ask for permission to follow up in a couple of weeks from now to show them something I'm working on that I think would address these problems. If you've identified a true problem worth solving, you should have no problem setting up this follow-up meeting. 

**07:20**
And the last thing I ask for is a referral to more people like them or if this is a multi-actor purchase, other influencers in the buying process that I should speak with.

### **Organized Notes: The 30-Minute Customer Discovery Method**

**Why Traditional Methods Fail [02:54]**
*   **Users don't know their real problems:** They focus on surface-level issues they are consciously aware of.
*   **Founder Bias (Spotlight Effect):** Mentioning a problem exaggerates its importance in the customer's mind.
*   **Opinions vs. Behavior:** Customers are poor predictors of their own future actions; they say what they think they *should* want.

**The "Study Behavior" Strategy [03:31]**
*   **Goal:** Shift from asking about problems to studying actual recent behavior.
*   **The "Bigger Context":** Understand the "circle" of the customer's world, including your solution and all current alternatives they use.

**The 30-Minute Interview Structure [03:39]**
1.  **Welcome & Setup (2 mins):** Introduce the "job" or space you are exploring.
2.  **Anchoring in Recent Usage (5 mins):** Ask them to walk through the *last* time they used a current alternative. Seek specificity (e.g., "Tuesday at 3 p.m.").
3.  **Exploring Triggers (5 mins):** Identify the exact moment something shifted to cause them to look for a solution.
4.  **Selection Process (5 mins):** Understand their hiring criteria and decision-making patterns.
5.  **Usage Patterns (10 mins):** Map the step-by-step usage story. Listen for "drama" (excitement or frustration) and specific workarounds.
6.  **Wrap-up (Final mins):** Use the "Hook" to summarize their problems better than they did. Set up a follow-up for a potential solution and ask for referrals.

**Key Implementation Phases [09:35]**
*   **Weeks 1-2:** Interview 10-15 actual users of existing alternatives.
*   **Weeks 3-4:** Perform pattern recognition and map "Customer Forces."
*   **Weeks 4-6:** Create a "**Mafia Offer**"—a solution that perfectly fits the discovered landscape of problems.

**Strategic Takeaway**
*   **Innovation isn't about the solution:** It's about discovering the problems customers didn't even know they had by analyzing their behavior timeline from trigger to outcome.
