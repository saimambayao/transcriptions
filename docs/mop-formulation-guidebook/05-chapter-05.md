# Chapter 5 — The PRLS MOP Model

This is the most important chapter in this guidebook. It presents the MOP format used by the **Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS)** of the Bangsamoro Parliament as the gold standard for procedural documentation. Study this chapter carefully. Use its templates. Adapt its structure for your agency.

The PRLS MOP succeeds because it is built on four principles: **output-based organization**, **four-column workflow tables**, **hierarchical approval flow**, and **standardized forms**. This chapter explains each principle in detail and provides a complete sample procedure you can use as a template.

## 5.1 Why the PRLS Format Is the Gold Standard

Most MOPs in government agencies are organized around organizational units: "Chapter III: Administrative Division," "Chapter IV: Finance Division." This approach has a fundamental weakness. It describes what each unit does, but it does not show how work flows across units to produce a specific output.

The PRLS format solves this problem. It organizes procedures around **outputs** -- the tangible products the agency delivers. Each procedure traces the complete journey of an output from the moment a request is received to the moment the finished product is delivered. Every person who touches the output appears in the workflow. Every handoff is documented. Every form is specified.

This output-based approach has five advantages:

1. **Traceability.** Every procedure traces back to a specific mandated function. You can prove that every step serves a purpose.
2. **Accountability.** Every step has a named responsible person. When something goes wrong, you know exactly where.
3. **Trainability.** New staff can read the workflow table and understand their role without extensive oral explanation.
4. **Measurability.** Because each step is explicit, you can measure processing time, identify bottlenecks, and set turnaround standards.
5. **Auditability.** Auditors can verify compliance step by step. The MOP serves as the audit criterion.

## 5.2 Output-Based Organization

### 5.2.1 The Four Major Output Categories

The PRLS model organizes all agency outputs into four major categories. Every MOA can adapt these categories to its own mandate:

| Category | Description | Examples |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Category A: Policy and Legislative Outputs** | Documents that establish, interpret, or analyze policy | Legislative analysis briefers, policy memoranda, position papers, committee reports |
| **Category B: Administrative and Management Outputs** | Documents that manage internal operations | Personnel actions, budget requests, procurement documents, office orders |
| **Category C: Research and Information Outputs** | Documents that compile, analyze, or present information | Research papers, data compilations, comparative analyses, statistical reports |
| **Category D: Correspondence and Communication Outputs** | Documents that communicate with internal or external parties | Official correspondence, transmittal memoranda, endorsement letters, reports to oversight bodies |

For a Ministry like MBHTE, the categories might be:

| Category | MBHTE Examples |
|----------|---------------|
| **A: Education Policy Outputs** | Curriculum guidelines, education standards, accreditation criteria |
| **B: Administrative Outputs** | Personnel actions, budget requests, procurement |
| **C: Research and Data Outputs** | Education statistics, school performance reports, needs assessments |
| **D: Service Delivery Outputs** | Scholarship certifications, school permits, equivalency credentials |

For a Commission like BWC (created by BAA 8), the categories might be:

| Category | BWC Examples |
|----------|-------------|
| **A: Policy Advisory Outputs** | Gender policy recommendations, GAD compliance reviews |
| **B: Administrative Outputs** | Personnel actions, budget requests, procurement |
| **C: Research and Monitoring Outputs** | Gender statistics, women's rights monitoring reports |
| **D: Advocacy and Program Outputs** | Training programs, awareness campaigns, assistance referrals |

### 5.2.2 Mapping Outputs to Mandates

Every output your agency produces should trace to a specific mandate. Build a mapping table:

| Output | Mandate Source | BAA/Legal Reference |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Legislative Analysis Briefer | Provide policy research and analysis to Parliament | BAA 13, Section [relevant section] |
| Gender Policy Review | Mainstream gender in all government programs | BAA 8, Section [relevant section] |
| Budget Preparation Report | Prepare and submit annual budget proposals | BAA 84, Section [relevant section] |

If an output cannot be traced to a mandate, question whether your agency should be producing it. If it can be traced, document the connection. This mapping feeds directly into the Mandate-to-Procedure Traceability Matrix described in Chapter 6.

## 5.3 The Four-Column Workflow Table

The heart of the PRLS MOP is the **four-column workflow table**. Every procedure is documented using this format. The table shows every step, every responsible person, every action, and every form used.

### 5.3.1 Column 1: Step Number

Simple sequential numbering: 1, 2, 3, etc. Each step represents one discrete action by one responsible person. When a different person takes over, that is a new step.

**Rules:**
- One action per step
- One responsible person per step
- Number steps consecutively without gaps
- Use sub-steps (1a, 1b) only when a single person performs multiple related actions within one handoff

### 5.3.2 Column 2: Responsible Person/Unit

State the **position title** of the person responsible for the step, not their name. MOPs survive personnel changes. Use the official title from the staffing pattern.

**Rules:**
- Use position titles, not names (e.g., "Director II," not "Dr. Ahmad")
- Include the division or unit in parentheses on first mention (e.g., "Staff Officer (Research Division)")
- Be specific. "Staff" is too vague. "Staff Officer II, Research Division" is correct.
- For steps that any staff member in a unit may perform, write "Designated Staff Officer, [Unit Name]"

### 5.3.3 Column 3: Action/Procedure

Describe the action in imperative mood. Start with a verb. Be specific enough that a new employee can follow the step without oral explanation.

**Rules:**
- Start with an action verb: "Receives," "Reviews," "Prepares," "Endorses," "Approves," "Transmits"
- Include specific details: form names, system names, routing destinations
- State the decision criteria when the step involves a judgment call
- Note what happens in alternative scenarios (e.g., "If incomplete, returns to the sender with a note specifying the missing requirements")

**Good example:** "Receives the incoming request. Logs it in the Document Tracking System with the date, sender, and subject. Attaches a Referral and Action Slip (Form 1). Forwards the request with the attached slip to the Director II within two (2) hours of receipt."

**Poor example:** "Processes the document." (Too vague. What exactly does "processes" mean?)

### 5.3.4 Column 4: Output/Form Used

State the document, form, or output produced at this step. If no new document is produced, state "N/A" or describe the status change (e.g., "Request logged in tracking system").

**Rules:**
- Reference forms by name and number: "Referral and Action Slip (Form 1)"
- Describe the output's status: "Draft briefer," "Reviewed briefer with annotations," "Approved briefer with Director's signature"
- Include the disposition of the document: where it goes next

## 5.4 Hierarchical Approval Flow

The PRLS model follows a six-stage approval flow. Every output moves through these stages, though simpler outputs may compress or skip stages. The flow ensures that work is received systematically, assigned to the right person, executed competently, reviewed for quality, approved by authority, and delivered to the client.

### 5.4.1 Reception Stage

**Purpose:** Receive incoming requests or tasks and log them for tracking.

**Typical responsible person:** Administrative Officer, Records Officer, or Designated Receiving Staff

**Standard actions:**
1. Receive the document or request
2. Check completeness of submitted documents
3. Log in the document tracking system (date, time, sender, subject, reference number)
4. Attach a Referral and Action Slip (Form 1)
5. Forward to the Director or Division Chief within a specified time (typically two hours)

**Key principle:** Nothing enters the workflow without being logged. Every document receives a tracking number. Every request is acknowledged.

> **Why this matters.** Without a reception stage, documents arrive at staff desks without tracking. They can be lost, delayed, or forgotten. The reception stage creates a paper trail from the first moment of contact.

### 5.4.2 Evaluation and Delegation Stage

**Purpose:** The director or division chief evaluates the request and assigns it to the appropriate staff member.

**Typical responsible person:** Director II, Division Chief, or Section Head

**Standard actions:**
1. Receive the request from the receiving staff
2. Review the Referral and Action Slip
3. Evaluate the nature, complexity, and urgency of the request
4. Assign the request to the appropriate staff officer or section
5. Write specific instructions on the Action Slip (e.g., "Prepare a legislative analysis briefer on this bill. Deadline: 5 working days.")
6. Return the request with the annotated Action Slip to the assigned staff through proper channels

**Key principle:** The supervisor does not just pass the document along. They add value by categorizing, prioritizing, and providing specific instructions. The Action Slip becomes a mini-terms-of-reference for the assignment.

### 5.4.3 Execution Stage

**Purpose:** The assigned staff member performs the substantive work: research, analysis, drafting, computation, or other task-specific actions.

**Typical responsible person:** Staff Officer, Analyst, Technical Staff

**Standard actions:**
1. Receive the assignment with the annotated Action Slip
2. Review the request and the Director's instructions
3. Perform the required work (research, analysis, drafting, computation)
4. Prepare the draft output following format standards
5. Conduct a self-review of the draft output
6. Submit the draft output to the immediate supervisor (Section Head or Chief of Division) with a transmittal note

**Key principle:** The staff officer works within the scope defined by the Director's instructions. If the scope needs to change (e.g., the research reveals that the request requires a different type of analysis), the staff officer consults the Director before proceeding.

### 5.4.4 Review Stage

**Purpose:** The supervisor reviews the draft output for accuracy, completeness, and quality before forwarding to the approving authority.

**Typical responsible person:** Chief of Division, Section Head, or Senior Staff

**Standard actions:**
1. Receive the draft output from the staff officer
2. Review for accuracy, completeness, and compliance with standards
3. Check for errors in facts, analysis, computation, or formatting
4. Annotate the draft with corrections or comments
5. If major revisions are needed, return to the staff officer with specific instructions
6. If minor revisions are needed, annotate and return for quick correction
7. If satisfactory, endorse to the Director for approval

**Key principle:** The review stage is a quality gate. Nothing reaches the approving authority without passing through at least one quality review. This protects both the staff officer (whose work is improved) and the Director (who receives polished outputs).

### 5.4.5 Approval Stage

**Purpose:** The approving authority reviews the endorsed output and provides final approval.

**Typical responsible person:** Director II, Executive Director, or Agency Head

**Standard actions:**
1. Receive the endorsed output from the reviewing officer
2. Review for policy alignment, strategic fit, and overall quality
3. If approved, sign the output and any accompanying transmittal documents
4. If revisions needed, return with specific instructions
5. Direct the mode and timing of delivery

**Key principle:** The approving authority focuses on substance and policy, not on formatting or computation. Those issues should have been caught at the Review Stage. If the approving authority is frequently sending outputs back for basic errors, the Review Stage needs strengthening.

### 5.4.6 Delivery Stage

**Purpose:** Deliver the approved output to the requesting party and close the tracking record.

**Typical responsible person:** Administrative Officer, Records Officer, or Designated Delivery Staff

**Standard actions:**
1. Receive the approved output from the Director
2. Prepare the Transmittal Memorandum (Form 2) if the output is being sent to an external party
3. Make copies for file retention
4. Deliver the output to the requesting party (in person, by official mail, or electronically)
5. Obtain acknowledgment of receipt (signature on receiving copy or electronic confirmation)
6. Update the Document Tracking System with the completion date and disposition
7. File the office copy with all attachments

**Key principle:** The delivery stage closes the loop. The tracking system shows the complete lifecycle: date received, date assigned, date completed, date delivered. This data feeds your performance metrics (Chapter 7).

## 5.5 Standardized Forms

The PRLS MOP uses four standard forms that every MOA should adapt and adopt.

### 5.5.1 Referral and Action Slips

The **Referral and Action Slip** is the routing control document. It is attached to every incoming request and accompanies the document throughout its lifecycle.

**Contents of the Referral and Action Slip:**

| Field | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Reference Number** | Unique tracking number (format: [Agency]-[Year]-[Sequential Number]) |
| **Date Received** | Date and time the document was received |
| **Received From** | Sender's name, position, and agency |
| **Subject** | Brief description of the request |
| **Referred To** | Name and position of the person to whom the request is assigned |
| **Action Required** | Specific instructions from the Director (e.g., "For research and analysis," "For comment and recommendation," "For appropriate action") |
| **Deadline** | Date by which the action must be completed |
| **Priority** | Urgent, Routine, or For Information |
| **Remarks** | Additional instructions or context |
| **Signature of Referring Officer** | Director or Division Chief's signature |
| **Date of Action Completion** | Filled in when the assigned person completes the task |
| **Action Taken** | Brief description of what was done |

### 5.5.2 Transmittal Memoranda

The **Transmittal Memorandum** accompanies every output delivered to an external party. It provides context, identifies the output, and creates a record of delivery.

**Standard format:**

```
TRANSMITTAL MEMORANDUM

TO:         [Recipient's Name, Position, Agency]
FROM:       [Sender's Name, Position, Agency]
DATE:       [Date]
SUBJECT:    [Brief Description of the Output Being Transmitted]

1. Transmitted herewith is [description of the output], prepared in
   response to [reference to the original request, including date and
   reference number].

2. [Brief summary of the output's key findings or content, if applicable.]

3. For your [information / review and comment / appropriate action].

[Signature]
[Name]
[Position]
```

### 5.5.3 Routing Slips

The **Routing Slip** tracks the document's movement within the office. Unlike the Referral and Action Slip (which assigns work), the Routing Slip records each handoff.

**Contents:**

| Column | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Date/Time** | When the document was received by this person |
| **Received By** | Name and position |
| **Action Taken** | What was done with the document |
| **Forwarded To** | Name and position of the next person |
| **Date/Time Forwarded** | When the document was sent to the next person |
| **Remarks** | Any notes |

### 5.5.4 Tracking Logs

The **Tracking Log** is the master register of all documents received and processed by the office. It provides a bird's-eye view of all active and completed transactions.

**Contents:**

| Column | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Reference Number** | Unique tracking number |
| **Date Received** | When the document arrived |
| **Source** | Who sent it |
| **Subject** | What it is about |
| **Assigned To** | Who is working on it |
| **Deadline** | When it is due |
| **Status** | Pending, In Progress, For Review, For Approval, Completed, Delivered |
| **Date Completed** | When the output was finished |
| **Date Delivered** | When it was sent to the requestor |
| **Remarks** | Notes on delays, returns, or special instructions |

## 5.6 Complete Sample Procedure: Producing a Legislative Analysis Briefer

This section presents a complete procedure in the PRLS format. Use it as a template for documenting procedures in your own MOP. This example shows the procedure for producing a Legislative Analysis Briefer in response to a bill referred to the agency for review.

**Output:** Legislative Analysis Briefer
**Category:** A (Policy and Legislative Outputs)
**Legal basis:** BAA 13 (mandate to provide policy research and analysis)
**Standard turnaround time:** 5 working days (routine), 3 working days (urgent)

### Procedure: Production of a Legislative Analysis Briefer

| Step | Responsible Person | Action/Procedure | Output/Form Used |
|------|-------------------|------------------|-----------------|
| **RECEPTION** | | | |
| 1 | Administrative Officer (Admin Unit) | Receives the referral letter or committee memorandum requesting analysis of a filed bill. Checks completeness: (a) referral letter, (b) copy of the bill, (c) committee resolution if applicable. Logs the request in the Document Tracking System with date, time, sender, subject, and reference number. Assigns a tracking reference number. | Document Tracking Log (Form 4) updated. Entry created. |
| 2 | Administrative Officer (Admin Unit) | Attaches a Referral and Action Slip (Form 1) to the request package. Marks the priority level (Urgent or Routine). Forwards the complete package to the Director II within two (2) hours of receipt. | Referral and Action Slip (Form 1) attached. Package forwarded. |
| **EVALUATION AND DELEGATION** | | | |
| 3 | Director II | Receives the request package from the Administrative Officer. Reviews the bill and referral letter. Evaluates the subject matter, complexity, and urgency. Identifies the appropriate Chief of Division based on subject matter expertise. Writes specific instructions on the Referral and Action Slip: type of analysis required, specific issues to address, deadline, and any special considerations. Signs the Referral and Action Slip. | Referral and Action Slip (Form 1) annotated with instructions and signed by Director II. |
| 4 | Director II | Forwards the annotated package to the designated Chief of Division. | Package forwarded to Chief of Division. |
| **DELEGATION (DIVISION LEVEL)** | | | |
| 5 | Chief of Division (assigned division) | Receives the package from the Director II. Reviews the Director's instructions on the Referral and Action Slip. Assigns the task to the appropriate Staff Officer based on subject matter expertise and current workload. Adds division-level instructions if needed. | Assignment communicated to Staff Officer. |
| **EXECUTION** | | | |
| 6 | Staff Officer (assigned) | Receives the assignment with the bill, referral letter, and annotated Referral and Action Slip. Reviews the bill and the Director's instructions. Identifies the research requirements. | Research plan noted. |
| 7 | Staff Officer (assigned) | Conducts research and analysis: (a) reviews the bill section by section, (b) identifies related laws, BAAs, and issuances, (c) checks compliance with BOL and BAA 13, (d) assesses policy implications, (e) identifies implementation requirements, (f) reviews relevant BDP 2023-2028 goals, (g) identifies stakeholder impact. Prepares the draft Legislative Analysis Briefer using the standard template (Form 5). | Draft Legislative Analysis Briefer prepared. |
| 8 | Staff Officer (assigned) | Conducts a self-review of the draft: checks factual accuracy, completeness, consistency, and formatting. Corrects any errors found. | Self-reviewed draft. |
| 9 | Staff Officer (assigned) | Submits the draft Legislative Analysis Briefer to the Chief of Division with a brief transmittal note summarizing key findings and any issues requiring guidance. | Draft briefer submitted with transmittal note. |
| **REVIEW** | | | |
| 10 | Chief of Division | Receives the draft briefer from the Staff Officer. Reviews for: (a) accuracy of legal citations, (b) completeness of analysis, (c) soundness of policy assessment, (d) compliance with format standards, (e) clarity of writing. Annotates the draft with corrections or comments. | Reviewed draft with annotations. |
| 11 | Chief of Division | If major revisions are needed: returns the draft to the Staff Officer with specific written instructions for revision. Staff Officer revises and resubmits (return to Step 8). If satisfactory or requiring only minor edits: makes minor corrections and prepares an endorsement note to the Director II. | Endorsed draft with endorsement note, or returned draft with revision instructions. |
| **APPROVAL** | | | |
| 12 | Director II | Receives the endorsed draft from the Chief of Division. Reviews for: (a) policy alignment, (b) strategic accuracy, (c) completeness of response to the original request, (d) overall quality. | Reviewed draft. |
| 13 | Director II | If revisions are needed: returns to the Chief of Division with instructions (return to Step 10). If approved: signs the Legislative Analysis Briefer. Directs the mode of transmittal (e.g., "Transmit to the Committee Chairperson via Transmittal Memorandum"). | Approved and signed Legislative Analysis Briefer. |
| **DELIVERY** | | | |
| 14 | Administrative Officer (Admin Unit) | Receives the approved briefer from the Director II. Prepares the Transmittal Memorandum (Form 2) addressed to the requesting party. Attaches the signed briefer. Makes three (3) copies: (a) original for the requesting party, (b) file copy, (c) copy for the assigned Staff Officer's reference. | Transmittal Memorandum (Form 2) prepared. Copies made. |
| 15 | Administrative Officer (Admin Unit) | Delivers the original briefer with the Transmittal Memorandum to the requesting party. Obtains acknowledgment of receipt (signature on receiving copy or electronic confirmation). Updates the Document Tracking System with the completion date, delivery date, and disposition. Files the office copy with all attachments (original request, Referral and Action Slip, draft with annotations, and receiving copy). | Document Tracking Log (Form 4) updated. File closed. |

## 5.7 Adapting the PRLS Model for Any MOA

The PRLS model was designed for a legislative research office, but its principles apply to any MOA. Here is how to adapt it:

**Step 1: Identify your outputs.** List every tangible product your agency delivers. Group them into four categories (adapt the category names to fit your mandate).

**Step 2: Map each output to a mandate.** Trace each output to a specific function defined in BAA 13 or your enabling BAA (see Chapter 6).

**Step 3: Document the workflow.** For each output, trace the complete lifecycle from reception to delivery. Identify every person who touches the output. Document every handoff.

**Step 4: Build the four-column table.** Use the format shown in Section 5.6. One step per row. One responsible person per step. Start with an action verb. Specify forms by name and number.

**Step 5: Follow the six-stage flow.** Structure every procedure around Reception, Evaluation/Delegation, Execution, Review, Approval, and Delivery. Simple procedures may compress stages (e.g., the Director both reviews and approves in a single step). Complex procedures may expand stages (e.g., multiple review levels before approval).

**Step 6: Create your forms.** Adapt the four standard forms (Referral and Action Slip, Transmittal Memorandum, Routing Slip, Tracking Log) to your agency's needs. Add agency-specific forms for specialized outputs.

**Step 7: Set turnaround standards.** For each output, define turnaround times for routine, urgent, and complex requests (see Chapter 7).

**Step 8: Test the procedure.** Before finalizing, walk through the procedure with the people who will execute it. Ask: "Can you follow these steps as written? Is anything missing? Is anything unnecessary?" Revise based on their feedback.

> **The test of a good MOP procedure.** A new employee, reading the workflow table for the first time, should be able to execute the procedure correctly without asking anyone for clarification. If they need to ask, the procedure is not detailed enough.
