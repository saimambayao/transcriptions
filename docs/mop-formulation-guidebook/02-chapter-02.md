# Chapter 2 — Planning and Preparation

A MOP built without planning is a MOP built twice. This chapter covers the three foundational steps before you write a single procedure: identifying your triggers, forming your team, and conducting a needs assessment.

## 2.1 Identifying the Need: Five Triggers for MOP Development

Five situations trigger the need for MOP development or revision. Recognizing your trigger helps you scope the effort correctly.

### 2.1.1 New Agency or Office Creation

When the BTA Parliament enacts a BAA that creates a new agency -- such as BAA 8 creating the Bangsamoro Women Commission or BAA 10 creating the Bangsamoro Youth Commission -- that agency starts with a mandate but no operational procedures. The enabling BAA defines what the agency must do. The MOP defines how.

**Action required.** Develop a complete MOP covering all nine standard chapters. Begin with the agency's mandate as defined in the enabling BAA, then map every mandated function to specific procedures.

### 2.1.2 Organizational Restructuring

When an existing MOA undergoes restructuring -- new divisions created, functions transferred, reporting lines changed -- existing MOPs become outdated. Procedures reference positions that no longer exist or divisions that have been merged.

**Action required.** Conduct a gap analysis comparing the current MOP against the new organizational structure. Revise affected chapters. A full rewrite is often more efficient than patching multiple chapters.

### 2.1.3 Major Policy or Legal Changes

When new BAAs, executive orders, or administrative directives change how your agency operates, your MOP must reflect those changes. BAA 84 (Budget System Act), for example, changed budget preparation procedures for every MOA. Any MOP written before BAA 84 needs a revised Financial Management chapter.

**Action required.** Identify all procedures affected by the new legislation. Draft revised procedures. Route through the amendment process described in Chapter 9.

### 2.1.4 Performance Gaps and Audit Findings

When COA audit reports, internal audits, or performance reviews reveal procedural weaknesses -- delays, errors, missing documentation, unclear accountability -- these findings point to MOP deficiencies.

**Action required.** Map each audit finding to a specific MOP procedure. Determine whether the procedure is missing, unclear, or not being followed. Revise accordingly.

### 2.1.5 Periodic Review Cycle

Every MOP should be reviewed at least once every three years, even without a triggering event. Operations evolve. Informal workarounds become standard practice. Forms change. Technology upgrades alter workflows. A periodic review catches these drifts before they become problems.

**Action required.** Schedule the review. Use the review process described in Chapter 9. Update as needed.

## 2.2 Forming the MOP Development Team

A MOP is not a solo project. Building a credible, implementable MOP requires diverse expertise. Assign six roles to your MOP Development Team. One person may fill multiple roles in smaller agencies, but every role must be covered.

### 2.2.1 Team Leader

The **Team Leader** manages the MOP development process from start to finish. This person sets the timeline, assigns tasks, resolves disagreements, and presents the final draft to the approving authority.

**Ideal profile.** A senior officer (Division Chief or equivalent) who understands the agency's operations across divisions. The Team Leader needs authority to convene meetings, request documents, and assign deadlines.

**Responsibilities:**
- Develop and manage the MOP work plan
- Coordinate all team activities
- Resolve conflicting inputs from SMEs
- Present progress reports to the agency head
- Submit the final draft for approval

### 2.2.2 Subject Matter Experts (SMEs)

**Subject Matter Experts** are the people who know how things actually work. They perform the procedures daily or supervise those who do. Every division or major functional area should have at least one SME on the team.

**Ideal profile.** Staff officers, section chiefs, or senior technical staff with three or more years of experience in their functional area.

**Responsibilities:**
- Describe current procedures accurately and completely
- Identify undocumented practices and informal workarounds
- Validate drafted procedures for accuracy
- Test procedures for completeness and feasibility

### 2.2.3 Quality Assurance Specialist

The **Quality Assurance (QA) Specialist** checks the MOP for internal consistency, completeness, and clarity. This person ensures that every procedure has a responsible person, every form is referenced correctly, and no steps are missing.

**Ideal profile.** An officer with attention to detail and experience in document review, internal audit, or quality management.

**Responsibilities:**
- Review all drafted procedures for completeness
- Check cross-references between chapters
- Verify that every referenced form exists in the Annexes
- Ensure consistent terminology throughout the MOP
- Conduct the final pre-approval quality review

### 2.2.4 Technical Writer

The **Technical Writer** transforms the SMEs' descriptions into clear, consistent, properly formatted procedures. This person enforces the format standards described in Chapter 4 and applies the PRLS MOP Model described in Chapter 5.

**Ideal profile.** An officer with strong writing skills and experience in technical documentation. Familiarity with the PRLS format is a significant advantage.

**Responsibilities:**
- Convert SME inputs into standardized procedure formats
- Apply consistent numbering, headings, and layout
- Build workflow tables in the four-column format
- Write introductory and transitional text for each chapter
- Ensure readability for the target audience

### 2.2.5 Legal Expert

The **Legal Expert** ensures that every procedure in the MOP is grounded in the agency's legal mandate. This person traces procedures to BAA 13, the enabling BAA, and other relevant legislation. They also flag procedures that may conflict with existing laws or regulations.

**Ideal profile.** A legal officer, attorney, or officer with legal training who is familiar with the Bangsamoro legal framework.

**Responsibilities:**
- Verify that all procedures trace to a legal mandate
- Build the mandate-to-procedure traceability matrix (Chapter 6)
- Flag potential legal conflicts
- Review the MOP for compliance with BAA 13, BAA 17, and other relevant BAAs
- Advise on regulatory and policy implications

### 2.2.6 Graphic Designer

The **Graphic Designer** creates the visual elements that make the MOP usable: organizational charts, workflow diagrams, decision trees, and page layouts.

**Ideal profile.** An officer or consultant with graphic design skills and experience producing government documents.

**Responsibilities:**
- Create organizational charts based on the agency's staffing pattern
- Design workflow diagrams for key procedures
- Produce decision trees for complex processes
- Apply consistent visual formatting throughout the document
- Prepare the final layout for printing and digital distribution

## 2.3 Conducting a Needs Assessment

Before gathering detailed information, conduct a structured needs assessment. This assessment defines the scope of your MOP and identifies the major gaps your MOP must address. Follow six steps.

### 2.3.1 Define the Scope

Determine what the MOP will cover. A Ministry-level MOP covers all divisions and functions. A Division MOP covers only that division's operations. An Office MOP covers a single office. See Chapter 10 for guidance on choosing the right scope.

Document your scope decision in writing. State clearly what is included and what is excluded. Share this with the agency head for confirmation before proceeding.

### 2.3.2 Identify Existing Documentation

Collect everything your agency already has: existing MOPs (even outdated ones), office orders, special orders, memoranda, standard operating procedures, process flowcharts, forms, templates, and checklists. Catalog each document with its date, author, and current status (active, superseded, or draft).

Many BARMM agencies have partial documentation scattered across divisions. A complete inventory prevents you from duplicating work that already exists.

### 2.3.3 Map Current Processes

Before you can document procedures, you need to understand what people actually do -- not what they are supposed to do, but what they do in practice. Map current processes through observation and interviews. Chapter 3 provides detailed methods.

At the needs assessment stage, focus on high-level process maps: major inputs, key steps, decision points, and final outputs. Save detailed step-by-step mapping for the information gathering phase.

### 2.3.4 Identify Gaps and Pain Points

Compare your existing documentation against your current processes. Look for five types of gaps:

1. **Missing procedures.** Functions your agency performs that have no documented procedure.
2. **Outdated procedures.** Written procedures that no longer match actual practice.
3. **Conflicting procedures.** Two or more documents that prescribe different steps for the same process.
4. **Incomplete procedures.** Written procedures that skip steps, omit forms, or fail to assign responsibility.
5. **Undocumented decisions.** Points in the workflow where staff make judgment calls without written criteria.

Document each gap. Rate its severity (critical, major, or minor). Prioritize critical and major gaps for immediate attention in the MOP.

### 2.3.5 Set Objectives and Success Criteria

Define what success looks like. Your MOP objectives should be specific and measurable. Examples:

- "Document all 15 service delivery procedures with complete workflow tables."
- "Reduce average processing time for permit applications by 20% within six months of MOP implementation."
- "Achieve 100% compliance with form usage within three months of MOP rollout."
- "Train all 45 staff members on the new MOP within 30 days of approval."

Write these objectives into your work plan. Use them to measure success during the monitoring phase (Chapter 9).

### 2.3.6 Develop the Work Plan

Build a detailed work plan with the following elements:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Activities** | Every major task from information gathering to final approval |
| **Responsible person** | The team member assigned to each activity |
| **Timeline** | Start and end dates for each activity |
| **Deliverables** | The specific output expected from each activity |
| **Dependencies** | Activities that must be completed before others can begin |
| **Resources** | Budget, equipment, software, and other resources needed |

A typical first-time MOP development effort for a Ministry takes 8-12 weeks. A Division MOP takes 4-8 weeks. An Office MOP takes 2-4 weeks. These timelines assume a dedicated team. If team members are working on MOP development alongside their regular duties, add 50% to the timeline.

## 2.4 Securing Management Buy-In

A MOP without management support is a MOP that collects dust. Before beginning the information gathering phase, present your work plan to the agency head. Cover three points:

**1. The problem.** Describe the specific operational issues your MOP will address. Use audit findings, complaint data, or processing delay statistics if available. Management responds to evidence, not abstractions.

**2. The solution.** Explain what the MOP will contain, how it will be developed, and how long it will take. Reference the PRLS MOP Model (Chapter 5) as the format standard. Show a sample workflow table if possible.

**3. The ask.** State what you need: time allocation for team members, access to documents and personnel, budget for printing and design, and a commitment to approve and implement the final product.

Document management's approval in a memorandum or office order. This document protects your team when you need to pull SMEs away from their regular duties for interviews and review sessions.

> **Practical tip.** Schedule a 30-minute briefing with the agency head. Bring a one-page summary of your work plan, a list of team members, and one example of an operational gap that the MOP will fix. Keep the briefing focused on results, not process.
