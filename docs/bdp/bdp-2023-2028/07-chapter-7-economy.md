# Chapter 7: Creating a Favorable Enabling Environment for an Inclusive, Resilient, and Robust Economy

Article V, Section 2 of the BOL states that the Bangsamoro government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines: agriculture, livestock, and food security; barter trade and countertrade; business name registration; cooperatives and social entrepreneurship; economic zones, industrial centers, and free ports; fishery, marine, and aquatic resources; Islamic banking and finance; labor, employment, and occupation; tourism development; and, trade and industry.

This chapter is one of the three chapters covering the economic development sector. This chapter covers only seven of the nine sub-sectors of the economic development sector, namely, agriculture, forestry, fisheries, and agrarian reform; trade and industry; tourism; ecozone development and freeport; investment; cooperatives and social enterprise; and labor and employment.

## Sectoral Assessment

### Sectoral Trends

**BARMM has huge potential for economic activities.** It is rich in natural resources that can be used to contribute to the growth of the national economy. The natural terrain of the BARMM is diverse, with lakes, rivers, marshes, mountain ranges, valleys, substantial stretches of fertile land, and maritime resources. However, this huge potential has not been optimized due to the violent conflict that fraught the region, weak institutional support, and the informality that defined the rules governing economic activities for decades.

### Land as an Economic Asset Has Not Been Optimized

A key economic asset that has been left idle and unproductive is land. BARMM has the largest land area in the country, accounting for 11.17 percent of the total land area, and hosts 10 of the 128 Key Biodiversity Areas (KBAs) in the country. About 628,401 hectares are classified as alienable and disposable while 665,151 hectares are classified as forest land. In addition, a total of 340,486 hectares are covered under the Comprehensive Agrarian Reform Program (CARP). These values are the highest in the country. It carries enormous potential as a source of income, food, employment, investment, and housing. But due to the absence of a comprehensive and inclusive land governance framework and the complexity that comes in the plurality of systems (legal, traditional, and informal systems), the potential of land as an economic asset has not been realized. Worse, it has been a significant cause of conflict, death, and displacement of civilians.

In an effort to address land conflict in the region, ministries who are directly and indirectly dealing with lands and its beneficiaries are now converging among themselves to strengthen collaboration with an end to improve delivery of public services while continuing the effort to provide free legal services, especially to agrarian reform beneficiaries and concerned landowners.

Maguindanao has the lion's share of the regional alienable and disposable (A&D) lands at 49 percent, followed by Lanao del Sur at 21 percent. In terms of forest land, Lanao del Sur registered the highest share at 38 percent, followed by Maguindanao at 30 percent. In terms of coverage under the CARP, Lanao del Sur registered the highest share at 48 percent followed by Maguindanao at 28 percent inclusive of Cotabato City and the SGA.

**Agriculture and fishery are BARMM's main sources of food, employment, and income.** It used to be the largest contributor to the region's overall economy 21 years ago, accounting for more than half of the region's economy. With the growth of the services sector, it is now the second-highest contributor to the region's economy, accounting for 36.41 percent in 2021. This structural transformation, however, does not mean that the sector is weakening or becoming less relevant. In fact, it is one of the biggest contributors that caused the region to be resilient at the time of the pandemic, registering the lowest decline among the regions in the Philippines in 2020.

### Seaweeds as BARMM's Top Commodity

BARMM is the country's largest seaweed producer, accounting for 48.42 percent of the country's seaweed production. Tawi-Tawi is the main source of seaweed production in BARMM, registering more than 90 percent of regional production. The municipalities in Sitangkai and Sibutu islands are the largest seaweed culture areas in Tawi-Tawi province. The comparative advantage and market potential for carrageenan as an export product of seaweeds remains high in the region.

To sustain the seaweed production, BARMM must invest in the post-harvest processing plant and modernized facilities/equipment or the overall value-chain development of the seaweeds industry, establish municipal-wide seaweed nursery, and strengthen seaweed research and development in order to have full control from production to marketing.

### High-Value Crops on the Rise in BARMM

Valued by small-scale farmers for its tolerance and growth, cassava is the second top commodity of BARMM based on percent share, accounting for 41.7 percent of the country's total cassava production. As a complement or alternative to rice and corn as a staple food, cassava was ranked significantly high in terms of volume of production and area harvested. Its yield in 2020 was close to the national average. Lanao del Sur was the largest contributor to regional cassava production in 2020, accounting for 47 percent.

BARMM is the country's second-largest producer of mangosteen, accounting for 22.4 percent of the country's production. It is the country's third largest producer of rubber, coffee, lanzones, and corn, contributing 18.4 percent, 17.4 percent, 15.3 percent, and 14 percent, respectively. In addition, the region is the second-largest producer of durian and the 5th largest producer of abaca.

### Bangsamoro Strategic Agricultural and Fisheries Development Areas (BSAFDA)

The region has 113 municipalities, 52 of these are coastal municipalities and 45 are inland municipalities. The second largest lake in the country is found in Lanao del Sur with an approximate area of 34,700 hectares. Lake Lanao is the deepest in the country and is considered one of the major tropical lakes in Southeast Asia. It is the source of hydroelectric power for the whole island of Mindanao aside from providing irrigation water and various fish products. Other lakes like Dapao in Lanao del Sur, Buluan and Balut in Maguindanao, Seit and Panamao in Sulu, Danao and Singuang in Mapun, Tawi-Tawi, and similar bodies of water are also potential resources of commercial scale fish products. The region has the largest share of swamp area with around 105,000 hectares and is being programmed for extensive agricultural and fishery development.

The region has the highest registered fisherfolk population in the country. As of December 2018, a total of 252,758 fisherfolks were registered in the Fisherfolks Registration (FishR) system. Of these, 162,167 are male and 90,951 are female; 102,996 are engaged in capture fishing; 45,275 are in aquaculture; 17,168 in fish vending; 23,251 in gleaning; 9,363 in fish processing; and 16,765 are engaged in other types of fishing activities.

For the period of 2014 up to 2018, fisheries production increased every year except in 2016 and 2017 due to typhoons and dry spells that affected the production of commercial and municipal fish products. Aquaculture has, however, continuously increased.

### Food Security Remains to be a Challenge in BARMM

This relative advantage in agriculture, forestry, and fishery did not translate to sustained food security. BARMM is a significant contributor to rice in Mindanao. The region contributes 16.5 percent to Mindanao palay production. Heavy investments in rice and corn yielded growth in production, but productivity remains low. The yield per hectare in 2020 was only 3.11 mt. compared to the country's average of 4.09 mt. (PSA 2022). In fact, this gap widened considering the average yield in the past years from 2011 to 2020. BARMM's productivity during this period was only 2.77 tons per hectare, significantly lower compared to the national average yield of 3.93 tons per hectare in the same period. As compared with other Mindanao regions, BARMM registered the lowest yield except for 2019.

### Barter Trade as an Income Support Mechanism

Barter trading has been the dominant type of trading in the BARMM for years. In the Sulu archipelago, barter is characterized by inter-familial transactions between Sama Badjaos from Tawi-Tawi and Sabah, Malaysia where consensual arrangements are bestowed tolerance by local authorities in Sabah. In Tawi-Tawi, the resources for each trading run of a wooden-hull vessel pool the resources of the vessel owner and his crew and financial capital from teachers, entrepreneurs, policemen and other individuals (Quitoriano, 2022). In Panglima Sugala (Tawi-Tawi), market days are characterized by food exchanges between farmers and fisherfolks based on relationship and social trust, not price mechanisms. When the value of a farmer's product is lower than the fisherfolks, the latter is given the privilege to access the farm to compensate for the gap in value. So, while barter trade is essential in the informal economy, it served as a potent safety net and social protection facility that are absent in the formal economy, particularly in times of crisis or periods of scarcity. One example was the continuing trade between the Sulu Archipelago and Sabah despite the restrictions caused by the COVID-19 pandemic (Franco, 2021).

### Most MSMEs in BARMM are in the Informal Sector

Due to the fragility of the region, a large part of the business sector, particularly the MSMEs, are in the informal sector. This implies that many operate beyond the rules, regulations, and support of the state, leading to low quality of products and services, low productivity, and limited legal and social protections for workers. More critically, the MSMEs' potential to contribute to broad-based growth and state building has not been optimized.

A starting point in formalizing the MSMEs is business name registration (BNR) which is a frontline service of the MTIT. The Philippines' BNR system is the portal for the registration of MSMEs under single-proprietorship ownership. This registration will provide legal identity and will gain the rights to use the chosen business name. Once registered, MSMEs engaged in trading, retailing, manufacturing, and services can receive various capacity development support such as enterprise skills training, digital literacy, product pricing and packaging.

### Untapped Tourism Potential

### Policy Supports Urgently Needed for Ecozone and Freeport Development

BARMM is encouraged to promote and accelerate sound and balanced industrial, economic, and social development through the establishment of special economic zones and free ports in suitable and strategic locations in the Bangsamoro area. However, the identified potential economic zone areas are untapped because there is inadequate community infrastructure that will support economic gains and accessibility to resources and a limited supply of water and electricity for use of the ecozone.

## Development Opportunities

**BARMM's rich natural resources.** BARMM is a unique region, having rich natural and cultural resources, natural tourist spots, and diverse wildlife that need to be explored and developed. It has vast tracks of idle lands which could be utilized for agriculture, particularly food security, agro-forestry, manufacturing, and services-related investments such as tourism, retail trade, housing, and among others.

The region is dubbed as the "unpolished gem" of Mindanao with its natural wealth, human resource, and presence of ethnocultural beliefs and traditions. The concept of diversification and value-adding is the key schemes to unlock these potentials.

The region also has a dynamic and relatively young population, with 42 percent belonging to the 15-39-year-old age bracket. Hence, the region has a large pool of highly trainable workforce.

BARMM has the unique opportunity to be a place for nature-based and cultural tourism. Nature-oriented tourism is one of the fastest-growing areas in the tourism industry. The region is a favored nature-based tourism destination as long as relative peace and security are sustained. Given its various natural assets, the region can provide several different nature-based tourism experiences including hiking and trekking/bushwalking, wildlife viewing/birdwatching, swimming and beach experiences, fishing and sailing, scuba diving, camping, biking, water rafting, kayaking, river and inter-island canoeing, and a host of other outdoor activities.

Nature-based tourism activities are wide-ranging and can cater to all agencies. It can range from passive activities such as sightseeing, scenic driving, relaxing by the beach, and dining among the mangroves to hard and soft adventure activities. The key is to provide nature-based tourism activities and destinations that enhance the broader tourism experiences of the visitors. The overall visitor experience in nature-based tourism is inextricably linked with all other aspects of the visitor's total experience - such as transport convenience, food, culture, accommodation, sanitation, and the perceptual quality and feel of the general environment. All of these elements complement each other and together compose an individual's tourism experience. Another unique selling proposition that Bangsamoro could tap into is its Islamic character, through the promotion of Halal tourism and Islamic culture.

**Strategic air and seaports with proximity to the Brunei Darussalam-Indonesia-Malaysia-Philippines East ASEAN Growth Area (BIMP-EAGA).** The region also has strategic air and seaports with proximity to the BIMP-EAGA, a window and access for more investment opportunities.

The Greater Sulu-Sulawesi Corridor is a maritime corridor that covers North Sulawesi in Indonesia, Sabah in Malaysia, and Mindanao and Palawan in the Philippines. The corridor is mainly determined by the geography of the Sulu-Sulawesi Seas, a highly biodiverse, globally significant biogeographic unit in the heart of the Coral Triangleâ€”the center of the world's highest concentration of marine biodiversity.

Moreover, there are strong historical trade links in the corridor, with trade concentrated between North Sulawesi and Mindanao, and between Sabah and Mindanao. The corridor is also the nerve center of barter trade in BIMP-EAGA, particularly in agriculture and aquaculture products. Transport connectivity consists of port-to-port trade flows and shipping services within the Sulu-Sulawesi seas.

Also, there was the enactment of R.A. 7718, or the Build-Operate and Transfer (BOT) law providing more focus on the Public-Private-Partnership (PPP) which gives more opportunity to both local and foreign investments in the country.

**Global and regional opportunities for tourism, halal, and Islamic finance.** In terms of competitiveness and sustainability, the implementation must include the complete package of assistance - MSME Development Cycle. These include most importantly the provision of high-quality, sophisticated, and standard-compliant production and processing facilities. These interventions are areas of opportunity to partner, converge, and collaborate with partner stakeholders and enablers. There are existing business models and technology that can be adopted for the benefit of MSMEs.

The Strategic Investment Priorities Plan recently approved by the national government identified three registrable investment activities that are unique to BARMM. These are the halal industry, Islamic banking, and BIMP-EAGA-related investment enterprises.

Further, it is privileged to be the only region in the country with its own Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI) that can dispense fiscal and non-fiscal incentives to investors, provided that the amount of investment is below one billion pesos.

There are existing systems on halal, Islamic financing, and e-commerce that the BARMM can explore.

The Bangsamoro Halal Board (BHB), is the policy-making body of the Bangsamoro Government on Halal Industry development and set the overall direction in formulating, advocating, coordinating, overseeing and assessing the implementation of the Bangsamoro Halal Industry Development and Promotion Program such as the following: 1) Adoption of the M.C. 1 series of 2021 otherwise known as the Halal Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR); 2) Operationalization of the Bangsamoro Standards and Halal Testing Laboratory (BHSTL) of the MOST that provides quality, reliable, and impartial analytical testing and calibration services that meet the needs of the customers and the requirements of ISO/IEC 17025:2017; and, 3) Implementation of various Halal PPAs of the MAFAR, MTIT, and MOST i.e. Halal production and establishment of slaughter houses, assistance to MSMEs on the conduct of training/orientation on Halal awareness, food safety, product packaging and labelling, promotion on the Halal Poultry and provision of subsidy for Halal certification and License-To-Operate (LTO), among others.

**Relative peace and stability in formerly conflict-affected communities.** Camps and other communities which suffered from a long history of violent conflict are now accessible.

**Fresh infrastructure investments.** The region has the presence of basic utilities and infrastructure support such as water, telecommunications, power, seaports, airports, roads, and bridges connecting people to markets, safeguards smooth transport of goods and services to ensure everyone including existing and potential investors have a comfortable working and livable place.

**Strong partnership between national government, private sector, and development partners.** There is a solid partnership with the ministries and development partners. The BPDA and MAFAR in collaboration with the World Food Programme (WFP) initiated the creation of the Bangsamoro Food Sufficiency Task Force (BFSTF) to ensure that policies, programs, projects, and activities on achieving food security shall be coordinated, responsive, and effective.

The presence of international development funding institutions as partners towards the economic agenda of the BARMM is just one of the areas of access to finance or financing. Again, appropriate proposed programs will lead to more economic activities' implementation.

Convergence programming is one strategy to explore to maximize the resources of other concerned and partner BARMM MOAs and enable the rights of the small enterprises and local industries equal access to livelihood opportunities.

Partnering with private or business sectors, as major industry players and actors, is an open area of opportunity to lobby policy measures in the creation of a business-friendly enabling environment.

**Public-Private Partnership (PPP).** LGUs are corporate entities. They are by law encouraged and have the authority to enter into PPP for the implementation of revenue-generating projects. The LGUs can leverage their assets such as land, funds from the national government, or own-sourced funds against the investments of the private sector who has the competency to design, construct and develop, operate, and ensure the financial viability/profitability of a tourism site. There are several PPP modalities that LGUs can use, depending on the nature of the project to be implemented. PPPs provide LGUs the opportunity to also earn revenues based on their asset input to the partnership enterprise.

**Inter-LGU Partnership.** If funds are insufficient, LGUs can partner and contribute their respective assets and funds in order to develop and operate a financially viable tourism site. LGUs, together, can also enter into a PPP. Under an inter-LGU partnership, the contribution made to the partnership by each LGU can be valued as the basis for the value of its capital and therefore share in the revenues. A separate management entity can be organized, or this can be outsourced to a contracted private sector.

**National-Local Partnership.** The province and/or municipalities can partner with national government agencies in order to develop and operate a tourism site. For example, a partnership with the DENR for the grant of usufruct rights or special purpose use of protected areas for tourism activities can be made wherein both parties generate revenues out of the proceeds.

**Grant Funds.** There are national government agencies, such as the Tourism Infrastructure and Enterprise Zone Authority (TIEZA) that provides financing for the preparation of master plans and feasibility studies for the development of tourism areas, as well as funds for the construction of facilities. The provincial government can avail of these grant funds to finance the detailed design planning and development of the tourism clusters proposed in the tourism development plan. Some development partners also provide technical assistance grants to conduct studies and prepare plans for certain tourism-related activities such as heritage conservation. This is particularly useful in the detailed planning and preparation of urban design guidelines for the cultural heritage districts of Bangsamoro.

**Expanding Governance to Include Enterprise Development.** The Bangsamoro provincial and municipal governments will benefit from a well-organized, competent enterprise development department that will initially concentrate on the creation of tourism-related public enterprises, the development of PPP and intergovernmental partnership tourism projects, and the mobilization/generation of resources for investments in tourism ventures that bring in revenue.

**Presence of a wide range of business opportunities.** Also, there was the enactment of RA 7718, or the Build-Operate and Transfer (BOT) law that provides more focus on the PPP which gives more opportunity to both local and foreign investments. In addition, the BARMM, being an autonomous government, could also create bills relevant to the boosting and surge of investments in the region.

## Priority Challenges to be Addressed in 2023-2028

### Agriculture, Fisheries, Agrarian Reform

One of the key challenges to be addressed in 2023-2028 is **low agriculture and fisheries productivity**. This is associated with:

**High production cost.** High production cost is due to several factors. These include the high cost of farm inputs (fertilizers, seeds, and labor); limited access to credit; and limited mechanization, among others. Other critically important factors include the limited technical and managerial capacity of farmers; inadequate agriculture infrastructure support, particularly irrigation facilities for rice; soil fertility and water management; and inadequately funded extension services and programs of government.

**High cost of post-production and marketing and high retail price.** High transportation and high fuel costs are among the biggest post-production costs. However, it is not only fuel cost that contributes to high transportation cost; the poor infrastructure facilities that include road network from FMRs; lack of consolidation and processing centers leading to high consolidation and logistics costs; and lack of milling facilities and power interruption leading to high milling cost.

High retail price is a direct threat to food security because while farmers retain a portion of their harvest for their own consumption, many of the poor will have to buy rice at high retail price. Though BARMM is considered self-sufficient in terms of rice production, some of the rice produced in the region are not consumed in the region.

- **Weak extension services.** This challenge is brought on by the inadequate manufacturing/reproduction of appropriate IEC materials for distribution and the sparse deployment of Agricultural Extension Workers (AEW) at the provincial/municipal level.

- **Low technology generated for commercialization.** Insufficient capacity building for R&D workers and a dearth of approved plantilla positions for research results in a lack of specialization and lack of a research strategy, both of which will weaken collaborations with academic institutions. This problem is also accounted for by a lack of research-related facilities and insufficient funding for R&D.

- **Weak agricultural regulation.** This is related to weak implementation of regulatory issuances, lack of pest and disease diagnostic, forecasting, and early warning, weak strategy for revenue generation, and limited monitoring and support to agricultural products.

- **Lack of highly reliable, prompt, and accurate agri-fishery laboratory test results, confirmatory diagnosis, and production and animal health recommendations.** This is related to the lack of logistics for laboratory activities and extension services, weak linkage with other regional and national laboratories, insufficient conduct of laboratory analyses, lack of laboratory facilities, machines, and equipment, and lack of agri-fishery laboratory database.

- **Coastal ecosystem degradation.** The coastal ecosystem is crucial for fisheries. This includes coral reefs, beds of submerged aquatic vegetation, and mangrove forests. Factors influencing its degradation are inappropriate utilization of inorganic fertilizer in seaweed farming, lack of a mechanism to sustain marine protected areas, weak participation of communities in coastal resource management, illegal cuttings, and conversion of mangrove areas into the fishpond, poor information dissemination, weak regulatory data and system support, and the lack of water delineation law.

- **Limited aquaculture and capture fisheries development intervention.** Currently, there is no established Mariculture Park that supports services for processing, transport, and marketing and an anchoring system for floating fish cages, fish landings, cold storage, etc. There is also a lack of supply of hatchery-bred fingerlings and formulated feed. Capacity building for fisherfolks is limited, and infrastructures, hatcheries and breeding stations are insufficient.

- **Illegal, unreported, and unregulated fishing.** This is attributed to weak enforcement of fishery laws, weak coordination with LGUs and law enforcement agencies, limited staff capacity building, insufficient Monitoring Control and Surveillance Floating Assets, and lack of an Integrated Marine Environment Monitoring System (IMEMS).

- **Low competitiveness in farming and fishing.** "The average age of the Filipino farmer is 57. Assuming an average life span of 70, we might reach a critical [shortage] of farmers in just 15 years." Due to a lack of access to land and funding as well as the fact that farming and fishing require a lot of labor and pay little, the younger generation lacks enthusiasm in engaging in these activities. Young farmers and fishermen have little access to support, opportunities, and creative programs, and there is a lack of information on the advantages of farming and fishing. The insufficient absorption capacity of farmers and fishermen, as well as limited access to loans and agri-fishery insurance, are further contributing elements to this dilemma.

- **Weak monitoring & evaluation.** This is due to the lack of a database system for a list of beneficiaries, a lack of modern technology in reporting and M&E, a shortage of technical staff with the necessary skills, and a lack of M&E tools.

The second key challenge to be addressed within 2023-2028 is the **intergenerational tenancy among farmers and inequitable land distribution**. This is associated with:

- **Stringent and lengthy procedures of land ownership.** Sensitivity of the beneficiary identification process, strict requirements for processing and acquiring lands, time-consuming technical procedures for cancellation and transfer actions, loss of control over the awarded lands due to leases and sales to financial actors, intermediaries, and landlords, the presence of inaccurate information and inadequate cooperation from concerned landowners, and poor implementation of a properly documented tenancy agreement/leasehold program are the factors that contribute to this issue.

- **Conflicts over land tenure and ownership**, which are common in BARMM, are another important element and the primary reason that rido or clan feuds break out.

The third core problem to be addressed under this sub-sector is the **High food insecurity of households at 44.5 percent in 2019**. This is caused by:

- **High post-harvest losses.** Poor FMR identification and implementation, insufficient financial support for post- and pre-harvest infrastructure, and a lack of information or an inventory of pre- and post-harvest facilities are some of the factors contributing to this problem. Due to this, farmers and organizations perform poorly, and old-fashioned post-harvest methods are still employed. Convergence among agencies is needed to have a responsive program.

- **High vulnerability to disaster.** This includes damages due to emerging pests and diseases which results in significant output losses.

- **Weak partnerships with private sectors and industry players.** Value chain analysis is crucial for identifying elements that either increase or decrease a market's efficiency. Lack of a food security council or outdated established councils as a result of the merger of agencies into one ministry, such as the Agrarian Reform Council and Agri fishery Council, the existence of a weak or limited aggregation system for smallholders, an outdated industrial plan, a lack of legislation governing the establishment of government-owned and controlled corporations (GOCCs) in BARMM and the absence of any GOCC, as well as the lack of an entrepreneurial mindset and functional ability among farmers and fishers contributes to this challenge.

- **Limited opportunity for market linkages activities.** This includes unorganized value chain linkages, insufficient funding for food product development and processing, and inadequate marketing expertise among farmers and fishermen. Limited support for Halal and organic production program, lack of transportation tools, a lack of mechanisms for inclusive funding for farmers and fishermen, and restricted access to domestic and international markets are all included.

### Trade and Industry

A core problem that needs to be addressed in 2023-2028 under trade and industry is the **low competitiveness and sustainability of MSMEs and industries**. This is mainly caused by the following:

- **Mismatch in skills and available resources.** The mismatch between the skill sets that the region's sectors require, and the skill training offered to the potential pool of workers represents a broader gap in vocational skill training. The majority of these resources are therefore not being fully utilized.

- **Technical competency of small entrepreneurs and industry players to maximize their resources is outdated to a no-zero-competency level.** The factors contributing to this challenge are the literacy and awareness of the community in the existing and available government programs. To cite an example, the information on the zero-waste management program in the coconut industry is not completely cascaded to the farmers to enable them to enjoy the maximum productivity potentials of the by-products of coconut. Further, certifications, licenses, registrations, permits, and other business operation processes are costly.

- **Limited access to production and processing facilities.** Compliance with product standards requires a large amount of capitalization more so that it needs facilities and equipment for the production and processing of value-added commodities. Hence, the absence of these facilities resulted in a limited number of competitive and standard-compliant products and services. Taking into account the economic status of the regions' MSMEs, these necessities remain a challenge.

- **Limited access to credit facilities by the MSMEs, farmers, and fisherfolks.** Only highly capitalized organizations and firms with long credit histories have access to formal financing institutions. Most MSMEs, farmers, and fisherfolks resort to an informal credit system that bears the high cost of borrowing.

- **Limited connectivity.** Connectivity in terms of mobile networks and telecommunication is a major challenge in Bangsamoro. Perhaps, the same could be said in other regions of the country; however, it is much more apparent in peripheral areas of Bangsamoro. In urban centers such as Cotabato City and other communities in the provinces, internet connectivity is heavily dependent on the signal of mobile networks. In many instances, particularly in BaSulTa areas, mobile signal is not even felt. This poor connectivity tremendously impacts the development of the local economy, thus, impeding the implementation of digital marketing and cashless transactions throughout the region.

### Investments

A core problem that needs to be addressed under this sub-sector within 2023-2028 is the **limited private investments in BARMM**. This is mainly due to the following:

- **Low business confidence.** There is a low or negative impression among investors in relation to BARMM as an investment destination. This is mainly brought by peace and order issues, limited infrastructure, poor connectivity, as well as inadequate logistic facilities, and an unstable political and policy landscape.

- **Underdeveloped Halal products and services.** The government has been pushing time and again for the promotion of halal products and services. However, the government failed to focus on the development and sustenance of the production of halal products for domestic and export markets. Halal industry development must be one of the identified priority areas under economic development.

- **High cost of utilities.** The high cost of utilities and other logistics needed for the operation of the business contributes to an "unfriendly" business environment, thus, a major challenge to start-up enterprises.

- **Weak linkages of the BARMM with the BIMP-EAGA economic integration (e.g., Halal industry and revival of barter trading).** The issue of weak linkages of the region with the BIMP EAGA market can be attributed to Limited number of infra-connectivity projects. Connecting the region to the international market through the expansion of roads and communication infrastructures will address the gap on connectivity; Limited number of standard-compliant production and processing facilities, Limited trading engagement on the international and inter-regional markets, Limited number of Halal-certified products and services; and Lack of Techno-Business Incubator.

- **Lack of industry roadmap.** The BARMM has yet to formulate its investment roadmap. On the part of the MTIT, the BOI will lead in the formulation of an investment roadmap and framework that will detail the current investment situation and outline the various interventions needed to promote the region as an investment destination of choice by investors.

  The Bangsamoro Investment Roadmap Framework will serve as a structured management tool for planning, forecasting, and promotion of investments and as a basis for effecting policies and implementing programs that will support the development and growth of investments including funding for strategic promotion activities, industry development, policy research, value proposition, and facilitation services.

- **Poor implementation of the ease of doing a business act.** Another reason for the low level of investments in the region is the poor implementation of the Ease of Doing Business and Effective Government Service Delivery Act of 2018 or RA 11032 in the BARMM, particularly in digitalizing government transactions.

- **Inadequate data/information on investment opportunities.** The inadequacy of data/information complementing the investment opportunities in the region such as Investment Development Plans, CLUP, LGU development plan, the potential areas for investment (land area), ease of doing business, clean land title, areas abundant of raw materials and the like contributed to the lack of information of investors on what the BARMM can offer. Investors just became satisfied with their establishment in other places because there is no sufficient data/information showcasing the region as an investment-friendly destination.

### Tourism

The fourth core problem under this sub-sector is the **underdeveloped tourism circuits in the region**.

- **Underdeveloped tourism circuits.** There are no established tourism circuits in the region. Tourism products in Bangsamoro are limited to nature tourism and beaches. While there exist many tourist destinations, a vast majority of them are still potential sites, with no existing support amenities, facilities, and tourism circuits to provide a complete tourist experience.

  Existing tourism assets are also generally underdeveloped or developing. For those already open for tourists, many are accessible only to local visitors coming from within Bangsamoro and neighboring regions.

  There are few tourism-related enterprises operating in the region. The majority of these are concentrated in urban centers and identified growth areas. In the mainland, hotels and accommodation facilities, as well as transport services are concentrated in Cotabato City.

  In Marawi City, a limited number of accommodations are observed in Marawi Resort, an academe-operated facility being the prime lodging option. In BaSulTa, there are no hotels, but a number of inns and resorts are available for accommodations; beach resorts however are very common.

### Ecozone Development and Freeport

A key problem to be addressed within 2023-2028 under this sub-sector is the **limited expansion of economic opportunities in industry and services**.

- **Low number of ecozone enterprises/locators registered.** The number of ecozone enterprises/locators registered has gone low due to a lot of factors. Among these are the limited number of market promotions and linkages, unimplemented program for market promotion and linkages, limited freeport and ecozone public and private engagements for investment promotion, and absence of policy that will institutionalize the operations of the economic zone. Moreover, there was no inclusion of the Investment Promotion Agency (IPA) that took place. There was also a poor coordination mechanism between the national and local government, limited data on inbound and outbound cargoes for local and international vessels, a lack of financial data of port users, no digitized revenue collections and operations systems, and a lack of skilled personnel to manage the digitized system.

- **Underdeveloped freeports and ecozones.** There were limited studies to support freeport and ecozone development. There's also a lack of Ecozone Roadmap. Moreover, there was a limited number of skilled personnel in the following: port management, maritime security, port health and safety, ecozone technical planning, tax incentives under the Corporate Recovery and Tax Incentives for Enterprises (CREATE) Act, and port environment compliance. There were also unfilled permanent positions at BEZA and Polloc Freeport and Economic Zone-BARMM (PFEZ-BARMM), and most of the current employees were employed through a contract of service.

  Lamitan City offers tremendous potential for barter trading. Hence, it is imperative that a seaport be expanded in order to accommodate large sea vessels for the export of regional goods.

- **Inadequate infrastructures to support improvements required in ecozone and freeport standards.** Currently, the regional ecozone and freeport don't have modernized equipment for operations. There are unfunded wharf extensions that are essential to the operations and inadequate capital investment in infrastructures, freeport, and ecozone development. Moreover, there is no Relocation Action Plan for informal settlers in order to maximize the potential of the ecozone areas.

- **Lack of reformed freeports and ecozone policies.** There is an absence of manual operations and reformed freeports and ecozone policies that will govern and strengthen the operations of the freeport and ecozone to cover rules, and tariff rates applicable for and granting of fiscal incentives, and environmental standards imposed on freeports and ecozones. Further, the BEZA Law is yet to be established.

- **Limited number of ecozones developed.** The development of economic zones in BARMM has been one of the strategies to stimulate the economic activities and growth of the region. However, the region has still no Ecozone Development Plan that will serve as a blueprint for the development of the economic zone in the BARMM. Likewise, the required infrastructure to reinforce the industrial agglomeration in the Bangsamoro area may not be on hand. For instance, the Bangsamoro region's road density (0.10) remains the lowest in the country which is not even half of the national average (0.25). The issues on the access to electricity and water supply of the proposed ecozones to be established are yet to be addressed by the Bangsamoro Government. It cannot be overemphasized that the creation of ecozones itself does not guarantee an increase in investments, rather, the development of infrastructure facilities is the key to enhancing the investment climate in the region.

  The PFEZ-BARMM is the only existing ecozone in the region. A Final Master Plan and Feasibility Study Report was issued to PFEZ-BARMM last October 2018 that focused on addressing its port services development. The Bangsamoro region still suffers from inadequate infrastructure due to the low capital investments granted for industrial development. As a result, the ecozone generates low revenue collection for the period 2019-2021.

### Cooperatives and Social Enterprise

A key problem to be addressed under this sub-sector is the **low functionality and unsustainable profitability of cooperatives**. This is mainly due to the following:

- **Low compliance rate.** Only 11.66 percent of registered cooperatives were able to secure their certificate of compliance with the requirements set by the law. With the new proposed targets set by the sector for cooperative and social enterprise â€” raising the number of compliances up to 50 percent will serve as the priority challenge for the cooperative and social enterprise sub-sector.

- **Lack of enacted Code relevant to cooperative and social enterprises.** The pending codification of the Cooperative Code and Social Enterprise Code currently slows down the full operationalization and institutionalization of both Cooperatives and SE. However, the recent development of its crafting shows great potential for Bangsamoro Cooperatives and Social Enterprises (BCSE) as it went through an extensive consultation to create a code uniquely for the Bangsamoro.

- **Inadequate participation of LGUs in matters relating to cooperatives and social enterprises.** Active participation of LGUs through the inclusion of cooperative and social enterprise development in their Local Government Development plan as well as the appointment of Municipal Cooperative Officers (MCOs) will pave a way for better operationalization of cooperatives and social enterprises. However, as of now, most LGUs in the Bangsamoro Region had no appointed MCOs. Hence, regional cooperative officers find it difficult to coordinate at the LGU level. In addition, to address this issue, the establishment of provincial extension offices of CSEA is necessary.

### Labor and Employment

A key problem to be addressed under this sub-sector within the period 2023-2028 is the **lack of decent employment (insufficient employment opportunities and low-quality jobs)**. This is primarily caused by inadequate labor supply and low labor demand.

**Inadequate labor supply is mainly caused by:**

- **Insufficient number of skilled workers.** This is caused by high/increased out-migration for work and the departure of professionals and non-professionals to seek better opportunities.

- **Increased and continuing vulnerability to trafficking, forced labor, and abusive labor conditions of overseas and local workers.** This is caused by poor terms of employment and working conditions, lack of decent work and a high degree of informality, continuous operation of unscrupulous private recruitment agencies, and inability to secure work and work experience.

- **Low skilled and inexperienced human resources with low productivity (job-skills mismatch).** This is mainly due to a lack of core employability skills, which is caused by limited access to quality education and training, a high number of dropouts, and low enrolment in vocational courses.

- **Limited and/or lack of access to decent work opportunities.** This is mainly caused by a lack of knowledge and awareness of decent work opportunities and a weak infrastructure system to support access to employment/investments.

**Low labor demand** can be explained by an insufficient number of industries/companies and limited public and private labor-intensive investments.

## Priority Strategies and Programs

### Priority Strategies

#### Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform

**Strategy 1:** Sustainably increase agriculture and fisheries productivity, resiliency, and access to markets and services of farmers and fisherfolks through the development and implementation of responsive policies, anticipatory planning, innovative and research-based programming, and partnership with LGUs, SUCs, private sector, and industry players;

**Strategy 2:** Provide agri-fishery infrastructure facilities and capacity-building support, and strengthen information dissemination to the farmers and fisherfolks;

**Strategy 4:** Strengthen agri-fishery data management planning, regulatory and quarantine, resource programming, implementation processes, and M&E to include systematic land acquisition, equitable land distribution, and tenurial security;

**Strategy 5:** Investing and professionalizing young farmers and fisherfolks through scholarship, exchange programs, and skills training;

**Strategy 6:** Enhance the primary and proactive engagement/leadership role of the LGUs in agricultural development initiatives;

**Strategy 7:** Develop capacities of LGUs to develop and implement data-driven agricultural development plans, identify local priorities and opportunities, and utilize LGU funds and resources;

**Strategy 8:** Strengthen capacities of farmer's organizations and agri-cooperatives as the driver of agricultural development in partnership with LGUs and other development stakeholders including private sectors;

**Strategy 9:** Continuous capacity development of the MAFAR's agricultural workers with emphasis on modern production technologies and digitalization;

**Strategy 10:** Design and implement a recognition and reward system for well-performing farmers and fisherfolks to uplift their dignity as this is also a way to encourage youth engagement in agriculture/aquaculture;

**Strategy 11:** Promote agro-entrepreneurship with emphasis on the value chain - from production to marketing;

**Strategy 12:** Encourage the adaption of agricultural intensification/climate-smart agricultural technologies and capability building for farmers/fishers on disaster preparedness mechanisms;

**Strategy 13:** Support the harmonization of agricultural development plans from the municipal/city, provincial, and to regional levels.

#### Trade and Industry

**Strategy 14:** Improve competitiveness and sustainability of MSMEs and industries by increasing support for institutional building, value adding and product development, market promotion, linkages and networking, and strengthening business registration, regulatory, and accreditation services;

**Strategy 15:** Strengthen investment promotion at the LGUs through intensive capacity building on investment promotion and generation, and provision of technical assistance on the formulation and passage of local investment code;

#### Tourism

**Strategy 16:** Increase the level of awareness and compliance on tourism accreditation through intensification of the implementation of tourism accreditation services and enhancement of consumers/client's welfare protection;

**Strategy 17:** Develop a tourism investment-friendly environment through strong partnerships and intensification of collaboration between and among LGUs, national agencies, and private sectors;

**Strategy 18:** Develop competitive tourism sites and destinations and strengthen tourism MSMEs through tourism products and service development and by building entrepreneurial capability and resiliency;

**Strategy 19:** Expand economic opportunities in industry and services by transforming Bangsamoro Economic Zone and Freeport into a vibrant environment for the economic hub of the region through infrastructure and facility modernization, digitalization, and institutionalization of policies and regulation;

**Strategy 20:** Enable mechanism for BEZA institutionalization and establishment of ecozones in the BARMM;

**Strategy 25:** Increase employability of Bangsamoro workers, youth, women, displaced workers, persons with special needs, IPs, and elderly, and increase decent work opportunities and facilitate capacity development through skills training for the Bangsamoro labor force; and

**Strategy 26:** Reduce the vulnerability of Bangsamoro workers and their families to trafficking, child labor, forced labor, discrimination, or economic shocks, and advance their rights and welfare through equitable and accessible social protection.

### Priority Programs

#### Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform

**Basic Integration for Harmonized Intervention (BInHI).** The BInHI program aims to increase agriculture productivity, resilience, and access to markets and services to farmers; provide infrastructure facilities and capacity building; and strengthen information dissemination to the farmers, and strengthen partnerships with LGUs, SUCs, private sector, and other industry players.

The program consists of 19 projects namely: (1) Productivity Enhancement Project for Small Landholder Farmers in the Bangsamoro; (2) Establishment of Regional Livestock Halal Production Center; (3) Animal Disease Control and Prevention (ADCP) Project; (4) Support to Agricultural Machineries, Equipment, Facilities and Infrastructure; (5) BARMM Agricultural Soil and Water Information System (BASIS) Project; (6) Agriculture Production and Pre-harvest Infrastructure Facilities; (7) Establishment of One Bangsamoro Agri-fishery Laboratory Complex (OBALC); (8) Periodic Regional Irrigation System Monitoring (PRISM) Project; (9) Field Access to Soil Testing (FAST) Project; (10) Feeds Analysis, Regulation, and Monitoring (FARM) Project; (11) Composting of Raw Organic Wastes and Nutrient Supplementation (CROWNS) Project; (12) Electronic Identification, Transaction, and Encryption (ELITE) Project; (13) Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform Control Panel for Animal Disease Recognition Enhancement Services (MAFARCARES) Project; (14) Bangsamoro Animal Laboratory Information Management System (BALIMS) Project; (15) Bangsamoro Electronic Animal Records System (BEARS) Project; (16) Bangsamoro Biosafety and Biosecurity (BBB) Project; (17) BARMM Animal Disease Monitoring and Surveillance Support (BADMoSS) Project; (18) Halal and Organic Production Development Project; and (19) Support to decommissioned and non-decommissioned MILF and MNLF combatants; and Support to Orphan Children within and outside Orphanage Centers.

**Agriculture Production and Pre-harvest Infrastructure Facilities Subprogram.** This aims to serve and manage Small Scaled Irrigation System and Housing for Ruminants, Poultry, and other Livestock. The subprogram consists of two projects namely: (1) Small Scaled Irrigation System; and (2) Housing for Ruminants, Poultry, and other Livestock.

**Agriculture Post Harvest Facilities Subprogram.** This aims to manage and implement interventions on High value crop Processing Facilities, Storage and Drying Facilities, and Marketing Facilities. The subprogram has three projects namely: (1) High Value Crop Processing Facilities; (2) Storage and Drying Facilities; and (3) Marketing Facilities.

**Other Agriculture Infrastructure Support Subprogram.** This aims to manage and implement Farm-to-Market Roads in the identified production, market, and food terminals areas in the region and other Bangsamoro communities outside the region's core territories. The subprogram has one project concentrating on FMR.

**MAFAR Initiatives on the Camps Transformation in the Bangsamoro and other Conflict affected Areas Subprogram.** This subprogram aims to cater the economic development needs of the MILF identified major camps and other Bangsamoro communities outside the region's core territories. This is also in support to the Camps Transformation enshrined in the BOL. This subprogram has three projects namely: (1) FMR Project; (2) Livelihood Assistance Project; (3) Pre- and Post-Harvest Facilities Project.

**Productivity Management Subprogram (PMS).** The PMS aims to measure productivity of major commodities in the region and monitor and evaluate the outcome and impact of the interventions on agriculture. This includes the regulation and quarantine of agriculture products, livestock and the monitoring and prevention of pests and diseases. The subprogram consists of seven projects namely: (1) Sustainable Planting and Replanting of Local Cultivars (SPRL); (2) Extension Support, Education and Training Services; (3) Monitoring and Evaluation headed by Policy, Planning, Monitoring, and Evaluation Division; (4) Crop Health Information System in BARMM (CHISB); (5) BARMM Regulation of Agricultural Commodities and Enterprises (BRACE); (6) Food Security Convergence Project; and (7) Production Areas Data Gathering and Hazard Vulnerability And Risk Assessment Project headed by Policy, Planning, Monitoring, and Evaluation Division.

**Agribusiness and Marketing Assistance Program.** This program aims to sustainably increase agriculture and fisheries' access to markets and services, and strengthen access of farmers, fisherfolks, and agrarian reform beneficiaries to capital insurance, loans/grants, resources, and equipment through a value chain approach. The program aims to promote rural agro-industrialization, particularly among the SMEs in rural communities, in collaboration with other investment promotion government agencies. It aimed also to provide business development services to farmers and enterprises including capacity building and facilitating their linkage with the business sector. The program consists of seven projects, namely: (1) Marketing Development for Agri-Fishery Enterprises Project; (2) MAFAR Local Exchange and Network of Goods in Any Kind of Emergency (MAFARLENGKE) Project; (3) Agribusiness Promotion Project; (4) MAFAR Loan for the Enhancement of Necessary Development Strategies (MAFARLENDS) Project; (5) Agribusiness Promotion Project; (6) Bangsamoro Farm Youth Challenge Project; and (7) Food Processing and Product Development Project.

**Integrated and Sustainable Development for Aquaculture/Capture (ISDA).** This program aims to increase fisheries production in a diversified and sustainable way, increase household income through greater market access, and improve small-scale fishery businesses and enterprises. It aims to provide a livelihood package for associations/cooperatives and individuals for their immediate generating income projects. This program consists of six projects, namely: (1) Fisheries Production Support Project; (2) Fisheries Law Enforcement and Regulatory Support Project; (3) Fisheries Research Support Project; (4) Establishment of Regional Fisheries Laboratory; (5) Fisheries Post-Harvest and Marketing Support Project; and (6) Fisheries Resource Management Project.

**Fisheries Post Harvest Facilities Subprogram.** This aims to strengthen the fisheries post-harvest lost and to manage the fish production in the region and other Bangsamoro communities outside the region's core territories. The subprogram has four projects namely: (1) Support to Seaweeds Farming; (2) Stilt Dryer with Mini Warehouse; (3) Small Scale Fish Processing Facility; and (4) Fish Processing Facility with Cold Storage.

**Other Fisheries Infrastructure Support Subprogram.** This aims to support fish landings, fish hatcheries, and fishponds in the region. The subprogram has three projects namely: (1) Hatchery & other Aquaculture Facilities; (2) Fish Landing/Terminal; and (3) Fishponds.

**Land Acquisition and Development for the Upliftment of Poor Agrarian Reform Beneficiaries (LUPA).** This program aims to address issues such as the presence of erroneous coverage, weak implementation of properly documented tenancy, strengthened observance of laws and procedures in land use conversion, weak monitoring of the status of awarded lands and their beneficiaries, and land conflict arising from the implementation of the CARP in the region. LUPA program has the following subprograms and projects: (1) Land Tenure Security Program (LTSP); (2) Land Tenure Sustainability Project for the protection of agrarian reform beneficiaries and Sustainable Food Security; (3) Camp Transformational Resettlement Project; (4) Sustainable Development for Agrarian Reform Beneficiaries in the Bangsamoro (SDAReBB); (5) Social Infrastructure Building (SIB); (6) Enterprise Development And Economic Support (EDES); (7) Land Acquisition and Distribution (LAD); (8) non-LAD Project; (9) post-LAD Project; (10) Agrarian Reform Community Development Support Project; and (11) Agrarian Justice Delivery Project.

**Bangsamoro Agri-Fishery EVIDENCE (Exploration of Value-Adding and Innovative Development based on Evidence, Nature-Friendly, Collaboration, and Excellence).** The program is responsible for the technology generations of midstream and applied research to evolve and improve agri-fishery technology packages for recommendation to farmers and fisherfolks; operationalize the existing research and development facilities; establishment of on-farm research through provincial research outreach stations (ROSs) for technology verification and evaluation; activate and orchestrate Bangsamoro Agri-Fishery Research, Development, and Extension Network members; responsible for the formulation of Regional Integrated Research, Development, and Extension Agenda and Programs (RIRDEAP); endorse Research, Development, and Extension (RDE) Agri-Fishery proposals from the network member agencies and State Universities and Colleges (SUCs) to national counterpart and other funding institutions; develop and maintain RDE information system; and provide overall management of RDE and its satellite stations and facilities. This program consists of six projects, namely: (1) Increased Productivity through Location-Specific and Climate-Smart Agri-fishery Technologies (INPLACE) Project; (2) Intensified Production of High-Quality Inputs for Sustainable Farming and Fishing Project; (3) Development of Halal Certified Agri-Fishery Food Products; (4) Plant and Animal Breeding Project; (5) Agri-fishery Chemical Laboratory Projects; and (6) Food Processing and Product Development Project.

#### Trade and Industry

**Bangsamoro MSME Development (BMSMED) Program.** This program aims to address the needs of MSMEs in the areas of technology development and transfer, financing, marketing, and training. BMSMED is mandated to promote and develop MSMEs in the region and is also tasked to review and formulate strategies geared towards the advancement of MSMEs in the areas of entrepreneurship development, institutional strengthening, and productivity improvement. Product development and innovation, enables localities and communities to determine, develop, support, and promote culturally rooted products or services where they can be the best at or best renowned of. This program consists of three projects namely: (1) Regional Initiative for Sustainable Enterprise (RISE); (2) Negosyo Center; and (3) Propelling Economic and Sustainable Opportunity (PESO).

**Growth Enhancement Approach towards Regional Economic Development (GEARED) Program.** This program aims to develop market-driven local industries to reach the economy of scale, capacitate the enablers and industry stakeholder, provide market opportunities and linkages to actors across the value chain, support the initiatives of the Chamber of Commerce and Industries, and address the problems of inconvenient business operating environments, and the limited number of competitive products and services. This program consists of four projects namely: (1) Industry Roadmap for Bangsamoro Coffee Project; (2) Industry Roadmap for Bangsamoro Fishing Industry Project; (3) Industry Roadmap Development for Bangsamoro Weaving Industry Project; and (4) Shared Service Facility (SSF).

**Accelerate Competitiveness by Engaging Private Sector (ACE) Program.** This is a support program for Chamber of Commerce and Industries (CCIs) in BARMM that aims to encourage participation in Policy formulation and foster partnership for viability of business. This program consists of three projects namely: (1) Trading Engagement on Inter-Regional and International Market Project (TEIIM); (2) Partnership and Collaboration on BIMP-EAGA Trading Activities; and (3) Strengthening and Developing Business Chamber.

**Bangsamoro City Municipality Competitive (BCMC) Program.** The City and Municipality Competitive Index (CMCI) is an annual ranking developed by National Competitiveness Council (NCC) through the Regional Competitiveness Committees (RCCs) to stimulate competition among Local Government Units (LGUs) based on the sum of their scores on 5 Pillars: Economic Dynamism, Government Efficiency, Infrastructure, Resiliency, and Innovation. This program consists of three projects namely: (1) Development of Local Economic Enterprise (DLEE/LED) Project; (2) Regional Inclusive Innovation Center Projects (RIIC); and (3) City and Municipality Competitive Index (CMCI).

**Consumer Protection and Promotion (CoPP) Program.** This involves consumer education, awareness, and advocacy programs by ensuring a consumer safe environment by undertaking programs that support informed and vigilant consumers. This program consists of three projects namely: (1) Rolling Store Project; (2) Bagwis Project; (3) Consumer Education and Advocacy Project.

**Regulation and Accreditation Program (RAP).** This is a program on enforcement of laws to protect consumers through monitoring and enforcement of Fair-Trade laws, handling consumer complaints, accrediting and issuance of business licenses, permits & Registration, and formation of consumer groups. This program consists of two projects namely: (1) Business legitimacy Project; and (2) Business Mapping Project.

**Halal Industry Development Program (HIDeP).** Halal is emerging as a global industry not only for Muslim consumers but spreading fast in western markets. BARMM is in an uphill struggle to realize economic growth to meet the needs of its people. The need to establish a sound policy and direction for Halal is deemed necessary. A policy that will not only address the Halal food industry development but the entire halal ecosystem from production, promotion, trade regulation, certification and research and development, among others.

With this, it is the avowed policy of the Bangsamoro government through the MTIT to introduce new strategies to enhance productivity and job generation to uplift the economic condition of the people of BARMM. One of the economic strategies launched by the Bangsamoro government is the promotion of Halal industries which is geared towards the strengthening of the agri-business sector to respond to the emerging need for Halal products and services. To sustain this economic drive, a host of requirements must be laid out. One of these is the need for a Halal Industry Development Plan to be formulated and implemented.

The plan will serve as the directional chart of BARMM's flagship economic thrust as it takes on the various industry challenges ahead. This will also enable a more focused effort and a more judicious allocation of resources for the regional government. Also, it would enable a more defined direction for the various stakeholders of the industry. This program consists of three projects namely: (1) Formulation of Halal Industry Development Plan; (2) Development of Halal Accreditation Guidelines; and (3) Halal Accreditation.

**Barter Trading Development Program (BTDeP).** The institutionalization of the barter trade is an investment for peace and inclusive, equitable, sustainable development, especially in the newly established Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao. The barter trading is a culture bound trading practice of the people in the Southern Philippines particularly people in the areas of the BARMM with the people of BIMP-EAGA and the ASEAN member states.

The program seeks to accredit, regulate, supervise, coordinate, and harmonize all policies, operations, programs, and activities of barter trade in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It is issued rules and regulations on the registration or accreditation of traders qualified to engage in traditional barter trade within the Barter Ports to be established, including imposition of reasonable registration fees and facilitation of all documentary requirements necessary to avail of the zero-tariff benefits under the ASEAN Free Trade Agreement and the ASEAN Trade in Goods Agreement; exercise regulatory powers over the operation of the barter trade in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region; review existing policies, rules and regulations of government agencies affecting barter trade and submit recommendations to the Chief Minister and the Parliament; Issue comprehensive guidelines governing barter trade, including but not limited to a mechanism for assessment of barter goods, a provision on allowable barter goods, and measures to prevent smuggling and circumvention of customs laws, rules and regulations. This program consists of two projects namely: (1) Regulatory and Accreditation; and (2) Policy Support.

#### Tourism

**Tourism Circuit Development Program (TCDep).** This program aims to increase the number of tourism circuits developed, strengthen LGU capacity in tourism circuit development, increase the number of tourism sites supported with road infrastructure, and increase the number of gateways with the established Tourism Information and Assistance Center (TIAC). This program consists of the following projects: (1) Development of Tourism Circuit Project; and (2) Support to Tourism Infrastructure Project.

#### Investments

**Bangsamoro Integrated Investment Program (BIIP).** This program aims to encourage investors through the passage of laws and incentives and strengthen investment promotion at the LGU through intensive capacity building on investment promotion and generation, and provision of technical assistance on the formulation and passage of the local investment code. The projects under this program are: (1) Regional Investment Project for Registered Business Enterprises (RBEs); and (2) Regional Investment Development and Partnership Project (RIDPP).

**Investment Generation and Development Program (IGDeP).** This program aims to increase investment leads generation, facilitate investment in the region, expand the horizon of knowledge to fill the gaps in showcasing the investment opportunities in the region, and strengthen LGU's capacity to promote and facilitate local investment. This program consists of four projects, namely: (1) Investment Development Project; (2) Development of BARMM Investment Facilitation Network Project (BIF-Net); (3) Development of Management Investment Data System; and (4) Networking on Investment for Local Executives (NILE) Project.

#### Ecozone and Freeport

**Bangsamoro Economic Zone Development Program (BEZDeP).** This program aims to increase the ecozone/enterprise investment generation, the number of ecozones processed for approval and legislation of the Bangsamoro Parliament, and the number of enterprises/locators registered. Ultimately, it aims to expand economic opportunities in industry and services by transforming Bangsamoro Economic Zone and Freeport into a vibrant environment for the economic hub of the region through infrastructure and facility modernization, digitalization, and institutionalization of policies and regulation.

This also covers the formulation, legislation, adoption, and promotion of the BEZA Law. The legislation of the BEZA Law will generate jobs, especially in the rural areas, increase their productivity and their individual and family income, and ultimately to improve the level and quality of their living condition.

Moreover, it also facilitates the construction of required infrastructure, such as light and power systems, water supply and distribution systems, telecommunication, and transportation, print and media facilities, buildings, structures, warehouses, roads, bridges, ports, and other facilities for the operation and development of the ECOZONE, including shipping and related business, stevedoring and port terminal services or concessions, incidental thereto and other facilities.

Furthermore, it encompasses ecozone development plans, key concepts, theories, best practices, and models as regards free ports, special economic zones, and others that were used to be able to give different perspectives and to provide a better appreciation of the project. This Program consists of two projects, namely: (1) Ecozone Investment Promotion Partnerships Project; and (2) Infrastructure Development on Ecozone and Enterprise Advancement (IDEEA).

**Freeport and Modernization Program (FMoP).** The program will play a vital role to the economic activities of the freeport and ecozone. These will contribute largely to the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region's economic, political, and trade relation with local and international. The program will realize the huge benefits that can be derived in establishing a quality port industry. Thus, by promoting regulations to support policies, guidelines, rules, and regulations help to access a sustainable port trading.

The program provides a quality service to the port users and become a competitive port destination upon complete realization. With the issuance of International Ship and Port facility Security (ISPS Code), ensures that the freeport and ecozone is fully secured and safe for local and international vessels to dock. The port is an agile industry that needs immediate intervention due to fast pacing of modern technology and new infrastructure innovations. The modernization and development program immerse into a competitive port not only in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region but be known internationally. The program consists of two projects, namely: (1) Port Facilities Improvement and Upgrading Project; and (2) Institutional Development and Strengthening on Revenue Collection and Port Operation are the main projects under the Freeport and Modernization Program.

#### Labor and Employment

**Employment Facilitation Program.** This major program aims to increase the employability of Bangsamoro workers, youth, women, displaced workers, differently-abled persons, and the elderly, and increase decent work opportunities. It consists of five sub-programs/projects, namely: (1) Enterprise-based Education and Training Program (Apprenticeship and Learnership); (2) Bridging Employment Program Assistance for the Youth (Special Program for the Employment of Students, Bangsamoro Government Internship Program, and JobStart); (3) Public Employment Services (Job Fairs, MOLE Job Portal, Technical Assistance to PESO, MBREAS, CGECGS); (4) Labor Market Information; and (5) Emergency Employment Program (Community Emergency Employment Program and Bangsamoro Rural Employment thru Entrepreneurial Development Program).

**Employment Preservation and Regulation Program.** This major program aims to ensure just and humane working conditions and attained harmonious labor-management relations and industrial peace toward productivity. It is comprised of seven sub-programs/projects, namely: (1) Employment Regulations Program (FEP, Registration of PRPA and Subcontractors, and IR/TIP); (2) Overseas Recruitment Regulations Program (Accreditation of PRAs, Annual Convention of PRAs or BARAS, and Annual Convention of OBWs); (3) Wage Regulatory Program (Minimum Wage Fixing, and TTWES); (4) Labor Enforcement Program (LI, TAV, ERS, Permits, Registration and Certificate, Reportorial Requirements, and Establishment Database); (5) Labor Case Management Program (SEnA Settlement Rate, Disposition and Enforcement Rate of LS, CM, and Compulsory Arbitration Cases including NOS/L); (6) Workers' Organizations Development and Empowerment Program (LEES, OSH Training, Entrepreneurship Development of BLO, and Registry of Unions and CBAs); and (7) Tripartism and Social Dialogue Program (BTIPC, ITCs, and LMC/GM).

This program includes two projects namely: (1) Strengthening of Technical and Operational Knowledge Project; and (2) Cooperative Financial and Material Support Project.

## Priority Policy and Legislative Agenda

**Bangsamoro Regional Crop Protection Center (RCPC).** The RCPC shall ensure proper and timely crop pest management actions through proactive pest identification, risk assessment, monitoring, surveillance, predicting occurrences, management, and control, including the identification, isolation, culture, and mass production of natural pest enemies.

**Institutionalization of Bangsamoro Agri-Fishery Training Institute (BAFTI).** The BAFTI shall be responsible for the training of all Agri-Fishery extension workers, farmers, fisherfolks, and ARBs and including the incorporation and extension of the matured technologies from the research and development to the target clientele through appropriate training and extension activities, to support increased productivity in agriculture and fisheries.

**Bangsamoro Crops Research Institute (BCRI).** The research institute is tasked to undertake rice, corn, and high-value crop research programs for the BARMM.

**Institutionalization of the Farmer and Fisherfolks Enterprise Development Program.** The institutionalization will ensure the sustainability and increase in agriculture and fisheries access to markets and services and strengthen access of farmers, fisherfolks, and agrarian reform beneficiaries to capital insurance, loans/grants, resources, and equipment through a value chain approach.

**Bangsamoro Fertilizer and Pesticide Authority (BFPA).** The BFPA will be responsible for the regulation of the supply of fertilizer and pesticides, rationalizing the manufacturer and marketing of fertilizer and pesticide to protect the public from risks by using the pesticides and awareness of the agriculture and fisheries sector in the application of these inputs. Also, responsible for the regulation and issuance of the permit to operate for the fertilizer and pesticide distributor in the Bangsamoro region.

**Amendment and adoption of applicable BFAR Fisheries Administrative Order based on the MAFAR structure.** The Fisheries Services is mandated to formulate fisheries standardized research, guidelines, policies, and procedures. Hence, the Bangsamoro Fisheries Administrative Order (BFAO) shall be created for the purpose of implementing its service mandate and to provide an effective direction in carrying out its functions which can be of effective help in ensuring the success of its implementation and objectives, pursuant to the MAFAR Structures. This serves as a guide for our regional fisheries operations and guides the implementation of the fisheries program and initiatives as a means of fostering the livelihood of our fisherfolks.

**Regional Fisheries Law Enforcement Council (RFLEC).** The law enforcement is part and partial of the operation of fisheries programs and activities to ensure efficient and effective enforcement of fishery laws, rules, and regulations. This is also providing standard operation procedures for law enforcement officers and other law enforcement partners like the coastguard, PNP, and others.

**Fisheries Adjudication Board (FAB).** The fisheries adjudication board serves as the ministry committee with sole authority to adjudicate related fishery cases in tandem with the created hearing officers of the ministry to investigate.

**Bay-wide Management Council (BMC).** The institutionalization of this Council will address the sub-sectoral objectives of improving food resiliency, climate change adaptation, and resource management in the fishery. The policy will strengthen the fishery resources management, protection, and conservation of our coastal resources to maintain the fish stocks and their environs. The Council will serve as a body that will provide technical advisory, recommendatory, and guidelines on the proper implementation of coastal fishery resources.

**Local Government Tourism Code (LGTC).** This will help facilitate boosting and harnessing the promising tourism economic potentials of our region. This will greatly help the provincial/city/municipal LGUs in developing and managing the local tourism assets.

**Bangsamoro Investments Code.** The code aims to establish the BBOI on a "more stable footing". It shall provide uniform fiscal incentives to investors for all the investment promotion agencies in the country, including the BBOI. This policy intends to help the achievement of sub-sector objectives on increasing investment generation in BARMM and will address key issue of limited private investments in BARMM.

**Application of PPP in the LGUs.** Equally important is the institutionalization of policy that hopes to make the PPP as one of the economic opportunities that will undoubtedly espouse context-based economic activities in harmony with the thrust of the LGUs.

**Inter-Ministry Committee on Investment.** The establishment of an inter-ministry mechanism tasked to champion investment-related programs and or initiatives in both soft packages and hard infrastructure will fire up an unprecedented high trend in investment relocation across the region.

**Establishment of Ease of Doing Business (ARTA).** This policy will help intensify the implementation of the Ease of Doing Business Act in BARMM (RA 11032), which streamlines the procedures in the processing of applications, through the established Business One Stop Shop (BOSS).

### Ecozone and Freeport Services

**Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA) Law.** BEZA an attached agency of the MTIT was created by virtue of RA 11054 and the Chapter 6 of the BAA 13, highlights the creation of BEZA and sets forth therein the creation of economic zones and freeports within the BARMM, as well as the general powers and functions of BEZA and its Executive Director.

The BOL and the BAA 13 are not sufficient to administer incentives and oversee economic zones and freeports within BARMM considering that the relevant provisions thereof merely authorize or empower the Bangsamoro government through its Bangsamoro Parliament to create BEZA and economic zones within the BARMM. It is, therefore, necessary to have a separate law clearly establishing BEZA and stating its functions and scope of authority, including the power to grant incentives as well as the incentives that may be granted, within the limits and bounds of the CREATE Act and other applicable laws.

**Crafting of Polloc Freeport and Economic Zone-BARMM Charter.** The PFEZ exist by virtue of the MMA Act 153 and Proclamation 01, dated 2010. It must have a charter to define the structure and function as an autonomous port. The freeport and ecozone should establish clear policies, rules, and regulations on the port operation and development.
