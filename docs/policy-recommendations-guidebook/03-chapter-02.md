# Chapter 2 — Understanding the Policy Environment

Before you write a single recommendation, understand the environment you are working in. The Bangsamoro policy environment is defined by law, development strategy, institutional structure, and the communities the government serves. This chapter maps that environment so you can navigate it with confidence.

---

## 2.1 The Bangsamoro Organic Law as the Governing Framework

The Bangsamoro Organic Law — Republic Act No. 11054 — is the foundation of everything. Every policy recommendation you write must be consistent with the BOL. Every program you propose must fall within the powers it grants. Every budget you estimate must align with the fiscal framework it establishes.

### 2.1.1 Enumerated Powers and Policy Domains

Article V, Section 2 of the BOL lists fifty-five subject areas over which the Bangsamoro government exercises exclusive authority. These enumerated powers define the policy space you work in. They include:

- Administrative organization and governance
- Creation of sources of revenue
- Education policy and institutions
- Health services and disease prevention
- Social welfare and development
- Labor and employment
- Trade and industry
- Agriculture, fisheries, and forestry
- Environmental protection and natural resources management
- Public works and infrastructure
- Tourism development
- Science and technology
- Cooperative development
- Shari'ah law and the Shari'ah justice system
- Cultural heritage preservation and development
- Youth, women, and marginalized sector development
- Halal industry regulation

**Before you begin any policy analysis, verify that your subject falls within these enumerated powers.** If it does, you have clear authority to recommend action. If it does not, check whether the general welfare clause applies (Section 2.1.3 below) or whether the subject falls under concurrent or reserved national powers.

### 2.1.2 Reserved and Concurrent Powers

Not all policy domains belong to the Bangsamoro government. The BOL reserves certain powers to the national government:

- Defense and external security
- Foreign affairs
- Monetary policy, currency, and banking regulation
- Citizenship and immigration
- Customs and tariff regulation
- Postal service

Other areas involve **concurrent authority** — both the Bangsamoro government and the national government can act, subject to coordination. These include:

- Disaster risk reduction and management
- Environmental protection (where national standards apply)
- Public health (particularly epidemic response)
- Social protection programs with national components

When your policy recommendation touches a concurrent area, you must address the coordination mechanism. Who leads — the Bangsamoro ministry or the national agency? How do budgets overlap? What memoranda of agreement exist? The Intergovernmental Relations Body (IGRB) established under the BOL is the primary forum for resolving these questions.

### 2.1.3 The General Welfare Clause

Article V, Section 3 of the BOL provides a general welfare clause:

> In addition to its powers and prerogatives under this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over all matters and affairs necessary for the promotion of the general welfare of the Bangsamoro people within its territorial jurisdiction.

This clause gives the Bangsamoro government the flexibility to address emerging issues not explicitly listed in Article V, Section 2 — provided the action promotes the general welfare and does not conflict with the Constitution, national law, or the BOL itself.

Use the general welfare clause carefully. It is not a blanket authorization to act on any subject. A policy recommendation that relies primarily on the general welfare clause should explain why the subject is not covered by an enumerated power and why the proposed action is necessary for the general welfare. Decision-makers and legal reviewers will ask.

## 2.2 The Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028

The **Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP) 2023-2028** is the strategic framework that guides all Bangsamoro government programs, projects, and activities. Where the BOL tells you what you can do, the BDP tells you what the government has prioritized.

### 2.2.1 Structure and Strategic Goals

The BDP 2023-2028 is organized around three overarching strategic goals:

1. **Moral governance** — Strengthening institutions, improving public trust, and ensuring transparent, accountable government.
2. **Meaningful self-determination** — Building the capacity for genuine self-governance across all levels of the Bangsamoro.
3. **Genuine socioeconomic development** — Improving the material well-being of Bangsamoro communities through inclusive economic growth and social service delivery.

These goals are operationalized through fifteen chapters covering sectors from education and health to infrastructure and fiscal management. Each chapter includes situation analyses, outcome indicators, targets, and priority strategies.

### 2.2.2 Using the BDP to Ground Policy Recommendations

Every policy recommendation you write should reference the BDP. Not because it is a bureaucratic requirement — though it is — but because the BDP provides the strategic logic that connects your specific recommendation to the government's broader agenda.

When grounding a recommendation in the BDP:

1. **Identify the relevant BDP chapter and section.** A recommendation on scholarship programs references the education chapter. A recommendation on halal certification references the trade and industry chapter.
2. **Cite the specific outcome indicators and targets.** The BDP sets measurable targets. Your recommendation should explain which targets it advances and by how much.
3. **Show alignment with priority strategies.** Each BDP chapter lists priority strategies. Your recommendation should map directly to one or more of these strategies.
4. **Acknowledge where your recommendation goes beyond the BDP.** If your proposal addresses an issue the BDP does not cover, say so. Explain why the issue deserves attention despite its absence from the plan. Decision-makers appreciate honesty about what is and is not in the existing strategic framework.

## 2.3 MOA Mandates and International Commitments

The Bangsamoro government's policy obligations extend beyond the BOL and the BDP. Two categories of external commitments shape the policy environment:

**Peace agreement commitments.** The Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (CAB) and its annexes — including the Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro (FAB), the Annex on Revenue Generation and Wealth Sharing, and the Annex on Normalization — contain specific commitments on governance, fiscal transfers, transitional justice, and socioeconomic development. Policy recommendations on topics like revenue sharing, decommissioning, or transitional justice must account for these commitments.

**International development goals.** The Bangsamoro government has aligned its development agenda with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and participates in monitoring frameworks coordinated by the National Economic and Development Authority (NEDA). When your recommendation touches an SDG-related target — poverty reduction, gender equality, education quality, environmental sustainability — reference the relevant SDG and the Philippines' reporting obligations.

## 2.4 National Laws and Their Applicability in the Bangsamoro

National laws apply in the Bangsamoro unless superseded by the BOL or a duly enacted BAA. This creates a layered legal environment that you must navigate carefully.

**General rule:** National laws continue to apply in the Bangsamoro until the Bangsamoro Parliament enacts its own legislation on the subject. Once the Parliament enacts a BAA on a subject within its enumerated powers, the BAA governs — and the corresponding national law no longer applies in the region to the extent of any inconsistency.

**Practical implications for policy work:**

- Before recommending a new program, check whether a national law already governs the subject. If so, determine whether the national law applies directly or has been superseded by a BAA.
- If no BAA exists on the subject, your recommendation may need to address the gap — either by proposing legislation or by recommending an executive program that operates within the existing national legal framework.
- When your recommendation involves coordination with national government agencies, cite the national law that governs the NGA's mandate and explain the coordination mechanism.

## 2.5 Stakeholder Mapping

Every policy recommendation affects people and institutions. Identifying those stakeholders early — and understanding their interests, influence, and positions — strengthens your analysis and prevents surprises during consultation and implementation.

### 2.5.1 Government Stakeholders

Start with the institutions inside the Bangsamoro government:

- **The Parliament.** If your recommendation requires legislation, the Parliament is your primary institutional stakeholder. Identify which committees have jurisdiction.
- **The Chief Minister and Cabinet.** If your recommendation requires executive action, it must pass through the Cabinet process. Identify which ministry is the lead implementing agency and which ministries have overlapping mandates.
- **The Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA).** BPDA coordinates the BDP and reviews programs for alignment with the development plan. Your recommendation will likely require BPDA endorsement.
- **Local government units.** Provinces, municipalities, cities, and barangays within the Bangsamoro have their own mandates under both national law and the BOL. If your recommendation affects local service delivery, LGUs are stakeholders.

### 2.5.2 Civil Society and the Private Sector

The Bangsamoro has an active civil society landscape — from large peace-building organizations to community-based cooperatives. Private sector actors — traders, entrepreneurs, industry associations — are stakeholders in any economic or regulatory policy.

Map civil society stakeholders by:

- **Sector.** Who works on the issue your recommendation addresses?
- **Geography.** Which organizations are active in the affected areas?
- **Track record.** Who has implemented relevant programs before?

### 2.5.3 Indigenous Peoples and Traditional Structures

The Bangsamoro is home to diverse communities, including non-Moro indigenous peoples whose rights are specifically protected under the BOL. Article IX, Section 4 of the BOL provides:

> The rights of the indigenous peoples in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region to their identity, their ancestral land, and self-governance shall be recognized and protected.

Policy recommendations that affect indigenous communities must address their specific rights and consultation requirements. Traditional governance structures — sultans, datus, tribal councils — also play a role in community decision-making and should be mapped as stakeholders.

### 2.5.4 International Development Partners

The Bangsamoro government works with multiple international development partners — the World Bank, JICA, USAID, UNDP, the European Union, and others. These partners fund programs, provide technical assistance, and bring international best practices.

When your recommendation involves an area where a development partner is active, include them in your stakeholder map. They may have data you need, programs you can build on, or coordination requirements you must satisfy.

## 2.6 Shari'ah and Cultural Considerations

The Bangsamoro is the only region in the Philippines with a Shari'ah justice system and specific provisions for Islamic law in governance. Policy recommendations must account for this.

**When Shari'ah considerations apply:**

- Policies affecting personal status — marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody — are governed by the Code of Muslim Personal Laws (PD 1083) and any superseding BAA.
- Policies on Islamic banking and finance must comply with both Republic Act No. 11439 (Islamic Banking Act) and relevant BOL provisions.
- Policies on education must account for the Madrasah education system and the integration of Islamic studies into the Bangsamoro education framework.
- Policies on halal industry regulation must comply with both national standards (NFA, DTI) and Bangsamoro regulatory frameworks.
- Policies on waqf (Islamic endowment) and zakat (obligatory alms) are within the Bangsamoro's exclusive authority.

**When cultural considerations apply:**

Not every cultural consideration is a Shari'ah question. Many policy issues — land use, environmental management, conflict resolution, youth development — are shaped by cultural practices and traditions that are not strictly Islamic in origin. The Bangsamoro is culturally diverse. The Tausug, Maguindanaon, Maranao, Sama, Yakan, and other communities have distinct practices and priorities.

Your stakeholder mapping should identify which cultural communities are affected by your recommendation. Your consultation process should include mechanisms for hearing from those communities. Your recommendation should acknowledge cultural considerations where they are relevant — but avoid reducing every issue to a cultural question. Some problems are technical, administrative, or fiscal. Address them on those terms.

### Practical Guidance for Navigating the Policy Environment

Map your policy environment before you begin your analysis. Use the following framework:

**Step 1: Legal scan.** Identify the BOL provisions, BAAs, and national laws that govern your subject. This takes one to two days of desk research. Do not skip it.

**Step 2: BDP alignment check.** Find the relevant BDP chapter, outcome indicators, and priority strategies. If your recommendation does not align with any BDP target, you need a strong justification for why it deserves attention.

**Step 3: Stakeholder inventory.** List every government agency, civil society organization, private sector actor, and community group that has a stake in your policy area. Classify each as a potential supporter, opponent, or neutral party.

**Step 4: Environmental scan.** Identify recent developments — new legislation, budget changes, political shifts, court decisions, international commitments — that affect your policy area. The policy environment is not static. What was true six months ago may not be true today.

**Step 5: Constraint mapping.** List the constraints that will shape your recommendation — legal limits, fiscal ceilings, implementation capacity, political feasibility, cultural sensitivity. These constraints are not obstacles to be ignored. They are parameters to be respected. A recommendation that ignores them will fail.

Document this environmental analysis as the foundation of your recommendation's Context and Rationale section (Chapter 4, Section 4.2.1). The better you understand the environment, the more realistic and actionable your recommendation will be.
