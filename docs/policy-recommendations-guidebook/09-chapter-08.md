# Chapter 8 — Quality Review and Approval

A good recommendation survives scrutiny. This chapter covers the review process that turns your draft into a document ready for the decision-maker's desk — from self-review through peer review, technical clearance, and formal transmittal.

---

## 8.1 The Quality Review Process

Quality review happens in four stages:

1. **Self-review.** You check your own work against a structured checklist before anyone else sees it.
2. **Peer review.** A colleague with relevant expertise reads and critiques the recommendation.
3. **Technical review chain.** The recommendation passes through legal, budget, and policy reviewers who assess compliance and feasibility.
4. **Executive approval.** The authorized official — a Minister, the Chief Minister, or an MP — approves the recommendation for action.

Each stage catches different types of problems. Self-review catches errors of fact, logic, and completeness. Peer review catches blind spots and weak arguments. Technical review catches legal, fiscal, and procedural issues. Executive approval ensures political alignment and institutional commitment.

Skip any stage and you increase the risk that a fixable problem becomes a public embarrassment.

---

## 8.2 The Self-Review Checklist

Before sharing your draft with anyone, review it yourself against the following checklist. This takes 30-60 minutes. It will save you days of revision.

### 8.2.1 Problem Definition

- [ ] Is the problem stated in specific, measurable terms?
- [ ] Does the problem statement focus on the affected population, not on the absence of a program?
- [ ] Is the problem within the Bangsamoro government's authority to address?
- [ ] Have you cited the source for every data point in the problem statement?

### 8.2.2 Evidence Base

- [ ] Does every factual claim have a cited source?
- [ ] Are your sources current? Have you noted the date of each data point?
- [ ] Have you included evidence that both supports and challenges your recommendation?
- [ ] Is your evidence registry complete and attached as an annex?

### 8.2.3 Legal Compliance

- [ ] Have you completed the Legal Compliance Checklist from Chapter 5?
- [ ] Have you cited the specific BOL provision that authorizes the recommended action?
- [ ] Have you identified and addressed relevant BAAs and national laws?
- [ ] Have you flagged any ultra vires risks?

### 8.2.4 Budget Feasibility

- [ ] Have you costed every element of your recommendation?
- [ ] Have you identified a specific funding source for each cost line?
- [ ] Have you presented multi-year cost projections?
- [ ] Have you assessed fiscal feasibility against the current BARMM budget?
- [ ] Are your budget tables complete — not templates, but actual figures?

### 8.2.5 Stakeholder Validation

- [ ] Have you consulted the stakeholders most affected by the recommendation?
- [ ] Have you documented your consultations and attached the documentation?
- [ ] Have you integrated stakeholder feedback into your analysis?
- [ ] Have you addressed consultation requirements for marginalized groups (where applicable)?

### 8.2.6 Implementation Readiness

- [ ] Have you identified the implementing agency and assigned clear responsibilities?
- [ ] Have you described the activities, timeline, and milestones?
- [ ] Have you assessed the implementing agency's capacity to deliver?
- [ ] Have you identified the top risks and proposed mitigation measures?
- [ ] Have you described the monitoring and evaluation plan?

If any item receives a "No," address it before requesting peer review. A draft that fails its own self-review will not survive external scrutiny.

## 8.3 Peer Review

### 8.3.1 Selecting Peer Reviewers

Choose peer reviewers who bring expertise you lack. If you are a sector specialist, ask a budget analyst to review your fiscal analysis. If you are a generalist, ask a sector expert to review your technical content. If you are confident in your analysis but uncertain about your writing, ask a colleague who writes clearly.

**Ideal reviewers bring:**

- **Subject-matter expertise** — they know the policy area and can assess whether your analysis is accurate
- **Institutional knowledge** — they understand the BARMM government's processes, politics, and constraints
- **Willingness to be candid** — a reviewer who says "looks good" without reading carefully is not helping you

Select two reviewers if time allows. Different perspectives strengthen the review.

### 8.3.2 Structured Review Feedback

Ask your reviewers to answer specific questions — not just "any comments." Structure their feedback:

1. **Is the problem clearly defined?** Would someone unfamiliar with the issue understand what the problem is and why it matters?
2. **Is the evidence persuasive?** Are there gaps, outdated sources, or unsupported claims?
3. **Are the alternatives genuinely different?** Or are they variations of the same approach?
4. **Is the recommended alternative convincingly justified?** Does the analysis support the recommendation over the other options?
5. **Is the budget realistic?** Are costs reasonable? Are funding sources identified?
6. **Is the implementation plan practical?** Could an implementing agency begin work based on this document?
7. **Is the document well-organized and clearly written?** Can the reader follow the logic from problem to recommendation?

### 8.3.3 Resolving Reviewer Comments

You will not agree with every comment. That is fine. The standard is not consensus — it is quality. When you receive a comment:

- **If the reviewer found an error, fix it.** Missing sources, incorrect figures, legal misstatements — these are non-negotiable corrections.
- **If the reviewer disagrees with your analysis, consider the disagreement carefully.** If the reviewer has evidence or reasoning you did not consider, revise your analysis. If you considered it and still disagree, note the disagreement in your document — transparency about contested points strengthens your credibility.
- **If the reviewer suggests restructuring, evaluate the suggestion against the four-section template.** The template is your organizing structure. Depart from it only if there is a compelling reason.

## 8.4 The Approval Chain

After self-review and peer review, your recommendation enters the formal approval chain. The exact chain depends on who originated the recommendation and who will act on it.

### 8.4.1 Technical Review

The first formal review is technical — conducted by the unit or office responsible for quality assurance in the originating agency.

- **In a ministry:** The Planning Division or Policy Unit reviews for alignment with the ministry's strategic plan and the BDP.
- **In a parliamentary office:** The Legislative Chief reviews for completeness and consistency with the MP's legislative agenda.
- **In a project management office:** The project director reviews for alignment with project scope and donor requirements.

The technical reviewer checks that the recommendation meets the standards of this guidebook — problem definition, evidence base, legal compliance, budget analysis, stakeholder consultation, and implementation readiness.

### 8.4.2 Legal Review

The legal unit or office reviews the recommendation for legal compliance:

- Does the recommendation fall within the BOL's grant of authority?
- Does it conflict with existing BAAs or national laws?
- Does it require legislation? If so, has the legislative path been identified?
- Does it create legal risks — liability, constitutional challenges, or enforcement gaps?

The legal reviewer may clear the recommendation, return it for revision, or flag issues for the decision-maker's attention.

### 8.4.3 Budget Review

The budget unit — or the MFBM for major policy proposals — reviews the financial analysis:

- Are costs accurately estimated?
- Are funding sources correctly identified and legally available?
- Does the recommendation comply with BAA 84 requirements?
- Is the proposal fiscally sustainable over the projected period?

The budget reviewer may approve the financial analysis, request adjustments, or flag fiscal constraints that the decision-maker needs to consider.

### 8.4.4 Executive Approval

The final step is executive approval — the authorized official reviews the recommendation and decides:

- **Approve.** The recommendation is accepted. Proceed to implementation planning.
- **Approve with modifications.** The official accepts the recommendation with specific changes. Incorporate the changes and update the document.
- **Defer.** The official accepts the analysis but delays action — perhaps until the next budget cycle, until a political condition is met, or until additional information is available.
- **Return for revision.** The official identifies significant problems and sends the recommendation back for further work. This is not failure — it is the review process working.

Document the decision and attach the approval documentation — a signed endorsement, a memorandum of approval, or meeting minutes — to the recommendation file.

## 8.5 Transmittal

### 8.5.1 Preparing the Transmittal Memorandum

Once approved, transmit the recommendation to the implementing agency, parliamentary committee, or other recipient. The transmittal memorandum should include:

- **From:** The originating office and official
- **To:** The receiving office and official
- **Date:** Transmittal date
- **Subject:** Title of the policy recommendation
- **Action requested:** What the recipient should do — review, implement, endorse, fund
- **Attachments:** The full recommendation document and all annexes

The transmittal memorandum is brief — one page. It points to the attached recommendation for the full analysis.

### 8.5.2 Supporting Annexes

Attach the following to your recommendation:

1. **Evidence registry** — your evidence trail (Chapter 3, Section 3.6)
2. **Legal Compliance Checklist** — completed and signed (Chapter 5, Section 5.8)
3. **Budget tables** — completed with actual figures (Chapter 6, Section 6.7)
4. **Stakeholder consultation documentation** — attendance lists, minutes, key findings (Chapter 7, Section 7.6)
5. **Peer review comments and responses** — the review table from Section 8.3.3 above
6. **Quality Review Checklist** — completed (Section 8.6 below)

### 8.5.3 Distribution and Filing

Distribute the approved recommendation to all relevant parties:

- The implementing agency or agencies
- The budget unit (for resource allocation)
- The legal unit (for reference and monitoring)
- The Parliament's relevant committee (if legislative action is needed)
- The BPDA (for BDP alignment tracking)

File the original document — with all annexes, review comments, and approval documentation — in the originating office's records. This file is the institutional memory of the recommendation. Future analysts working on the same topic will need it.
