# Chapter 3 — Evidence-Based Policy Analysis

Good policy starts with good evidence. This chapter covers the research methods, data sources, and analytical practices you need to produce policy recommendations that decision-makers can trust.

---

## 3.1 Why Evidence Matters

A policy recommendation without evidence is an opinion. Decision-makers receive many opinions. What they need — and what your job requires you to deliver — is analysis grounded in facts.

Evidence serves three purposes in a policy recommendation:

1. **It establishes the problem.** Without data, you cannot prove a problem exists or measure how serious it is. "Fisheries in Tawi-Tawi are declining" is a claim. "Total fish catch in Tawi-Tawi dropped from 42,000 metric tons in 2020 to 28,000 metric tons in 2024, a 33% decline, according to PSA-BARMM" is evidence.

2. **It supports your proposed solution.** When you recommend a specific intervention — a scholarship program, a regulatory reform, a new implementing structure — you need evidence that the intervention works. That evidence may come from pilot programs in the Bangsamoro, comparable programs in other regions, or academic research on the policy problem.

3. **It defends your recommendation against scrutiny.** Every recommendation will be questioned. A reviewer will ask: How do you know this will work? What is the basis for your cost estimate? Why did you reject the alternative? Evidence answers those questions. Without it, your recommendation will not survive the review process.

## 3.2 Research Methods for Policy Analysis

Policy research draws on multiple methods. The right method depends on your question, your time, and the data available. Most policy recommendations use a combination of methods.

### 3.2.1 Desktop Research and Document Review

Start here. Before you interview anyone or conduct a survey, review what already exists:

- **Legal documents.** The BOL, relevant BAAs, national laws, executive orders, and implementing rules that govern your subject.
- **Policy documents.** The BDP, sectoral development plans, agency strategic plans, and prior policy recommendations on the same or related topics.
- **Budget documents.** Annual budgets, budget execution reports, and financial statements from the relevant agencies.
- **Reports and evaluations.** Program evaluation reports, audit findings, committee reports, and published research.

Desktop research is fast, cheap, and essential. It prevents you from reinventing analysis that someone else has already done. It also reveals gaps — questions that existing documents cannot answer — which you then address through primary research.

**Practical tip:** Create a document inventory at the start of your analysis. List every relevant document, where you found it, what it covers, and what gaps remain. This inventory becomes part of your evidence trail (Section 3.6).

### 3.2.2 Key Informant Interviews

Key informant interviews (KIIs) collect qualitative data from people who know the subject. Your informants may include:

- Government officials responsible for implementing relevant programs
- Community leaders in affected areas
- Subject-matter experts in academia, civil society, or the private sector
- Beneficiaries of existing programs

**Design your interviews with structure.** Prepare an interview guide with open-ended questions. Focus on what the informant knows from direct experience. Avoid leading questions — do not ask "Don't you think the program is failing?" Ask "How would you describe the program's outcomes so far?"

**Document your interviews systematically.** Record the date, the informant's name and position (with their consent), the questions asked, and the key responses. This documentation becomes part of your evidence trail.

### 3.2.3 Focus Group Discussions

Focus group discussions (FGDs) are useful when you need to understand community perspectives — particularly from groups whose voices are underrepresented in official data. FGDs work well for:

- Understanding how communities experience a problem
- Testing whether proposed solutions are culturally appropriate and practically feasible
- Identifying unintended consequences that desk research might miss

**Composition matters.** A focus group that mixes government officials with community members will produce different results than separate groups. A focus group with only men will miss the perspectives of women. Design your groups deliberately.

**Size and number.** Plan for 8-12 participants per group. For a province-wide policy, conduct at least one FGD in each affected province. For a regional policy, conduct FGDs across at least three provinces to capture geographic variation.

### 3.2.4 Survey Design and Administration

Surveys provide quantitative data that complements your qualitative research. Use surveys when you need to:

- Measure the prevalence of a problem across a population
- Quantify demand for a proposed service or program
- Assess satisfaction with an existing program
- Collect demographic data on a target population

**Keep surveys short.** A survey with more than 30 questions will produce low response rates and unreliable answers. Focus on the questions that directly support your analysis.

**Sampling matters.** If you are surveying a population of 10,000 beneficiaries, you do not need to survey all 10,000. A properly designed sample of 400-500 respondents can produce statistically valid results. If you lack the time or resources for a statistically valid sample, say so in your analysis. Acknowledge the limitation.

**Administration.** In BARMM, face-to-face surveys often yield better results than online surveys, particularly in rural areas. Budget for enumerators, translation (especially for communities where Filipino or English is not the primary language), and data encoding.

### 3.2.5 Comparative Analysis

Comparative analysis looks at how other jurisdictions have addressed the same problem. This is useful because:

- It shows what works and what does not, based on actual implementation experience
- It provides cost benchmarks for budgeting your recommendation
- It identifies design features you may want to adopt or avoid

**Compare with care.** The Bangsamoro is unique — the only autonomous region in the Philippines, the only parliamentary system, the only jurisdiction with Shari'ah courts. Direct comparisons with other Philippine regions may miss important contextual differences. Compare the intervention design and outcomes, not the institutional context.

**Good comparators for BARMM policy work include:**

- Other Philippine autonomous or special regions (e.g., Cordillera Administrative Region for governance models)
- Southeast Asian Muslim-majority regions (e.g., Aceh in Indonesia, southern Thailand, Mindanao comparative studies)
- National government programs implemented in the Bangsamoro (e.g., 4Ps, DOST scholarship programs)
- Programs by international development partners operating in the Bangsamoro

## 3.3 Data Sources for BARMM Policy Work

### 3.3.1 PSA-BARMM and Official Statistics

The **Philippine Statistics Authority - Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (PSA-BARMM)** is the primary source of official statistics for the region. PSA-BARMM publishes:

- Population data from the national census
- Poverty incidence and food threshold statistics
- Employment and labor force data
- Agricultural production statistics
- Price indices and inflation data
- Birth, death, and migration statistics

**Access:** PSA regional data is available through the PSA website (psa.gov.ph) and through PSA-BARMM's Cotabato City office. Some datasets require a formal data request.

**Limitation:** PSA data is often published with a lag of 1-2 years. The most recent poverty statistics, for example, may be two years old. Acknowledge this in your analysis and supplement with more recent data from other sources when available.

### 3.3.2 Ministry and Agency Reports

Each Bangsamoro ministry and agency produces periodic reports:

- **Annual accomplishment reports** — summarize programs implemented, beneficiaries served, and budgets spent
- **Budget execution reports** — show how much of the approved budget was actually obligated and disbursed
- **Project monitoring reports** — track the progress of specific programs, projects, and activities

These reports are not always publicly available. You may need to request them through official channels. When citing ministry data, identify the specific report, its date, and the reporting period it covers.

### 3.3.3 Budget Execution Data

Budget data tells you what the government intended to spend (the General Appropriations Act), what it actually obligated, and what it disbursed. The gap between appropriation and disbursement — the **absorption rate** — is a powerful indicator of implementation capacity.

**Key sources:**

- The annual General Appropriations Act (BAA) for the BARMM budget
- The Ministry of Finance, Budget, and Management (MFBM) budget reports
- Commission on Audit (COA) annual audit reports for BARMM agencies

**How to use budget data in policy analysis:**

- A low absorption rate signals implementation problems — your recommendation should address them.
- A trend of declining allocations to a sector signals de-prioritization — your recommendation should argue for why the sector deserves more.
- A comparison between allocation and outcomes reveals efficiency — how much output does each peso produce?

### 3.3.4 Legislative Records and Committee Reports

The Bangsamoro Parliament's legislative records contain valuable policy information:

- **Committee reports** summarize the findings from committee hearings and investigations, including testimony from stakeholders.
- **Explanatory notes** of filed bills describe the policy problem the bill aims to address and the evidence base the author used.
- **Interpellation transcripts** record the questions and answers during plenary deliberations — often revealing implementation challenges and policy gaps.

Access legislative records through the Parliament's Bills and Index Division or the Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS).

### 3.3.5 International and Academic Sources

For comparative evidence and methodological guidance, draw on:

- **World Bank reports** on the Bangsamoro and Mindanao (the Bank has conducted extensive research on conflict, poverty, and governance in the region)
- **JICA, USAID, and UNDP program evaluations** — these often contain data not available elsewhere
- **Academic publications** — the University of the Philippines, Mindanao State University, and international researchers have published on many BARMM policy issues
- **SDG monitoring data** — the UN and Philippine statistical agencies track progress against SDG targets at the regional level

## 3.4 Conducting a Needs Assessment

A needs assessment is a structured analysis of the gap between current conditions and desired conditions. It answers the question: **what does the affected population need, and how far are they from having it?**

### 3.4.1 Defining the Problem

A needs assessment starts with a clear problem statement (see Chapter 1, Section 1.2.1). The problem should be stated in terms of the affected population — not in terms of the government's institutional interests.

**Weak problem statement:** "The Ministry of Agriculture does not have enough extension workers."

**Strong problem statement:** "Sixty percent of rice farmers in Maguindanao del Sur have no access to agricultural extension services. Average rice yield in the province is 2.1 metric tons per hectare — 40% below the national average of 3.5 metric tons."

The first statement describes a government capacity problem. The second describes a community need, supported by data, with a measurable gap.

### 3.4.2 Identifying the Affected Population

Define who is affected by the problem:

- **Geographic scope.** Which provinces, municipalities, or barangays?
- **Demographic profile.** How many people? What are their ages, gender distribution, and economic status?
- **Vulnerability factors.** Are certain groups disproportionately affected — women, youth, persons with disabilities, indigenous peoples, displaced populations?

Be specific. "Bangsamoro communities" is not a defined population. "Internally displaced persons in three relocation sites in Maguindanao del Norte, estimated at 12,400 individuals as of the 2024 DSWD displacement report" is a defined population.

### 3.4.3 Establishing Baselines and Benchmarks

Your needs assessment must establish two reference points:

1. **Baseline.** The current condition. Where are things right now? Use the most recent data available and cite your source.
2. **Benchmark.** The target condition. Where should things be? Your benchmark may come from the BDP targets, national averages, international standards (e.g., SDG targets), or evidence-based best practices.

The gap between baseline and benchmark is the need. Your policy recommendation proposes how to close that gap — partially or fully — within a defined timeframe.

## 3.5 Avoiding Common Analytical Pitfalls

Policy analysis in the Bangsamoro is subject to several recurring traps. Watch for them:

**Cherry-picking data.** Selecting only the evidence that supports your preferred solution while ignoring evidence that contradicts it. Present all relevant evidence — including inconvenient findings — and explain how your recommendation accounts for them.

**Confusing correlation with causation.** "Provinces with more health centers have lower maternal mortality" does not prove that building health centers reduces maternal mortality. Other factors — roads, trained personnel, cultural practices — may explain the difference. Be careful about causal claims.

**Relying on outdated data.** BARMM conditions change rapidly. Data from 2018 may not reflect conditions in 2026. Always note the date of your data and assess whether conditions have changed materially since it was collected.

**Assuming the problem is the absence of a program.** "There is no scholarship program for Bangsamoro students" is not a problem statement — it is a solution statement disguised as a problem. The problem is the low enrollment rate, the high dropout rate, or the unaffordability of tuition. Start with the problem, not the solution.

**Ignoring implementation capacity.** The best-designed program will fail if the implementing agency lacks the staff, budget, or systems to deliver it. Include an honest assessment of implementation capacity in your analysis. If the capacity is weak, your recommendation should address it — through training, hiring, technical assistance, or phased implementation.

## 3.6 Documenting Your Evidence Trail

Every piece of evidence in your recommendation should be traceable. When a reviewer asks "Where did this number come from?" you should be able to answer immediately.

**Maintain an evidence registry** for each policy recommendation. This is a simple table:

| No. | Data Point | Source | Date | Location / Access |
|-----|-----------|--------|------|-------------------|
| 1 | Rice yield per hectare, Maguindanao del Sur | PSA-BARMM, Agricultural Production Statistics 2024 | March 2025 | psa.gov.ph |
| 2 | Number of extension workers, Ministry of Agriculture | MOA Annual Report FY 2025 | January 2026 | Requested from MOA Planning Division |
| 3 | Maternal mortality rate, Bangsamoro | DOH-BARMM Health Statistics Yearbook 2024 | June 2025 | DOH-BARMM website |

This registry serves two purposes. First, it lets you verify and update your evidence if a reviewer challenges it. Second, it becomes an annex to your final recommendation document — showing the decision-maker exactly what evidence you relied on and where to find it.

Treat your evidence registry as a living document. Update it every time you add a new data point to your analysis. When you present your recommendation, attach the registry as an annex.
