# Evaluation: "Working with the Bangsamoro Government" Guidebook Plan

**Evaluator**: Claude (auditing session outputs, skills, competency frameworks, and guidebooks produced)
**Date**: 2026-03-26

---

## Overall Assessment: 75% Complete — Strong Foundation, 5 Critical Gaps

The plan has excellent bones. The 12-chapter structure covers the job seeker journey from "why join" through "how to grow." The competencies chapter (Ch 9) grounded in the CSC Competency Dictionary is the right anchor.

But it has **5 critical gaps** when measured against everything we've built in this session. The gaps aren't about missing topics — they're about insufficient depth in areas where we have rich source material that should be feeding into this guidebook.

---

## Gap Analysis: What's in Our Skills/Guidebooks That's Missing from the Plan

### GAP 1: CSW Is Underweight — It Should Be a Chapter, Not a Bullet Point

**The problem**: The plan mentions CSW in one bullet point in Section 9.5 ("Complete Staff Work (CSW) — The ADDRESS IT methodology"). But CSW is not just a competency. It is **THE way work gets done** in BARMM government.

**What we have**: A complete CSW Guidebook (33,394 words, 14 chapters), the ADDRESS IT framework, ACSCCRaPATFEE (12 competencies), 6 CSW template types, training design, assessment tool.

**Why it matters for job seekers**: A fresh graduate who understands CSW before Day 1 has a **massive advantage**. Every assignment they receive will need to be processed through ADDRESS IT. Every output they produce will be evaluated as CSW. A graduate who shows up knowing "Analyze, Define, Research, Explore, Submit, Sustain, Iterate" will impress their supervisor immediately.

**Fix**: Either:
- (a) Elevate Section 9.5 into a full sub-chapter with ADDRESS IT overview (A-DD-R-E-SS-IT in 2 pages), or
- (b) Add a new chapter: **"Chapter 9: How Work Gets Done — Complete Staff Work"** (move competencies to Ch 10). This chapter explains the CSW process from a fresh graduate's perspective: "Your supervisor gives you an assignment. Here's what happens next."

**Recommendation**: Option (b). CSW is too important to be a sub-section.

### GAP 2: No "Understanding Your Supervisor" Chapter (The Staff Side of Supervision)

**The problem**: We built a complete Supervision Guidebook (33,579 words) teaching supervisors how to manage. But this guidebook doesn't have the **staff-side mirror** — how to be managed effectively.

**What we have**: PPR grid (Performance-Potential-Readiness), SBI feedback model, 4-step check-in protocol, 6-step performance evaluation, coaching process. All from the supervisor's perspective.

**Why it matters for job seekers**: Fresh graduates fail not because they lack skills, but because they don't understand the supervision relationship. They don't know:
- What their supervisor expects in a check-in (hint: come prepared with updates, questions, and blockers)
- How to receive SBI feedback without getting defensive
- What the PPR assessment means for their career trajectory
- How performance evaluation actually works (results + behaviors, not just "being nice")
- When and how to ask for coaching vs. when to figure it out themselves

**Fix**: Add a chapter or major section: **"Working with Your Supervisor"** — the receiving end of everything in the Supervision Guidebook. Include:
- What your supervisor is evaluating (PPR — from YOUR perspective)
- How to prepare for check-ins (4-step protocol — YOUR role)
- How to receive feedback (SBI — what to do when you hear it)
- How to get coached (5-element process — YOUR responsibility)
- What your performance evaluation will look like
- The difference between a supervisor who coaches and one who just assigns

### GAP 3: No Parliament vs. MOA Career Track Distinction

**The problem**: The plan treats "joining BARMM government" as one thing. But joining the **Parliament Secretariat** is fundamentally different from joining **MAFAR** or **MOH**.

**What we have**: Parliament Staff Competencies (25 competencies split into Core, Leadership, and Functional by role — PAO, LSO, PIMAO). The Bill Drafting Guidebook, Speech Writing Guidebook, and Legislative Briefer skill are Parliament-specific. The MOP Guidebook, Policy Recommendations Guidebook, and Supervision system are MOA-focused.

**Why it matters for job seekers**: A graduate interested in legislative work needs different preparation than one interested in health service delivery or agricultural extension. The plan doesn't help the reader figure out **which track** suits them.

**Fix**: Add a chapter or major section: **"Choosing Your Path: Parliament vs. Ministry vs. Office"** covering:

| Track | What you'll do | Key competencies | Career ceiling |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Parliament** (legislative staff) | Draft bills, write briefers, prepare speeches, support committee work | Investigation, policy analysis, legal competence, CSW | SLSO I → Legislative Chief → Director |
| **Ministry** (technical staff) | Implement programs, deliver services, manage budgets, write policy recommendations | Technical expertise, project management, budget analysis, CSW | Staff → Division Chief → Director → Undersecretary |
| **Office/Agency** (specialized staff) | Focused mandate (youth, women, human rights, planning) | Stakeholder engagement, program design, M&E, CSW | Staff → Section Head → Executive Director |
| **LGU** (local governance) | Frontline service delivery, community engagement | Community relations, compliance, operations | Staff → Department Head → Administrator |

### GAP 4: No Practical Government Communication Chapter

**The problem**: The plan has no chapter on how to actually communicate in government. Writing a memo, formatting a transmittal, composing an email to a director, taking meeting minutes — these are skills every government worker needs on Day 1.

**What we have**: Transmittal memorandum templates (in /csw assets), MOPs with standardized forms, the BARMM guidebook voice standard. The Bill Drafting Guidebook teaches legislative writing. But we don't have a "government writing basics" resource for beginners.

**Why it matters for job seekers**: The #1 complaint about new government workers: "They don't know how to write a proper memo." Government communication has conventions — "Respectfully transmitted herewith," addressee hierarchy, reference numbers, subject lines, endorsement blocks. These aren't taught in college.

**Fix**: Add a chapter: **"Government Communication: Writing That Gets Read"** covering:
- The memo format (To, From, Date, Subject, body, signature block)
- The transmittal memorandum (when you submit work to your supervisor)
- Email etiquette in government (formal but efficient, CC rules, response expectations)
- Meeting minutes format
- Endorsement and routing ("1st Endorsement," "2nd Endorsement")
- The difference between "For information," "For appropriate action," and "For approval"
- Government acronyms survival guide

---

### GAP 5: Ethics and Legal Obligations Are Too Light

**The problem**: The plan mentions "accountability and integrity" in Chapter 11 with a bullet point about COA and CSC anti-corruption. But government workers have **specific legal obligations** that can result in criminal charges if violated.

**What we have**: BAA 17 (Civil Service Code), references to RA 6713 (Code of Conduct for Public Officials), SALN requirements, anti-graft provisions.

**Why it matters for job seekers**: A fresh graduate who accepts a gift, fails to file a SALN, or doesn't declare a conflict of interest can face administrative and criminal charges. These aren't theoretical risks. The plan should make this real.

**Fix**: Expand Chapter 11 or add a section: **"Your Legal Obligations as a Public Servant"** covering:
- **RA 6713** — Code of Conduct and Ethical Standards for Public Officials (the 8 norms)
- **SALN** (Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Net Worth) — you MUST file this annually. How to fill it out.
- **Anti-Graft and Corrupt Practices (RA 3019)** — what counts as graft, what gifts you can't accept
- **Conflict of interest rules** — when to disclose, when to recuse
- **Government procurement rules** — why you can't just buy things
- **COA audit** — what it is, why it matters, how to stay clean
- **Whistleblower protections** — what to do if you witness corruption

## Complexity Tier Upgrade

The original plan said **Standard (10 chapters, 150-200 pages)**. With 15 chapters, this is now **Comprehensive (250-300 pages)**.

This is appropriate. This guidebook is the ENTRY POINT to the entire BARMM governance system. It needs to be thorough enough that a fresh graduate in Zamboanga or Manila can read it and feel prepared to show up on Day 1 in Cotabato City.
