# Chapter 5 — Resolutions

A bill creates law. A resolution does not. That single difference changes almost everything about how you draft the document — its structure, its language, the process it follows, and the legal weight it carries after adoption. This chapter covers what you need to know to draft resolutions for the Bangsamoro Parliament, focusing on what is different from the bill-drafting process covered in Chapters 2 through 4.

---

## 5.1 How Resolutions Differ from Bills

Resolution No. 268, Section 4 of Rule XVII, defines a resolution in one sentence: *"A resolution is a document expressing the sentiment or opinion of the Parliament. It does not create or modify a law."* That definition tells you the three things that set resolutions apart from bills.

**First, resolutions express — they do not command.** A bill creates binding legal obligations. When the Parliament enacts a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act, everyone within its coverage must comply. A resolution, by contrast, states what the Parliament thinks, feels, requests, or recommends. It can urge a ministry to act. It can congratulate a citizen. It can condemn an atrocity. But it cannot force anyone to do anything.

**Second, resolutions do not go through the full legislative process.** Under Section 6 of Rule XVII of Resolution No. 268, simple and concurrent resolutions "shall not be subject to the ordinary procedure as prescribed in the Stages of Legislation." The Parliament or its committees can act on them promptly in a plenary session. This is the single most important procedural difference. While a bill must pass through First Reading, Second Reading, Committee Stage, Consideration and Deliberation Stage, and Third Reading before the Chief Minister signs it, most resolutions skip most of those stages.

**Third, resolutions have a different structure.** Bills have an enacting clause, numbered sections, separability clauses, and effectivity clauses. Resolutions have a WHEREAS preamble followed by an operative clause beginning with "RESOLVED." There is no enacting clause, no section numbering, and no effectivity date. The structure reflects the document's nature — it is a statement, not a statute.

Here is a side-by-side comparison:

| Feature | Bill | Resolution |
|---|---|---|
| **Legal effect** | Creates, amends, or repeals law | Expresses opinion, sentiment, or request |
| **Binding force** | Legally binding on all covered persons | Not legally binding (with narrow exceptions — see Section 5.2.4) |
| **Opening clause** | Enacting clause: "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament:" (or BTA variant) | WHEREAS preamble followed by operative "RESOLVED" clause |
| **Internal structure** | Numbered sections, subsections, paragraphs | WHEREAS recitals followed by one or more RESOLVED clauses |
| **Standard provisions** | Short title, definitions, separability clause, repealing clause, effectivity clause | None of these |
| **Legislative process** | Full stages of legislation (First Reading through Third Reading, Chief Minister approval) | Simplified — typically acted upon promptly in plenary (simple and concurrent resolutions) |
| **Approval** | Requires Chief Minister's signature | Attested by the Speaker and certified by the Secretary-General |
| **Publication** | Must be published in a newspaper of general circulation | Not required to be published |

One common mistake is to treat a resolution as a "lesser bill." It is not a bill at all. It is a different instrument for a different purpose. If the goal is to create a legal obligation — to require someone to do something, to set a penalty, to establish a program — you need a bill, not a resolution. If the goal is to state the Parliament's position, to commend, to request, to condemn, or to organize the Parliament's own internal affairs — that is what resolutions are for.

### When a Resolution Is the Wrong Tool

Sometimes a Member of Parliament wants to address a problem through a resolution when a bill would be the appropriate instrument. Watch for these warning signs:

- The resolution creates a new government body, office, or program.
- The resolution imposes penalties or sanctions.
- The resolution appropriates funds.
- The resolution requires private citizens or businesses to take specific actions.
- The resolution amends or repeals an existing BAA.

If any of these are true, advise the MP that a bill is needed. A resolution that tries to do these things will either be unenforceable or will be challenged for exceeding the scope of a resolution.

The reverse also happens. A bill that does nothing more than express the Parliament's sentiment — urging a national agency to act, congratulating someone, commemorating an event — should be a resolution. Using a bill for such purposes wastes the Parliament's legislative time and creates an empty law that nobody enforces because there is nothing to enforce.

## 5.2 Types of Resolutions

Resolution No. 268, Section 5 of Rule XVII, recognizes three types of resolutions. In practice, a fourth category exists: resolutions that, because of their subject matter, carry the force of law.

### 5.2.1 Proposed Resolutions

A proposed resolution (often called a "simple resolution" under Section 5(a) of Rule XVII) is the most common type. It originates from one or more Members of Parliament and expresses the Parliament's sentiment on a matter. The vast majority of resolutions adopted by the BTA Parliament are of this type.

Simple resolutions are used for:

- **Commendation and congratulations.** Recognizing Bangsamoro achievers — topnotchers in board examinations, athletes, scholars, public servants. Example: Resolution No. 469 (congratulating Bangsamoro topnotchers in the March 2024 Licensure Examination for Professional Teachers).

- **Condolences and sympathy.** Honoring deceased individuals who served the Bangsamoro cause. Example: Proposed Resolution No. 620 (recognizing the contributions of the late Commissioner Nasser A. Marohomsalic and expressing condolences to his family).

- **Condemnation and calls for action.** Denouncing acts of violence and urging investigation. Example: Resolution No. 700 (condemning the killing of Atty. Junisa Kimamao and calling for investigation).

- **Requests to government agencies.** Urging national or Bangsamoro agencies to take action on matters outside the Parliament's direct legislative authority. Example: Resolution No. 425 (requesting DPWH-National to prioritize repair of highways and bridges in the BARMM).

- **Internal procedural matters.** Actions affecting the Parliament's own procedures, such as adopting rules or creating special committees.

- **Urging policy action.** Requesting the Bangsamoro Government to take administrative action. Example: Proposed Resolution No. 710 (urging the Bangsamoro Government to reconstitute the Bangsamoro Council of Leaders).

When you see "Proposed Resolution No." in a document header, it refers to the proposed resolution as filed — before adoption. Once the Parliament adopts it, it becomes "Resolution No." with a different number assigned by the Secretary-General.

**Numbering convention.** The proposed resolution number (e.g., "P. P. Res. No. 588") and the adopted resolution number (e.g., "Resolution No. 469") are different. The proposed resolution number tracks the order of filing. The adopted resolution number tracks the order of adoption. Resolution No. 469 originated from P. P. Res. No. 588 — different numbers, same document. When citing an adopted resolution in other legislation, use the adopted resolution number.

**How common are simple resolutions?** The vast majority of resolutions in the BTA Parliament are simple resolutions. A review of the Parliament's adopted resolutions shows that commendation, condolence, and request resolutions dominate the output. These are the resolutions you will draft most often. Master this type first.

### 5.2.2 Joint Resolutions

Resolution No. 268 defines a "Joint Committee Resolution" under Section 5(c) of Rule XVII as "a resolution of two (2) or more committees expressing the will or action of the Members on certain matters." It requires a majority vote of all the committees concerned.

The terminology is specific to the Bangsamoro Parliament. In the Philippine Congress, a "joint resolution" typically refers to a measure adopted by both chambers (Senate and House). In the BTA Parliament — which is unicameral — "joint" refers to multiple committees acting together on a shared concern.

Joint committee resolutions are used when a matter falls within the jurisdiction of more than one committee. For instance, a resolution on food safety standards in the BARMM might involve both the Committee on Health and the Committee on Trade and Industry. Rather than having each committee issue separate resolutions, a joint committee resolution consolidates their position.

**Drafting tip:** When drafting a joint committee resolution, identify all committees with jurisdiction over the subject matter in the opening WHEREAS clauses. The operative clause should state that it is the joint resolution of the named committees.

### 5.2.3 Concurrent Resolutions

A concurrent resolution, under Section 5(b) of Rule XVII, is "a resolution issued concurrently by both the Bangsamoro Cabinet and the Parliament expressing their sentiments on certain matters."

This type is distinctive to the Bangsamoro system. Because the BTA Parliament operates under a parliamentary system — where the Cabinet sits within the Parliament — a concurrent resolution reflects the unified position of both the legislative body and the executive. It signals a stronger institutional commitment than a simple resolution because both branches are speaking with one voice.

Concurrent resolutions may be appropriate when:

- The Parliament and Cabinet want to jointly express a position to the national government.
- Both branches agree on a matter requiring coordinated executive and legislative action.
- The Parliament and Cabinet want to jointly commend or recognize an achievement.

Like simple resolutions, concurrent resolutions are not subject to the ordinary stages of legislation. Section 6 of Rule XVII places them alongside simple resolutions: both "shall not be subject to the ordinary procedure as prescribed in the Stages of Legislation herein provided."

### 5.2.4 Resolutions with the Force of Law

There is one category of resolution that carries binding legal effect: resolutions that govern the Parliament's own internal procedures. Resolution No. 268 is the best example. It establishes the Rules, Procedures, and Practices of the BTA Parliament — and those rules are binding on every Member of Parliament and every officer of the Parliament.

This authority comes from the Bangsamoro Organic Law itself. Section 25, Article VII of Republic Act No. 11054 provides that "the Parliament shall adopt its own rules for the conduct of its business." When the Parliament exercises this power through a resolution, the result is not merely an expression of sentiment — it is a binding set of rules.

Resolution No. 268 regulates everything from the election of the Speaker to the stages of legislation to the conduct of debates. Any violation of its provisions can be addressed through the mechanisms it establishes, including points of order and disciplinary proceedings.

Other resolutions that may carry binding internal effect include:

- Resolutions creating special or ad hoc committees.
- Resolutions adopting the calendar of sessions.
- Resolutions adopting guidelines for teleconferencing under Rule VI, Section 4.

The key distinction: these resolutions bind the Parliament and its members internally. They do not bind persons outside the Parliament. Only a bill enacted into law can do that.

## 5.3 The Resolution Drafting Process

### 5.3.1 How the Process Differs from Bills

Chapter 2 described a seven-stage process for drafting a bill, from legislative directive to filing. The resolution drafting process is shorter and less formal — but it still requires care.

Here is how the two processes compare:

| Stage | Bill | Resolution |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Direction from MP** | Yes — detailed legislative template | Yes — but typically a brief oral or written instruction |
| **2. Research** | Extensive — policy research, legal analysis, impact assessment, comparative matrix | Limited — verify facts, confirm legal authority, check for duplicates |
| **3. First draft** | Full bill with all structural components | WHEREAS preamble + operative clause |
| **4. Consultation** | With agencies, experts, constituencies | Usually not required — unless the resolution touches on policy |
| **5. Review** | Multiple rounds with policy, legal, and style review | Quick review for accuracy and form |
| **6. Presentation to MP** | Formal presentation with options | Brief review and approval |
| **7. Co-authorship and filing** | Secure co-authors, file with Bills and Index Division | Secure co-authors, file with Secretary-General |

The lighter process does not mean you can be careless. A resolution that misstates facts, gets a name wrong, misquotes a law, or uses sloppy language reflects poorly on the MP and the Parliament. The difference is in scope and formality, not in the standard of accuracy.

**What to verify before drafting a resolution:**

1. **The facts.** If the resolution congratulates someone for passing a board exam, verify the person's full name, the exam, the ranking, and the date. If it condemns an incident, verify the date, location, and known facts. Do not rely on social media posts or unverified news reports.

2. **The legal basis.** If the resolution cites the BOL, the Constitution, or a national law, verify the exact article, section, and wording. Misquoting a legal provision in a resolution is embarrassing and undermines credibility.

3. **Prior resolutions.** Check whether the Parliament has already adopted a resolution on the same subject. You do not want to file a resolution congratulating someone who was already congratulated in a previous session.

4. **The right instrument.** Confirm that a resolution — not a bill — is the correct vehicle. If the MP's intent is to create a legal obligation, redirect to a bill.

### 5.3.2 When the Full Legislative Process Applies

Not every resolution follows the simplified track. Under Resolution No. 268, the simplified process applies only to simple resolutions and concurrent resolutions. Other resolutions — particularly those that adopt internal rules or create committees — may go through a more deliberative process, including committee referral and plenary debate.

Resolution No. 268 itself went through a full deliberative process. It was not simply adopted on the spot. It involved extensive drafting, committee review, and plenary deliberation because it established the binding rules governing the Parliament's operations.

When a resolution will go through the full process, you should draft it with the same rigor as a bill. The WHEREAS preamble should be thoroughly researched. The operative clauses should be precise. The language should be reviewed for ambiguity. Expect interpellation — Members will ask questions about the resolution just as they would about a bill.

### 5.3.3 Simplified Process for Simple and Concurrent Resolutions

For simple and concurrent resolutions, the process under Section 6 of Rule XVII is streamlined: "The Parliament or the Committees concerned in a plenary session shall promptly act on these matters."

In practice, this means:

1. The resolution is filed with the Secretary-General, who assigns it a number.
2. It is reported in plenary during the "Resolutions, Messages, Memorials and Communications" portion of the Order of Business (Rule VIII, Section 1(g)(2)).
3. The Speaker may refer it to a committee or the Parliament may act on it directly.
4. If acted upon in plenary, the resolution is adopted by majority vote.

The simplified process can be remarkably fast. A resolution condemning an act of violence or congratulating a Bangsamoro achiever may be filed, reported, and adopted in a single session. But speed should not come at the cost of accuracy. Even on a compressed timeline, verify every fact and every citation.

## 5.4 Anatomy of a Resolution

### 5.4.1 The WHEREAS Preamble

The WHEREAS preamble is the heart of a resolution. Unlike a bill — where the explanatory note is a separate document and the body contains operative sections — a resolution's preamble does most of the work. It presents the facts, establishes the context, cites the legal basis, and builds the case for the operative clause that follows.

Each WHEREAS clause is called a "recital." Together, the recitals tell a story: here is the situation, here is why it matters, and here is what the Parliament should do about it.

**Format.** Each recital begins with **WHEREAS** in bold, followed by a comma and the substance of the recital. The recital ends with a semicolon. The final recital before the operative clause ends with a colon, introducing the "NOW, THEREFORE" transition. Here is the standard pattern:

```
WHEREAS, [first recital];

WHEREAS, [second recital];

WHEREAS, [final recital]:

NOW, THEREFORE, be it

RESOLVED, [operative clause].
```

Note the punctuation carefully:
- Semicolons after each WHEREAS recital (except the last).
- A colon after the final WHEREAS recital.
- "NOW, THEREFORE" is followed by a comma and "be it" on the same line or the next.
- "RESOLVED" is followed by a comma.

**Content of recitals.** Each recital should contain one point. Do not pack multiple ideas into a single WHEREAS clause. If you have two separate facts, use two separate recitals.

**Legal citations in recitals.** When a recital cites a law, provide the exact text. Use block quotes for lengthy citations. Identify the law by its full name and number on first reference:

> **WHEREAS**, Section 4, Article II of the 1987 Constitution of the Philippines states in part that *"The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people"*;

After the first reference, you may use a shortened form:

> **WHEREAS**, Section 6, Article IX of the Bangsamoro Organic Law explicitly provides that *"The Bangsamoro Government shall fully respect human rights"*;

### 5.4.2 Deductive Ordering of Recitals

The recitals should flow in a logical order — from the general to the specific, from the background to the immediate situation, from the facts to the conclusion. Think of it as a deductive argument:

1. **Legal and constitutional foundation.** Start with the broadest legal basis. If the resolution relates to human rights, begin with the Constitutional provision. If it relates to governance, begin with the BOL. This establishes the Parliament's authority and the legal framework.

2. **Background and context.** After the legal foundation, provide the factual context. What is the situation? What happened? What is the relevant history?

3. **Specific facts.** Narrow the focus to the specific event, person, or issue that prompted the resolution. Provide names, dates, places, and verifiable details.

4. **The Parliament's interest.** Explain why this matter is before the Parliament. What is the connection to the Bangsamoro people, the BARMM, or the Parliament's mandate?

5. **Conclusion and transition.** The final recital should lead naturally into the operative clause. It often expresses the Parliament's position or sentiment, setting up the "NOW, THEREFORE" transition.

Here is an example of good deductive ordering, drawn from the structure of Resolution No. 700:

| Order | Recital Content | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Constitutional duty of government to protect the people | Legal foundation |
| 2 | Bill of Rights — due process and equal protection | Legal foundation |
| 3 | BOL provisions on human rights | BOL authority |
| 4 | Facts of the incident — date, location, victims | Specific facts |
| 5 | Police report details | Supporting facts |
| 6 | Victim's background and service | Context |
| 7 | Quranic verse on sanctity of life | Cultural/religious dimension |
| 8 | Transition to operative clause | Conclusion |

Bad ordering is when recitals jump around — a fact, then a law, then another fact, then another law, then back to a different fact. This makes the resolution hard to follow. Organize your recitals so that a reader can follow the argument from beginning to end without confusion.

### 5.4.3 The Operative Clause

The operative clause is where the resolution states what the Parliament resolves to do. It begins after the "NOW, THEREFORE" transition and starts with "RESOLVED."

**The basic operative clause:**

```
NOW, THEREFORE, be it

RESOLVED, as it is hereby resolved by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament,
to [action or expression of sentiment].
```

The formula "as it is hereby resolved by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament" is the standard phrasing used across BTA resolutions. Use it. Do not improvise.

**Multiple operative clauses.** When the resolution has more than one action or expression, use additional RESOLVED clauses:

```
RESOLVED, as it is hereby resolved by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament,
to [first action];

RESOLVED, FURTHER, to [second action];

RESOLVED, FURTHERMORE, to [third action];

RESOLVED, FINALLY, to furnish a copy of this Resolution to [recipients] for their
information and appropriate action.
```

The progression is:
- RESOLVED — first operative clause
- RESOLVED, FURTHER — second operative clause
- RESOLVED, FURTHERMORE — third operative clause
- RESOLVED, FINALLY — last operative clause (typically the furnishing clause)

If there are only two operative clauses, use RESOLVED and RESOLVED, FURTHER. If there are three, add RESOLVED, FURTHERMORE. The "FINALLY" marker signals the last clause, which is almost always the furnishing clause — directing that copies be sent to relevant persons or agencies.

**The furnishing clause.** Nearly every resolution ends with a clause directing that copies be furnished to specific recipients. This is not mere formality — it ensures the resolution reaches the people who need to see it. Identify the right recipients:

- For a commendation: the person being commended.
- For a request to a government agency: the agency head, with copies to relevant offices.
- For a condemnation: the investigative agencies, the victims or their families, and other relevant authorities.

**The adoption line.** After the operative clauses, the word "ADOPTED" appears, followed by the date of adoption. BTA resolutions commonly include both the Islamic calendar date and the Gregorian date: *"ADOPTED, Sha'ban 19, 1446/February 18, 2025."*

### 5.4.4 Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Too many WHEREAS clauses.** A resolution commending a board exam topnotcher does not need fifteen recitals. State the facts, express the sentiment, and resolve. Three to five WHEREAS clauses are sufficient for a straightforward commendation. Save lengthy preambles for resolutions that require a detailed factual or legal foundation.

**Mistake 2: Recitals that argue instead of state.** WHEREAS clauses should present facts and cite law. They should not argue, advocate, or editorialize. Compare:

| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| WHEREAS, the deplorable and unconscionable failure of the DPWH to maintain roads in the BARMM has caused untold suffering... | WHEREAS, national highways and bridges in the BARMM require immediate repair, reconstruction, and maintenance... |

The first version argues. The second states a fact. Let the facts speak — the operative clause is where the Parliament expresses its position.

**Mistake 3: Operative clauses that try to legislate.** A resolution cannot create a program, appropriate funds, or impose penalties. If the operative clause says "the Ministry of Health shall establish a vaccination center in every municipality," that is not a resolution — that is a bill trying to disguise itself. A resolution can *urge* or *request* — it cannot *require*.

**Mistake 4: Getting names and titles wrong.** This is the most damaging error in a commendation or condolence resolution. Double-check every name, every title, every date, and every achievement. A resolution that misspells the name of the person being honored is worse than no resolution at all.

**Mistake 5: Inconsistent formatting.** The most common formatting errors:

- Forgetting bold on WHEREAS and RESOLVED.
- Using periods instead of semicolons between WHEREAS clauses.
- Omitting the "NOW, THEREFORE, be it" transition.
- Skipping the standard formula "as it is hereby resolved by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament."
- Inconsistent capitalization of Parliament, Speaker, Chief Minister, and other institutional titles.

**Mistake 6: Citing the wrong law or wrong provision.** If you cite Section 6, Article IX of the BOL, make sure it actually says what you claim it says. Verify every citation. This is especially important when citing the BOL, because BOL articles have many sections and it is easy to cite the wrong one.

**Mistake 7: No furnishing clause.** If the resolution requests action from an agency but never directs that a copy be sent to that agency, the resolution is an expression into the void. Always include a furnishing clause that identifies specific recipients.

**Mistake 8: Omitting the Islamic calendar date.** BTA resolutions use both the Islamic and Gregorian calendar in the adoption line. This is not optional — it reflects the Bangsamoro identity of the Parliament. Check the correct Islamic date and include it: *"ADOPTED, Dhu Al-Hijjah 21, 1445/June 27, 2024."*

**Mistake 9: Missing the "Certified Correct" and "Attested" signatures.** Every adopted resolution ends with two authentication blocks. The Secretary-General certifies correctness. The Speaker attests. If these are missing from the final document, the resolution is not properly authenticated. Your draft should include placeholder blocks for both:

```
Certified Correct:

(Signature)
PROF. RABY B. ANGKAL
Secretary-General

Attested:

(Signature)
ATTY. PANGALIAN M. BALINDONG
Speaker
```

## 5.5 Resolution No. 268 as Governing Framework

Resolution No. 268 is not just another resolution. It is the governing framework for everything the Parliament does. Adopted during the First Regular Session, it establishes the Rules, Procedures, and Practices of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament. Every drafter needs to know the provisions of Res. 268 that directly affect resolution drafting.

### The Key Provisions

**Rule XVII, Section 4** defines what a resolution is: "a document expressing the sentiment or opinion of the Parliament." This is the baseline. Whenever you are unsure whether something should be a bill or a resolution, return to this definition.

**Rule XVII, Section 5** establishes the three types of resolutions recognized by the Parliament: simple, concurrent, and joint committee resolutions. These are not interchangeable — each has a specific purpose and a specific process.

**Rule XVII, Section 6** is the provision you will rely on most often. It establishes the simplified process for simple and concurrent resolutions:

> *"Simple and concurrent resolutions shall not be subject to the ordinary procedure as prescribed in the Stages of Legislation herein provided. The Parliament or the Committees concerned in a plenary session shall promptly act on these matters."*

This provision is what allows a resolution to be filed and adopted in a single session. But note the limitation — it applies only to simple and concurrent resolutions. Joint committee resolutions and procedural resolutions (like Res. 268 itself) may require a fuller deliberative process.

**Rule XVII, Section 8** prescribes the form of a proposed resolution. It lists five elements in order:

1. **Number** — the number assigned by the Secretary-General.
2. **Authors** — the names of the Members who introduce the resolution. They must sign it.
3. **Title** — states the purpose and must accurately reflect the contents.
4. **Body** — the WHEREAS clauses presenting the justifications.
5. **Final Statement** — the operative clause expressing the Parliament's sentiment.

This is your structural checklist. Every resolution you draft must contain all five elements in this order.

**Rule XVII, Section 9** covers filing: "Bills and resolutions shall be signed by their author(s) and together with an electronic copy thereof, filed with the Secretary-General." The Secretary-General assigns the number. Note that both a printed and electronic copy are required, and in case of discrepancy, the printed copy prevails.

**Rule XVII, Section 10** gives the Speaker the duty to refer resolutions to appropriate committees. This means that even though simple resolutions follow a simplified process, the Speaker retains discretion to refer a resolution to committee if the subject matter warrants fuller deliberation.

**Rule XVIII, Section 2(a)** — the First Reading provision — applies to resolutions as well as bills: "The Secretary-General shall report all bills and resolutions to the Parliament for First Reading."

**Rule VIII, Section 1** — the Order of Business — places resolutions in the sequence as item (g)(2), "Resolutions, Messages, Memorials and Communications," and item (i), "Resolutions Referred to the Committee." This tells you where in a session day your resolution will be taken up.

### How to Use Res. 268 in Your Daily Work

Keep a copy of Res. 268 on your desk — or open on your screen. When you draft a resolution, check it against the following provisions:

1. **Is this the right type?** (Section 5 — simple, concurrent, or joint committee)
2. **Does it follow the prescribed form?** (Section 8 — number, authors, title, body, final statement)
3. **Is the process correct?** (Section 6 — simplified for simple and concurrent; fuller process for others)
4. **Has it been properly filed?** (Section 9 — signed, with electronic copy, filed with the Secretary-General)

### Res. 268 as a Model Resolution

Resolution No. 268 is also useful as a model of resolution drafting. Look at its preamble:

- The first WHEREAS recital cites Section 3, Article XVI of Republic Act No. 11054, establishing the legal authority of the BTA.
- The second recital provides historical context — the BTA 1 adopted its own rules.
- The third recital cites Section 25, Article VII, which authorizes the Parliament to adopt its own rules.
- The operative clause adopts the Rules.

That is three recitals — legal authority, historical context, specific authorization — followed by the operative clause. It is tight, logical, and well-ordered. It does not contain unnecessary recitals, it does not editorialize, and every recital serves a purpose. This is the standard to aim for.

### When Res. 268 Is Silent

Resolution No. 268 does not address every situation you will encounter. It says nothing about how to draft recitals, what to include in a commendation resolution, or how many WHEREAS clauses are appropriate. On these matters, you rely on Parliamentary practice — the accumulated conventions of how the Parliament has done things in the past — and on the general principles of good drafting covered in Chapter 6.

When Res. 268 is silent, look at how the Parliament has handled similar resolutions before. Consistency with past practice is a reasonable guide. But if past practice contains errors — misspellings, bad formatting, incorrect citations — do not repeat those errors. Follow good practice, not just established practice.

### Drafting Checklist for Resolutions

Before you file a resolution, run through this checklist:

**Content**
- [ ] All names are spelled correctly with correct titles.
- [ ] All dates are verified.
- [ ] All legal citations are verified against the original text.
- [ ] Facts have been confirmed through reliable sources.
- [ ] The resolution does not duplicate a previously adopted resolution.
- [ ] The resolution does not attempt to create a legal obligation (if it does, it should be a bill).
- [ ] The operative clause matches the purpose stated in the title.

**Structure**
- [ ] The caption includes the correct Parliament session and body name.
- [ ] The resolution number is assigned by the Secretary-General.
- [ ] Authors are listed with correct names and titles.
- [ ] The title is in all capitals and accurately reflects the content.
- [ ] WHEREAS recitals flow in deductive order — general to specific.
- [ ] Each WHEREAS clause contains one point.
- [ ] WHEREAS clauses end with semicolons (final one with a colon).
- [ ] "NOW, THEREFORE, be it" transition is present.
- [ ] Operative clause uses the standard formula.
- [ ] Additional RESOLVED clauses use FURTHER/FURTHERMORE/FINALLY progression.
- [ ] Furnishing clause identifies specific recipients.
- [ ] ADOPTED line includes both Islamic and Gregorian dates.
- [ ] Certified Correct and Attested blocks are present.

**Format**
- [ ] WHEREAS is bold.
- [ ] RESOLVED is bold.
- [ ] Italics are used correctly for legal citations and quoted text.
- [ ] Capitalization is consistent for institutional names and titles.

## Summary

Resolutions are the Parliament's voice. They express what the Parliament thinks, feels, recommends, and requests. They do not create law. They follow a different structure — WHEREAS preamble plus operative clause — and a different process — typically simplified under Section 6 of Rule XVII.

The drafter's job is to ensure that the resolution is factually accurate, legally grounded, properly formatted, and clearly written. A well-drafted resolution strengthens the Parliament's credibility. A poorly drafted one — with misspelled names, incorrect citations, or operative clauses that try to legislate — undermines it.

Know the types. Know the process. Know the format. And always check your work against Resolution No. 268.
