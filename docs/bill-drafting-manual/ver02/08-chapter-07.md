# Chapter 7: How a Bill Becomes a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act

A bill is just words on paper until it survives the legislative process. Drafting matters, but so does knowing the path your bill must travel from the moment it is filed until it is signed into law. The Bangsamoro Parliament follows a structured, multi-stage process governed by Resolution No. 268 — the Rules, Procedures, and Practices of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament. If you do not understand these stages, you cannot draft effectively. You will write provisions that get stripped in committee, frame arguments that miss the window for debate, or produce a bill that stalls because you failed to anticipate a procedural gate.

This chapter walks you through every stage of that process. It is written for the drafter — the person whose work begins before First Reading and whose attention should not end until the bill is enrolled and signed. At each stage, you will learn what happens procedurally, what the Rules require, and what that means for how you prepare your bill.

---

## 7.1 Types of Legislative Measures

Before understanding how a bill moves through Parliament, you need to understand what kinds of measures Parliament considers. Not every document that reaches the plenary floor is a bill, and the type of measure determines the procedure it follows.

### 7.1.1 Government Bills

A Government bill is a legislative initiative submitted by the Government of the Day — meaning the Chief Minister and the Cabinet — for Parliament's approval or amendment. Resolution No. 268, Rule XVII, Section 2(a) defines it this way:

> **Government Bill.** — Government bill refers to a legislative initiative that the Government of the Day submits to Parliament for approval or amendment.

Government bills carry a built-in advantage: they take precedence over Private Member bills. This is not merely a matter of courtesy. Rule XVII, Section 2(b) states plainly that "Government bills shall have precedence over Private Member bills without prejudice to consolidation." In practice, this means Government bills tend to move through the calendar faster and receive priority scheduling.

A Government bill can originate within a committee. If Members of a committee — statutory, parliamentary, special, ad hoc, or otherwise — discover through their meetings, hearings, or investigations that a new law is needed, they may first persuade the relevant Cabinet Minister to draft the bill. If the Chief Minister and Cabinet approve it, the measure enters Parliament as a Government bill. This pathway matters for drafters because it means a bill that starts as a committee initiative can be reframed as a Government bill if the executive branch adopts it, gaining the procedural advantages that come with that classification.

As a drafter working on a Government bill, you are typically working under the direction of a ministry or the Office of the Chief Minister. Your bill reflects government policy. This means your explanatory statement should connect the bill to the Government of the Day's legislative agenda and the Bangsamoro Development Plan.

### 7.1.2 Private Member Bills

A Private Member bill is introduced by a Member of Parliament who is not a Minister. Any Member who does not hold a Cabinet position may file a bill on any subject within Parliament's legislative competence.

> **Private Member Bill.** — A Private Member bill refers to a legislative initiative introduced by a Member of the Parliament who is not a Minister for approval or amendment.

While Private Member bills do not enjoy the scheduling priority of Government bills, they are not second-class legislation. Once referred to committee, a Private Member bill receives the same scrutiny and follows the same stages as any Government bill. Many significant laws in parliamentary systems worldwide have originated as Private Member bills.

One practical detail: at First Reading, the Speaker declares whether a bill is a Government bill or a Private Member bill. If you are filing a Private Member bill, the author may propose the inclusion of additional co-authors by making the appropriate request to the Majority Floor Leader. Building co-authorship early strengthens the bill's chances — it signals broader support before the bill even reaches committee.

### 7.1.3 Resolutions

A resolution expresses the sentiment or opinion of Parliament. It does not create or modify a law. Rule XVII, Section 4 is direct:

> **Resolution.** — A resolution is a document expressing the sentiment or opinion of the Parliament. It does not create or modify a law.

Parliament recognizes three types of resolutions:

1. **Simple Resolution** — Used for internal procedural actions, urgent matters requiring immediate parliamentary action, or expressions of opinion, sympathy, or commendation.
2. **Concurrent Resolution** — Issued concurrently by both the Bangsamoro Cabinet and Parliament, expressing their shared sentiments on certain matters.
3. **Joint Committee Resolution** — A resolution from two or more committees expressing the will or action of their Members on certain matters. Requires a majority vote of all committees concerned.

The critical distinction for drafters: simple and concurrent resolutions are not subject to the ordinary stages of legislation. Rule XVII, Section 6 exempts them:

> Simple and concurrent resolutions shall not be subject to the ordinary procedure as prescribed in the Stages of Legislation herein provided. The Parliament or the Committees concerned in a plenary session shall promptly act on these matters.

This means if you are drafting a resolution — say, a resolution urging the national government to act on a Bangsamoro concern, or a resolution expressing Parliament's position on a regional issue — your document will not go through the First Reading, Second Reading, Committee Stage, and Third Reading process described in this chapter. It will be acted upon promptly.

### 7.1.4 Other Measures

Two additional categories deserve mention.

**Popular Initiative.** Republic Act No. 11054, the Bangsamoro Organic Law, provides for participatory democracy. A Popular Initiative is a process whereby a bill is proposed directly by the people. Rule XVII, Section 2(c) references BAA No. 13 in defining it and notes that Parliament shall regulate the submission of Popular Initiative bills through guidelines that may form part of the Rules. This mechanism is not commonly invoked, but it exists in the Bangsamoro legal framework. If you are ever asked to draft implementing guidelines for a Popular Initiative bill, you need to understand both the organic law provisions and any future parliamentary guidelines on the subject.

**Committee-Initiated Bills.** While not a formal classification separate from Government or Private Member bills, committee-initiated measures deserve attention. Committees that discover the need for new legislation during their hearings and investigations may persuade a Cabinet Minister to draft and adopt the bill, converting it into a Government bill. Alternatively, a committee member who is not a Minister may choose to file it as a Private Member bill. The origin point matters because it affects classification, precedence, and the political dynamics around the bill.

## 7.2 First Reading

First Reading is the formal introduction of a bill to Parliament. It is brief, procedural, and largely ceremonial — but it marks the official beginning of the bill's legislative life.

### 7.2.1 Reading of Number, Title, and Author

Before a bill reaches First Reading, it must be properly filed. Rule XVII, Section 9 sets out the filing requirements:

> Bills and resolutions shall be signed by their author(s) and together with an electronic copy thereof, filed with the Secretary-General, who shall assign a number to each bill or resolution. The Secretary-General shall not receive a bill or resolution unless it is accompanied by an electronic copy thereof. In case of any discrepancy between the printed and the electronic copies of the bill or resolution, the printed copy shall prevail. The bill or resolution shall then be forwarded to the Office of the Speaker.

Once filed and numbered, the bill is included in the Order of Business under "Reference of Business: Bills on First Reading" — item (g)(1) under Rule VIII, Section 1.

At First Reading, the Secretary-General reports the bill to Parliament. The process involves only three things: the reading of the bill's number, its title, and the name of its author or authors. No debate occurs. No speeches are delivered. No committee referral happens yet.

Rule XVIII, Section 2(a) states:

> **First Reading.** — The Secretary-General shall report all bills and resolutions to the Parliament for First Reading. The First Reading shall involve only the reading of the number, title, and author of the bill or resolution, followed by a declaration of the Speaker whether the bill is a Government bill or a Private Member bill.

This is why your title matters so much. At First Reading, the title is all that Parliament hears. A title that is vague, misleading, or overly technical fails at its first public test. A title that is clear and purposeful — one that "spells out the purpose of the bill in general terms and must accurately reflect its contents," as Rule XVII, Section 7(c) requires — gives Parliament a sharp first impression.

### 7.2.2 Declaration as Government or Private Member Bill

Immediately after the reading of number, title, and author, the Speaker makes a declaration: is this a Government bill or a Private Member bill? This classification, as explained in Section 7.1, carries real consequences for scheduling priority.

If the bill is a Private Member bill, this is also the moment when the author may propose adding co-authors. The request goes to the Majority Floor Leader. While this may seem like a housekeeping detail, building a broader base of authorship at this early stage signals coalition support and can influence how seriously the bill is treated going forward.

After the Speaker's declaration, First Reading is complete. The bill now awaits Second Reading.

## 7.3 Second Reading

Second Reading is where the bill begins to receive substantive attention. It is the transition from filing to active legislative engagement. Three things happen at this stage: the bill is read in full (or deemed read), the author delivers the authorship speech, and the bill is referred to the appropriate committee.

### 7.3.1 Reading of the Bill

At Second Reading, the bill is read in full — unless copies have already been distributed to Members. Rule XVIII, Section 2(b) provides:

> A bill or resolution on Second Reading shall be read in full unless copies thereof have been distributed to the Members.

In practice, bills are almost always distributed in advance. The reading-in-full requirement is waived when Members already have the text. But the rule exists as a safeguard: no bill should move forward if Members have not had the opportunity to review its contents.

This is another reason why clear, well-organized drafting matters. When Members receive printed copies of your bill, the structure you chose — how you organized the articles, how you numbered the sections, whether your provisions read logically — determines whether they can follow the bill on first encounter. Members who struggle to parse a badly organized bill are more likely to resist it or raise unnecessary objections.

### 7.3.2 Authorship Speech

After the bill is read (or deemed read), the proponent delivers the authorship speech. This is the explanatory statement — the argument for why the bill exists and why Parliament should act on it.

The authorship speech is not a formality. It is the author's first and often most influential opportunity to frame the bill's purpose, explain the problem it addresses, and persuade colleagues that the bill deserves committee attention and ultimately passage. While the explanatory statement filed with the bill provides the written foundation, the authorship speech delivered on the floor is where the author makes the case in person.

As a drafter, you are likely the one preparing the explanatory statement that forms the backbone of this speech. Write it with the understanding that it will be delivered aloud before the full Parliament. It should be persuasive but grounded in fact. It should connect the bill to real problems in the Bangsamoro, cite relevant data or constitutional authority, and explain what the bill does in plain terms.

### 7.3.3 Referral to the Appropriate Committee

After the authorship speech, the bill is referred to the appropriate committee by the Majority Floor Leader. This is the gateway to the Committee Stage.

> Thereafter, the bill shall be referred to the appropriate committee by the Majority Floor Leader.

The referral is based on the bill's subject matter and the jurisdictions of Parliament's committees as defined in Rule XII. A bill on education goes to the Committee on Basic, Higher, and Technical Education. A bill on natural resources goes to the Committee on Environment, Natural Resources, and Energy. A bill establishing new courts or justice mechanisms goes to the Committee on Bangsamoro Justice System.

Sometimes the correct committee is not obvious, or a bill touches on the jurisdiction of multiple committees. Rule XVIII, Section 2(b) provides a mechanism for this:

> The Chairperson of a Committee to which a bill or resolution is referred may make a written request to the Committee on Rules for a change of referral of such bill or resolution, citing the grounds therefor. The Committee on Rules shall decide on the request within five (5) days from receipt thereof.

For you as a drafter, committee referral has practical consequences. The committee that receives your bill will scrutinize it line by line. If you know which committee will handle your bill — and you usually can predict this from the subject matter — you should understand that committee's priorities, its recent work, and the concerns of its chair. A bill drafted with awareness of the receiving committee's perspective has a smoother path through the Committee Stage.

The Speaker also has the duty to refer bills and resolutions to appropriate committees, as stated in Rule XVII, Section 10:

> The Speaker shall have the duty to refer the Government bills, Private Member bills or resolutions to the appropriate committees.

## 7.4 Committee Stage

The Committee Stage is where the real legislative work happens. If First Reading is the introduction and Second Reading is the presentation, the Committee Stage is the surgery. The committee examines the bill provision by provision, tests its assumptions, hears from experts, proposes amendments, and produces a report that tells Parliament whether the bill should proceed, and in what form.

Rule XVIII, Section 2(c) describes this stage:

> **Committee Stage.** — At this stage, the concerned committee shall examine, scrutinize and deliberate in detail and line by line the legislative proposal. It may consult, invite, and solicit opinions from experts, relevant ministries and offices, the public and other interested parties.
>
> The committee concerned may propose amendments or revisions to the proposal, and if there are no changes made, the bill is deemed submitted to the next stage. The Committee shall then render a report on the bill to the plenary.

### 7.4.1 Examination and Scrutiny

Line-by-line scrutiny is the heart of the Committee Stage. Each provision of your bill will be examined for its purpose, its legal soundness, its practical implications, and its consistency with existing law — particularly the Bangsamoro Organic Law.

Committees have broad power over the bills referred to them. Rule XII, Section 18 gives committees the power to adopt, amend, revise, or reject a bill entirely:

> A Committee shall have the power to adopt, amend, revise, or reject the Government bill or Private Member bill referred to it.

That last word — reject — is important. A committee can kill a bill. If the committee concludes that the bill is unnecessary, unconstitutional, poorly drafted, or politically unviable, it can decline to report it favorably. In practice, a bill that the committee declines to advance is effectively dead unless the Parliament intervenes through procedural means.

Committees meet during specific times. Rule XIII, Section 1 sets the schedule:

> The meetings, hearings, and consultations of the Committees and Sub-Committees are held in the mornings of Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday in the Executive Lounge of the Parliament building during the regular session of the Parliament, without prejudice to holding it, whenever necessary, on days and in venues outside of those herein mentioned.

Quorum for committee meetings is different from plenary quorum. Rule VII, Section 4(b) provides:

> **Quorum for Committee Meetings.** — Thirty percent (30%) of the Members of the Committee in meetings shall constitute a quorum.
>
> The presence of the Chairperson or any Member designated as Presiding Officer during a Committee Public Hearing or Consultation shall be sufficient to conduct business.

The lower quorum threshold for committees — 30% compared to 50% plus one for plenary — means committee work can proceed even when not all members attend. But it also means that a small number of engaged committee members can shape your bill significantly.

As a drafter, you should anticipate that the Committee Stage will test every element of your bill. Definitions will be questioned. Penalty provisions will be debated. Funding mechanisms will be challenged. Transitional provisions will be scrutinized for gaps. The better your bill is drafted — with clear definitions, justified penalty scales, realistic appropriations, and thorough transitional provisions — the smoother the Committee Stage will be.

### 7.4.2 Committee Hearings, Consultations, and Investigations

Committees do not operate in isolation. They are empowered — and expected — to seek outside input. Rule XIII, Section 1 provides:

> The Committees and Sub-Committees may organize public hearings and consultations for the purpose of obtaining more information and facts and a wider view on any prospective legislative measures from experts, stakeholders, and the public at large. These hearings and consultations are open to the general public.

The committee may also conduct research visits and on-site investigations. This is not window dressing. Committee members who visit affected communities, inspect existing programs, or witness conditions firsthand bring a level of understanding that written submissions cannot fully convey.

Committees can invite and consult experts and resource persons under Rule XIV, Section 4:

> The committee may invite and consult experts and resource persons having expertise or knowledge in a particular field in order to gather more facts, knowledge and information that will help form the conclusion to the matter under consideration.

Members of the Cabinet may also appear before committees, either voluntarily or upon request. Rule XIV, Section 1 states:

> The Chief Minister, or his/her duly authorized Members of the Cabinet, or any high ranking official of the Government, may attend any committee meetings or hearings, if he/she deems it necessary.

And committees have enforcement power. If a witness refuses to appear or produce documents, committees can issue subpoenas — subpoena ad testificandum (to compel testimony) and subpoena duces tecum (to compel documents). These must be signed by the Committee Chairperson (or Vice-Chairperson in the Chair's absence) and approved by the Speaker. Rule XV, Section 1 provides:

> The Parliamentary committees shall have the power to conduct inquiry in aid of legislation including the power to summon witnesses and take their testimony, and to issue subpoena and subpoena duces tecum, signed by the Chairperson of the Committee or in his/her absence by the Vice-Chairperson, and approved by the Speaker of the Parliament.

Committees also possess contempt power. A witness who defies a subpoena, refuses to be sworn, refuses to testify, or testifies falsely can be cited for contempt and detained under the custody of the Sergeant-at-Arms until they comply. Rule XVI, Sections 2 through 4 lay out these powers in detail.

For the drafter, committee hearings serve a dual purpose. They test the bill's assumptions against real-world input, and they build the legislative record that will justify the bill's provisions. If your bill creates a new regulatory framework, the committee hearings are where affected stakeholders will raise practical concerns — and where you may be called upon to explain or defend specific drafting choices.

### 7.4.3 Committee Amendments

After examining the bill and hearing from stakeholders, the committee may propose amendments or revisions. These committee amendments can be substantial. The committee can add provisions, delete provisions, rewrite entire sections, or restructure the bill. Rule XII, Section 18 grants committees the power to "adopt, amend, revise, or reject" any bill referred to them.

If the committee makes no changes, the bill as filed is deemed submitted to the next stage. But that is rare. Most bills emerge from committee in amended form, sometimes dramatically different from the version that was filed.

Committee amendments are later presented to the plenary during the Consideration and Deliberation Stage, where they must be debated and voted upon. This is an important distinction. Committee amendments are proposals, not fait accompli. The full Parliament must still approve them.

Rule XVII, Section 15 distinguishes between committee amendments and floor amendments:

> **Committee Amendment.** — Committee amendment refers to any alteration or changes by addition, deletion, substitution, or any amendment to a bill offered by the concerned committee.

### 7.4.4 The Committee Report

The committee's work culminates in a committee report. This is a formal document that captures the committee's deliberations, the views expressed, and the committee's recommendations. Rule XII, Section 16 sets out the requirements:

> Committees are required to prepare and submit a committee report which contains the discussions and the views expressed by the individual Members and all the invited resource persons and experts on the bill or resolution under its consideration as well as the amendments introduced and their justifications.
>
> The Members who participated in the final voting must sign the report, either in favor, with reservation, or dissenting.

The signing requirement is significant. Members who sign in favor endorse the committee's recommendation. Those who sign with reservation retain the right to raise specific objections at the plenary stage. Those who dissent record their opposition. Rule XII, Section 17 further provides:

> The Members of the Committee who do not support any legislative proposal adopted by a committee, may submit a reservation in which a Member may state his/her reason and/or what he/she thought should be done. The reservation shall be included in the committee report.

And there is an important constraint on committee members who signed the report without objection:

> Any Member who has signed the committee report cannot ask questions or make a statement contrary to the report, except about the points in the committee report he/she has objected to, abstained, and signed with reservation.

This rule prevents committee members from undermining their own committee's work on the floor — unless they formally reserved their position during the committee process.

The committee report is then printed, distributed, and forwarded to the Office of the Secretary-General. It must also be made available to the public in writing or electronic form.

For the drafter, the committee report is the bridge between your original bill and its plenary debate. The report tells Parliament what the committee found, what it changed, and why. If you drafted the bill well and the committee approved it with minimal amendments, the report will reflect that. If the committee made significant changes, the report explains the rationale — and the amended text becomes the version that Parliament debates.

## 7.5 Consideration and Deliberation Stage

Once the committee reports the bill to the plenary, it enters the Consideration and Deliberation Stage. This is the most dynamic phase of the legislative process — the stage where the full Parliament debates the bill, questions its provisions, proposes further amendments, and votes on whether to approve it on second reading.

Rule XVIII, Section 2(d) frames this stage:

> **Consideration and Deliberation Stage.** — The Members of the Parliament shall deliberate and debate the committee report, including proposed amendments, if there are any, in the plenary session, except when the proponent has already agreed to adopt the committee amendments.

This stage unfolds in a defined sequence: debate, interpellation, amendments, and voting.

### 7.5.1 Period of Debate

The Order of Business under Rule VIII, Section 1 lists the components of Second Reading consideration:

> (h) Bills on Second Reading:
> (1) Committee Reports on Bills;
> (2) Period of Interpellations;
> (3) Period of Amendments; and
> (4) Approval of Second Reading.

The debate begins when the committee report on the bill is taken up in plenary. The sponsor of the committee report — usually the committee chairperson — presents the report and its recommendations.

Debate follows a structured format. Rule XIX, Section 11(a) establishes the alternating-turns principle:

> If a debate ensues, turns for and against the bill shall be taken alternately: *Provided, however*, That any Committee Member who fails to enter his/her objection or to make of record his/her dissenting vote in the committee level, shall not be allowed to speak against the bill during the period of debate, although he/she may propose and speak or vote on amendments thereto.

This alternating format — speakers for the bill followed by speakers against, then for, then against — ensures that debate is balanced. It also creates a consequence for committee members who did not voice objections during the Committee Stage: they are barred from speaking against the bill on the floor, though they may still propose and vote on amendments.

Individual Members cannot speak indefinitely. Rule XIX, Section 6 sets a time limit:

> A Member shall not be allowed to speak for more than one (1) hour in debate on any question. However, the sponsor of any measure may consume as much time as he deems necessary to express the contents and purposes of the bill.

The sponsor enjoys unlimited speaking time. All other Members are capped at one hour per question. And under Rule XIX, Section 5, no Member may speak more than once on the same question during the same day, unless the Parliament grants leave by majority vote — and only after all other Members who wish to speak have spoken.

The Speaker must remain impartial during debate. Rule XIX, Section 10:

> The Speaker shall remain impartial during debate and deliberation of bills and resolutions in the plenary. When he/she desires to engage in debate on the merits of a bill or resolution under consideration with Members of the Parliament, he/she shall relinquish the chair, and the Deputy Speaker shall temporarily act as the presiding officer.

Debate closes when the sponsor makes a final statement (after all Members who wish to speak have spoken), when the Speaker declares the debate closed upon a duly seconded motion, or when no further speakers seek recognition.

### 7.5.2 Period of Interpellation

Interpellation is the questioning phase. Members registered on the list of interpellators may question the sponsor of the bill about its provisions, intent, and implications. The Majority Floor Leader maintains the list and determines the order of recognition.

Rule XIX, Section 3 governs the process:

> The Majority Floor Leader shall maintain a list of interpellators that serves as the guide in the order to be recognized in the discussion of the measure under consideration. Unless interrupted by procedural motions, the session proceeds with the list. In order to be registered in the list of interpellators, a Member shall register in writing to the Secretary-General which list shall be submitted to the Majority Floor Leader for the recognition of the interpellator.

The Secretary-General assists the Majority Floor Leader in maintaining this list, as provided in Rule V, Section 1(m):

> The Secretary-General shall assist the Majority Floor Leader in maintaining the list of interpellators which shall serve as guide during the interpellation period on a particular bill or resolution.

Interpellation is a test of the bill's substance. Members ask probing questions — about the bill's scope, its financial implications, its legal basis, its intended and unintended consequences. The answers given by the sponsor become part of the legislative record and can shape how provisions are later interpreted.

As the drafter, you may be called upon to prepare the sponsor for interpellation. This means anticipating the questions that Members will ask and preparing clear, defensible answers. If a provision is ambiguous, interpellation will expose it. If the bill's fiscal impact is unclear, interpellation will probe it. Think of interpellation as the stress test your bill faces in public.

### 7.5.3 Period of Floor Amendments

After debate and interpellation, the bill enters the period of amendments. This is where the text of the bill — as reported by the committee — can be further altered on the floor.

Rule XIX, Section 11(c) sets the sequence:

> With the debate closed, the consideration of amendments, if any, shall be in order.
>
> At this stage, a Committee Amendment followed by individual and Floor Amendment shall be observed.
>
> (1) Committee Amendment.
> (2) Individual or Floor Amendment.
> (3) After the period of amendments, the voting of the bill on Second Reading.

Committee amendments are taken up first. These are the changes proposed by the committee in its report. If the proponent (the sponsor presenting the committee report) has already agreed to adopt the committee amendments, they may be considered adopted without extensive debate. But if the proponent does not accept the committee amendments, the period of amendment continues.

Rule XVIII, Section 2(d) elaborates:

> If the proponent of the committee report does not accept the amendments, the period of amendment continues, and he/she can propose his/her own amendments. This is called the Committee Amendments which refer to the alterations, modifications, and changes made or proposed to be made to the bill that is offered by the proponent.
>
> Individual Members can also propose an amendment. This is called individual or Floor Amendments, which refers to any alteration, modification, and change made or proposed to be made to the bill by other Members of the Parliament.
>
> Both the committee amendments and individual or floor amendments shall be debated and voted upon.

Floor amendments must be presented concretely. Rule XVII, Section 12 requires:

> All bills and resolutions shall be subject to amendments; but in order that the amendments may be considered, they should be presented concretely and specifically in the course of the debate, indicating therein the page and the line in which the amendments are proposed to be made. The proponent may be required to present the amendment in writing in order to expedite the proceeding.

No amendment by substitution is entertained unless the text is submitted in writing. And no amendment may introduce a provision not germane to the bill's purpose — the prohibition against riders under Rule XVII, Section 13:

> A bill shall only have one (1) subject matter, and an amendment which seeks the inclusion of a provision not germane to the purpose of a bill (rider) shall not be allowed.

Similarly, a bill cannot be amended by substituting it with another bill covering a distinct subject, per Rule XVII, Section 14.

For the drafter, the period of floor amendments is where your bill's structure proves its worth. A well-organized bill — with clearly numbered sections, logical groupings, and consistent internal references — makes it easy for Members to propose targeted amendments. A poorly structured bill invites confusion, conflicting amendments, and delays.

### 7.5.4 Voting on Second Reading

After all amendments have been debated and resolved, the bill is put to a vote on second reading. This is the fourth component of the Second Reading process in the Order of Business: "Approval of Second Reading."

Approval on second reading requires a majority of the votes cast, provided a quorum exists. Rule XXI, Section 12 states the general voting rule:

> If the Parliament has established a quorum, the votes required to approve a motion is the majority of the votes cast provided that a motion to suppress or limit debate, to prevent the consideration of a question, and without due notice to rescind action previously taken, require a two-thirds (2/3) vote of all Members present.

If the bill is approved on second reading, it advances to Third Reading. If it is rejected, the bill is effectively defeated — though procedural motions for reconsideration remain theoretically available.

Approval on second reading is a significant milestone. It means that Parliament, having heard the committee report, debated the bill, subjected it to interpellation, and resolved all amendments, has approved the bill in substance. What remains is the final vote.

## 7.6 Third Reading and Final Passage

Third Reading — also called the Final Stage — is the last legislative hurdle before a bill is deemed passed by Parliament. The bill's text is now fixed. No further amendments are allowed. What remains is a final debate on whether to pass the bill, and a decisive vote.

Rule XVIII, Section 2(e) governs this stage:

> **Third Reading or Final Stage.** — During the third reading or final stage, there shall be a final debate on whether or not to pass the bill. No amendment can be considered at this time. The proponent of the bill or author of the motion shall have the right to close the debate.

### 7.6.1 Final Debate

The Third Reading debate is not about specific provisions — those were settled during the Consideration and Deliberation Stage. It is about the bill as a whole. Should Parliament enact this law? Does it serve the interests of the Bangsamoro? Is it consistent with the Organic Law? Members speak for or against passage.

The sponsor has the right to close the debate with a final statement. This closing speech is the last word before the vote, and it carries rhetorical weight.

There is a critical procedural prerequisite for Third Reading. Printed copies of the bill in its final form must be distributed to Members at least three days before voting. Rule XVIII, Section 2(e) and Rule XIX, Section 12 both require this:

> During the voting, the Majority Floor Leader shall only read the title of the bill, provided that printed copies of the bill shall have been distributed to the Members of the Parliament at least three (3) days before voting ensues.

This three-day distribution requirement ensures that Members have time to review the final text — including all amendments adopted at the Consideration and Deliberation Stage — before casting their votes. It is a safeguard against Members voting on a bill they have not read.

There is one exception: certified or emergency bills. If the Chief Minister certifies the necessity of immediate enactment to respond to a public calamity or emergency, the three-day requirement is waived and voting can occur the same day the bill is approved on second reading.

### 7.6.2 Voting and Passage

At Third Reading, the Majority Floor Leader reads only the title of the bill. Then the Speaker calls for the vote.

> The Speaker shall ask for a roll call vote by show of hands or by standing or sitting provided that any Member who wishes to abstain shall verbally manifest his abstention, without the need of explanation.

Members may make brief statements explaining their vote before the vote is taken. Rule XIX, Section 13 allows:

> During the final voting stage, Members of the Parliament may make a verbal statement on the final vote lasting not more than five (5) minutes or submit a short-written statement, which shall be included in the minutes of plenary proceedings. As a rule, the Speaker shall give Members leave to make a statement before the vote is taken.

Any Member may also declare before the vote that they will not participate.

A bill passes Third Reading by a majority of the votes cast, with a quorum present. Once passed, the bill has been approved by Parliament and moves to the post-passage process.

## 7.7 Post-Passage: Enrollment and Executive Action

Passage at Third Reading does not make a bill law. Several steps remain before the bill becomes a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act.

### 7.7.1 Enrollment and Authentication

After the bill passes Third Reading, the final printed text must be prepared — this is called enrollment. The enrolled bill incorporates all amendments adopted during the legislative process. It is the authoritative, clean text of the bill as approved by Parliament.

The enrolled bill is signed by the Speaker. Rule IV, Section 4(k) includes among the Speaker's duties:

> Sign all acts, resolutions, and bills that may be issued by or upon order of the Parliament.

The Secretary-General certifies and affixes the seal of Parliament on all approved bills, as required by Rule V, Section 1(h):

> Certify and affix the seal of the Parliament on all approved resolutions, bills, orders, and any other acts and legislative measures.

### 7.7.2 Transmittal to Chief Minister

The authenticated bill is then presented to the Chief Minister for executive action. Rule IV, Section 4(n) provides:

> Present to the Chief Minister every bill before signing into law.

This transmittal is a formal act. It initiates the executive review period during which the Chief Minister decides whether to sign the bill into law, veto it, or allow it to lapse into law through inaction.

### 7.7.3 Approval, Veto, and Inaction

Rule XVIII, Section 2(f) states:

> **Approval of the Bill.** — After going through the stages herein provided, the printed form of the approved bill shall be signed by the Speaker and the Chief Minister.

When the Chief Minister signs the bill, it becomes a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act.

The bill does not take effect upon signing, however. There is a publication requirement. Rule XVIII, Section 2(g) provides:

> **Effectivity.** — The enacted law shall take effect after fifteen (15) days following its complete publication in at least one (1) newspaper of general circulation within the BARMM.

This means the enacted law must be published in a newspaper of general circulation within the Bangsamoro Region. Only after fifteen days from complete publication does the law take effect — unless the law itself specifies a different effectivity date.

The Organic Law also requires that a certified true copy of all laws and resolutions approved by Parliament be submitted to the President and to the Congress of the Philippines. This duty falls to the Speaker under Rule IV, Section 4(o):

> Submit to the President and to the Congress of the Philippines a certified true copy of all laws and resolutions approved by the Parliament.

Regarding the veto power and the possibility of inaction — the Bangsamoro Organic Law governs these matters. The parliamentary system in the Bangsamoro operates differently from the presidential system at the national level. The Chief Minister, as head of the Government of the Day and a product of the parliamentary majority, is aligned with Parliament in ways that make vetoes less common than in a presidential system. But the constitutional framework preserves executive review as a check on legislation.

## 7.8 Swift Passage Procedure

Not every bill needs to go through the full committee scrutiny process. Resolution No. 268 provides for an expedited path called the Swift Passage Procedure.

Rule XVIII, Section 4:

> **Swift Passage Procedure.** — The Parliament shall, in exceptional cases, adopt the Swift Passage Procedure which is a speedy disposition of a bill in seven (7) working days that skips the detailed scrutiny and examination period in the Committee Stage provided in Section 2(c), Rule XVIII, of this Rules.
>
> This procedure allows cross-party or free conscience voting.

The Swift Passage Procedure has three defining characteristics:

1. **Speed.** The bill must be disposed of within seven working days. That is an aggressive timeline for any piece of legislation.
2. **Skips committee scrutiny.** The bill bypasses the detailed line-by-line examination that normally occurs at the Committee Stage. This does not mean the bill skips all stages — First Reading and Second Reading still occur — but the committee's role in examining and amending the bill is eliminated or compressed.
3. **Cross-party or free conscience voting.** Unlike ordinary bills where party discipline may apply, the Swift Passage Procedure explicitly allows Members to vote according to their own conscience rather than along party lines.

The Swift Passage Procedure is reserved for "exceptional cases." Resolution No. 268 does not elaborate on what constitutes an exceptional case, leaving that determination to Parliament's discretion. In practice, this procedure might be invoked for bills addressing urgent policy needs that do not rise to the level of a public calamity or emergency (which would trigger the certification process under Section 3 of Rule XVIII) but still require faster-than-normal action.

There is also a separate emergency provision. Rule XVIII, Section 3 addresses bills certified by the Chief Minister in cases of public calamity or emergency:

> No bill shall become a law unless it has passed the stages of legislation except when the Chief Minister certifies to the necessity of its immediate enactment to respond to a public calamity or emergency. For this purpose, the Chief Minister shall immediately forward to the Speaker the certified measure. Within twenty-four (24) hours from receipt of the certification, the Parliament shall act for the immediate passage of an emergency bill.

The emergency certification is more extreme than the Swift Passage Procedure. It allows passage within 24 hours and is the only circumstance where a bill can become law without passing through all the ordinary stages of legislation.

For the drafter, these accelerated procedures have consequences. If your bill may be subject to Swift Passage, it needs to be impeccable at the time of filing — because there will be no extended committee period to refine it. Every definition, every penalty, every operative clause must be right from the start. You will not have the luxury of committee hearings to surface and correct problems.

## 7.9 The Order of Business

Understanding where your bill appears in the daily agenda tells you when it will be considered and what comes before and after. The Order of Business is the Parliament's daily roadmap. Rule VIII, Section 1 prescribes it in full:

> The daily Order of Business shall be as follows:
>
> (a) Call to Order;
> (b) Invocation or Prayer;
> (c) National Anthem;
> (d) Bangsamoro Hymn;
> (e) Roll Call;
> (f) Reading and Approval of Journal of the Previous Session;
> (g) Reference of Business:
> (1) Bills on First Reading; and
> (2) Resolutions, Messages, Memorials and Communications;
> (h) Bills on Second Reading:
> (1) Committee Reports on Bills;
> (2) Period of Interpellations;
> (3) Period of Amendments; and
> (4) Approval of Second Reading.
> (i) Resolutions Referred to the Committee:
> (1) Committee Reports;
> (2) Period of Interpellations;
> (3) Period of Amendments; and
> (4) Approval.
> (j) Unfinished Business;
> (k) Privilege Hour (refer to Rule IX);
> (l) Question Hour (refer to Rule XI); and
> (m) Bills on Third Reading.

Several features of this order deserve attention.

**Bills on First Reading appear under Reference of Business.** This is item (g)(1). All new bills enter Parliament's awareness at this point. The fact that First Reading is grouped under "Reference of Business" reflects its purely procedural nature — it is a matter of recording and referral, not deliberation.

**Bills on Second Reading come after.** Item (h) is where the substantive legislative work occurs during plenary. The committee report is presented, interpellation happens, amendments are proposed, and the vote on second reading is taken.

**Third Reading comes last.** Item (m) places Bills on Third Reading at the end of the Order of Business. This is significant. Third Reading votes happen after all other business has been addressed. Members have already been through the day's debates, questions, and privilege speeches before they vote on final passage. A bill that reaches Third Reading on a day when the preceding agenda has been contentious or exhausting may face a different voting environment than one considered on a calmer day.

**Unfinished Business has its own slot.** Item (j) ensures that matters left incomplete from previous sessions are taken up before new privilege speeches and question hours. If your bill was under consideration when the previous session adjourned, it resumes here.

**The Privilege Hour and Question Hour have dedicated places.** The Privilege Hour — up to sixty minutes every Thursday — is for Members to speak on matters of general interest. The Question Hour — sixty minutes every first Wednesday without a Chief Minister's Hour — is for questioning Cabinet members. Neither directly involves legislation, but both can influence the political climate around pending bills.

**The Chief Minister's Hour.** On the first Wednesday of every quarter, the Order of Business includes the Chief Minister's Hour immediately after the Roll Call. During this hour, the Chief Minister or a designated Cabinet member reports on the Government's accomplishments and status. Members may ask clarificatory questions. This periodic accountability moment can create openings for Government bills tied to the reported priorities.

The Order of Business is distributed to all Members and posted on Parliament's website at least three days before commencement of session, per Rule VIII, Section 2. Any Member may move to amend the Order of Business after the Roll Call. If no amendment is raised, the Order of Business is deemed adopted.

Plenary sessions commence at one o'clock in the afternoon on Mondays through Thursdays of the third and fourth week of the month, unless Parliament decides otherwise, per Rule VI, Section 4.

## Summary

The Bangsamoro legislative process is structured to balance speed with deliberation, executive leadership with parliamentary independence, and majority rule with minority rights. Each stage serves a purpose:

- **First Reading** introduces the bill and classifies it.
- **Second Reading** presents the bill's substance and refers it to the relevant committee.
- **Committee Stage** subjects the bill to expert scrutiny, public input, and line-by-line examination.
- **Consideration and Deliberation Stage** opens the bill to the full Parliament for debate, interpellation, and amendment.
- **Third Reading** provides a final up-or-down vote on the bill as a whole.
- **Post-passage** stages ensure proper authentication, executive review, and publication.

For you as a drafter, the most important takeaway is this: your bill does not exist in a vacuum. It will be read aloud, questioned, amended, and debated. The choices you make at the drafting table — how you structure the bill, how you word its provisions, how you frame its explanatory statement — will be tested at every stage of this process. Draft with that reality in mind.

A bill that is well-drafted does not merely survive the legislative process. It makes the process work. It gives the committee something solid to examine. It gives the sponsor something defensible to present. It gives Parliament something clear to vote on. And it gives the Bangsamoro people a law that says what it means and means what it says.
