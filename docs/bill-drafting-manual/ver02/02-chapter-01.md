# Chapter 1 — The Bangsamoro Parliament and Its Legislative Power

Before you draft a single provision, you need to understand the institution you are drafting for and the boundaries of its authority. This chapter covers the legal foundations of the Bangsamoro Parliament, the hierarchy of laws that governs the region, the scope and limits of Bangsamoro legislative power, and the role of Shari'ah in Bangsamoro legislation.

Every section here has a direct bearing on your drafting work. If the Parliament lacks authority over a subject, your bill is void. If your bill conflicts with a higher law, it will be struck down. If your provisions ignore the role of Shari'ah where it applies, they will create confusion in the courts. Get the foundations right, and the drafting that follows will stand on solid ground.

---

## 1.1 The Parliament Under the Bangsamoro Organic Law

### 1.1.1 Creation and Mandate

The Bangsamoro Parliament exists because of the Bangsamoro Organic Law — Republic Act No. 11054, signed on July 27, 2018, and ratified through a plebiscite in January and February 2019. The BOL replaced the old Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao framework established under RA 6734 and RA 9054.

The BOL's purpose is stated in Article I, Section 3:

> The purpose of this Organic Law is to establish a political entity, provide for its basic structure of government in recognition of the justness and legitimacy of the cause of the Bangsamoro people and the aspirations of Muslim Filipinos and all indigenous cultural communities in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao to secure their identity and posterity, allowing for meaningful self-governance within the framework of the Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines.

Two phrases in that provision matter for drafters. First, "meaningful self-governance" — the BOL grants real legislative authority, not merely advisory power. When the Parliament enacts a BAA within its competence, that BAA carries the force of law. Second, "within the framework of the Constitution and the national sovereignty as well as territorial integrity of the Republic of the Philippines" — the Parliament's authority is genuine, but it operates inside constitutional limits. It is not a sovereign legislature. It is an autonomous one.

The BOL adopted a parliamentary form of government for the Bangsamoro. Article IV, Section 3 states:

> The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall have a democratic political system that allows its people to freely participate in the political processes within its territorial jurisdiction. The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall have a parliamentary form of government.

This is the only region in the Philippines that uses a parliamentary system. The distinction matters. In a parliamentary system, the executive and legislative powers are fused. The Chief Minister is elected by the Parliament from among its members. The Cabinet is drawn from the Parliament. Legislative and executive authority flow from the same body.

Article VII, Section 2 makes this fusion of powers explicit:

> The powers of government shall be vested in the Parliament which shall exercise those powers and functions expressly granted to it in this Organic Law, and those necessary for, or incidental to, the proper governance and development of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It shall set policies, legislate on matters within its authority, and elect a Chief Minister who shall exercise executive authority on its behalf.

For the drafter, the practical effect is this: when you draft a bill, you are not writing for a legislature that merely passes laws and hands them off to a separate, co-equal executive. You are writing for an institution where the Chief Minister and the majority of the Cabinet sit as Members of Parliament, where executive priorities and legislative action are closely linked, and where the parliamentary majority that passes a bill is often the same group that will implement it. This creates opportunities for tighter coordination between law and implementation — but it also means your draft must be precise about which functions are legislative and which are executive.

### 1.1.2 Composition and Structure

**Size and Seat Allocation**

The BOL sets the Parliament at eighty members. Article VII, Section 6 is brief:

> The Parliament shall be composed of eighty (80) members, unless otherwise increased by the Congress of the Philippines.

Note the qualifier. Only Congress can increase the number of seats — the Bangsamoro Parliament cannot expand its own membership.

Those eighty seats are divided into three categories under Article VII, Section 7:

- **Party Representatives** — one-half of the members (40 seats), elected through proportional representation based on the entire Bangsamoro territorial jurisdiction. Registered regional political parties submit ranked lists. Seats are allocated based on the percentage of votes each party receives.

- **Parliamentary District Representatives** — not more than forty percent (40%) of the members (up to 32 seats), elected from single-member parliamentary districts through direct plurality vote. The BOL assigns the initial redistricting function to the BTA, and grants the Parliament the power to redistrict thereafter.

- **Reserved Seats and Sectoral Representatives** — at least ten percent (10%) of the members (at least 8 seats). This category includes two reserved seats each for non-Moro indigenous peoples and settler communities, plus one sectoral seat each for women, youth, traditional leaders, and the Ulama.

The allocation of parliamentary district seats has been the subject of significant legislative and judicial activity. The BTA enacted BAA 58 and later BAA 77 to establish parliamentary districts. Both were struck down by the Supreme Court in October 2025 (discussed in Section 1.3.5 below). The BTA subsequently enacted BAA 86, the Parliamentary District Act of January 2026, which created 32 single-member districts distributed across the region: Lanao del Sur (9 districts), Maguindanao del Norte (5), Maguindanao del Sur (5), Basilan (4), Tawi-Tawi (4), Cotabato City (3), and the Special Geographic Area (2).

**The Transition Period**

During the transition period, the Bangsamoro Parliament operates as the Bangsamoro Transition Authority — an interim body that exercises both legislative and executive powers. Article XVI, Section 2 of the BOL created the BTA:

> There is hereby created a Bangsamoro Transition Authority which shall be the interim government in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region during the transition period. The Moro Islamic Liberation Front shall lead the Bangsamoro Transition Authority, without prejudice to the participation of the Moro National Liberation Front in its membership.

The BTA's members are appointed by the President. The BOL provides for eighty members, though the actual number may differ based on judicial rulings and legislative developments (see Section 1.3.5 on the Sulu ruling).

Resolution No. 268 (Rule III, Section 3) prescribes eight Deputy Speakers. In practice, the BTA has elected additional Deputy Speakers beyond the prescribed eight. The Parliament also elects a Speaker, a Majority Floor Leader, and a Minority Floor Leader among its officers.

For drafting purposes, the BTA is treated as the Parliament. Article XVI, Section 3 of the BOL states:

> For purposes of mechanisms for intergovernmental relations with the National Government and local government units in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, the Bangsamoro Transition Authority shall be deemed as the Bangsamoro Government for the duration of the transition period.

The BTA exercises the full legislative power of the Parliament. BAAs enacted during the transition carry the same legal force as those enacted by a future elected Parliament. Your drafts should work for both — write timeless provisions that do not depend on whether the body enacting them is a transitional or an elected one.

**Term of Office**

Under Article VII, Section 11, members of the elected Parliament will serve three-year terms with a limit of three consecutive terms:

> The term of office of the members of the Parliament shall be three (3) years: *Provided*, That no member shall serve for more than three (3) consecutive terms. Voluntary renunciation of office for any length of time or the dissolution of the Parliament by the *Wali* shall not be considered as an interruption in the continuity of the service for the full term for which the member was elected.

**Qualifications**

Article VII, Section 12 sets the qualifications for membership:

> No person shall be a member of the Parliament unless the person is a citizen of the Philippines, at least twenty-five (25) years of age on the day of the election, able to read and write, and a registered voter in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.

**The Speaker and Officers**

On the first day of each session, members elect a Speaker, a Deputy Speaker, and other officers by majority vote of all members (Article VII, Section 23). The Speaker presides over the Parliament. In practice, the BTA has elected multiple Deputy Speakers.

**Key Procedural Rules from the BOL**

Several procedural rules are embedded directly in the BOL rather than left to the Parliament's own rules. These bind the drafter:

- **One-subject rule.** Article VII, Section 25(a): "Every bill passed by the Parliament shall embrace only one subject which shall be expressed in the title thereof." This is not optional. A bill that bundles unrelated subjects, or whose title does not reflect its content, is vulnerable to constitutional challenge.

- **Three-readings rule.** Article VII, Section 25(b): "No bill shall become a law in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region unless it has passed three (3) readings on separate days and printed copies thereof in its final form have been distributed to members of the Parliament three (3) days before its passage, except when the Chief Minister certifies to the necessity of its immediate enactment to meet a public calamity or emergency."

- **Presentment to the Chief Minister.** Article VII, Section 25(c): "Every bill passed by the Parliament shall, before it becomes a law, be presented to the Chief Minister. The Chief Minister shall sign the bill within thirty (30) days after the date of its receipt, otherwise, it shall become a law as if the Chief Minister had signed it." There is no veto power in the BOL's design — the Chief Minister either signs or the bill becomes law by inaction.

- **Transmission to the President and Congress.** Article VII, Section 25(d): "The Speaker of the Parliament shall, within ten (10) working days from their approval thereof, submit to the President and to the Congress of the Philippines a certified true copy of all laws and resolutions approved by the Parliament."

### 1.1.3 The Bangsamoro Autonomy Act as the Parliament's Legislative Output

The primary legislative product of the Bangsamoro Parliament is the **Bangsamoro Autonomy Act**, or BAA. This is the Bangsamoro equivalent of a Republic Act at the national level or an ordinance at the local level — though a BAA occupies a higher position than a local ordinance in the legal hierarchy.

BAAs are numbered sequentially. They range from the Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA 13) and the Electoral Code (BAA 35) to specific measures establishing offices, fixing boundaries, and creating programs.

A BAA, once signed by the Chief Minister (or once thirty days have elapsed from presentment without signature), carries the force of law within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It is enforceable by the courts, binding on government officials, and applicable to all persons within the region's territorial jurisdiction to the extent it falls within the Parliament's legislative competence.

The Parliament also passes resolutions. These are generally used for internal matters (adopting rules, expressing the sense of the Parliament) or for actions that do not require the force of law. Resolutions are not presented to the Chief Minister for signature. The distinction between a bill (which becomes a BAA) and a resolution matters for your drafting — if the measure requires legal force and imposes obligations or confers rights, it must be enacted as a BAA.

The numbering and cataloguing of BAAs is straightforward but worth noting. BAAs are numbered sequentially from BAA No. 1 onward. There is no numbering reset between BTA compositions — a BAA enacted by the third BTA continues the sequence from the second BTA. This reflects a principle of legislative continuity. Under the BTA system, bills filed during one composition were not archived when a new composition was appointed; they continued to be deliberated by the succeeding composition without refiling. Whether this continuity principle will carry over to the elected Parliament is a question that the Parliament's Rules of Procedure will eventually need to address.

When citing a BAA in a new bill, use the full designation: "Bangsamoro Autonomy Act No. [number]." For well-known codes, the short title is acceptable after the first reference — for example, "the Bangsamoro Administrative Code" after first identifying it as BAA No. 13.

## 1.2 The Hierarchy of Laws in the Bangsamoro

One of the drafter's first tasks on any bill is to identify which higher laws apply to the subject matter. Bangsamoro legislation operates within a layered legal structure. If your BAA conflicts with any higher layer, it is void to the extent of the conflict. Understanding this hierarchy is not academic — it is a daily drafting requirement.

### 1.2.1 The 1987 Constitution

The 1987 Philippine Constitution sits at the top. No BAA may violate any constitutional provision. The relevant constitutional provisions for Bangsamoro legislation include:

- **Article X (Local Government)**, particularly Section 15 (creation of autonomous regions in Muslim Mindanao and the Cordilleras), Section 18 (Congress shall enact an organic act; only areas voting favorably in a plebiscite shall be included), and Section 20 (scope of organic act provisions).
- **Article VI** (legislative power generally), **Article III** (Bill of Rights), **Article II** (declaration of principles), and **Article XIV** (education, science, culture, and sports) — all of which constrain what the Parliament may enact.
- The **due process** and **equal protection** clauses (Article III, Sections 1 and 2) apply to all Bangsamoro legislation just as they apply to national legislation.

When you draft a bill, run it against the Constitution first. If a provision restricts a constitutional right, you need a compelling basis in the BOL or the Constitution itself. If a provision creates a classification (this group gets a benefit; that group does not), you need a rational basis for the distinction.

### 1.2.2 The Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054)

The BOL is the second layer. It is the source of the Parliament's authority, and it also limits that authority. A BAA may not contradict the BOL. If the BOL requires something — that every bill embrace only one subject, for instance — the BAA must comply.

The BOL also contains substantive mandates that constrain your drafting. For example:

- The BOL guarantees religious freedom (Article IX, Section 5). A BAA that restricts religious practice would conflict with the organic law itself, not just the Constitution.
- The BOL guarantees the rights of non-Moro indigenous peoples (Article IV, Section 9; Article IX, Section 3). A BAA that diminishes those rights violates the organic law.
- The BOL protects existing local government privileges (Article VI, Section 10): "The privileges already enjoyed by local government units under Republic Act No. 7160 ... shall not be diminished."

Treat the BOL as your drafting constitution. Before you write any provision that touches a subject covered by the BOL, read the relevant BOL provisions. If your bill is consistent with them, proceed. If it is not, revise the bill — not the BOL.

### 1.2.3 National Laws and Their Implementing Rules and Regulations

National laws enacted by Congress occupy the third layer. The relationship between national law and Bangsamoro law is more complex than simple hierarchy, because the BOL carves out areas of exclusive Bangsamoro authority. In those areas, the Parliament legislates without being overridden by ordinary national legislation. In areas of shared or overlapping authority — and there are many — national law and Bangsamoro law must be harmonized.

Several national laws apply directly to the Bangsamoro. The BOL itself references them:

- Republic Act No. 7160 (Local Government Code of 1991) continues to apply to constituent LGUs, and its privileges "shall not be diminished."
- The Civil Service Commission retains authority; the Bangsamoro Civil Service Code must be "in accordance with existing national laws" (Article VII, Section 39).
- The Commission on Audit remains the exclusive auditor of the Bangsamoro Government (Article XII, Section 2).
- The Commission on Elections, through the Bangsamoro Electoral Office, administers elections (Article VII, Section 40).

Implementing rules and regulations (IRRs) issued by national agencies to implement national laws also apply in the Bangsamoro to the extent the underlying national law applies. A BAA cannot override a national agency's IRR in an area of national authority. But in areas of Bangsamoro authority, the Parliament can enact its own implementing frameworks.

The practical rule for drafters: when your bill touches a subject where national law also applies, identify the national law, read it, and draft your bill to work alongside it — not to contradict it.

### 1.2.4 Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts

BAAs sit below the Constitution, the BOL, and applicable national laws. They are the Parliament's primary output. Within the hierarchy, a BAA enacted later in time can amend or repeal an earlier BAA, subject to the usual rules: express repeal is preferred over implied repeal, specific provisions prevail over general ones, and repealing clauses must be clear.

Among BAAs, some carry special weight because the BOL specifically mandated their enactment. Article XVI, Section 4(a) of the BOL names five priority codes: the Bangsamoro Administrative Code, Revenue Code, Electoral Code, Local Government Code, and Education Code. The same section adds that the BTA "may also enact a Bangsamoro Civil Service Code." Beyond these, the Investment Code has been identified as a priority based on broader governance and economic development needs.

The status of the priority codes:

1. Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13, enacted 2020)
2. Bangsamoro Civil Service Code (BAA No. 17, enacted 2021)
3. Bangsamoro Education Code (BAA No. 18, enacted 2021)
4. Bangsamoro Electoral Code (BAA No. 35, enacted 2023)
5. Bangsamoro Local Government Code (BAA No. 49, enacted 2023)
6. Bangsamoro Revenue Code (pending)
7. Bangsamoro Investment Code (pending)

These codes form the backbone of the Bangsamoro legal system. When you draft a bill that touches a subject covered by an existing code, your bill must be consistent with the code — or must expressly amend it. Do not create silent conflicts between your bill and an existing code.

### 1.2.5 Executive Orders, Administrative Orders, and Regulations

Below BAAs sit the executive issuances of the Bangsamoro Government. The Chief Minister issues executive orders to implement BAAs and direct the bureaucracy. Ministers issue administrative orders and department circulars within their areas of responsibility.

These issuances cannot contradict the BAA they implement. They cannot expand the scope of a BAA beyond what the Parliament intended. They cannot impose requirements that the BAA does not authorize.

For the drafter, this layer matters when you write delegation clauses. When your bill says "the Minister shall issue the implementing rules and regulations," you are authorizing executive action. Be clear about what you are delegating. Specify standards if the subject is important. Set deadlines if implementation is time-sensitive. A vague delegation clause produces unpredictable IRRs.

### 1.2.6 Local Ordinances

At the bottom of the hierarchy are the ordinances of constituent local government units — provinces, cities, municipalities, and barangays within the Bangsamoro. These ordinances cannot contradict any BAA, any national law, the BOL, or the Constitution.

When your bill affects local government operations — and many bills do — consider how it will interact with existing local ordinances. If your BAA creates a new regulatory framework, it may implicitly repeal conflicting local ordinances. Make this explicit if that is the intent. If local governments are expected to pass implementing ordinances of their own, say so and give them a timeframe.

## 1.3 Scope and Limitations of Bangsamoro Legislative Power

This section is the drafter's map. It tells you where the Parliament's authority reaches and where it stops. Read it carefully. Refer back to it whenever a proposed bill raises jurisdictional questions — and many will.

### 1.3.1 Enumerated Powers (BOL Article V, Section 2)

The Bangsamoro Parliament's primary source of legislative authority is Article V, Section 2 of the BOL, which lists fifty-five subject areas over which the Bangsamoro Government exercises authority. The provision reads:

> Subject to Section 20, Article X of the Constitution and this Organic Law, the Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters without prejudice to the general supervision of the President of the Republic of the Philippines:

The enumerated powers are listed alphabetically from (a) to (ccc). They cover a broad range of subjects. Here are the fifty-five items:

This is a long list, and it is your starting point for any bill. When a Member or committee asks you to draft legislation on a particular subject, your first question should be: does this subject fall within one of these fifty-five items? If yes, the Parliament has authority. If not, check the general welfare clause (Section 1.3.2) and the residual clause (Section 1.3.3).

A few observations for the drafter:

- The list is broad but not unlimited. Some subjects that might seem natural for regional legislation — criminal law, national defense, foreign affairs, currency, customs — are not on the list. They belong to the National Government.
- Some items overlap with national authority. "Labor, employment, and occupation" is an enumerated Bangsamoro power, but the national Labor Code also applies. "Education and skills training" is enumerated, but DepEd, CHED, and TESDA maintain national jurisdiction. In these areas, the drafter must harmonize Bangsamoro legislation with national law.
- Some items are quite specific ("Hajj and Umrah," "business name registration"), while others are broad ("administration of justice," "trade and industry"). The broad items give the Parliament significant room, but that room is bounded by other provisions of the BOL and the Constitution.

### 1.3.2 The General Welfare Clause (BOL Article V, Section 3)

Beyond the fifty-five enumerated powers, the Parliament has authority under the general welfare clause. Article V, Section 3 provides:

> The Bangsamoro Government shall exercise the powers expressly granted, those necessarily implied therefrom, as well as powers necessary, appropriate, or incidental for its efficient and effective governance and those which are essential to the promotion of general welfare. Within its territorial jurisdiction, the Bangsamoro Government shall ensure and support, among other things, the preservation and enrichment of culture, promote health and safety, enhance the right of the people to a balanced ecology, encourage and support the development of appropriate and self-reliant scientific and technological capabilities, improve public morals, enhance economic prosperity and social justice, promote full employment among its residents, maintain peace and order, and preserve the comfort and convenience of its inhabitants.

This clause serves three functions:

1. **Express powers.** It confirms the Parliament may exercise the powers expressly granted in the BOL.
2. **Implied powers.** It grants the Parliament powers "necessarily implied" from the express powers. If the Parliament has authority over health, it may enact health-related regulatory measures even if the specific regulatory mechanism is not named in Article V, Section 2.
3. **Incidental powers.** It grants powers "necessary, appropriate, or incidental" for efficient and effective governance. This is the broadest category.

For drafting purposes, the general welfare clause is useful when your bill falls within the spirit of the enumerated powers but does not fit neatly into one of the fifty-five items. A bill creating a regional consumer protection framework, for example, might draw on the enumerated powers over "trade and industry" and "regulation of the manufacture and distribution of food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco," supplemented by the general welfare clause's mandate to "enhance economic prosperity and social justice."

Do not rely on the general welfare clause alone for bills that are clearly outside the BOL's scope. The clause expands the enumerated powers — it does not replace them. A bill on foreign affairs, for instance, cannot be justified by the general welfare clause no matter how beneficial it might be for Bangsamoro residents.

### 1.3.3 The Residual Clause: Powers Reserved to Congress

Article V, Section 1 contains the residual clause:

> All powers, functions, and responsibilities not granted by the Constitution or by national law to the Bangsamoro Government shall be vested in the National Government.

This is the mirror image of the enumerated powers. What the BOL does not give to the Bangsamoro, the National Government retains. The residual clause means that when you cannot find authority for a bill in Article V, Sections 2 or 3 — or in any other provision of the BOL — the subject belongs to the National Government, and the Parliament cannot legislate on it.

The residual clause is your backstop analysis. When in doubt, apply it: is this power granted to the Bangsamoro by the Constitution or by national law (including the BOL, which is a national law enacted by Congress)? If yes, the Parliament may legislate. If not, it may not.

### 1.3.4 Areas of Overlapping Authority

Many of the enumerated powers overlap with subjects that national agencies also regulate. Education is a Bangsamoro power — but DepEd sets national curriculum standards. Labor is a Bangsamoro power — but the Labor Code is a national law. Disaster risk reduction and management is a Bangsamoro power — but the National DRRM Council operates under RA 10121. Health is enumerated — but DOH retained hospitals continue to be funded nationally, and PhilHealth operates under its own charter.

These overlaps are not accidental. The BOL deliberately grants the Bangsamoro authority over subjects where national legislation also exists. The intent is shared governance, not total separation. But the result is a drafting challenge: how do you write a BAA that asserts Bangsamoro authority without colliding with national law?

The BOL anticipated this problem and created intergovernmental relations mechanisms to manage it. Article VI establishes several:

- **The Intergovernmental Relations Body** (Article VI, Section 2) — the primary mechanism. It coordinates and resolves issues between the National Government and the Bangsamoro Government through "regular consultation and continuing negotiation in a non-adversarial manner." The BOL adds: "The Intergovernmental Relations Body shall exhaust all means to resolve issues brought before it. Unresolved issues shall be elevated to the President, through the Chief Minister."
- **The Philippine Congress-Bangsamoro Parliament Forum** (Article VI, Section 3) — coordinates legislative initiatives between Congress and the Parliament. When a Bangsamoro bill touches an area where congressional action may also be needed, this forum is the coordination channel.
- **The Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board** (Article VI, Section 4) — addresses fiscal matters, revenue imbalances, and shared taxation issues. Co-chaired by the Secretary of Finance and the Bangsamoro Finance Minister.
- **The Intergovernmental Infrastructure Development Board** (Article VI, Section 6) — synchronizes national and Bangsamoro infrastructure plans. Composed of Bangsamoro ministry heads and the Secretary of Public Works and Highways.
- **The Intergovernmental Energy Board** (Article VI, Section 7) — resolves energy issues between national and Bangsamoro regulatory bodies.
- **The Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board** (Article VI, Section 8) — ensures integration of economic, social, and environmental considerations. The Parliament creates it, and it includes representatives from both governments.
- **The Council of Leaders** (Article VI, Section 9) — advises the Chief Minister on governance. Its membership includes congressional representatives from the Bangsamoro, provincial governors, mayors of chartered cities, and representatives of traditional leaders, indigenous communities, women, settlers, the Ulama, and youth.

For the drafter, overlapping authority creates specific obligations:

**Acknowledge the overlap.** When your bill touches an area of shared jurisdiction, do not pretend the national law does not exist. If you are drafting an education bill, acknowledge DepEd's role. If you are drafting a labor bill, acknowledge the Labor Code. Ignoring national authority does not eliminate it — it just makes your bill look poorly researched.

**Include coordination clauses.** A bill on education should specify the relationship between the Bangsamoro Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education and the national DepEd, CHED, and TESDA. A bill on disaster risk reduction should reference the National DRRM Council and RA 10121. These clauses prevent jurisdictional confusion during implementation.

**Reference the intergovernmental mechanisms.** Consider whether the bill should include provisions for referral to the Intergovernmental Relations Body in case of disputes. For fiscal matters, the Intergovernmental Fiscal Policy Board is the appropriate body. For infrastructure, the Infrastructure Development Board. Naming the correct mechanism in your bill signals that the Parliament understands the cooperative framework.

**Draft "without prejudice" clauses carefully.** The phrase "without prejudice to national laws" appears frequently in Bangsamoro legislation. It is a signal of harmonization — the BAA is asserting Bangsamoro authority while acknowledging national authority in the same space. But use it with care. A "without prejudice" clause that is too broad can gut your bill's operative provisions. A provision that says "The Ministry shall regulate labor standards, without prejudice to the Labor Code" may mean the BAA adds nothing beyond what the Labor Code already provides. Be specific about what the BAA adds, modifies, or supplements.

**Know the hard limits.** Some overlaps are resolved by absolute prohibitions. The BOL contains one that has already caused difficulty. Article VI, Section 10 states:

> Nothing in this Organic Law shall be construed to allow the Bangsamoro Government to create legislative districts.

The Parliament may create *parliamentary* districts — these are distinct from *legislative* (congressional) districts. But the line between redistricting for parliamentary purposes and creating what amounts to a legislative district has proven contentious, as the Supreme Court's decisions on BAA 77 and BAA 58 demonstrate.

**Watch for "floor" provisions.** The BOL contains several provisions that set floors rather than ceilings. Article VI, Section 10 says that privileges already enjoyed by LGUs under the Local Government Code "shall not be diminished." This means a BAA can give LGUs *more* authority or *more* resources — but never less. When your bill regulates local government operations, check it against the LGC floor.

### 1.3.5 Judicial Doctrines Affecting Bangsamoro Legislation

Two recent Supreme Court decisions have reshaped the boundaries of Bangsamoro legislative power. Every drafter should know them.

**Province of Sulu v. Medialdea (G.R. Nos. 242255, 243246, 243693) — September 9, 2024**

This case challenged the inclusion of Sulu in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The Province of Sulu voted against ratification of the BOL in the 2019 plebiscite — 54.3% voted "No." Despite this, the COMELEC initially counted Sulu as part of the BARMM because the overall vote in the combined ARMM territory favored ratification.

The Supreme Court ruled that Sulu is not part of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The Court held that Article X, Section 18 of the Constitution requires that "only the provinces, cities, and geographic areas voting favorably" shall be included in the autonomous region. Since Sulu voted against the BOL, it could not be compelled to join.

The Court upheld the BOL itself as constitutional but applied the **doctrine of operative fact** — all acts performed by the Bangsamoro Government in Sulu before the decision were presumed valid.

The ruling became final on November 26, 2024. President Marcos subsequently issued Executive Order No. 91 (July 30, 2025), realigning Sulu under Region IX (Zamboanga Peninsula).

**What this means for the drafter:** The Bangsamoro Parliament has no legislative authority over Sulu. BAAs do not apply there. When you draft a bill, do not include Sulu in the territorial scope. The current BTA has 77 members, not 80, because the three Sulu seats were eliminated. Any bill that defines geographic coverage of a program or institution should reference the current territorial composition of the BARMM, which excludes Sulu.

**Ali et al. v. BTA (G.R. No. E-02219) and Macapaar et al. v. COMELEC (G.R. No. E-02235) — October 1, 2025 and September 30, 2025**

These consolidated cases challenged BAA 77 and BAA 58, the BTA's two attempts at redistricting. The Supreme Court declared both BAAs unconstitutional.

BAA 58 was the first redistricting law enacted by the BTA. BAA 77 replaced it. Both attempted to define parliamentary districts for the first Bangsamoro elections. The Supreme Court found constitutional defects in the redistricting — the specific grounds involved the standards for redistricting, population requirements, and the boundaries drawn.

The BTA responded by enacting BAA 86 on January 20, 2026. BAA 86 established 32 single-member parliamentary districts: Lanao del Sur (9), Maguindanao del Norte (5), Maguindanao del Sur (5), Basilan (4), Tawi-Tawi (4), Cotabato City (3), and the Special Geographic Area (2).

**What this means for the drafter:** The Supreme Court is actively reviewing Bangsamoro legislation. The Court will strike down BAAs that exceed the Parliament's authority or fail to comply with constitutional standards. When you draft a bill that touches sensitive constitutional territory — redistricting, taxation, property rights, the creation or alteration of political boundaries — take extra care. Research whether the Supreme Court has addressed similar issues. Build your constitutional arguments into the bill's framework from the start.

These two decisions also reinforce a broader point: Bangsamoro legislative power, while substantial, is subject to judicial review. The Supreme Court has jurisdiction over all Bangsamoro legislation, and it will exercise that jurisdiction when asked.

### 1.3.6 What This Means for the Drafter

Pull these threads together, and you have a practical framework for assessing any proposed bill:

**Step 1 — Check the enumerated powers.** Does the bill fall within one of the fifty-five subjects listed in Article V, Section 2? If yes, the Parliament has primary authority. Identify the specific item.

**Step 2 — Check for implied or incidental authority.** If the bill does not fit neatly into one of the fifty-five items, does it fall within the general welfare clause of Article V, Section 3? Is the proposed legislation "necessarily implied" from an enumerated power, or "necessary, appropriate, or incidental" for effective governance? If yes, proceed — but document your reasoning.

**Step 3 — Apply the residual clause.** If neither Step 1 nor Step 2 yields authority, the residual clause of Article V, Section 1 applies. The power belongs to the National Government. Stop drafting. Advise the requesting Member or committee that the subject is outside the Parliament's competence.

**Step 4 — Check for constitutional limits.** Even if the Parliament has authority, the Constitution still applies. Does the bill comply with the Bill of Rights? Does it respect due process and equal protection? Does it comply with constitutional requirements for organic legislation (Article X)?

**Step 5 — Check for conflicts with the BOL.** Does the bill conflict with any substantive provision of the BOL? Does it comply with the procedural requirements (one-subject rule, three-readings rule)?

**Step 6 — Check for conflicts with national law.** Does the bill touch an area where national law also applies? If so, is the bill consistent with national law, or does it conflict? If there is a conflict, which law prevails — and how can you draft the bill to avoid the conflict altogether?

**Step 7 — Check for conflicts with existing BAAs.** Does the bill conflict with an existing BAA, including the priority codes? If so, draft an express amendment or repealing clause.

**Step 8 — Consider judicial precedent.** Has the Supreme Court addressed the subject? Are there existing rulings — like *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea* or *Ali et al. v. BTA* — that affect the analysis?

This eight-step framework is not a checklist you fill out once and file away. It is a way of thinking about every bill you draft. The best drafters run this analysis instinctively. Until it becomes instinct, run it deliberately.

**A worked example.** Suppose a committee asks you to draft a bill establishing a Bangsamoro Halal Certification Authority with the power to certify products, inspect food establishments, and impose fines for fraudulent halal labeling.

- *Step 1:* Enumerated powers — "regulation of the manufacture and distribution of food, drinks, drugs, and tobacco" (tt), "trade and industry" (zz), "Hajj and Umrah" (aa) for related religious standards. Authority exists.
- *Step 2:* Not needed — enumerated powers cover it.
- *Step 3:* Not needed.
- *Step 4:* Constitutional check — does the bill impose a classification? Yes: it regulates halal certification, which is tied to Islamic standards. Does it respect religious freedom? It must. Non-Muslim producers who do not seek halal certification should not be penalized. The bill should regulate the *use of the halal label*, not mandate that all food meet halal standards.
- *Step 5:* BOL check — does it conflict with BOL provisions on religious freedom (Article IX, Section 5) or the rights of non-Muslims? Draft it to ensure it does not.
- *Step 6:* National law check — does the National Meat Inspection Service or the Food and Drug Administration have existing halal regulations? If so, draft the bill to supplement rather than contradict them. Include a coordination clause with the relevant national agency.
- *Step 7:* Existing BAA check — has the Parliament enacted any measure on halal certification already? If so, amend it rather than creating a parallel framework.
- *Step 8:* Judicial precedent — no known rulings on Bangsamoro halal authority, but the general principles from *Sulu v. Medialdea* about respecting constitutional limits apply.

That analysis takes fifteen minutes. It saves months of litigation.

## 1.4 Shari'ah in Bangsamoro Legislation

The role of Shari'ah in the Bangsamoro legal system is established by the BOL itself. It is not a matter of political preference or cultural symbolism. The BOL embeds Shari'ah into the constitutional framework of the region. A drafter who ignores it will produce incomplete legislation. A drafter who misapplies it will produce unconstitutional legislation. This section gives you the foundations you need to handle Shari'ah-related provisions correctly.

### 1.4.1 BOL Provisions on Shari'ah

Article X of the BOL establishes the justice system in the Bangsamoro, and Shari'ah is central to it. Section 1 sets the frame:

> The Bangsamoro justice system shall be administered in accordance with the unique cultural and historical heritage of the Bangsamoro.
>
> The dispensation of justice in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region shall be in consonance with the Constitution, *Shari'ah*, traditional or tribal laws, and other relevant laws.
>
> *Shari'ah* or Islamic law forms part of the Islamic tradition derived from religious precepts of Islam, particularly the *Qur'an* and *Sunnah*.
>
> *Shari'ah* shall apply exclusively to cases involving Muslims. Where a case involves a non-Muslim, *Shari'ah* law may apply only if the non-Muslim voluntarily submits to the jurisdiction of the *Shari'ah* court.
>
> The traditional or tribal laws shall be applicable to disputes of indigenous peoples within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.
>
> The provisions of this Article shall not prejudice the rights of non-Muslims and nonindigenous peoples.

This provision establishes several principles that bind the drafter:

1. Shari'ah is a recognized source of law in the Bangsamoro, alongside the Constitution, national laws, and traditional or tribal laws.
2. Shari'ah applies *exclusively* to cases involving Muslims. This is not discretionary — the word is "exclusively."
3. Non-Muslims are not subject to Shari'ah unless they *voluntarily submit* to Shari'ah court jurisdiction.
4. The rights of non-Muslims and non-indigenous peoples are protected from prejudice.

The BOL identifies the sources of Shari'ah in Article X, Section 3:

> The following are the sources of *Shari'ah*:
>
> Principal Sources:
>
> (a) *Al-Qur'an* or the Koran;
>
> (b) *Al-Sunnah* or traditions of Prophet Muhammad SAW;
>
> Secondary Sources:
>
> (a) *Al-Ijma* or Consensus; and
>
> (b) *Al-Qiyas* or Analogy.

This formal enumeration of sources matters for drafting. When your bill invokes Shari'ah principles, those principles must be traceable to these recognized sources. The Parliament's legislative power regarding Shari'ah is defined in Article X, Section 4:

> The Parliament shall have the power to enact laws on personal, family, and property law jurisdiction.
>
> The Parliament has the power to enact laws governing commercial and other civil actions not provided for under Presidential Decree No. 1083, as amended, otherwise known as the *"Code of Muslim Personal Laws of the Philippines,"* and criminal jurisdiction on minor offenses punishable by *arresto menor* or *ta'zir* which must be equivalent to *arresto menor* or fines commensurate to the offense.

This is both a grant of power and a limitation. The Parliament can legislate on:
- Personal law (marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody, family relations)
- Family law
- Property law
- Commercial and civil actions not already covered by PD 1083 (the Code of Muslim Personal Laws)
- Criminal jurisdiction limited to minor offenses — *arresto menor* or the Shari'ah equivalent (*ta'zir* at the *arresto menor* level)

The Parliament *cannot* expand Shari'ah criminal jurisdiction beyond minor offenses. This is a hard limit. A bill that imposes imprisonment beyond *arresto menor* through a Shari'ah framework exceeds the BOL's grant of power.

**The Shari'ah Court System**

The BOL creates a three-tiered Shari'ah court system:

- **Shari'ah Circuit Courts** (Article X, Section 5) — jurisdiction over personal law disputes, minor offenses, communal property disputes, and civil actions within specified monetary thresholds.
- **Shari'ah District Courts** (Article X, Section 6) — jurisdiction over custody, guardianship, estate settlement, and higher-value civil actions, plus appellate jurisdiction over Circuit Court decisions.
- **Shari'ah High Court** (Article X, Section 7) — the highest Shari'ah court in the Bangsamoro, composed of five justices, with appellate jurisdiction over District Court decisions. Its decisions are "final and executory except on questions of law which may be raised before the Supreme Court following the procedure for appeals from the Court of Appeals to the Supreme Court."

All Shari'ah courts form part of the Philippine judicial system, subject to the supervision of the Supreme Court (Article X, Section 2). The Parliament does not control Shari'ah court appointments — the Supreme Court appoints Shari'ah court personnel (Article X, Section 14), while Shari'ah judges are appointed through the Judicial and Bar Council process under the Constitution.

### 1.4.2 When Shari'ah Considerations Apply

For the drafter, the critical question is: when does Shari'ah apply to the subject of my bill?

**Shari'ah applies when:**

- The bill governs personal status matters (marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody, family relations) and the persons affected are Muslims.
- The bill governs property relations between Muslims.
- The bill creates or modifies jurisdiction for Shari'ah courts.
- The bill addresses commercial or civil matters between Muslims that are not covered by PD 1083.
- The bill imposes penalties on minor offenses through a Shari'ah framework (*ta'zir* at *arresto menor* level).

**Shari'ah does not apply when:**

- The bill governs secular matters that affect all residents regardless of religion — taxation, public works, environmental protection, transportation, education (outside of Madaris provisions), and similar subjects.
- The persons affected are not Muslim, unless a non-Muslim has voluntarily submitted to Shari'ah court jurisdiction.
- The bill involves criminal offenses beyond *arresto menor*.
- The subject falls outside the Parliament's Shari'ah legislative authority under Article X, Section 4.

**Gray areas exist.** Some bills touch both secular governance and matters where Shari'ah has a role. These mixed-jurisdiction bills are among the hardest to draft correctly.

Consider a few examples:

- *Inheritance taxation.* A revenue bill that taxes inherited property involves fiscal policy — a secular function. But for Muslims, the rules of inheritance are governed by Shari'ah, which prescribes specific shares for heirs (*faraid*). The drafter must write the tax provisions so they apply regardless of how the estate is divided, without interfering with the Shari'ah division itself. The tax falls on the value transferred, not on the religious rules governing the transfer.

- *Marriage registration.* A bill standardizing the registration of births, marriages, and deaths involves civil registration — an enumerated Bangsamoro power (Article V, Section 2[ss]). But Muslim marriages performed under Shari'ah have their own requirements (consent, *mahr*, witnesses). The drafter must ensure that registration requirements do not impose conditions that conflict with Shari'ah marriage law. At the same time, the bill should not exempt Muslim marriages from registration — civil records serve public purposes (vital statistics, property rights, citizenship documentation) that are distinct from religious validity.

- *Women's protection.* A bill on violence against women raises questions about how Shari'ah family law interacts with national anti-violence statutes (RA 9262, the Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act). The BOL's women's rights provisions (Article IX, Sections 11 and 12) are strong. But the drafter must be careful not to create penalties or procedures that conflict with Shari'ah court jurisdiction over family matters.

In each of these cases, the answer is not to choose one framework over the other. It is to draft provisions that let both frameworks operate in their proper domains, with clear boundaries and explicit coordination provisions where the domains overlap.

### 1.4.3 Practical Guidance for Drafters

When you draft a bill that involves or could involve Shari'ah, follow these guidelines:

**1. Identify the Shari'ah dimension early.** During policy consultation, ask: does this bill affect personal law, family law, or property relations among Muslims? If the answer is yes — even partially — you need to account for Shari'ah in your draft.

**2. Preserve the exclusivity principle.** The BOL says Shari'ah applies "exclusively to cases involving Muslims." Your bill should not extend Shari'ah to non-Muslims, and it should not exempt Muslims from Shari'ah in matters of personal law. Respect the boundary in both directions.

**3. Reference PD 1083 correctly.** Presidential Decree No. 1083, the Code of Muslim Personal Laws, predates the BOL and remains in force. The BOL's grant of Shari'ah legislative power to the Parliament covers matters "not provided for" under PD 1083, plus the ability to amend or supplement PD 1083's provisions through BAAs. When your bill addresses a subject already covered by PD 1083, be clear about whether you are supplementing, amending, or working alongside the existing code.

**4. Draft Shari'ah-sensitive provisions with care.** When a bill includes a provision rooted in Shari'ah, state the basis clearly. If a provision defines the rules of inheritance for Muslims, for example, identify the Shari'ah basis. This is not just good practice — it helps courts interpret the provision correctly and helps the Darul Ifta (the Bangsamoro's official Islamic legal opinion body) advise on implementation.

**5. Include savings clauses for non-Muslims.** When a bill touches personal or family law, include a savings clause making clear that non-Muslims are governed by the Civil Code or other applicable law. This protects the rights guaranteed by Article X, Section 1 of the BOL: "The provisions of this Article shall not prejudice the rights of non-Muslims and nonindigenous peoples."

**6. Coordinate with the Shari'ah court system.** If your bill creates rights, obligations, or procedures that will be enforced in Shari'ah courts, make sure the provisions are consistent with the jurisdiction and procedures of those courts. The Shari'ah courts are part of the Philippine judicial system under the Supreme Court's supervision — the Parliament cannot alter their procedures without coordinating with the judiciary.

**7. Consult the Bangsamoro Darul Ifta.** When a bill raises questions about the correct interpretation of Shari'ah — whether a particular provision is consistent with the Qur'an and Sunnah, or whether an *ijma* (consensus) exists on a particular point — the Darul Ifta is the appropriate body to consult. The drafter is not expected to be a Shari'ah scholar. But the drafter is expected to know when to seek expert guidance.

**8. Do not mix secular enforcement with Shari'ah sanctions.** Keep the two systems clear. If a violation carries a penalty under general law (imprisonment, fines under the Revised Penal Code framework), do not layer a separate Shari'ah penalty on the same act unless the BOL specifically authorizes it. Dual penalties for the same conduct create due process problems.

**9. Use the correct terminology.** Shari'ah terms should be rendered accurately and consistently. Use *Shari'ah* (with the apostrophe and italics, following BOL convention), not "Shariah" or "Sharia." Use *Qur'an* and *Sunnah* with correct transliteration. Use *ta'zir*, *mahr*, *ijma*, and *qiyas* correctly. If a term needs definition for legal purposes, define it in the definitions section of your bill.

**10. Remember: the Constitution governs.** Shari'ah in the Bangsamoro operates within the constitutional framework. No BAA can invoke Shari'ah to override the Constitution. The Bill of Rights applies to all persons in the Bangsamoro, regardless of religion. Due process and equal protection are constitutional guarantees that no Shari'ah-based provision may abrogate.
