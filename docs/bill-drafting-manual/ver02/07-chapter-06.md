# Chapter 6 — Legislative Style

Every Bangsamoro Autonomy Act you draft will be read by people who did not write it and did not participate in the deliberations that produced it. Ministry officials will read it to figure out what they must do. Citizens will read it to know their rights. Lawyers will read it to advise clients. Judges will read it to settle disputes. Your job is to write so that all of them understand the same thing.

Legislative style is not about elegance. It is about precision. A novel can be ambiguous and still be great. A law that is ambiguous has failed.

This chapter is your daily reference. It covers everything from sentence structure to punctuation to the specific words you should and should not use when drafting Bangsamoro legislation.

---

## 6.1 Writing for the Reader

### 6.1.1 The Implementer's Perspective

The first reader of a BAA is not the Member of Parliament who sponsored it. It is the ministry official who must implement it.

When a BAA creates a new program, someone at a ministry must figure out: What exactly does this law require my office to do? By when? With what resources? What happens if we do not comply? If your draft does not answer those questions clearly, the implementer will either do nothing, do the wrong thing, or spend months seeking clarification.

Put yourself in the implementer's position. As you draft each provision, ask:

- **Who must act?** Is the responsible body named — not vaguely described, but specifically identified?
- **What must they do?** Is the action stated in concrete terms — not aspirational language, but a specific obligation?
- **When must they do it?** Is there a deadline or timeline?
- **How will they know they have complied?** Are the standards of compliance measurable?
- **What happens if they fail?** Is there a consequence?

If you cannot answer these questions by reading the provision, neither can the implementer.

**Example — vague vs. clear:**

| Vague | Clear |
|---|---|
| The Ministry of Health shall take steps to improve healthcare in the region. | The Ministry of Health shall establish at least one barangay health station in every municipality that does not have one within one year from the effectivity of this Act. |

The first version tells the ministry to do something good. The second tells it exactly what to do and by when.

### 6.1.2 The Citizen's Perspective

The second reader is the citizen affected by the law. This person may not be a lawyer. They may not speak English as a first language. They may have limited formal education. They still have a right to understand the laws that govern them.

This does not mean you should write legislation at a fourth-grade reading level. Legal precision sometimes requires technical language. But it does mean:

- Prefer short sentences over long ones.
- Prefer common words over rare ones.
- Prefer direct statements over indirect constructions.
- Prefer active voice over passive voice.
- Define technical terms before using them.

When you have a choice between a word that a lawyer would use and a word that everyone would understand, choose the word everyone would understand — unless the legal term has a specific meaning that no common word can capture.

## 6.2 Clarity and Simplicity

### 6.2.1 Plain Language in Legislation

Plain language in legislation means writing that is clear enough for the intended audience to find what they need, understand what they find, and use what they understand.

**Rules for plain language in Bangsamoro legislation:**

1. **Use short sentences.** A sentence should express one idea. If you find yourself using semicolons to connect three or four clauses, break the sentence into separate sentences.

2. **Use common words.** Write "before" not "prior to." Write "after" not "subsequent to." Write "about" not "with respect to." Write "under" not "pursuant to" (except in formal citations). Write "must" or "shall" not "is obligated to."

3. **Use concrete words.** Write "the Ministry of Education" not "the appropriate agency." Write "within 30 days" not "within a reasonable period." Write "submit a written report" not "provide the necessary documentation."

4. **Avoid nominalizations.** Nominalizations turn verbs into nouns and make sentences heavier. Write "the Minister shall decide" not "the Minister shall make a determination." Write "the board shall investigate" not "the board shall conduct an investigation." Write "the agency shall implement" not "the agency shall undertake the implementation of."

5. **Cut unnecessary words.** Legislative drafting generates verbal clutter. Every word should earn its place.

| Wordy | Direct |
|---|---|
| for the purpose of | to |
| in order to | to |
| with regard to | about; regarding |
| in the event that | if |
| at such time as | when |
| in the amount of | for |
| by reason of | because |
| on the part of | by |
| in accordance with | under |
| during the period of | during |
| notwithstanding the fact that | although; even though |
| in cases where | where; when |
| it shall be the duty of the Minister to | the Minister shall |
| there is hereby created | this Act creates |
| is authorized and empowered to | may |

### 6.2.2 Avoiding Ambiguity

Ambiguity in legislation creates lawsuits, administrative confusion, and uneven enforcement. There are three types of ambiguity to guard against.

**Syntactic ambiguity** — the sentence structure allows two different readings.

> *"The Minister shall appoint a representative of the Ministry and the local government unit."*

Does this mean one person who represents both? Or one representative of the Ministry plus one representative of the LGU? Rewrite:

> *"The Minister shall appoint one representative of the Ministry and one representative of the local government unit."*

**Semantic ambiguity** — a word has more than one meaning.

> *"The officer shall secure the premises."*

Does "secure" mean lock the building or obtain the building? Context usually resolves this, but do not rely on context when you can choose an unambiguous word:

> *"The officer shall lock and guard the premises."*

**Referential ambiguity** — a pronoun or reference could point to more than one thing.

> *"The chairperson shall submit the report to the Minister. He shall review it within 30 days."*

Who is "he" — the chairperson or the Minister? Rewrite:

> *"The chairperson shall submit the report to the Minister. The Minister shall review the report within 30 days."*

**The "and/or" problem.** Never use "and/or" in legislation. It creates ambiguity about whether a condition is conjunctive (all items required) or disjunctive (any one item sufficient). Choose "and" or "or" and be clear about which you mean. If you mean "one or more of the following," say so.

**The dangling modifier.** A modifier that does not clearly attach to the right noun produces absurd or ambiguous results:

> *"Having been convicted of a crime, the Minister shall revoke the license."*

This says the Minister has been convicted of a crime. Rewrite:

> *"The Minister shall revoke the license of any licensee who has been convicted of a crime."*

**Pronoun ambiguity in "which" and "that" clauses.**

> *"The Ministry shall establish training centers in municipalities that do not have hospitals, which shall be funded by the BGAA."*

What shall be funded — the training centers or the hospitals? If you mean the training centers:

> *"The Ministry shall establish training centers in municipalities that do not have hospitals. The training centers shall be funded by the Bangsamoro General Appropriations Act."*

Whenever a relative clause ("which," "that," "who") could attach to more than one noun, rewrite the sentence to remove the ambiguity.

**The scope of "or" and "and" with multiple items.** When a list has three or more items connected by "and" or "or," the connection can be read in different ways:

> *"teachers, health workers, and social workers employed by the Bangsamoro Government"*

Does "employed by the Bangsamoro Government" apply to all three groups, or only to social workers? If it applies to all, restructure:

> *"teachers, health workers, and social workers, all of whom are employed by the Bangsamoro Government"*

Or use a list format with the qualifying phrase in the introductory clause:

> *"the following personnel employed by the Bangsamoro Government: (a) teachers; (b) health workers; and (c) social workers."*

## 6.3 Internal Consistency

### 6.3.1 Consistent Use of Terms

Use the same word to mean the same thing throughout the entire bill. Do not switch between synonyms. If Section 2 defines "child development center," do not call it a "daycare facility" in Section 15 and an "ECCD center" in Section 22. The reader will wonder whether these are three different things or the same thing.

This rule applies with special force to defined terms. Once you define a term in the Definitions section, use that exact term — and only that term — throughout the bill. If you defined "Ministry" to mean the Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education, write "Ministry" every time. Do not switch to "MBHTE" or "the education ministry" or "the concerned ministry."

**Practical test:** Search your draft for every instance of the key terms. Make sure each term is used consistently. If you find variations, standardize them.

### 6.3.2 Consistent Structure Across Provisions

When provisions address parallel subjects, use parallel structure. If Sections 10 through 15 each establish a different committee, each section should follow the same pattern: creation, composition, functions, meetings, quorum, reports. The reader who understands the structure of Section 10 should be able to navigate Sections 11 through 15 without difficulty.

Inconsistent structure forces the reader to re-learn the pattern for each provision. Consistent structure lets the reader focus on content.

**Example of inconsistent structure:**

> Section 10. *The Technical Committee.* — A Technical Committee is hereby created, composed of five members appointed by the Minister. The Committee shall meet quarterly.
>
> Section 11. *Powers of the Oversight Board.* — The Oversight Board shall have the power to review, approve, or reject all project proposals. It shall be composed of seven members. Meetings shall be held monthly.

Section 10 starts with creation, then composition, then meetings. Section 11 starts with powers, then composition, then meetings. For consistency, both should follow the same order.

**Recommended pattern for provisions creating bodies:**

When your bill creates multiple committees, boards, or offices, use the same sequence for each:

1. Creation — "There is hereby created a [name]."
2. Composition — "The [name] shall be composed of..."
3. Qualifications — "Members shall meet the following qualifications..."
4. Term — "Members shall serve a term of..."
5. Powers and functions — "The [name] shall have the following powers and functions..."
6. Meetings and quorum — "The [name] shall meet..."
7. Compensation — "Members shall receive..."
8. Reports — "The [name] shall submit..."

Not every body needs all eight elements. But those that appear should be in the same order.

**Consistent formatting across sections.** Beyond content, keep the formatting consistent:

- If Section 3 uses "(a), (b), (c)" for its subsections, do not switch to "(1), (2), (3)" in Section 7 without reason.
- If Section 4 puts the section heading in italics after a bold section number (**Section 4.** *Short Title.* —), follow the same pattern throughout.
- If Section 10 indents its list items, Section 15 should indent its list items the same way.

These may seem like small matters. They are not. Inconsistent formatting signals careless drafting. It makes the reader uncertain whether the inconsistency is intentional — whether (1) and (a) signal different levels of hierarchy or are simply typos. Consistent formatting is a sign of a drafter who pays attention to detail.

## 6.4 Completeness

### 6.4.1 Checking for Gaps in Coverage

A bill with gaps is like a bridge with missing sections. It looks functional until someone tries to cross it.

Check your draft for common gaps:

- **Transition provisions.** What happens to existing programs, personnel, contracts, and pending cases when the new law takes effect?
- **Edge cases.** What if the deadline falls on a weekend or holiday? What if the responsible official is absent or incapacitated? What if the required quorum cannot be met?
- **Overlap with existing law.** Does the bill cover subjects already regulated by another BAA or national law? If so, which law prevails?
- **Geographic coverage.** Does the bill apply to all of BARMM or only to certain provinces or municipalities?
- **Temporal coverage.** Does the bill apply retroactively to existing situations or only prospectively?

### 6.4.2 Every Duty Has an Accountable Body

When a provision says "the Bangsamoro Government shall ensure access to clean water," who exactly is responsible? The Chief Minister? The Ministry of Public Works? The local government? "The Bangsamoro Government" is not an accountable body — it is a collective abstraction.

Every duty in your bill should identify a specific, named body that is responsible for carrying it out. If the duty falls on multiple bodies, specify how they share or divide the responsibility.

| Incomplete | Complete |
|---|---|
| The government shall provide free textbooks. | The Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education shall provide free textbooks to all public elementary and secondary schools in the BARMM. |
| Appropriate agencies shall coordinate disaster response. | The Ministry of Public Order and Safety shall coordinate disaster response in consultation with the Ministry of the Interior and Local Government and the Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority. |

### 6.4.3 Every Violation Has a Consequence

If a bill prohibits something but prescribes no penalty, the prohibition is toothless. If a bill requires an agency to submit quarterly reports but says nothing about what happens when the agency fails to report, the requirement will be ignored.

For every obligation, ask: What happens if the obligated party does not comply? For every prohibition, ask: What is the consequence of violation?

The consequence does not have to be criminal. It can be administrative (suspension, revocation of license, removal from office), civil (damages, injunction), or regulatory (fine, closure order). But some consequence must exist. Otherwise, you are writing aspiration, not law.

## 6.5 Writing Provisions That Can Be Enforced

An enforceable provision has four elements:

1. **A clear legal subject** — who is bound by the provision.
2. **A clear legal action** — what the subject must do, must not do, or may do.
3. **A clear standard** — how compliance or violation is measured.
4. **A clear consequence** — what happens upon compliance or violation.

Test each provision by asking: If this provision were violated, could a government official, a prosecutor, or a judge determine (a) who violated it, (b) what they did wrong, (c) that it was indeed wrong under the stated standard, and (d) what the consequence should be?

**Example — unenforceable vs. enforceable:**

| Unenforceable | Enforceable |
|---|---|
| All business establishments in the BARMM shall practice good governance. | Every business establishment in the BARMM with gross annual revenue exceeding one million pesos shall submit an audited financial statement to the Ministry of Trade, Industry, and Tourism within 90 days after the close of each fiscal year. Failure to submit shall result in a fine of ten thousand pesos for each month of delay. |

The first provision is a statement of aspiration. Nobody can enforce "good governance" because there is no way to measure compliance, identify a violator, or impose a consequence. The second provision tells you who must act, what they must do, when they must do it, and what happens if they do not.

**The enforceability checklist.** Run through this checklist for every substantive provision in your draft:

| Question | If the answer is no... |
|---|---|
| Is the legal subject identified by name? | Add the specific body or person. |
| Is the action described in concrete, measurable terms? | Replace vague language with specific obligations. |
| Is there a deadline or trigger event? | Add one. Open-ended obligations are ignored. |
| Is there a way to determine compliance? | Add a standard, a reporting requirement, or a verification mechanism. |
| Is there a consequence for non-compliance? | Add a penalty, sanction, or other enforcement mechanism. |
| Can the provision be applied without further legislation? | If not, include an IRR provision with a deadline and minimum content requirements. |

**More examples from Bangsamoro context:**

| Provision | Enforceable? | Why or Why Not |
|---|---|---|
| The Bangsamoro Government shall promote sustainable development. | No | No specific actor, no specific action, no standard, no consequence. |
| The Minister of Environment shall submit to the Parliament a biannual report on forest cover in the BARMM, including deforestation rates and reforestation progress, within 60 days after the end of each half of the fiscal year. | Yes | Named actor, specific action, measurable deliverable, deadline. |
| Food establishments shall comply with halal standards. | Partly | Named subject, general action, but no standard specified and no consequence stated. |
| Every food establishment in the BARMM shall display a halal compliance certificate issued by the Bangsamoro Darul Ifta' or a recognized halal-certifying body. Failure to display the certificate shall be penalized by a fine of five thousand pesos (PhP 5,000.00) for the first offense and ten thousand pesos (PhP 10,000.00) for each subsequent offense. | Yes | Named subject, specific action, identified certifying body, specific penalties. |

## 6.6 Avoiding Common Pitfalls

### 6.6.1 Over-Legislation

Over-legislation means writing provisions so detailed that they are impossible to follow, cannot adapt to changed circumstances, or regulate matters better left to administrative discretion.

**Signs of over-legislation:**

- The bill specifies the exact organizational chart of a ministry, down to the number of divisions and the titles of division chiefs.
- The bill dictates the specific curriculum content of an educational program, including the number of hours for each subject.
- The bill sets specific peso amounts for budgets that will be outdated before the IRR is issued.
- The bill prescribes step-by-step procedures that an agency cannot modify without amending the law.

When you find yourself drafting this kind of detail, stop. Ask: Is this a matter that should be in the law, or should the law delegate it to the implementing rules and regulations?

The general principle: a BAA should set the policy, the framework, the obligations, and the standards. The IRR should set the procedures, the organizational details, and the operational specifics. If you put operational detail in the law, you make the law rigid. Every change — even a minor procedural adjustment — will require an amendment.

### 6.6.2 Vague Delegations

The opposite of over-legislation is under-legislation — delegating too much to the executive without providing standards.

A provision that says "the Minister shall issue rules and regulations to carry out the purposes of this Act" is acceptable. A provision that says "the Minister shall determine the coverage, benefits, and obligations under this Act" is not — because it delegates the substance of the law, not just the implementation details.

When you delegate authority to issue implementing rules, set boundaries:

- State the general policy that the rules must follow.
- Identify the specific subjects the rules must address.
- Set deadlines for issuing the rules.
- Require consultation with stakeholders before issuance.

**Example — vague vs. bounded delegation:**

| Vague | Bounded |
|---|---|
| The Ministry shall promulgate rules to implement this Act. | The Ministry shall promulgate implementing rules and regulations within 60 days from the effectivity of this Act. The rules shall, at a minimum, prescribe (a) the qualifications for registration, (b) the schedule of fees, (c) the procedure for filing complaints, and (d) the grounds for revocation of licenses. |

### 6.6.3 Unfunded Mandates

A bill that requires a ministry to build a hospital in every municipality but appropriates no funds for construction is an unfunded mandate. It creates a legal obligation without providing the resources to fulfill it.

Every bill that creates new programs, positions, or obligations should address funding. The two standard approaches:

1. **Specific appropriation.** The bill itself appropriates a specific amount. This is appropriate when the cost is known and the Parliament intends to commit the funds immediately.

2. **Inclusion in the annual budget.** The bill provides that funding shall be included in the annual Bangsamoro General Appropriations Act. This is appropriate when the cost will vary year to year or when the program will be phased in over time.

Either way, the bill should contain an appropriation section. A bill without one is a promise without resources.

**How to spot an unfunded mandate.** As you draft, keep a running list of every obligation your bill creates. For each one, ask: Does this cost money? If yes, where does the money come from? Here is a checklist:

| Obligation | Cost? | Funding Source |
|---|---|---|
| Establish child development centers in every barangay | Yes — construction, equipment, staffing | Section [X] appropriation clause |
| Submit quarterly reports to Parliament | Minimal — staff time | Covered by existing MOOE |
| Create a new coordinating council | Yes — office space, staff, travel | Section [X] appropriation clause |
| Conduct annual inspections of all facilities | Yes — inspectors, transport, per diem | Need to add appropriation |
| Train all barangay health workers | Yes — training materials, venue, facilitators | Need to add appropriation |

If any obligation has a "Yes" in the cost column and no identified funding source, you have an unfunded mandate. Fix it before filing.

**The appropriation clause formula in BAAs:**

> *Funding is hereby appropriated from the Bangsamoro General Appropriations for [the purpose], and, thereafter, such amount as may be needed for [continued operations/implementation].*

This is the pattern used in BAA No. 25 and similar BAAs. It works for bills where the exact cost is not yet known. For bills with a known cost, state the amount:

> *The amount of fifty million pesos (PhP 50,000,000.00) is hereby appropriated from the Bangsamoro General Appropriations for the initial implementation of this Act.*

## 6.7 Learning from Enacted BAAs

The best way to learn legislative style is to read enacted legislation. BAAs that have been adopted by the Parliament, signed by the Chief Minister, and published have survived every stage of legislative scrutiny. They are not perfect — no legislation is — but they represent the Parliament's working standard.

Study these BAAs for:

- **Structure.** How are sections organized? What comes first, second, last?
- **Definitions.** How are terms defined? What formula is used?
- **Operative language.** How are obligations, prohibitions, and permissions expressed?
- **Standard provisions.** How are separability, repealing, and effectivity clauses written?
- **Citation style.** How are the BOL, national laws, and other BAAs referenced?

When you encounter a drafting problem — how to phrase a particular provision, how to structure an amendment, how to write a penalty clause — look at how similar BAAs handled it. The accumulated body of enacted BAAs is your best style guide.

Pay particular attention to:

- **BAA No. 11** (Power of Appointment) — a concise BAA with clean section structure.
- **BAA No. 13** (Bangsamoro Administrative Code) — a comprehensive codification.
- **BAA No. 18** (Bangsamoro Education Code) — a major policy bill covering an entire sector.
- **BAA No. 25** (Datu Blah Sinsuat Hospital Upgrading Act) — a typical local bill with clear structure.
- **BAA No. 49** (Bangsamoro Local Government Code) — a comprehensive framework with detailed institutional provisions.

**What to study in each BAA:**

Take BAA No. 11 (Power of Appointment). It is only eight sections. Look at how it handles each standard component:

- **Short title** (Section 1): Uses the formula "This act shall be known as..." — a traditional phrasing.
- **Purpose** (Section 2): One sentence stating the law's objective — selection and appointment based on merits, with gender and ethnic balance.
- **Definitions** (Section 3): Defines only two terms — "Power of Appointment" and "Heads of Office." No unnecessary definitions.
- **Substantive provisions** (Sections 4-5): Section 4 uses lettered subsections (a), (b), (c), (d) to list different appointing authorities — clear, parallel structure. Section 5 states the policy on appointments in a single sentence.
- **Final provisions** (Sections 6-8): Repealing clause, separability clause, effectivity clause — in that order, each one sentence.

Now compare that with BAA No. 25 (Datu Blah Sinsuat Hospital Upgrading Act). This is a local bill with ten sections:

- **Short title** (Section 1): Uses the formula "This Act shall be known as the 'Datu Blah Sinsuat District Hospital Upgrading Act of 2022'."
- **Declaration of Policy** (Section 2): Anchors the policy in Section 22, Article IX of the BOL — establishing the constitutional basis.
- **Main operative provision** (Section 3): Specific and measurable — upgrades from Level I with 10-bed capacity to Level II with 100-bed capacity. Notice the precision: not "upgrade the hospital" but "upgrade from its present level with a ten (10) bed capacity into a Level II hospital with a one hundred (100) bed authorized capacity."
- **Integration clause** (Section 4): Links the hospital to the national Universal Health Care Act — demonstrating how to connect regional legislation with national frameworks.
- **Administration** (Section 5): Places the hospital under the Ministry of Health — naming the specific supervising body.
- **Appropriation** (Section 6): Does not specify a peso amount but says funding "is hereby appropriated from the Bangsamoro General Appropriations." This is a common approach when the exact cost is not yet known.
- **IRR provision** (Section 7): The MOH-BARMM shall promulgate IRR within 60 days — a bounded delegation with a deadline.

These patterns are your templates. When you face a drafting decision — how detailed should the appropriation clause be? how should I structure the IRR provision? — look at how enacted BAAs have handled the same question.

**Common patterns across BAAs:**

| Component | Standard Pattern in BAAs |
|---|---|
| Short title | "This Act shall be known as the '[name].'" |
| Enacting clause | "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament:" (timeless form) or "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament in session assembled:" (BTA period) |
| Section numbering | "Section 1." or "SEC. 1." — both forms appear; be consistent within one bill |
| Section heading format | Bold section number, italicized heading, em dash: **Section 3.** *Definition of Terms.* — |
| Effectivity | "This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in a newspaper of general circulation in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao." (or "...of regional circulation.") |
| Separability | "If any provision of this Act or the application of such provision is declared invalid, the remainder of this Act shall not be affected by such declaration." |
| Repealing | "All laws and regulations under the BARMM government... not consistent with this Act are hereby repealed." |
| Approval block | Speaker's signature, then "This Act was passed by the Bangsamoro Parliament on [date].", Secretary-General's signature, then Chief Minister's "APPROVED:" with signature and date |

## 6.8 The Structure of a Legal Rule

### 6.8.1 Legal Subject, Legal Action, and Conditions

Every operative provision in a BAA can be broken down into three components:

1. **Legal subject** — the person, body, or entity that is bound by the rule.
2. **Legal action** — what the subject must do, must not do, or may do.
3. **Conditions** — the circumstances under which the rule applies.

When you draft, make sure all three are present and unambiguous.

**Example:**

> *The Chief Minister [legal subject] shall appoint [legal action] the members of the Commission within 30 days from the effectivity of this Act [condition].*

When any component is missing, the provision is incomplete:

| Missing Component | Example | Problem |
|---|---|---|
| No legal subject | "The report shall be submitted within 30 days." | Who must submit the report? |
| No legal action | "The Ministry of Health, in coordination with the Ministry of the Interior and Local Government." | What must they do? |
| No conditions | "The Minister shall revoke the license." | Under what circumstances? Always? Upon what finding? |

### 6.8.2 The Exception-Case-Condition-Rule Pattern

Complex provisions often follow this pattern:

1. **General rule** — the default obligation or prohibition.
2. **Conditions** — when the rule applies.
3. **Cases** — specific situations within the rule.
4. **Exceptions** — when the rule does not apply.

Draft in this order: state the general rule first, then the conditions, then the cases, then the exceptions. Do not start with the exception. Do not bury the general rule inside a proviso.

**Example — poorly ordered:**

> *Provided, That no license shall be required if the establishment is operated by the Bangsamoro Government, the Minister may require all food establishments in the BARMM to obtain a halal certification license, which shall be valid for two years and renewable upon application, subject to the schedule of fees prescribed by the Ministry.*

The reader has to reach the middle of the sentence to find the general rule. Rewrite:

> *The Minister may require all food establishments in the BARMM to obtain a halal certification license. The license shall be valid for two years and renewable upon application, subject to the schedule of fees prescribed by the Ministry. This requirement shall not apply to food establishments operated by the Bangsamoro Government.*

Now the general rule comes first, the details follow, and the exception is stated separately.

### 6.8.3 Three Types of Normative Provisions

Legislative provisions fall into three categories:

**Obligations** — provisions that require the subject to do something.

> *The Ministry shall submit an annual report to the Parliament.*

**Prohibitions** — provisions that forbid the subject from doing something.

> *No member of the Commission shall hold any other office or employment during their term.*

**Permissions** — provisions that allow the subject to do something they would not otherwise be authorized to do.

> *The Minister may extend the deadline by an additional 30 days upon written request.*

Know which type you are writing. Obligations use "shall." Prohibitions use "shall not" or "no person shall." Permissions use "may." Do not mix them. If a provision says "shall" in one clause and "may" in the next, the reader cannot tell whether the second clause is an obligation or a permission.

## 6.9 Shall, May, and Must

### 6.9.1 When to Use Each

These three words carry more legal weight than any other words in legislation. Use them correctly.

| Word | Meaning | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **shall** | Imposes a mandatory obligation | When you want to require a person or body to do something. "The Minister **shall** appoint the members within 30 days." |
| **shall not** | Imposes a mandatory prohibition | When you want to forbid a person or body from doing something. "No member **shall** hold any other office during the term." |
| **may** | Grants discretion or permission | When you want to allow — but not require — a person or body to do something. "The Commission **may** hire consultants as needed." |
| **must** | Imposes a condition or prerequisite | When you want to state a condition that must be met before something can happen. "The applicant **must** be at least 21 years of age." Use sparingly — "shall" is preferred for obligations in Bangsamoro legislation. |

The Bangsamoro Parliament follows the convention of the Philippine Congress: "shall" is the standard word for obligations. Use "shall" as your default for mandatory provisions.

"May" is never mandatory. If you write "the Minister may submit a report," you are saying the Minister has the choice. If you mean the Minister is required to submit a report, write "shall."

"Must" is occasionally used for conditions or prerequisites, but "shall" can serve the same purpose. The safest approach: use "shall" for all obligations and prohibitions, "may" for all permissions, and reserve "must" for the rare case where you need to state a prerequisite distinct from an obligation.

### 6.9.2 Common Errors

**Error 1: Using "shall" for things that are not obligations.**

> *"The BARMM shall be composed of the provinces of Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, Lanao del Sur, Sulu, Basilan, and Tawi-Tawi."*

The BARMM does not have an obligation to be composed of these provinces. This is a statement of fact, not a command. Write:

> *"The BARMM is composed of..."*

Use "shall" only for obligations and prohibitions. For descriptions, definitions, and statements of fact, use "is," "means," "includes," or "consists of."

**Error 2: Using "may" when you mean "shall."**

> *"The implementing agency may promulgate the necessary rules and regulations."*

If the IRR is required, this should be "shall." If you write "may," the agency can choose not to issue rules — and without rules, the law may be unimplementable.

**Error 3: Using "shall" with no identifiable subject.**

> *"It shall be ensured that all children have access to basic education."*

Who shall ensure this? Write:

> *"The Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education shall ensure that all children in the BARMM have access to basic education."*

**Error 4: Using "shall" for the speaker of the legislation.**

> *"This Act shall be known as the Bangsamoro Education Code."*

The Act does not have an obligation to be known by that name. This is a legislative declaration, not a command. Acceptable alternatives: "This Act shall be known as..." is traditional and widely used in Philippine legislation, so it is tolerable. But if you want to be precise: "This Act is the Bangsamoro Education Code."

**Error 5: Double modals.**

> *"The Commission shall be required to submit a report."*

"Shall" already makes it mandatory. "Be required to" is redundant. Write:

> *"The Commission shall submit a report."*

## 6.10 Normative vs. Aspirational Language

Normative language creates a rule. Aspirational language states a goal. Both have a place in legislation, but they serve different purposes and should not be confused.

**Normative provisions** belong in the substantive body of the bill. They tell people what they must do, must not do, or may do. They are enforceable.

**Aspirational provisions** belong in the Declaration of Policy section — and only there. They state the objectives and principles that guide the law. They are not directly enforceable, but they provide context for interpreting the substantive provisions.

The problem arises when aspirational language appears in substantive sections:

> **Section 15.** *Community Participation.* — The Bangsamoro Government recognizes the importance of community participation in governance and shall endeavor to create an enabling environment for the meaningful engagement of all sectors in the development process.

This provision sounds important. But what does it require? What is an "enabling environment"? What is "meaningful engagement"? Who is accountable? How would you enforce this?

Compare:

> **Section 15.** *Community Participation.* — Each ministry shall conduct at least two public consultations per year on its proposed programs and projects. The consultations shall be held in locations accessible to affected communities. Notice of each consultation shall be published at least 15 days in advance. The ministry shall prepare a written summary of the feedback received and publish it within 30 days after the consultation.

The second version is enforceable. It specifies who must act, what they must do, when, and how compliance is measured.

**A quick test.** For every provision in the substantive body of your bill, ask: "Can someone violate this?" If the answer is no — if there is no way for anyone to fail to comply — the provision is aspirational. Move it to the Declaration of Policy or delete it.

**Common aspirational phrases that masquerade as operative provisions:**

| Aspirational | Operative Alternative |
|---|---|
| The Bangsamoro Government shall promote the welfare of children. | The Ministry of Social Services shall allocate at least five percent (5%) of its annual budget to child welfare programs. |
| The Parliament recognizes the importance of cultural heritage. | (Move to Declaration of Policy — or delete, because recognition without action is empty.) |
| Peace and development shall be pursued in all programs. | Each ministry shall include a peace and development impact assessment in every program proposal submitted to the BPDA. |
| The rights of indigenous peoples shall be respected. | No government project shall proceed on ancestral domain without the free, prior, and informed consent of the affected indigenous peoples, documented through a written agreement witnessed by the National Commission on Indigenous Peoples. |
| Education shall be accessible to all. | The MBHTE shall ensure that a public elementary school exists within every barangay or within a three-kilometer radius of every residential cluster in the BARMM. |

The pattern is consistent. Aspirational language uses abstract nouns (welfare, importance, peace, rights, accessibility). Operative language uses concrete verbs with named actors and measurable standards (allocate, include, proceed, ensure).

**Where aspirational language belongs.** The Declaration of Policy section is the right home for aspirational statements. That is where you articulate the guiding principles and objectives of the law. These statements inform how the operative provisions are interpreted, but they are not themselves enforceable. Place them there, and keep the substantive sections operative.

## 6.11 Active Voice and Present Tense

### 6.11.1 Why Active Voice

Active voice identifies who must act. Passive voice hides the actor.

> **Passive:** *The report shall be submitted within 30 days.*
>
> **Active:** *The chairperson shall submit the report within 30 days.*

The passive version does not tell you who must submit the report. The active version does. In legislation, that difference matters. If the provision is violated, the passive version makes it harder to determine who is responsible.

Active voice is also shorter and more direct. It produces clearer sentences with less room for misinterpretation.

### 6.11.2 Converting Passive to Active

To convert passive to active, find the actor and make them the subject of the sentence.

| Passive | Active |
|---|---|
| Applications shall be processed within 15 days. | The licensing office shall process applications within 15 days. |
| Penalties shall be imposed for violations. | The Minister shall impose penalties for violations. |
| The fund shall be administered in accordance with the rules. | The Ministry of Finance shall administer the fund in accordance with the rules. |
| Reports shall be made available to the public. | The Secretary-General shall make reports available to the public. |
| A certificate shall be issued upon completion of the program. | The training center shall issue a certificate upon completion of the program. |

Sometimes passive voice is acceptable:

- When the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant: "This Act may be cited as..."
- When the emphasis should be on the action, not the actor: "No bill shall become a law unless it has passed the stages of legislation."
- When the same provision applies to multiple actors: "All government contracts exceeding one million pesos shall be subject to public bidding."

But these are exceptions. Default to active voice.

## 6.12 Sentence Length and Structure

### 6.12.1 One Idea Per Sentence

Long sentences are the most common source of confusion in legislation. A sentence that stretches across eight lines, contains three subordinate clauses, two provisos, and a parenthetical — that sentence will be misread.

The rule is simple: one idea per sentence. If a sentence contains more than one obligation, more than one condition, or more than one exception, break it apart.

**Before — one sentence, multiple ideas:**

> *The Ministry shall establish child development centers in all barangays that do not currently have one, provided that priority shall be given to barangays classified as geographically isolated and disadvantaged areas, and provided further that the Ministry shall coordinate with the local government units in the selection of sites, and the centers shall be operational within two years from the effectivity of this Act.*

**After — one idea per sentence:**

> *The Ministry shall establish a child development center in every barangay that does not have one.*
>
> *Priority shall be given to barangays classified as geographically isolated and disadvantaged areas.*
>
> *The Ministry shall coordinate with local government units in the selection of sites.*
>
> *All centers shall be operational within two years from the effectivity of this Act.*

Each sentence can stand on its own. Each can be read, understood, and applied independently.

### 6.12.2 Breaking Provisions into Lists

When a provision contains multiple items — conditions, requirements, exceptions, categories — use a list instead of a run-on sentence.

**Before — everything in one sentence:**

> *The applicant must be a Filipino citizen, at least 25 years of age, a resident of the BARMM for at least five years immediately preceding the date of application, a holder of a college degree from a recognized institution, and must not have been convicted of any crime involving moral turpitude.*

**After — as a list:**

> *The applicant must meet all of the following qualifications:*
>
> *(a) Filipino citizen;*
>
> *(b) at least 25 years of age;*
>
> *(c) resident of the BARMM for at least five years immediately preceding the date of application;*
>
> *(d) holder of a college degree from a recognized institution; and*
>
> *(e) has not been convicted of any crime involving moral turpitude.*

The list format makes each item visible. The reader can check each qualification independently. See Section 6.14 for detailed rules on list formatting.

## 6.13 Drafting Definitions

### 6.13.1 "Means" vs. "Includes"

The two most important words in a definitions section are "means" and "includes." They do different things.

**"Means"** creates an exhaustive definition. The term means exactly what the definition says and nothing else.

> *"Child" means a person below 18 years of age.*

A person who is 18 years old is not a child under this definition. There is no room for interpretation.

**"Includes"** creates an illustrative definition. The term includes the listed items but may also include other things not listed.

> *"Educational institution" includes public schools, private schools, madaris, and community learning centers.*

This definition lists four types of educational institutions but leaves room for others. A technical vocational school might be considered an educational institution even though it is not listed.

**When to use which:**

- Use "means" when you want a closed definition that cannot be expanded. This is appropriate when precision is essential — for example, defining the age of coverage, the geographic scope, or the amount of a penalty.

- Use "includes" when you want an open definition that can accommodate items you have not listed. This is appropriate when the category is broad and you want to provide examples without limiting the scope.

- Avoid using "means and includes" together. It creates confusion: is the definition exhaustive (because of "means") or illustrative (because of "includes")? Pick one. If you need to combine a core definition with examples, write: "'Educational institution' means any organized institution that provides instruction. It includes, but is not limited to, public schools, private schools, madaris, and community learning centers."

### 6.13.2 The Substitution Test

After writing a definition, apply the substitution test: replace every occurrence of the defined term in the bill with the definition. Does the sentence still make sense?

**Definition:**

> *"Halal" means permissible under Islamic law.*

**Provision:**

> *All food products sold in public markets shall bear a halal certification label.*

**Substitution:**

> *All food products sold in public markets shall bear a permissible-under-Islamic-law certification label.*

This works. The substitution produces a sentence that makes sense.

**Bad definition:**

> *"Halal" means food that is prepared and handled in compliance with Islamic dietary laws, which is permissible for consumption by Muslims.*

**Substitution:**

> *All food products sold in public markets shall bear a food-that-is-prepared-and-handled-in-compliance-with-Islamic-dietary-laws-which-is-permissible-for-consumption-by-Muslims certification label.*

The substitution does not work. The definition is too long and too specific for the way the term is used. Revise the definition or revise the provision.

### 6.13.3 What Not to Define

Not every term needs a definition. Define a term only if:

- It has a specific meaning in the context of the bill that differs from its ordinary meaning.
- It is a technical term that the reader may not know.
- It is used frequently and a definition will prevent repetition of a longer phrase.

**Do not define:**

- Terms that have an obvious, commonly understood meaning. You do not need to define "building," "road," or "teacher" unless you are giving them a specialized meaning.

- Terms that are defined in the BOL or in a national law that applies to the BARMM. Instead, incorporate those definitions by reference: "As used in this Act, terms defined in the Bangsamoro Organic Law shall have the meanings given to them therein."

- Terms that appear only once in the bill. If a term is used once, define it in context rather than in a separate Definitions section.

- Terms in a way that contradicts their ordinary meaning. Defining "child" to mean a person below 25 will confuse everyone. If you need a broader age category, use a different term — "youth," "young person," or whatever fits.

**Formatting the definitions section.** Definitions should be listed in alphabetical order. Each definition is a separate subsection. Two common formats appear in BAAs:

**Format A — numbered subsections:**

> *SEC. 3. Definition of Terms. —*
>
> *(1) "Power of Appointment" — refers to the authority exercised by the official concerned...*
>
> *(2) "Heads of Office" — refer to heads of Ministries, Offices, Commissions...*

**Format B — lettered subsections with "as used in this Act":**

> *Section 3. Definition of Terms. — As used in this Act:*
>
> *(a) "Child" means a person below eighteen (18) years of age.*
>
> *(b) "Child Development Center" means a facility...*

Either format works. Be consistent. Format B, with the "As used in this Act" lead-in, is slightly clearer because it reminds the reader that these definitions apply only within this particular law.

**The "refers to" vs. "means" question.** In BAA drafting practice, both "refers to" and "means" appear in definitions. Technically, "means" is the stronger word — it closes the definition. "Refers to" is softer and can be read as merely pointing to something rather than defining it. The cleaner practice is to use "means" for exhaustive definitions and "includes" for illustrative ones. But if your principal MP or the committee prefers "refers to," it is not wrong — it is an established convention in Philippine legislation.

## 6.14 Using Lists

### 6.14.1 Conjunctive Lists

A conjunctive list requires all items to be satisfied. The connecting word is "and."

> *The applicant must submit the following documents:*
>
> *(a) a completed application form;*
>
> *(b) two recent passport-size photographs;*
>
> *(c) proof of residency in the BARMM; and*
>
> *(d) a certificate of good moral character.*

The "and" before item (d) signals that all four documents are required. The applicant must submit all of them.

### 6.14.2 Disjunctive Lists

A disjunctive list requires any one item to be satisfied. The connecting word is "or."

> *A license may be revoked on any of the following grounds:*
>
> *(a) fraud in obtaining the license;*
>
> *(b) violation of any condition of the license;*
>
> *(c) conviction of a crime involving moral turpitude; or*
>
> *(d) failure to operate for a continuous period of one year.*

The "or" before item (d) signals that any single ground is sufficient for revocation.

### 6.14.3 Formatting and Punctuation

Follow these rules when formatting lists in legislation:

**1. Introduce the list with a complete introductory clause followed by a colon.**

> *The Board shall have the following powers and functions:*

Not:

> *The Board:* — (incomplete introductory clause)

**2. Use lowercase letters in parentheses for list items: (a), (b), (c).**

This is the convention in BAAs and in the BOL. For sub-items within a list item, use Arabic numerals in parentheses: (1), (2), (3).

**3. End each item with a semicolon, except the last.**

Place "and" or "or" after the semicolon of the second-to-last item. End the final item with a period.

```
(a) first item;
(b) second item;
(c) third item; and
(d) final item.
```

**4. Keep list items grammatically parallel.**

Every item in the list should be the same part of speech or follow the same grammatical pattern. If the first item starts with a verb, all items should start with a verb.

| Not parallel | Parallel |
|---|---|
| (a) review all applications; (b) approval of qualified applicants; (c) to issue licenses | (a) review all applications; (b) approve qualified applicants; (c) issue licenses |

**5. Do not mix conjunctive and disjunctive connections in a single list.**

If the list is conjunctive, use "and" before the last item. If disjunctive, use "or." Never use "and/or." If some items are required and some are optional, split them into separate lists:

> *The applicant must submit:*
>
> *(a) a completed application form; and*
>
> *(b) proof of residency.*
>
> *The applicant may also submit one or more of the following supporting documents:*
>
> *(a) a letter of recommendation;*
>
> *(b) evidence of relevant work experience; or*
>
> *(c) a certificate of additional training.*

## 6.15 Punctuation

Punctuation in legislation is not decorative. A misplaced comma can change the meaning of a provision.

**Commas.** Use commas to separate items in a series, to set off introductory clauses, and to mark parenthetical phrases. Always use the serial comma (the comma before "and" or "or" in a series of three or more items):

> *The Ministry shall coordinate with the local government units, the private sector, and civil society organizations.*

Without the serial comma:

> *The Ministry shall coordinate with the local government units, the private sector and civil society organizations.*

The second version could be read to mean that "the private sector and civil society organizations" is a single unit. The serial comma removes that ambiguity.

**Semicolons.** Use semicolons to separate items in a list (see Section 6.14.3) and to separate independent clauses that are closely related:

> *The chairperson shall preside over all meetings; in the chairperson's absence, the vice-chairperson shall preside.*

**Colons.** Use colons to introduce lists and to introduce block quotes of cited text:

> *Section 6, Article IX of the BOL provides: "The Bangsamoro Government shall fully respect human rights."*

**Periods.** End every sentence and every provision with a period. Do not end a section heading with a period — the heading ends with a period-em-dash combination: "**Section 1.** *Short Title.* —"

**Em dashes.** In Bangsamoro legislation, the em dash (—) separates the section heading from the section text:

> **Section 3.** *Definition of Terms.* — As used in this Act:

Do not use hyphens (-) or en dashes (–) for this purpose. Use the em dash (—).

**Quotation marks.** Use quotation marks for short titles, defined terms on first definition, and direct quotations:

> *This Act shall be known as the "Bangsamoro Early Childhood Care and Development Act."*

**Parentheses.** Use parentheses for list item letters and numbers — (a), (b), (1), (2) — and for cross-references and clarifications:

> *Section 37, Article XIII of Republic Act No. 11054 (hereinafter "BOL")...*

**The proviso.** The word *Provided* in Philippine legislative tradition introduces a condition, exception, or qualification. It is italicized and followed by a comma:

> *...shall be valid for two years and renewable upon application: Provided, That the applicant has not been found in violation of any provision of this Act.*

Use provisos sparingly. When possible, state exceptions as separate sentences rather than embedding them in provisos. Multiple nested provisos (*Provided, further, That... Provided, furthermore, That...*) produce unreadable text.

**Example — nested provisos vs. separate sentences:**

Nested provisos (hard to read):

> *The Minister shall appoint members of the Board: Provided, That at least two members shall be women: Provided, further, That at least one member shall represent indigenous peoples: Provided, furthermore, That no member shall be related within the fourth civil degree of consanguinity or affinity to the Minister.*

Separate sentences (clear):

> *The Minister shall appoint the members of the Board. At least two (2) members shall be women. At least one (1) member shall represent indigenous peoples. No member shall be related within the fourth civil degree of consanguinity or affinity to the Minister.*

Both versions say the same thing. The second is far easier to read and far less likely to be misinterpreted.

**The "to wit" convention.** In Philippine legislative tradition, "to wit" (italicized) introduces a list. It appears in the BOL and in BAAs:

> *The following are the appointing authorities, to wit:*

This is acceptable in Bangsamoro legislation. However, a simple colon after the introductory clause accomplishes the same thing:

> *The following are the appointing authorities:*

Either form is correct. Be consistent within one bill.

**Hyphens.** Use hyphens for compound adjectives that precede a noun: "merit-based process," "10-bed capacity," "Bangsamoro-owned corporations." Do not use hyphens for compound adjectives that follow a noun: "the process is merit based," "the capacity is 10 beds."

## 6.16 Capitalization

Capitalize the following in Bangsamoro legislation:

| Term | Capitalized Form | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| The autonomous region | Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM) | Always use the full official name on first reference |
| The organic law | Republic Act No. 11054 or the Bangsamoro Organic Law (BOL) | Capitalize when referring to the specific law |
| The national constitution | 1987 Constitution of the Philippines | Capitalize "Constitution" |
| Parliament | Bangsamoro Parliament; the Parliament | Capitalize when referring to the specific body |
| Executive titles | Chief Minister, Speaker, Deputy Speaker, Minister, Secretary-General | Capitalize when used as a title or referring to the specific officeholder |
| Government bodies | Ministry of Health, Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority | Capitalize the full official name |
| Legislative outputs | Bangsamoro Autonomy Act, Resolution No. 268 | Capitalize specific enactments |
| Defined terms | As defined in the bill | Do not capitalize merely because a term is defined — capitalize only if the defined term is a proper noun |
| Act/Law | "this Act," "the Act" | Capitalize when referring to the specific bill being drafted |

**Do not capitalize:**

- Common nouns used generically: "the ministry" (when not referring to a specific ministry), "a resolution" (when not referring to a specific resolution), "the government" (when not referring to a specific government).
- Words for emphasis. Capitalization is not a substitute for boldface. Do not write "the Minister SHALL submit" — use standard capitalization.

## 6.17 Abbreviations and Acronyms

**First-reference rule.** On first use, spell out the full name followed by the abbreviation or acronym in parentheses. After that, you may use the abbreviation.

> *The Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE) shall... The MBHTE shall...*

**In the definitions section.** If an abbreviation is used frequently, define it:

> *"MBHTE" refers to the Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education.*

Or include it in the full definition:

> *"Ministry" refers to the Ministry of Basic, Higher and Technical Education (MBHTE).*

**Common abbreviations in Bangsamoro legislation:**

**Do not assume the reader knows the abbreviation.** Even common abbreviations like "LGU" or "IRR" should be spelled out on first use.

## 6.18 Legal Citation

### 6.18.1 Citing the BOL and the Constitution

**The Bangsamoro Organic Law:**

- Full citation: "Republic Act No. 11054, otherwise known as the 'Organic Law for the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao'"
- Short form (after first reference): "the BOL" or "R.A. No. 11054"
- Citing a specific provision: "Section 2, Article V of R.A. No. 11054" or "Section 2, Article V of the BOL"

Note the order: Section first, then Article. This follows Philippine legislative convention.

**Examples of BOL citations as they appear in actual BAAs:**

> *Section 37, Article XIII of Republic Act No. 11054 otherwise known as the "Organic Law for the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao", provides that the national government shall fund and implement the construction and maintenance of national roads...*

> *...in consonance with the provisions of Article VII, Section 32 of R.A. No. 11054...*

> *Section 22, Article IX of Republic Act No. 11054, otherwise known as the "Organic Law for the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao" [provides for healthcare]...*

**The 1987 Constitution:**

- Full citation: "the 1987 Constitution of the Philippines"
- Short form: "the Constitution"
- Citing a specific provision: "Section 1, Article III of the Constitution"

**Examples of constitutional citations:**

> *Section 4, Article II of the 1987 Constitution of the Philippines states in part that "The prime duty of the Government is to serve and protect the people"...*

> *Section 1, Article III of the Bill of Rights states that "No person shall be deprived of life, liberty, or property without due process of law..."*

When citing constitutional provisions, you may refer to the specific article by name ("the Bill of Rights" for Article III) for clarity, but always include the section and article numbers.

### 6.18.2 Citing National Laws and IRRs

**Republic Acts:**

- Full citation: "Republic Act No. 10611, otherwise known as the 'Food Safety Act of 2013'"
- Short form: "R.A. No. 10611" or "the Food Safety Act"
- For the first reference, include both the number and the popular name (if it has one).

**Cite format table for national legislation:**

| Type | Full Citation | Short Form |
|---|---|---|
| Republic Act | Republic Act No. 11223, otherwise known as the "Universal Health Care Act" | R.A. No. 11223 or the UHC Act |
| Presidential Decree | Presidential Decree No. 442, otherwise known as the "Labor Code of the Philippines" | P.D. No. 442 or the Labor Code |
| Executive Order | Executive Order No. 292, otherwise known as the "Administrative Code of 1987" | E.O. No. 292 |
| Administrative Order | Administrative Order No. 2020-0015 of the Department of Health | A.O. No. 2020-0015 |

**Implementing Rules and Regulations:**

- Cite the parent law and the specific provision: "Section 5 of the Implementing Rules and Regulations of R.A. No. 10611"
- IRRs do not have Republic Act numbers of their own. Always identify them by their parent law.

### 6.18.3 Citing BAAs and Resolutions

**Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts:**

- Full citation: "Bangsamoro Autonomy Act No. 13, otherwise known as the 'Bangsamoro Administrative Code'"
- Short form: "BAA No. 13" or "the Administrative Code"
- Citing a specific provision: "Section 3, Book II of BAA No. 13"

**The BAA citation pattern for different structural units:**

| Structure | Citation Format | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Section only | Section [number] of BAA No. [number] | Section 4 of BAA No. 11 |
| Section within a Book | Section [number], Book [Roman numeral] of BAA No. [number] | Section 3, Book II of BAA No. 13 |
| Section within an Article | Section [number], Article [Roman numeral] of BAA No. [number] | Section 5, Article III of BAA No. 18 |
| Subsection | Section [number]([letter]) of BAA No. [number] | Section 4(b) of BAA No. 11 |

**Resolutions:**

- Full citation: "Resolution No. 268 of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament"
- Short form: "Resolution No. 268"
- Citing a specific provision: "Section 6, Rule XVII of Resolution No. 268"

Note that Resolution No. 268 uses "Rule" instead of "Article" or "Book" as its primary structural unit. When citing its provisions, use "Rule" followed by "Section."

### 6.18.4 Internal Cross-References

When one section of a bill refers to another section of the same bill:

> *"...as provided in Section 12 of this Act."*

Or:

> *"...subject to the qualifications prescribed in Section 5(a)."*

**Rules for internal cross-references:**

1. **Be specific.** Do not write "as provided elsewhere in this Act." Cite the exact section.

2. **Use section numbers, not section titles.** Titles may be amended; numbers are more stable. Write "Section 12" not "the section on penalties."

3. **When referencing a subsection, include the section number.** Write "Section 5(a)" not just "(a)."

4. **Minimize cross-references.** Every cross-reference forces the reader to flip to another part of the document. If the substance can be stated directly, state it directly. Use cross-references only when the referenced provision is too long to repeat or when you need to ensure consistency with a provision that may be amended.

5. **Check all cross-references before filing.** If you renumber sections during revision, every cross-reference that pointed to the old number must be updated. This is one of the most common sources of error in legislation.

6. **Use "of this Act" for references within the same bill.** Do not write just "Section 12" — write "Section 12 of this Act" to distinguish it from sections of other laws.

**Cross-reference checklist before filing:**

- List every internal cross-reference in the bill.
- Verify that the referenced section exists.
- Verify that the referenced section says what you think it says.
- If sections were renumbered during revision, update all references.
- If a referenced section was deleted during revision, update or delete the cross-reference.

## 6.19 Words and Phrases to Avoid

### 6.19.1 Archaic Legalisms

Archaic legalisms are words that lawyers have carried forward from centuries of legal tradition. They add no meaning. They make legislation harder to read. Replace them.

| Archaic | Modern Equivalent |
|---|---|
| aforementioned | this; that; the (refer to the specific item by name) |
| aforesaid | this; that; the |
| herein | in this Act; in this section |
| hereof | of this Act; of this section |
| hereinafter | (use the short form directly) |
| hereinbefore | (refer to the specific section by number) |
| hereunder | under this Act; under this section |
| hereinabove | (refer to the specific section by number) |
| therein | in it; in that section |
| thereof | of it; of that section |
| thereto | to it; to that |
| thereby | by that; as a result |
| therefrom | from it; from that |
| therewith | with it; with that |
| whereas | (acceptable only in resolution preambles) |
| whereby | under which; by which |
| wherefore | for that reason (or omit entirely) |
| whosoever | anyone who; any person who |
| whatsoever | any; of any kind |
| howsoever | in any way; however |
| notwithstanding | despite; even though; regardless of |
| heretofore | before this; previously; until now |
| hitherto | until now |
| forthwith | immediately |
| inter alia | among others (use the English) |
| *in toto* | entirely; in full (use the English) |
| *ipso facto* | by that fact; automatically (use the English) |
| *pro forma* | as a matter of form (use the English) |
| *ab initio* | from the beginning (use the English) |
| *motu proprio* | on the Speaker's own initiative (acceptable in Res. 268 context; otherwise use English) |

Note: Some Latin phrases — like *ex officio*, *motu proprio*, and *Provided, That* — are so embedded in Philippine legislative tradition that they are standard usage. Use them when they are the established convention, but do not add new Latin phrases when English alternatives exist.

### 6.19.2 Redundant Doublets

Redundant doublets are pairs of words that mean the same thing. They come from the English legal tradition of pairing a Latin or French word with an Anglo-Saxon synonym to make sure everyone understood. That reason no longer exists. Use one word, not two.

| Redundant Doublet | Use Instead |
|---|---|
| null and void | void |
| cease and desist | stop; cease |
| due and payable | due |
| each and every | each; every |

### 6.19.3 Vague and Ambiguous Terms

Certain words and phrases are so vague that they give the reader no useful information. Avoid them in substantive provisions.

| Vague Term | Problem | Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| reasonable | What is reasonable to one person is unreasonable to another. | State a specific standard: "within 30 days," "not exceeding 10% of the total cost." |
| adequate | What is adequate? | State a specific standard: "sufficient to serve all barangays," "meeting the standards prescribed by the Ministry." |
| appropriate | Who decides what is appropriate? | Name the specific action or delegate the determination to a named body. |
| as soon as possible | This could mean tomorrow or next year. | State a deadline: "within 15 days." |
| substantial | How much is substantial? | State a percentage, amount, or standard. |
| duly | What does "duly" add to "authorized"? Nothing. | Delete it. Write "authorized" not "duly authorized." |
| proper | Same problem as "appropriate." | Specify the standard. |
| necessary | Everything in a law should be necessary. | Delete it or specify what is needed. |
| from time to time | When? | State a frequency: "quarterly," "at least once a year," "as the Minister determines." |
| sufficient | How much is sufficient? | State the standard. |
| other purposes | What purposes? | Delete the phrase or list the purposes. |
| and for other purposes | A traditional long-title closing phrase. Acceptable in long titles by convention. Do not use in substantive provisions. | — |
| such other functions as may be assigned | By whom? Under what authority? | Limit to: "such other functions as may be assigned by [specific authority] in writing." |
| in the interest of public welfare | Whose judgment? What standard? | Name the specific interest: "to prevent the spread of communicable disease." |

**Problem phrases that appear in Bangsamoro bills and resolutions:**

Beyond the general vague terms above, certain phrases recur in BARMM legislation that you should watch for:

| Phrase | Problem | Better Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "the Bangsamoro Government shall ensure" (without specifying which body) | No accountable body | Name the specific ministry or office |
| "in coordination with relevant agencies" | Which agencies? | "in coordination with the Ministry of Health and the Ministry of the Interior and Local Government" |
| "as may be necessary" | Who decides what is necessary? | State the standard or delegate the determination to a named body with criteria |
| "the Ministry shall take appropriate action" | What action? | "the Ministry shall issue a compliance order" or "the Ministry shall file a complaint" |
| "subject to existing laws, rules and regulations" | Which laws? This adds nothing — all government action is already subject to existing law | Delete the phrase, or cite the specific laws that apply |
| "shall be given due consideration" | This creates no obligation. Due consideration of what? By whom? To what standard? | State the actual obligation: "shall be included in the evaluation criteria" |

**When traditional phrases are acceptable.** Some phrases that are vague in ordinary language have acquired specific legal meaning through long use. "For other purposes" in a long title is one example — Philippine courts have accepted it as a standard closing. "Provided, That" is another — it introduces a condition or exception. These are conventions. You do not need to replace them. But you should not extend the convention by inventing new vague phrases and hoping they will be understood.

## 6.20 Numbers, Dates, and Amounts

**Numbers.** Spell out numbers one through nine. Use figures for 10 and above. But in legislation, the convention is to state both — the written number first, followed by the figure in parentheses:

> *...within thirty (30) days...*
>
> *...not fewer than five (5) members...*
>
> *...a term of three (3) years...*

This dual-expression convention exists because a typographical error in either the word or the figure can be caught by comparing the two. Follow it consistently.

**Percentages.** Write the number followed by "percent" spelled out, with the figure in parentheses:

> *...at least fifty percent (50%) of all members...*

Do not use the % symbol alone without the spelled-out form in the text of a bill.

**Money amounts.** State the amount in words followed by the figure in parentheses, preceded by the currency:

> *...a fine of not less than ten thousand pesos (PhP 10,000.00) and not more than fifty thousand pesos (PhP 50,000.00)...*

Use "PhP" for Philippine pesos. Include centavos (.00) for exact amounts.

**Dates.** In the text of a bill, spell out the month and use figures for the day and year:

> *...on or before December 31, 2028...*

Do not use all-numeric date formats (12/31/2028 or 31-12-2028) in the text of a bill. They are ambiguous.

In the adoption line of a resolution, the BTA convention includes both the Islamic calendar and Gregorian dates:

> *ADOPTED, Sha'ban 19, 1446/February 18, 2025.*

**Age.** Express age in years with both forms:

> *...at least twenty-one (21) years of age...*

**Ordinal numbers.** Express in text with the figure in parentheses:

> *...on the first (1st) day of session...*
>
> *...the third (3rd) regular session...*

## 6.21 Consistency in Terminology

### 6.21.1 One Term, One Meaning

This is the golden rule of legislative drafting: use the same word to mean the same thing throughout the bill, and never use the same word to mean two different things.

If "Ministry" refers to the Ministry of Health in the definitions section, do not use "Ministry" elsewhere to refer to the Ministry of Education. If two ministries are involved, define "Ministry of Health" and "Ministry of Education" separately, and use the specific names.

If "license" refers to a business license in Section 10 and a driver's license in Section 20, the reader will be confused. Use "business license" and "driver's license" to distinguish them.

Common violations of this rule:

| Inconsistency | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Office" used for both the Office of the Chief Minister and the office of the licensing board | Which office? | Use the full name each time, or define each separately. |
| "Fund" used for the general appropriation and for a specific trust fund | Which fund? | Name each fund specifically: "General Fund," "ECCD Trust Fund." |
| "Report" used for the annual report, the quarterly report, and the investigative report | Which report? | Qualify each: "annual report," "quarterly status report," "investigation report." |
| "Committee" used for a committee of the Parliament and a committee created by the bill | Which committee? | Use the specific name: "Committee on Education," "Regional ECCD Coordinating Committee." |

### 6.21.2 Aligning with BOL and Existing BAA Terminology

When your bill refers to concepts, bodies, or structures established by the BOL or by existing BAAs, use the same terminology they use. Do not invent new names for things that already have official names.

**Key terminological alignments:**

| Correct (BOL/BAA Term) | Incorrect (Invented Alternatives) |
|---|---|
| Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao | Bangsamoro Region; Bangsamoro Homeland; the Region (unless previously defined) |
| Chief Minister | Regional Governor; Head of the Bangsamoro Government |
| Bangsamoro Parliament | BTA Legislature; Regional Assembly; Bangsamoro Congress |
| Bangsamoro Autonomy Act | Bangsamoro Act; Regional Law; BTA Legislation |
| Ministry (for Bangsamoro government bodies) | Department (which refers to national agencies) |
| Minister (for heads of Bangsamoro ministries) | Secretary (which refers to heads of national departments) |
| Member of the Parliament (MP) | Congressman; Representative; Assemblyman |
| Local Government Unit (LGU) | Local Government |
| Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR) | Implementing Guidelines (unless you mean something different from IRR) |

This alignment matters because the BOL and existing BAAs use specific terms with specific legal meanings. If you use different terms, a reader — or a court — may conclude that you meant something different.

**Example of misalignment:**

A bill that refers to the "Secretary of Health" when the BARMM has a "Minister of Health" creates confusion. Does the bill refer to the national Department of Health secretary or the regional Ministry of Health minister? In the BARMM context, the correct term is "Minister of Health." Use it.

**When in doubt, check:**

1. The BOL (R.A. No. 11054) — for the names of Bangsamoro government bodies, the powers of the Parliament, and the structure of the autonomous region.
2. BAA No. 13 (Administrative Code) — for the organizational structure and names of ministries and offices.
3. The specific BAA that covers the subject matter — for terms and definitions already established in that area of law.

If a BAA has already defined "child development center" in a specific way, your bill should either use the same definition or explicitly state that it is using a different definition and why.

## Summary of Key Rules

This chapter covered many rules. Here are the ones to remember every time you sit down to draft:

1. **Write for the reader who must act on the law.** The implementer and the citizen, not the drafter, are the primary audience.

2. **One idea per sentence.** Break long sentences into shorter ones.

3. **Use active voice.** Name the actor. "The Minister shall" not "It shall be."

4. **"Shall" means mandatory. "May" means discretionary.** Do not confuse them.

5. **One term, one meaning.** Never use the same word for two different things.

6. **Define only what needs defining.** Use "means" for closed definitions, "includes" for open ones.

7. **Every duty needs a responsible body.** Every prohibition needs a consequence.

8. **Cite precisely.** Section first, then Article. Verify every citation.

9. **Use lists for multiple items.** "And" for all required, "or" for any one sufficient.

10. **Align with BOL and BAA terminology.** Do not invent new names for established concepts.

11. **Cut every unnecessary word.** If a word adds no meaning, delete it.

12. **Check every cross-reference.** If you renumber sections, update every reference.
