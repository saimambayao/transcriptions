# Chapter 10 — Amendments

A bill does not always arrive at the plenary in its final form. More often, it arrives in a form that needs work. Committee members spot problems during scrutiny. Floor deliberations reveal gaps. A Member raises a concern during interpellation that no one anticipated. The response to all of these is the amendment.

Amendments are how the Parliament refines legislation. They are the mechanism by which a draft bill — sometimes rough, sometimes incomplete, sometimes overtaken by new information — becomes a law that the Parliament as a whole can support. If bill drafting is architecture, amendments are the renovation work. They insert rooms that were missing, remove walls that block the light, and replace materials that turned out to be unsuitable.

For the drafter, amendments represent some of the most demanding work in the legislative process. You must draft language that fits seamlessly into an existing text — matching its style, numbering, and terminology — while achieving a specific change that the sponsoring MP or committee has requested. You must do this quickly, often during a session, sometimes while the debate is still underway. And you must do it precisely, because a poorly drafted amendment can create more problems than the defect it was meant to fix.

This chapter covers the types of amendments recognized in the Bangsamoro Parliament, the procedures that govern them, and the exact language and format you should use when drafting them. It draws on the Rules, Procedures, and Practices of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament — Resolution No. 268 — as the governing procedural framework.

---

## 10.1 Types of Amendments

Resolution No. 268 recognizes two broad categories of amendments that can be presented during plenary deliberation:

**Committee Amendments.** These are alterations or changes — by addition, deletion, substitution, or any combination — offered by the committee that considered the bill. Committee amendments arise from the committee's scrutiny of the bill during the committee stage. They reflect the committee's collective judgment about what the bill needs.

**Floor Amendments (Individual Amendments).** These are alterations or changes offered by an individual Member of Parliament during the period of amendments in the plenary. Floor amendments reflect a particular Member's view that the bill, even as modified by the committee, still needs further adjustment.

Both types follow the same basic mechanics. An amendment proposes to change the text of a bill in one or more of the following ways:

- **Insertion** — adding new language where none existed before. This could be a new word, a new phrase, a new subsection, a new section, or even an entirely new article.
- **Deletion** — removing existing language from the bill. This could be a single word, a phrase, a subsection, a section, or an entire article.
- **Substitution** — replacing existing language with new language. This combines deletion and insertion into a single operation: you take out the old text and put in the new text.

These three operations — insertion, deletion, and substitution — are the building blocks of all amendment drafting. Every amendment, no matter how complex, reduces to one or some combination of these operations.

There is a fourth category that deserves separate treatment: the **substitute bill**. A substitute bill replaces the entire text of the original bill with a new version. It is, in effect, a complete rewrite offered as an amendment. Substitute bills follow special procedures and raise particular drafting challenges, which Section 10.5 addresses.

### Important Constraints

Resolution No. 268 imposes several rules on amendments that every drafter must know:

**Germaneness.** An amendment that seeks to include a provision not germane to the purpose of the bill — called a "rider" — is not allowed. The one-subject rule applies to amendments just as it applies to bills. If a bill deals with fisheries regulation, you cannot amend it to include provisions about road construction. The amendment must relate to the subject matter of the bill being amended.

**Specificity.** Amendments must be "presented concretely and specifically in the course of the debate, indicating therein the page and the line in which the amendments are proposed to be made." This is a procedural requirement, but it also imposes a drafting obligation. You cannot offer a vague amendment — "improve the penalty provision" — and leave it to the plenary to figure out the language. You must state exactly what text you propose to insert, delete, or substitute, and where.

**Writing requirement for substitution amendments.** "No amendment by substitution shall be entertained unless the text thereof is submitted in writing." This means the drafter must prepare a written text before the amendment can be formally moved on the floor.

**Withdrawal.** Any amendment may be withdrawn before a vote is taken on it. Once the plenary votes, the amendment stands or falls.

**Preservation of deleted language.** Whenever a Member wishes to retain words or phrases that the committee deleted, that Member must present an amendment as if those words or phrases had never appeared in the bill. In other words, the amendment must affirmatively propose inserting the language back — it cannot simply argue that the committee's deletion was wrong.

## 10.2 Committee Amendments

Committee amendments emerge during the committee stage of the legislative process. After a bill is referred to a committee following Second Reading, the committee examines it in detail — line by line, section by section. During this scrutiny, the committee may decide that certain provisions need to be changed, added, or removed.

### How Committee Amendments Arise

The committee stage is where most of the substantive work on a bill takes place. The committee holds hearings, invites resource persons, consults stakeholders, and deliberates among its members. Through this process, the committee develops a set of proposed changes to the original bill.

These changes might reflect:

- Technical corrections identified during hearings (a deadline that is impractical, a definition that is too narrow or too broad)
- Substantive policy adjustments based on testimony from implementing agencies or affected communities
- Legal issues flagged by the Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS) or the committee's own legal staff
- Compromises reached among committee members with different views on a provision
- Alignment issues with existing BAAs or national law that became apparent during scrutiny

The committee incorporates these changes into the bill and produces a committee report. The committee report contains the revised text of the bill — including all committee amendments — along with the discussions, justifications, and the votes of the members who participated in the final committee vote.

### Procedure in Plenary

When the bill comes to the plenary for the Consideration and Deliberation Stage, committee amendments are taken up first, before any floor amendments. The procedure works as follows:

1. The sponsor of the committee report presents the bill to the plenary, explaining what the committee did and why.
2. After the period of interpellation, the period of amendments begins.
3. The sponsor presents the committee amendments to the plenary.
4. If the proponent (the original author of the bill) accepts the committee amendments, they are adopted without further debate.
5. If the proponent does not accept the committee amendments, the period of amendment continues, and the proponent may propose alternative amendments.
6. Committee amendments that are contested are debated and voted upon.

### Drafting Committee Amendments

When you draft committee amendments for a committee report, present them in a way that makes the changes immediately clear. The committee report should show what the original bill said and what the committee version says. There are two common formats:

**Narrative format.** Describe each amendment in prose:

> The Committee proposes the following amendments to the bill:
>
> 1. Section 3(a) — The phrase "within thirty (30) days" is amended to read "within sixty (60) days" to allow sufficient time for the implementing agency to complete the required consultations.
>
> 2. Section 7 — A new subsection (d) is inserted to read as follows: "(d) Coordination with local government units. — The Ministry shall coordinate with the local government units in the implementation of the programs under this Act."
>
> 3. Section 12 — The section is deleted in its entirety. The Committee found that the penal provision was duplicative of existing penalties under BAA No. 18.

**Comparative matrix format.** Present a two-column or three-column table showing the original provision and the committee version side by side. This format is more work to prepare but far easier for Members to follow during deliberation:

| Original Bill | Committee Amendment |
|---|---|
| SEC. 5. *Eligibility.* — Any person who has been a resident of the Bangsamoro for at least one (1) year... | SEC. 5. *Eligibility.* — Any person who has been a resident of the Bangsamoro for at least **three (3) years**... |
| SEC. 10. *Penalties.* — Any person who violates this Act shall be punished by a fine of not less than Ten Thousand Pesos (PhP10,000.00)... | SEC. 10. *Penalties.* — Any person who violates this Act shall be punished by a fine of not less than **Fifty Thousand Pesos (PhP50,000.00)**... |

When you prepare committee amendments, bold or underline the changed text so that the changes are visible at a glance. This is not a legal requirement, but it is good practice that will save time during plenary deliberation.

## 10.3 Floor Amendments

Floor amendments — also called individual amendments — are proposed by Members of Parliament during the period of amendments in the plenary. Unlike committee amendments, which reflect a collective decision by the committee, floor amendments represent an individual Member's view that the bill still needs revision.

### When Floor Amendments Are Offered

Floor amendments are offered after committee amendments have been disposed of. The sequence during the Consideration and Deliberation Stage is:

1. Committee report is presented
2. Period of interpellation
3. Period of amendments:
   - a. Committee amendments (presented and voted on first)
   - b. Floor amendments (presented and voted on after committee amendments)
4. Voting on Second Reading

A Member who wishes to propose a floor amendment registers with the Majority Floor Leader, who maintains a list of Members proposing amendments. The Member is then recognized by the Speaker to present the amendment.

### Procedure for Proposing a Floor Amendment

When a Member is recognized to propose a floor amendment, the process typically unfolds as follows:

1. The Member states the specific provision to be amended, identifying the page, line, section, and subsection.
2. The Member reads or describes the proposed amendment.
3. The sponsor of the bill responds — accepting, rejecting, or proposing a modification of the amendment.
4. If the amendment is accepted by the sponsor, it may be adopted without further debate.
5. If the amendment is rejected by the sponsor, the plenary debates the amendment.
6. The plenary votes on the amendment.

For substitution amendments specifically, Resolution No. 268 requires that the text be submitted in writing. In practice, it is good discipline to prepare all floor amendments in writing, regardless of type. A written amendment reduces confusion, provides a clear record, and ensures that the text the plenary votes on is the text the Member intended.

### Practical Tips for Drafting Floor Amendments

Floor amendments are often drafted under pressure. A Member hears something during interpellation that prompts a concern. A provision that seemed clear in the committee report turns out to be ambiguous when read in the context of the full bill. A stakeholder contacts the Member's office between sessions with a specific request.

Whatever the trigger, the drafter must produce amendment language quickly and accurately. Here are guidelines:

**Get the exact text of the provision being amended.** Before you draft anything, read the provision as it appears in the committee report version of the bill. Do not work from memory. Do not work from an earlier version. The text you are amending is the text in the committee report, as further modified by any committee amendments already adopted in plenary.

**State the amendment with surgical precision.** Identify the exact location — article, section, subsection, paragraph, sentence, line — where the change applies. State exactly what is being inserted, deleted, or substituted. Leave no room for confusion about which words are being changed.

**Match the style of the bill.** If the bill uses "shall," your amendment uses "shall." If the bill refers to the implementing agency as "the Ministry," your amendment says "the Ministry" — not "the implementing agency" or "said Ministry." Consistency is not optional. A floor amendment that introduces different terminology for the same concept creates an internal conflict in the bill.

**Keep amendments focused.** One amendment, one change. If you need to make three changes to three different sections, propose three separate amendments. Bundling multiple changes into a single amendment creates problems: the plenary may support one change but not the other two, and a bundled amendment forces an all-or-nothing vote.

**Prepare a brief justification.** When the Member presents the amendment, they will need to explain why. Prepare a one-paragraph justification that the Member can read or paraphrase. Keep it factual. State the problem, state the solution, and explain why the proposed text is better than the current text.

## 10.4 Drafting Amendment Language

This section provides the standard formats for drafting amendments in the Bangsamoro Parliament. Each format includes the exact phrasing you should use, followed by examples. These formats apply to both committee amendments and floor amendments, though the procedural context differs.

### 10.4.1 Insertion

An insertion amendment adds new language to a bill without changing any existing text. The new language could be a word, a phrase, a subsection, a section, or an entire article.

#### Inserting a Word or Phrase

When you are inserting one or more words into an existing provision, identify the exact location and specify the text to be inserted.

**Format:**

> On page [X], line [Y], after the word "[existing word]," insert the words "[new words]."

**Example:**

> On page 4, line 12, after the word "consultation," insert the words "with the affected communities."

This would change a provision from:

> ...shall conduct a consultation before issuing the permit.

to:

> ...shall conduct a consultation with the affected communities before issuing the permit.

#### Inserting a Subsection

When you are adding a new subsection to an existing section, identify the section, specify where the new subsection goes, and provide the full text.

**Format:**

> A new subsection (x) is hereby inserted in Section [X] to read as follows:
>
> "(x) [full text of new subsection]."

**Example:**

> A new subsection (e) is hereby inserted in Section 8 to read as follows:
>
> "(e) *Coordination with Local Government Units.* — The Ministry shall coordinate with the provincial and municipal governments within whose jurisdiction the project is to be implemented. No project shall commence without a written endorsement from the relevant local government unit."

When inserting a subsection into an existing list, check whether the insertion requires renumbering of subsequent subsections. If Section 8 currently has subsections (a) through (d) and you are inserting a new (c), the existing (c) becomes (d) and the existing (d) becomes (e). Your amendment must account for this. State it explicitly:

> A new subsection (c) is hereby inserted in Section 8, and the existing subsections (c) and (d) are accordingly redesignated as subsections (d) and (e), respectively.

#### Inserting a New Section

When you are adding an entirely new section, identify the location in the bill where it should appear.

**Format:**

> A new Section [X] is hereby inserted after Section [Y] to read as follows:
>
> "SEC. [X]. *[Section heading].* — [full text of new section]."

**Example:**

> A new Section 15 is hereby inserted after Section 14 to read as follows:
>
> "SEC. 15. *Annual Report.* — The Ministry shall submit an annual report to the Parliament, through the appropriate committee, on the implementation of this Act. The report shall include, at a minimum:
>
> (a) The number of applications received, approved, and denied;
>
> (b) The total amount of funds disbursed;
>
> (c) A summary of compliance and enforcement actions taken; and
>
> (d) Recommendations for legislative or administrative improvements."

If the insertion of a new section requires renumbering of subsequent sections, state this clearly:

> A new Section 15 is hereby inserted after Section 14, and Sections 15 through 22 of the bill are accordingly renumbered as Sections 16 through 23, respectively. All internal cross-references are adjusted accordingly.

That last phrase — "all internal cross-references are adjusted accordingly" — is important. If Section 18 of the original bill contains a reference to "Section 15," and your amendment renumbers Section 15 to Section 16, the cross-reference must be updated. The amendment should note this. Better still, draft the amendment so that it avoids renumbering altogether — for example, by inserting "Section 14-A" instead of a new "Section 15." The choice depends on the preference of the sponsor and the conventions of the Parliament.

#### Inserting a New Article

The same principles apply when inserting an entirely new article (a group of related sections). Use the following format:

> A new Article [X] is hereby inserted after Article [Y] to read as follows:
>
> "ARTICLE [X]
>
> [ARTICLE HEADING]
>
> SEC. [X]. [Section heading]. — [text]
>
> SEC. [X+1]. [Section heading]. — [text]"

### 10.4.2 Deletion

A deletion amendment removes existing language from a bill. This could be a word, a phrase, a clause, a subsection, a section, or an entire article.

#### Deleting a Word or Phrase

**Format:**

> On page [X], line [Y], delete the words "[words to be deleted]."

**Example:**

> On page 6, line 3, delete the words "and their dependents."

This would change a provision from:

> ...shall provide medical assistance to all qualified beneficiaries and their dependents.

to:

> ...shall provide medical assistance to all qualified beneficiaries.

#### Deleting a Subsection

**Format:**

> Subsection (x) of Section [X] is hereby deleted.

If deleting a subsection requires redesignation of subsequent subsections, state this:

> Subsection (c) of Section 8 is hereby deleted, and subsections (d) and (e) are accordingly redesignated as subsections (c) and (d), respectively.

**Example:**

> Subsection (b) of Section 5 is hereby deleted. The existing subsections (c), (d), and (e) are accordingly redesignated as subsections (b), (c), and (d), respectively.

#### Deleting an Entire Section

**Format:**

> Section [X] is hereby deleted.

Or, if the deletion is from an existing law rather than from the bill under consideration:

> Section [X] of [BAA No. XX], otherwise known as the "[short title]," is hereby repealed.

**Example:**

> Section 12 of the bill is hereby deleted.
>
> Sections 13 through 20 are accordingly renumbered as Sections 12 through 19, respectively. All internal cross-references are adjusted accordingly.

Note the distinction between "deleted" and "repealed." You delete provisions from a bill that has not yet been enacted. You repeal provisions of a law that is already in force. The difference matters. An amendment to a pending bill deletes text. An amendatory bill — a bill that amends an existing BAA — repeals provisions. Use the correct term.

#### Deleting an Entire Article

**Format:**

> Article [X] is hereby deleted in its entirety. The subsequent articles are renumbered accordingly, and all internal cross-references are adjusted.

### 10.4.3 Substitution

A substitution amendment replaces existing language with new language. It combines a deletion and an insertion into a single operation.

#### Substituting a Word or Phrase

**Format:**

> On page [X], line [Y], the word "[old word]" is hereby amended to read "[new word]."

Or:

> On page [X], line [Y], delete the words "[old words]" and insert in lieu thereof the words "[new words]."

**Example:**

> On page 3, line 8, the word "may" is hereby amended to read "shall."

This changes a discretionary provision into a mandatory one — a common floor amendment. The drafter should be prepared to explain the legal effect of this change (discussed in Chapter 6).

Another example:

> On page 7, line 15, delete the words "One Hundred Thousand Pesos (PhP100,000.00)" and insert in lieu thereof the words "Two Hundred Fifty Thousand Pesos (PhP250,000.00)."

#### Substituting a Subsection

**Format:**

> Subsection (x) of Section [X] is hereby amended to read as follows:
>
> "(x) [full text of new subsection]."

**Example:**

> Subsection (c) of Section 9 is hereby amended to read as follows:
>
> "(c) *Compliance Period.* — The establishment shall comply with the standards prescribed under this Act within one hundred eighty (180) days from the effectivity of the implementing rules and regulations. The Ministry may grant a one-time extension of ninety (90) days upon written application showing good cause."

#### Substituting an Entire Section

**Format:**

> Section [X] is hereby amended to read as follows:
>
> "SEC. [X]. *[Section heading].* — [full text of the amended section]."

**Example:**

> Section 6 is hereby amended to read as follows:
>
> "SEC. 6. *Composition of the Board.* — The Board shall be composed of seven (7) members, as follows:
>
> (a) The Minister, or a duly designated representative, as Chairperson;
>
> (b) Two (2) representatives from the Ministry of the Interior and Local Government;
>
> (c) One (1) representative from the Ministry of Finance;
>
> (d) One (1) representative from the private sector, nominated by the Bangsamoro Chamber of Commerce and Industry;
>
> (e) One (1) representative from civil society, nominated by accredited civil society organizations in the Bangsamoro; and
>
> (f) One (1) representative from the academe, nominated by public and private universities in the Bangsamoro.
>
> The members under subsections (d), (e), and (f) shall be appointed by the Chief Minister upon the recommendation of the Board Chairperson."

#### Substituting Language in an Existing Law (Amendatory Bills)

When a bill amends a section of an existing Bangsamoro Autonomy Act, the format is slightly different because you must identify the law being amended:

**Format:**

> Section [X] of [BAA No. XX], otherwise known as the "[short title]," is hereby amended to read as follows:
>
> "SEC. [X]. *[Section heading].* — [full text of the amended section]."

**Example:**

> Section 66 of Bangsamoro Autonomy Act No. 18, otherwise known as the "Bangsamoro Education Code," is hereby amended to read as follows:
>
> "SEC. 66. *Medium of Teaching and Learning in Basic Education.* — For Kindergarten and Grade 1 to 3, the medium of teaching and learning shall be the mother tongue of the learners. Beginning Grade 4, the primary medium of instruction and learning shall be English, Filipino, and Arabic. The Ministry shall issue additional rules on the medium of instruction and learning in Basic Education, including the availability of qualified Arabic language teachers and appropriate instructional materials."

When amending an existing law, always reproduce the entire section being amended, not just the changed portions. If you only show the changed words, readers must compare the amendment with the original law to understand the full provision as amended. Reproducing the entire section — with the changes incorporated — makes the amendment self-contained and reduces error.

### Summary of Standard Phrases

Keep these phrases in your working vocabulary. They are the standard formulations for amendment language in the Bangsamoro Parliament:

| Operation | Standard Phrase |
|---|---|
| Insert words | "after the word '[X],' insert the words '[Y]'" |
| Insert subsection | "A new subsection (x) is hereby inserted in Section [X] to read as follows:" |
| Insert section | "A new Section [X] is hereby inserted after Section [Y] to read as follows:" |
| Delete words | "delete the words '[X]'" |
| Delete subsection | "Subsection (x) of Section [X] is hereby deleted" |
| Delete section | "Section [X] is hereby deleted" |
| Repeal existing law section | "Section [X] of [BAA No. XX] is hereby repealed" |
| Substitute words | "the word '[X]' is hereby amended to read '[Y]'" |
| Substitute words (alternative) | "delete the words '[X]' and insert in lieu thereof the words '[Y]'" |
| Substitute subsection | "Subsection (x) of Section [X] is hereby amended to read as follows:" |
| Substitute section | "Section [X] is hereby amended to read as follows:" |
| Amend existing law section | "Section [X] of [BAA No. XX] is hereby amended to read as follows:" |
| Renumber | "Sections [X] through [Y] are accordingly renumbered as Sections [X+1] through [Y+1]" |

## 10.5 Substitute Bills

A substitute bill replaces the entire text of the original bill with a new version. It is the most sweeping form of amendment and raises particular procedural and drafting challenges.

### When a Substitute Bill Is Appropriate

Substitute bills typically arise in one of these situations:

**The committee stage produced fundamental changes.** Sometimes the committee's scrutiny reveals that the original bill's approach is unworkable and a completely different structure is needed. Rather than offer dozens of individual amendments — which would be confusing and difficult to follow — the committee prepares a substitute bill that incorporates all changes into a clean, coherent text.

**Multiple bills on the same subject need to be consolidated.** When several Members file separate bills on the same topic, the committee may consolidate them into a single substitute bill that draws the best elements from each.

**The bill was overtaken by events.** New information, a change in policy direction, or a Supreme Court decision may require such extensive revision that starting over with a new text makes more practical sense than patching the original.

**Negotiations among Members produce a compromise text.** When competing factions in the Parliament hold different views on a bill, the negotiated compromise may take the form of a substitute bill that replaces the version reported out of committee.

### Procedural Rules

Resolution No. 268 addresses substitute bills directly. Section 14 of Rule XVII states:

> "A bill or resolution shall not be amended by substituting it with another which covers a subject distinct from that proposed in the original bill or resolution."

This is the key constraint. A substitute bill must cover the same subject as the original. You cannot use the substitute bill mechanism to introduce an entirely new legislative measure that has nothing to do with the original bill. The germaneness requirement applies.

The substitute bill must go through the same approval process as any other amendment. If offered as a committee amendment, it is presented to the plenary as part of the committee report. If offered as a floor amendment, it must be submitted in writing — as required for all substitution amendments under Resolution No. 268 — and voted upon by the plenary.

### Drafting a Substitute Bill

When you draft a substitute bill, you are essentially drafting a new bill that will take the place of the original. The substitute bill should:

**Use a fresh numbering system.** Start from Section 1. Do not try to maintain the numbering of the original bill if the structure has changed significantly. A clean numbering system signals to Members that this is a new text, not a patchwork of amendments to the old one.

**Carry the same bill number.** The substitute bill does not receive a new bill number. It carries the number of the original bill. This maintains the legislative record and avoids confusion about which bill is being acted on.

**Include all provisions, not just the changed ones.** A substitute bill must be a complete text. Members must be able to read it as a self-contained bill. Do not assume that readers will refer to the original version for unchanged provisions. If a provision from the original bill is retained in the substitute, reproduce it in full.

**Explain the changes in the committee report.** If the substitute bill originates from the committee, the committee report should explain why a substitute was necessary rather than individual amendments. A comparative matrix showing the differences between the original and the substitute is extremely helpful.

**Preserve the long title or amend it.** If the substitute bill changes the scope of the original bill, the long title must be amended to reflect the new scope. The one-subject rule still applies. Ensure that the long title accurately describes what the substitute bill does.

### Format for Proposing a Substitute Bill

When a committee proposes a substitute bill, the committee report typically includes language such as:

> The Committee on [name], after thorough deliberation, recommends the approval of the attached substitute bill in lieu of [Bill No. XX] filed by [author]. The substitute bill incorporates the committee's amendments and consolidates provisions from [Bill No. XX], [Bill No. YY], and [Bill No. ZZ], which cover the same subject matter.

When a floor amendment proposes a substitute bill, the Member submits the complete text in writing and moves:

> Mr./Madam Speaker, I move that a substitute bill be adopted in lieu of the present text of [Bill No. XX]. The substitute bill has been submitted in writing to the Secretary-General. The substitute covers the same subject matter as the original bill and incorporates amendments that address the concerns raised during the period of interpellation.

### Drafting Tips for Substitute Bills

**Start from the policy objectives, not from the original text.** If the committee decided that a substitute is needed, it usually means the original bill's approach was flawed at a structural level. Do not try to edit the original bill into the substitute. Start fresh with the policy objectives and build the new bill from the ground up.

**Track the differences.** Even though you are drafting a new text, you need to know how it differs from the original. Prepare a comparison document — a comparative matrix or a marked-up version of the original — that shows every change. This will be needed for the committee report and for the plenary debate.

**Check the substitute for internal consistency.** Because substitute bills are often drafted quickly under time pressure, they are prone to internal inconsistencies — a defined term that is not actually used in the body, a cross-reference to a section number that changed, a penalty provision that does not match the prohibitions. Read the substitute bill from beginning to end before finalizing it.

**Do not forget the final provisions.** When drafting a substitute in haste, it is easy to focus on the substantive provisions and forget the housekeeping: separability clause, repealing clause, effectivity clause. Check the final provisions against the standard bill structure (Chapter 3).

### Amendments to Substitute Bills

Once the plenary adopts a substitute bill in lieu of the original, that substitute becomes the text under consideration. Further amendments — both committee amendments and floor amendments — are then made to the substitute bill's text, not to the original bill. This seems obvious, but it is a common source of confusion. Once a substitute is adopted, the original bill no longer exists as a working text. All subsequent references should be to the substitute.

## A Note on Amendatory Bills

This chapter has focused on amendments to pending bills — that is, bills that are still moving through the legislative process. But there is another category of amendment that the drafter encounters regularly: the **amendatory bill**. An amendatory bill is a new bill that proposes to amend an existing, enacted Bangsamoro Autonomy Act.

Amendatory bills follow the full legislative process. They are filed, read, referred to committee, scrutinized, debated, and voted on just like any other bill. The difference is in their content. Instead of establishing new law, they modify law that already exists.

The drafting conventions for amendatory bills include:

**The long title must identify the law being amended.** This is required by Resolution No. 268, Section 7(g) of Rule XVII: "If a bill proposes to amend or revise an existing law, the title must mention the law being amended or revised."

Example:

> AN ACT AMENDING SECTIONS 5, 12, AND 18 OF BANGSAMORO AUTONOMY ACT NO. 29, OTHERWISE KNOWN AS THE "BANGSAMORO CIVIL SERVICE CODE," AND FOR OTHER PURPOSES

**Each amended section must be reproduced in full.** When you amend a section of an existing law, do not show only the changed words. Reproduce the entire section as it will read after the amendment takes effect. This is the standard practice in the Bangsamoro Parliament and in Philippine legislation generally.

**Use the standard amendatory clause.** The body of the bill uses the format described in Section 10.4.3:

> Section [X] of Bangsamoro Autonomy Act No. [XX], otherwise known as the "[short title]," is hereby amended to read as follows:

**Include a repealing clause if necessary.** If the amendatory bill deletes entire sections or provisions of the existing law, include a specific repealing clause:

> Section [X] of Bangsamoro Autonomy Act No. [XX] is hereby repealed.

Do not use general repealing clauses ("all laws, decrees, orders, or parts thereof inconsistent with this Act are hereby repealed or modified accordingly") as a substitute for identifying the specific provisions being repealed. General repealing clauses create uncertainty about what survives and what does not. Be specific.

## Summary

Amendments are the Parliament's tool for refining legislation. Whether offered by a committee after weeks of scrutiny or by a Member during a floor debate, every amendment follows the same basic structure: it identifies a specific part of the text, states the change to be made, and provides the new language.

The drafter's job is to make that change cleanly and precisely. Match the style of the bill. Maintain the numbering. Check the cross-references. And always — always — put the amendment in writing before it is moved on the floor. The parliamentary record depends on it. The quality of the resulting law depends on it. The people who must live under that law depend on it.
