# Chapter 11

# The Legislative Sentence — Language, Style, and Word Choice

The most frequently cited need in interviews with Members of Parliament was "language and tone guidance," mentioned nine times across respondents. This chapter responds to that demand. It addresses the structure of the legislative sentence, the use of modal verbs, the rules of style that distinguish legislative writing from other forms of legal prose, and the specific conventions that govern bills in the Bangsamoro Parliament.

The principles presented here are drawn from two authoritative sources — the Oregon Bill Drafting Manual and Mark Segal's Legislative Drafting — and adapted throughout for the conventions and requirements of the Bangsamoro Parliament. Where Bangsamoro parliamentary practice diverges from international convention, the Bangsamoro practice is stated and explained.

---

## 11.1 The Anatomy of a Legislative Sentence

Every substantive provision in a bill is, at its core, a sentence that answers five questions:

1. **Who** — the legal subject, the person or body to whom the provision is addressed.
2. **Must / May / Must Not** — the type of norm being imposed: an obligation, a permission, or a prohibition.
3. **Do What** — the targeted behavior or action that is required, permitted, or forbidden.
4. **When and How** — the conditions, circumstances, procedures, or exceptions that govern the norm.
5. **Where** — the jurisdictional scope, which in Bangsamoro legislation is usually implicit but may be stated when a provision applies only to a specific province, city, or geographic area.

A well-drafted provision answers each of these questions clearly and in the correct order. The most complex legislative sentence is structured as follows:

```
EXCEPTION --> CASE --> CONDITION --> RULE (Legal Subject + Legal Action)
```

The components operate as follows:

- **Exception** narrows the scope of the provision by excluding certain situations. It is placed first so that the reader who falls within the exception can stop reading.
- **Case** identifies the factual situation to which the provision applies. It is placed second so that the reader knows immediately whether the provision is relevant to the matter at hand.
- **Condition** states any prerequisite that must be satisfied before the rule applies.
- **Rule** states the norm itself: who must, may, or must not do what.

**Example.** The following provision contains all four components:

> *Except as provided in Section 12 of this Act,* [exception] *when a cooperative applies for registration,* [case] *if the cooperative has submitted all documentary requirements,* [condition] *the CSEA shall issue a certificate of registration within thirty (30) days of receipt of the complete application.* [rule]

The principle behind this ordering is reader efficiency. A reader who falls within the exception need not read further. A reader whose situation does not match the case need not read further. Only the reader whose situation matches both the case and the condition must reach the rule. This structure reduces misapplication and aids implementation.

Not every legislative sentence requires all four components. Many provisions state only a rule. Others state a case and a rule, or a condition and a rule. The drafter should include only those components that the provision requires, but should always place them in the correct order when multiple components are present.

## 11.2 Three Types of Normative Provisions

Substantive provisions fall into three categories, each with distinct legal effect:

**Prohibitions** prevent a legal subject from taking a specified action. Prohibitions are the strongest form of normative provision because they restrict liberty. They must be drafted with precision, because an imprecise prohibition may capture conduct that was not intended to be forbidden, or may fail to capture conduct that was.

> *No ministry shall enter into a contract of engagement exceeding three (3) years without the prior approval of the Parliament.*

**Obligations** require a legal subject to take a specified action. Obligations impose duties and must identify clearly who must act, what the action is, and when it must be performed. An obligation without a deadline or a triggering event is an obligation that cannot be enforced.

> *The Ministry of Finance shall submit a quarterly expenditure report to the Committee on Ways and Means within fifteen (15) days after the end of each quarter.*

**Rights and Permissions** authorize a legal subject to take an action at their discretion. Permissions are weaker than obligations; they confer power but do not compel its exercise.

> *The Chief Minister may designate any member of the Cabinet to act on behalf of the Chief Minister during periods of official travel.*

The drafter's first task in drafting any substantive provision is to determine which of these three categories it falls into. The choice determines the modal verb, the sentence structure, and the enforcement mechanism.

## 11.3 Shall, May, Must — The Modal Verbs

The modal verbs — shall, may, and must — are the most important words in legislative drafting. Their misuse is the single most common source of ambiguity in legislation. The following table sets out the correct use of each:

| Word | Meaning | Use When | Example |
|------|---------|----------|---------|
| **shall** | Imposes an obligation or duty | The legal subject is required to perform an act | "The Ministry shall prepare an annual report." |
| **shall not** | Creates a prohibition | The legal subject is forbidden from performing an act | "No person shall discharge pollutants into a waterway." |
| **may** | Confers discretion or permission | The legal subject is authorized but not required to act | "The Chief Minister may extend the deadline by thirty (30) days." |
| **must** | Imposes an obligation (equivalent to "shall") | When clarity requires an alternative to "shall" | "An applicant must submit proof of residency." |
| **must not** | Creates a prohibition (equivalent to "shall not") | When clarity requires an alternative to "shall not" | "A member must not vote on a matter in which the member has a personal interest." |

### Rules for Modal Verbs

**Rule 1: Never use "will" for obligations.** The word "will" denotes future contingency, not legal obligation. In ordinary English, "will" describes what is expected to happen; in legislation, it creates uncertainty about whether the action is mandatory or merely predicted. Use "shall" or "must" for obligations.

- Wrong: "The Ministry will submit the report by March 31."
- Right: "The Ministry shall submit the report by 31 March."

**Rule 2: Never use "should."** The word "should" expresses a recommendation, not a command. It has no legal force. A provision stating that an agency "should" do something cannot be enforced, because "should" implies that non-compliance is acceptable.

- Wrong: "The implementing agency should consult with affected communities."
- Right: "The implementing agency shall consult with affected communities."

**Rule 3: Never use "shall" where "is" suffices.** When a provision states a fact or defines a status rather than imposing a duty, use the present tense. "Shall" is reserved for duties and prohibitions.

- Wrong: "The Bangsamoro Electoral Office shall be an independent body."
- Right: "The Bangsamoro Electoral Office is an independent body."

**Rule 4: Use "shall" only with a legal subject that can perform an act.** A person, body, or office can "shall." An inanimate object cannot. "The report shall be submitted" is passive and obscures responsibility. "The Ministry shall submit the report" is active and identifies who must act.

**Rule 5: Do not combine "shall" with "may" in the same provision unless two distinct norms are intended.** A provision that says an agency "shall and may" do something is contradictory unless one part imposes an obligation and another confers separate discretionary power.

## 11.4 Active Voice Over Passive

Active voice identifies the legal subject — the actor — and makes the obligation, prohibition, or permission explicit. Passive voice obscures who must act. In legislation, obscuring the actor is not a stylistic choice; it is a drafting defect, because it creates doubt about who bears the legal obligation.

| Passive (avoid) | Active (preferred) |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Notice shall be given at least ten (10) days in advance. | The implementing agency shall give notice at least ten (10) days in advance. |
| The report shall be submitted within thirty (30) days. | The Ministry of Finance shall submit the report within thirty (30) days. |
| Applications may be filed during office hours. | An applicant may file an application during office hours. |
| Penalties shall be imposed for violations of this section. | The appropriate adjudicating body shall impose penalties for violations of this section. |
| Funds shall be allocated for the implementation of this Act. | The Bangsamoro Government shall allocate funds for the implementation of this Act. |

There is one situation in which passive voice is acceptable: when the identity of the actor is genuinely irrelevant and the emphasis is properly on the recipient of the action. For example, "Every person accused of an offense is presumed innocent" is a passive construction that is acceptable because the provision establishes a right of the accused, not a duty of any particular actor. Such cases are rare. When in doubt, use active voice.

---

## 11.5 Present Tense

The law speaks continuously. A statute is not a narrative describing events in the past or predictions about the future. It states the law as it applies at the moment of application, whenever that moment may be. For this reason, all normative provisions must be drafted in the present tense.

| Wrong (future tense) | Right (present tense) |
|----------------------|----------------------|
| A person who will violate this section will be subject to a fine. | A person who violates this section is subject to a fine. |
| The Ministry will submit the report within thirty days. | The Ministry shall submit the report within thirty (30) days. |
| The provisions of this Act will take effect fifteen days after publication. | This Act takes effect fifteen (15) days after its complete publication in the Official Gazette or in a newspaper of general circulation. |

The use of "shall" in imposing obligations is not a future-tense construction. "The Ministry shall submit" does not mean "the Ministry will submit at some point"; it means "the Ministry has a present and continuing duty to submit." This distinction is fundamental. The drafter who uses "will" in place of "shall" converts an enforceable obligation into a mere prediction.

## 11.6 Brevity and Sentence Structure

Legislative sentences must be as short as the subject permits. The goal is one idea per sentence. When a provision contains multiple conditions, requirements, or elements, the drafter should break them into a tabulated list rather than compressing them into a single compound sentence.

**Compound sentence (avoid):**

> The Chief Minister shall, upon the recommendation of the Ministry of the Interior and Local Government, and after consultation with the affected local government units, and with the concurrence of the Parliament, declare a state of emergency in any province, city, or municipality within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region when conditions of public disorder, calamity, or epidemic exist that threaten public safety.

**Tabulated list (preferred):**

> The Chief Minister may declare a state of emergency in any province, city, or municipality within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region when all of the following conditions are met:
>
> (a) The Ministry of the Interior and Local Government has recommended the declaration;
>
> (b) The affected local government units have been consulted;
>
> (c) The Parliament has given its concurrence; and
>
> (d) Conditions of public disorder, calamity, or epidemic exist that threaten public safety.

### Conjunctive and Disjunctive Lists

When a provision contains a list, the introductory clause must specify whether the list is conjunctive (all items must be satisfied) or disjunctive (any one item suffices).

- **Conjunctive:** Use "all of the following" or "each of the following" in the introductory clause, and place "and" before the final item.
- **Disjunctive:** Use "any of the following" in the introductory clause, and place "or" before the final item.

Never leave the relationship ambiguous. A list that ends without "and" or "or" before the final item forces the reader to guess whether all items or any single item is sufficient. This ambiguity has caused delays and disputes in the interpretation of legislation worldwide, including in Bangsamoro bills.

## 11.7 Words to Avoid

Legislative language must be plain, precise, and modern. Three categories of words and phrases should be avoided in all Bangsamoro bills.

### Archaic Legalisms

These words were inherited from centuries-old English legal tradition. They add no meaning and impede comprehension.

| Avoid | Replace With |
|-------|-------------|
| hereby | (delete — adds nothing) |
| henceforth | from the effectivity of this Act |
| herein, hereinabove, hereinafter | in this Act / above / below |
| heretofore | before this Act / previously |
| hereunder | under this section / under this Act |
| whatsoever, whosoever, wheresoever | (delete — "any" suffices) |
| aforementioned, aforesaid | (delete, or name the specific provision) |
| forthwith | immediately |
| thereof, therewith, thereto | of it / with it / to it (or restate the noun) |
| inter alia | among others / including |

**Example.**
- Archaic: "The provisions hereinabove mentioned shall hereby be deemed applicable to all entities whatsoever."
- Plain: "This section applies to all entities."

### Redundant Doublets

These paired words are relics of a period when English legal drafters combined Latin, French, and Anglo-Saxon terms for emphasis. In modern drafting, one word suffices.

| Avoid | Replace With |
|-------|-------------|
| null and void | void |
| full and complete | full *or* complete |
| true and correct | true *or* correct |
| each and every | each |
| sole and exclusive | sole *or* exclusive |
| cease and desist | cease *or* stop |
| power and authority | authority |
| rules and regulations | rules (unless technically distinct) |
| due and payable | due |
| made and entered into | made *or* entered into |
| authorize and empower | authorize |
| alter or modify | amend |
| force and effect | effect |

### Wordy Phrases

These phrases can be replaced with shorter, clearer alternatives without loss of meaning.

| Avoid | Replace With |
|-------|-------------|
| in the event that | if |
| in close proximity to | near |
| at the time of | when |
| due to the fact that | because |
| for the purpose of | to |
| in order to | to |
| with respect to | about / regarding |
| in accordance with | under |
| notwithstanding anything to the contrary | despite |
| be deemed to be | is |
| at such time as | when |
| during such time as | while |
| until such time as | until |
| a period of time not less than | for at least |
| not later than | by |
| prior to | before |
| subsequent to | after |
| in excess of | more than |
| a sufficient number of | enough |
| is applicable to | applies to |

### The "And/Or" Problem

The conjunction "and/or" is contradictory. "And" means all items in a list must be present; "or" means any one item suffices. Combining them creates ambiguity about whether the drafter intended a conjunctive, disjunctive, or both reading. Courts and implementing agencies are left guessing.

Despite this, "and/or" appears in BAA Nos. 4, 5, 6, 8, 9, 10, 12, 13, 17, and 18 — ten of the twenty-two laws enacted by the First Parliament. This pattern reflects a habit inherited from ordinary legal writing that has no place in legislative drafting. Future bills should avoid "and/or" entirely.

The fix is simple: decide which meaning is intended and use the correct conjunction.

- If ALL conditions must be met, use "and"
- If ANY ONE condition suffices, use "or"
- If EITHER or BOTH may apply, restructure the sentence: "A, B, or both" or "A, or B, or both A and B"

## 11.8 Consistency in Terminology

A cardinal rule of legislative drafting is that the same word must carry the same meaning throughout a bill, and different words must signal different meanings. Consistency in terminology is not a stylistic preference; it is a legal requirement. Courts construe legislation on the presumption that the legislature uses words deliberately. If a bill uses "officer" in one section and "official" in another, a court may conclude that two different categories of persons were intended. If only one category was meant, the drafter has introduced an ambiguity that may produce litigation.

The practical rules are:

- **Once a term is defined, use only the defined term.** Never substitute a synonym. If the bill defines "Covered Entity," do not later refer to the same class as "affected institution," "covered agency," or "entity covered by this Act."
- **Do not use the same word to mean different things.** If "fund" is used in one section to mean the trust fund established by the bill and in another section to mean general government appropriations, confusion is inevitable. Use a distinct term for each meaning.
- **Maintain consistency with existing law.** If an existing Bangsamoro Autonomy Act uses the term "local government unit" to describe provinces, cities, and municipalities, the drafter should use "local government unit" rather than inventing a new term such as "constituent political subdivision" unless a different concept is intended.
- **Cross-reference defined terms from other laws when necessary.** If a bill relies on a term defined in the Bangsamoro Administrative Code or the BOL, it should either reproduce the definition or incorporate it by reference: "'Ministry' has the meaning assigned to it in Section 4 of the Bangsamoro Administrative Code (BAA No. 13)."

## 11.9 Definitions Best Practices

The Definition of Terms section is one of the most consequential parts of any bill. A poorly drafted definition can distort the operation of every substantive provision that uses the defined term. The following rules govern the drafting of definitions.

### "Means" vs. "Includes" vs. "Refers to" vs. "Does not include"

- **"Means"** creates an exhaustive definition. The term is limited to what follows. If the definition states that "'Covered Entity' means a ministry, bureau, or attached agency," then a government-owned corporation is not a covered entity, even if the drafter intended it to be.
- **"Includes"** creates a partial or illustrative definition. The term encompasses what follows but is not limited to it. If the definition states that "'Covered Entity' includes ministries, bureaus, and attached agencies," a government-owned corporation might also be a covered entity if it shares the characteristics of the listed items.
- **"Refers to"** is used to link a defined term to a concept defined elsewhere: "'BOL' refers to Republic Act No. 11054, otherwise known as the Bangsamoro Organic Law."
- **"Does not include"** explicitly excludes a category that might otherwise fall within the definition. It is typically used in combination with "means" or "includes": "'Public officer' means any person holding a position in the Bangsamoro Government but does not include an independent contractor."

### The Substitution Test

A well-drafted definition should pass the substitution test: the drafter should be able to replace the defined term with its definition in every section of the bill and produce a grammatically correct and substantively coherent sentence. If the substitution produces an awkward or nonsensical result, the definition needs revision.

**Example.** If the bill defines "'Applicant' means any natural or juridical person who submits an application under this Act," then every provision using "Applicant" should read correctly when the definition is substituted in. "The Applicant shall submit two copies" becomes "Any natural or juridical person who submits an application under this Act shall submit two copies" — which is coherent. The definition passes.

### Additional Rules

- List definitions in **alphabetical order**.
- **Define only terms that require definition** — terms with a specialized meaning in the bill, terms that are ambiguous in ordinary usage, or terms whose scope must be narrowed or expanded for the purposes of the bill. Do not define common words that carry their ordinary meaning.
- **Do not include substantive provisions in definitions.** A definition that says "'Implementing Agency' means the Ministry of Education, which shall promulgate rules and regulations within ninety (90) days" is defective because it embeds an obligation in a definition. The obligation belongs in a substantive section.
- **Avoid circular definitions.** A definition that defines a term by reference to itself — "'Registration' means the process of registering" — defines nothing.

## 11.10 Normative vs. Aspirational Language

Legislation that proclaims goals rather than establishing norms is less likely to achieve those goals. The distinction between normative and aspirational language is fundamental to effective drafting.

**Aspirational language** expresses a desire, a goal, or a policy direction. It is appropriate in the Declaration of Policy section, where the bill's purposes are stated in broad terms to guide interpretation. It is not appropriate in substantive provisions, which must specify who must or may do what, under what circumstances, and with what consequences for non-compliance.

**Normative language** creates enforceable legal norms — obligations, prohibitions, and permissions — that identify a legal subject, a legal action, and the conditions under which the norm operates.

| Aspirational (avoid in substantive sections) | Normative (correct) |
|----------------------------------------------|---------------------|
| The government shall endeavor to promote sustainable development in the region. | The Ministry of Environment shall submit an annual environmental impact assessment to the Parliament within thirty (30) days of the end of each fiscal year. |
| It is the policy of the Bangsamoro Government to support the livelihood of fisherfolk. | The Ministry of Agriculture shall establish a Fisherfolk Livelihood Support Program and shall allocate not less than five percent (5%) of its annual budget to the program. |
| The Bangsamoro Government shall ensure that all children have access to quality education. | Every child of school age residing within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region is entitled to free basic education in any public school. The Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education shall ensure that sufficient school facilities and qualified teachers are available in every municipality. |

The test for whether a provision is normative or aspirational is straightforward: Can it be enforced? If a court or implementing agency cannot determine what specific action is required, by whom, and by when, the provision is aspirational. Aspirational provisions in substantive sections are not harmless — they create the impression that something has been accomplished when, in law, nothing has been commanded.

## 11.11 Numbers, Dates, and Amounts

### Amounts

Bangsamoro parliamentary convention requires that monetary amounts be written in **words and figures**: "Five Million Pesos (PHP 5,000,000.00)." This dual expression reduces the risk of typographical error and provides certainty when one form contains a mistake. The word form controls in case of discrepancy.

### Numbers

- Spell out numbers **one through nine** in text: "three copies," "seven days," "nine members."
- Use figures for **10 and above**: "15 days," "30 members," "180 days."
- **Exception:** Always use figures for section numbers, article numbers, percentages, and dates, regardless of the number's size: "Section 3," "Article VII," "5%," "25 March 2026."

### Dates

Bangsamoro bills follow the day-month-year format without internal commas:

- Correct: "25 March 2026"
- Avoid: "March 25, 2026"

When a deadline is measured from a triggering event, state the number of days, not a calendar date, and specify whether the days are calendar days or working days:

- "within thirty (30) calendar days from receipt of the application"
- "within fifteen (15) working days from the date of filing"

## 11.12 Bangsamoro Parliamentary Conventions

The following conventions have been established in the Bangsamoro Parliament and must be observed in all bills.

### Dash After Section Title

Bangsamoro bills place a period after the section title, followed by an en-dash ( — ) or em-dash ( --- ), before the text of the section begins:

```
Section 1. Short Title. — This Act shall be known as the
"Bangsamoro Cooperative Development Act."
```

Some enacted BAAs use an em-dash. Either is acceptable, but the drafter must be consistent within a single bill. Do not use a colon or a hyphen.

### Defined Terms

In the Definition of Terms section, defined terms are set in **bold** and enclosed in quotation marks:

```
(a) **"Cooperative"** — refers to a duly registered association of
persons with a common bond of interest...
```

### Foreign Words

Non-English terms — including Arabic, Maranao, Tausug, and Maguindanaon words — are set in *italics*:

```
The *Darul-Ifta'* shall serve as the official body for the
issuance of Islamic legal opinions (*fatwa*).
```

### Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him)

On first mention, the full honorific is used: "Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him)." Subsequent mentions may use the Arabic honorific symbol where the bill follows Islamic convention, as in BAA No. 20 (Bangsamoro Halal Act).

### The *Provided, That* Convention

Bangsamoro bills follow the Philippine legislative convention of using *Provided, That* (capitalized P and T, italicized) for inline exceptions and qualifications:

```
The Ministry shall allocate funds for the program: Provided, That
no allocation shall exceed ten percent (10%) of the annual budget.
```

However, where the exception is complex or lengthy, a separate subsection or a new sentence beginning with "However" is preferred. Overuse of provisos produces dense, difficult prose.

### Gender-Neutral Language

Bangsamoro bills use gender-neutral terms: "Chairperson" rather than "Chairman," "he or she" rather than "he" alone. When a provision refers to a hypothetical individual, "a person," "any individual," or "he or she" are acceptable. Avoid constructions that assume a single gender for officeholders.

### The Enacting Clause

Every bill must include the enacting clause in the form prescribed at the time of filing. During the transition period, the prescribed form was:

```
Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority in Parliament assembled:
```

Under the regular Bangsamoro Government, the enacting clause will reflect the elected Parliament's official designation. In all cases, this clause may not be altered, abbreviated, or paraphrased.

## 11.13 Common Drafting Mistakes

Review of bills filed before the Parliament has identified several recurring drafting errors. Awareness of these mistakes can help drafters avoid them.

**Compound sentences that obscure meaning.** The most common structural defect is the use of long compound sentences that combine multiple conditions, obligations, and exceptions in a single provision. Such sentences are difficult to read, harder to amend, and frequently produce disagreement about their meaning during interpellation. The remedy is tabulation: break compound sentences into lettered or numbered subsections.

**Inconsistent formatting across provisions.** Bills sometimes vary their internal formatting — using numbered lists in one section, lettered lists in another, and inline text in a third, with no discernible principle governing the choice. The remedy is to adopt a uniform convention at the outset and maintain it throughout the bill.

**Ambiguous use of "and/or."** The construction "and/or" is imprecise because it leaves unclear whether the provision requires both items, either item, or any combination. Replace "and/or" with the appropriate conjunctive ("and") or disjunctive ("or") construction, or use "or both" when that is the intended meaning.

**Sophisticated vocabulary instead of plain language.** Some drafters use complex or formal language in the belief that legislation requires elevated prose. It does not. Legislation requires precise prose. "Utilize" adds nothing that "use" does not convey. "Subsequent to" is no more precise than "after." "In the event that" is no more precise than "if." Plain language reduces misunderstanding and improves implementation.

**Missing deadlines.** Obligations that do not specify a deadline cannot be enforced. "The implementing agency shall promulgate the IRR" is incomplete. "The implementing agency shall promulgate the IRR within ninety (90) days from the effectivity of this Act" is enforceable.

**Aspirational provisions in substantive sections.** As discussed in Section 11.10, substantive provisions that express goals rather than commands are unenforceable. Every substantive section must identify who must act, what the act is, and when it must be performed.

**Failure to designate an implementing agency.** A bill that creates obligations but does not specify which ministry, office, or body is responsible for implementation creates an accountability gap. Every obligation must be assigned to a named entity.

## 11.14 Quality Review Checklist

Before a bill is filed, the drafter should review the entire text against the following checklist. Each item should be verified independently.

**Arrangement**

- [ ] Sections follow the standard Bangsamoro bill sequence: short title, declaration of policy, definition of terms, substantive provisions, penal provisions, transitory provisions, separability clause, repealing clause, effectivity clause.
- [ ] Sections are numbered sequentially without gaps.
- [ ] Subsections use the correct Bangsamoro lettering convention: (a), (b), (c) for subsections; (1), (2), (3) for paragraphs within subsections.
- [ ] The logical flow moves from general to specific, from rights to obligations to prohibitions.

**Style and Word Choice**

- [ ] Active voice is used throughout, except where passive voice is justified.
- [ ] Present tense is used for all normative provisions.
- [ ] Sentences are concise — one idea per sentence.
- [ ] Compound sentences have been broken into tabulated lists where appropriate.
- [ ] No archaic legalisms: "hereby," "henceforth," "aforesaid," and similar terms have been removed.
- [ ] No redundant doublets: "null and void," "each and every," "rules and regulations," and similar pairs have been reduced to a single term.
- [ ] No wordy phrases: "in the event that," "for the purpose of," "due to the fact that," and similar constructions have been simplified.
- [ ] "And/or" does not appear anywhere in the bill.
- [ ] Plain language is used throughout; no unnecessarily sophisticated vocabulary.

**Modal Verbs**

- [ ] "Shall" is used only to impose obligations, never as a future-tense marker.
- [ ] "May" is used only to confer discretion or permission.
- [ ] "Shall not" or "must not" is used for prohibitions.
- [ ] "Will" does not appear in normative provisions.
- [ ] "Should" does not appear anywhere in the bill.

**Definitions**

- [ ] All defined terms are used consistently throughout the bill.
- [ ] Definitions are listed in alphabetical order.
- [ ] "Means" is used for exhaustive definitions; "includes" for illustrative definitions.
- [ ] No substantive provisions are embedded in definitions.
- [ ] Every defined term passes the substitution test.
- [ ] No circular definitions.

**Cross-References**

- [ ] All internal cross-references (e.g., "as provided in Section 5") are correct and point to the intended provision.
- [ ] Cross-references to external laws cite the correct law number, section, and title.
- [ ] If an external law has been amended, the cross-reference accounts for the amendment.

**Constitutional and Legal Compliance**

- [ ] The bill's subject matter falls within the enumerated powers of the Bangsamoro Parliament under Article V, Section 2, or the incidental powers under Section 3 of the BOL.
- [ ] No provision conflicts with the Philippine Constitution.
- [ ] No provision conflicts with the BOL.
- [ ] No provision conflicts with an existing Bangsamoro Autonomy Act, unless the bill expressly repeals or amends that provision.
- [ ] No provision conflicts with applicable national law in areas where Bangsamoro and national authority overlap.

**Title**

- [ ] The long title accurately describes the scope and content of the bill.
- [ ] Every substantive provision in the bill is covered by the long title.
- [ ] The short title is concise and distinctive.

**Gender and Cultural Sensitivity**

- [ ] Gender-neutral language is used throughout.
- [ ] Islamic honorifics and conventions are observed where applicable.
- [ ] Foreign terms are italicized with English translations where necessary.

**Closing Provisions**

- [ ] Separability clause is included.
- [ ] Repealing clause is included and specifies the provisions being repealed.
- [ ] Effectivity clause is included and specifies when the Act takes effect.
- [ ] IRR provision is included where the bill requires implementing rules, with a clear deadline.

# Glossary of Legislative Terms

The following terms are used throughout this manual and in the legislative practice of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament. They are arranged in alphabetical order.

**Amendment.** A change to the text of a bill or an existing law. Amendments may be proposed during the committee stage or during the period of amendments on second reading. An amendment may add, delete, or modify provisions. Amendments to existing Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts are accomplished through amendatory bills that identify the specific provision being amended and set out the new text.

**Appropriation.** The legislative authorization to spend public funds for a specified purpose. Under the BOL, the Bangsamoro Government exercises fiscal autonomy, and the annual budget — the General Appropriations Act — is enacted by the Parliament. No public funds may be disbursed without an appropriation authorized by law.

**BAA (Bangsamoro Autonomy Act).** The designation given to a bill that has been approved by the Parliament on third reading and signed by the Chief Minister, or that has lapsed into law. BAAs are numbered sequentially and constitute the primary body of Bangsamoro regional legislation. As of the current legislative period, eighty-one BAAs have been enacted.

**Bill.** A proposed law submitted to the Parliament for consideration. A bill must pass three readings on separate days before it can become a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act. Bills may originate from Members of Parliament (private member bills), from the Cabinet (cabinet bills), or from committees (committee bills).

**BOL (Bangsamoro Organic Law).** Republic Act No. 11054, signed on 27 July 2018 and ratified by plebiscite in January and February 2019. The BOL is the foundational law of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region and serves as the regional equivalent of a constitution. All Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts must be consistent with it.

**Cabinet Bill.** A bill that originates from the executive branch — the Chief Minister or a member of the Cabinet — and is filed in the Parliament as part of the government's legislative program. Cabinet bills typically reflect the policy priorities endorsed by the Parliament through its approval of the Chief Minister's platform of government.

**Caption.** See *Long Title*.

**Catchline.** The descriptive title of a section within a bill, placed immediately after the section number. In Bangsamoro parliamentary convention, the catchline is followed by a period, an en-dash or em-dash, and the text of the section. Example: "Section 5. *Covered Entities.* — The following entities are covered by this Act..."

**Chief Minister.** The head of government of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, elected by and from among the members of the Parliament. The Chief Minister exercises executive authority on behalf of the Parliament, presides over the Cabinet, and signs bills into law. Under Article VII, Section 25(c) of the BOL, the Chief Minister has thirty days to sign or veto a bill after receipt; failure to act within that period results in the bill becoming law.

**Committee Bill.** A bill that originates from a standing committee of the Parliament, often as a result of the committee's consolidation of multiple related bills on the same subject. Committee bills are drafted by committee staff and reported to the plenary with a committee report.

**Committee Report.** The formal document prepared by a committee after its deliberation on a bill. The committee report contains the committee's findings, its recommendation (to approve, amend, or reject the bill), and the text of the bill as amended by the committee. The committee report accompanies the bill when it is calendared for second reading.

**Overlapping Authority.** Areas where the Bangsamoro Government's enumerated powers under Article V, Section 2 overlap with matters where national legislation also operates — such as education standards, labor, and disaster preparedness — so that both the Parliament and the Congress of the Philippines may legislate. When a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act conflicts with a national law in such an area, the conflict is resolved with reference to the BOL and the Philippine Constitution.

**Declaration of Policy.** The section of a bill, typically Section 2, that sets out the purposes and policy objectives of the legislation. The Declaration of Policy guides the interpretation of the bill's substantive provisions and may state aspirational goals. However, it does not create enforceable obligations; those must be stated in the substantive provisions.

**Definition of Terms.** The section of a bill, typically Section 3, that defines words and phrases used in the bill with a specialized or technical meaning. Definitions are listed in alphabetical order. The definitions section uses "means" for exhaustive definitions and "includes" for illustrative definitions.

**Effectivity.** The date or event upon which a law takes effect. The effectivity clause is the final section of a bill and typically provides that the Act takes effect fifteen days after its complete publication in the Official Gazette or in a newspaper of general circulation. Some bills designate a specific date of effectivity or provide that the Act takes effect immediately upon approval.

**Enacting Clause.** The formulaic clause that precedes the body of the bill and signifies the Parliament's authority to enact the legislation. The drafter must use the form prescribed at the time of filing (see Section 11.12). This clause may not be altered or omitted.

**Enrolled Bill.** The final, official copy of a bill as approved by the Parliament on third reading, certified by the Speaker and the Secretary General, and presented to the Chief Minister for signature. The enrolled bill is the authoritative text of the legislation.

**Enumerated Powers.** The matters over which the Bangsamoro Government exercises authority, as listed in Article V, Section 2 of the BOL. The enumeration runs from subsection (a) through (ccc) — over fifty subject areas including administrative organization, ancestral domain management, social services, and regional economic development. These are supplemented by implied and incidental powers under Article V, Section 3 (the general welfare clause). All matters not covered by these provisions fall to the National Government under the residual clause (Article V, Section 1). See also *Residual Clause*.

**First Reading.** The initial stage of the legislative process. On first reading, the bill is filed, assigned a number, read by title, and referred to the appropriate standing committee or committees for deliberation. No debate or amendment takes place on first reading.

**GAA (General Appropriations Act).** The annual budget legislation enacted by the Parliament, authorizing the expenditure of public funds for the programs, projects, and operations of the Bangsamoro Government for a given fiscal year.

**Interpellation.** The stage of the legislative process, occurring during second reading, in which Members of Parliament who are not sponsors of the bill may ask questions of the sponsor regarding the bill's provisions, intent, and implications. Interpellation follows the presentation of the committee report and precedes the period of amendments. It is the primary mechanism through which the plenary examines a bill's merits and identifies potential issues.

**IRR (Implementing Rules and Regulations).** The detailed rules promulgated by the executive branch to implement the provisions of a law. Many BAAs authorize the implementing agency to issue an IRR within a specified period, typically ninety to one hundred eighty days from effectivity. The IRR must be consistent with the law it implements and may not expand or diminish the rights and obligations established by the law.

**Long Title.** The full descriptive title of a bill, appearing at the beginning of the document. The long title describes the scope and subject matter of the bill and serves as a guide to its contents. Under Philippine and Bangsamoro convention, no provision may be included in a bill that is not embraced in its long title. Also referred to as the *caption*.

**Majority Floor Leader.** The Member of Parliament designated to manage the order of business on the floor during plenary sessions on behalf of the majority. The Majority Floor Leader coordinates the scheduling of bills for consideration, manages the flow of debate, and facilitates the passage of the government's legislative program.

**Manifestation.** A brief statement made by a Member of Parliament during a plenary session to raise a matter of record, make an announcement, or express a position that does not require formal legislative action. Manifestations are part of the parliamentary record but do not carry the force of law or resolution.

**MP (Member of Parliament).** An individual appointed or, upon the holding of parliamentary elections, elected to serve as a member of the Bangsamoro Parliament. MPs exercise legislative power, participate in committee deliberations, and vote on bills and resolutions. Under the parliamentary system, MPs who are appointed to the Cabinet exercise executive functions concurrently.

**Minority Floor Leader.** The Member of Parliament designated to represent the interests of the minority in the plenary. The Minority Floor Leader ensures that dissenting views are heard during deliberations and that the rights of minority members are protected under the Rules of the Parliament.

**Order of Business.** The prescribed sequence of activities during a plenary session, as established by the Rules of the Parliament. The order typically includes the call to order, roll call, approval of minutes, reference of business, unfinished business, business for the day (committee reports, bills on second or third reading), and adjournment.

**Parliamentary Resolution.** A formal expression of the will of the Parliament that does not carry the force of law. Resolutions may express the sense of the Parliament on matters of public concern, commend individuals or institutions, request executive action, or authorize internal parliamentary business. Resolutions do not require presentment to the Chief Minister and do not undergo the three-readings process required for bills.

**Penal Provisions.** The sections of a bill that prescribe penalties for violations of the bill's substantive provisions. Penal provisions are placed after the substantive sections and before the transitory and final provisions. Penalties must be proportionate to the offense, implementable by the designated enforcement body, and consistent with the constitutional prohibition against cruel, degrading, or inhuman punishment.

**Plenary.** A session of the Parliament in which all members are convened for the transaction of legislative business, as distinguished from committee sessions. Bills are debated, amended, and voted upon in plenary. A quorum — a majority of all members — must be present for the Parliament to transact business.

**Private Member Bill.** A bill filed by an individual Member of Parliament, as distinguished from a cabinet bill or a committee bill. Any MP may file a bill on any subject within the Parliament's jurisdiction, subject to the rules on form and filing.

**Privilege Speech.** An address delivered by a Member of Parliament during a plenary session, invoking parliamentary privilege, on a matter of public importance or affecting the rights and dignity of the Parliament or its members. Privilege speeches are entered into the parliamentary record and may serve as the basis for further legislative action.

**Proposed Bill.** A bill that has been filed and assigned a number but has not yet been enacted into law. A proposed bill remains pending before the committee to which it is referred or before the plenary at second or third reading. At the end of a session, unfiled or unapproved bills may be refiled in the succeeding session.

**Quorum.** The minimum number of members required to be present for the Parliament to transact business. Under Article VII, Section 26 of the BOL, a majority of all the members of the Parliament constitutes a quorum. The absence of a quorum prevents the body from taking action on any legislative matter.

**Repealing Clause.** The section of a bill that identifies laws, provisions, or portions of laws that are repealed — that is, rendered inoperative — by the new legislation. Repealing clauses should be explicit, identifying the specific provisions being repealed by number and title. General repealing clauses ("all laws inconsistent herewith are hereby repealed") are disfavored because they create uncertainty about which provisions have been affected.

**Residual Clause.** The principle established in Article V, Section 1 of the BOL: "All powers, functions, and responsibilities not granted by the Constitution or by national law to the Bangsamoro Government shall be vested in the National Government." This means the Parliament may not legislate on any matter that falls outside the enumerated powers (Sec. 2) and incidental powers (Sec. 3). See also *Enumerated Powers*.

**Resolution.** See *Parliamentary Resolution*.

**Saving Clause.** A provision in a bill that preserves the validity and effect of actions taken, rights acquired, or proceedings commenced under a law that is being repealed or amended. The saving clause prevents the new law from retroactively invalidating matters that were lawful under the prior law.

**Second Reading.** The stage of the legislative process at which the bill, as reported by the committee, is presented to the plenary for debate. Second reading consists of three phases: the sponsorship (presentation of the committee report), the period of interpellation (questioning by non-sponsor MPs), and the period of amendments (during which changes to the bill may be proposed and voted upon). The bill is approved on second reading by a majority vote.

**Separability Clause.** A standard provision included in virtually all Bangsamoro bills, stating that if any section, provision, or part of the Act is declared unconstitutional or invalid, the remaining provisions shall not be affected and shall continue in full force and effect. The separability clause prevents a judicial finding of invalidity against one part of the law from invalidating the entire Act.

**Session.** A period during which the Parliament meets to transact business. Under the Rules of the Parliament, sessions may be regular or special. The regular session follows the legislative calendar adopted by the Parliament. Special sessions may be called by the Speaker or by a majority of members to address urgent matters.

**Short Title.** The brief, distinctive name by which an Act is known, stated in Section 1 of the bill. The short title provides a convenient reference for citing the law. Example: "This Act shall be known as the 'Bangsamoro Youth Development Act.'"

**Speaker.** The presiding officer of the Bangsamoro Parliament, elected by and from among the members. The Speaker maintains order during plenary sessions, rules on points of order, signs enrolled bills, and represents the Parliament in its relations with other branches of government and external institutions.

**Sponsor.** The Member of Parliament who presents a bill to the plenary on behalf of the committee that deliberated on it. The sponsor defends the bill during the period of interpellation, answers questions from fellow members, and proposes or accepts amendments during the period of amendments. The sponsor may or may not be the author of the original bill.

**Substantive Provisions.** The core sections of a bill that establish the rights, obligations, prohibitions, institutional structures, and operational mechanisms that constitute the law's operative content. Substantive provisions follow the Definition of Terms and precede the Penal, Transitory, and Final Provisions. They are the heart of the bill.

**Sunset Clause.** A provision that sets an expiration date for a law or for specific provisions within a law. After the sunset date, the law or provision ceases to have effect unless renewed by the Parliament. Sunset clauses are used to ensure periodic legislative review of programs, agencies, or regulatory measures that may no longer be necessary or effective.

**Third Reading.** The final stage of the legislative process before presentment to the Chief Minister. On third reading, the bill in its final form — incorporating all amendments approved on second reading — is voted upon by the plenary without further debate or amendment. Printed copies of the bill in its final form must be distributed to members at least three days before the vote, except when the Chief Minister certifies the bill as urgent.

**Transitory Provisions.** The sections of a bill that govern the transition from the prior legal regime to the new one. Transitory provisions address matters such as the status of existing contracts, pending applications, current officeholders, and ongoing programs that may be affected by the new law. They ensure continuity and prevent disruption during the shift to the new legal framework.

**Veto.** The power of the Chief Minister to decline to sign a bill passed by the Parliament. Under Article VII, Section 25(c) of the BOL, the Chief Minister may veto a bill by declining to sign it within the thirty-day period following receipt. However, if the Chief Minister neither signs nor vetoes a bill within thirty days, the bill lapses into law as if the Chief Minister had signed it. The BOL does not provide for a legislative override of the veto, distinguishing the Bangsamoro system from the national government's veto-override mechanism.
