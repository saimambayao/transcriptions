# Chapter 9

# The Body of a Bill — Section by Section

The body of a bill is the operative text that, upon enactment, becomes law. Chapter 7 identified the three groups of provisions — General, Main, and Final — that compose the body. This chapter examines each standard section type in detail, providing the drafter with precise guidance on purpose, formulation, and placement. Throughout, examples are drawn from enacted Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts so that every principle is grounded in the Parliament's own legislative practice.

The section types described below follow the standard sequence set out in Section 6.3 of this manual. Not every bill will contain every section type. A simple bill adopting an official symbol may require only a Short Title, a Declaration of Policy, a single substantive provision, a Separability Clause, and an Effectivity Clause. A comprehensive code establishing a new institution will require nearly all of them. The drafter's task is to select the sections that the bill's subject matter demands and to arrange them in the order that best serves the reader's comprehension.

## 9.1 Short Title

The Short Title is ordinarily the first section of the body. Its purpose is to provide an abbreviated name by which the act may be cited in subsequent legislation, legal proceedings, academic commentary, and ordinary discourse. Without a Short Title, a law must be cited by its full long title or by its BAA number — both of which are cumbersome in running text and parliamentary debate.

### Formulation

The standard formula for the Short Title is:

> "This Act shall be known as the '[Name] Act of [Year].'"

The name should be concise and descriptive. It should convey the bill's subject matter in as few words as possible while remaining distinctive enough to avoid confusion with other acts. The year should be the year of expected enactment, not the year of filing, since the Short Title will be associated with the law for the duration of its existence.

The section heading should read "Short Title" — not "Title," "Name," or "Citation." The term "Short Title" is the settled legislative convention in the Bangsamoro Parliament and in Philippine legislative drafting generally.

### Examples from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 7 provides the simplest formulation:

> **Section 1. *Short Title.*** — This Act shall be known as the Bangsamoro Hymn Act of 2020.

BAA No. 20 uses quotation marks around the name:

> **Section 1. Short Title.** — This Act shall be known as "The Bangsamoro Dual Calendar Act of 2021."

BAA No. 5 likewise uses quotation marks:

> **Section 1. Title.** — This Act shall be known as the "Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office Act of 2019".

Note that BAA No. 5 uses "Title" rather than "Short Title" as its section heading. While acceptable in enacted law, the preferred heading is "Short Title," which more precisely describes the section's function: providing an abbreviated citation form distinct from the long title that appears in the caption. Drafters should adopt the preferred formulation in new bills.

### Drafting Guidance

The Short Title should not repeat the full long title. The long title of BAA No. 20 runs to thirty-seven words; its Short Title reduces this to eight words. This compression is the Short Title's entire purpose.

Where a bill creates or operationalizes an institution, the Short Title typically incorporates the institution's name: "Bangsamoro Human Rights Act of 2019" (BAA No. 4), "Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office Act of 2019" (BAA No. 5). Where a bill mandates an action or establishes a program, the Short Title names the subject matter: "The Bangsamoro Dual Calendar Act of 2021" (BAA No. 20), "Bangsamoro Hymn Act of 2020" (BAA No. 7).

Avoid unnecessarily long Short Titles. If the Short Title approaches the length of the long title, it is no longer performing its abbreviating function. Avoid also the temptation to make the Short Title clever or poetic; legislative citation demands precision, not flair.

## 9.2 Declaration of Policy

The Declaration of Policy is a preliminary provision that states the broad guiding principles and the necessity for the legislation. It appears after the Short Title and before the substantive provisions. Its function is interpretive rather than operative: it does not itself create rights or impose duties, but it provides the framework within which the substantive provisions are to be understood.

### Purpose and Legal Effect

The Declaration of Policy serves three functions. First, it connects the bill to the constitutional and organic law framework by identifying the values and mandates that the legislation seeks to advance. Second, it provides courts and implementing agencies with interpretive guidance when the substantive provisions are ambiguous — a court confronted with two plausible readings of an operative section may prefer the reading that better serves the declared policy. Third, it communicates to the public the purpose and rationale of the law in language that is broader and more accessible than the technical operative provisions.

A Declaration of Policy is not, however, a source of independent legal obligation. No right can be asserted, no duty imposed, and no penalty enforced solely on the basis of a Declaration of Policy provision. Its force is interpretive, not operative. The drafter should therefore ensure that every policy objective stated in this section is carried into effect by a specific substantive provision elsewhere in the bill. A policy declaration that is not supported by operative provisions is an aspiration without legal consequence.

### Formulation

The Declaration of Policy typically opens with the phrase: "It is hereby declared the policy of the Bangsamoro Government that..." followed by a statement of the principles that animate the legislation. It should cite the constitutional or BOL provisions that authorize or compel the legislative action.

### Example from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 20 provides a representative Declaration of Policy:

> **Section 2. Declaration of Policy.** — It is hereby declared the policy of the Bangsamoro Government that in the exercise of its right to self-governance, it shall be free to pursue its political, economic, social, and cultural development as provided in Republic Act (R. A.) No. 11054 otherwise known as the Organic Law for the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao. Towards this end, the Bangsamoro Government adopts and mandates the dual use of *Hijri* and Gregorian Calendars in the conduct of its business recognizing the primacy of the predominant way of life in the region and encourage all-inclusive, understanding, coexistence and harmony in all levels of governance.

This provision identifies the legal basis (RA 11054), the principle (the right to self-governance and cultural development), and the specific legislative response (mandating dual calendar use). It connects the bill's subject to the broader constitutional framework without itself creating the operative mandate, which is accomplished by Section 5 of the same act.

BAA No. 4 offers a more succinct formulation:

> **Section 2. Declaration of Policy.** — The government values the dignity of every human person and guarantees full respect for human rights. It shall give the highest priority to the enactment of measures that protect and enhance the right of all people to human dignity at all stages of human life.

BAA No. 7 is more concise still:

> **Section 2. *Declaration of Policy.*** — In the exercise of its right to self-governance, the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao is free to adopt its Bangsamoro Hymn that conveys and preserves the peoples' identity, history, heritage, and aspirations.

The length and detail of the Declaration of Policy should be proportionate to the bill's complexity. A bill establishing a comprehensive institution like the Human Rights Commission warrants a more extensive policy declaration than a bill adopting an official hymn.

## 9.3 Purpose or Objective

The Purpose or Objective section states the specific aims of the legislation. Where the Declaration of Policy articulates broad principles, the Purpose section translates those principles into concrete goals that the bill is designed to achieve. This section strengthens the law against constitutional challenge by demonstrating that the legislation pursues a legitimate governmental purpose and that its means are rationally related to its ends.

### Distinction from Declaration of Policy

The distinction between the Declaration of Policy and the Purpose section is one of abstraction. The Declaration of Policy says, in effect, "This is what we believe and value." The Purpose section says, "This is what we intend to accomplish." Some bills combine both into a single provision; others separate them. The drafter should use separate provisions when the distinction between the animating principles and the specific objectives is clear enough to warrant independent treatment.

### Example from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 20 separates its Declaration of Policy (Section 2) from its Objectives (Section 6). The Objectives provision reads:

> **Section 6. Objectives.** — This law aims to:
>
> (a) Put into action inclusiveness in all public affairs, transactions, and develop awareness among the constituents of the Bangsamoro of the *Hijri* and Gregorian Calendars;
> (b) Familiarize the calendar originated from the migration of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) from Makkah to Madinah;
> (c) Fulfill equally the declaration of the Islamic Holidays;
> (d) Distinguish *Hijri* calendar from Gregorian and other calendars;
> (e) Promote all Muslim Holidays as stipulated in the Presidential Decree (P.D.) No. 1083 otherwise known as the Code on Muslim Personal Laws of the Philippines;
> (f) Think of the historical event of the *Hijrah* of the Holy Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), from Makkah to Madinah to escape persecution;
> (g) Establish the dual use of *Hijri* and Gregorian Calendars within the core territory of the BARMM; and
> (h) Recognize and inculcate in the minds of the Bangsamoro people that the most important migration in the glorious history of Islam was the migration of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) from Makkah to Madina in the Month of *Rabi Al-Awwal*, in the 13th year of the Prophetic mission.

This enumerated format is appropriate when a bill has multiple distinct objectives. The lettered paragraphs provide clarity and facilitate reference during committee deliberation and floor debate.

### Drafting Guidance

Every objective stated in this section should correspond to at least one substantive provision in the body of the bill. An objective that is listed but not operationalized is a promise without fulfillment. Conversely, a substantive provision that does not serve any stated objective may be questioned as beyond the bill's stated purpose.

## 9.4 Definition of Terms

The Definition of Terms section assigns precise meanings to words and phrases used throughout the bill. It is one of the most consequential provisions in any piece of legislation, because the meaning of a defined term controls the scope and application of every provision in which that term appears. A poorly drafted definition can expand or contract a bill's reach in ways the drafter did not intend; a well-drafted definition eliminates the interpretive disputes that delay implementation and invite litigation.

### Placement and Arrangement

The Definition of Terms section should appear early in the bill, after the Declaration of Policy and Purpose provisions and before the substantive provisions. Definitions must be available to the reader before the terms they define are encountered in the operative text. Defined terms should be arranged in alphabetical order, a convention that facilitates quick reference and ensures that no term is inadvertently omitted or duplicated.

### Types of Definitions

Legislative definitions fall into three categories, distinguished by their relationship to the ordinary meaning of the term being defined:

**Delimiting definitions** narrow the ordinary meaning of a term to a specific subset of its usual scope. A delimiting definition says, in effect, "For purposes of this Act, this word means only this particular thing, even though in ordinary usage it might mean something broader." When a bill defines "employee" to mean only persons in the service of the Bangsamoro Government, as BAA No. 5 does, it is using a delimiting definition that excludes private-sector employees from the term's scope within the Act.

**Extending definitions** expand the ordinary meaning of a term to include things it would not normally cover. An extending definition says, "For purposes of this Act, this word includes not only its ordinary meaning but also this additional thing." Such definitions are typically signaled by the word "includes."

**Restricting definitions** explicitly exclude certain things from a term's meaning. A restricting definition says, "For purposes of this Act, this word does not include this particular thing." The signal phrase is "does not include."

### Key Definition Words

The choice of connecting word in a definition carries legal significance:

**"Means"** introduces an exhaustive definition. When a bill provides that a term "means X, Y, and Z," it means exactly X, Y, and Z — nothing more. The list is closed. A court may not add to it by implication. This is the strongest and most precise form of definition and should be used whenever the drafter intends to control the term's meaning completely.

**"Includes"** introduces a partial or illustrative definition. When a bill provides that a term "includes X, Y, and Z," it means at least X, Y, and Z, but may also encompass other things within the term's ordinary meaning. The list is open. This formulation is appropriate when the drafter wants to ensure that certain borderline cases are covered without foreclosing the possibility that other cases may also fall within the term's scope.

**"Refers to"** is a fallback formulation that points the reader to an external source for the term's meaning — typically another statute, a regulatory body's definition, or a commonly understood standard. This formulation is useful when incorporating established definitions from other laws.

**"Does not include"** operates as an exclusion. It preserves the term's ordinary or defined meaning but carves out specified exceptions. This formulation is often used in combination with "means" or "includes" to create precise boundaries.

### Example from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 20 illustrates a well-structured Definition of Terms section that employs different definition techniques:

> **Section 3. Definition of terms.** — The following terms, as used in this Act, shall mean:
>
> (a) **"*Hijri* Calendar"** — Muslims around the world use Islamic calendar following the *Hijrah* (also known as the lunar calendar) to determine the dates of religious events and observances. It is based on 12 lunar months. A new month begins when a new moon is sighted and consists of 354 or 355 days. It consists of the following 12 months in numerical order, such as:
>
> 1. *Muharram*
> 2. *Safar*
> 3. *Rabi Al-Awwal*
> 4. *Rabi Al-Thani/Rabi Al-Akhir*
> 5. *Jumada Al-Awwal*
> 6. *Jumada Al-Thani/Jumada Al-Akhirah*
> 7. *Rajab*
> 8. *Sha'ban*
> 9. *Ramadhan* (the month of fasting)
> 10. *Shawwal*
> 11. *Dhul Al-Qa'dah*
> 12. *Dhul Al-Hijjah* (month of *Hajj*)
>
> (b) **"Gregorian Calendar"** — is a solar dating system used by most of the world. It is named after Pope Gregory XIII, who issued the papal bull *inter gravissimas* in 1582, announcing calendar reforms for all of Catholic Christendom.
>
> (c) **"*Hijrah*"** — means the migration of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) from Makkah to Madinah (*Yathrib*) upon invitation in order to escape persecution.

Note that paragraph (c) uses the word "means," signaling an exhaustive definition. The definitions in paragraphs (a) and (b) take a more descriptive, encyclopedic approach — a style that, while found in enacted law, is more verbose than optimal drafting practice would recommend. The drafter should prefer definitions that state the essential legal meaning of the term without incorporating background information that belongs in the explanatory note rather than the operative text.

BAA No. 5 offers a more concise and legally functional approach:

> **Section 2. Definition of Terms.** — For purposes of this Act, the following terms are hereby defined as follows:
>
> (a) "Attorney-General" refers to the person appointed as such under this Act.
> (b) "Employee", when used with reference to a person in the public service, includes any person in the service of the Bangsamoro Government or any of its ministries, agencies, offices, or instrumentalities.
> (c) "Legal officer" refers to a person appointed to carry out a legal service in the Bangsamoro ministries, agencies, offices, or instrumentalities.
> (d) "Legal service" refers to services involving legal duties such as issuance of legal opinions, initiation or resolution of administrative cases, provision of legal advice, representation in courts, and/or law related matters.
> (e) "Chief Minister" refers to the Head of Government of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.
> (f) "Office" refers to the Bangsamoro Attorney-General's Office.
> (g) "Officer" as distinguished from "clerk" or "employee", refers to a person whose duties, not being of a clerical or manual nature, involve the exercise of discretion in the performance of the functions of the Bangsamoro Government.

Here, each definition is tightly focused on its legal function. The term "includes" in paragraph (b) signals an extending definition; the term "refers to" in paragraphs (a), (c), (d), (e), and (f) directs the reader to an external referent. These are the kinds of definitions that serve well in practice.

### Drafting Guidance

Define every term that is used in a technical or specialized sense. Do not define terms that are used in their ordinary meaning — doing so clutters the section without adding value. Once a term is defined, use it consistently throughout the bill and do not vary the wording; variation invites the inference that a different meaning was intended. A defined term should appear in the bill's operative text in the exact form in which it was defined.

## 9.5 Interpretation

The Interpretation section establishes rules for construing the act as a whole. Unlike the Definition of Terms, which assigns meanings to individual words, the Interpretation section provides instructions that govern how the entire bill — including its definitions — should be read.

### Purpose

An Interpretation clause is not always necessary. Many simple bills can be understood without one. But where a bill addresses a subject that involves competing interests, evolving standards, or areas of potential ambiguity, an Interpretation clause serves as the drafter's instruction to future readers about how to resolve doubt.

### Example from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 4 includes a concise Interpretation clause:

> **Section 8. Rules of Interpretation.** — This Act shall be liberally construed in favor of the enjoyment and exercise of human rights.

This provision instructs courts and implementing agencies that, when confronted with an ambiguous provision in the Human Rights Commission Act, they should adopt the interpretation that favors human rights protection. This is a substantive directive with real consequences for adjudication and implementation.

### Common Formulations

The most common types of Interpretation clauses in legislative drafting include:

A **liberal construction** clause, directing that the act be construed broadly to achieve its purpose. This formulation is appropriate for remedial or protective legislation.

A **strict construction** clause, directing that penal or restrictive provisions be construed narrowly. This is appropriate for laws that impose criminal penalties or restrict individual liberty.

A **headings disclaimer**, providing that section headings and article titles are for convenience of reference only and do not affect the interpretation of the act. This prevents a party from arguing that a provision should be read narrowly because its heading does not fully describe its content.

A **gender and number** clause, providing that the masculine includes the feminine and the singular includes the plural, or vice versa, unless the context indicates otherwise.

The drafter should include an Interpretation clause when the bill's subject matter makes one advisable. Where no special interpretive rule is needed, the section may be omitted without consequence.

## 9.6 Scope, Coverage, or Applicability

The Scope section identifies the persons, entities, transactions, or territories to which the bill applies. It draws the boundary line between those who are subject to the law and those who are not. Precision in this section is essential, because the scope provision determines the entire reach of the law's operative provisions.

### When to Include

A Scope section is not always necessary. If the bill's applicability is obvious from its substantive provisions — for example, a bill that creates a single government office and defines its powers does not need a separate section stating that it applies to that office — the drafter may omit it. A Scope section becomes necessary when the bill applies to a defined class of persons, entities, or activities that is not self-evident from the operative text, or when the bill needs to make explicit exclusions.

### Types of Scope

The Scope provision may address one or more of the following:

**Subject matter scope** — the types of activities, transactions, or conduct governed by the act.

**Personal scope** — the persons or entities to whom the act applies (e.g., all government employees, all educational institutions, all persons within the BARMM).

**Temporal scope** — the period during which the act is in effect, if it has a limited duration.

**Territorial scope** — the geographic area within which the act applies, typically the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, though some acts may extend to Bangsamoro offices located outside the region's territory.

### Example from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 20 provides a detailed Scope provision:

> **Section 4. Agencies Covered.** — All ministries, bureaus, offices, commissions, administrations, boards, committees, institutes, corporations whether performing governmental or proprietary function, or any other units of the BARMM, as well as provincial local government units, city local government units, municipal local government units, barangay local government units, and public and private schools, including basic, higher and technical education.

This provision enumerates every category of entity to which the dual calendar requirement applies. Its breadth is deliberate: the drafters intended the requirement to be universal across the BARMM's institutional landscape, and the Scope provision leaves no room for an agency to claim exemption on the ground that it was not specifically named.

## 9.7 Substantive Provisions

Substantive provisions are the heart of any bill. They are the operative rules that accomplish the bill's principal purpose by imposing duties, conferring rights, creating prohibitions, establishing powers, or regulating conduct. Everything else in the bill — the Short Title, the definitions, the administrative machinery, the penalties, the final provisions — exists to support, implement, or preserve the substantive provisions.

### Logical Ordering

The arrangement of substantive provisions within a bill is not arbitrary. The drafter should follow principles of logical ordering that guide the reader from the general to the specific, from the rule to the exception, and from the creation of authority to its exercise. The principal ordering rules are:

**General before specific.** A provision that establishes a general rule should precede provisions that address particular applications of that rule. In BAA No. 4, the general mandate of the Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission (Section 10: "the Commission shall promote and protect human rights and, during armed conflict, uphold international humanitarian law") precedes the specific enumeration of the Commission's powers and functions (Section 27), which details how the mandate is to be carried out.

**Main rule before exception.** Where a provision establishes a rule subject to exceptions, the rule should be stated first and the exceptions should follow. This prevents the reader from encountering qualifications before understanding what is being qualified.

**Creating bodies before defining their functions.** When a bill establishes an institution, the provision creating the institution should precede the provisions that define its composition, qualifications, powers, and organizational structure. BAA No. 4 follows this pattern: Section 9 creates the Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission, Sections 13 through 16 address its composition and appointment, and Section 27 enumerates its powers and functions.

**Chronological stages.** Where a bill regulates a process that unfolds in stages — such as an application, review, approval, and appeal process — the provisions should follow the chronological order of the process.

**Importance order.** Where no other ordering principle applies, the most important provisions should precede the less important ones.

**Mandatory before permissive.** Provisions that impose mandatory requirements ("shall") should precede provisions that grant discretionary authority ("may").

### Example from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 20 demonstrates the logical ordering of substantive provisions in a simple bill. After the general provisions (Sections 1 through 4), the substantive heart of the act is contained in Section 5:

> **Section 5. Dual Use of Calendar.** — Dual use of calendar shall mean all official documents and communications such as memorandum, office order and other forms of formal correspondence shall use and allow at the same time the *Hijri* calendar and the Gregorian calendar with respect to the year, month and date of said communication or formal correspondence.
>
> In writing the date of official documents, communications, and other forms of formal correspondence, the date on the *Hijri* calendar shall appear first before the date on the Gregorian calendar.

This provision accomplishes the bill's central purpose: mandating dual calendar use and specifying the order in which the two dates appear. The general rule (dual use is mandatory) is stated before the specific rule (Hijri date appears first).

BAA No. 4, as a more complex bill, distributes its substantive provisions across multiple articles. The rights and standards that the Commission is charged with protecting (Article II: Policy and Principles) precede the creation of the institution that will protect them (Article III: Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission), which in turn precedes the enumeration of the institution's specific powers (Article IV: Powers and Functions). This structure — values, then institution, then powers — guides the reader through an escalating level of specificity.

## 9.8 Administrative Provisions

Administrative provisions establish the implementing machinery for the substantive rules. They designate the agency responsible for enforcement, define its internal structure, prescribe its powers, and provide for the personnel and resources necessary to carry out the law. Administrative provisions are the bridge between the law as written and the law as implemented.

### Distinction from Substantive Provisions

The distinction between substantive and administrative provisions is functional, not formal. A substantive provision says what must be done; an administrative provision says who does it and how. Consider BAA No. 20. The substantive provision (Section 5) mandates dual calendar use in all official documents. The administrative provisions (Sections 7 and 8) identify the implementing office and define its powers:

> **Section 7. Implementing Office.** — The Office of the Chief Minister of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao, through the Bangsamoro *Darul-Ifta'* shall implement this law with the highest degree of integrity, efficiency, responsibility and accountability.

> **Section 8. Powers and Functions.** — The Bangsamoro *Darul-Ifta'* shall, in implementation of this law, have the following powers and functions:
>
> a) Promulgate *fatwa* (religious verdicts) pertaining to the whole coverage of Islamic Jurisprudence;
> b) Lead the promotion of intrareligious and interreligious dialogues for the attainment of peaceful co-existence;
> c) Serve as consultant to the Office of the Chief Minister pertaining to religious affairs;
> d) Collaborate and exchange information pertaining to *Ramadhan* moon-sighting locally and internationally specially among the nearby South East Asian Muslim Countries;
> e) Perform moon-sighting every 29th day of *Hijri* month to accurately determine the monthly *Hijri* or Lunar calendar; and
> f) Perform other functions as may be provided by law or assigned by the Chief Minister.

Section 5 creates the obligation; Sections 7 and 8 provide the institutional machinery to carry it out. Without the administrative provisions, the substantive mandate would lack an enforcement mechanism.

### Elements of Administrative Provisions

A comprehensive set of administrative provisions for a bill that creates or empowers an institution typically includes:

**Designation of the implementing agency** — identifying the ministry, office, or commission responsible for carrying out the law.

**Organizational structure** — defining the agency's internal divisions, offices, and reporting relationships. BAA No. 4 devotes Sections 19 through 26 to the organizational structure of the Human Rights Commission, specifying its Commission Proper, Office of the Chairperson, Office of the Secretariat, and Provincial Director Offices.

**Composition and qualifications** — specifying the number of officers, their qualifications, and the manner of their appointment. BAA No. 4 provides detailed qualifications for the Chairperson and Commissioners (Section 14) and disqualifications (Section 15).

**Powers and functions** — enumerating the specific actions the agency is authorized to take. These should be stated in mandatory terms ("shall") where the action is required, and in permissive terms ("may") where it is discretionary.

**Staffing and compensation** — addressing the personnel complement and salary structure. BAA No. 4 provides that the Chairperson shall receive compensation equivalent to Salary Grade 28 and the Commissioners equivalent to Salary Grade 27 (Section 18).

The drafter should ensure that every substantive provision in the bill has a corresponding administrative mechanism. A duty without an enforcer is a dead letter.

## 9.9 Enforcement Provisions (Penal)

Enforcement provisions prescribe the consequences for violating the law's mandatory requirements or prohibitions. They are the mechanism by which the law ensures compliance. Without enforcement provisions, a bill that creates duties or prohibitions relies entirely on voluntary compliance — a posture that experience shows to be insufficient for most regulatory purposes.

### Types of Penalties

Enforcement provisions may establish three types of sanctions, which may be used individually or in combination:

**Criminal penalties** consist of fines, imprisonment, or both, imposed through prosecution before a court of law. Criminal penalties are appropriate for serious violations that warrant the full weight of the state's coercive power. They must comply with the constitutional requirements of due process, proportionality, and the prohibition against excessive fines and cruel, unusual, or degrading punishment.

**Civil penalties** create a cause of action by which an injured party may seek damages, injunctive relief, or other judicial remedies. Civil penalties are appropriate when the violation causes identifiable harm to a specific person or entity and the goal is to make the injured party whole.

**Administrative penalties** consist of sanctions imposed by the implementing agency without recourse to the courts in the first instance — such as suspension or revocation of licenses, permits, or appointments; disqualification from government service; or cancellation of government benefits. Administrative penalties are appropriate for regulatory violations where speed and administrative expertise are more important than the procedural formalities of judicial proceedings.

### Principles of Enforcement Drafting

Three principles govern the drafting of enforcement provisions:

**Proportionality.** The penalty must be proportionate to the gravity of the violation. A minor regulatory infraction should not be punished by imprisonment; a serious offense should not be met with a nominal fine. The drafter must calibrate sanctions to the nature and severity of the prohibited conduct.

**Implementability.** The penalty must be capable of actual enforcement. A provision that imposes imprisonment for a violation that no prosecutor will ever pursue and no court will ever hear is an empty threat that undermines the law's credibility. The drafter should consider whether the implementing agency has the capacity to detect violations and whether the judicial or administrative system has the capacity to adjudicate them.

**Deterrence.** The penalty must be sufficient to deter the prohibited conduct. A fine that is less than the economic benefit of the violation provides no incentive for compliance. The drafter should consider the economic and social context of the regulated activity when setting penalty levels.

### Example from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 7, the Bangsamoro Hymn Act, contains a straightforward criminal penalty provision:

> **Section 6. *Penalty.*** — Any person found willfully violating any provision of this Act shall upon conviction shall be punished by a fine of not less Fifty Thousand Pesos (Php 50,000.00) but not more than One Hundred Thousand Pesos (Php 100,000.00) or by an imprisonment for not more than six (6) months or both fine and imprisonment at the discretion of the court.

This provision illustrates several features of a well-drafted penal clause. It identifies the class of violators ("any person"), it requires a mental element ("willfully"), it specifies a range of penalties rather than a fixed amount (allowing the court to calibrate the sanction to the circumstances), and it provides for alternative or cumulative penalties ("or both fine and imprisonment at the discretion of the court"). The inclusion of a mental element — "willfully" — is significant: it means that an inadvertent or accidental failure to comply does not trigger criminal liability, a safeguard that reflects the proportionality principle.

### Structural Placement

Enforcement provisions should be placed after the substantive provisions and after the administrative provisions, but before the final provisions. This placement reflects the logical sequence of a bill: the law first states what must be done (substantive), then who ensures it is done (administrative), then what happens if it is not done (enforcement), and finally the technical matters of appropriation, separability, and effectivity (final provisions).

Where a bill contains multiple types of violations with different penalty levels, the enforcement provisions should be organized in descending order of severity, or by subject matter corresponding to the substantive provisions they enforce.

## 9.10 Final Provisions

Final provisions address the operational, transitional, and technical matters necessary to bring the law into full effect and to manage its relationship with the existing legal order. They appear at the end of the bill, after all substantive, administrative, and enforcement provisions. While they are sometimes dismissed as "boilerplate," final provisions carry substantial legal consequence. A missing Separability Clause can cause the invalidation of an entire act on account of a single defective provision. A poorly drafted Repealing Clause can inadvertently undo legislation that the Parliament intended to preserve. A deficient Effectivity Clause can leave the law's commencement date uncertain.

The standard final provisions, in the order they should appear, are as follows.

### 9.10.1 Oversight

An Oversight provision designates a parliamentary committee to monitor the law's implementation. This provision bridges the gap between enactment and implementation by creating an institutional mechanism for the Parliament to hold the implementing agency accountable.

The standard formulation is:

> "The Committee on [relevant subject area] of the Bangsamoro Parliament shall exercise oversight over the implementation of this Act."

Not every bill requires an Oversight provision. Simple bills that create no new institutional machinery — such as bills adopting official symbols — may not warrant one. But bills that create new agencies, establish programs, or appropriate substantial funds should include an Oversight provision to ensure that the Parliament can monitor whether the law is being faithfully executed.

### 9.10.2 Reporting and Review

A Reporting provision requires the implementing agency to submit periodic reports to the Parliament or to a designated committee on the progress of implementation. A Review provision goes further by requiring a formal evaluation of the law's effectiveness after a specified period.

The standard formulation for a reporting provision is:

> "The [implementing agency] shall submit to the Bangsamoro Parliament, through the Committee on [relevant subject area], an annual report on the implementation of this Act within thirty (30) days after the end of each fiscal year."

A review provision might read:

> "Within five (5) years from the effectivity of this Act, the [implementing agency] shall conduct a comprehensive review of its implementation and submit its findings and recommendations to the Bangsamoro Parliament."

These provisions serve the Parliament's oversight function by ensuring a regular flow of information from the executive to the legislative branch.

### 9.10.3 Rule-Making / Implementing Rules and Regulations

A Rule-Making provision directs the implementing agency to promulgate Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR) that elaborate the law's general provisions into specific, operational standards. The IRR fills in the details that are too granular for legislation — procedures, forms, schedules, and technical requirements — while remaining within the bounds set by the statute.

The standard formulation is:

> "Within [sixty (60) / ninety (90)] days from the effectivity of this Act, the [implementing agency] shall promulgate the Implementing Rules and Regulations necessary for the effective implementation of this Act."

The drafter should specify the deadline for issuance, which is typically sixty or ninety days. Where the subject matter is complex or requires extensive consultation, a longer period may be justified. The drafter may also specify that the IRR must be developed in consultation with identified stakeholders or subject to public comment.

### 9.10.4 Appropriation

The Appropriation provision allocates funds for the law's implementation. Every bill that requires the expenditure of public funds must include such a provision, identifying the amount, the funding source, and the mechanism for subsequent appropriations.

The standard two-part formulation provides for initial funding from existing sources and subsequent funding through the annual budget process:

> "The initial amount of [amount in words and figures] shall be sourced from [identified fund]. Subsequent funding requirements shall be included in the Bangsamoro Appropriations Act."

### Examples from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 20 provides a concrete example:

> **Section 9. Budget.** — For the operation and implementation of this law, the Bangsamoro *Darul-Ifta'* shall have an initial funding of Ten Million Pesos (Php 10,000,000.00) which shall be sourced from any available funds of the Bangsamoro Government and every year thereafter shall be included in the regular budget of agency subject to usual accounting, audit rules, and regulations.

BAA No. 4 uses a more detailed formulation that separates personal services from operational expenses:

> **Section 60. Appropriations.** — The initial amount of Thirty Million Pesos (P 30,000,000.00) for Personal Services and Twenty Million pesos (P 20,000,000.00) for the initial operation of the office shall be sourced from the Miscellaneous Personnel Benefits Fund and the Contingent Fund, respectively, subject to existing rules and regulations. Subsequent funding requirements shall be included in the Bangsamoro Appropriations Act. The office shall include in its proposed budget the necessary amount to enable it to achieve its mandate.

BAA No. 5 follows a similar pattern:

> **Section 19. Appropriations.** — The initial amount of Fifteen Million Pesos (P15,000,000.00) for Personal Services and Three Million Pesos (P3,000,000.00) for the initial operation of the BAGO shall be sourced from the Miscellaneous Personnel Benefits Fund and the Contingent Fund, as appropriated, subject to existing rules and regulations. Additional funding for Personal Services and Capital Outlay may be requested from MPBF and Contingent Fund. Subsequent funding requirements shall be included in the Bangsamoro Appropriations Act.

The drafter should note the consistent pattern across these BAAs: initial funding is sourced from existing government funds (the Miscellaneous Personnel Benefits Fund, the Contingent Fund, or general available funds), while subsequent funding is channeled through the annual Bangsamoro Appropriations Act. This two-tier structure ensures that the law can be implemented immediately upon enactment without waiting for the next budget cycle, while providing for sustainable funding through the regular appropriations process.

### 9.10.5 Transitory Provisions

Transitory provisions manage the transition from the old legal regime to the new one. They address practical questions that arise when a new law displaces or modifies an existing institutional arrangement: What happens to the personnel of an abolished or reorganized agency? What happens to pending cases or proceedings? What regulations remain in force until the new IRR is issued?

BAA No. 4 includes extensive transitory provisions addressing the transition from the former Regional Human Rights Commission (RHRC) to the new Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission:

> **Section 56. Transfer of Properties.** — All assets, capital, records, accounts, contracts funds, receivables, equipment, and facilities of the RHRC shall be transferred to the Commission. No liabilities shall be absorbed by the Commission.

> **Section 57. Hold-Over Capacity.** — The Chairperson and other responsible officers of the RHRC holding appointive positions shall continue to perform their functions in a hold-over capacity, subject to the implementation of the phase out in the Transition Plan approved by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority.

> **Section 58. Separation of the RHRC Personnel.** — The separation of RHRC personnel shall be subject to the applicable incentives provided by Section 10, Article XVI of RA 11054.

> **Section 59. Retirement of the RHRC Chairperson and Commissioners.** — The Chairperson and the Commissioners of the RHRC, whose term of office shall be prematurely terminated, shall receive the same retirement benefits and privileges at a level not lower than those fixed for the Chairperson and Commissioners, respectively, of the National Labor Relations Commission.

These provisions ensure institutional continuity during the transition. Without them, the creation of the new Commission could leave a gap in which no body is exercising the human rights protection mandate, assets are unaccounted for, and personnel are left without legal clarity about their status. Transitory provisions are essential whenever a bill reorganizes, abolishes, or replaces an existing institution.

### 9.10.6 Sunset Clause

A Sunset Clause provides that the act, or specified provisions thereof, will expire on a stated date or upon the occurrence of a stated event, unless the Parliament acts to renew or extend it. Sunset Clauses are appropriate for legislation that addresses a temporary condition, establishes a pilot program, or creates emergency powers that should not persist indefinitely.

The standard formulation is:

> "This Act shall remain in force for a period of [number] years from its effectivity, unless sooner renewed or extended by the Bangsamoro Parliament."

Sunset Clauses are not common in Bangsamoro legislation to date, but they are an available tool that the drafter should consider when the subject matter warrants a built-in expiration mechanism.

### 9.10.7 Saving Clause

A Saving Clause preserves rights, obligations, or proceedings that accrued or commenced under the prior law. It prevents the new law from retroactively destroying vested rights or disrupting ongoing proceedings.

The standard formulation is:

> "Rights and obligations that have accrued, and proceedings that have commenced, under the law or regulation repealed or amended by this Act shall not be affected by such repeal or amendment and shall continue to be governed by the applicable provisions of the prior law or regulation."

A Saving Clause is necessary whenever a bill repeals or substantially amends an existing law under which persons may have acquired rights or initiated proceedings. Without it, the repeal of the prior law could extinguish those rights and proceedings, a result that is often neither intended nor just.

### 9.10.8 Separability Clause

The Separability Clause ensures that if any provision of the act is declared invalid or unconstitutional by a competent court, the remaining provisions continue in force. Without this clause, the invalidation of a single provision could, under certain doctrines of statutory construction, result in the invalidation of the entire act.

The standard formulation used in Bangsamoro legislation is:

> "If any provision or part hereof is held invalid or unconstitutional, the remainder or the provisions not otherwise affected shall remain valid and subsisting."

This formulation, drawn from BAA No. 20, is the most common version in the Parliament's legislative practice. However, enacted BAAs show minor variations. BAA No. 4 uses a slightly different phrasing:

> **Section 61. Separability Clause.** — If any provision of this Act is held unconstitutional, other provisions not affected thereby shall remain valid and binding.

BAA No. 5 offers yet another variation:

> **Section 21. Separability Clause.** — The provisions of this Act are hereby declared to be separate and in the event one or more of such provisions are held unconstitutional, the validity of other provisions shall not be affected thereby.

BAA No. 7 takes a more expansive approach:

> **Section 8. *Separability Clause.*** — Any provision of this Act that will be found by a competent court to be contrary to the Constitution or the Organic Law, such provision shall be severed from this Act, and such action shall not affect the enforce ability of the remaining provisions hereof.

All four formulations accomplish the same legal purpose, but the drafter should adopt a single, consistent formulation for all new bills. The formulation from BAA No. 20 — or a close variant — is recommended as the standard:

> "If any provision of this Act is held invalid or unconstitutional, the remainder of the provisions not otherwise affected shall remain valid and subsisting."

The Separability Clause should be included in every bill without exception. There is no circumstance in which the Parliament would intend the invalidation of a single provision to bring down an entire act, and the inclusion of this clause is costless.

### 9.10.9 Repealing Clause

The Repealing Clause identifies which existing laws, or provisions thereof, are repealed, modified, or rendered inoperative by the new act. Repeal is the mechanism by which the legal system maintains internal coherence: when a new law covers the same subject as an old one, the Repealing Clause clarifies whether the old law continues in force, is partially modified, or is entirely displaced.

There are two types of repeal:

**Express repeal** occurs when the Repealing Clause specifically identifies the laws or provisions that are repealed. This is the preferred method because it leaves no room for doubt about which laws survive and which do not. An express repeal names the specific act, section, or provision being repealed:

> "Section [X] of Bangsamoro Autonomy Act No. [Y] is hereby repealed."

**Implied repeal** (also called a general repealing clause) occurs when the Repealing Clause states broadly that all laws inconsistent with the new act are repealed, without naming them specifically. This method is less precise and can create uncertainty about which laws have been affected. However, it is common in Bangsamoro legislative practice.

### Examples from Enacted BAAs

BAA No. 20 uses a general repealing clause:

> **Section 10. Repealing Clause.** — Any law or issuance, executive order, letter of instruction, administrative order, rule or regulation contrary to, or inconsistent with, the provisions of this Act are hereby repealed, modified or amended accordingly.

BAA No. 4 uses a similar formulation:

> **Section 62. Repealing Clause.** — All other regional acts, decrees, instructions, rules, and regulations or parts thereof inconsistent with the provisions of this Act are hereby repealed or modified accordingly.

BAA No. 5 follows the same pattern:

> **Section 22. Repealing Clause.** — All laws, decrees, orders, rules and regulations, and other issuances or parts thereof, which are inconsistent with this Act, are hereby repealed or amended accordingly.

BAA No. 7 is substantively identical:

> **Section 7. *Repealing Clause.*** — All previous laws, Executive Orders, other decrees, and rules and regulations or parts thereof inconsistent herewith are hereby repealed or modified accordingly.

All four BAAs employ implied repeal. While this approach is established in the Parliament's practice, the drafter should prefer express repeal where the specific laws being displaced can be identified. Express repeal provides certainty and prevents litigation over whether a particular prior law was impliedly repealed. Where both express and implied repeal are warranted — because the bill repeals specific provisions and may also affect other laws that the drafter cannot comprehensively identify — the drafter should combine both:

> "Section [X] of Bangsamoro Autonomy Act No. [Y] is hereby expressly repealed. All other laws, decrees, orders, rules and regulations, or parts thereof, inconsistent with this Act are hereby repealed or modified accordingly."

### 9.10.10 Effectivity

The Effectivity Clause states when the act takes effect. This provision determines the precise moment at which the law becomes enforceable and rights and obligations under it begin to accrue. Absent an Effectivity Clause, the date on which a law takes effect may be uncertain, creating a period during which neither the implementing agency nor the public knows whether the law is in force.

The standard formulation in Bangsamoro legislation provides for a fifteen-day period following publication:

> "This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in a newspaper of general or regional circulation."

This is the formulation used by BAA No. 20:

> **Section 12. Effectivity Clause.** — This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in a newspaper of general or regional circulation.

BAA No. 4 uses a slightly different formulation that specifies the type of newspaper:

> **Section 63. Effectivity Clause.** — This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in a newspaper of regional circulation in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region.

BAA No. 5 provides an additional detail — publication must be "complete":

> **Section 23. Effectivity Clause.** — This Act shall take effect after fifteen (15) days following its complete publication in at least one (1) newspaper of general circulation in the Bangsamoro region.

The fifteen-day publication requirement serves due process: it ensures that persons affected by the law have notice of its existence and content before it takes effect. The drafter should adopt this formulation as the standard for all new bills.

In exceptional cases, a law may take effect immediately upon publication, or upon a specified date. BAA No. 14, which extended the availability of 2020 appropriations, took effect "immediately after its publication" — a formulation appropriate for urgent fiscal measures where delay would defeat the law's purpose. Immediate effectivity should be reserved for extraordinary circumstances and should be supported by a certification of necessity from the Chief Minister.

The recommended standard formulation for new Bangsamoro legislation is:

> "This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days after its publication in the Bangsamoro Gazette and in at least one (1) newspaper of general circulation in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region."

The reference to the Bangsamoro Gazette — the official publication of the Bangsamoro Government — alongside a newspaper of general circulation provides dual assurance of publication and public notice.

# Chapter 10

# Resolutions

## 10.1 Distinction from Bills

The Bangsamoro Parliament produces two categories of legislative measures: bills and resolutions. Chapter 2 of this manual introduced the distinction between them. This chapter addresses the drafting of resolutions in detail.

The fundamental difference between a bill and a resolution is one of legal effect. A bill, upon passage through the three-readings process and approval by the Chief Minister, becomes a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act with the force and effect of law. It creates, amends, or repeals legal obligations that are binding on all persons within the scope of the law. A resolution, by contrast, is an expression of the Parliament's collective will that does not carry the force of law. It does not create binding legal obligations, does not require the three-readings process applicable to bills, and does not require presentment to the Chief Minister for signature.

This distinction governs every aspect of drafting. A resolution does not need the full apparatus of a bill — no definition of terms, no enforcement provisions, no separability clause, no effectivity clause. Its structure is simpler, its language is often less technical, and its purpose is to communicate a position, make a request, or authorize an internal parliamentary action rather than to alter the legal landscape.

The distinction also means that a resolution cannot accomplish what only a bill can do. A resolution cannot create a criminal offense, impose a tax, establish a government agency, or appropriate public funds. If the intended action requires binding legal force, the appropriate instrument is a bill, not a resolution. The drafter must make this determination at the outset and select the correct instrument before beginning to write.

## 10.2 Types of Resolutions

Resolutions serve a wide range of parliamentary purposes. The most common types, classified by their operative function, include the following:

**Urging resolutions** call upon the executive branch, a government agency, or another body to take a specified action. An urging resolution does not compel the action; it communicates the Parliament's desire that the action be taken. Example: a resolution urging the Ministry of Health to prioritize the construction of rural health units in underserved municipalities.

**Requesting resolutions** ask a government agency, official, or other body to provide information, submit a report, or take a particular step. Like urging resolutions, they carry moral and political weight but not legal compulsion.

**Commending resolutions** recognize and commend individuals, organizations, or communities for distinguished achievements, public service, or contributions to the Bangsamoro people. These resolutions serve the Parliament's ceremonial function and build institutional relationships.

**Endorsing resolutions** express the Parliament's support for a policy, program, project, or initiative — whether proposed by the executive branch, a national government agency, an international organization, or a civil society group.

**Directing resolutions** are used for internal parliamentary business — directing a committee to conduct an inquiry, directing the Secretary-General to transmit a document, or directing the preparation of a report. These resolutions exercise the Parliament's authority over its own operations.

**Expressing resolutions** communicate the Parliament's position on a matter of public concern — expressing sympathy, solidarity, concern, or opposition. These resolutions have no operative legal effect but carry the weight of the Parliament's institutional voice.

**Declaring resolutions** make formal parliamentary declarations — declaring a week of observance, declaring support for a national or international initiative, or declaring the Parliament's position on a question of governance.

The boundaries between these categories are not rigid. A single resolution may combine elements of urging and endorsing, or of directing and requesting. The drafter should identify the resolution's primary operative purpose and structure the document accordingly.

## 10.3 The WHEREAS Preamble

The body of a resolution consists of two parts: the preamble and the operative clause. The preamble is composed of a series of recitals, each beginning with the word "WHEREAS," that set out the factual and policy context for the resolution's operative action. The operative clause begins with the formula "NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED..." and states the action that the Parliament is taking.

### Purpose of the Preamble

The preamble performs the function that the Declaration of Policy and the Explanatory Note perform for a bill: it explains why the resolution is being adopted. Because a resolution lacks the elaborate structural apparatus of a bill — no Long Title, no separate Explanatory Note — the preamble carries the entire burden of providing context and justification.

### Deductive Structure

The WHEREAS clauses should follow a deductive structure, moving from the broadest context to the most specific ground for the resolution's action. The recommended progression is:

**Global or universal context.** The first WHEREAS clause may invoke an international framework, principle, or convention that is relevant to the resolution's subject. This establishes that the matter under consideration is not parochial but connected to broader norms.

**Islamic context.** Where the resolution touches upon matters of Islamic governance, faith, or culture — as many Bangsamoro resolutions do — a WHEREAS clause grounding the resolution in Islamic principles, Quranic values, or the teachings of the Prophet (peace be upon him) is appropriate. This reflects the identity of the Bangsamoro as a Muslim-majority region and the centrality of Islamic values to its governance framework.

**National context.** A WHEREAS clause may invoke the Philippine Constitution, a national statute, or a policy of the national government that is relevant to the resolution's subject. This establishes the resolution's place within the broader Philippine legal order.

**BARMM context.** A WHEREAS clause invoking the Bangsamoro Organic Law, a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act, or a policy of the Bangsamoro Government connects the resolution to the regional governance framework.

**Specific facts.** The final WHEREAS clauses present the specific facts, events, or circumstances that give rise to the resolution — the particular achievement being commended, the specific problem being addressed, the particular action being urged.

This deductive progression — from the universal to the particular — gives the resolution intellectual coherence and persuasive force. A resolution that begins with the specific facts and then invokes general principles reads as a justification constructed after the fact. A resolution that begins with principles and descends to facts reads as a reasoned application of established norms to a particular situation.

### Form and Grammar

Each WHEREAS clause is a dependent clause that does not end with a period. The clauses are separated by semicolons, and the final WHEREAS clause is followed by a colon, which introduces the operative clause. The word "WHEREAS" is written in ALL CAPS and followed by a comma.

The standard form is:

> WHEREAS, [recital of fact or principle];
>
> WHEREAS, [recital of fact or principle];
>
> WHEREAS, [recital of fact or principle]:

Note that the final recital ends with a colon, not a semicolon, signaling the transition to the operative clause.

## 10.4 The Operative Clause

The operative clause states the action that the Parliament is taking. It begins with the standard formula:

> "NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED, as it is hereby resolved,"

This formula is followed by the specific action — urging, requesting, commending, endorsing, directing, expressing, or declaring, as the case may be.

Where the resolution takes more than one operative action, subsequent actions are introduced by the formula:

> "RESOLVED FURTHER,"

The operative clause should be stated with precision. Even though a resolution does not carry the force of law, the Parliament's institutional credibility depends on the clarity and specificity of its expressions of will. A resolution that urges "the government to do something about education" is less effective — and less dignified — than one that urges "the Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education to allocate additional resources for the construction of school buildings in the provinces of Basilan and Tawi-Tawi during Fiscal Year 2026."

## 10.5 Resolution No. 268 as Governing Framework

The Parliament's own operations are governed by Resolution No. 268, which constitutes the Rules of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament. This resolution is itself a product of the Parliament's rule-making authority and illustrates the most consequential use of the resolution instrument: the establishment of the procedural framework within which all other parliamentary business — including the consideration of bills — takes place.

Resolution No. 268 prescribes the order of business, the committee system, the conduct of plenary sessions, the amendment process, and the voting procedures that determine how both bills and resolutions move through the legislative process. Every drafter should be familiar with its provisions, as discussed in Section 2.6 of this manual.

The adoption of Resolution No. 268 demonstrates that while resolutions do not have the force of law in the sense that they bind persons outside the Parliament, they can have binding force within the Parliament itself. The rules adopted by resolution govern the conduct of Members of Parliament, the operations of committees, and the procedures of the plenary. In this respect, a resolution governing parliamentary procedure occupies a position within the Parliament analogous to the position that a Bangsamoro Autonomy Act occupies within the region: it is the governing instrument within its sphere of authority.

## 10.6 Drafting Guidance for Resolutions

The drafter of a resolution should observe the following principles:

**Brevity.** A resolution should accomplish its purpose in as few words as the subject allows. Because a resolution does not carry the force of law, it does not require the elaborate definitional, administrative, and enforcement apparatus of a bill. Excessive length in a resolution suggests that the drafter has chosen the wrong instrument — a bill may be more appropriate.

**Precision in the operative clause.** The operative clause is the heart of the resolution. It should state exactly what the Parliament is doing — who is being urged to do what, who is being commended for what, what position is being declared — with enough specificity that the resolution's recipient or audience understands precisely what is expected or expressed.

**Appropriate tone.** The tone of a resolution reflects the Parliament's institutional voice. Commending resolutions should be generous but measured. Urging resolutions should be firm but respectful of the separation of functions between the legislative and executive branches. Expressing resolutions on matters of public concern should convey the gravity of the Parliament's position without descending into rhetoric that would be more appropriate in a press statement than in an official parliamentary instrument.

**Correct instrument selection.** The drafter must ensure that the resolution does not attempt to accomplish what only a bill can do. If the intended action requires the creation of legal obligations, the appropriation of funds, the establishment of an institution, or the imposition of penalties, the appropriate instrument is a bill. A resolution that purports to create binding obligations outside the Parliament is ultra vires and will not be given legal effect.
