# Glossary of Legislative Terms

This glossary defines terms used in the Bangsamoro Parliament and throughout this manual. Definitions are drawn from the Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054), Resolution No. 268 (the Rules, Procedures, and Practices of the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament), enacted Bangsamoro Autonomy Acts, and general Philippine legislative practice. Terms are listed in alphabetical order.

---

**Adjournment.** The termination of a session for the day, with the hour and day of the next session being set. An adjournment *sine die* is the final termination of a regular session.[^1]

**Amendatory Bill.** A bill that proposes to amend, revise, or repeal provisions of an existing Bangsamoro Autonomy Act. The long title of an amendatory bill must identify the law being amended.[^2]

**Amendment.** Any alteration or change to a bill or resolution by addition, deletion, substitution, or any combination thereof. Amendments may be committee amendments or floor amendments.[^3]

**Appropriation.** The legislative authorization to spend public funds for a specific purpose. An appropriation provision in a bill identifies the funding source and the amount allocated for implementation.

**Authorship Speech.** The speech delivered by the sponsor of a bill during Second Reading, explaining the purpose, background, and provisions of the bill. Also referred to as the explanatory statement.[^4]

**Bangsamoro Autonomy Act (BAA).** The legislative output of the Bangsamoro Parliament. A BAA is a bill that has passed through all stages of legislation and has been signed into law by the Speaker and the Chief Minister.

**Bangsamoro Development Plan (BDP).** The medium-term plan that sets the development priorities and targets of the Bangsamoro Government. The BDP 2023-2028 is the current plan. Bills should align with the BDP where relevant.

**Bangsamoro Expenditure Program (BEP).** The annual budget of the Bangsamoro Government, equivalent to the national General Appropriations Act. The BEP allocates funds to ministries, offices, and programs for a given fiscal year.

**Bangsamoro Organic Law (BOL).** Republic Act No. 11054, also known as the Organic Law for the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (OLBARMM). The BOL is the charter of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. It defines the region's powers, structure, and fundamental rights. All legislation enacted by the Parliament must conform to the BOL.

**Bill.** A proposed measure, or amendment or repeal of an existing law, presented to the Parliament for its consideration.[^5]

**Bill Number.** The number assigned to a bill in the order it is received by the Office of the Secretary-General.

**Blue Ribbon Committee.** The Committee on Good Government, Accountability of Public Officers and Investigation. It has jurisdiction over matters including investigation of malfeasance, misfeasance, and nonfeasance in office.[^6]

**Caption.** The heading at the top of a bill identifying the Parliament and the session. The caption includes the official designation "Republic of the Philippines, Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao, Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament."

**Certified Bill.** A bill certified by the Chief Minister as requiring immediate enactment to respond to a public calamity or emergency. A certified bill is exempt from the three-day distribution requirement before Third Reading voting.[^7]

**Chief Minister.** The head of the Bangsamoro Government's executive branch, who exercises executive authority during the transition period. The Chief Minister signs bills into law, may certify bills as urgent, and presents the Government of the Day's policies and programs to the Parliament.[^8]

**Committee Amendment.** An alteration or change to a bill offered by the committee that scrutinized the bill during the committee stage. Committee amendments are presented to the plenary before floor amendments.[^9]

**Committee of the Whole.** The act of the Parliament constituting itself into a committee to consider a bill or resolution, rather than referring it to a standing committee.[^10]

**Committee Report.** A document prepared by a committee that contains the discussions, views, amendments, and justifications concerning a bill or resolution under its consideration. Members who participated in the final committee vote must sign the report.[^11]

**Committee Stage.** The stage of legislation during which the concerned committee examines, scrutinizes, and deliberates in detail and line by line the provisions of a bill. The committee may propose amendments or revisions and produces a committee report.[^12]

**Concurrent Resolution.** A resolution issued concurrently by both the Bangsamoro Cabinet and the Parliament expressing their sentiments on certain matters.[^13]

**Consideration and Deliberation Stage.** The stage of legislation during which the Members of the Parliament deliberate and debate the committee report, including proposed amendments, in plenary session. This stage includes the period of interpellation and the period of amendments.[^14]

**Compliance/Reporting Clause.** A provision requiring an implementing agency to submit periodic reports — typically annual — to the Parliament, through the relevant committee, on the status, progress, or outcomes of a law's implementation. Compliance/reporting clauses are increasingly common in newer BAAs (e.g., BAA 73, 78) as an oversight mechanism.[^15]

**Conflict/Inconsistency Clause.** A provision in a bill that addresses the relationship between the new law and existing laws on the same subject. The most common formulation is included in the repealing clause: "All laws... inconsistent with the provisions of this Act are hereby repealed, amended, or modified accordingly." Some bills include a standalone conflict clause that specifies which law prevails in case of inconsistency.

**Cross-Reference.** A reference within a bill to another section of the same bill or to a provision of another law. Cross-references must be accurate and should be verified whenever the bill is revised.

**Declaration of Policy.** A section near the beginning of a bill that states the legislative intent and policy objectives the bill seeks to achieve.

**Definition of Terms.** A section of a bill that assigns specific meanings to words and phrases used in a technical or specialized sense. Definitions control the meaning of terms throughout the bill.

**Dual Calendar.** The system mandated by BAA No. 20 (Bangsamoro Dual Calendar Act of 2021)[^60] requiring all official documents and communications of the Bangsamoro Government to use both the Hijri (Islamic lunar) calendar and the Gregorian calendar simultaneously. The Hijri date appears first, followed by a slash, then the Gregorian date — for example, "Safar 26, 1447/August 20, 2025." This requirement applies to BAA passage lines, official correspondence, memoranda, and other formal documents.

**Deputy Speaker.** Under Resolution No. 268 (Rule III, Section 3), eight Deputy Speakers are elected by a majority vote of all Members. In practice, the BTA has elected additional Deputy Speakers beyond the prescribed eight. Deputy Speakers assist the Speaker and take turns presiding over sessions when the Speaker does not preside. They serve as ex officio members of all committees with a right to vote.[^16]

**Effectivity Clause.** The final section of a bill that announces when the law shall take effect. Under the BOL, laws take effect after fifteen (15) days following complete publication in at least one newspaper of general circulation within the BARMM.[^17]

**Enacting Clause.** The clause that states the authority under which a bill will be enacted. The standard formulation is: "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament:" — during the BTA period, the form is: "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament in session assembled:"[^18]

**Enumerated Powers.** The fifty-five subject areas listed in Article V, Section 2 of the BOL over which the Bangsamoro Government has legislative authority. These include education, health, agriculture, environmental protection, local governance, and many others.

**Executive Session.** A session that is generally closed to the public, involving matters of regional security, public safety, public health, or subject matters that may be offensive to decency and public morals.[^19]

**Explanatory Note.** A document attached to a bill that sets out the purposes, background, and reasons for introducing the bill. Although described as optional in the Rules, it is included with every bill filed in practice.[^20]

**First Reading.** The first stage of legislation, during which the Secretary-General reports the bill to the Parliament by reading its number, title, and author, followed by the Speaker's declaration of whether it is a Government bill or a Private Member bill.[^21]

**Floor Amendment.** An alteration or amendment to a bill offered by an individual Member of Parliament during the period of amendments in plenary session. Also called an individual amendment.[^22]

**General Welfare Clause.** The provision in Article V, Section 3 of the BOL that gives the Bangsamoro Government the power to enact legislation for the general welfare of the Bangsamoro people, in addition to the enumerated powers. This clause broadens the Parliament's legislative authority beyond the fifty-five enumerated subjects.

**Germaneness.** The requirement that an amendment must relate to the subject matter of the bill being amended. An amendment that introduces a provision not germane to the purpose of the bill — a "rider" — is not permitted.[^23]

**Hijri Calendar.** The Islamic lunar calendar, based on twelve lunar months of 354 or 355 days, beginning from the migration (*Hijrah*) of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) from Makkah to Madinah. The twelve months are: Muharram, Safar, Rabi Al-Awwal, Rabi Al-Thani, Jumada Al-Awwal, Jumada Al-Akhirah, Rajab, Sha'ban, Ramadhan, Shawwal, Dhul Al-Qa'dah, and Dhul Al-Hijjah. Under BAA No. 20, the Hijri calendar is used alongside the Gregorian calendar in all official Bangsamoro documents. See also **Dual Calendar**.

**Government Bill.** A legislative initiative submitted to the Parliament by the Government of the Day for approval or amendment. Government bills have precedence over Private Member bills.[^24]

**Government of the Day.** The executive branch of the Bangsamoro Government, led by the Chief Minister and the Cabinet. Government bills reflect the legislative agenda of the Government of the Day.

**Implementing Rules and Regulations (IRR).** The detailed rules issued by the implementing agency to carry out the provisions of a law. Many bills include a provision requiring the promulgation of an IRR within a specified period from the law's effectivity.

**Interpellation.** The period during plenary deliberation when Members of Parliament may ask the sponsor of a bill questions about its provisions, purpose, and effects. The Majority Floor Leader maintains a list of interpellators.[^25]

**Joint Committee Resolution.** A resolution of two or more committees expressing the will or action of the Members on certain matters, requiring the majority vote of all the committees concerned.[^26]

**Journal.** A concise and accurate account of the proceedings of what has taken place in every plenary session. The Journal is certified by the Secretary-General and is open to the public.[^27]

**Mandatory Review Clause.** A provision that requires the Parliament or a designated body to review the law after a specified period — typically three (3) to five (5) years — to evaluate its effectiveness and determine whether amendments, extensions, or repeal are warranted. Mandatory review clauses are used for pilot programs, newly created institutions, or laws with evolving subject matter. Distinguished from a sunset clause in that a mandatory review does not automatically terminate the law.

**Long Title.** The formal title of a bill that describes its purpose and content. The long title must accurately reflect the bill's contents and comply with the one-subject, one-title rule.[^28]

**Mace.** A symbol of the Parliament's authority, displayed at the foot of the Speaker's rostrum whenever the Parliament is in session. It serves as the warrant for the Sergeant-at-Arms to enforce order.[^29]

**Majority Floor Leader.** The Member who directs deliberations on the floor, chairs the Committee on Rules, prepares the Order of Business, and determines the order of recognition for Members to speak. The Majority Floor Leader also acts as the Whip for the Majority.[^30]

**Member of Parliament (MP).** The official title of Members of the Bangsamoro Parliament.[^31]

**Minority Floor Leader.** The Member who speaks on behalf of the Minority, serves as vice chair of the Committee on Rules, and is an ex officio member of all committees with the right to vote.[^32]

**Motion.** A proposal made by a Member of Parliament upon which the Parliament or any of its committees shall take proper action. Motions may be main motions, subsidiary motions, incidental motions, or privileged motions.[^33]

**One-Subject Rule.** The requirement that a bill shall only have one subject matter. An amendment that introduces a provision not related to the bill's subject is a prohibited rider.[^34]

**P.B. No. (Parliamentary Bill Number).** The bill numbering prefix adopted in the later sessions of the 2nd BTA Parliament (BAA 54 onward), replacing the earlier "BILL NO." format. The P.B. No. is assigned by the Office of the Secretary-General upon filing. Examples: "P.B. No. 190," "P.B. No. 341." When a bill results from consolidation of multiple filed bills, the format is: "P.B. Nos. [N] and [N]."[^35]

**Order of Business.** The daily agenda of the Parliament, prepared by the Majority Floor Leader and distributed to Members. The standard Order of Business includes, among other items, bills on First Reading, bills on Second Reading, the period of interpellation, the period of amendments, and bills on Third Reading.[^36]

**Parliamentary Committee.** A permanent committee that exercises continuing responsibility in the legislative activity of the Parliament. Parliamentary committees are organized under the Rules and include the Committee on Rules, the Committee on Accounts, the Committee on Ways and Means, and others.[^37]

**Period of Amendments.** The stage within the Consideration and Deliberation Stage during which committee amendments and floor amendments are proposed, debated, and voted upon.[^38]

**Point of Order.** A motion that seeks to call the attention of the Speaker whenever a Member believes that proper decorum or procedure is not being followed. No Member may speak for longer than five minutes on a point of order.[^39]

**Policy Research and Legal Services (PRLS).** The Parliament's office that provides research and technical support to Members and committees in the preparation and analysis of legislation.

**Popular Initiative.** The process whereby a bill is proposed directly by the people as part of participatory democracy in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region. The Parliament shall regulate popular initiative through guidelines.[^40]

**Private Member Bill.** A legislative initiative introduced by a Member of Parliament who is not a Minister. Private Member bills are subordinate to Government bills in order of precedence.[^41]

**Privilege Hour.** A sixty-minute period held every Thursday of the regular sessions during which any Member may speak on any matter of general interest. Privilege speeches are not to be interrupted except by a point of order or a motion to adjourn.[^42]

**Proposed Resolution.** A document expressing the sentiment or opinion of the Parliament. It does not create or modify a law. Proposed resolutions are the standard legislative vehicle for expressing the Parliament's position on non-legislative matters.[^43]

**Quorum.** The minimum number of Members required to conduct business. In plenary sessions, a quorum is fifty percent (50%) plus one (1) of all Members. In committee meetings, a quorum is thirty percent (30%) of the committee members.[^44]

**Repealing Clause.** A provision in a bill that identifies specific laws, sections, or provisions that are repealed or modified by the new legislation. A specific repealing clause identifies the exact provisions being repealed. A general repealing clause provides that all inconsistent laws are repealed — but this should supplement, not replace, specific repeals.

**Residual Clause.** The principle that powers not expressly granted to the Bangsamoro Government under the BOL are reserved to the national government. This is the counterpart to the enumerated powers. The drafter must ensure that a bill does not intrude upon powers reserved to Congress.

**Resolution No. 268.** The resolution adopted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority Parliament that provides for the Rules, Procedures, and Practices of the Parliament. It is the primary procedural authority for all legislative proceedings in the Bangsamoro.

**Rider.** An amendment that introduces a provision not germane to the subject of the bill. Riders are prohibited under the one-subject rule.[^45]

**Second Reading.** The stage during which a bill is read in full (or copies are distributed), the sponsor delivers the authorship speech, and the bill is referred to the appropriate committee.[^46]

**Secretary-General.** The officer accountable to the Parliament and the Speaker who manages the administrative operations of the Parliament, records proceedings, certifies Journals, serves as custodian of records, and performs other duties as prescribed.[^47]

**Separability Clause.** A provision stating that if any portion of the law is declared unlawful or unconstitutional, the other provisions not affected by such declaration shall continue to be in full force and effect.[^48]

**Sergeant-at-Arms.** The officer responsible for the discipline and security of the Parliament, including maintaining order in the session hall, implementing instructions of the Speaker, and serving processes issued by the Parliament.[^49]

**Shari'ah.** Islamic law. Under the BOL, Shari'ah governs personal, family, and property relations involving Muslims in the Bangsamoro. The Parliament has authority to legislate on matters involving Shari'ah within the scope of the BOL (Article X, Section 4), including the jurisdiction and procedures of Shari'ah courts (Article X, Sections 7-10).[^50]

**Suppletory Clause.** A provision that identifies a national law or other legal source that applies in a supplementary capacity when the bill itself does not contain an applicable provision on a particular matter. For example, BAA 82 (Bangsamoro Labor and Employment Code) provides: "In cases where this Code contains no applicable provision concerning labor, employment, or occupation, the Labor Code of the Philippines and other relevant social legislation shall be applied suppletorily." The suppletory clause typically includes a proviso that the suppletory law must not be contrary to existing Bangsamoro laws.[^51]

**Short Title.** A brief, commonly used name assigned to a law within its preliminary provisions — for example, "This Act shall be known as the 'Bangsamoro Education Code.'" The short title is used for convenience in citation.

**Simple Resolution.** A measure used by the Parliament to take action affecting its own procedures, address urgent matters requiring immediate action, or express an opinion, sympathy, or commendation. Simple resolutions are not subject to the ordinary stages of legislation.[^52]

**Speaker of the Parliament.** The Presiding Officer of the Parliament, elected by a majority vote of all Members. The Speaker opens, conducts, and closes sessions; signs all acts, resolutions, and bills; and exercises administrative supervision over the legislative personnel.[^53]

**Sponsorship Speech.** See **Authorship Speech**. The terms are used interchangeably, though "authorship speech" is the term used in Resolution No. 268.

**Statutory Committee.** A permanent committee provided under the BOL and the Rules. The Parliament has seventeen statutory committees covering subjects such as finance, education, environment, health, and justice, among others.[^54]

**Substitute Bill.** A complete replacement of a bill's text offered as an amendment. A substitute bill must cover the same subject as the original bill — it may not introduce a subject distinct from the original. The substitute must be submitted in writing.[^55]

**Sunset Clause.** A provision that sets an expiration date for a law or a program, after which it ceases to have effect unless renewed by the Parliament. Sunset clauses are used for pilot programs, temporary measures, or laws that require periodic review.

**Swift Passage Procedure.** A procedure for the speedy disposition of a bill in seven (7) working days that skips the detailed committee scrutiny stage. The Swift Passage Procedure allows cross-party or free conscience voting. It is used in exceptional cases.[^56]

**Third Reading.** The final stage of legislation, during which there is a final debate on whether to pass the bill. No amendment may be considered at Third Reading. Printed copies of the bill must have been distributed to Members at least three days before voting, unless the bill is certified as urgent.[^57]

**Transitory Provisions.** Provisions in a bill that address the transition from an existing regime to the new regime established by the bill. Transitory provisions may cover existing licenses, pending applications, current officeholders, and other situations that require a bridge between the old law and the new.

**Ultra Vires.** A Latin term meaning "beyond the powers." A bill that exceeds the Parliament's legislative authority — because it conflicts with the Constitution, the BOL, or invades powers reserved to the national government — is ultra vires and may be struck down by the courts.

**Unfinished Business.** Matters being considered by the Parliament at the time of adjournment of the preceding session. Their consideration shall be resumed until disposed of.[^58]

**Wali.** The honorary head of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region who performs ceremonial functions, including opening the inaugural session of the Parliament through the banging of the agong.[^59]

**Whereas Clause.** A recital in the preamble of a resolution that states the justifications, reasons, or factual basis for the resolution. Whereas clauses are numbered and arranged in a logical, deductive sequence.
