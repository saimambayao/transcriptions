# Chapter 11 — Quality Review

Drafting a bill is only half the work. The other half is making sure it holds up.

A bill that seems clear on the day you finish drafting it may turn out to be ambiguous when a committee member reads it under the pressure of a hearing. A provision that covers every scenario you imagined may fail to cover the one scenario nobody thought of. A penalty clause that seems proportionate on paper may be unconstitutional when measured against the BOL's human rights provisions. These are not hypothetical problems. They happen. They happen in every legislature, and they happen in the Bangsamoro Parliament.

Quality review is how you catch these problems before the Parliament votes. It is the systematic process of reading your own work with fresh eyes — or, better, with hostile eyes — and asking whether the bill does what it is supposed to do, says what it is supposed to say, and survives the scrutiny that will come from committees, the floor, implementing agencies, courts, and the public.

This chapter gives you the tools for that process. It begins with a comprehensive self-review checklist. It introduces the interpellation test — a practical exercise that forces you to read your bill the way an opposition Member would. It catalogs the most common drafting mistakes, with examples and corrections. And it addresses specialized reviews that every Bangsamoro bill should undergo: gender sensitivity, human rights compliance, and BOL conformity.

## 11.1 The Drafter's Self-Review Checklist

Before you submit a draft to anyone — before the MP sees it, before it goes to co-authors, before it is filed — run through this checklist. It is organized into four categories: legal authority, structure and completeness, language and style, and internal consistency. Each item is a question. If the answer is "no" or "I'm not sure," stop and fix the problem.

This checklist can also serve as the basis for Appendix E of this manual — a detachable quality review checklist for daily use.

### 11.1.1 Legal Authority

These items verify that the bill is within the Parliament's power and does not conflict with superior law.

1. Does the bill fall within the enumerated powers of the Bangsamoro Government under Article V, Section 2 of the BOL?[^1]
2. If the bill relies on the general welfare clause (Article V, Section 3), is there a reasonable connection between the bill's subject and the welfare of the Bangsamoro people?[^2]
3. Does the bill conflict with any provision of the 1987 Constitution?
4. Does the bill conflict with any provision of the BOL itself?
5. Does the bill intrude on powers reserved to Congress under the Constitution or the BOL (the residual clause)?
6. Does the bill conflict with any national law that applies in the Bangsamoro?
7. Have you checked whether any Supreme Court decisions affect the bill's subject matter?
8. If the bill imposes taxes or fees, does it comply with the fiscal provisions of the BOL (Article XII)?[^3]
9. If the bill creates a government body, does it comply with the BOL provisions on bureaucratic structure?
10. If the bill deals with personal, family, or property relations involving Muslims, does it respect the jurisdiction of the Shari'ah courts?

### 11.1.2 Structure and Completeness

These items verify that the bill contains all necessary parts and that nothing is missing.

11. Does the bill have a long title that accurately describes its content and scope?
12. Does the long title comply with the one-subject, one-title rule?
13. Does the bill include the proper enacting clause? The standard form is "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament:" — or, during the BTA period, "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority in Parliament assembled:"[^4]
14. Does the bill include a short title?
15. Does the bill include a declaration of policy?
16. Does the bill include a definition of terms section, and are all terms used in a technical or specialized sense defined?
17. Does the definition section avoid defining words in their ordinary meaning?
18. Does the bill include a scope and coverage section that makes clear who and what the bill applies to?
19. Are the substantive provisions organized in a logical sequence — from general to specific, from rights to obligations, from prohibitions to penalties?
20. Does every duty or obligation in the bill identify who bears that duty?
21. Does every right created by the bill identify who may exercise it and who must respect it?
22. Does every prohibition have a corresponding penalty or enforcement mechanism?
23. Does the bill identify the implementing agency or agencies?
24. Does the bill include provisions for implementing rules and regulations (IRR) if the subject matter requires detailed rulemaking?
25. Does the IRR provision include a timeline for promulgation?
26. Does the bill include an appropriation or funding provision, or does it state that funding shall come from the annual Bangsamoro Expenditure Program?
27. Does the bill include a separability clause?
28. Does the bill include a repealing clause that specifically identifies provisions of existing law that are being repealed or modified?
29. Does the bill include an effectivity clause?
30. Does the bill include transitory provisions if they are needed — for example, to address existing conditions, pending applications, or the transition from the old regime to the new one?

### 11.1.3 Language and Style

These items verify that the bill is written clearly and follows the conventions of Bangsamoro legislative drafting.

31. Is every sentence in the active voice where possible?
32. Is the bill written in the present tense?
33. Does the bill use "shall" for mandatory duties, "may" for permissive authority, and "must" for conditions?
34. Are the uses of "shall," "may," and "must" consistent throughout?
35. Does any sentence exceed four lines of text? If so, can it be broken into shorter sentences or a numbered list?
36. Does the bill avoid archaic legalisms ("hereinbefore," "hereinafter," "aforementioned," "whereas" in the body)?
37. Does the bill avoid redundant doublets ("null and void," "cease and desist," "each and every")?
38. Are numbers written in both words and figures — "thirty (30) days" — as is the convention?
39. Are monetary amounts written in both words and figures with the abbreviation PhP?
40. Does the bill use a defined term consistently — the same term every time, never a synonym?
41. Are cross-references accurate? Does every reference to "Section X" actually point to the correct section?
42. Are lists formatted correctly — with consistent punctuation, consistent numbering, and proper use of "and" or "or" before the final item?
43. Does the bill avoid vague phrases like "as may be necessary," "within a reasonable time," or "to the extent practicable" without further specificity?

### 11.1.4 Internal Consistency

These items verify that the bill does not contradict itself.

44. Does the bill use the same term for the same concept throughout? (Not "Ministry" in one section and "Department" in another for the same agency.)
45. If the bill refers to another law by short title, is the short title correct?
46. If the bill quotes the BOL, is the quotation verbatim?
47. Are the section numbers sequential with no gaps or duplicates?
48. Does the table of contents (if included) match the actual section headings?
49. If the bill lists qualifications for an office or position, are the same qualifications repeated consistently in all relevant provisions — or are they stated once and cross-referenced?
50. Does the penalty provision cover all prohibited acts defined in the bill? Are any prohibited acts missing a penalty?
51. Does the bill's coverage match its declaration of policy? (If the declaration says the bill applies to "all persons," does the substantive provision also apply to "all persons" — or does it narrow the scope without explanation?)
52. Are all timelines internally consistent? (Does the bill give an agency 30 days to process an application in one section and 60 days in another for the same application?)
53. If the bill creates a board or committee, does it specify the same number of members in the composition section as in the quorum and voting provisions?

### 11.1.5 Standard Clause Compliance

These items verify that the bill's standard clauses use the prescribed template wording and match established Bangsamoro legislative conventions.

54. Does the Separability Clause use the prescribed template wording? (See Appendix F for the recommended standard text.)
55. Does the Effectivity Clause use the prescribed template wording — specifically, "fifteen (15) days after its publication in a newspaper of regional circulation in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao"?
56. Does the Repealing Clause correctly identify the specific laws, sections, or BAAs being repealed? A general repealing clause alone is insufficient if identifiable provisions are being superseded.
57. If the bill includes an IRR Clause, does it specify the timeframe for promulgation — either sixty (60) days or ninety (90) days from effectivity?
58. If the bill includes a Suppletory Clause, does it identify the specific national law that applies suppletorily and include a proviso that the suppletory law must not be contrary to existing Bangsamoro laws?
59. If the bill includes a Mandatory Review Clause, does it set a specific review date and identify the responsible body?

### 11.1.6 Bangsamoro-Specific Compliance

These items verify compliance with requirements unique to the Bangsamoro legislative context.

60. Are dual Hijri-Gregorian dates used in the passage line and other date references per BAA No. 20 (Bangsamoro Dual Calendar Act of 2021)?[^5] The Hijri date must appear first, followed by the Gregorian date, separated by a slash.
61. Does the bill use the correct enacting clause for the current BTA period? The most common formulation is "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority in Parliament assembled:" (used in approximately 50 of 89 enacted BAAs).[^6]
62. Does the bill number use the current prefix format? The Parliament shifted from "BILL NO." to "P.B. No." (Parliamentary Bill Number) in the 2nd BTA's later sessions.
63. Does the bill's header use the current institutional name — "BANGSAMORO TRANSITION AUTHORITY PARLIAMENT" — and the correct location — "Bangsamoro Government Center, Cotabato City"?

### 11.1.7 BOL Compliance — Common Error Checks

These items address the most common BOL compliance errors identified through pattern analysis of all 89 enacted BAAs.[^7]

64. If the bill creates a government body or commission, does it comply with the BOL's provisions on administrative organization (Article V, Section 2(b)) and does it specify composition, powers, functions, and funding?
65. If the bill imposes taxes or fees, does it fall within the taxing powers granted under BOL Article XII — and does it avoid the excluded categories (income taxes, customs duties, and other categories listed in Article XII, Section 9)?[^8]
66. If the bill creates a municipality, does it comply with the plebiscite requirement under BOL Article V, Section 2(l) and include a COMELEC-supervised process through the Bangsamoro Electoral Office?[^9]
67. If the bill deals with health, does it cite Section 22, Article IX of the BOL (the right to health) and integrate the Universal Health Care Act (RA 11223)?[^10]
68. Does the bill use terminology consistent with existing BAAs? (For example: "Ministry" not "Department" for BARMM agencies; "Bangsamoro Expenditure Program" not "General Appropriations"; "Bangsamoro Autonomy Act" not "Regional Act.")
69. If the bill grants authority to an implementing agency, does it specify sufficient standards and limits to prevent overbroad delegation — including caps on fees, penalties, and rulemaking scope?

### 11.1.8 Shari'ah Compliance Review

The BOL establishes Shari'ah as a source of law in the Bangsamoro, particularly over personal, family, and property relations involving Muslims (Article X).[^11] Despite this, ver02 of this manual contained zero guidance on Shari'ah compliance. This section fills that gap with practical checks every drafter should apply.

70. Does the bill's subject matter touch on personal, family, or property relations involving Muslims? If so, have the relevant Shari'ah principles been identified and respected?
71. Does the bill create provisions that could conflict with established Islamic jurisprudence (*fiqh*) on the subject — for example, inheritance distribution, marriage and divorce procedures, or *waqf* (Islamic endowment) administration?
72. If the bill regulates financial transactions, does it account for Islamic banking and finance principles (BOL Article V, Section 2(ii))? Does it avoid requiring or imposing interest-based (*riba*) mechanisms without providing Shari'ah-compliant alternatives?
73. If the bill regulates food, drugs, or commercial products, does it address *halal* certification or compliance where relevant?
74. Does the bill respect the jurisdiction of the Shari'ah courts as defined in the BOL (Article X, Sections 7-10)? Does it avoid creating administrative processes that would encroach on matters within Shari'ah court jurisdiction?
75. If the bill affects the observance of Islamic obligations — such as prayer times, Friday congregational prayers, Ramadan, Hajj, or Zakat — does it include accommodations or exemptions where appropriate?
76. Has a person with knowledge of Islamic law been consulted during the drafting process? If the bill touches Shari'ah-sensitive areas, this consultation should be documented in the explanatory note.

## 11.2 The Interpellation Test

The self-review checklist catches technical problems. The interpellation test catches substantive ones.

Here is the exercise. Put down the draft. Walk away from it for an hour, or a day if you have the time. Then sit down and read the bill as if you were a Member of Parliament who is skeptical of it. Not hostile — skeptical. You are a thoughtful MP who wants to understand what the bill does, whether it will work, and what it will cost. You are looking for weaknesses, not because you want the bill to fail, but because you want to make it better before it reaches the floor.

Ask yourself these questions about every major provision:

### Policy Questions

- What problem does this bill solve? Is the problem real, documented, and significant enough to justify legislation?
- Has the Parliament already addressed this problem through an existing BAA? If so, why is a new bill needed instead of amending the existing law?
- Who benefits from this bill? Who bears the costs?
- Will the bill actually achieve its stated objective? What could go wrong?
- Are there unintended consequences? Will the bill create new problems while solving old ones?
- Is this bill the right tool? Could the same objective be achieved through an executive order, an administrative regulation, or an appropriation?

### Legal Questions

- Where does the Parliament get the power to legislate on this subject? Can you point to the specific BOL provision?
- Does this bill conflict with any existing BAA? If so, does the repealing clause address the conflict?
- Could a constitutional challenge be raised against any provision? On what grounds?
- Does the bill delegate too much authority to the executive? Does it set sufficient standards to guide the exercise of that delegated authority?

### Implementation Questions

- Which agency will implement this bill? Does that agency have the capacity — staff, budget, expertise — to do so?
- How much will implementation cost? Where will the money come from?
- Does the bill require the creation of new offices, positions, or systems? If so, is there funding for them?
- What happens if the implementing agency does not issue the required IRR within the prescribed timeline? Does the bill have a fallback mechanism?
- How will compliance be monitored? Who checks whether the law is being followed?

### Enforcement Questions

- If someone violates this bill, who detects the violation?
- Who investigates? Who prosecutes?
- Are the penalties proportionate to the offense? Are they high enough to deter violations? Are they so high that they might be struck down as excessive?
- Can a small business owner, a farmer, or a fisherfolk reasonably be expected to understand and comply with this bill?

### Drafting Questions

- Is there a provision in this bill that a reasonable person could read two different ways? If so, which reading did the drafter intend, and can the ambiguity be resolved by revising the text?
- Is there a gap — a scenario that should be covered but is not?
- Is there a contradiction between two provisions?
- Does the bill say anything that is purely aspirational and cannot be enforced? If so, does it belong in the bill, or should it be in the explanatory note?

### The Test in Practice

The interpellation test is not theoretical. It simulates what will actually happen when the bill reaches the plenary. A Member will stand up and ask these exact questions. If your bill cannot withstand them, it is not ready for filing.

Sit down with the sponsoring MP and run through the questions. Or assign a colleague to play the role of the interpellator. The goal is to identify every soft spot in the bill before it reaches the committee report stage. Fix the soft spots. Strengthen the weak provisions. Add what is missing. Cut what is indefensible.

A bill that survives the interpellation test is a bill that will survive the plenary.

## 11.3 Common Drafting Mistakes

Below are specific mistakes that appear repeatedly in legislative drafts — not just in the Bangsamoro Parliament, but in legislatures everywhere. Each entry states the mistake, gives an example, and shows how to fix it.

**1. Unfunded mandate.**
The bill creates obligations for an agency without appropriating funds or identifying a funding source.

*Problem:* "The Ministry of Health shall establish a primary care clinic in every municipality."

*Fix:* Add an appropriation section: "The amount necessary for the implementation of this Act shall be included in the annual Bangsamoro Expenditure Program. For the first year of implementation, the amount of [X] is hereby appropriated from [source]."

**2. Missing implementing agency.**
The bill says something must be done but does not say who does it.

*Problem:* "A comprehensive inventory of all agricultural lands shall be conducted within one (1) year."

*Fix:* "The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Agrarian Reform shall conduct a comprehensive inventory of all agricultural lands within one (1) year from the effectivity of this Act."

**3. Penalty without prohibition.**
The bill penalizes an act that is not actually prohibited anywhere in the bill's substantive provisions.

*Problem:* The penal section says "Any person who violates Section 8 of this Act shall be fined..." but Section 8 only describes how the permit application process works. It does not prohibit anything.

*Fix:* Either add a clear prohibition ("No person shall operate a [facility] without a valid permit issued under Section 8 of this Act") or remove the penalty reference to Section 8.

**4. Prohibition without penalty.**
The bill prohibits an act but prescribes no consequence for violation.

*Problem:* "No person shall discharge industrial waste into waterways within the Bangsamoro." The bill says nothing more — no fine, no imprisonment, no administrative sanction.

*Fix:* Add a penalty provision. Or, if the intention is that existing penal laws apply, state this explicitly: "Violations of this section shall be subject to the penalties prescribed under [specific law]."

**5. Vague timeline.**
The bill sets a deadline using imprecise language.

*Problem:* "The implementing rules and regulations shall be promulgated within a reasonable time after the effectivity of this Act."

*Fix:* "The implementing rules and regulations shall be promulgated within ninety (90) days from the effectivity of this Act."

**6. Undefined technical term.**
The bill uses a specialized term without defining it, leaving its meaning open to dispute.

*Problem:* A bill on environmental protection refers to "sustainable practices" throughout but never defines what qualifies as sustainable.

*Fix:* Add a definition: "'Sustainable practices' refers to methods of resource use, production, or development that meet present needs without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs, as determined by the standards prescribed in the implementing rules and regulations."

**7. Inconsistent terminology.**
The bill uses different words for the same thing.

*Problem:* Section 3 refers to the "Board of Directors." Section 7 refers to the "Governing Board." Section 12 refers to the "Board." All three mean the same body.

*Fix:* Choose one term — "the Board" — define it in the definitions section, and use it consistently throughout.

**8. Conflicting provisions.**
Two sections of the same bill contradict each other.

*Problem:* Section 5 says the permit is valid for three (3) years. Section 14 says the permit must be renewed annually.

*Fix:* Determine which is correct and amend the other. If both are partly correct (e.g., the permit is valid for three years but requires annual compliance reporting), draft both provisions to reflect the actual intent clearly.

**9. Dangling cross-reference.**
A provision refers to a section number that does not exist or that refers to the wrong provision because sections were renumbered during revision.

*Problem:* "The exemptions provided under Section 14 of this Act shall not apply to..." But the bill only has 12 sections. Section 14 was deleted during revision, and the cross-reference was never updated.

*Fix:* Run a systematic check of every cross-reference in the bill before finalizing. Match each reference to its target.

**10. Overbroad delegation.**
The bill gives an agency open-ended authority without sufficient standards.

*Problem:* "The Ministry shall have the power to issue rules and regulations to carry out the purposes of this Act, including the power to impose fees and penalties as it may deem necessary."

*Fix:* Specify limits. "The Ministry shall have the power to issue rules and regulations to carry out the purposes of this Act: *Provided*, That any fees imposed under such rules shall not exceed [amount or formula], and any administrative penalties shall not exceed [amount or sanction], subject to the requirements of due process."

**11. Redundant provision.**
The bill restates something that is already in the BOL or an existing BAA, adding nothing.

*Problem:* "The Members of the Parliament shall enjoy parliamentary immunity as provided by law." This adds nothing beyond what the BOL already provides.

*Fix:* Remove the provision. If the drafter wants to call attention to the existing immunity provision for context, do so in the explanatory note, not in the body of the bill.

**12. Aspirational provision masquerading as law.**
The bill includes a provision that expresses a goal but creates no rights, duties, or enforceable obligations.

*Problem:* "The Bangsamoro Government shall endeavor to promote the holistic well-being of youth in the region."

*Fix:* Either remove the provision or convert it into an enforceable obligation: "The Ministry of Social Services and Development shall establish a youth development program in every province and city within the Bangsamoro. The program shall include, at a minimum: (a)... (b)... (c)..."

**13. Missing transition provisions.**
The bill creates a new regime but does not address what happens to existing arrangements.

*Problem:* A bill creates a new licensing system for a regulated industry but says nothing about businesses that already hold licenses under the old system.

*Fix:* Add a transitory provision: "All licenses issued under [prior law] that are valid on the date of effectivity of this Act shall remain in force until their expiration. Licensees shall apply for renewal under this Act at least sixty (60) days before the expiration of their existing licenses."

**14. Gender-exclusive language.**
The bill uses masculine pronouns as the default.

*Problem:* "The Chairperson shall submit his report within thirty (30) days."

*Fix:* "The Chairperson shall submit a report within thirty (30) days." Or, if a pronoun is needed: "The Chairperson shall submit his or her report within thirty (30) days."

**15. Using "and/or."**
This construction is ambiguous. Does it mean both conditions must be met, or either one?

*Problem:* "A fine and/or imprisonment shall be imposed."

*Fix:* Decide what you mean. If you mean both: "A fine and imprisonment shall be imposed." If you mean either or both: "A fine, imprisonment, or both, shall be imposed."

**16. Passive voice obscuring responsibility.**
The bill says something must be done but uses the passive voice, leaving unclear who is responsible.

*Problem:* "The necessary funds shall be allocated."

*Fix:* "The Ministry of Finance, Budget and Management shall allocate the necessary funds."

**17. No sunset clause for a pilot program.**
The bill establishes a temporary or experimental program but does not include an expiration date.

*Problem:* A "pilot program" is created with no stated end date, no evaluation mechanism, and no trigger for expansion or termination.

*Fix:* Add a sunset clause: "This program shall operate for a period of three (3) years from the effectivity of this Act. Within six (6) months before the expiration of this period, the implementing agency shall submit to the Parliament, through the appropriate committee, an evaluation report on the program's effectiveness. The Parliament may extend the program upon review of the evaluation."

**18. Quorum and voting provisions that do not add up.**
The bill creates a body and specifies a quorum or voting threshold that is mathematically problematic.

*Problem:* A board has seven (7) members, and the bill requires a "two-thirds vote of all members" for certain decisions. Two-thirds of seven is 4.67 — not a whole number.

*Fix:* "A vote of at least five (5) members shall be required for..." Or specify: "a vote of two-thirds of all members, rounded up to the nearest whole number."

**19. General repealing clause with no specific repeals.**
The bill ends with "All laws, decrees, orders, rules and regulations, or parts thereof, inconsistent with the provisions of this Act are hereby repealed or modified accordingly" but does not identify any specific law being repealed.

*Problem:* Nobody — not the implementing agency, not the courts, not the public — knows what has been repealed.

*Fix:* Identify specific provisions: "Section 12 of BAA No. 5 and Section 8(c) of BAA No. 19 are hereby repealed." If a general repealing clause is also desired, place it after the specific repeals as a safety net — but never as a substitute.

**20. Incomplete effectivity clause.**
The bill omits or truncates the effectivity clause.

*Problem:* "This Act shall take effect upon its approval." But under the BOL and the Constitution, laws require publication before taking effect.

*Fix:* "This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days following its complete publication in at least one (1) newspaper of general circulation within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao."

## 11.4 Gender Sensitivity Review

Every bill filed in the Bangsamoro Parliament should be reviewed for gender sensitivity. This is not a matter of political correctness. It is a legal obligation rooted in the BOL, the Bangsamoro Development Plan, and the Philippines' commitments under international law, including the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW).

Article IX, Section 11 of the BOL requires that "there shall be at least one (1) woman to be appointed to the Bangsamoro Cabinet" and that the Parliament shall enact a law "ensuring the representation of women in other decision-making and policy-determining bodies of the Bangsamoro Government."[^12] Article XIII, Section 5 further provides that the Bangsamoro Government "shall guarantee full and direct participation of women in governance and development process, ensuring that women shall benefit equally in the implementation of development programs and projects."[^13] The Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028 identifies gender mainstreaming as a cross-cutting strategy across all sectors. These commitments must be reflected in legislation.

### What a Gender Sensitivity Review Covers

A gender sensitivity review examines the bill at three levels:

**Language.** Does the bill use gender-neutral or gender-inclusive language? The standard in modern legislative drafting is to avoid masculine pronouns as the default. Instead of "he" or "his," use "the person," "the official," or "he or she" and "his or her." Instead of "chairman," use "chairperson." Instead of "fireman," use "firefighter." The goal is not to police vocabulary but to ensure that the law, as written, treats women and men equally in its language.

Where the bill addresses a role that has historically been associated with one gender, consider whether the language inadvertently excludes the other. A bill on "fishermen's cooperatives" may unintentionally signal that women who fish are not covered. Changing the term to "fisherfolk cooperatives" resolves the issue without changing the substance.

**Substance.** Does the bill affect women and men differently? If so, does it account for those differences? Consider:

- Does the bill provide for maternity protections if it regulates employment?
- Does the bill ensure women's representation on boards, committees, and decision-making bodies it creates?
- If the bill addresses land rights, does it recognize the property rights of women, including married women?
- If the bill establishes benefit programs, are the eligibility criteria designed in a way that women can realistically meet?
- Does the bill address gender-based violence if relevant to its subject matter?
- Does the bill include sex-disaggregated data collection requirements for monitoring and evaluation?

**Impact.** Will the bill have a disproportionate impact on women, even if its language is facially neutral? A bill that requires applicants to appear in person at a provincial office during business hours may disproportionately burden women who have caregiving responsibilities. A bill that requires collateral for a loan program may disproportionately exclude women who do not hold property titles in their own names.

### The Gender Review Checklist

Apply these specific checks to every bill:

1. Does the bill use gender-neutral language throughout?
2. Does the bill provide for women's representation in any body it creates?
3. If the bill regulates employment, does it include maternity and parental leave protections?
4. Does the bill require sex-disaggregated data collection for monitoring purposes?
5. Does the bill allocate a portion of its budget to gender and development (GAD) programs, consistent with existing GAD budget requirements?
6. Does the bill contain provisions that could disproportionately disadvantage women, even if facially neutral?
7. Have women's organizations and gender experts been consulted during the drafting process?
8. Does the bill align with the Bangsamoro Development Plan's gender mainstreaming strategy?

## 11.5 Human Rights Review

The BOL contains a comprehensive set of rights provisions in Article IX (Basic Rights) that go beyond restating the rights in the Philippine Constitution.[^14] The 1987 Constitution's own Bill of Rights (Article III of the Constitution) applies to all persons in the Philippines, including everyone within the Bangsamoro. The BOL's Article IX adds Bangsamoro-specific rights — transitional justice, reparation for unjust dispossession, indigenous peoples' rights, customary rights, religious freedom, human rights protections, labor rights, women's protection, and the rights of settlers and non-Moro indigenous communities.

Every bill must be reviewed against these provisions. A bill that violates human rights — even unintentionally — is a bill that invites litigation, invalidation, and public opposition.

### The Human Rights Review Process

**Step 1: Identify the rights at stake.** For every bill, ask: Which fundamental rights does this bill touch? A bill regulating assembly may implicate freedom of assembly. A bill establishing a regulatory body with investigative powers may implicate the right against unreasonable searches. A bill on social media may implicate freedom of expression. Identify all affected rights.

**Step 2: Check the BOL Basic Rights and the Constitution's Bill of Rights.** Read Article IX (Basic Rights) of the BOL and Article III (Bill of Rights) of the 1987 Constitution, and determine whether any provision of the bill conflicts with any right recognized there. Pay particular attention to:

- The right to due process and equal protection (1987 Constitution, Article III, Sections 1-2)
- The right to freedom of expression, assembly, and association (Constitution, Article III)
- The right against unreasonable searches and seizures (Constitution, Article III)
- The rights of the accused in criminal proceedings (Constitution, Article III)
- The right to privacy (Constitution, Article III)
- The rights of indigenous peoples within the Bangsamoro (BOL, Article IX, Section 3)
- The rights of women (BOL, Article IX, Sections 11-12)
- Customary rights and non-discrimination (BOL, Article IX, Section 4)

**Step 3: Check the bill's penalty provisions.** If the bill imposes criminal penalties, verify that:

- The prohibited acts are described with sufficient specificity (no vague or overbroad prohibitions)
- The penalties are proportionate to the offense
- Due process protections are observed (notice, hearing, right to counsel)
- The bill does not impose retroactive penalties
- The bill does not create a penalty so severe that it amounts to cruel or unusual punishment

**Step 4: Check administrative provisions for due process.** If the bill creates administrative proceedings — license revocation, disciplinary proceedings, permit cancellation — verify that the affected person has the right to:

- Receive written notice of the charges or grounds
- Present evidence and witnesses
- Be represented by counsel
- Receive a written decision stating the reasons
- Appeal the decision to a higher authority or to a court

**Step 5: Check the bill against international obligations.** The Philippines is a signatory to numerous international human rights instruments that are binding in the Bangsamoro. Check the bill against:

- The International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)
- The International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR)
- The Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW)
- The Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC)
- The United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP)
- The Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (CAB), to the extent its provisions inform the interpretation of BOL rights

### Common Human Rights Issues in Legislation

- Licensing or permit requirements that restrict the exercise of fundamental rights (e.g., requiring a permit for peaceful assembly)
- Broad investigative powers without judicial oversight
- Administrative detention provisions that bypass the courts
- Provisions that discriminate — directly or indirectly — based on ethnicity, religion, sex, or other protected categories
- Penal provisions that lack mens rea (intent) requirements for serious offenses
- Collective punishment provisions (penalizing a community for the acts of individuals)

## 11.6 BOL Compliance Review

The Bangsamoro Organic Law is the supreme law of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region, subordinate only to the Philippine Constitution.[^15] Every bill enacted by the Parliament must conform to the BOL. A bill that conflicts with the BOL is ultra vires — beyond the Parliament's power — and may be struck down by the courts.

The BOL compliance review is a systematic check of the bill against the relevant provisions of the BOL. It is more specific than the general legal authority check in the self-review checklist (Section 11.1.1). It requires you to read the BOL with the bill beside you and compare them, provision by provision.

### How to Conduct a BOL Compliance Review

**Step 1: Identify the relevant BOL provisions.** Read the bill and list every BOL article that touches on the bill's subject matter. For most bills, this includes:

- Article V (Powers of Government) — Does the bill fall within the enumerated or general welfare powers?
- Article XII (Fiscal Autonomy) — Does the bill involve taxation, revenue, or appropriation?
- Article IX (Basic Rights) — Does the bill affect fundamental rights, including transitional justice, indigenous peoples, women, labor, or human rights?
- Article VII (Parliament) — Does the bill comply with the rules on legislative procedure?
- Article X (Bangsamoro Justice System) — Does the bill touch on Shari'ah jurisdiction or court procedures?

**Step 2: Check for direct conflicts.** Read each relevant BOL provision and ask: Does anything in this bill directly contradict what the BOL says? Examples:

- Article XII, Section 9 of the BOL provides that the taxing power of the Bangsamoro Government "shall not extend to" income taxes (except on banks and financial institutions), customs duties, and several other categories.[^16] A bill imposing a regional income tax would directly conflict with this provision.
- Article IX, Section 11 of the BOL requires the Parliament to enact a law "ensuring the representation of women in other decision-making and policy-determining bodies." A bill that creates a governing body with no provision for women's representation may conflict with this requirement.
- The BOL requires that laws take effect after publication. A bill with an effectivity clause that bypasses publication conflicts with the BOL.

**Step 3: Check for implicit conflicts.** Some conflicts are not obvious on the surface. They arise when the bill's provisions, taken as a whole, undermine the spirit or intent of the BOL even if no single provision directly contradicts a specific section. Examples:

- A bill that concentrates power in a single agency to such a degree that it effectively undermines the decentralization principle embedded in the BOL
- A bill that creates a regulatory framework so burdensome that it effectively nullifies a right recognized in the BOL
- A bill that delegates so much authority to the executive that it effectively transfers legislative power away from the Parliament

**Step 4: Check boundary issues.** The BOL divides powers between the Bangsamoro Government and the national government. Some powers are exclusive to the Bangsamoro. Others are concurrent. Still others are reserved to Congress. The boundary is not always sharp. For bills that operate near this boundary, ask:

- Is this subject among the fifty-five enumerated powers in Article V, Section 2?
- If not, does the general welfare clause apply?
- Is there a national law on the same subject? If so, does the BOL give the Parliament authority to legislate on the subject despite the national law?
- Has the Supreme Court issued any ruling on the scope of Bangsamoro authority over this subject?

**Step 5: Document your findings.** Prepare a brief BOL compliance memo — even if it is just a one-page summary — stating which BOL provisions you reviewed, your conclusion, and any areas of concern. This memo serves as an internal record and can be shared with the sponsoring MP or the committee.

## 11.7 Bangsamoro Parliamentary Conventions

Every legislature develops practices and conventions that are not written in the formal rules but are understood by experienced Members and staff. The Bangsamoro Parliament, though young, has already established several such conventions. Knowing them will help you produce drafts that align with how the Parliament actually works, not just how the rules say it should work.

### Conventions on Bill Drafting and Format

**The explanatory note is expected but not required.** Resolution No. 268 describes the explanatory statement as "optional" (Rule XVII, Section 7(a)).[^17] In practice, every bill filed in the Parliament includes one. The explanatory note has become a de facto requirement. Omitting it invites questions about the bill's purpose and weakens the sponsoring MP's position during deliberation.

**The committee report accompanies every bill reported out.** Section 16 of Rule XII requires committees to prepare a report containing discussions, views, amendments, and justifications.[^18] In practice, the committee report has evolved into a substantial document — sometimes longer than the bill itself — that serves as the primary reference during plenary deliberation. Drafters should anticipate contributing to the committee report by preparing amendment summaries and justifications.

**Government bills take precedence.** Resolution No. 268 provides that government bills have precedence over private member bills "without prejudice to consolidation."[^19] In practice, the legislative calendar is driven by the government's priority agenda. Drafters working on government bills should be prepared for accelerated timelines. Drafters working on private member bills should plan for the possibility that the bill may wait in the calendar for a longer period.

**Consolidation is preferred over competition.** When multiple bills cover the same subject, the committee typically consolidates them into a single bill or a substitute bill rather than choosing one and discarding the others. This means your draft should be written with the awareness that its provisions may be merged with provisions from another bill. Use clear, modular drafting that allows individual sections to be extracted and combined with sections from other bills.

### Conventions on Legislative Process

**Interpellation is substantive, not ceremonial.** The period of interpellation in the Bangsamoro Parliament has developed into a rigorous examination of the bill. Members ask detailed questions about specific provisions, demand justifications for policy choices, and probe for weaknesses. Prepare for interpellation as if it were a cross-examination. The sponsoring MP must be able to explain every provision of the bill — which means the drafter must be able to explain it to the MP first.

**Floor amendments during the period of amendments are serious proposals.** In some legislatures, floor amendments are rarely adopted. In the Bangsamoro Parliament, floor amendments are a regular feature of plenary deliberation. Members who register with the Majority Floor Leader to propose amendments typically come prepared. Drafters should monitor the list of registered interpellators and amendment proponents to anticipate what changes may be proposed.

**The Majority Floor Leader controls the flow of business.** The Majority Floor Leader determines the order of business, maintains the list of interpellators, and sets the sequence in which Members are recognized to speak. Drafters should coordinate with the Majority Floor Leader's office when preparing for plenary deliberation of a bill, especially regarding the timing of committee report presentation and the expected schedule for the period of amendments.

**Swift Passage Procedure is genuinely exceptional.** Resolution No. 268 provides for a Swift Passage Procedure that allows a bill to skip the detailed committee scrutiny and pass in seven working days.[^20] In practice, this procedure is reserved for genuine emergencies and bills that have broad consensus. Drafters should not plan around the assumption that a bill will receive swift passage unless there is clear and explicit guidance from the Chief Minister's office or the Speaker.

### Conventions on Relationships

**The PRLS is a resource, not a gatekeeper.** The Policy Research and Legal Services supports the Parliament and its committees with research and technical expertise. In practice, the relationship between individual MPs' staff and the PRLS is collaborative. Drafters should engage the PRLS early in the research phase — not as a last-minute review — and treat its input as an important quality check.

**Committee chairs have significant influence over the legislative agenda within their jurisdiction.** A bill referred to a committee is effectively in the hands of the committee chair, who decides when it will be scheduled for hearing, how many hearings it will receive, and how aggressively the committee will push it toward a report. Drafters working on a bill should maintain a working relationship with the committee secretariat.

**The Secretary-General's office is the official custodian of the legislative record.** All bills, resolutions, committee reports, and journals pass through the Secretary-General's office. Drafters must ensure that the correct and final version of any document — bill, amendment, committee report — is the version filed with the Secretary-General. Versions circulated informally do not count. The official record is what the Secretary-General's office has on file.

### Conventions on Style and Substance

**Numbers are written in words and figures.** This convention — "thirty (30) days," "Five Hundred Thousand Pesos (PhP500,000.00)" — is universal in Bangsamoro legislation. Do not omit either the words or the figures.

**Monetary amounts use the Philippine peso with the PhP abbreviation.** The format is: "Two Hundred Thousand Pesos (PhP200,000.00)."

**The enacting clause is standard.** The timeless form is "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Parliament:" — during the BTA period, the form is "Be it enacted by the Bangsamoro Transition Authority in Parliament assembled:" Do not vary from the applicable standard formulation.

**The effectivity clause follows a standard form.** "This Act shall take effect fifteen (15) days following its complete publication in at least one (1) newspaper of general circulation within the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao." This formulation tracks the requirement under the BOL and is used consistently in enacted BAAs.

**Section headings are italicized in enrolled bills.** When the bill is enrolled — prepared for signature by the Speaker and Chief Minister — section headings appear in italics. Follow this convention in your drafts.

**The bill format uses "SEC." for section numbering.** Sections are numbered with Arabic numerals preceded by "SEC." — for example, "SEC. 1.," "SEC. 2.," and so on. Articles are numbered with Roman numerals.

### Why Conventions Matter

Parliamentary conventions are not law. They can change. They can be overridden. But ignoring them creates unnecessary friction. A bill that arrives at the committee in a nonstandard format requires the committee secretariat to reformat it. A bill that omits the explanatory note raises questions that could have been answered before filing. A bill that ignores the established terminology for Bangsamoro institutions signals that the drafter is unfamiliar with the Parliament's work.

Following conventions is not about conformity for its own sake. It is about efficiency. A bill that looks like other bills, uses language like other bills, and follows the same structure as other bills lets the reader focus on what matters: the substance of the legislation. Everything else should be invisible.
