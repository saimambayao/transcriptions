# Chapter 5 — Step 2: Define and Deliberate the Problem or Need (DD)

A vague problem produces a vague solution. If you cannot state the problem in one or two sentences, you do not understand it yet. Step 2 forces clarity. You articulate exactly what is wrong, deliberate with stakeholders to confirm your understanding, dig for root causes, and set measurable objectives.

This is where most weak CSW falls apart. Staff who skip straight from context to research end up collecting data without direction. They answer questions nobody asked.

## 5.1 Articulating the Problem Statement

### 5.1.1 Specific, Measurable, and Aligned

A good problem statement has three qualities:

**Specific** — It names the exact issue, not a broad category. "BARMM has an education problem" is not a problem statement. "42% of daycare centers in BARMM lack qualified ECCD teachers, resulting in non-compliance with RA 12199 standards" is a problem statement.

**Measurable** — It includes a quantifiable dimension. How many people are affected? What is the gap between current and desired states? What is the cost of the problem?

**Aligned** — It connects to the organization's mandate and the BDP. A problem that falls outside your office's mandate may be real, but it is not your problem to solve through CSW.

### 5.1.2 The Problem Statement Template

Use this structure to draft your problem statement:

> **[Who/What]** is experiencing **[specific problem]**, as evidenced by **[data/indicator]**. This results in **[negative consequences]**. The gap between the current state (**[baseline]**) and the desired state (**[target]**) is **[quantified gap]**. This falls within the mandate of **[your office/entity]** under **[legal basis]**.

**Example — Good problem statement:**

> An estimated 1,247 out of 2,985 public daycare centers in BARMM (42%) operate without qualified ECCD teachers, as documented in the MBHTE 2025 Status Report. This non-compliance with RA 12199 minimum standards results in substandard early learning outcomes for approximately 62,000 children aged 3-5. The gap between the current qualified-teacher ratio (58%) and the national standard (100%) is 42 percentage points. Addressing this gap falls within the mandate of MBHTE under BOL Article VI, Section 13 and is aligned with BDP Goal 2, Strategy 2.3.

**Example — Weak problem statement:**

> There are not enough ECCD teachers in BARMM. The situation needs improvement.

The weak version lacks specificity, data, consequences, and institutional alignment. It gives the analyst no direction for research or option generation.

## 5.2 Engaging Stakeholders in Deliberation

You defined the problem in draft form. Now test it against the people who live with it.

**Deliberation** means structured discussion with stakeholders who have relevant knowledge, authority, or experience. This is not a courtesy call. It is a data-gathering activity that strengthens your problem definition.

### Who to consult:

| Stakeholder Type | What They Contribute | Method |
|-----------------|---------------------|--------|
| **Subject matter experts** | Technical accuracy, data validation | Interview, consultation |
| **Affected communities** | Ground-truth perspective on the problem | Focus group, community dialogue |
| **Implementing agencies** | Operational reality, feasibility constraints | Working meeting |
| **Decision-maker (preliminary)** | Direction confirmation, priority clarification | Brief check-in |
| **Colleagues and peers** | Alternative perspectives, blind spot identification | Informal discussion |

### How to deliberate effectively:

1. **Share your draft problem statement** before the meeting. Do not ask "What do you think the problem is?" Ask "Is this an accurate description of the problem?"
2. **Listen for corrections.** Stakeholders may reframe the problem in ways you did not anticipate. A community leader may say the teacher shortage is a symptom of a salary problem. That reframing changes your root cause analysis.
3. **Document inputs.** Record who said what. Note areas of consensus and disagreement.
4. **Revise the problem statement** based on deliberation findings.

> **TIP:** Deliberation is not consensus-building. You do not need everyone to agree on the problem statement. You need to ensure your definition is accurate, complete, and defensible. Note dissenting views in your CSW and explain why you maintained your definition.

## 5.3 Root Cause Analysis

### 5.3.1 The 5 Whys Method

The simplest root cause tool. Start with the problem and ask "Why?" five times. Each answer becomes the input for the next question.

**Example — ECCD teacher shortage:**

| Round | Question | Answer |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **Why 1** | Why do 42% of daycare centers lack qualified ECCD teachers? | Because qualified applicants do not apply for daycare teacher positions in BARMM. |
| **Why 2** | Why don't qualified applicants apply? | Because the salary (SG 8-9) is below the market rate for ECCD-qualified graduates. |
| **Why 3** | Why is the salary below market rate? | Because daycare teacher positions are classified under local government plantilla at entry-level salary grades. |
| **Why 4** | Why hasn't the classification been updated? | Because LGUs lack the fiscal capacity to reclassify positions, and BARMM has not legislated salary supplements for ECCD teachers. |
| **Why 5** | Why hasn't BARMM legislated salary supplements? | Because no CSW has been produced to present the evidence and recommend legislative action. |

The 5 Whys reveal that the root cause is not "lack of teachers" (a symptom) but a structural salary and classification problem that requires legislative and fiscal intervention.

### 5.3.2 Fishbone (Ishikawa) Diagram

For complex problems with multiple contributing factors, use a fishbone diagram. Organize causes into categories:

| Category | Possible Causes |
|----------|----------------|
| **People** | Skills gaps, staffing shortages, resistance to change |
| **Process** | Unclear procedures, bureaucratic delays, lack of coordination |
| **Policy** | Outdated regulations, conflicting mandates, missing guidelines |
| **Resources** | Budget constraints, infrastructure gaps, equipment shortages |
| **Environment** | Political dynamics, geographic challenges, conflict-affected areas |
| **Data** | Missing baseline data, unreliable metrics, no monitoring system |

Map your specific CSW issue onto these categories. Identify which category contains the dominant causes. This directs your research (Step 3) and option generation (Step 4).

> **TIP:** Use both tools. Start with the 5 Whys for a quick linear analysis. Then use the fishbone to check whether you missed lateral causes. The combination covers both depth and breadth.

## 5.4 Assessing Consequences of Inaction

Your problem statement describes what is wrong. Now describe what happens if nothing changes.

Decision-makers need to understand the cost of inaction. This is your "so what?" section. If the consequences are minor, the CSW may not be a priority. If the consequences are severe, the urgency becomes clear.

**Structure your consequences assessment:**

| Timeframe | Consequence | Impact |
|-----------|------------|--------|
| **Short-term (0-12 months)** | What happens immediately if the problem continues? | Quantify: affected persons, budget cost, compliance risk |
| **Medium-term (1-3 years)** | How does the problem compound over time? | Quantify: cumulative impact, opportunity cost |
| **Long-term (3+ years)** | What systemic damage results from sustained inaction? | Describe: institutional reputation, development trajectory, public trust |

**Example:**

> **Short-term:** 62,000 children continue receiving substandard ECCD. BARMM fails to meet RA 12199 compliance deadline (June 2026). Risk of national government intervention.
>
> **Medium-term:** A generation of BARMM children enters elementary school with weaker cognitive and social foundations. Achievement gaps between BARMM and national averages widen. Estimated 15-point gap in National Achievement Test scores by 2028.
>
> **Long-term:** Human capital deficit undermines BDP Goal 2 (Social Development). BARMM's education competitiveness declines. Economic productivity losses estimated at PHP 8-12 billion annually by 2035 (based on World Bank human capital index methodology).

## 5.5 Establishing SMART Objectives

With the problem defined and consequences established, set objectives for your CSW effort.

**SMART criteria:**

| Criterion | What It Means | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Specific** | Names exactly what you aim to achieve | Increase the proportion of BARMM daycare centers with qualified ECCD teachers |
| **Measurable** | Includes a quantifiable target | From 58% to 80% |
| **Achievable** | Realistic given constraints | Requires legislative action + MFBM budget allocation |
| **Relevant** | Aligned with mandate and BDP | BOL Article VI, Section 13; BDP Goal 2, Strategy 2.3 |
| **Time-bound** | Has a deadline | By the end of fiscal year 2028 |

**Combined SMART objective:**

> Increase the proportion of BARMM public daycare centers with qualified ECCD teachers from 58% to 80% by FY 2028, through a combination of salary supplements, training programs, and scholarship incentives, aligned with BOL Article VI, Section 13 and BDP Goal 2, Strategy 2.3.

Write 2-3 SMART objectives per CSW output. One primary objective and 1-2 secondary objectives. Do not write more than three — focus matters more than comprehensiveness.

## 5.6 Documenting the Problem Statement

Your Step 2 output is a **documented problem statement** that includes:

1. **The problem statement** (specific, measurable, aligned)
2. **Stakeholder deliberation summary** (who was consulted, what they said)
3. **Root cause analysis** (5 Whys and/or fishbone findings)
4. **Consequences of inaction** (short, medium, long-term)
5. **SMART objectives** (2-3 objectives with targets and timelines)

This document becomes Section 3 of your General CSW (GN03), Section I-III of your Legislative Briefer, or the Problem Statement chapter of your Policy Recommendation. It anchors everything that follows.

> **CAUTION:** Do not change your problem statement once you begin Step 3 (Research) unless new evidence forces a revision. Scope creep in the problem definition is the most common cause of CSW projects that run over deadline. If the problem changes, restart the problem definition process. Do not patch.

## 5.7 Why This Step Matters — Consequences of Skipping

When you skip problem definition:

**You solve the wrong problem.** Without a clear statement, your research wanders. You collect data on symptoms instead of causes. Your options address surface issues while the root cause persists.

**You waste resources.** A well-defined problem narrows the research scope (Step 3) and focuses option generation (Step 4). An undefined problem means researching everything and exploring options that do not connect.

**Decision-makers cannot act.** A CSW output without a clear problem statement leaves the decision-maker asking: "What exactly am I deciding?" They cannot approve what they do not understand.

**Stakeholders lose trust.** When you present a recommendation that does not match their experience of the problem, stakeholders disengage. They see the CSW as disconnected from reality.

**Real BARMM example of skipping Step 2:**

A Parliament committee staff member was asked to prepare a briefer on health service delivery in island municipalities. The staff member skipped problem definition and went directly to research. The resulting briefer presented 40 pages of health statistics, facility inventories, and program descriptions. But it never stated what problem the committee should address.

The Committee Chairperson asked: "So what is the problem, and what should we do about it?"

The staff member could not answer. The briefer was shelved. Two months of work produced zero actionable output.

A single paragraph — "Island municipalities in BARMM have 1 physician per 47,000 population versus the DOH standard of 1:20,000, resulting in maternal mortality rates 3.2 times the national average" — would have given the committee the clarity it needed.

---

**Chapter Summary**

- A problem statement must be specific, measurable, and aligned with your office's mandate
- Use the problem statement template to structure your articulation
- Deliberate with stakeholders to validate and refine the problem definition
- Apply the 5 Whys and fishbone diagram to identify root causes, not symptoms
- Assess consequences of inaction across short, medium, and long-term timeframes
- Set 2-3 SMART objectives with quantified targets and deadlines
- Document the complete problem statement as the anchor for all subsequent ADDRESS IT steps
- Skipping this step produces unfocused research, irrelevant options, and CSW that decision-makers cannot act on
