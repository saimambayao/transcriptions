# Chapter 13 — Writing Standards and Quality Assurance

Your analysis can be brilliant. Your research can be thorough. Your recommendation can be optimal. But if the writing is unclear, the document is incomplete, or the review process is skipped, the CSW fails at the last mile.

This chapter establishes the writing standards and quality assurance practices that separate professional CSW from amateur paperwork.

## 13.1 The BARMM Guidebook Voice

CSW in the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region should have a consistent voice: **professional, direct, evidence-based, and accessible.**

### Voice Characteristics

| Characteristic | What It Means | Example |
|---------------|-------------|---------|
| **Professional** | Formal but not stilted. No slang. No bureaucratic jargon without explanation. | Write "the agency's utilization rate decreased" not "the utilization rate went down bad" |
| **Direct** | State conclusions clearly. Use active voice. | Write "This report recommends Option B" not "Option B is being recommended by this report" |
| **Evidence-based** | Every claim cites a source. Opinions are identified as such. | Write "The dropout rate is 35% (MBHTE, 2025)" not "The dropout rate is high" |
| **Accessible** | A reader unfamiliar with the topic can understand the document. | Define technical terms on first use. Avoid acronyms without expansion. |

### Sentence and Paragraph Standards

- **Average sentence length:** 15-20 words. Maximum 30 words per sentence.
- **Maximum paragraph length:** 5 sentences. One-sentence paragraphs are acceptable for emphasis.
- **Voice:** 80%+ active voice. Use passive only when the actor is unknown or irrelevant.
- **Mood:** Imperative for instructions ("Submit the report by Friday"). Indicative for analysis ("The data shows a declining trend").

### Prohibited Phrases

The following phrases add bulk without adding meaning. Replace them.

| Prohibited Phrase | Replacement |
|------------------|-------------|
| "It is important to note that" | (Delete — just state the note) |
| "In order to" | "To" |
| "It should be noted" | (Delete — just state it) |
| "With regard to" | "About" or "On" |
| "In the context of" | "In" or "For" |
| "As previously mentioned" | (Delete — or cite the section) |
| "For the purposes of" | "For" |
| "In light of the foregoing" | "Given this" or "Therefore" |
| "The aforementioned" | Name the thing again |
| "It is recommended that" | "This report recommends" |
| "Pursuant to the provisions of" | "Under" |
| "Notwithstanding the foregoing" | "Despite this" or "However" |

> **TIP:** Read your CSW aloud. If a sentence makes you pause to figure out its meaning, rewrite it. If a paragraph forces you to re-read, split it or simplify the structure.

## 13.2 Clarity, Completeness, Conciseness, Objectivity

Every CSW output is evaluated against four quality dimensions.

### Clarity

**Test:** Can a reader understand your document on the first reading?

| Clarity Problem | Fix |
|----------------|-----|
| Ambiguous pronoun references | Replace "it" and "they" with specific nouns |
| Long, multi-clause sentences | Break into two sentences |
| Undefined acronyms | Spell out on first use, then use the acronym |
| Vague quantities ("several," "many," "some") | Replace with specific numbers |
| Abstract language | Use concrete examples |

### Completeness

**Test:** Does the document contain every section required by its CSW type template?

- [ ] All required sections are present and filled (no placeholders)
- [ ] Data covers the full scope of the analysis (not just convenient data)
- [ ] All options in the evaluation matrix have been scored on all criteria
- [ ] The implementation plan includes timeline, resources, and responsibilities
- [ ] The Executive Summary covers problem, recommendation, benefits, and action requested
- [ ] Legal citations are complete (Article, Section, full BAA number)

### Conciseness

**Test:** Can any sentence be removed without losing meaning?

- Delete filler phrases (see prohibited phrases above)
- Eliminate redundancy ("completely finished" becomes "finished")
- Remove hedging language ("it might perhaps be possible to consider" becomes "consider")
- Use tables instead of paragraphs when presenting comparative data
- Aim for the shortest document that is still complete

### Objectivity

**Test:** Would someone who disagrees with your recommendation still find the analysis fair?

- Present all sides of an argument before stating your position
- Distinguish facts from opinions
- Report unfavorable findings as transparently as favorable ones
- Avoid advocacy language in the analysis sections (save it for the recommendation)
- Identify and disclose your assumptions

> **CAUTION:** The most common objectivity failure in BARMM CSW is advocacy disguised as analysis. If you have a preferred outcome, you may unconsciously downplay evidence against it. Guard against this by asking a peer to read your options analysis and check for bias.

## 13.3 The "So What" Test

Every section of your CSW must answer: "So what?" If the reader can ask "so what?" and your document has no answer, the section is missing its purpose.

### Applying the "So What" Test

| Section | Data/Finding | "So What?" | Actionable Interpretation |
|---------|-------------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Budget comparison | "The agency's MOOE increased by 23% from FY 2025 to FY 2026" | So what? | "The 23% MOOE increase is driven by a PHP 45M addition for the new scholarship program, which aligns with BDP Goal 2. The committee should verify whether the program design justifies this scale of investment." |
| Research findings | "42% of daycare centers lack qualified teachers" | So what? | "This 42% gap means 62,000 children receive substandard early learning, placing BARMM at risk of non-compliance with RA 12199 by June 2026" |
| Stakeholder analysis | "The LGU Mayors' League expressed concerns about fiscal capacity" | So what? | "LGU fiscal constraints mean the national government or BARMM block grant must cover the salary supplement cost. A local cost-sharing model is not feasible." |

Every data point should connect to a consequence, a decision, or a next step. Data without interpretation is noise.

## 13.4 Peer Review Process

No CSW should be submitted without at least one peer review.

### Peer Review Protocol

| Step | Action | Timeframe |
|------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | Author completes self-review using the QA checklist (Appendix F) | Before sharing |
| 2 | Author sends draft to a peer reviewer (different person from the author) | Allow 1-2 working days |
| 3 | Peer reviewer reads the entire document and provides written comments | Written feedback, not verbal |
| 4 | Author reviews feedback and incorporates valid points | Same day or next day |
| 5 | Author and reviewer discuss any disagreements | Brief meeting |
| 6 | Author submits revised draft to supervisor | After incorporating feedback |

### What Peer Reviewers Should Check

| Category | Questions |
|----------|----------|
| **Logic** | Does the recommendation follow from the analysis? Are there logical gaps? |
| **Data** | Are the numbers correct? Are sources cited? Are there data inconsistencies? |
| **Completeness** | Are any required sections missing or incomplete? |
| **Clarity** | Is the document understandable on first reading? Are there confusing passages? |
| **Formatting** | Does the document follow the template? Are tables, headings, and citations consistent? |
| **Sensitivity** | Are there political, cultural, or security sensitivities the author may have missed? |

> **TIP:** Choose a peer reviewer who knows the subject well enough to challenge your analysis, but is not so close to the project that they share your blind spots. The best reviewer is someone in a related but different role.

## 13.5 Shari'ah and Gender Review

BARMM CSW has two additional quality dimensions that most Philippine government CSW does not: Shari'ah alignment and gender responsiveness.

### Shari'ah Review

**When required:** Any CSW that touches family law, personal status, halal industry, Islamic finance, waqf (endowment), zakat (obligatory charity), education with Islamic content, or any matter within the Shari'ah justice system's jurisdiction.

**What to check:**

| Element | Question |
|---------|---------|
| **BOL Article X compliance** | Does the recommendation align with the BOL's provisions on Shari'ah? |
| **Consistency with Islamic principles** | Does the proposed action conflict with established Islamic jurisprudential positions? |
| **Dar al-Ifta consultation** | For matters requiring fatwas or religious opinions, has the Dar al-Ifta been consulted? |
| **Language and framing** | Is the document respectful of Islamic terminology and concepts? |

Not every CSW requires Shari'ah review. But when the subject matter intersects with Islamic governance, law, or values, do not skip it.

### Gender Review

**When required:** All CSW should be reviewed for gender responsiveness, but it is especially critical for CSW involving social services, education, health, economic programs, personnel policies, and budgeting.

**What to check:**

| Element | Question |
|---------|---------|
| **Sex-disaggregated data** | Does the research present data broken down by sex where relevant? |
| **Gender-differentiated impact** | Does the impact analysis assess effects on women, men, and gender-diverse persons separately? |
| **GAD compliance** | Does the recommendation align with the Philippine Commission on Women (PCW) framework, adapted for BARMM? |
| **GAD budget allocation** | For budget proposals, is the minimum GAD allocation reflected? |
| **Inclusive language** | Does the document use gender-inclusive language? |

> **BANGSAMORO NOTE:** Gender review in BARMM must navigate the intersection of Islamic values and national GAD frameworks. This requires sensitivity and expertise. When in doubt, consult with both the Bangsamoro Women Commission and relevant Islamic scholars to ensure your CSW respects both frameworks.

## 13.6 The Transmittal Memorandum as Quality Gate

The transmittal memorandum (Chapter 8, Section 8.11) is more than a routing document. It is a quality gate.

When your supervisor signs the transmittal memo, they certify three things:

1. **Accuracy** — The facts in the CSW are correct to the best of their knowledge
2. **Completeness** — All required sections are filled and all necessary analysis has been conducted
3. **Quality** — The CSW meets professional standards and is decision-ready

### Quality Gate Checklist (Before Supervisor Signature)

The supervisor should verify before signing:

- [ ] Executive Summary is present and accurate
- [ ] Problem statement is specific and measurable
- [ ] Research findings are cited with sources
- [ ] At least 2-3 options were genuinely evaluated
- [ ] Recommendation is justified with evidence
- [ ] Implementation plan is actionable
- [ ] Call to action is clear and specific
- [ ] Legal citations are accurate (BOL, BAAs, national laws)
- [ ] Names and titles of officials are correct
- [ ] No grammatical errors or typos
- [ ] Template format is followed
- [ ] Shari'ah review completed (if applicable)
- [ ] Gender review completed (if applicable)

If any item fails, the CSW goes back for revision. The supervisor should not sign an incomplete or substandard transmittal.

> **TIP:** Build a culture where returning CSW for revision is normal, not punitive. Quality revision is a learning opportunity. The supervisor's feedback sharpens the analyst's skills. The analyst who never gets feedback never improves.

---

**Chapter Summary**

- Use the BARMM guidebook voice: professional, direct, evidence-based, accessible
- Maintain sentence and paragraph length standards. Eliminate prohibited phrases.
- Evaluate every CSW against four dimensions: clarity, completeness, conciseness, objectivity
- Apply the "So What" test to every data point and finding
- Conduct mandatory peer review before supervisor submission
- Complete Shari'ah review (where applicable) and gender review (for all CSW)
- Treat the transmittal memorandum as a quality gate — the supervisor's signature certifies accuracy, completeness, and quality
