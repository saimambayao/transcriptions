# Chapter 8 — Step 5: Submit Proposal or Report (SS)

Your analysis is complete. Your options are evaluated. Your recommendation is ready. Now you must package everything into a document the decision-maker can act on.

This is where most CSW outputs fail or succeed. Brilliant analysis buried in poor writing is useless. A clear, well-structured report with a compelling executive summary and a crisp call to action is what moves decisions forward.

## 8.1 Developing the Report Structure

Choose your CSW type (Chapter 3) and follow its section structure. Regardless of type, every CSW output shares a common architecture:

| Component | Purpose | Write When |
|-----------|---------|------------|
| **Executive Summary** | One-page overview of the entire document | Write LAST |
| **Background/Context** | What the assignment is and why it matters | From Step 1 notes |
| **Problem Statement** | What exactly needs to be addressed | From Step 2 output |
| **Research Findings** | What the evidence shows | From Step 3 output |
| **Options Analysis** | What alternatives exist and how they compare | From Step 4 output |
| **Recommendation** | What you advise and why | From Step 4 conclusion |
| **Implementation Plan** | How the recommendation would be carried out | Write now |
| **Call to Action** | What the decision-maker should approve | Write now |

Assemble the document in this order, but write the **Executive Summary last**. You cannot summarize what you have not yet written.

## 8.2 Writing the Executive Summary (Write Last)

The Executive Summary is the most-read part of your CSW. Many decision-makers read only this section before deciding whether to read further or sign immediately.

### Rules for Executive Summaries

1. **One page maximum.** If it exceeds one page, it is no longer a summary.
2. **Write it last.** After every other section is complete.
3. **Cover four elements:**
   - The problem (1-2 sentences)
   - The recommended solution (2-3 sentences)
   - The expected benefits (1-2 sentences)
   - The action requested from the decision-maker (1 sentence)
4. **No jargon.** Write as if the reader has no prior knowledge of the issue.
5. **No new information.** Every fact in the Executive Summary must appear in the body.

**Example Executive Summary:**

> An estimated 42% of public daycare centers in BARMM operate without qualified ECCD teachers, affecting approximately 62,000 children aged 3-5. This non-compliance with RA 12199 standards threatens BARMM's education outcomes and BDP Goal 2 targets.
>
> This report recommends a combined approach: (1) a salary supplement authorized by new legislation to close the 40% gap between daycare teacher salaries and market rates, and (2) a teacher bridging program through administrative order to upgrade existing teachers' qualifications within 24 months. The estimated cost is PHP 340 million over three years.
>
> Implementation of this recommendation is expected to increase qualified-teacher coverage from 58% to 80% by FY 2028, directly advancing BDP Strategy 2.3 (Expanding Access to Quality Education).
>
> **Action requested:** Approval to prepare the legislative proposal (salary supplement BAA) and the administrative order (bridging program) for submission to the Committee on Education.

## 8.3 Presenting the Problem and Research Findings

This section consolidates your Step 2 and Step 3 outputs. Do not copy-paste your research notes. Present findings in a structured narrative that builds the case for action.

### Presentation Guidelines

**Lead with the most important finding.** Do not build suspense. State the key fact first, then support it with detail.

**Use visual aids.** Tables, charts, and graphs communicate data faster than paragraphs.

| Visual Aid | Use For |
|-----------|---------|
| **Tables** | Comparisons, multi-year data, criteria matrices |
| **Bar charts** | Budget comparisons, performance metrics |
| **Trend lines** | Multi-year trends, progress tracking |
| **Maps** | Geographic distribution of services or problems |
| **Flowcharts** | Process descriptions, decision trees |

**Cite every data point.** Decision-makers need to know where your numbers come from. Uncited data is unverified data.

**Distinguish facts from analysis.** "The utilization rate is 47%" is a fact. "The low utilization rate suggests weak absorptive capacity" is analysis. Present facts first, then your analytical interpretation.

## 8.4 Discussing Options Explored

Present all options you evaluated, not just the recommended one. Show the decision-maker the full landscape of possibilities.

For each option, include:

1. **Brief description** — What the option is and how it works
2. **Evaluation results** — Scores against the five criteria
3. **Key advantages** — Why this option has merit
4. **Key disadvantages** — Why this option falls short
5. **Why it ranked where it did** — Clear reasoning for its position

Include the Options Evaluation Matrix (Section 7.5) as a summary table.

> **TIP:** Be fair to all options. If you present one option with enthusiasm and the others with disdain, the decision-maker will suspect bias. Present all options objectively. Let the criteria-based evaluation carry your argument.

## 8.5 Presenting the Recommendation

State your recommendation clearly and directly.

**Structure:**

1. **The recommendation** — One paragraph stating exactly what you recommend
2. **Why this option** — Reference its evaluation scores and key advantages
3. **How it addresses the problem** — Connect directly to your problem statement and SMART objectives
4. **Expected outcomes** — Quantified benefits aligned with BDP targets
5. **Key risks** — What could go wrong and how to mitigate it

Do not hedge. "It is suggested that perhaps the ministry might consider..." is weak. "This report recommends that the Ministry adopt Option C" is direct.

## 8.6 Developing the Implementation Plan

A recommendation without an implementation plan is a wish. Decision-makers need to see how the approved option will translate into action.

### Implementation Plan Components

| Component | Content |
|-----------|---------|
| **Actions** | Specific steps to implement the recommendation |
| **Timeline** | Phased schedule with start dates, milestones, and completion dates |
| **Responsible parties** | Who leads each action (by position, not individual name) |
| **Resources required** | Budget, personnel, equipment, infrastructure |
| **Dependencies** | What must happen before each action can start |
| **Risks and mitigation** | What could go wrong and how you will handle it |
| **Monitoring indicators** | How you will track progress |

**Implementation Plan Template:**

| Phase | Action | Timeline | Responsible | Resources | Risk/Mitigation |
|-------|--------|----------|------------|-----------|-----------------|
| Phase 1 | Draft BAA for salary supplements | Month 1-2 | Legislative staff | Staff time | Risk: delayed committee calendar. Mitigation: early coordination with committee chair. |
| Phase 2 | Issue administrative order for bridging program | Month 2-3 | MBHTE Planning | Staff time, legal review | Risk: legal challenge. Mitigation: pre-clearance with legal counsel. |
| Phase 3 | Deploy bridging training to priority provinces | Month 4-12 | MBHTE, TEIs | PHP 85M (Year 1) | Risk: low enrollment. Mitigation: mandatory participation for unqualified daycare teachers. |

## 8.7 The Call to Action

End your CSW with a clear, specific call to action. Tell the decision-maker exactly what you are asking them to approve.

**Good call to action:**

> **Recommended action:** Approve the preparation and filing of a BAA establishing salary supplements for ECCD daycare teachers (estimated annual cost: PHP 95 million from the block grant education allocation), and the issuance of an administrative order establishing the ECCD Teacher Bridging Program.

**Weak call to action:**

> We hope the committee will consider these recommendations and take appropriate action as deemed fit.

The first version tells the decision-maker what to sign. The second leaves them guessing.

## 8.8 Review and Refinement

Before sending your CSW to your supervisor, review it yourself. Then have a peer review it.

### Self-Review Checklist

- [ ] Does the Executive Summary capture the full document in one page?
- [ ] Is the problem statement specific, measurable, and aligned?
- [ ] Are all data points cited with sources?
- [ ] Are there at least 2-3 genuine options (no strawmen)?
- [ ] Is the evaluation matrix consistent (same criteria, same scoring)?
- [ ] Does the recommendation follow logically from the analysis?
- [ ] Is the implementation plan actionable (specific steps, dates, owners)?
- [ ] Is the call to action clear and specific?
- [ ] Are all BOL, BAA, and legal citations accurate?
- [ ] Are names and titles of officials correct?
- [ ] Is the document free of grammatical errors and typos?
- [ ] Does it follow the appropriate CSW template format?

### Peer Review

Ask a colleague to read your CSW and answer three questions:

1. "After reading this, do you understand the problem?"
2. "Do you find the recommendation convincing?"
3. "Is anything confusing, missing, or unconvincing?"

Incorporate feedback before transmitting to your supervisor.

## 8.9 Obtaining Supervisor Review and Clearance

Your direct supervisor reviews and clears the CSW before it reaches the decision-maker. This is not a rubber stamp. It is a quality gate.

**What supervisors check:**

| Check | What They Look For |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Accuracy** | Are the facts correct? Are citations verified? |
| **Completeness** | Are all required sections filled? Are there gaps? |
| **Alignment** | Does this align with the office's position and priorities? |
| **Sensitivity** | Are there political, cultural, or security sensitivities the analyst may have missed? |
| **Quality** | Is the writing clear, professional, and decision-ready? |

**Engage your supervisor, do not surprise them.** Brief them informally before submitting the formal draft. If they have concerns about your direction, it is better to hear them early than after you have written 30 pages.

After supervisor clearance, attach the **Transmittal Memorandum** (Section 8.11) and submit through the appropriate channel.

## 8.10 Submitting to the Decision-Maker

When the CSW reaches the decision-maker, be prepared to:

1. **Present in person** if asked. Have a 5-minute verbal summary ready. Do not read the document aloud. Highlight the problem, the recommendation, and the action requested.
2. **Answer questions.** The decision-maker may challenge your data, question your options, or probe your reasoning. Know your material thoroughly.
3. **Accept the decision.** The decision-maker may approve, disapprove, or modify your recommendation. All three outcomes are valid. CSW is complete when the decision-maker can decide — the specific decision is theirs.
4. **Follow up.** After submission, confirm receipt. Check whether additional information is needed. Track the decision timeline.

## 8.11 The Transmittal Memorandum

Every CSW output is submitted with a **Transmittal Memorandum**. This is the cover memo that introduces the document and requests action.

### Transmittal Memorandum Format

| Element | Content |
|---------|---------|
| **TO** | Decision-maker (name and title) |
| **FROM** | Analyst/author (name and title), endorsed by supervisor |
| **DATE** | Submission date |
| **SUBJECT** | CSW title and reference number |
| **Purpose** | One-sentence statement of why you are submitting this document |
| **Summary** | 2-3 sentence summary of the key finding and recommendation |
| **Action Requested** | What the decision-maker should approve or decide |
| **Enclosure** | The CSW document (with page count) |
| **Prepared by** | Analyst's name, position, and signature |
| **Reviewed by** | Supervisor's name, position, and signature |

The transmittal memo is not a summary of the entire CSW. It is a routing document. Keep it to one page.

> **TIP:** The transmittal memorandum is your quality gate. Your supervisor's signature on the transmittal means they have reviewed and endorsed the CSW. Do not submit without it. An unsigned transmittal signals that no supervisor has cleared the work.

## 8.12 Why This Step Matters — Consequences of Skipping

When you submit poorly:

**The decision-maker cannot act.** If the CSW is disorganized, the executive summary is missing, or the call to action is vague, the document sits on the desk. No decision is made. The problem persists.

**Your analysis is wasted.** You may have done excellent research and option evaluation, but if the presentation is unclear, the decision-maker never sees the quality of your work.

**Your supervisor's reputation suffers.** The supervisor who endorsed your CSW is associated with its quality. Poor submission reflects on your entire office.

**The decision is delayed.** If the CSW needs revision after submission, you lose the decision window. For legislative briefers, this may mean missing the committee deliberation date entirely.

**Real BARMM example of submitting poorly:**

A budget briefer was transmitted to the Committee on Finance and Budget for an agency's FY 2026 review. The briefer was 35 pages long but lacked an executive summary. The budget comparison section (BB06) contained accurate data but no variance analysis — just raw numbers. The recommendation section (BB12) said "further study is recommended" without specifying what to study or what action the committee should take.

The Committee Chairperson returned the briefer with a single note: "Finished work only, please."

The analyst had spent three weeks on research (Step 3). The problem was not research quality. It was output quality. The data was there. The analysis was there. The packaging was not.

---

**Chapter Summary**

- Structure your CSW output using the appropriate template. Follow the section order.
- Write the Executive Summary last. One page. Four elements: problem, recommendation, benefits, action requested.
- Present research findings with visual aids and citations. Lead with the most important finding.
- Show all options evaluated, not just the recommended one. Be fair and objective.
- State your recommendation directly. No hedging.
- Include a specific, actionable implementation plan with timeline, responsibilities, and resources.
- End with a clear call to action. Tell the decision-maker exactly what to approve.
- Self-review, peer-review, and supervisor clearance are mandatory quality gates.
- Submit with a Transmittal Memorandum signed by your supervisor.
- Skipping this step means wasted analysis, delayed decisions, and damaged credibility.
