# Chapter 10: How Work Gets Done: Complete Staff Work

Every assignment you receive in government follows a process. That process has a name: **Complete Staff Work (CSW)**. Learn it now, because it will shape every document you write, every recommendation you make, and every problem you solve for the rest of your government career.

---

## What Complete Staff Work Means

**Complete Staff Work** is a doctrine, not just a concept.[^1] It means that when you give your supervisor a document, that document should be so thorough, so well-researched, and so clearly written that your supervisor only needs to do one thing: **approve or disapprove**.

Your supervisor should not need to:
- Ask you for missing information
- Rewrite your sentences
- Check your legal citations
- Call you back to explain what you meant
- Fix your formatting

If your supervisor has to do any of those things, your staff work is **not complete**.

Think of it this way. A doctor does not hand a patient a half-finished diagnosis and say, "Can you look up the rest?" A mechanic does not return a car with three wheels attached and say, "I ran out of time." **Your CSW output is your professional product.** It should be finished, accurate, and ready for decision.

> **The CSW Standard**: The decision-maker should only need to approve or disapprove. Everything else is your job.

---

## The ADDRESS IT Process

In the Bangsamoro government, CSW follows a 7-step process called **ADDRESS IT**.[^2] This is the methodology used across BARMM — in the Parliament, in ministries, in offices, and in local government units.

**ADDRESS IT** is a mnemonic. Each letter (or pair of letters) represents one step:

| Step | Letter(s) | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Step 1** | **A** | **Analyze** the Context[^3] |
| **Step 2** | **DD** | **Define** and **Deliberate** the Problem or Need[^4] |
| **Step 3** | **R** | **Research** Needed Information[^5] |
| **Step 4** | **E** | **Explore** Options[^6] |
| **Step 5** | **SS** | **Submit** Proposal or Report[^7] |
| **Step 6** | (SS cont.) | **Sustain** Efforts to Implement the Solution[^8] |
| **Step 7** | **IT** | **Iterate** and Improve the Solution[^9] |

The process is **cyclical**.[^20] Step 7 can loop back to Step 1 when circumstances change or new problems emerge. Government work is never truly "done" — policies evolve, communities grow, and new challenges appear.

Let us walk through each step as if you have just received your first assignment.

---

## Step 1: Analyze the Context

Your supervisor says: *"I need a report on why our feeding program in Basilan is behind schedule."*

Before you start writing, **stop and analyze**. This step lays the foundation for everything that follows.

### What to do in Step 1

**Understand the assignment.** What exactly does your supervisor want? A report? A recommendation? A memo for the Minister? Clarify ambiguities now — not after you have written 10 pages in the wrong direction.

**Assess your office's function.** How does this assignment connect to your office's mandate under **BAA 13**[^10] (the Bangsamoro Administrative Code)? If you work for the Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD), the feeding program falls squarely within your mandate. If you work for the Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management (MFBM), your angle might be the budget utilization rate instead.

**Identify stakeholders.** Who is affected by this feeding program? The beneficiaries (children and families in Basilan), the implementing LGUs, the municipal social workers, the provincial government, development partners who co-funded the program. List them. You will need their perspectives later.

**Examine internal and external factors.** Internally: does your office have enough staff to monitor the program? Is the budget released on time? Externally: are there access issues in Basilan's island municipalities? Are there security concerns? Is it typhoon season?

**Review relevant laws and policies.** What BAA or memorandum order governs the feeding program? What national laws apply (RA 11037,[^11] the Masustansyang Pagkain para sa Batang Pilipino Act)? What are the BDP targets for child nutrition (Goal 4: Inclusive, Responsive, and Quality Social Services)?[^12]

**Gather preliminary data.** Pull whatever information is readily available — last quarter's monitoring report, the program's approved work plan, disbursement records.

### Why this step matters

Skipping contextual analysis is the most common mistake new staff make. You rush to write the report without understanding the full picture. The result: a document that addresses symptoms instead of causes, ignores key stakeholders, or recommends actions outside your office's mandate.

> **Fresh graduate tip**: When your supervisor gives you an assignment, do not immediately start typing. Spend the first 30 minutes reading, asking questions, and mapping the landscape. Those 30 minutes will save you hours of rework.

---

## Step 2: Define and Deliberate the Problem or Need

Now that you understand the context, **define the problem clearly**. This sounds simple. It is not.

### What to do in Step 2

**Articulate the problem.** Write it in one sentence. Not "The feeding program has issues" but "The Basilan feeding program has reached only 38% of its FY 2026 target beneficiaries as of March 2026, primarily due to delayed fund release and insufficient municipal social workers in 3 island municipalities."

**Identify root causes.** Use the **"5 Whys" method**[^21]:
1. Why is the program behind? *Because only 38% of beneficiaries have been served.*
2. Why only 38%? *Because 3 island municipalities have not started implementation.*
3. Why haven't they started? *Because the funds were released 2 months late.*
4. Why were funds late? *Because the municipality did not submit liquidation reports for the previous tranche.*
5. Why didn't they submit? *Because they have no trained staff to prepare the reports.*

The root cause is not "the program is slow." The root cause is **capacity and compliance gaps at the municipal level**.

**Engage stakeholders.** Talk to the municipal social workers, the provincial coordinator, the budget officer. Their perspectives will reveal things that data alone cannot show. In the Bangsamoro context, this deliberation reflects the Islamic principle of **shura** — collective consultation before decision-making.[^13]

**Establish scope.** Define what your CSW covers and what it does not. You are analyzing the Basilan feeding program delays, not redesigning the entire national feeding strategy.

**Set SMART objectives.**[^22] Your CSW should aim for something specific: *"Identify the root causes of implementation delays in 3 Basilan municipalities and recommend corrective actions achievable within Q3 FY 2026."*

### Why this step matters

A poorly defined problem leads to a poorly designed solution. If you define the problem as "the program is slow," your recommendation might be "speed it up" — which tells your supervisor nothing useful. If you define it as a capacity gap, your recommendation can be specific: deploy a technical assistance team, conduct liquidation report training, or adjust the disbursement schedule.

---

## Step 3: Research Needed Information

With your problem defined, **gather the evidence**. This step separates CSW from guesswork.

### What to do in Step 3

**Develop a research plan.** What information do you need? Where will you find it? How long will it take? A simple research plan prevents you from drowning in data or running out of time.

**Identify sources.** For a BARMM staff member, your sources include:
- **Government data**: MSSD monitoring reports, MFBM disbursement records, PSA statistics
- **Legal references**: BAAs, national laws, memorandum circulars,[^14] executive orders
- **BDP targets**: The relevant chapter of the 2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028[^15]
- **Stakeholder input**: Interviews with municipal staff, field reports from social workers
- **Best practices**: How other BARMM provinces or national programs handle similar challenges

**Evaluate source credibility.**[^23] Not all data is equal. An official MSSD report is more reliable than an unverified social media post. A PSA-validated statistic is more credible than an estimate from a single barangay captain.

**Synthesize findings.** Do not just collect data — analyze it. Look for patterns, contradictions, and gaps. If three municipalities report the same problem (no trained staff), that is a systemic issue, not an isolated case.

**Document everything.** Keep a record of every source you consulted, every person you interviewed, every document you reviewed. Your supervisor may ask, "Where did you get this number?" You should be able to answer immediately.

### Why this step matters

CSW built on insufficient or unreliable data will fail. Your supervisor and the decision-maker above them will lose trust in your work if your facts are wrong. In government, **credibility is your most valuable asset**. Once lost, it takes years to rebuild.

---

## Step 4: Explore Options

You have the context, the problem definition, and the evidence. Now **generate solutions** — more than one.

### What to do in Step 4

**Generate multiple options.**[^24] Never present only one solution. Decision-makers need choices. For the Basilan feeding program, your options might be:

| Option | Description | Cost | Timeline | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Option A** | Deploy MSSD technical assistance team to 3 municipalities for 2 weeks | PHP 150,000 (travel, per diem) | 1 month | Low — uses existing staff |
| **Option B** | Conduct online liquidation training for all Basilan municipal social workers | PHP 50,000 (platform, materials) | 2 weeks | Medium — internet connectivity issues |
| **Option C** | Transfer implementation to provincial government with MSSD oversight | PHP 0 additional | 2 months (transition) | High — capacity at provincial level unverified |

**Evaluate each option** against clear criteria:
- **Feasibility**: Can you actually do this with available resources?
- **Effectiveness**: Will it solve the root cause?
- **Efficiency**: Does it use resources wisely?
- **Sustainability**: Will the fix last, or will the problem recur?
- **Alignment**: Does it support BDP goals and your office mandate?[^25]

**Conduct cost-benefit analysis.**[^26] Option B costs the least but may not work due to connectivity. Option A costs more but directly addresses the capacity gap. Option C costs nothing upfront but risks further delays.

**Recommend the best option.** Based on your analysis, recommend one option and explain why. Provide your reasoning clearly so the decision-maker can follow your logic.

### Why this step matters

Presenting a single option forces the decision-maker into a yes-or-no choice. Presenting multiple options with analysis demonstrates **thoroughness** and **respect for the decision-maker's authority**. It also protects you — if the decision-maker disagrees with your top recommendation, they can choose an alternative without sending you back to start over.

---

## Step 5: Submit the Proposal or Report

This is where your staff work becomes a **physical document** that moves through the approval chain.

### What to do in Step 5

**Structure your document.**[^27] A typical CSW output follows this structure:
1. **Executive Summary** — 1-2 paragraphs capturing the problem, recommendation, and expected benefit
2. **Background and Context** — why this issue matters
3. **Problem Statement** — what you found in Step 2
4. **Research Findings** — what the evidence shows (Step 3)
5. **Options Explored** — the alternatives and your analysis (Step 4)
6. **Recommended Solution** — your recommendation with supporting rationale
7. **Implementation Plan** — specific actions, timelines, responsible persons, resources needed
8. **Conclusion and Call to Action** — what you need the decision-maker to do

**Write clearly.**[^28] Government writing is direct, structured, and action-oriented. Short sentences. Numbered paragraphs. Bold key terms. No jargon without definition. (See Chapter 13 for detailed writing guidance.)

**Review and proofread.**[^29] Check every fact, every citation, every number. Run the quality checklist: Is it accurate? Is it complete? Is it properly formatted? Are all attachments included?

**Get supervisor clearance.** Before your document reaches the decision-maker, your immediate supervisor must review and endorse it. This is not optional — it is part of the CSW process.[^16] Your supervisor's endorsement signals: *"I have reviewed this work and I am satisfied with its quality."*

**Submit through proper channels.** Attach a **transmittal memorandum**[^17] (see Chapter 13) and route it through the established approval chain. Do not skip levels. Do not hand it directly to the Minister if it should go through your Division Chief first.

### The transmittal chain

Your CSW follows this path:

```
Your desk → Supervisor review → Supervisor endorsement →
Division Chief (if applicable) → Director → Decision-maker
```

At each level, the reviewer may:
- **Approve and forward** — your work passes
- **Return with comments** — revise and resubmit
- **Reject** — start over (rare if you followed Steps 1-4 properly)

### Why this step matters

Even excellent analysis fails if it is poorly written, badly formatted, or submitted through the wrong channel. The decision-maker may receive dozens of documents each day. Yours must be **clear enough to read in 10 minutes** and **complete enough to act on immediately**.

---

## Step 6: Sustain Efforts to Implement the Solution

Your CSW does not end when the decision-maker approves it. Now you **implement**.

### What to do in Step 6

**Mobilize resources.** If your recommendation was approved, coordinate with the relevant offices to secure the budget, personnel, and logistics needed.

**Develop action plans.**[^30] Break the approved solution into specific tasks with deadlines and responsible persons. Share the action plan with everyone involved.

**Monitor progress.** Track whether implementation is on schedule. Use simple tools — a checklist, a shared spreadsheet, or a tracking memo. Report progress to your supervisor regularly.

**Engage stakeholders.** Keep everyone informed — the implementing units, the beneficiaries, the partner offices. In the Bangsamoro context, this includes **community engagement** in the local language and through culturally appropriate channels.

**Provide support.** If the implementing team encounters obstacles, help remove them. CSW is not "submit and forget." Your ownership continues until the solution is working.

### Why this step matters

Approved solutions that are never implemented are the most common waste in government. The filing cabinets of every ministry contain brilliant recommendations that never became action. **Sustained follow-through is what separates effective staff from paper-pushers.**

---

## Step 7: Iterate and Improve the Solution

Government work is never finished. After implementation, you **evaluate and improve**.

### What to do in Step 7

**Monitor and evaluate.**[^31] Is the solution working? Are the KPIs being met? In the feeding program example: after deploying the technical assistance team, did the 3 municipalities start implementing? Did beneficiary numbers increase?

**Collect feedback.** Ask the municipal social workers: Was the training useful? What challenges remain? Ask the beneficiaries: Are you receiving the feeding supplies on time?

**Identify improvements.** Based on the data and feedback, identify what can be done better. Maybe the training needs a follow-up session. Maybe the disbursement schedule needs further adjustment.

**Document lessons learned.**[^32] Write a brief after-action review. What worked? What did not? What would you do differently? This becomes institutional knowledge — the next person who handles a similar issue will benefit from your experience.

**Restart if necessary.** If monitoring reveals that the problem has fundamentally changed — perhaps a new policy directive changes the feeding program's design — go back to Step 1 and start a new CSW cycle.

### Why this step matters

Without iteration, the same problems recur. Lessons from one project never inform the next. The organization fails to learn and improve. **The best government workers are not those who produce one perfect document. They are those who continuously improve their work over time.**

## CSW Types You Will Encounter

Not all CSW looks the same. Here are the most common types you will produce in BARMM:[^33]

| CSW Type | Purpose | Where used | Key feature |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Legislative Briefer**[^18] | Prepares a legislator for a committee hearing or plenary session | Parliament | Comprehensive bill analysis with recommendation |
| **Budget Briefer** | Analyzes a budget proposal for decision-makers | MFBM, Parliament Committee on Appropriations | Financial data, fiscal impact, BDP alignment |
| **Policy Recommendation** | Proposes a policy change with evidence and analysis | Any MOA | Options analysis, cost-benefit, implementation plan |
| **Project Proposal** | Requests approval and funding for a new project | Any MOA | Work plan, budget, timeline, expected outcomes |
| **Legal Opinion** | Provides legal analysis on a question or issue | Legal divisions, Parliament | Legal basis, jurisprudence, interpretation |
| **General CSW** | Covers any other staff work product (reports, assessments, reviews) | Any office | Depends on the assignment |

As a new staff member, you will most likely start with **general CSW** — responding to specific assignments from your supervisor. As you gain experience, you may be asked to produce briefers, policy recommendations, or project proposals.

## What "Completed" Actually Means

Here is a checklist to verify that your staff work is truly complete before you submit it:[^34]

| Check | Question | Yes/No |
|---|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | Are all facts, figures, and citations verified? | |
| **Completeness** | Does the document cover everything the assignment requires? | |
| **Clarity** | Can the decision-maker understand it without calling you for explanation? | |
| **Structure** | Does it follow the standard format for this type of CSW? | |
| **Legal basis** | Are relevant laws, BAAs, and policies cited correctly? | |
| **BDP alignment** | Does the recommendation connect to a BDP goal or priority? | |
| **Attachments** | Are all supporting documents included and properly labeled? | |
| **Formatting** | Is it properly formatted (margins, fonts, page numbers, headers)? | |
| **Proofreading** | Is it free of spelling, grammar, and typographical errors? | |
| **Transmittal** | Does it have a transmittal memorandum with the correct action phrase? | |
| **Supervisor clearance** | Has your supervisor reviewed and endorsed it? | |

If you answered "No" to any of these, your work is not yet complete. Go back and fix it before submitting.

## ADDRESS IT in Practice: A Quick Example

Here is how ADDRESS IT applies to a common first assignment — preparing a memorandum requesting approval of a training schedule.[^35]

| Step | What you do | Time |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Analyze** | Read the training policy, check the budget, identify participants, confirm venue availability | 1 hour |
| **2. Define** | "The office needs to conduct mandatory CSW training for 25 new staff by Q2 FY 2026 per OP MC 72-2019"[^19] | 30 minutes |
| **3. Research** | Check available trainers, review past training schedules, confirm per diem rates, verify CSC requirements | 2 hours |
| **4. Explore** | Option A: 3-day face-to-face in Cotabato City. Option B: 5-day blended (online + 2-day face-to-face). Option C: Self-paced online only. | 1 hour |
| **5. Submit** | Write the memo with your recommendation (Option B — balances cost and effectiveness), attach the proposed schedule, budget estimate, and participant list. Route through your supervisor. | 2 hours |
| **6. Sustain** | After approval, coordinate logistics, send invitations, prepare materials, monitor attendance | Ongoing |
| **7. Iterate** | After the training, collect feedback, assess learning outcomes, adjust the next cycle | Post-training |

Total time for Steps 1-5: approximately one working day. That is reasonable for a routine CSW assignment. Complex assignments (legislative briefers, policy recommendations) may take days or weeks.

Keep this card at your desk until ADDRESS IT becomes second nature:[^36]

| **A** | Analyze Context | *What is the situation? Who is involved? What laws apply?* |
| **DD** | Define and Deliberate | *What exactly is the problem? What are the root causes?* |
| **R** | Research | *What does the evidence say? What have others done?* |
| **E** | Explore Options | *What are the possible solutions? Which is best and why?* |
| **SS** | Submit Proposal or Report | *Is my document complete and decision-ready?* |
| **(SS)** | Sustain Implementation | *Is implementation on track? Are stakeholders engaged?* |
| **IT** | Iterate and Improve | *Is the solution working? What can be done better?* |

## Key Takeaways

- **CSW is a doctrine.** Your output should be so complete that the decision-maker only needs to approve or disapprove.
- **ADDRESS IT is your process.**[^37] Seven steps, cyclical, applicable to every assignment from a simple memo to a complex policy recommendation.
- **Start with analysis, not writing.** The most common mistake is jumping to Step 5 (Submit) without doing Steps 1-4.
- **Document everything.** Your research, your sources, your reasoning. Government decisions must be traceable and defensible.
- **Get supervisor clearance.** No CSW output goes to the decision-maker without passing through your supervisor first.
- **Follow through.** Submission is not the end. Implementation (Step 6) and improvement (Step 7) are where your work creates real impact.

Your degree got you through the door. Your ability to produce complete staff work will determine how far you go.
