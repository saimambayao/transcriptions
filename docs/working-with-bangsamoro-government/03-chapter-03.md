# How the Government Is Organized

BAA 13 is the blueprint. Learn it the way an architect learns a floor plan — not to memorize every room, but to know where everything is, how the spaces connect, and where you fit.

**Bangsamoro Autonomy Act No. 13** — the Bangsamoro Administrative Code — defines the structure, powers, and functions of every ministry, office, and agency in the Bangsamoro government.[^1] Before you apply for any position, you need to know what the government looks like from the inside. This chapter gives you that map.

---

## 3.1 The Three Branches at a Glance

The Bangsamoro government operates under a **parliamentary system** — the only one in the Philippines.[^2] Unlike the national government's separation of executive and legislative branches, the Bangsamoro system integrates them. Here is the high-level structure:

| Branch | Head | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Parliament (BTA)** | Speaker | Enacts laws (BAAs), approves budgets, conducts oversight |
| **Executive (Cabinet)** | Chief Minister | Implements laws, manages ministries, delivers services |
| **Wali** | Wali | Ceremonial head, represents moral and cultural authority |

The **Chief Minister** is both head of government and a Member of Parliament. The **Cabinet ministers** are appointed by the Chief Minister with Parliament's consent.[^3] This means the executive is drawn from and accountable to the legislature — a fundamental difference from the presidential system used nationally and in LGUs.

What does this mean for you? If you join the Parliament, your work supports lawmaking and oversight. If you join a ministry, your work implements the laws that Parliament enacts. The two tracks are distinct but tightly connected. A legislative briefer you draft in Parliament may shape a program that a ministry implements. A field report you write in a ministry may prompt a parliamentary inquiry.

## 3.2 The Parliament

### Composition

The BOL (Article VII, Section 6) provides for **80 Members of Parliament**.[^4] During the transition, MPs are appointed by the President.[^5] Currently, **77 MPs are sworn in** (as of March 2025).[^6]

The Parliament is led by:
- **The Speaker** — presiding officer who directs parliamentary proceedings
- **The Deputy Speaker(s)** — assist the Speaker
- **The Secretary General** — the chief administrative officer of the Parliament, who heads the Secretariat[^7]

### Committees

Parliament work is organized through **committees**. Each committee has jurisdiction over specific subject areas — education, health, agriculture, finance, local government, and so on. Committees are where most legislative work happens: hearings, bill deliberations, report preparation.

There are two types:
- **Statutory (Standing) Committees** — permanent committees organized by subject area (e.g., Committee on Agriculture, Committee on Education, Committee on Finance)
- **Parliamentary (Procedural) Committees** — handle internal parliament operations (e.g., Rules, Accounts, Ethics)

### The Secretariat

The **Parliament Secretariat** is the administrative and support structure that enables MPs to do their work. It is headed by the Secretary General and staffed by professional civil servants. The Secretariat includes several key offices:

**If you want to work in Parliament, these are the offices you would join:**

- **Legislative Services** — supports the legislative process, including bill drafting, committee support, and legislative research
- **Parliamentary Operations** — manages plenary sessions, committee hearings, scheduling, and records
- **Administrative Services** — handles human resources, finance, procurement, and general administration for the Parliament

### Parliamentary Staff Roles

Three staff roles appear throughout Parliament work. Understanding them helps you decide which one matches your skills:

**Legislative Staff Officer (LSO)** — The analytical backbone. LSOs research issues, draft bills, prepare legislative briefers, write committee reports, and provide policy analysis. If you enjoy reading, writing, and research, this is your role. Entry level: SG 11.

**Political Affairs Officer (PAO)** — The constituency link. PAOs handle constituent concerns, coordinate with LGUs and communities, and manage the MP's field activities. If you enjoy community engagement and stakeholder relations, this is your role.

**Public Information and Media Affairs Officer (PIMAO)** — The communications specialist. PIMAOs manage the MP's public communications, social media, press releases, and media relations. If you have skills in communications, journalism, or digital media, this is your role.

Each MP typically has a small team that includes one or more of each role. The committee itself may also have dedicated LSOs assigned by the Secretariat.

## 3.3 The Chief Minister and Cabinet

### The Chief Minister

The **Chief Minister** is the head of government. Under the BOL (Article VII, Section 31), the Chief Minister is elected by the Parliament from among its members by a majority vote.[^8] The Chief Minister exercises executive power, appoints Cabinet ministers, and directs the overall operations of the Bangsamoro government.[^9]

### The Office of the Chief Minister (OCM)

The OCM is the executive staff and administrative office of the Chief Minister. It is separate from the line ministries. The OCM houses:

**Executive Offices:**
- Office of the Senior Minister
- Office of the Chief of Staff
- Cabinet Secretariat
- Bangsamoro Attorney General's Office (BAGO)

**Bangsamoro Management Support System (BMSS):**
- Technical Management Service (TMS)
- Administrative Management Service (AMS)
- Finance Management Service (FMS)
- Intelligence and Security Service (ISS)
- Procurement Service (PS)
- Clinic
- Bangsamoro Satellite Coordinating Offices
- Bangsamoro Liaison Office in Metro Manila
- Bangsamoro Library and Archives

**Three Councils under the OCM:**
1. Bangsamoro Disaster Risk Reduction and Management Council (DRRMC)
2. Bangsamoro Economic and Development Council (BEDC)
3. Bangsamoro Regional Peace and Order Council (BRPOC)

If you want to work in strategic coordination, policy advice, or executive operations, the OCM offices are where that work happens. Positions here tend to be closer to decision-making but also more politically sensitive.

## 3.4 The 15 Line Ministries

Line ministries are where most government employees work. Each ministry has a specific mandate defined in BAA 13,[^10] and many have enabling BAAs that flesh out their powers and functions. Here is the complete list:

### 1. Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries, and Agrarian Reform (MAFAR)

**Mandate:** Agricultural development, fisheries management, and agrarian reform across BARMM.

**Why it matters:** BARMM has the largest agricultural land area in the Philippines. It is the top producer of seaweed (48.42% national share) and cassava (41.70%). There are 110,513 hectares of undistributed agricultural land under CARP (per MAFAR/DAR data as cited in BDP 2023-2028).[^11] MAFAR needs agricultural technicians, agrarian reform officers, fisheries specialists, and program managers.

### 2. Ministry of Basic, Higher, and Technical Education (MBHTE)

**Mandate:** Comprehensive education system — from basic education through higher education and technical-vocational training.

**Enabling BAA:** BAA 18 (Bangsamoro Education Code)[^12]

**Why it matters:** BARMM has the lowest literacy rates, highest dropout rates, and lowest completion rates in the Philippines.[^13] MBHTE needs teachers, curriculum developers, education planners, and administrative staff at every level.

### 3. Ministry of Environment, Natural Resources, and Energy (MENRE)

**Mandate:** Environmental protection, natural resource management, and energy development.

**Why it matters:** BARMM's territory includes Lake Lanao (the second largest lake in the Philippines), the Mindanao River Basin, the Sulu-Celebes Sea marine ecosystem, and multiple watersheds. Environmental management directly affects communities dependent on fishing, farming, and water resources.

### 4. Ministry of Finance, and Budget and Management (MFBM)

**Mandate:** Fiscal management — revenue collection, budget preparation, expenditure management, and financial accountability.

**Enabling BAA:** BAA 84 (Bangsamoro Budget System Act)[^14]

**Why it matters:** MFBM manages the annual block grant (5% of net national internal revenue — exceeding PHP 80 billion).[^15] It needs budget analysts, accountants, procurement officers, and financial management specialists. Every peso the government spends passes through MFBM's systems.

### 5. Ministry of Health (MOH)

**Mandate:** Health service delivery across the region.

**Why it matters:** Maternal mortality above the national average. Highest stunting, wasting, and underweight rates in the Philippines.[^16] Insufficient health professionals at all levels — from provincial hospitals to barangay health stations. MOH needs nurses, midwives, doctors, nutritionists, and health program officers.

### 6. Ministry of Human Settlements and Development (MHSD)

**Mandate:** Housing, shelter programs, and human settlements planning.

**Why it matters:** Displacement from conflict (especially the 2017 Marawi siege), natural disasters, and the ongoing need for social housing in urban and rural areas. MHSD works on resettlement, housing programs, and community development.

### 7. Ministry of Indigenous Peoples' Affairs (MIPA)

**Mandate:** Protection and promotion of the rights, culture, and welfare of indigenous peoples within BARMM.

**Enabling BAA:** BAA 64[^17]

**Why it matters:** BARMM is home to indigenous peoples including the Teduray, Lambangian, and Dulangan Manobo, among others.[^18] MIPA ensures that development programs respect indigenous rights, ancestral domains, and cultural traditions.

### 8. Ministry of the Interior and Local Government (MILG)

**Mandate:** Coordination with and supervision of local government units (LGUs) — provinces, cities, municipalities, and barangays.

**Enabling BAA:** BAA 49 (Bangsamoro Local Governance Code)[^19]

**Why it matters:** MILG oversees 116 municipalities and 2,590 barangays (BDP 2023-2028 figures; exclude adjustments from Sulu's exclusion from BARMM per SC ruling).[^20] It assesses LGU functionality, coordinates disaster response through BRDRRMO (an attached agency), and supports local governance capacity building. If you want to work at the intersection of regional and local government, MILG is where that happens.

### 9. Ministry of Labor and Employment (MOLE)

**Mandate:** Employment promotion, labor standards, and workers' welfare.

**Enabling BAA:** BAA 82 (Bangsamoro Labor Code)[^21]

**Why it matters:** With underemployment at 9% and a growing working-age population, MOLE manages employment programs, labor dispute resolution, and overseas worker protection (coordinated with BAA 19 on overseas Bangsamoro workers).[^22]

### 10. Ministry of Public Order and Safety (MPOS)

**Mandate:** Public order, law enforcement coordination, and community safety.

**Why it matters:** BARMM's peace and security situation directly affects development. MPOS coordinates with national law enforcement agencies (PNP, NAPOLCOM) and supports the normalization process under the CAB.

### 11. Ministry of Public Works (MPW)

**Mandate:** Infrastructure development — roads, bridges, buildings, water systems, and public facilities.

**Why it matters:** Infrastructure backlog across all five provinces and Cotabato City. Island provinces (Basilan, Tawi-Tawi) face connectivity challenges. MPW needs engineers, project managers, construction inspectors, and infrastructure planners.

### 12. Ministry of Science and Technology (MOST)

**Mandate:** Science, technology, and innovation for regional development.

**Why it matters:** Technology adoption, research and development, and digital infrastructure are growing priorities. MOST supports innovation in agriculture, health, education, and governance.

### 13. Ministry of Social Services and Development (MSSD)

**Mandate:** Social welfare programs, social protection, and community development.

**Why it matters:** MSSD runs livelihood programs, humanitarian assistance, and social protection services for the most vulnerable communities. It coordinates with DSWD on national social protection programs being devolved to BARMM.

### 14. Ministry of Trade, Investments, and Tourism (MTIT)

**Mandate:** Trade promotion, investment facilitation, and tourism development.

**Attached agencies:** Bangsamoro Economic Zone Authority (BEZA), Bangsamoro Halal Board

**Why it matters:** BARMM's economic potential — in agriculture, halal industry, marine resources, and cultural tourism — remains largely untapped. MTIT and its attached agencies drive economic development strategy.

### 15. Ministry of Transportation and Communications (MOTC)

**Mandate:** Transportation networks and communications infrastructure.

**Why it matters:** Connectivity between mainland and island provinces is a persistent challenge. Flight routes, seaport development, road networks, and digital connectivity all fall under MOTC.

## 3.5 Attached Agencies and Offices Under the OCM

Beyond the 15 line ministries, **10 agencies** are attached to the Office of the Chief Minister.[^23] These agencies have specialized mandates:

| Agency | Acronym | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority | BPDA | Development planning, M&E, BDP coordination |
| Bangsamoro Board of Investments | BBOI | Investment promotion and facilitation |
| Bangsamoro Information Office | BIO | Government communications and public information |
| Bangsamoro ICT Office | BICTO | Information and communications technology |
| Office for Settler Communities | OSC | Welfare of settler communities in BARMM |
| Office for Other Bangsamoro Communities | OOBC | Bangsamoro communities outside BARMM territory |
| Bangsamoro Pilgrimage Authority | BPA | Hajj and Umrah coordination |
| Development Academy of the Bangsamoro | DAB | Capacity building and training |
| Bangsamoro Darul-Ifta' | BDI | Islamic jurisprudence and religious guidance |
| Cooperatives and Social Enterprise Authority | CSEA | Cooperative development and social enterprises |

**BPDA** deserves special mention. It coordinates the entire Bangsamoro Development Plan — the government's roadmap. If you are interested in strategic planning, data analysis, monitoring and evaluation, or development research, BPDA is where that work concentrates.

## 3.6 Commissions and Independent Bodies

### Bangsamoro Commissions (Attached to OCM)

Four commissions address specific sectors. Each was created by a specific BAA:[^24]

| Commission | Acronym | Enabling BAA | Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bangsamoro Women Commission | BWC | BAA 8[^25] | Gender equality, women's empowerment |
| Bangsamoro Youth Commission | BYC | BAA 10[^26] | Youth development, participation |
| Bangsamoro Sports Commission | BSC | BAA 12[^27] | Sports development |
| Bangsamoro Commission for the Preservation of Cultural Heritage | BCPCH | BAA 13, Title XVI[^28] | Cultural heritage preservation |

### Independent Bodies

Two bodies operate independently from the executive:

| Body | Acronym | Enabling BAA | Function |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bangsamoro Human Rights Commission | BHRC | BAA 4[^29] | Human rights monitoring and protection |
| Bangsamoro Internal Auditing Body | BIAB | BAA 13, Title XVII[^30] | Internal audit and accountability |

If you are passionate about human rights, gender, youth, sports, or cultural heritage, these commissions and bodies offer focused career paths. They are smaller than ministries, which means fewer positions but also more direct involvement in your area of interest.

## 3.7 Local Government Units

BARMM's territory is organized into local government units at four levels:

| Level | Count | Key Officials |
|---|---|---|
| **Provinces** | 5 (Lanao del Sur, Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, Basilan, Tawi-Tawi) | Governor, Vice-Governor, Provincial Board Members |
| **Cities** | Cotabato City + Lamitan City + Marawi City | Mayor, Vice-Mayor, City Council Members |
| **Municipalities** | 116 (including 8 SGA municipalities)[^31] | Mayor, Vice-Mayor, Municipal Council Members |
| **Barangays** | 2,590[^31] | Barangay Captain, Barangay Council |

The **8 SGA municipalities** (Pahamuddin, Kadayangan, Nabalawag, Tugunan, Malidegao, Ligawasan, Kapalawan, and Old Kaabakan) were created by BAAs 41-48.[^32] These are the newest municipalities in BARMM — the 63 barangays in North Cotabato that voted to join during the 2019 plebiscite.

LGU governance in BARMM operates under **BAA 49 (Bangsamoro Local Governance Code)**,[^33] which defines the structure, powers, and responsibilities of local governments within the autonomous region. BAA 49 differs from the national Local Government Code (RA 7160) in ways specific to the Bangsamoro context — including recognition of indigenous governance structures and the integration of moral governance principles.[^34]

### LGU Functionality Assessment

MILG assesses all LGUs based on **seven elements of functionality**:

1. Presence of government center with facilities
2. Working elected and appointed mandatory officials
3. Functional local special bodies
4. Functional local legislative body
5. Approved mandated plans and budget
6. Availability of basic services
7. Sound fiscal management

As of 2021, **59% of 70 assessed LGUs** were compliant — up from 50% in 2020.[^35] Island provinces register lower compliance due to geographic challenges. If you join an LGU, you will likely work on improving one or more of these elements.

## 3.8 How to Read BAA 13

BAA 13 is a long law. You do not need to read it cover to cover. But you do need to know how to navigate it. Here is the structure:

**Book I** — General provisions, principles, and the Bangsamoro government structure
**Book II** — The Parliament (composition, powers, procedures)
**Book III** — The Chief Minister and the Executive (OCM, Cabinet, executive functions)
**Book IV** — Line Ministries (mandate, powers, organizational structure for each of the 15 ministries)
**Book V** — Other offices, agencies, and commissions

For your purposes, the most useful sections are in **Book IV** — where you will find the mandate section, the powers section, and the organizational structure section for the specific ministry or office you want to join. Each ministry's entry in BAA 13 tells you:

- **Mandate** — what the ministry exists to do
- **Powers and functions** — what it is authorized to do
- **Organizational structure** — how it is internally organized (bureaus, services, divisions)

When you research a target office before applying, BAA 13 is your primary source. When an interviewer asks, "What do you know about this ministry?" your answer should draw from what BAA 13 says about its mandate and powers.

## 3.10 Intergovernmental Relations: BARMM and the National Government

Because BARMM is autonomous but still part of the Philippines, coordination with the national government is constant. The BOL (Article VI) establishes several mechanisms:[^36]

| Mechanism | Purpose | Status |
|---|---|---|
| **IGRB** (Intergovernmental Relations Body) | Resolve intergovernmental issues | Operational |
| **PCBPF** (Philippine Congress-Bangsamoro Parliament Forum) | Legislative coordination | Convened November 2022 |
| **BSDB** (Bangsamoro Sustainable Development Board) | Sustainable development coordination | Established via BAA 31[^37] |
| **Joint Normalization Committee** | Peace agreement implementation | Operational |

If you work in any ministry that coordinates with a national counterpart (MAFAR with DA, MOH with DOH, MBHTE with DepEd, MFBM with DBM), you will encounter these intergovernmental mechanisms. Understanding that BARMM has **exclusive powers, shared powers, and residual powers** (those not granted to BARMM remain with the national government) helps you navigate jurisdictional questions.[^38]

## 3.11 Actionable Tool: "Know Your Target Office" Worksheet

Before you apply to any office, fill out this worksheet. It takes 30 minutes of research and demonstrates preparation in interviews. Use BAA 13 and the ministry's website or Facebook page as your sources.

| Question | Your Research |
|---|---|
| **Office name and acronym:** | ___ |
| **Created/established by:** | BAA 13 and/or specific BAA (e.g., BAA 18 for MBHTE) |
| **Mandate (from BAA 13):** | One sentence — what does this office exist to do? |
| **Key powers (from BAA 13):** | List 3-5 specific powers or functions |
| **Current leadership:** | Minister/Director/Head name and title |
| **Number of staff (if available):** | From the office's website or annual report |
| **Key programs or outputs:** | What does this office produce or deliver? List 3-5 |
| **BDP alignment:** | Which of the 6 BDP goals does this office serve? |
| **12-Point Priority Agenda alignment:** | Which priority does this office support? |
| **Position I am targeting:** | Specific position title and salary grade |
| **Why I want to join this office:** | 2-3 sentences connecting your skills/motivation to the office's mandate |

**How to use this worksheet:** Bring it to your interview preparation (Chapter 9). When an interviewer asks, "Why this office?" your answer comes from the last row. When they ask, "What do you know about our mandate?" your answer comes from the third row. This worksheet is your cheat sheet — except it is not cheating, because the information is public and the effort to find it proves your seriousness.

## What Comes Next

You now know the structure — Parliament, ministries, agencies, commissions, LGUs. The next chapter covers the rules that govern your employment: **BAA 17, the Bangsamoro Civil Service Code**.[^40] That law determines how you get hired, how you get evaluated, how you get promoted, and how you can be disciplined. It is the employee handbook for the entire Bangsamoro government.
