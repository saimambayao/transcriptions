# The Bangsamoro Story: From Struggle to Self-Governance

You are joining a government that was born from a peace agreement. That history shapes everything — every institution, every policy, every relationship between the government and its people. You do not need to become a historian, but you do need to understand why the Bangsamoro government exists, what it replaced, and what it promises.

This chapter gives you that foundation. It covers the story in three acts: the centuries-long struggle for self-determination, the peace process that led to the Bangsamoro Organic Law, and the transition period you are entering.

---

## 2.1 The Bangsamoro Struggle — A Brief, Necessary History

### Before the Colonizers

Long before Spain arrived in the Philippines, the Bangsamoro homeland was organized into **independent sultanates and political systems**. These were not tribal chieftaincies. They were structured governments with written laws, diplomatic relations, and trade networks that stretched across Southeast Asia.

The major political entities included:

- **Sultanate of Sulu** — established in 1457, the oldest Islamic institution in the Philippines. It maintained trade and diplomatic ties with Borneo, China, and the Malay world.
- **Sultanate of Maguindanao** — a powerful sultanate that controlled much of Mindanao and maintained its own legal and administrative systems.
- **Sultanate of Buayan** — a sister sultanate of Maguindanao.
- **Sultanate of Kabuntalan** — a confederation in the Cotabato region.
- **Royal House of Ranao** — the *Pat a Pangampong* (Four Lake Kingdoms) of Lanao, centered around Lake Lanao.
- **Iranun Confederation** — a maritime power known for naval strength.

These sultanates governed through **Islamic legal traditions (Shari'ah)**, maintained independent political systems, conducted trade with Southeast Asian neighbors, and preserved distinct cultural identities. This is the foundation of the Bangsamoro identity: a people who governed themselves, practiced their faith, and defended their homeland for centuries before the Philippine state existed.

### Colonial Resistance: 300+ Years

Spain arrived in the Philippines in 1521 and spent over 300 years trying to conquer the Bangsamoro. They called the Muslim inhabitants "Moros" — from their experience fighting the "Moors" in the Iberian Peninsula. The term was meant as a slur. The Bangsamoro reclaimed it as a badge of resistance.

**The key fact**: The Bangsamoro were **never fully conquered by Spain**. Despite aggressive military campaigns and anti-Muslim policies, the sultanates maintained effective self-governance throughout the colonial period. This is not a minor footnote. It is the historical basis for the Bangsamoro claim to self-determination — they were independent before colonization and never surrendered that independence by force.

### The American Period and Forced Integration

When Spain ceded the Philippines to the United States in 1898 through the Treaty of Paris, Mindanao was administered separately as the **"Moro Province."** The Americans recognized that the Bangsamoro were distinct from the rest of the Philippines.

But in 1935, the Bangsamoro homeland was integrated into the Philippine Commonwealth under the new Constitution — over what the Bangsamoro Organic Law itself describes as the **"vehement resistance of the Bangsamoro."**[^1] This forced integration is the root of the modern conflict. The Bangsamoro did not consent to becoming part of the Philippine state on the terms that were imposed.

### Post-Independence: The Modern Struggle

After Philippine independence in 1946, the Bangsamoro struggle for self-determination continued through three channels:

- **Political advocacy** — legal and democratic efforts to secure autonomy
- **Armed resistance movements** — the Moro National Liberation Front (MNLF, founded 1969) and later the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF, founded 1977)
- **Peace negotiations** — decades of talks between the Philippine government and Bangsamoro fronts

These three channels — politics, arms, and negotiation — ran in parallel for decades. The government you are joining is the product of the third channel: peace negotiations that succeeded.

### What This History Means for You

You may think: "I am applying for a budget analyst position — why do I need to know about sultanates and colonial resistance?" Because the Bangsamoro identity is not academic. Your colleagues carry it. The communities you serve remember it. The government's foundational documents reference it.

When the BOL's preamble speaks of the "Bangsamoro people" as a people with a "distinct historical and cultural identity," it is drawing on these centuries. When the 12 Pillars of Moral Governance include "Sense of Patriotism" and "Cultural Understanding," they are not borrowing abstract values — they are codifying what the Bangsamoro have practiced for generations.

You do not need to become a scholar of Bangsamoro history. But you need to know enough to respect it, understand references to it, and avoid the common mistake of treating the Bangsamoro struggle as mere insurgency. It was, and remains, a movement for self-determination rooted in a pre-colonial political identity.

## 2.2 Why ARMM Failed

Before BARMM, there was ARMM — the **Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao**, created in 1989 through Republic Act 6734 (later amended by RA 9054). ARMM was later expanded through RA 9054 following the 1996 Final Peace Agreement between the Philippine government and the MNLF.

ARMM lasted 30 years (1989-2019). It failed.[^2] Understanding why it failed helps you understand why BARMM was designed differently.

**Limited autonomy.** ARMM followed a **unitary system** — the regional governor and departments were essentially extensions of the national government. The region had minimal control over its own budget, legislation, or institutional design.

**Weak fiscal powers.** ARMM did not have genuine fiscal autonomy. Its budget depended almost entirely on national government allocations, with limited ability to generate its own revenue.

**Governance problems.** ARMM's 25 regional departments were often poorly staffed, poorly managed, and plagued by patronage. Accountability mechanisms were weak.

**Institutional design.** ARMM replicated the national government's presidential structure at the regional level. The regional governor held executive power. There was no parliament. Legislative functions were limited.

The Bangsamoro Organic Law was designed to fix every one of these failures. Where ARMM had a governor, BARMM has a **Chief Minister accountable to Parliament**. Where ARMM had departments, BARMM has **15 line ministries** with clear mandates under BAA 13.[^3] Where ARMM had limited fiscal powers, BARMM has a **constitutionally guaranteed block grant** equal to 5% of net national internal revenue, plus the power to create its own revenue sources.[^4]

### Why This Matters for You

When you join BARMM, you will hear people say "we are not ARMM." This is not a throwaway line. It is a statement of institutional identity. The entire organizational design of BARMM — the parliamentary system, the civil service code, the ministry structure, the moral governance framework — was built to prevent the failures of ARMM from repeating.

Understanding ARMM's shortcomings helps you appreciate why certain BARMM systems work the way they do. The **merit and fitness principle** in BAA 17 exists because ARMM's hiring was notoriously patronage-driven.[^5] The **SPMS performance management system** exists because ARMM had weak accountability mechanisms. The **parliamentary oversight function** exists because ARMM's executive branch operated with insufficient checks.

You are joining a government that was designed as a corrective. That design is intentional and well-documented. When you encounter a process that seems cumbersome — a multi-step approval chain, a qualification standard that seems demanding, a reporting requirement that takes time — ask yourself: "What problem was this designed to solve?" The answer is usually in ARMM's history.

## 2.3 The Peace Process: From Conflict to Agreement

### The Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro (2012)

In 2012, the Government of the Philippines (GPH) and the Moro Islamic Liberation Front (MILF) signed the **Framework Agreement on the Bangsamoro (FAB)**.[^6] This agreement established the framework for a new autonomous entity to replace ARMM. It was signed under the Aquino III administration and represented a breakthrough after years of negotiations — and setbacks, including the failed Memorandum of Agreement on Ancestral Domain (MOA-AD) in 2008.

### The Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (2014)

Two years later, the GPH and MILF signed the **Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (CAB)** — the comprehensive peace agreement that became the blueprint for the BOL.[^7] The CAB has four annexes, and each one shapes the government you are joining:

**Annex on Transitional Arrangements and Modalities** — This annex created the transition mechanism from ARMM to BARMM. It established the **Bangsamoro Transition Authority (BTA)** as the interim government. The BTA you may work under today exists because of this annex.

**Annex on Revenue Generation and Wealth-Sharing** — This annex established the block grant mechanism, natural resource sharing arrangements, and the Bangsamoro government's tax powers. When you work with the budget, you are implementing provisions from this annex.

**Annex on Power-Sharing** — This annex defines which powers belong to the national government (reserved), which belong exclusively to the Bangsamoro government, and which are shared (concurrent). Understanding this annex helps you know what BARMM can and cannot do.

**Annex on Normalization** — This annex covers the decommissioning of MILF forces, socioeconomic development programs for former combatants, and transitional justice and reconciliation. This is the annex that makes BARMM different from any other government job — you are implementing a peace agreement.

### Why This History Matters for Your Work

You may wonder: why does a fresh graduate applying for a budget analyst position need to know about the CAB? Because the CAB is not ancient history. It is the **legal and political foundation** of the government you are joining.

When your office prepares its annual budget proposal, the block grant formula comes from the CAB's wealth-sharing annex. When your ministry coordinates with a national government agency, the power-sharing annex determines who has jurisdiction. When your MP files a bill, the bill's constitutional basis often traces back to the powers enumerated in the CAB and codified in the BOL.

Colleagues, supervisors, and community members will reference the peace process casually — "that's a CAB commitment," "this is part of normalization," "the BDP targets flow from the peace agreement." If you do not know what they mean, you will be lost. If you do, you will understand not just what you are doing, but why.

## 2.4 The Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act 11054)

### What the BOL Is

The **Bangsamoro Organic Law** is Republic Act 11054, signed by President Rodrigo Duterte on July 27, 2018, and ratified through a plebiscite in January-February 2019.[^8] It replaced RA 6734 (as amended by RA 9054), which created the ARMM.[^9]

The BOL is the foundational law of the Bangsamoro government. Everything — every ministry, every commission, every budget allocation, every BAA that the Parliament enacts — derives its authority from the BOL. Think of the BOL as the Bangsamoro's constitution within the Philippine Constitution.

### What the BOL Established

The BOL created an **autonomous government with real powers** — not a symbolic autonomy, but a government with its own parliament, its own civil service, its own budget, and its own legal system. Here are the key provisions, organized by article:

| Article | Subject | What You Need to Know |
|---|---|---|
| **I** | Name and Purpose | Establishes the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao |
| **II** | Bangsamoro Identity | Defines who the Bangsamoro are — a people with a distinct identity |
| **III** | Territory | Defines the geographic scope (5 provinces + Cotabato City + SGA)[^10] |
| **IV** | General Principles | Guiding principles of governance |
| **V** | Powers of Government | **55 enumerated areas** of competence (Section 2)[^11] |
| **VI** | Intergovernmental Relations | How BARMM coordinates with the national government |
| **VII** | Bangsamoro Government | The Parliament, Chief Minister, and Cabinet |
| **VIII** | Wali | Ceremonial head — moral and cultural authority |
| **IX** | Basic Rights | Rights of all people in BARMM |
| **X** | Justice System | Shari'ah courts and the justice system |
| **XI** | National Defense and Security | Defense, public order and safety, coast guard services |
| **XII** | Fiscal Autonomy | Block grant (Sec. 15-16), revenue powers, 75% tax share (Sec. 10) |
| **XIII** | Regional Economy and Patrimony | Social justice, economic system, natural resources, cooperatives |
| **XIV** | Rehabilitation and Development | Special Development Fund (Sec. 2): PHP 5B/year for 10 years |
| **XV** | Plebiscite | Ratification process for the Organic Law |
| **XVI** | BTA / Transition | The BTA and the transition period |
| **XVII** | Amendments | Process for amending the Organic Law |
| **XVIII** | Final Provisions | Separability, repealing clause, effectivity |

### The 2019 Plebiscite: The People Chose This

The BOL did not take effect automatically. It was submitted to the people through a **plebiscite** held in two stages: January 21 and February 6, 2019. The residents of the affected areas voted on whether to be part of the new Bangsamoro region.

The results:

- The former ARMM provinces voted overwhelmingly in favor
- **Cotabato City** voted to join BARMM
- **63 barangays** in North Cotabato (from Carmen, Kabacan, Midsayap, Pigkawayan, and Pikit) voted to join — these became the **Special Geographic Area (SGA)**[^12]
- **Isabela City** (Basilan) voted **not** to join — it remains outside BARMM
- **Sulu** voted against the BOL in the plebiscite — rejecting it by 54.3% — and the Supreme Court later confirmed that Sulu could not be part of BARMM because the province voted against it[^13]

This plebiscite matters because it gives the Bangsamoro government **democratic legitimacy**. The people chose this government. When you serve in BARMM, you serve a government that the people voted for.

### Territory: Five Provinces and More

The Bangsamoro territory today consists of:

- **Lanao del Sur** — capital: Marawi City. Home to Lake Lanao, the Royal House of Ranao heritage, and the ongoing Marawi rehabilitation.
- **Maguindanao del Norte** — split from the former Maguindanao province via RA 11550, ratified in 2022.[^14]
- **Maguindanao del Sur** — the other half of the former Maguindanao. Home to the Bangsamoro Government Center.
- **Basilan** — an island province south of Zamboanga. Capital: Lamitan City. Isabela City is not part of BARMM.
- **Tawi-Tawi** — the southernmost province of the Philippines. Capital: Bongao. Known as the seaweed capital.
- **Cotabato City** — the commercial hub and current administrative center. Joined BARMM by plebiscite. (Note: BAA 37 designates Parang, Maguindanao del Norte as the permanent seat of government.)
- **Special Geographic Area (SGA)** — 63 barangays in North Cotabato that voted to join. These have been organized into **8 new municipalities** (Pahamuddin, Kadayangan, Nabalawag, Tugunan, Malidegao, Ligawasan, Kapalawan, and Old Kaabakan) through BAAs 41-48.[^15]

**Sulu is not part of BARMM.** In the case of *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea* (G.R. Nos. 242255, 243246, 243693), the Supreme Court ruled on September 9, 2024 (finalized November 26, 2024) that Sulu could not be part of BARMM because the province voted against the BOL — rejecting it by 54.3%.[^16] This violated Article X, Section 18 of the Constitution, which provides that only areas voting favorably in the plebiscite can join an autonomous region.[^17] Executive Order 91 (July 30, 2025) realigned Sulu under Region IX.[^18]

The SC ruling applied the **doctrine of operative fact** — meaning that actions taken during the period when Sulu was considered part of BARMM (including BTA appointments of members from Sulu) remain valid. The ruling did not invalidate those prior acts.

## 2.5 Key BOL Provisions Every Government Worker Should Know

You do not need to memorize the entire BOL. But you need to know these provisions well enough to recognize them when they come up in your work.

### Article V, Section 2 — The 55 Enumerated Powers

This is the most operationally significant section of the BOL. Section 2 of Article V lists **55 areas of competence** — from (a) administration of justice to (ccc) water supply. These are the subjects over which the Bangsamoro government has authority. They include:

- Administrative organization
- Creation of sources of revenue
- Ancestral domain and natural resources management
- Social services
- Education
- Environmental protection
- Labor and employment
- Disaster risk reduction
- Culture and language preservation
- Cooperatives
- Public works
- Tourism

When you draft a policy, prepare a budget proposal, or write a legislative briefer, your first question should always be: **"Does BARMM have competence over this subject?"** The answer is in Article V, Section 2.

> "The Bangsamoro Government shall exercise its authority over the following matters... (n) Creation of sources of revenues."
> — Republic Act 11054, Article V, Section 2(n)

### Article XII — Fiscal Autonomy

This article gives BARMM genuine financial independence:

- **Annual Block Grant** (Art. XII, Sec. 15-16) — equal to 5% of the net national internal revenue of the BIR and Bureau of Customs[^19]
- **Special Development Fund** (Art. XIV, Sec. 2) — PHP 5 billion per year for 10 years (PHP 50 billion total)[^20]
- **Revenue generation** — power to create own revenue sources (taxes, fees, charges)
- **75% tax share** (Art. XII, Sec. 10) — of national taxes collected within BARMM[^21]

When you see budget discussions, block grant allocations, or fiscal reports, these provisions are the legal basis.

### Article VII — The Bangsamoro Government

This article establishes the parliamentary system:

- **Bangsamoro Parliament** — the legislative body, with **80 Members of Parliament** under the BOL (Art. VII, Sec. 6)[^22]
- **Chief Minister** — head of government, elected by Parliament from among its members
- **Cabinet** — ministers appointed by the Chief Minister with Parliament consent
- **Wali** — the ceremonial head, representing the moral and cultural authority of the Bangsamoro people

Currently, 77 MPs are sworn in (as of March 2025).[^23] The Parliament is led by the Speaker, supported by the Secretary General and the Secretariat.

### Article X — The Shari'ah Justice System

BARMM has its own justice system for matters governed by Islamic law:

- **Shari'ah courts** with jurisdiction over personal law (marriage, divorce, inheritance, custody), property relations among Muslims, and cases involving Muslims within BARMM
- **District and Circuit Shari'ah Courts**
- **Shari'ah High Court** as the highest Shari'ah court in BARMM

This is unique to BARMM. No other region in the Philippines has its own Shari'ah court system. If your work touches family law, personal status, inheritance, or community disputes among Muslims, you will encounter this system.

### Article IX — Basic Rights

This article guarantees rights for all people in BARMM — not just Muslims, not just Bangsamoro, but everyone. Key provisions include:

- Right to life, liberty, and property
- Freedom of religion, expression, and assembly
- Right to education (Section 16) — integrated system of quality education
- Right to culture and identity (Section 24) — preserve and promote Bangsamoro culture
- Women's rights and protection from violence
- Indigenous peoples' rights
- Right to adequate social services

Section 24 — the cultural preservation mandate — is especially significant. It is the legal basis for many programs you may work on, from cultural heritage preservation to mother tongue-based education.

### Article VI — Intergovernmental Relations

Because BARMM is autonomous but still part of the Philippines, it must coordinate with the national government. Article VI establishes mechanisms for this coordination — including the Intergovernmental Relations Body (IGRB), which resolves issues between the Bangsamoro and national governments.

**Why this matters for you:** If you work in any ministry, you will encounter situations where BARMM's authority overlaps with a national agency. MAFAR coordinates with the Department of Agriculture. MOH coordinates with the Department of Health. MBHTE coordinates with the Department of Education. Article VI defines the rules for these interactions. The power-sharing arrangement specifies which powers are **exclusive** to the Bangsamoro, which are **reserved** to the national government, and which are **concurrent** (shared).

When you are unsure whether your office has jurisdiction over a matter, Article V (enumerated powers) and Article VI (intergovernmental relations) are where you look first.

## 2.6 The Transition Period — Where You Are Entering

### What "Transition" Means

The BTA — Bangsamoro Transition Authority — is the **interim government**. It holds both legislative and executive powers. Its members are appointed by the President, not elected. The BTA was originally supposed to serve until 2022, but the transition has been extended:

- **RA 11593** (2021) — extended the BTA mandate, resetting elections to 2025[^24]
- **RA 12064** (2024) — further extended the transition to 2028[^25]

The current BTA (appointed by President Marcos under RA 12064) has been in place since March 2025. Regular elections for the Bangsamoro Parliament are scheduled for 2028.

### Three BTA Compositions

The BTA has been reconstituted three times:

1. **First BTA** (February 2019 - September 2022) — appointed by President Duterte
2. **Second BTA** (September 2022 - February 2025) — reconstituted by President Marcos under RA 11593
3. **Current BTA** (March 2025 - present) — appointed by President Marcos under RA 12064

Each reconstitution brought new members but maintained institutional continuity. Bills filed during one BTA composition were not archived — they continued to be deliberated by the succeeding composition without refiling.[^26]

### Why Transition Creates Opportunity

The transition period is both urgent and opportunity-rich for new staff:

**Urgency.** The government must build institutions, enact priority codes, deliver services, and prepare for elections — all within a defined timeline. There is more work than there are people to do it.

**Opportunity.** Because the government is still forming, entry-level staff can have outsized impact. You are not joining a 100-year-old bureaucracy with rigid hierarchies and filled positions. You are joining a government that is still deciding how to organize itself.

**Priority codes.** The BTA is tasked with enacting seven priority codes during the transition. As of 2026, five have been enacted:

| Code | BAA | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Bangsamoro Administrative Code | BAA 13 (October 2020) | Enacted |
| Bangsamoro Civil Service Code | BAA 17 (February 2021) | Enacted |
| Bangsamoro Education Code | BAA 18 (May 2021)[^27] | Enacted |
| Bangsamoro Electoral Code | BAA 35 (2023)[^28] | Enacted |
| Bangsamoro Revenue Code | — | Pending |
| Bangsamoro Investment Code | — | Pending |
| Bangsamoro Local Governance Code | BAA 49[^29] | Enacted |

Each code creates new institutions, new positions, and new functions — which means new staff are needed to implement them.

### The Shift from Appointed to Elected

The most significant event ahead is the **transition from an appointed BTA to an elected Bangsamoro Parliament**. When elections happen, the government will change from a transitional body to a permanent one. Coterminous staff (those whose appointments are tied to the appointing authority) may be affected. Permanent staff (those with plantilla positions and security of tenure) will continue.

This is why Chapter 4 covers appointment types in detail. Understanding whether your position is permanent, coterminous, or contractual determines how the transition affects you.

### Common BOL Misconceptions

As a newcomer, you may encounter these misunderstandings. Knowing the correct answers helps you avoid repeating them:

**"BARMM is a separate country."** No. BARMM is an autonomous region within the Republic of the Philippines. The BOL explicitly states that "The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region is an integral, indivisible, and inseparable part of the territory of the Republic of the Philippines."[^30]

**"BARMM can do anything — it has full autonomy."** Not quite. BARMM has 55 enumerated powers (Art. V, Sec. 2), but powers not granted to BARMM remain with the national government (Art. V, Sec. 1 — the residual clause).[^31] Defense, foreign affairs, coinage, and customs are examples of powers that remain national.

**"Sulu is part of BARMM."** No longer. The Supreme Court ruled in *Province of Sulu v. Medialdea* (finalized November 2024) that Sulu is excluded because the province voted against the BOL.[^32] Sulu was realigned under Region IX via EO 91 (July 2025).

**"The BTA is the permanent government."** No. The BTA is a transitional body. Its members are appointed by the President. The permanent Bangsamoro government will be established through elections, currently scheduled for 2028.

**"Only Muslims can work in BARMM."** Absolutely not. The BOL guarantees rights for all people in BARMM regardless of religion (Art. IX). The government employs people of all faiths. The moral governance framework is inspired by Islamic principles but applies to all staff — and respecting it does not require converting to Islam.
