# Chapter 11: Competencies of a Bangsamoro Government Worker

Your degree got you in the door. Your competencies determine how far you go.

You may not have work experience yet. That is fine. You already have many of the competencies this chapter describes — from school, from community work, from life. This chapter helps you **name** them, **develop** them, and **show** them in interviews and on the job.

---

## What Competencies Are

The Civil Service Commission (CSC) defines competencies as **knowledge, skills, and abilities (KSAs) demonstrated through observable behaviors**.[^1] That last part matters. A competency is not something you claim to have. It is something people can **see you do**.

- **Knowledge** is what you know (facts, laws, theories, procedures)
- **Skills** are what you can do (write a memo, analyze data, facilitate a meeting)
- **Abilities** are your capacity to perform (critical thinking, leadership, adaptability)

In BARMM government, competencies fall into five categories. The first four are standard across government. The fifth is unique to the Bangsamoro.

| Category | What it covers | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Thinking Skills** | Critical, creative, systems, and strategic thinking | Every CSW assignment starts with analysis |
| **Communication Skills** | Written and oral communication | Your output is judged by how well you write and speak |
| **Collaboration Skills** | Teamwork, coordination, stakeholder engagement | Government is never solo work |
| **Values-Based Competencies** | Integrity, professionalism, accountability, service | The foundation of public trust |
| **Bangsamoro Competencies** | BOL literacy, cultural competence, moral governance, CSW, peace process awareness | What makes BARMM different from every other employer[^2] |

Each category builds on the others. Strong thinking without communication produces analysis no one reads. Strong communication without values produces persuasive documents that mislead. All five categories together produce a **competent Bangsamoro government worker**.

## 11.1 Thinking Skills

Thinking skills are the foundation of all analytical government work. Every time your supervisor hands you an assignment, they are asking you to **think before you act**.

### Critical Thinking[^26]

**What it is.** Examining issues from multiple angles, questioning assumptions, distinguishing evidence from opinion, and drawing logical conclusions.

**In government.** Every CSW assignment requires you to analyze before you recommend. When your supervisor asks, "Why is the feeding program behind schedule?" — critical thinking is what keeps you from answering with the first explanation that comes to mind. Instead, you examine the data, question the obvious narrative, and identify the actual root cause.

**What it looks like at different levels:**

| Level | Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Entry** | Presents information clearly; identifies key issues; draws logical conclusions |
| **Intermediate** | Analyzes complex issues and root causes; synthesizes information from multiple sources |
| **Advanced** | Conducts comprehensive research on multifaceted issues; develops innovative solutions; builds analytical capacity in others |

**How fresh graduates demonstrate it.** Your thesis research required you to formulate a research question, review literature, analyze data, and defend your conclusions before a panel. That **is** critical thinking. So is debating in class, evaluating case studies, or questioning a professor's assumption during a lecture.

### Creative Thinking[^27]

**What it is.** Generating novel ideas, approaches, and solutions. Challenging conventional thinking. Proposing alternatives that others have not considered.

**In government.** BARMM is building systems from scratch. Many offices do not have established procedures, standard templates, or proven workflows. When your supervisor says, "We need a better way to track beneficiary complaints," creative thinking is what helps you design a solution that does not exist yet — perhaps a simple shared spreadsheet, a community feedback hotline, or a text-based reporting system for areas with limited internet.

**How fresh graduates demonstrate it.** Student organizations run on creative thinking. You planned events with no budget, designed recruitment campaigns with no marketing team, and found solutions to problems your advisers had never seen. That counts.

### Systems Thinking[^28]

**What it is.** Understanding how parts connect to form a whole. Seeing that your output affects another office's input, which affects a community's outcome.

**In government.** A budget decision at MFBM affects whether MSSD can release funds, which affects whether a municipal social worker can distribute feeding supplies, which affects whether a child in Basilan eats today. Systems thinking helps you see these connections — and avoid actions that solve one problem while creating another.

**This is not just about your office.** BARMM has 15 ministries, 13 executive offices, 5 provinces, 116 municipalities, and 2,590 barangays[^29] (BDP 2023-2028 figures; exclude adjustments from Sulu's exclusion from BARMM per SC ruling).[^30] Every policy, every program, and every budget line connects to something else. A government worker who sees only their own desk will miss the bigger picture.

**How fresh graduates demonstrate it.** If you have ever organized a multi-day event and coordinated logistics, food, speakers, transportation, and volunteers simultaneously — you practiced systems thinking. If you have ever noticed that one failed component (a delayed speaker, a broken projector) cascaded into other problems — you understood systems.

### Strategic Thinking

**What it is.** Seeing the big picture and planning toward it. Connecting your daily tasks to long-term goals. Anticipating consequences before they happen.

**In government.** Strategic thinking means connecting your memo to a BDP goal,[^3] your project proposal to the 12-point priority agenda, and your IPCR targets to your office's mandate under BAA 13.[^4] It means asking: "If we implement this recommendation, what happens next year? In five years?"

**What it looks like:**

| Level | Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Entry** | Understands organizational goals; aligns work with office mandate |
| **Intermediate** | Connects work to BDP priorities and BOL mandates; anticipates second-order effects |
| **Advanced** | Shapes organizational strategy; integrates cross-cutting concerns (gender, indigenous peoples, peace); drives long-term institutional reform |

**How fresh graduates demonstrate it.** If you have ever planned a student organization's activities for an entire semester — setting priorities, allocating limited funds, sequencing events so each built on the last — you practiced strategic thinking. If you have ever decided to take specific elective courses because they aligned with your career goal, that was strategic thinking too.

## 11.2 Communication Skills

In government, **writing is working**. Your supervisor judges your competence primarily through your written output. A staff member who writes well is trusted with bigger assignments. A staff member who writes poorly will spend their career doing data entry.

### Written Communication[^31]

**What it is.** Clear, concise, structured writing that enables the reader to understand and act.

**In government.** You will produce memos, briefers, policy recommendations, reports, transmittal memoranda, endorsements, minutes, letters, and emails. Every day. (Chapter 13 teaches you the specific formats.)

**Government writing is NOT academic writing:**

| Academic writing | Government writing |
|---|---|
| Long sentences, complex syntax | Short sentences, simple structure |
| Passive voice ("It was determined that...") | Active voice ("The committee determined...") |
| Builds to a conclusion at the end | States the conclusion FIRST, then supports it |
| Demonstrates the writer's knowledge | Enables the reader's decision |
| Hedging ("It appears that there may be...") | Direct ("There is a shortage of...") |

**Common mistakes fresh graduates make:**
- Writing 300-word paragraphs when 50 words would suffice
- Using "the undersigned" instead of "I" (the CSC has discouraged this)[^5]
- Burying the main point in paragraph four instead of paragraph one
- Using academic citations (APA format) instead of inline references
- Forgetting to state what action they want the reader to take

**How fresh graduates demonstrate it.** Your thesis, term papers, and reports show writing ability. But recognize that you will need to **unlearn** some academic writing habits. Government writing is shorter, more direct, and always action-oriented.

### Oral Communication[^32]

**What it is.** Presenting ideas clearly, adapting to different audiences, listening actively, and speaking with appropriate confidence.

**In government.** You will brief your supervisor, present to committees, facilitate stakeholder consultations, speak in meetings, and engage communities. The contexts range from formal (committee hearings, budget defense) to informal (supervisor check-ins, team discussions) to public (community consultations, press interactions).

**The multilingual dimension.** BARMM operates in Filipino, English, and Arabic.[^6] Community engagement often requires regional languages — Meranaw, Maguindanaon, Tausug, Sama, Yakan, and others. If you speak any of these, it is a professional asset. If you do not, learn basic greetings and respectful phrases in the language of your assigned community.

**How fresh graduates demonstrate it.** Class presentations, thesis defense, debate competitions, student journalism, peer tutoring, event hosting — all of these developed your oral communication skills. In your interview, you demonstrate this competency in real time.

## 11.3 Collaboration and Teamwork Skills

Government is never solo work. Every output involves multiple people, offices, and stakeholders. The ability to work effectively with others is not optional — it is essential.

### Working in Teams[^33]

**What it is.** Contributing to group goals, respecting diverse viewpoints, sharing information willingly, and supporting colleagues.

**In government.** Your CSW output will pass through multiple reviewers. Your project will involve colleagues from different divisions. Your program will require coordination with other ministries. You cannot succeed alone.

**What it looks like:**

| Level | Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Entry** | Works cooperatively with colleagues; shares information willingly; respects diverse viewpoints |
| **Intermediate** | Coordinates across offices and ministries; resolves team conflicts; facilitates inter-agency cooperation |
| **Advanced** | Builds and leads cross-functional teams; establishes inter-entity partnerships; creates collaborative frameworks |

### Cross-Functional Coordination[^34]

**What it is.** Working with people outside your office — other MOAs, Parliament committees, LGUs, national agencies, and development partners.

**In government.** A feeding program involves MSSD (implementation), MFBM (budget release), MILG (LGU coordination), MOH (nutrition standards), and the LGU (service delivery). No single office can do it alone. Cross-functional coordination is how things actually get done.

### Stakeholder Engagement[^35]

**What it is.** Consulting communities, civil society organizations (CSOs), the private sector, and academe. Gathering input, building consensus, and maintaining relationships.

**In BARMM.** Stakeholder engagement means working across **13 ethnolinguistic groups**[^36] with different languages, customs, and perspectives. A community consultation in Tausug-speaking Tawi-Tawi requires different approaches than one in Meranaw-speaking Lanao del Sur. Cultural sensitivity is not a bonus skill — it is a core competency.

### Shura (Consultation)[^37]

**What it is.** The Islamic principle of collective decision-making. In Arabic, *shura* means consultation.

**In BARMM.** Shura is not just a value — it is a governance practice. Major decisions are made through consultation, not unilateral directives. Committee hearings, stakeholder forums, community assemblies, and inter-agency coordination meetings are all expressions of shura. As a government worker, you participate in this practice every time you consult before deciding.

**How fresh graduates demonstrate collaboration.** Group projects, student organization leadership, volunteer work, community service, sports teams, family responsibilities that required coordination — all of these show teamwork capacity. In your interview, describe a specific team achievement and your role in it using the STAR method.

## 11.4 Values-Based Competencies

Technical competence without values is dangerous. A government worker who can write excellent memos but lacks integrity produces polished documents that mislead decision-makers. Values are the foundation of public trust.

### Integrity and Ethics

**What it is.** Honesty, transparency, and compliance with laws and organizational policies. In BARMM: **amanah** (trustworthiness) — a core Islamic value and one of the 12 Pillars of Moral Governance.[^7]

**In government.** Every document you produce is a public record. Every recommendation you make affects real people. Every budget you process uses public funds. Integrity means:
- Reporting facts accurately, even when they are unfavorable
- Declaring conflicts of interest
- Not using your position for personal benefit
- Following procurement rules even when shortcuts are tempting
- Maintaining confidentiality on sensitive matters

**What the ACSCCRaPATFEE framework says:**[^8]

| Level | Behavior |
|---|---|
| **Entry** | Acts honestly; follows ethical guidelines; declares conflicts of interest; maintains confidentiality |
| **Intermediate** | Navigates ethical dilemmas; ensures compliance with BOL and BAAs; promotes transparency |
| **Advanced** | Shapes ethical frameworks for the organization; leads anti-corruption initiatives; serves as moral compass |

### Professionalism and Service Orientation[^38]

**What it is.** Putting the public first. Showing up on time. Meeting deadlines. Treating colleagues and constituents with respect. Maintaining composure under pressure.

**In BARMM.** You serve the Bangsamoro people — including marginalized communities, conflict-affected families, indigenous peoples, and underserved barangays. Professionalism means treating the barangay captain from a remote island municipality with the same respect you give the Minister.

### Results Orientation

**What it is.** Delivering outputs on time and to standard. Setting goals and meeting them. Not just working hard, but working toward measurable outcomes.

**In government.** Your work is measured through the **Strategic Performance Management System (SPMS)**.[^9] Your Individual Performance Commitment and Review (IPCR) sets specific targets. At year-end, you are rated on a 5-point scale: Poor, Unsatisfactory, Satisfactory, Very Satisfactory, Outstanding. Your rating affects your Performance-Based Bonus, promotion ranking, and career trajectory.

### Accountability[^39]

**What it is.** Taking responsibility for your work — both successes and failures. In BARMM: **adl** (justice) and transparency in governance.[^40]

**In government.** When your CSW contains an error, own it. When your project falls behind schedule, report it honestly. When your supervisor asks why a deadline was missed, explain the cause and propose a solution — do not make excuses or blame others.

**How fresh graduates demonstrate values.** Values are shown through behavior, not statements. Returning extra change. Citing sources instead of plagiarizing. Completing your share of a group project even when others slack off. Admitting when you are wrong. These behaviors, from your everyday life, are evidence of the values government demands.

## 11.5 The Bangsamoro Dimension

These competencies are **not in the CSC Competency Dictionary**.[^10] They are unique to BARMM — a recognition that working in the Bangsamoro government requires knowledge and sensitivities that no other Philippine government employer demands.

### BOL Literacy[^41]

**What it is.** Understanding Republic Act 11054,[^11] the Bangsamoro Organic Law — the law that created the autonomous government you are joining.

**Why it matters.** Every decision you make, every policy you recommend, and every program you implement operates within the BOL's framework. If you do not know what BARMM can and cannot do under the BOL, you will propose actions outside your government's competence — embarrassing yourself and your office.

**What you should know:**
- **Article III** — Territory[^12] (5 provinces, Cotabato City, 63 barangays in North Cotabato as SGA municipalities; Sulu excluded per Supreme Court ruling, now Region IX)[^42]
- **Article V, Section 2** — The 55 areas of competence[^13] (what BARMM can do that other regions cannot)
- **Article VII** — The Bangsamoro Government structure, including the Parliament[^14]
- **Article X** — The Shari'ah justice system[^15]
- **Article XII** — Fiscal autonomy (block grant, revenue powers)[^16]

### Bangsamoro Cultural Competence[^43]

**What it is.** Awareness of the Bangsamoro people's diversity, history, and identity. Understanding that "Bangsamoro" encompasses 13 ethnolinguistic groups[^17] — Meranaw, Maguindanaon, Tausug, Sama, Yakan, Iranun, Kalibugan, Palawanon, Molbog, Jama Mapun, Badjao, Sangil, and Teduray — plus settler communities, Other Bangsamoro Communities, and indigenous peoples.

**Why it matters.** You will work with colleagues and communities whose languages, customs, food, clothing, kinship systems, and conflict resolution practices differ from yours. Cultural competence is not about knowing every tradition. It is about **approaching differences with respect and curiosity rather than judgment**.

**Practical examples:**
- Learning basic greetings in the language of your assigned community
- Understanding that time, hierarchy, and hospitality operate differently across groups
- Respecting Islamic practices (prayer times, halal food, Ramadan observance) without being asked
- Recognizing that "the Bangsamoro story" is not a single story — it is many stories from many peoples

### Complete Staff Work (ADDRESS IT)

**What it is.** The CSW methodology described in Chapter 10. ADDRESS IT is not just a process — it is a competency. Your ability to produce complete, accurate, decision-ready staff work is the single most important functional skill in BARMM government.

**Why it is listed as a Bangsamoro competency.** While CSW exists in concept across Philippine government, the ADDRESS IT framework is specific to BARMM.[^18] It was designed for this government's context — autonomous governance, transitional period, system-building, peace agreement implementation. Knowing ADDRESS IT sets you apart from applicants who only know the generic CSC version.

### Moral Governance Practice[^44]

**What it is.** Living the **12 Pillars of Moral Governance**[^19] — not just reciting them.

The 12 pillars are: integrity, accountability, transparency, patriotism, excellence, cultural understanding, loyalty to God, justice, inclusivity, objectivity, dialogue, and balance.[^45]

**Why it matters.** These are not aspirational values on a poster. They are cited in performance evaluations, referenced in legislation, and expected in daily conduct. When you prepare a budget, **transparency** means making the numbers verifiable. When you engage a community, **dialogue** means genuinely listening. When you face pressure to cut corners, **integrity** means refusing.

### Peace Process Awareness[^46]

**What it is.** Understanding that your work implements the Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro (CAB)[^20] — a peace agreement that ended decades of armed conflict.

**Why it matters.** BARMM is not just another government agency. It is the institutional expression of the Bangsamoro people's right to self-governance, won through negotiation after decades of war. Every program you run, every policy you draft, every barangay you serve delivers **peace dividends** to communities that have known conflict for generations.

**What this means for your work.** The feeding program you track in MSSD is not just nutrition data — it is a tangible benefit of peace for a family in a conflict-affected barangay. The bill you brief in Parliament is not just legislation — it is the exercise of self-governance that the CAB promised. When your work feels tedious, remember what it represents.

## 11.6 Functional Competencies by Track

Beyond the universal competencies above, each career track requires **specialized functional skills**. These are the competencies that make a legislative staff officer different from a municipal planner or a media affairs officer.

### Parliament: Political Affairs Officers (PAOs) — 5 Competencies[^21]

| # | Competency | What it means in practice |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Stakeholder Engagement, Community Organizing, and Constituency Building** | Managing the MP's relationship with constituents; organizing community dialogues; building political support for legislation |
| 2 | **Representation Skills** | Speaking on behalf of the MP at events; articulating the MP's positions; liaison with other offices |
| 3 | **Social Marketing, Advocacy, and Lobbying** | Building public support for the MP's legislative agenda; engaging CSOs and media; persuading colleagues |
| 4 | **Project, Program, and Events Management** | Organizing committee hearings, public consultations, and constituency events; managing logistics and budgets |
| 5 | **Monitoring, Reporting, and Evaluation** | Tracking the MP's legislative scorecard; reporting on committee progress; evaluating program impact |

### Parliament: Legislative Staff Officers (LSOs) — 5 Competencies[^22]

| # | Competency | What it means in practice |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Investigation and Research Skills** | Conducting legislative research; gathering evidence for committee hearings; reviewing proposed bills against existing law |
| 2 | **Policy Making, Analysis, and Evaluation** | Analyzing policy options; drafting policy papers; evaluating the impact of proposed and enacted legislation |
| 3 | **Legal and Legislative Competence** | Understanding the BOL, BAAs, and national laws; drafting bills and resolutions; ensuring legal consistency |
| 4 | **Attention to Details and Quality** | Producing error-free briefers, committee reports, and bill drafts; cross-referencing legal citations; maintaining version control |
| 5 | **Oversight Skills** | Supporting the committee's oversight function; monitoring implementing agencies; preparing oversight reports |

### Parliament: Public Information and Media Affairs Officers (PIMAOs) — 5 Competencies[^23]

| # | Competency | What it means in practice |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Strategic Communications** | Developing the MP's communication strategy; crafting key messages; managing public perception |
| 2 | **Documentation and Editing Skills** | Photographing and recording events; editing video and written content; maintaining media archives |
| 3 | **Social Media Skills** | Managing the MP's official social media accounts; creating engaging content; monitoring online engagement |
| 4 | **Print and Digital Media Skills** | Writing press releases; designing infographics and publications; producing newsletters and digital content |
| 5 | **Media Handling Skills** | Coordinating press conferences; preparing media kits; briefing the MP before media interviews; managing media relations |

### Ministry, Office/Agency, and LGU Tracks[^47]

| Track | Key functional competencies |
|---|---|
| **Ministry (technical)** | Program management, budget analysis, technical expertise in your sector (health, education, agriculture, etc.), regulatory compliance |
| **Ministry (administrative)** | Records management, procurement processing, HR administration, financial management, property and supply management |
| **Office/Agency** | Program design and evaluation, multi-stakeholder coordination, monitoring and evaluation (M&E), policy brief writing, data analysis |
| **LGU** | Community relations and engagement, frontline service delivery, local regulatory compliance, basic development planning, multi-tasking across functions |

## 11.7 How Competencies Are Assessed

Your competencies are measured at every stage of your government career. Knowing how assessment works helps you prepare.[^48]

| Stage | Assessment Method | What is measured | Your preparation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Hiring** | Competency-Based Interview (CBI) using the STAR method | Specific competencies from the position description | Prepare 5 STAR stories covering thinking, communication, collaboration, values, and Bangsamoro awareness |
| **Probation** (Months 1-6) | Supervisor observation against position description | Whether you can perform the job's core functions | Focus on producing quality CSW outputs and building relationships |
| **Annual evaluation** | SPMS/IPCR with competency ratings | Results (what you achieved) and behaviors (how you achieved them) | Track your outputs, document your achievements, connect your work to BDP goals |
| **Promotion** | Comparative Assessment with competency scoring[^25] | Education (25%), Experience (25%), Training (15%), Eligibility (10%), Interview (25%) | Accumulate training hours, build a track record of rated outputs, prepare for another CBI |
| **Training** | Pre/post competency assessment | Whether training improved your competency level | Engage fully in training; apply new skills immediately on the job |

### The ACSCCRaPATFEE Framework

The 12 competencies of a CSW practitioner are organized using the **ACSCCRaPATFEE** mnemonic.[^24] This framework maps each competency to specific steps in the ADDRESS IT process:

| # | Competency | Mnemonic | Primary ADDRESS IT Steps |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Analytical and Critical Thinking | **A** | Step 1 (Analyze), Step 3 (Research) |
| 2 | Creativity and Innovation | **C** | Step 4 (Explore Options) |
| 3 | Strategic Thinking | **S** | Step 1 (Analyze), Step 4 (Explore), Step 7 (Iterate) |
| 4 | Communication | **C** | Step 5 (Submit), Step 6 (Sustain) |
| 5 | Collaboration and Teamwork | **C** | Step 2 (Deliberate), Step 6 (Sustain) |
| 6 | Research and Analysis | **Ra** | Step 3 (Research) |
| 7 | Problem-Solving and Decision Making | **P** | Step 2 (Define), Step 4 (Explore) |
| 8 | Attention to Details and Quality | **A** | Step 5 (Submit) |
| 9 | Time and Attention Management | **T** | All steps (cross-cutting) |
| 10 | Flexibility and Adaptability | **F** | Step 7 (Iterate), Step 6 (Sustain) |
| 11 | Emotional Intelligence and Accountability | **E** | Step 2 (Deliberate), Step 6 (Sustain) |
| 12 | Ethics and Integrity | **E** | All steps (cross-cutting) |

You do not need to memorize this table. But understand the principle: **every competency connects to a specific part of your work process.** If your supervisor identifies a weakness in your Step 3 (Research), the competency to develop is #6 (Research and Analysis). If your Step 5 outputs have frequent errors, focus on #8 (Attention to Details and Quality).

**Instructions.** Rate yourself honestly on each competency category. Use a 4-point scale:

- **3 = Adequate** — I can do this but need more practice

### Interpreting Your Results

**Categories where you scored 3-4.** These are your strengths. Highlight them in your cover letter and prepare STAR stories around them for interviews. You can confidently say: "I have demonstrated [this competency] through [specific experience]."

**Categories where you scored 1-2.** These are your development areas. They do not disqualify you — they guide your preparation:

| Development area | What to do BEFORE applying | What to do ON the job |
|---|---|---|
| Thinking skills | Practice case analysis; read policy documents; join a debate or critical thinking group | Ask your supervisor for feedback on your analysis; study how senior colleagues structure their CSW |
| Communication | Start writing — blog posts, reports, summaries. Practice presentations. | Volunteer to draft memos and minutes; ask colleagues to review your writing; study Chapter 13 |
| Collaboration | Join group activities; volunteer for team-based projects; seek leadership roles in organizations | Proactively offer help to colleagues; participate actively in meetings; join inter-office working groups |
| Values | Reflect on ethical dilemmas you have faced; study the 12 Pillars of Moral Governance | Observe how senior colleagues handle ethical situations; hold yourself accountable daily |
| Bangsamoro | Read the BOL (RA 11054), the BDP 2023-2028, and key BAAs; study Bangsamoro history and culture | Attend office orientation sessions; ask colleagues about cultural practices; learn basic greetings in local languages |

## Key Takeaways

- **Competencies are observable behaviors**, not self-declarations. You must be able to show, not just claim, your abilities.
- **Five categories** matter in BARMM: thinking, communication, collaboration, values, and Bangsamoro-specific competencies.
- **Systems Thinking** helps you see how your work connects to the larger government ecosystem. A budget memo is never just about numbers — it is about the services those numbers fund.
- **The Bangsamoro dimension is real.** BOL literacy, cultural competence, moral governance practice, and peace process awareness are not theoretical concepts. They shape daily work in every BARMM office.
- **Functional competencies differ by track.** Parliament staff (PAOs, LSOs, PIMAOs) need different specialized skills than ministry technical staff, agency planners, or LGU frontline workers.
- **Assessment is continuous.** From hiring (CBI) through probation, annual evaluation (SPMS/IPCR), promotion, and training — your competencies are measured at every stage.
- **Start where you are.** Use the self-assessment to identify gaps. Develop your weak areas before applying and continue developing on the job. No one starts as an expert. The best government workers are those who **keep growing**.

Your degree proved you can learn. Your competencies prove you can **contribute**.
