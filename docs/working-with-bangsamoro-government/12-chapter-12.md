# Chapter 12: Working with Your Supervisor

Your supervisor's job is to develop you. Your job is to make that possible.

This chapter is written from **your perspective** — the staff member. Your supervisor has their own guidebook (the Supervision Guidebook)[^1] that teaches them how to manage, coach, and evaluate you. This chapter teaches you what to expect from them, what they expect from you, and how to build the kind of working relationship that advances your career.

---

## What Your Supervisor Expects From You

Before you worry about whether your supervisor is fair, competent, or easy to work with, understand what **every supervisor expects** from their staff. These expectations are universal — they apply whether you work in Parliament, a ministry, an office, or an LGU.

### 1. Come Prepared[^15]

When your supervisor calls a meeting, a check-in, or a review — **arrive ready**. That means:
- Know the status of your assignments
- Bring your notes, your data, and your questions
- Have a brief summary ready: *"Here is where I am, here is what I need, here is what is blocking me."*
- Do not wait for your supervisor to ask you what is happening. Tell them.

**What this looks like in practice.** Your supervisor schedules a 30-minute check-in for Monday morning. Before you walk in, review your pending assignments, note which ones are on track and which are behind, prepare your questions, and have a list of decisions you need from them. That 30-minute meeting will be productive. Without preparation, it becomes 30 minutes of your supervisor pulling information from you one question at a time.

### 2. Receive Feedback Without Defensiveness

Your supervisor will give you feedback — both positive and corrective. The corrective feedback is not a personal attack. It is **professional development**. Your ability to receive it gracefully is itself a competency (Emotional Intelligence and Accountability — ACSCCRaPATFEE #11).[^2]

**The SBI model from your end.** Your supervisor may use the **Situation-Behavior-Impact (SBI)** model to give you feedback.[^3] Here is what it sounds like and how to respond:

| Your supervisor says | What they mean | How to respond |
|---|---|---|
| "In yesterday's committee hearing **[Situation]**, you did not present the options analysis **[Behavior]**, which meant the committee could not make a decision **[Impact]**." | Your incomplete briefer caused a real problem. | "Thank you for pointing that out. I should have included the options analysis. I will make sure the next briefer has the full analysis section before I submit it." |
| "During last week's staff meeting **[S]**, you volunteered to help the Basilan team with their data **[B]**, and that allowed them to meet their deadline **[I]**." | Your proactive collaboration made a real difference. | "Thank you. I noticed they were struggling and thought I could help. I am glad it worked out." |

**What NOT to do when receiving feedback:**
- Do not argue or get defensive ("But that is not what I meant...")
- Do not blame others ("The data was not available because Finance did not send it...")
- Do not shut down or go silent
- Do not dismiss it ("It was not a big deal anyway...")

**What TO do:**
- Listen fully before responding
- Acknowledge the feedback ("I understand")
- Ask clarifying questions if needed ("Can you help me understand what the options analysis should have included?")
- Commit to specific improvement ("Next time, I will complete the options analysis and have you review it before the hearing")
- Follow through — and let your supervisor see the change

### 3. Take Ownership of Your Development[^16]

Your supervisor will coach you, train you, and provide opportunities. But **they cannot develop you against your will**. You must be an active participant in your own growth.

**What ownership looks like:**
- Asking your supervisor: *"What should I be learning in my first 6 months?"*
- Requesting training opportunities before they are offered
- Seeking feedback proactively: *"How was my last memo? What can I improve?"*
- Studying on your own — reading the BOL, reviewing BAAs, learning the office's systems
- Watching how senior colleagues work and learning from their approach

**What it does NOT look like:**
- Waiting to be told what to learn
- Attending training only when forced
- Never asking for feedback
- Repeating the same mistakes because you never sought to understand why

### 4. Produce CSW That Does Not Need Rework[^17]

This is the practical test. Your supervisor measures your competence primarily through your written output. If every memo you submit comes back with red marks, your supervisor's confidence in you decreases. If your work is consistently clean, complete, and well-structured, their confidence grows — and so do your assignments.

**The standard (from Chapter 10):** Your supervisor should only need to approve or disapprove. If they have to rewrite your sentences, check your citations, fill in missing data, or fix your formatting — your staff work is not complete.

## The Check-In Rhythm

Your supervisor should conduct regular **check-ins** — at least monthly, often more frequently during your first year. Check-ins are structured conversations, not casual chats. They follow a **4-step protocol**.[^4]

### The 4-Step Check-In Protocol (From Your Perspective)

**Step 1: Discuss Roles and Responsibilities**

Your supervisor shares the office's direction, clarifies expectations, and connects your work to the bigger picture. **Your role:** listen actively, ask questions, make sure you understand what is expected. If something is unclear, this is the time to clarify — not three weeks later when a deadline is missed.

*What to ask:*
- "Has anything changed in our priorities since our last check-in?"
- "Are there new assignments I should know about?"
- "How does my current project connect to the office's OPCR targets?"

**Step 2: Review Goals and Assignments**

You and your supervisor review your current workload against your goals (IPCR targets, assignment deadlines, project milestones). **Your role:** come prepared with a status update for every active assignment. Be honest about what is behind schedule and why.

*What to bring:*
- A list of your active assignments with status (on track / behind / completed)
- Specific questions about priorities ("I have three deadlines this week — which is most urgent?")
- Updates on completed work ("I submitted the feeding program memo last Tuesday — have you had a chance to review it?")

**Step 3: Evaluate Progress and Provide Coaching**

Your supervisor assesses how you are doing — not just what you are producing, but how you are producing it. They may use the **SBI model** to give feedback (see above). They may also provide positive feedback to reinforce what you are doing well.

**Your role:** receive feedback openly. Ask follow-up questions. If your supervisor says your analysis was strong but your formatting needs work, write it down and fix it. If they praise your initiative, keep doing it.

*What to ask:*
- "What is one thing I could do differently to improve my output?"
- "Are there specific skills I should be developing right now?"
- "Can you show me an example of what a strong [memo / briefer / report] looks like in our office?"

**Step 4: Determine Next Steps**

Agree on what you will do before the next check-in. Your supervisor may assign new tasks, set development goals, or ask you to follow up on specific items. **Your role:** write down the agreed actions. Do them. Report back at the next check-in.

*What to confirm:*
- "So my priorities for the next two weeks are [X, Y, Z] — is that correct?"
- "I will complete the policy recommendation by Friday and submit it through you for review."
- "Can we schedule the next check-in for [date]?"

## How You Are Being Assessed: The PPR Framework

Your supervisor uses the **Performance-Potential-Readiness (PPR)** framework to assess where you stand.[^5] Understanding this framework helps you see your career from your supervisor's perspective — and take action accordingly.

### Step 1: Performance (Your Current Role)

Your supervisor evaluates how well you are performing **right now** — in the position you currently hold. Performance includes both **results** (what you achieve) and **behaviors** (how you achieve them).

- **Results**: Are you meeting your IPCR targets? Are your CSW outputs complete and on time? Are your projects progressing?
- **Behaviors**: Are you collaborative? Professional? Responsive to feedback? Reliable?

A staff member who produces excellent briefers but is consistently late, combative in meetings, and unwilling to help colleagues is not performing well — because behaviors matter as much as results.

### Step 2: Potential (Your Future Capacity)

If your performance is solid, your supervisor assesses your **potential** — your capacity to grow into a larger role. Indicators of high potential include:

- Ability to solve complex problems (not just routine ones)
- Persistence through setbacks (you do not give up when things get hard)
- Drive to take on challenging work (you volunteer for difficult assignments)
- Improvement based on feedback (you do not make the same mistake twice)

**Important:** Potential is assessed only when you are meeting or exceeding your current role's expectations.[^18] If your performance is low, your supervisor focuses on closing the performance gap first — not on your future potential.

### Step 3: Readiness (For a New Assignment)

Readiness means you already have the skills, knowledge, and abilities for a **specific new role or assignment**. This is assessed when there is a concrete opportunity — a promotion, a lateral move, a stretch assignment, a project leadership role.

### The 4-Quadrant Coaching Guide

Based on your PPR assessment, your supervisor places you in one of four quadrants.[^6] Each quadrant has a different coaching focus:

| Quadrant | Performance | Potential | What your supervisor will do | What you should do |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Low-Low** | Low | Low | **CHANGE** — Revisit goals and expectations, emphasize ownership, coach for behavior change | Be honest about your challenges. Ask for clear expectations. Accept coaching without defensiveness. Work hard to improve results. |
| **Low-High** | Low | High | **CHANGE** — Clarify goals, show how your behavior affects outcomes, schedule frequent check-ins | You have potential, but you are not delivering yet. Focus on results. Show your supervisor that their investment in you is paying off. |
| **High-Low** | High | Low | **DEVELOP** — Explore your interests and aspirations, identify development opportunities, keep you engaged | You are doing well now but may plateau. Ask about stretch assignments. Express interest in growth areas. Volunteer for cross-functional projects. |
| **High-High** | High | High | **SUSTAIN, REINFORCE, RECOGNIZE** — Help you become a mentor, consider stretch assignments, prepare you for promotion | Keep performing. Mentor junior colleagues. Take on leadership opportunities. Make your case for promotion through consistent excellence. |

**Where do you want to be?** The High-High quadrant. You get there by delivering results, behaving professionally, learning continuously, and demonstrating potential through initiative.

**Where most fresh graduates start.** Realistically, most new staff begin in the Low-High quadrant[^19] — you have potential (your education, your motivation), but you have not yet demonstrated results in government. Your supervisor knows this. Your job in the first 6-12 months is to move from Low-High to High-High by converting your potential into performance.

## How to Receive Feedback

Feedback is your fastest path to improvement. Yet most people resist it. Understanding how feedback works in your office will help you respond constructively.

### The SBI Model from the Receiving End

Your supervisor structures feedback using **Situation, Behavior, Impact**:[^7]

**Corrective feedback example:**
> "In the committee hearing last Thursday **[Situation]**, you read directly from your briefer instead of presenting the key points conversationally **[Behavior]**. The MPs lost interest after 5 minutes and started checking their phones **[Impact]**."

**Your response:**
1. **Acknowledge.** "Thank you for telling me. I did not realize I was reading verbatim."
2. **Understand.** "Should I prepare bullet-point talking points instead of reading the full text?"
3. **Commit.** "For the next hearing, I will practice presenting from notes rather than the full document."
4. **Follow through.** At the next hearing, present from talking points. Your supervisor notices.

**Positive feedback example:**
> "In the inter-agency coordination meeting yesterday **[S]**, you volunteered to draft the joint action plan and completed it within 24 hours **[B]**. That allowed us to submit the plan to the Minister on time and positioned our office as the lead coordinator **[I]**."

**Your response:**
1. **Accept graciously.** "Thank you. I saw the urgency and wanted to help us meet the deadline."
2. **Learn from it.** This tells you that initiative, speed, and quality are valued. Keep doing it.

### Five Feedback Traps to Avoid[^20]

| Trap | What it sounds like | Why it hurts you |
|---|---|---|
| **Defensiveness** | "But I did include the data — it was on page 7" | Your supervisor stops giving you feedback because it always becomes an argument |
| **Deflection** | "The real problem was that Finance did not send the numbers on time" | Your supervisor sees you as someone who does not take responsibility |
| **Silence** | [Says nothing, looks at the floor] | Your supervisor cannot tell if you understood or plan to change |
| **Over-apologizing** | "I am so sorry, I am so sorry, I will never do it again" | Emotional responses make supervisors uncomfortable giving future feedback |
| **Immediate pushback** | "That is not fair — I worked on this all weekend" | Effort is not the same as quality. Your supervisor is evaluating the output, not the hours |

**The right approach:** Listen. Acknowledge. Ask for clarification. Commit to improvement. Demonstrate the change.

## Your Performance Evaluation

Once a year, your supervisor conducts a formal **performance evaluation**. This follows the **6-step evaluation protocol**.[^8] Understanding what your supervisor is doing at each step helps you prepare.

### Step 1: Your Supervisor Gets Your Input

Your supervisor asks you to summarize your key accomplishments. Provide this in writing using the **Situation-Action-Result** format:
- *Situation:* "The feeding program in Basilan was 62% behind target as of March."
- *Action:* "I conducted root cause analysis, proposed a 3-option corrective plan, and coordinated the TA deployment to 3 municipalities."
- *Result:* "The program reached 87% of target by year-end — a 25-percentage-point improvement."

**Important:** Your supervisor will NOT ask you to rate yourself (self-ratings create defensiveness).[^21] They only want your accomplishments in narrative form.

### Step 2: Your Supervisor Gets Input from Others

Your supervisor may ask colleagues, partners, or subordinates about your work. This is not about collecting gossip — it is a **multi-source feedback** process to get a complete picture.

**What you can do:** Build strong working relationships with colleagues so that when asked, they have positive and specific things to say about your collaboration.

### Step 3: Your Supervisor Evaluates Results (the "What")

Your supervisor assesses what you accomplished against your IPCR targets. They look at:
- **Context** — what circumstances affected your work (budget delays, staffing gaps, emergencies)
- **Accomplishment** — what you actually delivered
- **Impact** — what difference your work made

### Step 4: Your Supervisor Evaluates Behaviors (the "How")

Results are not enough. Your supervisor evaluates **how** you achieved them. Did you collaborate or work in isolation? Did you meet deadlines or consistently miss them? Did you support team goals or only pursue your own assignments?

This is where the competencies from Chapter 11 matter. Your supervisor assesses you against the 5-10 most important competencies for your position.

### Step 5: Your Supervisor Writes the Review

Your supervisor prepares a written evaluation with:
- A **rating** for both results and behaviors (using the SPMS scale:[^9] Poor through Outstanding)
- A **narrative** explaining why each rating was given
- **Development recommendations** for the coming year

### Step 6: Your Supervisor Discusses the Evaluation with You

This is a face-to-face conversation. Your supervisor:
- Summarizes the check-in discussions throughout the year
- Shares the rating and narrative
- Asks for your thoughts
- Focuses on development for the next year

**The golden rule of evaluations:** If check-ins were done well throughout the year, **the evaluation should contain no surprises**.[^22] Every piece of feedback in the evaluation should have been discussed at least once during a check-in.

### How to Prepare for Your Evaluation

| Preparation | What to do |
|---|---|
| **Track your work all year**[^23] | Keep a running list of completed assignments, achievements, and impact data. Do not rely on memory in December. |
| **Save your outputs** | Keep copies of your best CSW products — briefers, memos, reports, project proposals. These are evidence. |
| **Document BDP alignment** | For each major output, note which BDP goal or priority agenda item it supports. |
| **Note your training** | Record every training, seminar, and workshop you attended, with dates and topics. |
| **Prepare your accomplishments** | Write 3-5 accomplishments in Situation-Action-Result format before the evaluation meeting. |
| **Identify your development needs** | Be honest about areas where you struggled and propose what training or support would help. |

## When Things Go Wrong

Not every supervisor-staff relationship works smoothly. Here is what to do when challenges arise.

### Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)

If your supervisor determines that your performance is consistently below standard, they may place you on a **Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)**.[^10] This is a formal, documented intervention — not a punishment.

**What a PIP includes:**
- Specific performance gaps identified
- Measurable improvement targets
- Timeline for improvement (typically 30-90 days)
- Support the supervisor will provide (coaching, training, resources)
- Consequences if improvement is not achieved

**What you should do if placed on a PIP:**
1. **Take it seriously.** A PIP is a warning that your employment may be at risk.
2. **Understand the gaps clearly.** Ask your supervisor: "What specifically do I need to change?"
3. **Follow the plan.** Meet every milestone, attend every check-in, document your progress.
4. **Ask for help.** Use the coaching and training offered. Seek mentorship from senior colleagues.
5. **Do not take it personally.** A PIP means your supervisor still sees potential — otherwise they would move directly to disciplinary action.

### Progressive Discipline

If performance does not improve or if you commit a workplace violation, the **2017 Rules on Administrative Cases in the Civil Service (2017 RACCS)**[^11] governs the disciplinary process. The progression is:

| Step | Action | Typical grounds |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Written warning** | Minor violations (tardiness, dress code, minor discourtesy) |
| 2 | **Reprimand** | Repeated minor violations or first moderate offense |
| 3 | **Suspension** (1-90 days) | Serious violations (gross negligence, insubordination, unauthorized absence) |
| 4 | **Dismissal** | Grave offenses (dishonesty, corruption, grave misconduct, conviction of a crime involving moral turpitude) |

**Your rights under due process:**
- You must be informed of the charges in writing
- You have the right to answer and present evidence
- You have the right to a formal hearing if the offense is grave
- You can appeal to the CSC or the appropriate body
- You cannot be dismissed without due process — even if you are guilty

### When the Problem Is Your Supervisor

Sometimes the challenge is not your performance but the supervisory relationship. If your supervisor is:
- **Unclear about expectations** — request a written set of priorities
- **Unavailable for check-ins** — propose a schedule and follow up in writing
- **Giving inconsistent direction** — document instructions and confirm in email
- **Hostile or abusive** — document incidents, report to HR, and know your rights under the 2017 RACCS[^14] and RA 7877[^12] (Anti-Sexual Harassment Act) or RA 11313[^13] (Safe Spaces Act)

**The professional response:** Address issues through proper channels. Document everything. Seek guidance from HR or a trusted senior colleague. Do not escalate on social media, gossip with colleagues, or retaliate. Your professionalism in difficult situations demonstrates the values-based competencies from Chapter 11.

## Building a Productive Relationship

The best supervisor-staff relationships are built on **mutual investment**.[^24] Here is what that looks like:

| Your supervisor's investment in you | Your investment in the relationship |
|---|---|
| Clarifies expectations regularly | Delivers on those expectations |
| Gives honest feedback using SBI | Receives feedback without defensiveness |
| Creates development opportunities | Takes ownership of your development |
| Advocates for your promotion when ready | Produces work that justifies advocacy |
| Protects you from unfair demands | Protects your supervisor from surprises |
| Coaches you through mistakes | Learns from mistakes and does not repeat them |

**The last point deserves emphasis.** "Protects your supervisor from surprises" means: if something goes wrong with your assignment, tell your supervisor **before** they hear about it from someone else. If a deadline will be missed, flag it early. If a stakeholder complained, report it immediately. Supervisors can manage problems. They cannot manage problems they do not know about.

## Key Takeaways

- **Come prepared** to every check-in. Your supervisor's time is limited. Make meetings productive by bringing status updates, questions, and proposed next steps.
- **Receive feedback as a gift**, not an attack. The SBI model (Situation, Behavior, Impact) structures feedback so it is specific and actionable. Your job is to listen, acknowledge, and improve.
- **Understand how you are being assessed.** The PPR framework (Performance, Potential, Readiness) places you in one of four quadrants. Your goal is to move to High-High through consistent results and professional behaviors.
- **The 4-step check-in protocol** structures your monthly conversations: roles and responsibilities, goals and assignments, progress and coaching, next steps.
- **The 6-step evaluation protocol** is your annual formal review. Prepare by tracking accomplishments all year using Situation-Action-Result format.
- **No surprises.** If check-ins are done well, nothing in your evaluation should be unexpected. If something surprises you, the check-in process broke down — raise this with your supervisor.
- **When things go wrong**, respond professionally. A PIP is a chance to improve, not a death sentence. Due process protects you. And if the problem is your supervisor, address it through proper channels with documentation.
- **Your supervisor cannot develop you against your will.**[^25] Take ownership. Ask for feedback. Seek training. Study on your own. The staff members who grow fastest are those who **pursue development, not wait for it**.
