# Chapter 9: The Interview and Assessment

The interview is 25% of your score. For a fresh graduate with limited experience, it might be the difference between getting hired and getting a regret letter. Prepare like it matters -- because it does.

---

You submitted your application. The HRMPSB screened it and found that you meet the qualification standards. Now they want to meet you. This chapter covers everything you need to know about the government interview: the format, the questions, the scoring, the preparation, and the follow-up. If you prepare using this chapter, you will walk into the panel more prepared than most applicants -- including those with more experience than you.

---

## 9.1 What to Expect: The Interview Format

Government interviews are structured. They are not casual conversations. Here is what you will encounter:

**Panel interview:** Typically **3-5 members** of the HRMPSB.[^1] The panel usually includes:
- The HR head or HR officer
- A division chief or supervisor from the hiring unit
- A subject matter expert
- An employee representative
- Sometimes an external resource person

**Duration:** 20-45 minutes depending on the position level. Entry-level interviews (SG 11-15) are usually 20-30 minutes.

**Format:** **Competency-Based Interview (CBI)**[^2] -- structured questions designed to assess specific competencies from the position description. This is not a freestyle Q&A. Each question targets a specific competency. Each panelist scores independently.

**Scoring:** Each panelist uses a **standardized rubric**. They rate your answers on a scale (typically 1-5) for each competency assessed. Your interview score is the averaged total.

**Recording:** Some offices record interviews. You may be asked to sign a consent form before the interview begins. This is standard procedure for documentation purposes.

## 9.2 Competency-Based Interviews (CBI) -- The STAR Method

Every CBI question asks you to describe a **past situation** where you demonstrated a specific competency. The panel is not interested in what you would hypothetically do. They want to know what you **actually did**.

The standard framework for answering is **STAR**:[^3]

| Element | What It Is | How to Answer |
|---|---|---|
| **S** -- Situation | Set the scene. Where? When? What was happening? | Be specific. "During my thesis research on agricultural cooperatives in Maguindanao in April 2025..." |
| **T** -- Task | What was your specific role or responsibility? | Clarify YOUR role, not the team's. "I was responsible for conducting interviews with 30 cooperative leaders across 5 municipalities..." |
| **A** -- Action | What did YOU specifically do? | Use "I" not "we." "I designed a bilingual questionnaire in Filipino and Maguindanaon, coordinated with barangay captains for introductions, and scheduled interviews over three weeks..." |
| **R** -- Result | What happened? Quantify if possible. | Show the outcome. "We completed all 30 interviews on schedule. The thesis earned a grade of 1.25, and the cooperative federation adopted two of our recommendations." |

### STAR Rules

- **Be specific.** "I helped with a project" tells the panel nothing. "I managed the logistics for a youth summit with 200 participants from 5 provinces" tells them everything.
- **Use "I" not "we."** The panel is assessing you, not your team. Even in team projects, clarify what you personally contributed.
- **Quantify results.** Numbers make your answers credible. "30 interviews," "200 participants," "3-week timeline," "grade of 1.25" are all stronger than vague descriptions.
- **Keep it under 2 minutes.** A good STAR answer is 90-120 seconds. Longer answers lose the panel's attention.

### Where Fresh Graduates Find STAR Stories

You do not need work experience to have STAR stories. Your "situations" come from:

- **Thesis or capstone research** -- research design, data collection, stakeholder engagement, writing under deadline
- **Student organization leadership** -- planning events, managing budgets, coordinating teams, resolving conflicts
- **Volunteer work and community service** -- disaster response, barangay programs, community outreach
- **Part-time jobs, OJT, or internships** -- real work experience even if short-term
- **Family responsibilities** -- managing a household IS project management. Coordinating family events IS logistics.
- **Personal challenges overcome** -- resilience, problem-solving, adapting to difficult circumstances

## 9.3 Common Interview Questions for BARMM Positions

### Governance Awareness Questions

Expect these in **every** BARMM interview. They test whether you understand the government you are joining.

**"What do you know about the Bangsamoro Organic Law?"**

How to prepare: Review Chapter 2. Know that RA 11054[^4] established BARMM, was ratified by plebiscite in 2019, gives the Bangsamoro government 55 enumerated powers, creates a parliamentary system, and provides fiscal autonomy through the Annual Block Grant and Special Development Fund.[^5] You do not need to recite sections. Demonstrate understanding.

**"Can you name the 6 goals of the Bangsamoro Development Plan?"**

How to prepare: Review Chapter 5. List them:[^16]
1. Stable, just, and accountable government
2. Equitable, competitive, and robust economy
3. Peaceful, safe, and resilient Bangsamoro communities
4. Inclusive, responsive, and quality social services
5. Rich and diverse Bangsamoro culture and identity preserved and recognized
6. Strategic, adequate, and climate-resilient infrastructure

Then connect one goal to the office you are applying to. "Since I am applying to MSSD, I am most drawn to Goal 4. MSSD's social protection programs directly advance inclusive and quality social services."

**"What is the mandate of [this office] under BAA 13?"**

How to prepare: Before every interview, look up the office's mandate in BAA 13 (the Bangsamoro Administrative Code).[^6] You studied this in Chapter 3. State the mandate in your own words, not word-for-word recitation.

**"What does moral governance mean to you?"**

How to prepare: Review the 12 Pillars of Moral Governance from Chapter 5.[^7] Choose 2-3 pillars that resonate with you and explain why. Connect them to how you would conduct yourself in the position.

**"How does BARMM differ from ARMM? Why did ARMM fail?"**

How to prepare: ARMM (Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao) had limited powers and was widely seen as ineffective. BARMM has a parliamentary system, enumerated powers over 55 areas,[^8] its own civil service code,[^9] fiscal autonomy,[^10] and was created through a peace agreement (the Comprehensive Agreement on the Bangsamoro, signed March 27, 2014).[^15] The key difference is genuine autonomy with real powers, not just a name change.

### Competency Questions (STAR Format)

These questions assess your core competencies. Prepare a STAR story for each.

**Critical Thinking:**
"Tell me about a time you had to analyze a complex problem and propose a solution."
- Thesis research, data analysis, case studies, organizational problem-solving

**Cultural Competence:**
"Describe a situation where you worked with people from different backgrounds."
- Group projects with diverse classmates, volunteer work in multicultural communities, student exchange, community immersion

**Results Orientation:**
"Give an example of when you had to meet a tight deadline with limited resources."
- Academic deadlines, event planning with a small budget, thesis submission, OJT deliverables

**Openness to Feedback:**
"Tell me about a time you received negative feedback. How did you respond?"
- Thesis defense revisions, supervisor corrections during OJT, constructive criticism from org advisers

**Initiative and Project Management:**
"Describe a project you led from start to finish."
- Student organization events, community projects, research from proposal to defense, family or community occasions you organized

### BARMM-Specific Questions

These questions are unique to BARMM. They test your motivation and cultural fit.

**"Why do you want to work in BARMM government specifically?"**

This is your most important answer. Do not say "I need a job" or "Government work has benefits." Connect your motivation to:
- The peace process and what BARMM represents
- Specific development challenges you want to help address
- Personal connection to the Bangsamoro community (if applicable)
- The opportunity to build systems in a new government

**"How would you handle working with colleagues from different ethnolinguistic groups?"**

BARMM has 13 ethnolinguistic groups.[^11] Acknowledge the diversity. Share an experience working with people different from you. Show respect, openness, and willingness to learn.

**"What role does Islamic governance play in public service?"**

Answer respectfully regardless of your faith. The 12 Pillars of Moral Governance[^12] are rooted in Islamic principles (amanah/trustworthiness, adl/justice, shura/consultation) but apply to all government workers. Focus on values: integrity, accountability, service orientation.

**"What contribution can you make to the Bangsamoro transition?"**

This is a vision question. Speak about the specific skills you bring, the work you want to do, and how you see yourself contributing to building institutions that will serve the first elected Bangsamoro government.

## 9.4 For Fresh Graduates: Compensating for Limited Experience

The panel knows you are a fresh graduate. They do not expect 5 years of government experience. They expect potential, preparation, and authenticity. Here is how to stand out.

### Lead with Preparation

The single most powerful thing you can say in an interview:

> "I may not have government experience, but I have studied your office's mandate under BAA 13, read the relevant BDP chapter, and prepared a draft [output relevant to the position] to show what I can contribute."

Most applicants -- even experienced ones -- have not read the BOL, BAA 13, or the BDP. If you have, you are already ahead.

### Reframe Academic Work as Professional Competence

Your academic experience is real experience. Frame it as such:

| Academic Experience | How to Frame It |
|---|---|
| Thesis research | "I conducted primary research involving interviews with 30 stakeholders across 5 municipalities" |
| Student org presidency | "I led a team of 15 officers, managed an annual budget of PHP 50,000, and organized 8 events" |
| Volunteer work | "I coordinated a disaster response program that distributed relief goods to 200 families" |
| OJT / Internship | "I completed 600 hours of on-the-job training at [organization], where I [specific deliverable]" |
| Group thesis | "I managed the data collection and analysis components for a team of 4 researchers" |

### Show Learning Speed

Fresh graduates win interviews by demonstrating that they learn fast:

> "In my OJT at [organization], I was given a beneficiary tracking database with no instructions. Within two weeks, I had learned the system, identified three data entry errors, and proposed a validation checklist that the office adopted."

The panel takes away: this person can be productive quickly without hand-holding.

### Demonstrate BOL/BDP Knowledge

When you reference the BOL or BDP naturally in your answers, the panel notices. For example:

> "I am drawn to this position because MSSD's work on social protection directly supports BDP Goal 4. I read in the BDP Chapter 8 that BARMM has the highest stunting rates in the Philippines. I want to be part of the team addressing that."

### Bring a Portfolio

A physical folder with your best academic output is unusual and impressive. Include:
- Thesis or capstone abstract (1-2 pages)
- A sample research paper or policy brief
- Event documentation from student organization activities
- Any certificates of recognition

You do not need to hand it over unless asked. Having it signals preparation.

## 9.5 Written Exams and Skills Tests

Some positions require additional assessments beyond the panel interview.

### What to Expect

- **Analytical writing test**: Write a memo, policy brief, or essay on a given topic (common for planning and policy positions)
- **Multiple choice exam**: General knowledge, government policies, position-specific content
- **Practical exercise**: Draft a sample document relevant to the position (e.g., a legislative briefer for LSO positions, a project proposal for planning positions)
- **Technical exam**: Subject matter test for specialized positions (health, agriculture, education, finance, engineering)

### How to Prepare

- **Review the office's recent outputs.** If you are applying to a Parliament committee, read its published committee reports. If you are applying to MSSD, review its published program guidelines.
- **Study the relevant BAA.** The written exam may ask about provisions of BAA 13, BAA 17, or the BAA specific to the office.
- **Practice government writing.** Government writing is different from academic writing. It is shorter, more direct, action-oriented, and follows specific formats (memo, briefer, endorsement). Review the memo format in Chapter 13 if this guidebook's later chapters are available, or ask the HR office what format the office uses.
- **For technical positions:** Review your professional subject matter. An Agricultural Technician position will test agricultural knowledge. A Nurse position will test clinical knowledge.

## 9.6 Cultural Appropriateness

How you present yourself matters. These guidelines help you make the right impression.

### Dress Code

**Business formal.** The interview is a professional setting.

- **For men**: Long-sleeved polo or barong tagalog, slacks (not jeans), closed shoes (not sandals). A necktie is optional but appropriate.
- **For women**: Modest business attire. Consider that many BARMM offices observe Islamic dress norms. Avoid sleeveless tops. Wear knee-length or longer skirts/slacks. A headscarf is not required unless you choose to wear one, but dress modestly.

### Greetings

**"Assalamu Alaikum"** (peace be upon you) is the standard greeting in BARMM offices. It is appropriate regardless of your faith. The standard response is **"Wa Alaikum Assalam"** (and upon you, peace).

If you are unsure, **"Good morning"** or **"Good afternoon"** is always acceptable.

### Addressing Panelists

- **"Sir"** or **"Ma'am"** is always safe
- Use professional titles when known: **"Director,"** **"Attorney,"** **"Engineer,"** **"Doctor"**
- For Members of Parliament: **"Honorable"** (e.g., "Honorable Member" or "Your Honor")
- When in doubt, "Sir/Ma'am" works

### Demeanor

- **Respectful confidence.** You are not begging for a job. You are presenting your qualifications.
- **Look panelists in the eye** when speaking. This signals sincerity.
- **Do not interrupt.** Wait for the panelist to finish the question completely before answering.
- **"Thank you for the question"** before answering buys you 3-5 seconds of thinking time. Use it.
- **Sit upright** but not stiffly. Lean slightly forward to show engagement.
- **Speak clearly.** If you tend to speak quickly, slow down.

### Punctuality

**Arrive 30 minutes early.** Use the extra time to review your notes, observe the office environment, and compose yourself. Being late is disqualifying in most offices. If unforeseen circumstances delay you, call the contact number on the interview notice immediately.

### Phone

**Silent mode, not vibrate.** Do not check your phone during the interview. If it rings, ignore it. Checking your phone tells the panel that something else is more important than this interview.

## 9.7 After the Interview -- What Happens Next

Understanding the post-interview process helps you manage expectations and plan your next steps.

### Step 1: HRMPSB Deliberation

After all candidates have been interviewed, the panel consolidates individual scores into a **Comparative Assessment ranking**. Each candidate receives a total score based on the five criteria:

- Education (25%)
- Experience (25%)
- Training (15%)
- Eligibility (10%)
- Interview (25%)

### Step 2: Shortlist Submission

The HRMPSB submits the **top 3-5 candidates** to the appointing authority. The appointing authority is typically:
- The **Minister** (for ministry positions)
- The **Secretary General** (for Parliament Secretariat positions)
- The **Executive Director** (for office/agency positions)
- The **Mayor** or **Governor** (for LGU positions)

### Step 3: Appointing Authority Decision

The appointing authority makes the final selection. They may or may not follow the HRMPSB ranking. The appointing authority has discretion, but the appointment must be justified and documented.

### Step 4: Appointment Letter

If selected, you receive an **appointment letter** specifying:
- Position title and plantilla item number
- Salary grade and monthly salary
- Effectivity date (when you start)
- Type of appointment (permanent, temporary, coterminous)
- Reporting instructions

### Step 5: Oath of Office

Before you begin work, you take an **oath of office**. This is usually administered by the appointing authority or an authorized officer. The oath commits you to faithfully discharge your duties and uphold the Constitution and the Bangsamoro Organic Law.

### Step 6: Pre-Employment Requirements

After appointment but before starting work, you typically need to:
- Complete pre-employment medical examination
- Open a government bank account (for salary disbursement)
- Register with GSIS, PhilHealth, and Pag-IBIG (if not yet registered)
- Submit additional documentary requirements as specified by the HR office

### Timeline

Expect **2-8 weeks** from interview to appointment. Some offices move quickly (2-3 weeks). Others take longer due to administrative processes. This is normal.

### If Not Selected

You will receive a **regret letter**. This is not a failure. Consider the following:

- **You may request feedback** from the HRMPSB. Ask what competencies to develop. Frame it as professional development, not a complaint.
- **Apply again.** Most successful government workers were not hired on their first attempt. Each interview is practice for the next one.
- **Update your tracker.** Note what went well and what you would do differently. Use this to prepare for your next opportunity.
- **Build your weak areas.** If the feedback points to experience gaps, look for COS or volunteer opportunities. If it points to training gaps, attend seminars. If it points to interview performance, practice with a friend using the mock interview tool at the end of this chapter.

## 9.8 The Appointment Types You May Receive

Understanding what each appointment type means helps you make informed decisions if you receive an offer.

| Type | What It Means | Security of Tenure | Benefits |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Permanent**[^13] | You met all qualification standards. You hold a plantilla position. | Full tenure -- you can only be removed through a formal administrative process | Full: GSIS, PhilHealth, Pag-IBIG, leave credits, PBB, step increments |
| **Temporary**[^14] | You met all QS except eligibility. Given up to 2 years to pass the CSC exam. | Limited -- appointment expires if you do not obtain eligibility within the period | Full benefits while appointed |
| **Coterminous**[^17] | Your appointment is tied to the appointing authority's term (common in political staff positions) | Ends when the appointing authority leaves office | Full benefits while appointed |
| **COS**[^18] | Contract of Service -- project-based, no plantilla position | None -- contract can be terminated at the end of the period | Limited -- typically no GSIS or leave credits |
| **JO** | Job Order -- task-based, no employer-employee relationship | None | None |

**For fresh graduates:** A **permanent appointment** is the gold standard. A **temporary appointment** is excellent -- you have up to 2 years to pass the CSC exam while enjoying full benefits and building experience. COS is a strong stepping stone if plantilla positions are not yet available.

## Key Takeaways

- The interview is a **Competency-Based Interview (CBI)** with a panel of 3-5 HRMPSB members. It is worth **25% of your total score**.
- Use the **STAR method** (Situation, Task, Action, Result) for every competency question. Be specific. Use "I" not "we." Quantify results.
- Expect three types of questions: **governance awareness** (BOL, BDP, BAA 13, moral governance), **competency questions** (STAR format), and **BARMM-specific questions** (motivation, cultural fit, transition contribution).
- **Fresh graduates compensate** for limited experience through preparation (read the BOL, BDP, BAA 13), reframing academic work as professional competence, and demonstrating learning speed.
- **Cultural appropriateness** matters: dress in business formal, greet with "Assalamu Alaikum" or "Good morning," address panelists respectfully, arrive 30 minutes early, keep your phone silent.
- After the interview: the HRMPSB ranks candidates, submits the top 3-5 to the appointing authority, and the authority makes the final decision. Expect **2-8 weeks** from interview to appointment.
- **If not selected**: request feedback, build your weak areas, and apply again. Most successful government workers were not hired on their first attempt.
- Complete the **Interview Preparation Worksheet** before every interview. It is 30 minutes of preparation that can determine the outcome.
