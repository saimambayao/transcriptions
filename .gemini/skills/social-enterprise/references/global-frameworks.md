## EU/OECD Definition

### Three Dimensions of Social Enterprise

| Dimension | Description | Indicators |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Entrepreneurial** | Continuous economic activity | Revenue from sales, paid staff, market orientation |
| **Social** | Explicit social purpose | Mission statement, beneficiary focus, community benefit |
| **Governance** | Inclusive ownership/control | Stakeholder participation, profit distribution limits, asset lock |

### EU Operational Criteria

1. Engaged in economic activity (producing goods/services)
2. Explicit social aim as primary objective
3. Limits on profit distribution (reinvestment priority)
4. Organizationally independent from government
5. Inclusive governance (participatory or democratic)

**Source**: European Commission Social Economy Gateway

## B-Corp Certification

### Foundation Requirements (Mandatory)

1. **Legal Incorporation**: 12+ months in operation
2. **Stakeholder Governance**: Adopt B Corp Legal Requirement
3. **Risk Assessment**: Complete B Lab risk profiling tool

### Seven Impact Topics

| Topic | Focus Areas |
|-------|-------------|
| **Purpose & Stakeholder Governance** | Mission alignment, inclusive decision-making |
| **Fair Work** | Quality employment, positive workplace culture |
| **JEDI** | Justice, Equity, Diversity, Inclusion |
| **Human Rights** | Dignified treatment, rights respect |
| **Climate Action** | Climate crisis combat, impact mitigation |
| **Environmental Stewardship** | Minimize negative, pursue positive outcomes |
| **Collective Action** | Lead toward equitable, regenerative systems |

### Certification Requirements

- 20-124 specific requirements (based on size/sector)
- Continuous improvement over 5 years (Year 0, 3, 5 phases)
- Annual fee based on revenue
- Recertification every 3 years

**Excluded Industries**: Firearms, weapons, tobacco, recreational marijuana, gambling, >5% fossil fuel services

**Source**: B Lab Standards 2024-2025

## Social Business Model Canvas

### 14 Components

| # | Component | Description | Key Questions |
|---|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | **Social Mission** | Target group + action + change | Who do we serve? What change do we create? |
| 2 | **Social Value Proposition** | Offerings for beneficiaries/customers/funders | What unique value do we provide? |
| 3 | **Key Stakeholders** | People/orgs interacting with SE | Who influences our success? |
| 4 | **Channels** | Delivery mechanisms | How do we reach beneficiaries? |
| 5 | **Relationships** | Connection-building methods | How do we maintain engagement? |
| 6 | **Activities** | Critical actions for delivery | What must we do excellently? |
| 7 | **Resources** | Key assets needed | What assets are essential? |
| 8 | **Key Delivery Partners** | Supporting organizations | Who helps us deliver? |
| 9 | **Social Impact Measurement** | Assessment strategy | How do we know we're succeeding? |
| 10 | **Costs** | Program, operations, fundraising | What are our major expenses? |
| 11 | **Revenue** | Grants, donations, earned income | Where does money come from? |
| 12 | **Surplus** | Profit redistribution strategy | What happens to excess funds? |
| 13 | **Competition/Coopetition** | Similar organizations | Who else serves this need? |
| 14 | **Macro-Environment (PESTEL)** | External factors | What forces affect us? |

**Key Difference from Traditional BMC**: Social mission as central organizing principle, not profit.

**Source**: Swarthmore Social Business Model Canvas, Tandemic

## Theory of Change

### Six Core Components

```
INPUTS → ACTIVITIES → OUTPUTS → OUTCOMES → IMPACT
                         ↑
                   ASSUMPTIONS & CONTEXT
```

| Component | Definition | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| **Inputs** | Resources invested | Funding, staff, materials, partnerships |
| **Activities** | Actions taken | Training, product development, outreach |
| **Outputs** | Direct products (effort) | 200 people trained, 50 products sold |
| **Outcomes** | Changes in behavior/conditions | 143 gained job-ready skills |
| **Impact** | Long-term systemic change | Reduced unemployment in community |
| **Assumptions** | What must be true | Market exists, beneficiaries will engage |

### Key Distinction

- **Outputs** = Effort (what you did)
- **Outcomes** = Transformation (what changed)

### Five-Step Development Process

1. **Define stakeholder-centered outcomes** - Focus on beneficiary change
2. **Design data architecture first** - Build measurement before finalizing theory
3. **Integrate qualitative and quantitative** - Numbers + narratives
4. **Build continuous learning cycles** - Real-time insights, not annual reports
5. **Evolve with evidence** - Theory as living system

**Source**: Sopact, NPC, IDinsight

## IRIS+ Impact Measurement

### Five Dimensions of Impact

| Dimension | Question | Metrics Focus |
|-----------|----------|---------------|
| **What** | What outcomes matter? | Outcome categories, SDG alignment |
| **Who** | Who experiences change? | Beneficiary demographics, underserved status |
| **How Much** | Scale, depth, duration? | Number reached, intensity, time period |
| **Contribution** | What's our unique role? | Additionality, counterfactual |
| **Risk** | What could go wrong? | Evidence quality, execution risk |

### IRIS+ Core Metrics Sets

Pre-defined metric packages for common impact themes:
- Financial Inclusion
- Quality Jobs
- Affordable Housing
- Clean Energy
- Sustainable Agriculture
- Health & Wellness
- Education

### Framework Alignment

IRIS+ aligns with 50+ frameworks:
- Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)
- UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)
- Impact Management Project (IMP)
- B Impact Assessment

**Source**: GIIN (Global Impact Investing Network)

## SROI (Social Return on Investment)

### Definition

Methodology measuring value that doesn't have easy financial metrics (environmental, social benefits).

### SROI Ratio

```
SROI = (Social Value Created - Investment) / Investment
```

Example: SROI of 3:1 means every $1 invested creates $3 in social value.

### Seven Principles

1. **Involve stakeholders** - Understand change from their perspective
2. **Understand what changes** - Articulate outcomes clearly
3. **Value things that matter** - Use financial proxies
4. **Only include what is material** - Focus on significant outcomes
5. **Do not over-claim** - Account for attribution, displacement
6. **Be transparent** - Document methodology and assumptions
7. **Verify the result** - Independent assurance

### Process Steps

1. Establish scope and identify stakeholders
2. Map outcomes through stakeholder engagement
3. Evidence outcomes and give them value
4. Establish impact (deadweight, attribution, displacement)
5. Calculate SROI
6. Report, use, embed

**Source**: Social Value International, SROI Network
