## Needfinding Fundamentals

### Core Principle

Needfinding is about discovering real user needs through direct observation and conversation. It cannot be skipped because it spans three interconnected phases: **Understand**, **Observe**, and **Define Point of View**.

### Key Insight

The decisive point in needfinding is: **Find out which assumptions you have made in your mind and become aware of them.**

### Self-Reflection Questions

Before needfinding, ask yourself:

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| When was the last time we "walked in our customer's shoes"? | Check proximity to users |
| When was the last time we mastered the daily grind at exactly the spot where our users are standing? | Validate real-world experience |
| How do we know what our customers have difficulties with? | Identify assumption gaps |
| What are the reasons that our customers are happy? | Understand positive drivers |
| What triggered the Wow! effect when they experienced our product? | Identify delight factors |

### Integrating Needfinding into Daily Life

You don't need to take time off work. Integrate needfinding into everyday situations:
- Shopping lines
- Public transport
- Bus stops
- Community events
- Any place where target users naturally gather

## Critical Assumptions Method

A 5-step process to identify and validate assumptions before building solutions.

### Step 1: Formulate Your Idea

Write your idea in one sentence and visualize it.

**Example**: "Senior walks for retired 'couch potatoes.'"

### Step 2: Formulate Need Assumptions

Identify what motivates users and what prevents them from acting.

**Key Questions**:
- What does the user want to achieve by applying our idea?
- What motivates the user to use our idea?
- What prevents the user from using our idea?

**Example Assumptions**:
1. Couch potatoes want to exercise (need) to prevent chronic diseases (need)
2. Retirees don't have the necessary daily structure (trigger) to exercise regularly (need)
3. Senior citizens want to feel healthy (need) so they can go on excursions with grandchildren
4. Senior citizens feel uncomfortable (emotional state/blocker) exercising with young people

*Write each assumption on a separate Post-it.*

### Step 3: Identify Critical Assumptions

Reflect on which assumptions you know least about and which are most decisive for your idea.

**Warning Signs**:
- Built solution on a "mental castle in the air"
- Only validated with friends/colleagues who won't dampen enthusiasm
- Haven't observed real users in their natural environment

### Step 4: Be Ready for Random Encounters

Prepare questions for chance meetings with target users:
- Where can you meet users during the week?
- What opening question would prompt stories about their everyday life?
- What follow-up questions would reveal hidden needs?

### Step 5: Prioritize Using the Matrix

```
                    DECISIVE
                        |
     +------------------+------------------+
     |                  |                  |
     |   Review Last    |   Review First   |
     |                  |                  |
KNOWING ----------------+---------------- IGNORANT
     |                  |                  |
     |   Skip for Now   |   Review Second  |
     |                  |                  |
     +------------------+------------------+
                        |
                    INCIDENTAL
```

**Priority Order**:
1. **Review First**: Decisive + Ignorant (highest risk)
2. **Review Second**: Incidental + Ignorant
3. **Review Last**: Decisive + Knowing
4. **Skip for Now**: Incidental + Knowing

## Needfinding Interview Protocol

A 6-phase structure for conducting effective needfinding interviews.

### Phase 1: Introduction

**Purpose**: Create comfort and establish trust.

**Actions**:
- Introduce yourself and explain the reason for the interview
- Explain the course of the interview
- Emphasize there is no "true" or "false"
- Ask permission to document (video, photos, audio)
- Make respondents feel appreciated
- Communicate that their knowledge and experience are valuable

### Phase 2: Actual Beginning

**Purpose**: Establish connection to the problem space.

**Actions**:
- Allow interviewees to introduce themselves
- Start with a general, open question about the theme
- Based on answers, go deeper with clarifying questions
- Focus on making them feel comfortable and winning trust

### Phase 3: Create Reference

**Purpose**: Anchor the conversation in specific experiences.

**Actions**:
- Find a recent example the person remembers well
- Bring them closer to the topic and problems
- Be patient if not all problems surface immediately
- Continue building trust
- Assure them their answers are important and helpful
- Ask for more experiences and stories until desired depth is reached

### Phase 4: Grand Tour

**Purpose**: Explore deeply and find hidden insights.

**Actions**:
- Deepen critical topics
- Search for contradictions
- Get to the bottom of details (tangible and emotional)
- Goal: Things that were hidden come to light
- When trust is established, they share needs that would remain hidden in normal interviews

### Phase 5: Reflection

**Purpose**: Synthesize and validate understanding.

**Actions**:
- Pause and express gratitude for important findings
- Summarize main points from your perspective
- Listen as they add things, point out inconsistencies, emphasize items
- Ask "why" to dig deeper if necessary
- Optionally switch to more general level for theories and explanations

### Phase 6: Wrap-up

**Purpose**: Capture final insights and close gracefully.

**Critical**: Don't turn off the recording device! Often the most intriguing things occur at the very end.

**Actions**:
- Thank them for the conversation, time, and insights
- Give them opportunity to ask questions
- After interview, reflect by summarizing most important findings
- Document both content and approach insights

## Question Maps

A non-linear interview preparation tool that mirrors natural conversation flow.

### Why Question Maps vs Questionnaires?

| Questionnaire | Question Map |
|---------------|--------------|
| Linear structure | Topical islands |
| Top-to-bottom | Ad-hoc navigation |
| Fixed sequence | Conversation-guided |
| Rigid | Flexible |

### Building a Question Map

1. **Center**: Place main topic (e.g., "Seniors")
2. **Create Islands**: Theme clusters around center
3. **Add Questions**: Specific questions within each island
4. **Connect**: Draw relationships between islands

### Example: Seniors Health Project

```
                    +-------------+
                    |  Nutrition  |
                    |  - Changes  |
                    |  - Rhythm   |
                    +------+------+
                           |
        +-------------+    |    +-----------------+
        | Being Happy |----+----| Life in Old Age |
        | - Meaning   |    |    | - What changes? |
        +-------------+    |    | - What's hard?  |
                           |    +-----------------+
                    +------+------+
                    |   SENIORS   |
                    +------+------+
                           |
                    +------+------+
                    | Sports in   |
                    |   Old Age   |
                    | - Motivation|
                    +-------------+
```

### Enriching the Map

After each interview:
- Add supplementary questions that arose
- Note new situations encountered
- Document "situation-inspired ideation" (ideas that emerge during needfinding)
- Extend your question horizon

## Open Questions Library

Organized by purpose to help prepare interviews.

### Tracing Behaviors

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "Why do you smile when you say that?" | Uncover hidden emotions |
| "How did it happen that...?" | Trace origins |
| "Who has taught you that?" | Understand influences |
| "How do you know how it works?" | Reveal knowledge sources |
| "What works?" / "What doesn't work?" | Identify pain points |

### Gaining Clarity

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "What exactly do you mean by...?" | Clarify ambiguous terms |
| "How would you describe it in your own words?" | Get authentic language |

### Exploring Actively

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "You say this is difficult. What exactly was/is difficult about it?" | Dig into challenges |
| "A difficult task. Why exactly is it difficult for you?" | Understand personal context |

### Asking About Sequence

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "What is your first memory of...?" | Find origin stories |
| "What happened before/afterward?" | Map sequence |
| "How did you do it before?" | Understand evolution |
| "When was the first/last time that you...?" | Identify frequency patterns |

### Asking for Examples

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "What was the last app you downloaded?" | Get concrete examples |
| "With whom did you discuss it?" | Map social context |

### Exploring Exceptions

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "When didn't it work, then?" | Find failure points |
| "Did you have problems with...before?" | Discover history |

### Understanding Connections

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "How do you communicate with...?" | Map communication patterns |
| "From whom did you hear that?" | Trace information sources |
| "Who helped you with it?" | Identify support network |
| "How did you hear of it?" | Understand discovery channels |

### Informing Outsiders (Simplification Technique)

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "If you had to explain it to an exchange student, what would you say?" | Get simplified explanation |
| "How would you explain this to your grandparents?" | Test for jargon |
| "How would you describe it to a small child?" | Reveal core concept |

### Comparing Processes

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "What is the difference between your home and that of your friend?" | Identify variations |
| "What is the difference when you do this on the road instead of at home?" | Map context differences |

### Imagining the Future

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| "How do you think you'll do it in 2030?" | Project future behavior |
| (Follow-up: "What if it were like that already today?") | Bridge to present |

## The 6 WH Questions

A systematic approach for comprehensive understanding.

### The Question Matrix

| What | Who | Why | Where | When | How |
|------|-----|-----|-------|------|-----|
| What is the problem? | Who is involved? | Why is the problem important? | Where does the problem occur? | When did the problem begin? | How could this problem be an opportunity? |
| What would we like to know? | Who is affected by the situation? | Why does it occur? | Where was it already resolved before? | When do people want to see results? | How could it be solved? |
| What are the assumptions that are scrutinized? | Who decides? | Why was it not yet solved? | Where did similar situations exist? | When can the project be started? | What has already been tried to resolve the problem? |

### 7-Step Application Process

1. Create a set of WH questions
2. Make a list of possible sub-questions
3. Try to answer all WH questions
4. If a WH question doesn't make sense in context, skip it
5. In problem interviews, dig deeper by probing and repeating questions
6. Find more than one answer to every question (conflicting answers are particularly interesting)
7. Evaluate answers only at the end; filter by relevance to solution

## Lead User Method

Involving trend-leading users as innovation partners.

### What Are Lead Users?

Users who:
- Have needs and requirements earlier than the mass market
- Hope for particularly high benefit from solutions
- Have strong drive to solve problems they face
- Often create interim solutions or prototypes themselves

**Famous Lead User Innovations**: Mountain bike, hyperlink structure of WWW, GEOX shoes

### 3-Step Lead User Process

#### Step 1: Identify Needs and Trends

- Scan secondary sources (future researchers, trend reports, trend scouting)
- Identify market experts and technology experts
- Determine important early trends and future needs

#### Step 2: Search for Lead Users and Lead Experts

- Search in the target market
- Identify analogous markets by abstracting your questions
- Transfer findings to your context

#### Step 3: Develop Solution Concepts

- Conduct large workshop with lead users, lead experts, internal marketing, and technicians
- Develop rudimentary ideas into strong innovation concepts
- Involve lead users heavily in development and prototype process (co-creation)

### Warning: Mind the Gap

Be aware of the gap between:
- Lead users / Early adopters
- Early majority

**Risk**: Focusing too much on lead users can create a "white elephant" - high risk, low implementation likelihood, hard to stop, with few actual customers.

**Best Practice**: Always recognize the needs of "normal" customers alongside lead users.

## Digging Deeper Techniques

Methods to uncover hidden needs and real motivations.

### Core Principle

Take a look behind the scenes. Dig deeper when searching for real needs!

### Technique 1: Listen for Stories

Listen attentively to personal stories and experiences. These reveal authentic needs better than abstract answers.

### Technique 2: Differentiate Needs from Solutions

If you already have a solution in mind, find the problem and the need for the solution.

```
Need → One solution → Problem → Alternative solutions
```

### Technique 3: Find Workarounds

Search for the workarounds, remedies, and quick fixes users have created. These signal unmet needs.

### Technique 4: Spot Contradictions

Recognize contradictions between what interviewees say and what they actually do.

**Example**: "I cannot walk on stilts!" (said while walking on stilts)

### Technique 5: Set Aside Your Own Experiences

Forget your own problems and wishes. Your needs are not their needs.

## Building Empathy

Techniques for developing deep user understanding.

### The Three Keys to Empathy

#### 1. Understand the Language of the Customer

**Method**:
- Listen actively
- Ask about words that can be understood differently
- If you see conspicuous body language (e.g., eye rolling while saying "incredibly exciting"), dig deeper
- Ask: "We could see how you rolled your eyes. What did you mean?"

**Result**: Better understanding of customer language leads to better understanding of needs.

#### 2. Experience the World of the Customer

**Method**:
- Adopt the user's perspective
- Go to their authentic environment
- Recognize that your thought patterns can inhibit empathy
- Experience the world through their eyes

#### 3. The Pollo Effect (Open Mind)

**Concept**: Dare to ask naive questions. "Pollo" is Spanish for chicken - nobody wants to be the chicken, but it's necessary for immersion.

**Method**:
- Imagine you are an alien, an apprentice from a strange galaxy
- Everything is new and inexplicable
- As non-threatening extraterrestrial beings, ask questions in an unbiased way
- This curiosity motivates users to share more

### 10 Steps to Empathetic Needfinding

1. **Change perspective**: Observe the world from the other side
2. **Give full attention**: Be mindful, present, and precise
3. **Listen actively**: Use looks, gestures, facial expressions to be present
4. **Reflect on behavior**: How do I come across to other people?
5. **Read signals**: What do facial expressions, gestures, and voice tell you?
6. **Question your readiness**: Is my opinion without prejudice?
7. **Ask open questions**: How might the future look?
8. **Explore feelings and needs**: How do you feel today?
9. **Express your own feelings**: "I wish..."
10. **Act with empathy**: How can I help?

### Mindfulness as Foundation

Empathy requires mindfulness:
- Focus attention purposefully
- Be more precise with perception
- Be more present
- Focus on the current moment
- Involve all senses
- Perceive without prejudice

**Barriers to Empathy**:
- Stressful everyday life
- Pressure to succeed
- Hectic situations
- Being wiped out
- Emotional states (anger, rage, fear)
- Unfilled needs
- Unconscious beliefs
- Prejudices and evaluation patterns

### Post-Interview Reflection

After each interview and observation, ask:

1. Where did the person reveal the biggest problems?
2. What is the need behind the problem?
3. What innovation would make everyday life easier for this person?

## Key Learnings Summary

1. **Find the persona in real life** and interview them
2. **Forget initial assumptions** and focus on user behavior
3. **Observe and listen carefully** in conversation
4. **Document exactly** to correct assumptions later
5. **Walk in the user's footsteps** - accompany them for a day
6. **Identify extreme users** (e.g., seniors who do strenuous sports)
7. **Review iteratively** with other users; never stop being curious
8. **Plan interviews diligently**; create a question map
9. **Ask many WH questions**; pay attention to contradictions
10. **Pay attention to the end** - it often yields important insights
11. **Use lead users** to recognize future needs earlier
12. **Take a look behind the scenes**; combine various methods
