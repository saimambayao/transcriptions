## User Profile Canvas

A structured canvas for creating comprehensive personas.

### Canvas Components

| Section | Key Questions |
|---------|---------------|
| **Name** | Who is this persona? |
| **Persona Description** | Age, gender, location, marital status, hobbies, education, position, social environment, way of thinking |
| **Jobs-to-be-Done** | What task does the product support? What are the goals? Why does it make sense? |
| **Use Cases** | How, where, and by whom is the product used? What happens before/after? How does the customer get information? |
| **Gains** | How do current products make the customer happy? |
| **Pains** | What causes bad feelings? What are the worries? |

### The "Persona Twins" Warning

Brief descriptions can be deceiving. Example: Prince Charles and Ozzy Osbourne share identical brief descriptions (born 1948, grew up in England, married twice, has children, successful, rich, takes vacations in the Alps, likes dogs) yet are completely different users.

**Key Insight**: Always dig one level deeper to understand needs in greater detail.

### Creating Personas - 10-Step Process

1. **Find the User**: Quantitative data collection, AEIOU method
2. **Build Hypothesis**: Describe groups of similar users/segmentations
3. **Confirm**: Gather data/evidence, use empathy maps
4. **Find Patterns**: Categorization, apply Jobs-to-be-Done
5. **Create Personas**: Detailed persona descriptions
6. **Define Situations**: Use cases, customer journey, user profile canvas
7. **Validate**: Interview people who know the personas
8. **Disseminate Knowledge**: Posters, meetings, campaigns, videos
9. **Create Scenarios**: Narrative storytelling, hook canvas
10. **Continuous Development**: Usability tests, revisions

## Jobs-to-be-Done Framework

Focus on customer behavior and needs rather than product features.

### Core Concept

Ask: **Why would customers buy my product or service?** Not what features it has.

### The Milkshake Example

**Problem**: How to increase milkshake sales by 15%?

**Conventional Approach**: Change toppings, flavors, cup sizes (incremental innovation)

**Jobs-to-be-Done Approach**: Discovered two customer types:

| Customer Type | Time | Situation | Jobs-to-be-Done | Ideal Product |
|---------------|------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Morning Commuter** | AM | Driving to work | Breakfast substitute + driving diversion | Large, nutritious, thick |
| **Afternoon Parent** | PM | With whining child | Satisfy child + make mother feel good | Small, thin, low-calorie |

### Digital Jobs-to-be-Done Pattern

```
When [situation]
I want to [action]
so I can [outcome]
```

**Example - Adobe Photoshop**:
- When I take a photograph with my digital camera
- I want to be able to edit it like a professional
- so I can show perfect pictures

**Example - Instagram**:
- When I take a photograph with my cell phone
- I want to edit it easily and simply
- so I can share it quickly with my friends

## Hook Framework

Based on Alex Cowan's work on habit-forming products and services.

### The Hook Canvas - 5 Components

```
[External Trigger] ──> [Internal Trigger] ──> [Action] ──> [Variable Reward] ──> [Investment]
        │                                                                              │
        └──────────────────────────<─── LOOP ───<──────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Component Details

| Component | Key Questions |
|-----------|---------------|
| **1. External Trigger** | What relevant triggers exist for your personas? What external events prompt product use? |
| **2. Internal Trigger** | What does the user want? What existing action triggers are generally valid? How can we replicate user actions? |
| **3. Action** | What is the simplest action for reward? Have we minimized effort to perform the action? |
| **4. Variable Reward** | How is the user rewarded? Does the reward develop beyond the original goal? Is there community reward? |
| **5. Investment** | How does the persona release the next action? What possibilities close the loop better? |

### Examples

| Product | External Trigger | Internal Trigger | Action | Reward | Investment |
|---------|-----------------|------------------|--------|--------|------------|
| **Tinder** | "Super Like" notification | Loneliness, curiosity | Swipe | Match, connection | Profile building |
| **Facebook** | Friend post notification | Boredom, FOMO | Check feed | Social validation | Following, comments |

## Future User Method

For radical innovation projects with 10+ year horizons.

### Concept

If product launches in 10 years targeting 30-40 year olds, study today's 20-30 year olds.

### 5-Step Process

1. **Describe Current Target Customer** (e.g., Stefan, 34 in 2018)
2. **Research 12 Years Ago**: What lifestyle, values, decisions did they have at 22?
3. **Study Today's Equivalent Age**: Describe future user who exists today (e.g., Lukas, 22 in 2018)
4. **Compare Both at Same Age**: What changed between Stefan at 22 and Lukas at 22?
5. **Extrapolate**: Project future user to target age (Lukas at 34 in 2030)

### Key Factors to Track

- Changes in values
- Lifestyle evolution
- Technology/media usage
- Product habits
- Communication patterns

## AEIOU Observation Method

Structured framework for field observations. Captures both real world and digital behavior.

### The Five Categories

| Letter | Category | Key Questions |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **A** | Activities | What happens? What are people doing? What tasks? What before/after? |
| **E** | Environment | What does the environment look like? Nature and function of the space? |
| **I** | Interaction | How do systems interact? Are there interfaces? How do users interact? |
| **O** | Objects | What objects/devices are used? Who uses them? In which environment? |
| **U** | User | Who are the users? What roles? Who influences them? |

### Application Tips

- Adapt questions to specific observations
- Have experienced facilitator accompany first contact
- Consider both physical and digital environments
- Document with photos/videos (with permission)

## Critical Assumptions Matrix

For validating assumptions before building solutions.

### The Matrix

```
                    DECISIVE
                        │
     ┌──────────────────┼──────────────────┐
     │                  │                  │
     │   Review Last    │   Review First   │
     │                  │                  │
KNOWING ────────────────┼──────────────── IGNORANT
     │                  │                  │
     │   Skip for Now   │   Review Second  │
     │                  │                  │
     └──────────────────┼──────────────────┘
                        │
                    INCIDENTAL
```

### Process

1. **Formulate idea in one sentence**
2. **List need assumptions**: What does user want to achieve? What motivates use? What prevents use?
3. **Identify critical assumptions**: What do we know least about? What's most decisive for our idea?
4. **Prioritize for review**: Test decisive + ignorant assumptions first
5. **Prepare for random encounters**: Be ready to validate with real users

### Need Assumption Examples

For "Senior Walks" health app for retirees:
- Couch potatoes want to exercise to prevent chronic diseases
- Retirees lack daily structure for regular exercise
- Seniors feel uncomfortable exercising with young people
- Seniors want to feel healthy to enjoy time with grandchildren

## Design Brief Template

Structured project description before starting design work.

### Design Brief Components

| Section | Contents |
|---------|----------|
| **Why?** | Problem description |
| **Who?** | Target customer/user |
| **What?** | Goals to achieve |
| **With what?** | Available materials, resources |
| **Who else?** | Competition, alternatives |
| **How much?** | Budget, restrictions |
| **When?** | Schedule, scenarios |
| **How?** | Next steps |

### Key Elements

**Definition of design space and scope**:
- Which activities to support and for whom?
- What do we want to learn about the user?

**Existing approaches**:
- What already exists? How can elements help?
- What is missing in existing solutions?

**Design principles**:
- Where is more creativity demanded?
- What limitations exist? Core functions?
- Whom to involve at what point?

**Scenarios**:
- What does desirable future look like?
- Which scenarios are plausible?

**Implementation challenges**:
- Who must be involved early?
- What is the culture for radical proposals?

## Question Maps

Non-linear interview preparation tool.

### Why Question Maps vs Questionnaires?

| Questionnaire | Question Map |
|---------------|--------------|
| Linear structure | Topical islands |
| Top-to-bottom | Ad-hoc navigation |
| Fixed sequence | Conversation-guided |
| Rigid | Flexible |

### Building a Question Map

1. **Center**: Place main topic (e.g., "Seniors")
2. **Create Islands**: Theme clusters around center
3. **Add Questions**: Specific questions within each island
4. **Connect**: Draw relationships between islands

### Example: Seniors Health Project

```
                    ┌─────────────┐
                    │  Nutrition  │
                    │  - Changes  │
                    │  - Rhythm   │
                    └──────┬──────┘
                           │
        ┌─────────────┐    │    ┌─────────────────┐
        │ Being Happy │────┼────│  Life in Old    │
        │ - Meaning   │    │    │    Age          │
        └─────────────┘    │    │  - What changes?│
                           │    │  - What's hard? │
                    ┌──────┴──────┐    └─────────────┘
                    │   SENIORS   │
                    └──────┬──────┘
                           │
                    ┌──────┴──────┐
                    │ Sports in   │
                    │   Old Age   │
                    │ - Motivation│
                    └─────────────┘
```

### Enriching the Map

After each interview:
- Add supplementary questions that arose
- Note new situations encountered
- Document "situation-inspired ideation"
