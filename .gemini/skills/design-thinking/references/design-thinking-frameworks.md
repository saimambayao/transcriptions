## Problem Types

Understanding the type of problem you're facing determines which approach to use.

### Three Categories of Problems

| Type | Characteristics | Approach |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| **Well-Defined** | Clear problem, known solution path | Standard engineering, analytical methods |
| **Ill-Defined** | Unclear problem, multiple possible solutions | Design thinking, iterative exploration |
| **Wicked** | Interconnected, no clear definition, no stopping rule | Systemic design, multi-stakeholder collaboration |

### Well-Defined Problems

- **Characteristics**: Clear parameters, known constraints, measurable outcomes
- **Examples**: Optimizing delivery routes, fixing a software bug, balancing a budget
- **Approach**: Traditional problem-solving, analysis, optimization

### Ill-Defined Problems

- **Characteristics**: Ambiguous requirements, multiple valid solutions, user needs unclear
- **Examples**: "How might we improve customer experience?", new product design, service innovation
- **Approach**: Design thinking—empathize first, then define, ideate, prototype, test

### Wicked Problems

**Characteristics** (from Rittel & Webber):
1. No definitive formulation—problem changes as you understand it
2. No stopping rule—always room for improvement
3. Solutions are not true/false but good/bad
4. No immediate test—consequences unfold over time
5. Every attempt counts—can't easily undo interventions
6. No enumerable set of solutions
7. Every wicked problem is unique
8. Can be considered symptom of another problem
9. Discrepancies can be explained numerous ways
10. Planner has no right to be wrong—social accountability

**Examples**: Climate change, poverty, education reform, healthcare access

**Approach**: Systems thinking, collaborative design, continuous iteration, stakeholder engagement

### Problem Type Decision Guide

```
Is the problem clearly defined with known solution paths?
│
├── YES → Well-Defined Problem → Use analytical methods
│
└── NO → Are stakeholders in agreement about the problem?
         │
         ├── YES → Ill-Defined Problem → Use design thinking
         │
         └── NO → Wicked Problem → Use systemic design + DT
```

## Divergent and Convergent Thinking

The foundation of design thinking is the rhythm between expanding possibilities and making decisions.

### Two Modes of Thinking

| Mode | Purpose | Mindset | Activities |
|------|---------|---------|------------|
| **Divergent** | Create options | Expansive, generative | Brainstorm, explore, "yes and..." |
| **Convergent** | Make choices | Analytical, selective | Evaluate, decide, prioritize |

### Divergent Thinking

**Goal**: Generate maximum options without judgment

**Rules**:
- Defer judgment—no criticism
- Encourage wild ideas
- Build on others' ideas
- Go for quantity
- Be visual

**Key Insight**: The goal is volume, not quality. Quality comes later through convergent selection.

### Convergent Thinking

**Goal**: Select the best options through analysis

**Activities**:
- Dot voting
- Decision matrices
- Feasibility assessment
- Stakeholder alignment

**Key Insight**: Convergence requires explicit criteria and transparent decision-making.

### The Groan Zone

**The critical transition between divergent and convergent thinking.**

```
DIVERGENT          GROAN ZONE           CONVERGENT
(Expanding)        (Transition)         (Focusing)

   \              ↓↓↓↓↓↓↓                 /
    \            Discomfort              /
     \           Confusion              /
      \          Frustration           /
       \         Breakthrough!        /
        \           ↓↓↓↓            /
         \_________________________/
```

**What happens in the Groan Zone**:
- Ideas seem incompatible
- Team feels stuck
- Pressure to "just pick something"
- Discomfort is highest

**Why it matters**: The groan zone is where breakthroughs happen. Rushing through it produces mediocre solutions. Staying with the discomfort allows novel combinations to emerge.

**How to navigate**:
1. **Acknowledge it** - Name the phase, normalize discomfort
2. **Don't rush** - Give time for synthesis
3. **Use techniques** - Affinity mapping, clustering, forced connections
4. **Facilitate actively** - Help team find patterns, not impose solutions

### Rhythms of Diverge/Converge

| Phase | d.school | Double Diamond | Playbook Macro Cycle |
|-------|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Diverge 1** | Empathize | Discover | Initial Ideas → Benchmarking |
| **Converge 1** | Define | Define | Dark Horse selection |
| **Diverge 2** | Ideate | Develop | Funky Prototype exploration |
| **Converge 2** | Prototype/Test | Deliver | Vision Prototype → Final |

**Expert Tip**: Design thinking is not linear. You may need multiple diverge-converge cycles within a single phase, especially in ideation and prototyping.

## Stanford d.school 5-Phase Model

The most widely adopted design thinking framework, developed at Stanford's Hasso Plattner Institute of Design.

### The 5 Phases

| Phase | Purpose | Key Activities |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| **1. Empathize** | Understand users deeply | Field research, interviews, observation, immersion |
| **2. Define** | Frame the right problem | Synthesize insights, create POV statements, identify needs |
| **3. Ideate** | Generate solutions | Brainstorming, Crazy 8s, mind mapping, SCAMPER |
| **4. Prototype** | Build to think | Rapid prototyping, storyboards, role-playing, mockups |
| **5. Test** | Learn and refine | User testing, feedback sessions, iteration |

### Core Principles

1. **Human-Centered**: Start with empathy for users
2. **Bias Toward Action**: Build to learn, don't just discuss
3. **Radical Collaboration**: Diverse teams, multiple perspectives
4. **Embrace Experimentation**: Fail fast, learn quickly
5. **Show Don't Tell**: Communicate visually and tangibly

### When to Use

- New product or service development
- Improving existing experiences
- Solving complex, ill-defined problems
- Building team alignment around user needs

## Double Diamond Framework

Developed by the UK Design Council, this visual framework emphasizes divergent and convergent thinking.

### The 4 Phases

```
        DISCOVER          DEFINE           DEVELOP          DELIVER
       (Diverge)        (Converge)        (Diverge)       (Converge)

    \               /                 \               /
     \             /                   \             /
      \           /                     \           /
       \         /                       \         /
        \       /                         \       /
         \     /                           \     /
          \   /                             \   /
           \ /                               \ /
            V                                 V
      Problem Definition               Solution Delivery
```

| Phase | Focus | Activities |
|-------|-------|------------|
| **Discover** | Explore problem space | Stakeholder interviews, desk research, user observation |
| **Define** | Focus the challenge | Insights synthesis, problem framing, design brief |
| **Develop** | Explore solutions | Co-design, ideation, concept development, prototyping |
| **Deliver** | Implement solution | Testing, refinement, launch, evaluation |

### Key Insight

The "diamond" shape represents:
- **Divergence**: Opening up possibilities, exploring widely
- **Convergence**: Focusing in, making decisions

### When to Use

- Government and public sector innovation
- Service design projects
- Complex systemic challenges
- When visual process communication is needed

## IDEO 3-Phase Model

Tim Brown's streamlined approach emphasizing the intersection of desirability, feasibility, and viability.

### The 3 Phases

| Phase | Purpose | Key Questions |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| **Inspiration** | Understand context | What problems exist? What constraints apply? |
| **Ideation** | Generate options | What ideas solve the problem? How might we...? |
| **Implementation** | Execute solution | How do we build it? How do we scale it? |

### Three Lenses of Innovation

Every solution must balance:

```
           DESIRABILITY
          (Human Needs)
               /\
              /  \
             /    \
            /  DT  \
           /________\
          /          \
    FEASIBILITY ---- VIABILITY
    (Technology)    (Business)
```

| Lens | Question | Focus |
|------|----------|-------|
| **Desirability** | Do people want this? | Human needs, behaviors, emotions |
| **Feasibility** | Can we build this? | Technical possibilities, constraints |
| **Viability** | Should we build this? | Economic sustainability, business model |

### Five Characteristics of Design Thinkers

1. **Empathy** - Imagine the world from multiple perspectives
2. **Integrative Thinking** - See beyond obvious, explore alternatives
3. **Optimism** - Believe better solutions exist
4. **Experimentalism** - Pose questions, explore constraints
5. **Collaboration** - Work across disciplines

## Framework Comparison

| Aspect | d.school (5-Phase) | Double Diamond | IDEO (3-Phase) |
|--------|-------------------|----------------|----------------|
| **Phases** | 5 | 4 | 3 |
| **Focus** | Process steps | Diverge/converge | Innovation balance |
| **Best For** | Learning, workshops | Complex systems | Business innovation |
| **Iteration** | Between any phases | Within diamonds | Overlapping phases |
| **Origin** | Stanford University | UK Design Council | IDEO/Tim Brown |

### Choosing a Framework

| Context | Recommended Framework |
|---------|----------------------|
| Educational/training | d.school 5-Phase |
| Government/public sector | Double Diamond |
| Product innovation | IDEO 3-Phase |
| Social enterprise development | d.school or Double Diamond |
| Cooperative innovation | Double Diamond (stakeholder focus) |

## Design Thinking Mindset

Beyond methods and frameworks, design thinking requires specific mindsets and attitudes.

### Core Mindsets (from The Design Thinking Playbook)

| Mindset | Description | Practice |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| **Beginner's Mind** | Approach problems without preconceptions | Ask "why" like a child, question assumptions |
| **Empathy** | Deeply understand others' perspectives | Observe, listen, suspend judgment |
| **Radical Collaboration** | Diverse teams, shared ownership | Include stakeholders, cross-functional work |
| **Optimism** | Believe better solutions are possible | Focus on opportunities, not just constraints |
| **Bias to Action** | Learn by doing, not just discussing | Build to think, prototype early |
| **Embrace Ambiguity** | Tolerate uncertainty and complexity | Stay in the question, resist premature closure |

### The Five Characteristics of Design Thinkers

1. **Empathy** - Imagine the world from multiple perspectives
2. **Integrative Thinking** - See all salient aspects, explore alternatives beyond obvious
3. **Optimism** - Believe better solutions exist regardless of constraints
4. **Experimentalism** - Pose questions, explore constraints creatively
5. **Collaboration** - Cross-disciplinary enthusiasm

### Mindfulness in Design Thinking

**10 Steps to Empathetic Engagement**:

1. Change perspective
2. Give full attention
3. Listen actively
4. Reflect on your behavior
5. Read signals
6. Question your readiness for empathy
7. Ask open questions
8. Explore feelings and needs
9. Express your own feelings
10. Act with empathy

**Expert Tip**: Mindfulness practices enhance empathy capacity. Teams that practice active listening and presence generate deeper insights during user research.

### Common Mindset Traps

| Trap | Description | Remedy |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Solutioning too early** | Jumping to answers before understanding | Stay in empathy longer, ask "why" 5 times |
| **Falling in love with ideas** | Attachment to specific solutions | Test with users, embrace feedback |
| **Analysis paralysis** | Over-researching, not acting | Set timeboxes, prototype to learn |
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeking evidence that confirms beliefs | Actively seek disconfirming data |
| **Expert blindness** | Assuming you know what users need | Always test assumptions with real users |

## Theoretical Foundations

### Herbert Simon (1969)

"The Sciences of the Artificial" established design as a rigorous discipline:
- Design is about changing existing situations into preferred ones
- Design involves satisficing (good enough solutions) not optimizing
- Design thinking is a way of reasoning applicable beyond traditional design

### Donald Schon (1983)

"The Reflective Practitioner" introduced:
- Reflection-in-action: Learning while doing
- Knowing-in-action: Tacit knowledge in practice
- The importance of iteration and experimentation

### Key Academic Sources

| Author | Work | Contribution |
|--------|------|--------------|
| Herbert Simon | The Sciences of the Artificial (1969) | Design as decision science |
| Donald Schon | The Reflective Practitioner (1983) | Reflective practice theory |
| Tim Brown | Change by Design (2009) | Business application of DT |
| Nigel Cross | Design Thinking (2011) | Cognitive aspects of design |
| Roger Martin | The Design of Business (2009) | Design thinking for strategy |
