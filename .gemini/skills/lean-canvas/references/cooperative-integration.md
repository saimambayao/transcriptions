## How Cooperative Business Models Differ

Cooperative business models are fundamentally different from investor-owned enterprises. Understanding these differences is essential for effective business modeling.

| Aspect | Investor-Owned Enterprise | Cooperative Enterprise |
|--------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| **Ownership** | Shareholders (investors) | Members (users/workers) |
| **Purpose** | Maximize shareholder returns | Maximize member benefit |
| **Profit Distribution** | Dividends based on shares | Patronage refund based on usage (Art. 86, RA 9520) |
| **Governance** | One share, one vote | One member, one vote (democratic) |
| **Customer Relationship** | Customers are separate from owners | Members are BOTH owners AND customers |
| **Value Creation** | Capture value from customers | Create value FOR member-owners |
| **Community Role** | Optional CSR | Core principle (7th ICA Principle) |

**Key Distinctions:**

1. **Members as Owners AND Customers**
   - Members use the cooperative's services (customers)
   - Members own the cooperative through share capital (owners)
   - This dual role shapes every aspect of the business model

2. **Patronage-Based Value Distribution**
   - Net surplus distributed based on usage, not investment (Art. 86)
   - Higher patronage = higher refund (incentivizes member engagement)
   - Creates alignment between member behavior and cooperative success

3. **Democratic Governance as Core Feature**
   - One member, one vote regardless of share capital
   - Board elected by and accountable to members
   - General Assembly is supreme authority
   - Governance IS part of the value proposition

4. **Community Benefit Alongside Financial Sustainability**
   - 7th ICA Principle: Concern for Community
   - Statutory funds for community development (CETF)
   - Social impact embedded in business model, not separate

5. **Cooperative Principles Embedded in Model**
   - ICA 7 Principles are operational guidelines, not just values
   - RA 9520 codifies principles into legal requirements
   - Business model must align with cooperative identity

## Cooperative Lean Canvas Adaptations

The standard Lean Canvas requires adaptation for cooperative contexts. Here's how each block translates:

| Standard Block | Cooperative Adaptation | Key Questions |
|----------------|------------------------|---------------|
| **Customer Segments** | **Member Segments** | Who are your member-owners? What are their characteristics? |
| **Value Proposition** | **Member Value Proposition** | What benefits do members receive as owners AND customers? |
| **Revenue Streams** | **Revenue + Patronage Structure** | How do you generate income AND distribute surplus? |
| **Key Metrics** | **Member-Centric Metrics** | Member participation, patronage volume, democratic engagement |
| **Unfair Advantage** | **Cooperative Advantage** | Member loyalty, community trust, cooperative identity |

**Detailed Block Adaptations:**

```text
COOPERATIVE LEAN CANVAS STRUCTURE:
|
+-- PROBLEM (Same as standard)
|   +-- #1: Core problem members face
|   +-- #2: Why existing solutions fail members
|   +-- #3: Market/systemic barriers
|
+-- MEMBER SEGMENTS (Adapted)
|   +-- Primary members: Regular users of services
|   +-- Secondary members: Occasional users
|   +-- Prospective members: Non-members who could benefit
|   +-- Early adopters: Champions who will recruit others
|
+-- MEMBER VALUE PROPOSITION (Adapted)
|   +-- Economic benefits (patronage, better prices)
|   +-- Social benefits (community, networking)
|   +-- Democratic benefits (voice, ownership)
|   +-- Educational benefits (training, capacity)
|
+-- SOLUTION (Same but member-focused)
|   +-- Core service/product for members
|   +-- Governance mechanism
|   +-- Member engagement features
|
+-- CHANNELS (Adapted)
|   +-- Member acquisition channels
|   +-- Service delivery channels
|   +-- Member engagement/communication channels
|
+-- REVENUE STREAMS (Adapted)
|   +-- Transaction-based: Fees, margins, interest
|   +-- Membership-based: Share capital, membership fees
|   +-- Patronage structure: How surplus is allocated
|   +-- Reserve funds: Statutory allocations (Art. 86)
|
+-- COST STRUCTURE (Same with additions)
|   +-- Operations costs
|   +-- Member services costs
|   +-- Governance costs (GA, elections, committees)
|   +-- Statutory fund contributions (CETF, RF, GF)
|
+-- KEY METRICS (Adapted)
|   +-- Financial: Net surplus, asset growth, liquidity
|   +-- Member: Participation rate, patronage volume, retention
|   +-- Democratic: Voting turnout, committee engagement
|   +-- Impact: Community benefit, member satisfaction
|
+-- UNFAIR ADVANTAGE (Cooperative-specific)
    +-- Cooperative identity & principles
    +-- Member loyalty & trust
    +-- Community embeddedness
    +-- Democratic legitimacy
    +-- Patronage-based alignment
```

## Member Value Proposition Framework

The Member Value Proposition (MVP) is the heart of cooperative business modeling. Unlike investor-owned businesses, cooperatives must articulate value across four dimensions:

### 1. Economic Benefits

| Benefit Type | Description | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| **Patronage Refund** | Share of net surplus based on usage (Art. 86) | "Receive up to 70% of net surplus as patronage refund" |
| **Better Prices** | Collective buying/selling power | "Save 15-25% on farm inputs through bulk purchasing" |
| **Access to Services** | Services otherwise unavailable | "Access affordable credit at 12% vs. 36% from informal lenders" |
| **Lower Transaction Costs** | Economies of scale | "One-stop shop for inputs, credit, and marketing" |
| **Interest on Share Capital** | Return on investment (Art. 86) | "Earn up to limited interest on share capital" |

### 2. Social Benefits

| Benefit Type | Description | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| **Community** | Belonging to a group with shared interests | "Join 500+ fellow farmers in your municipality" |
| **Networking** | Professional and personal connections | "Connect with buyers, suppliers, and experts" |
| **Collective Voice** | Advocacy power on common issues | "Represent farmer interests in policy discussions" |
| **Mutual Support** | Help during difficulties | "Access emergency loans and member assistance programs" |
| **Identity** | Pride in cooperative membership | "Be part of the cooperative movement" |

### 3. Democratic Benefits

| Benefit Type | Description | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| **Ownership** | True ownership stake in the enterprise | "Own your cooperative, not just use it" |
| **Voting Rights** | Equal voice in major decisions | "One member, one vote on all matters" |
| **Participation** | Opportunity to serve and lead | "Serve on committees, run for Board positions" |
| **Transparency** | Access to financial and operational information | "Receive annual reports, attend General Assembly" |
| **Accountability** | Ability to hold leaders responsible | "Elect and remove directors through democratic process" |

### 4. Educational Benefits (ETF/CETF Funded)

| Benefit Type | Description | Example |
|--------------|-------------|---------|
| **Training** | Skill development programs | "Free training on modern farming techniques" |
| **Capacity Building** | Management and leadership development | "Leadership courses for aspiring officers" |
| **Financial Literacy** | Money management skills | "Learn savings, budgeting, and investment" |
| **Technical Assistance** | Expert support for business operations | "Access agronomist consultations" |
| **Cooperative Education** | Understanding cooperative principles | "Learn your rights and responsibilities as member" |

**Crafting the Member Value Proposition Statement:**

```text
MEMBER VALUE PROPOSITION FORMULA:

For [MEMBER SEGMENT]
Who [PROBLEM/NEED]
Our cooperative provides [SOLUTION/SERVICE]
That delivers [ECONOMIC BENEFIT]
Unlike [ALTERNATIVE]
We offer [SOCIAL + DEMOCRATIC + EDUCATIONAL BENEFITS]
Because as member-owners [PATRONAGE/GOVERNANCE ADVANTAGE]

EXAMPLE (Credit Cooperative):

For sari-sari store owners in Cotabato City
Who need affordable working capital
Our cooperative provides microloans at fair rates
That delivers 50% lower interest than informal lenders
Unlike banks that reject small borrowers
We offer community support, financial training, and a voice in governance
Because as member-owners you share in our success through patronage refunds
```

## Cooperative Revenue Model

Cooperative revenue models differ fundamentally from investor-owned enterprises due to patronage-based distribution and statutory fund requirements.

### Revenue Model Components

```text
COOPERATIVE REVENUE FLOW:
|
+-- GROSS INCOME
|   +-- Transaction-Based Revenue
|   |   +-- Service fees (loans, marketing, etc.)
|   |   +-- Product margins (trading operations)
|   |   +-- Interest income (credit operations)
|   |   +-- Commission income (marketing services)
|   |
|   +-- Membership-Based Revenue
|       +-- Membership fees (one-time or annual)
|       +-- Share capital contributions
|
+-- LESS: OPERATING EXPENSES
|   +-- Cost of goods sold
|   +-- Administrative expenses
|   +-- Governance costs (GA, elections, meetings)
|   +-- Member service costs
|
+-- = NET SURPLUS
|
+-- ALLOCATION OF NET SURPLUS (Art. 86, RA 9520)
    |
    +-- STATUTORY ALLOCATIONS (Mandatory)
    |   +-- Reserve Fund (RF): At least 10%
    |   +-- Education & Training Fund (ETF): At least 3%
    |   +-- Community Development Fund (CDF): At least 3%
    |   +-- Optional Fund: As determined by by-laws
    |
    +-- DISTRIBUTABLE SURPLUS
        +-- Interest on Share Capital: Limited rate
        +-- Patronage Refund: Based on member transactions
```

### Revenue Types by Cooperative Category

| Cooperative Type (Art. 6, RA 9520) | Primary Revenue Sources | Patronage Basis |
|-----------------------------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Credit Cooperative** | Interest spread, fees | Loan/savings volume |
| **Consumer Cooperative** | Product margins | Purchase volume |
| **Producer/Marketing Cooperative (Art. 23)** | Marketing commission, value-added margins | Produce sold through coop |
| **Service Cooperative** | Service fees | Services utilized |
| **Multipurpose Cooperative** | Diversified (combination) | Weighted by service category |

### Patronage Refund Structure (Art. 86)

```text
PATRONAGE REFUND CALCULATION:
|
+-- Step 1: Determine Net Surplus
|   Net Surplus = Total Revenue - Total Expenses
|
+-- Step 2: Allocate to Statutory Funds
|   Statutory = RF (10%+) + ETF (3%+) + CDF (3%+) + Optional
|
+-- Step 3: Calculate Distributable Surplus
|   Distributable = Net Surplus - Statutory Allocations
|
+-- Step 4: Allocate Interest on Capital
|   Interest = Share Capital x Limited Interest Rate
|
+-- Step 5: Calculate Patronage Pool
|   Patronage Pool = Distributable - Interest on Capital
|
+-- Step 6: Distribute Based on Patronage
    Individual Refund = (Member Patronage / Total Patronage) x Patronage Pool
```

### Revenue Model Design Questions

1. **Sustainability**: Can the margin/fee structure cover costs AND generate surplus?
2. **Affordability**: Are prices/fees competitive enough to attract member patronage?
3. **Equity**: Does the patronage basis fairly reflect member contributions?
4. **Simplicity**: Can members easily understand how they benefit?
5. **Alignment**: Does the model incentivize behaviors that strengthen the coop?

## Business Model by Cooperative Type

Different cooperative types require distinct business model configurations based on RA 9520 classifications (Art. 6).

### Credit Cooperative Business Model

**Core Business**: Financial intermediation - mobilize member savings, provide member loans

| Canvas Block | Content |
|-------------|---------|
| **Problem** | Limited access to formal credit; high informal lending rates; lack of savings options |
| **Member Segments** | Savers, Borrowers, Mixed (save AND borrow) |
| **MVP** | Higher interest than banks (savers), lower rates than commercial (borrowers), ownership + governance |
| **Solution** | Savings products, loan products, financial education |
| **Revenue** | Interest spread, service fees, investment income |
| **Key Metrics** | Net interest margin, portfolio at risk, savings per member, GA attendance |
| **Unfair Advantage** | Member trust, community knowledge (better credit assessment), patronage alignment |

### Consumer Cooperative Business Model

**Core Business**: Collective purchasing - buy in bulk, sell to members at savings

| Canvas Block | Content |
|-------------|---------|
| **Problem** | High retail prices, limited quality product access, fragmented purchasing power |
| **Member Segments** | Regular shoppers, bulk buyers, occasional purchasers |
| **MVP** | Lower prices through bulk, patronage refund, quality assurance |
| **Solution** | Retail stores, bulk buying program, delivery services |
| **Revenue** | Product margins, delivery fees, non-member sales (higher margin) |
| **Key Metrics** | Gross margin, inventory turnover, avg purchase per member, shopping frequency |
| **Unfair Advantage** | Member loyalty (owners shop at their store), bulk purchasing power |

### Producer/Marketing Cooperative Business Model (Art. 23)

**Core Business**: Collective selling - aggregate member products, access better markets

| Canvas Block | Content |
|-------------|---------|
| **Problem** | Low farmgate prices, no market access, post-harvest losses |
| **Member Segments** | Small producers (<1ha), medium (1-5ha), large (>5ha) |
| **MVP** | Better prices through collective bargaining, market access, post-harvest facilities |
| **Solution** | Consolidation, quality standards, contract marketing |
| **Revenue** | Marketing commission, processing fees, value-added margins |
| **Key Metrics** | Trading volume, price premium vs. farmgate, member income increase |
| **Unfair Advantage** | Volume aggregation, quality consistency, buyer contracts |

### Service Cooperative Business Model

**Core Business**: Shared services - provide common services at scale

| Canvas Block | Content |
|-------------|---------|
| **Problem** | High cost of individual service access, lack of specialized services |
| **Member Segments** | Heavy users, regular users, occasional users |
| **MVP** | Affordable access through shared costs, quality local services |
| **Solution** | Equipment rental, technical services, shared facilities |
| **Revenue** | Service fees, membership fees, equipment rental rates |
| **Key Metrics** | Utilization rate, revenue per asset, cost savings vs commercial rates |
| **Unfair Advantage** | Shared investment in expensive assets, local availability |

### Multipurpose Cooperative Business Model

**Core Business**: Integrated services - multiple business lines serving member needs

| Canvas Block | Content |
|-------------|---------|
| **Problem** | Members have multiple related needs, separate coops per service is inefficient |
| **Member Segments** | By primary need, by lifecycle, by volume |
| **MVP** | One-stop shop, integrated services, cross-subsidization, multi-activity patronage |
| **Solution** | Credit, marketing, consumer store, support services |
| **Revenue** | Diversified (interest spread, commissions, margins, fees) |
| **Key Metrics** | By business unit + consolidated, multi-service usage |
| **Unfair Advantage** | Integrated value chain, diversified revenue, deep member relationships |

## Practical Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Agricultural Marketing Cooperative

**Context**: 150 vegetable farmers in Maguindanao wanting to form a marketing cooperative, selling to institutional buyers at P24/kg vs P15/kg farmgate.

**Canvas Summary:**
- Problem: Low farmgate prices, no consistent supply, quality inconsistency
- MVP: "60% higher prices (P24/kg vs P15/kg), with patronage refund based on volume"
- Revenue: 5% marketing commission + quality premium share
- Year 1 Target: 150,000 kg, P3.6M gross sales, P30K net surplus

### Scenario 2: Pivoting a Struggling Consumer Cooperative

**Context**: 500-member consumer cooperative with declining sales, negative surplus for 2 years, competition from convenience stores.

**Pivot recommended**: Bulk Purchasing Club model
- MVP: "15-25% savings on monthly essentials through bulk purchasing"
- Transition plan: 3 phases over 12 months
- Success metrics: Active members 50->300, monthly orders P100K->P600K

### Scenario 3: Credit Cooperative Revenue Diversification

**Context**: 2,000-member credit coop with P50M assets, compressed interest spread.

**Added services**: Micro-insurance + remittance/bills payment
- New revenue: P480K/year (26% increase)
- Low risk: Agency model, established partners
- Integration: Cross-sell with existing loans and savings
