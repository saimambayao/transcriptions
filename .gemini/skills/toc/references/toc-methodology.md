# Theory of Change Methodology

## Overview

Theory of Change (ToC) is a comprehensive planning methodology that maps how and why a desired change is expected to happen. It uses backward mapping from long-term goals to identify all necessary preconditions, assumptions, and interventions needed for change.[1]

**Key Statistics:**

- Adopted exponentially since 1995 by philanthropies, government agencies, NGOs, and UN
- Over 50% of nonprofits now use ToC
- 80% of ToC documents refreshed within past year

## History and Origins

### Key Figures

- **Carol Weiss** - Pivotal figure, worked with Aspen Institute's Roundtable on Community Change
- **1995** - Publication of "New Approaches to Evaluating Comprehensive Community Initiatives"
- **ActKnowledge** (New York) - Pioneered implementation standards and TOCO software
- **2013** - Center for Theory of Change established

### Evolution

1. Late 1950s: Kirkpatrick's Four Levels of Training Evaluation
2. 1960s-70s: Stufflebeam's CIPP Model (Context, Input, Process, Product)
3. 1995: Carol Weiss formalizes Theory of Change at Aspen Institute
4. 2000-2002: Wide dissemination by Aspen Roundtable
5. 2002: TheoryOfChange.org launched
6. Present: Standard methodology across development sector

## Core Components

### 1. Long-Term Goals (Impact)

The ultimate change you want to see in the world. Should be:

- Clearly defined and measurable
- Sustained change (measured in years)
- Beyond your program's direct control
- Inspirational but achievable

### 2. Outcomes Pathway

Visual representation showing how change happens through interconnected outcomes:

- **Short-term outcomes**: Changes in weeks to months
- **Medium-term outcomes**: Changes in months to 1-2 years
- **Long-term outcomes**: Changes leading to impact (2-5 years)

### 3. Preconditions

The "necessary and sufficient conditions" for achieving each outcome:

- What must be true for the next level to occur?
- Are these conditions within program influence?
- What sequence is required?

### 4. Assumptions

Explicit beliefs about why the pathway will work:

- Causal assumptions: Why A leads to B
- Contextual assumptions: External conditions required
- Implementation assumptions: How activities create change

### 5. Interventions/Activities

Specific actions the program will take:

- Directly linked to outcomes
- Resourced and feasible
- Evidence-based where possible

### 6. Indicators

Measures to track progress:

- Outcome indicators: Evidence of change
- Process indicators: Activity completion
- Context indicators: Environmental factors

## Backward Mapping

The distinctive feature of Theory of Change methodology.[3]

### What It Is

Starting with the desired end state and working systematically backward to identify:

- What must be true immediately before the goal?
- What preconditions enable those outcomes?
- What comes before those preconditions?
- Continue until reaching current state

### Four-Stage Process

1. **Initial Framework**: Establish long-term goal and major outcome categories
2. **Detailed Mapping**: Identify specific outcomes at each level
3. **Connect Outcomes**: Draw arrows showing logical connections
4. **Iterative Refinement**: Test logic, fill gaps, resolve contradictions

### Why It Works

- Forces explicit articulation of assumptions
- Reveals logical inconsistencies
- Identifies where evidence is weak
- Creates shared understanding among stakeholders

**Key Quote**: "The debate is often the most valuable component as it forces organizations to make underlying assumptions explicit and collectively agree on change logic."[3]

## NPC Ten Steps Framework

A practical framework for developing Theory of Change:[5]

| Step | Focus | Key Questions |
|------|-------|---------------|
| 1. Situation Analysis | Context | What's the problem? What causes it? |
| 2. Target Groups | People | Who specifically will change? |
| 3. Impact | Long-term | What sustained change do we seek? |
| 4. Outcomes | Short/medium-term | What changes lead to impact? |
| 5. Activities | Interventions | What will we do? |
| 6. Change Mechanisms | How | Why do activities cause outcomes? |
| 7. Sequencing | Pathway | What order must changes occur? |
| 8. Diagram | Visualization | How do we show the ToC? |
| 9. Stakeholders | Enabling factors | Who else must be involved? |
| 10. Assumptions | Risks | What could prevent success? |

## ToC vs Logic Models vs Logframes

| Aspect | Theory of Change | Logic Model | Logframe |
|--------|-----------------|-------------|----------|
| **Format** | Diagram (flexible) | Flowchart | Matrix |
| **Purpose** | Strategic design | Visual summary | Project monitoring |
| **Causality** | Explains "why" | High-level | Limited |
| **Complexity** | Multi-pathway | Linear | Single pathway |
| **Flexibility** | Adaptable | Moderate | Rigid |
| **Scope** | Big picture | Program | Project |

### Key Distinction

- **ToC asks**: "If we do X, then Y will change *because*..."
- **Logframe asks**: "We plan to do X, which will give Y result"

### Complementary Use

1. Start with ToC to explore contextual change (conceptual "why")
2. Extract program's pathway into Logframe (operational "what")
3. Reference both in evaluations
4. Document evidence linking each step[7]

## Quality Criteria

### Plausibility

- Does the logic make sense?
- Is there evidence supporting causal claims?
- Are assumptions reasonable?

### Feasibility

- Can the program realistically influence outcomes?
- Are resources sufficient?
- Is the timeline realistic?

### Testability

- Can outcomes be measured?
- Are indicators valid and reliable?
- Can assumptions be tested?

## Best Practices

### Development Process

- Include diverse stakeholders in creation
- Make it a collaborative, facilitated process
- Allow sufficient time for debate and refinement
- Document the process, not just the output

### Living Document

- Review and update regularly (at least annually)
- Adapt based on learning and evidence
- Use for ongoing strategic decisions
- Connect to M&E systems

### Communication

- Create multiple versions for different audiences
- Develop clear visual diagram
- Write narrative explanation
- Link to operational plans
