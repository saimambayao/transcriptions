## Workflow: Explaining Cooperatives

This workflow provides structured guidance for explaining cooperative concepts clearly to different audiences using RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) as the authoritative reference.

### 1. Core Cooperative Concepts to Explain

#### What is a Cooperative? (RA 9520, Art. 3)

> **Legal Definition**: A cooperative is an autonomous and duly registered association of persons, with a common bond of interest, who have voluntarily joined together to achieve their social, economic, and cultural needs and aspirations by making equitable contributions to the capital required, patronizing their products and services and accepting a fair share of the risks and benefits of the undertaking in accordance with universally accepted cooperative principles.

**Key Elements to Emphasize**:
- **Autonomous**: Self-governing, independent from government or private entities
- **Duly registered**: Must be registered with CDA (or CSEA in BARMM)
- **Common bond of interest**: Shared purpose or community
- **Voluntary membership**: No one is forced to join
- **Equitable contribution**: Fair share based on capacity
- **Patronage**: Members use the cooperative's services
- **Risk and benefit sharing**: Both gains and losses are shared

#### ICA 7 Cooperative Principles (RA 9520, Art. 4)

| Principle | Description | Practical Example |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **1. Voluntary & Open Membership** | Open to all who can use services and accept responsibilities | Anyone in the community can join if they meet requirements |
| **2. Democratic Member Control** | One member, one vote regardless of capital | A member with 1 share and a member with 100 shares have equal voting power |
| **3. Member Economic Participation** | Members contribute equitably and control capital democratically | Patronage refund based on transactions, not shares |
| **4. Autonomy & Independence** | Self-help organizations controlled by members | Coop decides its own policies without external interference |
| **5. Education, Training & Information** | Invest in member and community education | Mandatory Education & Training Fund (ETF) allocation |
| **6. Cooperation Among Cooperatives** | Work together through local, national, and international structures | Federations, unions, leagues |
| **7. Concern for Community** | Sustainable development of communities | Community Development Fund (CDF) for community projects |

#### Cooperative vs Corporation vs Partnership (Art. 3-4)

| Aspect | Cooperative | Corporation | Partnership |
|--------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Purpose** | Service to members | Profit maximization | Profit for partners |
| **Ownership** | Members (users) | Shareholders (investors) | Partners |
| **Voting** | One member, one vote | One share, one vote | Per agreement |
| **Returns** | Based on patronage | Based on shares | Per agreement |
| **Control** | Democratic | Capital-based | Per agreement |
| **Surplus Distribution** | Patronage refund | Dividends | Partner share |
| **Registration** | CDA/CSEA | SEC | SEC/DTI |

| Type | Primary Activity | Examples |
|------|------------------|----------|

| **Marketing** | Market linkage for products | Agricultural product consolidation |
| **Service** | Rendering services to members | Transport, health, housing |
| **Multipurpose** | Combination of activities | Credit + marketing + consumer store |
| **Advocacy** | Represent and lobby for sectors | Women, fisherfolk, transport |
| **Agrarian Reform** | Beneficiaries of agrarian reform | ARBC, CLOA holders |
| **Cooperative Bank** | Banking operations | Licensed rural/thrift banks |
| **Dairy** | Dairy production and marketing | Milk production coops |
| **Education** | Educational services | School cooperatives |
| **Electric** | Electric distribution | Rural electric cooperatives |
| **Financial Services** | Broader financial services | Insurance, investments |
| **Fishermen** | Fishing and aquaculture | Municipal fishers |
| **Health Services** | Healthcare provision | HMO-type services |
| **Housing** | Housing for members | Low-cost housing |

#### Categories by Level (Art. 6)

| Level | Composition | Examples |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Primary** | 15+ natural persons | Barangay coop, company-based coop |
| **Secondary** | 2+ primary cooperatives | Federation (same type), Union (multi-type) |
| **Tertiary** | 2+ secondary cooperatives | National leagues, regional federations |
| **Apex (4th Level)** | 2+ tertiary cooperatives | National cooperative organizations |

### 2. Cooperative Governance Explanations

#### Governance Structure (Art. 37-43)

```
                    ┌─────────────────────┐
                    │   General Assembly  │ ← Supreme Authority
                    │  (All Members)      │
                    └──────────┬──────────┘
                               │ Elects
            ┌──────────────────┼──────────────────┐
            │                  │                  │
    ┌───────▼───────┐  ┌───────▼───────┐  ┌──────▼──────┐
    │     Board of  │  │    Audit      │  │  Election   │
    │    Directors  │  │   Committee   │  │  Committee  │
    │  (Policy)     │  │  (Oversight)  │  │  (Elections)│
    └───────┬───────┘  └───────────────┘  └─────────────┘
            │ Elects
    ┌───────▼───────┐
    │   Officers    │
    │   (GM, etc.)  │
    │ (Operations)  │
    └───────────────┘
```

**General Assembly (GA)** - Art. 37
- Supreme authority of the cooperative
- Composed of all members in good standing
- Meets at least once a year (annual GA)
- Powers: Elect BOD, approve amendments, distribute net surplus, dissolve coop

**Board of Directors (BOD)** - Art. 38-39
- Policy-making body
- 5-15 directors (odd number preferred)
- Term: Maximum 2 years, staggered terms recommended
- Powers: Manage affairs, appoint general manager, approve contracts

**Officers** - Art. 42
- Elected by BOD from among themselves OR appointed
- Minimum: Chairperson, Vice-Chairperson, Secretary, Treasurer
- General Manager: Appointed by BOD, manages daily operations

**Statutory Committees** (Art. 43)

| Committee | Composition | Function |
|-----------|-------------|----------|
| **Audit Committee** | 3+ members elected by GA | Internal audit, financial oversight |
| **Election Committee** | 3+ members elected by GA | Supervise all elections |
| **Mediation/Conciliation** | 3+ members elected by GA | Resolve member disputes |
| **Ethics Committee** | 3+ members elected by GA | Enforce code of ethics |
| **Education & Training** | Appointed by BOD | Plan member education programs |

#### One Member, One Vote Principle (Art. 4, Principle 2)

**Explanation Points**:
- Regardless of share capital contribution, each member has ONE vote
- This prevents wealthy members from controlling the cooperative
- Ensures democratic participation of all members
- Exception: In laboratory cooperatives (educational), voting may differ

**Common Questions**:
- "But I invested more, shouldn't I have more votes?" → Explain cooperative is not a corporation; returns come through patronage, not voting control
- "What if someone buys many shares?" → Still one vote; excess shares earn interest but not extra votes

### 3. Cooperative Finance Explanations

#### Share Capital vs Deposits (Art. 46-47)

| Aspect | Share Capital | Deposits |
|--------|--------------|----------|
| **Nature** | Ownership stake | Savings with the coop |
| **Voting Rights** | Required for voting (paid-up) | None |
| **Returns** | Interest on share capital (limited) | Interest rate (savings rate) |
| **Withdrawal** | Upon resignation/termination | Anytime with notice |
| **Risk** | Bears business risk | Protected (not at risk) |
| **Transferability** | Only to other members | N/A |

**Key Distinction**: Share capital makes you an owner; deposits are your savings.

#### Net Surplus vs Profit (Art. 2, 86)

| Aspect | Cooperative Net Surplus | Corporate Profit |
|--------|------------------------|------------------|
| **Definition** | Excess of revenue over costs | Revenue minus expenses |
| **Source** | From member transactions | From all customers |
| **Treatment** | Returned to members via patronage | Distributed as dividends |
| **Distribution Basis** | Volume of transactions | Number of shares |
| **Taxation** | Exempt (for member transactions) | Taxable |

**Explanation**: Cooperatives don't technically "profit" from members—they return excess collections through patronage refund. Surplus from non-member transactions is taxable.

#### Patronage Refund Calculation (Art. 86)

**What is Patronage Refund?**
The return of net surplus to members based on their volume of business with the cooperative, not based on their share capital.

**Formula**:
```
Member's Patronage Refund = (Member's Transactions / Total Member Transactions) × Distributable Surplus
```

**Example**:
```
Total Member Transactions: ₱10,000,000
Member A's Transactions:   ₱500,000 (5% of total)
Distributable Surplus:     ₱200,000

Member A's Patronage Refund = 5% × ₱200,000 = ₱10,000
```

**Key Points**:
- Based on how much you USE the coop, not how much you invested
- Encourages patronage (using coop services)
- Can be paid in cash or credited to share capital

#### Interest on Share Capital (Art. 86)

- Limited interest may be paid on share capital
- Rate determined by BOD, approved by GA
- Cannot exceed market rate
- Paid AFTER statutory fund allocations
- Different from patronage refund

#### Statutory Funds (Art. 86-87)

| Fund | Minimum Allocation | Purpose |
|------|-------------------|---------|
| **Reserve Fund (RF)** | 10% of net surplus | Cover losses, expansion, unforeseen contingencies |
| **Education & Training Fund (ETF)** | 10% of net surplus | Member education, training programs |
| **Community Development Fund (CDF)** | 3% of net surplus | Community projects, not just members |
| **Optional Fund (OF)** | Per by-laws | Land and building, social services, etc. |

**Calculation Example**:
```
Net Surplus:          ₱1,000,000

Reserve Fund (10%):   ₱100,000 → Cannot be distributed, builds coop capital
ETF (10%):            ₱100,000 → For seminars, training, education
CDF (3%):             ₱30,000  → Community outreach, not member-exclusive
Optional Fund:        ₱70,000  → As defined in by-laws

Available for Distribution: ₱700,000 (for interest on capital + patronage refund)
```

### 4. Common Cooperative Questions

#### "How do I join a cooperative?" (Art. 29)

**Step-by-Step Explanation**:
1. **Attend Pre-Membership Education Seminar (PMES)** - Mandatory orientation about cooperative principles, rights, responsibilities
2. **Submit Application** - Application form with personal information
3. **Pay Membership Fee** - One-time, non-refundable fee (varies per coop)
4. **Pay Initial Share Capital** - Minimum subscribed share capital as per by-laws
5. **Approval by BOD** - Application reviewed and approved
6. **Receive Certificate of Membership** - Official recognition as member

**Requirements Typically Include**:
- Must meet common bond (residence, occupation, association)
- Must be of legal age (or minor with guardian approval)
- Must agree to by-laws and policies
- Must not be a member of similar cooperative (some cases)

#### "What are the benefits of membership?" (Art. 29-30)

**Rights and Privileges**:
1. **Vote and be Voted** - Participate in governance
2. **Patronage Refund** - Share in surplus based on transactions
3. **Interest on Share Capital** - Earn interest on investment
4. **Access to Services** - Loans, products, services at member rates
5. **Training and Education** - Free or subsidized seminars
6. **Information Access** - Inspect books, receive reports

**Responsibilities**:
1. **Patronize the Cooperative** - Use its products and services
2. **Pay Subscribed Capital** - Complete share capital subscription
3. **Attend General Assemblies** - Participate in decision-making
4. **Follow By-Laws** - Abide by rules and policies

#### "How is patronage refund calculated?"

See detailed calculation in Section 3 above. Key points to emphasize:
- Based on transaction volume, NOT share capital
- Encourages active use of cooperative services
- Calculated after statutory fund allocations

#### "What happens to my share capital when I leave?" (Art. 30)

**Upon Voluntary Withdrawal**:
1. Submit written resignation to BOD
2. Share capital refunded AFTER:
   - All obligations/loans are settled
   - Cooperative can afford it (financial stability)
3. May be paid in installments if large amount
4. Membership fee is NON-refundable
5. Patronage refund for the year may be prorated

**Upon Death**:
- Share capital goes to designated beneficiary or legal heirs
- Transfer to heirs who may become members
- Or refunded to estate

**Upon Expulsion/Termination**:
- Due process required
- Share capital returned after obligations settled
- May forfeit certain privileges

#### "How are decisions made in a cooperative?" (Art. 37-40)

**Decision Hierarchy**:

| Level | Who Decides | What Decisions |
|-------|-------------|----------------|
| **General Assembly** | All members | Major decisions: by-law amendments, surplus distribution, dissolution, elect BOD |
| **Board of Directors** | Elected directors | Policy decisions: business plans, budgets, hiring GM, major contracts |
| **Officers/Management** | Appointed officers | Operational decisions: daily operations, routine transactions |
| **Committees** | Elected/appointed members | Specialized decisions: audit findings, election procedures, disputes |

**Voting Requirements**:
- Simple majority for most decisions
- 2/3 vote for major decisions (amendments, dissolution, merger)
- Quorum required (usually majority of members or as per by-laws)

### 5. Explaining to Different Audiences

#### To Potential Members

**Focus Areas**:
- **Benefits**: What will I get from joining?
- **Democratic Participation**: Your voice matters, one member one vote
- **Patronage Refund**: The more you use, the more you earn back
- **Community**: You're joining a community, not just a service

**Avoid**:
- Complex legal terms
- Detailed statutory requirements
- Internal governance details (save for later)

**Sample Explanation**:
> "A cooperative is like a business owned by its members—that's you! Unlike banks or corporations where rich investors control everything, here you have one vote just like everyone else. When the coop makes money from your transactions, it comes back to you through patronage refund. Plus, you get access to loans, training, and services at member-friendly rates."

#### To Existing Members

**Focus Areas**:
- **Rights**: You can vote, inspect books, receive reports
- **Responsibilities**: Patronize, pay capital, attend GAs
- **Participation**: Attend GA, join committees, run for BOD
- **Patronage Calculation**: How your refund is computed

**Sample Explanation**:
> "As a member, you have the right to vote in the General Assembly, run for the Board of Directors, and receive your patronage refund. Remember, your refund is based on how much you transact with the coop—so the more you borrow, save, or buy from us, the bigger your refund. Attending the GA is important because that's where we decide on surplus distribution and elect our leaders."

#### To Officers (BOD, Committees)

**Focus Areas**:
- **Duties and Liabilities**: Legal responsibilities under RA 9520
- **Fiduciary Duty**: Act in best interest of cooperative, not self
- **Compliance Requirements**: Reports, meetings, disclosures
- **Governance Best Practices**: Conflict of interest, decision-making

**Reference Points**:
- Art. 39-41: BOD duties and liabilities
- Art. 43: Committee functions
- Art. 53: Reportorial requirements

**Sample Explanation**:
> "As a director, you have fiduciary duty to the cooperative. This means you must act in good faith, with due diligence, and in the best interest of the coop and its members—not your personal interest. You're personally liable if you approve transactions that benefit yourself or violate the law. Make sure you attend board meetings, review financial reports, and ensure compliance with CDA/CSEA requirements."

#### To Regulators (CDA/CSEA Staff)

**Focus Areas**:
- **Compliance Status**: Registration, reports, audits
- **Regulatory Requirements**: MC compliance, submission deadlines
- **Legal Provisions**: Specific RA 9520 articles
- **FRAMES Indicators**: For CSEA-specific compliance

**Reference Points**:
- Art. 53: Reportorial requirements
- Art. 54-55: Examination and inquiry powers
- Art. 56: Cancellation of registration
- CSEA Memorandum Circulars

#### To General Public

**Focus Areas**:
- **Cooperative Difference**: Not for profit, but for service
- **Community Impact**: How coops help the community
- **Social Enterprise Model**: Business with social mission
- **Accessibility**: Anyone can join and participate

**Sample Explanation**:
> "Cooperatives are businesses owned and run by their members for their mutual benefit. Unlike corporations that maximize profit for investors, cooperatives prioritize service to members and community development. When you buy from a cooperative or join one, you're supporting a business model where people come before profit. In BARMM, cooperatives play a vital role in community economic development, especially in underserved areas."

### 6. Practical Scenarios

#### Scenario 1: Explaining Patronage Refund to a New Member

**Context**: Maria just joined the cooperative and received her first patronage refund. She's confused why her refund is smaller than Juan's even though they joined at the same time.

**Explanation Approach**:

1. **Acknowledge the Confusion**
   > "I understand it seems unfair that Juan got more. Let me explain how patronage refund actually works."

2. **Clarify the Basis**
   > "Patronage refund is NOT based on how much share capital you have or how long you've been a member. It's based on how much you USED the cooperative's services during the year."

3. **Show the Calculation**
   > "Let's look at your transactions this year:
   > - You borrowed ₱20,000 and bought ₱10,000 worth of goods = ₱30,000 total transactions
   > - Juan borrowed ₱100,000 and bought ₱50,000 worth of goods = ₱150,000 total transactions
   > - Juan's transactions are 5x more than yours, so his refund is proportionally higher."

4. **Explain the Principle**
   > "This is based on Principle 3—Member Economic Participation. The more you patronize (use) the coop, the more you get back. It encourages members to transact with their own cooperative instead of going elsewhere."

5. **Encourage Participation**
   > "Next year, if you increase your transactions—maybe get a bigger loan for your business or buy more supplies from our store—your patronage refund will be higher too!"

#### Scenario 2: Clarifying BOD Responsibilities to Newly Elected Directors

**Context**: Three new directors were just elected. They're asking what exactly they need to do and if they can still work their regular jobs.

**Explanation Approach**:

1. **Clarify the Role**
   > "As Board of Directors, you're responsible for the POLICY direction of the cooperative—not the day-to-day operations. Think of it like a company's board: you set the direction, the General Manager implements it."

2. **Enumerate Key Duties (Art. 39)**
   - Attend all board meetings (usually monthly)
   - Review and approve budgets, plans, and policies
   - Appoint and supervise the General Manager
   - Ensure compliance with laws and by-laws
   - Protect the assets and interests of the cooperative
   - Report to the General Assembly

3. **Address the Job Question**
   > "Yes, you can keep your regular jobs. Being a director is not a full-time position—most directors have other occupations. However, you MUST attend board meetings and be available for important decisions. You receive per diem for meetings, not a salary."

4. **Explain Liabilities (Art. 41)**
   > "However, be aware that directors can be personally liable if they:
   > - Vote for contracts where they have personal interest (conflict of interest)
   > - Approve illegal actions or violations
   > - Act in bad faith or with gross negligence
   > - Fail to perform their duties diligently"

5. **Set Expectations**
   > "Your term is 2 years. During this time, attend the mandatory training for cooperative officers (MICO) if you haven't already. This is required by CDA/CSEA."

#### Scenario 3: Describing FRAMES Compliance to Cooperative Manager

**Context**: A cooperative manager asks what FRAMES is and how it affects their annual compliance reporting to CSEA.

**Explanation Approach**:

1. **Introduce FRAMES**
   > "FRAMES stands for Financial, Regulatory, Administrative, Membership, Economic, and Social development. It's the compliance rating system used by CSEA (and CDA) to assess how well cooperatives are performing across key areas."

2. **Explain Each Component**

   | Component | What It Measures | Key Indicators |
   |-----------|------------------|----------------|
   | **F - Financial** | Financial health | Capital adequacy, liquidity, asset quality, profitability |
   | **R - Regulatory** | Legal compliance | Registration status, annual reports, audited FS, elections |
   | **A - Administrative** | Governance quality | Board meetings, policies, internal controls, training |
   | **M - Membership** | Member participation | Growth, GA attendance, patronage, education |
   | **E - Economic** | Business performance | Revenue growth, service diversification, efficiency |
   | **S - Social** | Community impact | CDF utilization, community projects, social programs |

3. **Connect to Reporting**
   > "Your annual compliance report to CSEA covers these areas. When you submit:
   > - Audited Financial Statements → Financial component
   > - Annual Report → Multiple components
   > - Training Certificates → Administrative component
   > - General Assembly Minutes → Membership component
   > - CDF Utilization Report → Social component"

4. **Emphasize Consequences**
   > "Your FRAMES rating affects:
   > - Eligibility for government programs and assistance
   > - Registration renewal
   > - Regulatory action (suspension, dissolution if non-compliant)
   > - Public perception and trust"

5. **Recommend Actions**
   > "To maintain good FRAMES compliance:
   > 1. Submit all reports on time
   > 2. Conduct annual audits
   > 3. Hold regular BOD meetings with minutes
   > 4. Document all training conducted
   > 5. Utilize CDF properly with documentation
   > 6. Maintain accurate membership records"
