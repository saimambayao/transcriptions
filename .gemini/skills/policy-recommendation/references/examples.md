# Policy Recommendation Examples

## Example 1: Executive Summary (Generic — MAFAR Agricultural Policy)

**Policy Recommendation: Sustainable Rice Production Support Program for BARMM Provinces**
Reference: MAFAR-PR-2026-003

MAFAR crop yield data for crop years 2023–2025 shows that average palay production across BARMM's five provinces stands at 2.8 metric tons per hectare — 31% below the national average of 4.1 metric tons per hectare. The PSA 2024 Agricultural Census further documents that 68% of BARMM rice farmers operate on parcels smaller than two hectares, with limited access to certified seeds, irrigation infrastructure, and post-harvest facilities. These compounding constraints suppress farm income and perpetuate food security vulnerabilities across the region.

This policy recommendation proposes a Sustainable Rice Production Support Program with three integrated components: (1) a subsidized certified seed distribution system coordinated through MAFAR provincial offices, (2) rehabilitation of small-scale irrigation canals serving an estimated 14,000 hectares of rainfed farmland in Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, and Lanao del Sur, and (3) establishment of eight community-managed post-harvest facilities strategically located to reduce post-harvest losses, currently estimated at 18% of total yield.

The program aligns with Chapter 6 of the 2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023–2028 (accelerating agri-fishery and rural development) and MAFAR's statutory mandate under BAA No. 31 to develop and implement agricultural programs for BARMM. Total program cost is estimated at PHP 320 million over four years, to be sourced from the BARMM annual appropriations and supplemented by DA-National technical assistance grants available under the Philippine Plan of Action for Nutrition and agricultural modernization programs.

The institutional framework positions MAFAR as lead agency for technical design, seed procurement, and agricultural extension. MILG coordinates with LGUs on land-use clearances, barangay mobilization, and community facility management. DA-National provides technical assistance on certified seed variety selection, irrigation engineering, and post-harvest technology transfer. Risk assessment identifies three primary concerns: procurement delays in seed supply chains, LGU capacity variation in facility management, and climate vulnerability of target farmlands to La Nina flooding. Mitigation strategies are detailed in Section 4.3.

## Example 2: Executive Summary (Agency-Specific — OOBC Education Policy)

**Policy Recommendation: Establishing Bangsamoro-Responsive Education Support Programs for Other Bangsamoro Communities in the National Capital Region**
Reference: OOBC-PR-2025-007

The OBC-MANA conducted across 12 barangays in Metro Manila between June and August 2024 identified education access as the primary concern among 67% of surveyed Bangsamoro households. Of the estimated 15,000 school-age OBC children in the NCR, approximately 4,200 (28%) report difficulty accessing culturally responsive education, and 1,800 (12%) experience disrupted enrollment due to documentation barriers related to their displacement from BARMM.

This policy recommendation proposes a three-pronged intervention: (1) establishment of Bangsamoro Education Resource Centers in five LGUs with the highest OBC concentration, (2) a documentation assistance program to resolve enrollment barriers, and (3) a Bangsamoro cultural curriculum supplement developed in coordination with MBHTE and DepEd-Central Office.

The proposed program aligns with Goal 4 of the 2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan (inclusive, responsive, and quality social services) and the OOBC mandate under BAA No. 13 to coordinate services for Bangsamoro communities outside the autonomous region. Implementation requires an estimated PHP 45 million over three years, sourced from the BARMM Special Development Fund.

The institutional framework positions OOBC as lead agency, with MBHTE providing curriculum development support and MILG facilitating LGU coordination. A phased rollout beginning with Quezon City and Taguig — the two LGUs with the largest OBC populations — allows for iterative learning before expansion.

Risk assessment identifies three primary concerns: LGU cooperation variability, staff recruitment challenges in NCR, and potential overlap with existing DepEd programs. Mitigation strategies are detailed in Section 4.2.

## Style Before/After

These pairs demonstrate the anti-pattern corrections from the Style Rules section.

### AI Pattern: "By" + gerund opening

**Before:**
> By establishing these resource centers, the program will address the educational needs of OBC children.

**After:**
> The resource centers will address the educational needs of OBC children through three core services: documentation assistance, supplemental cultural curriculum, and academic tutoring.

### AI Pattern: -ing dependent clauses

**Before:**
> The data, reflecting the severe educational disparities in OBC communities, indicating urgent intervention is needed.

**After:**
> The data reveal severe educational disparities in OBC communities. Urgent intervention is needed.

### AI Pattern: Bullet-point overuse

**Before:**
> Policy goals:
> - Increase enrollment rates
> - Improve cultural responsiveness
> - Reduce documentation barriers
> - Establish resource centers
> - Train local educators
> - Coordinate with DepEd
> - Monitor student outcomes
> - Ensure sustainability

**After:**
> The program pursues three interconnected goals. First, it seeks to increase enrollment rates among OBC children by removing documentation barriers that currently prevent 12% of school-age children from completing registration. Second, it will improve cultural responsiveness through a Bangsamoro curriculum supplement developed jointly by MBHTE and DepEd-Central Office. Third, it establishes a network of resource centers staffed by trained educators who can monitor student outcomes and coordinate with local DepEd divisions.

### AI Pattern: Vague action statements

**Before:**
> By leveraging the existing partnerships between OOBC and various LGUs, the program aims to create synergies that will enhance educational outcomes.

**After:**
> OOBC will formalize MOAs with five NCR LGUs to co-fund and co-manage the resource centers. Each MOA specifies the LGU's contribution (facility space and utilities) and OOBC's commitment (staffing and curriculum materials).
