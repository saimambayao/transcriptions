# Policy Analysis Quick-Reference Guide
Six core methodologies for BARMM policy recommendations.

---

## 1. Bardach's Eightfold Path
*The gold standard for structured policy analysis (Bardach & Patashnik, 7th ed. 2023, Sage/CQ Press)*

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| 1. Define the problem | State what is wrong and how large the gap is |
| 2. Assemble evidence | Gather data, studies, and expert input fast |
| 3. Construct alternatives | List feasible policy options (including "do nothing") |
| 4. Select criteria | Choose values to judge alternatives (efficiency, equity, legality) |
| 5. Project outcomes | Forecast results for each alternative under each criterion |
| 6. Confront tradeoffs | Rank alternatives when criteria conflict |
| 7. Decide | Commit to a recommendation — be specific |
| 8. Tell your story | Write a clear, audience-appropriate brief |

**BARMM Example — Halal Industry Development Policy:**
1. Problem: BARMM halal export share remains below 2% of national halal trade despite majority-Muslim population
2. Evidence: MAFAR data on certified producers, DTI-BARMM halal export stats
3. Alternatives: (a) subsidize halal certification, (b) establish regional halal hub, (c) status quo
4. Criteria: economic impact, equity for smallholders, BOL mandate alignment
5. Outcomes: Option (b) yields higher export growth but requires ₱200M capital
6. Tradeoff: equity vs. scale — Option (b) benefits large producers more
7. Decision: Phased approach — certify smallholders first, then establish hub
8. Story: Brief to BTA Parliament's Committee on Trade and Investment

## 2. Problem Tree Analysis
*Maps causal chains: root causes → core problem → effects. Use before designing interventions.*

**How to construct:**
- **Trunk (core problem):** The condition to be changed — specific and measurable
- **Roots (causes):** Immediate causes on the first tier; underlying causes below them
- **Branches (effects):** Direct effects on first tier; long-term consequences above them
- Convert the tree to an **Objective Tree** by restating each node as a positive condition (the solution mirror)

**BARMM Example — Agricultural Productivity Gap in MAFAR Communities:**
- **Core problem:** Low crop yield per hectare among smallholder farmers in MAFAR provinces
- **Root causes:** Limited access to irrigation (immediate) → inadequate rural infrastructure budget (underlying) → fragmented land tenure (deep root)
- **Root causes:** Poor extension services (immediate) → shortage of trained agricultural technicians (underlying)
- **Effects:** Food insecurity (direct) → higher poverty incidence in rural BARMM (long-term) → outmigration of youth (long-term)
- **Objective Tree:** "Smallholders have access to reliable irrigation" → guides BTA committee to draft a targeted appropriations measure

## 3. Stakeholder Analysis & Power-Interest Matrix
*Identifies who matters, what they want, and how to engage them. Run before consultation.*

**4 Quadrants (Power on Y-axis, Interest on X-axis):**

| Quadrant | Label | Engagement Strategy |
|----------|-------|---------------------|
| High power, high interest | **Key Players** | Involve closely; co-design |
| High power, low interest | **Keep Satisfied** | Consult periodically; prevent opposition |
| Low power, high interest | **Keep Informed** | Communicate openly; mobilize as advocates |
| Low power, low interest | **Monitor** | Minimal engagement; watch for shifts |

**BARMM Example — Education Access Policy:**
- **Key Players:** DEPED-BARMM, BTA Education Committee, CHED-BARMM, DepEd Central
- **Keep Satisfied:** National government agencies (DepEd Central, DBM) — high budget authority, low regional interest
- **Keep Informed:** IP and Moro community leaders, parent-teacher associations, CSOs
- **Monitor:** Private schools, international donors without active BARMM programs
- Note: Ulama and tribal leaders may start as "Keep Informed" but shift to Key Players on curriculum content — re-assess quarterly

## 4. Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA)
*Converts all costs and benefits to monetary values to compare interventions. Strongest for infrastructure and services.*

**When to use:** Investment decisions with quantifiable outcomes (roads, health facilities, irrigation systems).

**Net Present Value (NPV) simply stated:** Future benefits and costs are worth less than present ones. NPV discounts them to today's value. If NPV > 0, benefits outweigh costs over time.

**Key formula:** NPV = Σ (Benefits_t − Costs_t) / (1 + r)^t where r = discount rate, t = year

**Key limitations:**
- Hard to monetize social goods (peace dividends, cultural preservation)
- Sensitive to discount rate assumptions — small changes flip the result
- Often underweights distributional effects — who bears costs vs. who gets benefits
- Not suitable as the sole criterion for equity-driven or rights-based policies

**BARMM Example — Road Infrastructure Investment:**
- Costs: construction (₱500M), maintenance (₱20M/yr over 20 years), displacement of communities
- Benefits: reduced travel time (monetized at average wage), increased farm-gate prices, improved health facility access
- Discount rate: 8–10% (NEDA social discount rate)
- Complement CBA with a poverty incidence map to flag equity gaps the numbers miss

## 5. Theory of Change + Logic Model
*ToC explains WHY the intervention works; Logic Model maps HOW it operates. Use together.*

**Theory of Change (ToC) narrative structure:**
1. **Long-term goal:** The ultimate change you seek (e.g., reduced poverty)
2. **Preconditions:** What must be true before the goal is reached
3. **Assumptions:** What must hold for each step to lead to the next
4. **Interventions:** The activities that trigger change
5. **Rationale:** Evidence or theory explaining the causal links

**Logic Model chain:**

```
Inputs → Activities → Outputs → Outcomes → Impact
```

- **Inputs:** Budget, staff, equipment, partner agencies
- **Activities:** Training, infrastructure, service delivery, policy enactment
- **Outputs:** # of beneficiaries trained, km of road built (measurable, short-term)
- **Outcomes:** Behavior/condition change in 1–3 years (increased income, improved skills)
- **Impact:** Long-term systemic change attributable to the program

**BARMM Example — Livelihood Program for Fisherfolk:**
- Inputs: ₱50M BARMM MSSD budget, BFAR-BARMM technical staff, partner LGUs
- Activities: Skills training in post-harvest, equipment grants, market-linkage events
- Outputs: 2,000 fisherfolk trained; 500 cold storage units distributed
- Outcomes: 30% income increase for participants; reduced post-harvest losses
- Impact: Sustainable reduction in coastal poverty incidence in Maguindanao del Sur
- Key assumption: Markets and buyer networks are accessible and stable

## 6. OECD/DAC Evaluation Criteria
*The six-criteria framework for assessing policies and programs. Apply at design AND evaluation stages.*

| Criterion | Core Question | When to Emphasize |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Relevance** | Does this address an actual need and align with national/regional priorities? | Policy design, problem framing |
| **Coherence** | Is this consistent with other policies? Does it avoid duplication or conflict? | Multi-agency programs; BOL alignment checks |
| **Effectiveness** | Is it achieving its stated objectives? | Mid-term and final evaluations |
| **Efficiency** | Are results achieved with reasonable resources? Is there a better use of funds? | Budget justifications; CBA companion |
| **Impact** | What are the broader effects — intended and unintended? On whom? | Long-term assessments; equity audits |
| **Sustainability** | Will benefits last without continued intervention? Are systems in place? | Exit strategy design; institutional capacity review |

**Notes for BARMM policy recommendations:**
- **Coherence** is especially critical: all policies must align with BOL (RA 11054), the BDP 2023–2028, and existing BAAs — cite the specific provisions
- **Sustainability** should address whether BARMM LGUs or line agencies can absorb and continue the program after grant/project funding ends
- Apply all 6 criteria as a checklist when writing the policy analysis section of a recommendation brief — do not omit Coherence or Sustainability
