# Legislative Drafting Principles

Synthesized principles for the Bangsamoro Parliament, adapted from international best practices.

## The ABC of Drafting

Every provision must achieve three things: **Accuracy**, **Brevity**, and **Clarity**.

- Accuracy: say exactly what is meant — no more, no less
- Brevity: use the fewest words that achieve accuracy
- Clarity: write so a person reading in good faith understands, and a person reading in bad faith cannot misunderstand

## The Legislative Sentence

A normative provision has five components. Every substantive section should answer these:

1. **Who** — the target group or legal subject (who must act, or who is affected)
2. **Must/May/Must Not** — the type of norm (obligation, right, or prohibition)
3. **Do What** — the targeted behavior or action
4. **When and How** — conditions, circumstances, exceptions
5. **Where** — jurisdiction (usually implicit for BARMM legislation)

### Sentence Structure

The most complex legislative sentence has these parts, in this order:

```
EXCEPTION → CASE → CONDITION → RULE (Legal Subject + Legal Action)
```

Example:
- **Exception**: Except as provided in Section 5 of this Act,
- **Case**: when an application for registration is filed,
- **Condition**: if the applicant has satisfied all requirements,
- **Rule**: the CSEA shall issue a certificate of registration to the applicant.

Place the case at the beginning so the reader knows immediately whether the provision applies to their situation.

## Three Types of Normative Provisions

### 1. Prohibitions (strongest)
Prevent target groups from taking certain actions.

| Strength | Formulation | Use when |
|----------|------------|----------|
| Strong (preferred) | "shall not", "must not", "is prohibited from" | Default for prohibitions |
| Acceptable | "may not" | Passive constructions only |
| Weak (avoid) | "should not", "will not", "cannot" | Never use — lacks legal force |

### 2. Obligations
Require target groups to take specific actions.

| Strength | Formulation | Use when |
|----------|------------|----------|
| Strong (preferred) | "shall", "must" | Default for obligations |
| Acceptable | "is required to" | When clarity demands it |
| Weak (avoid) | "should", "ought to", "is expected to" | Never use — aspirational |

### 3. Rights (permissions)
Authorize target groups to take actions at their discretion.

| Strength | Formulation | Use when |
|----------|------------|----------|
| Preferred | "may", "is entitled to", "is authorized to" | Default for rights |
| Weak (avoid) | "might", "could be allowed to" | Never use — conditional |

### Key Rule: "Shall" vs. "May" vs. "Must"

- **"Shall"** — imposes an obligation or duty. Use only in the legal action, never for future tense.
- **"May"** — confers a right, privilege, or discretionary power.
- **"Shall not" / "Must not"** — creates a prohibition.
- Never use **"will"** in normative provisions — it denotes future contingency, not obligation.
- Never use **"shall be"** where **"is"** suffices. "Every partner is an agent" not "shall be an agent."

## Normative vs. Aspirational Language

Legislation that proclaims goals rather than establishing norms is less likely to achieve those goals. Declaration of Policy sections may state aspirations, but all substantive provisions must be normative — they must specify who must or may do what, under what circumstances.

**Aspirational (avoid in substantive sections):**
> "The government shall endeavor to promote sustainable development in the region."

**Normative (correct):**
> "The Ministry of Environment shall submit an annual environmental impact assessment to the Parliament within thirty (30) days of the end of each fiscal year."

The normative version identifies the target group (MENRE), the obligation (submit assessment), and the timing (30 days after fiscal year end).

## Style and Grammar

### Present Tense
The law speaks continuously. Use present tense for all normative provisions. The statute is construed as referring to the time when it is applied, not when it is drafted.

- Wrong: "A person who will drink intoxicating liquors... will be guilty of a misdemeanor."
- Right: "A person who drinks intoxicating liquors... is guilty of a misdemeanor."

### Active Voice
Active voice identifies the legal subject clearly and makes the obligation explicit. Passive voice obscures who must act.

- Passive (avoid): "Notice shall be given at least 10 days in advance."
- Active (preferred): "The implementing agency shall give notice at least ten (10) days in advance."

### Brevity
- Sentences: no more than 25 words per sentence where possible
- Paragraphs: no more than 75 words
- Use short, common words over long, technical ones
- One thought per sentence, one relationship per paragraph

### Consistency
- Use the same word for the same meaning throughout
- Use different words only when different meanings are intended
- If a term is defined, use only the defined term — never synonyms

### Tabular Arrangement
When a provision lists multiple rights, duties, or conditions, break them into lettered subsections rather than cramming them into one long sentence. This matches BTA convention:
- Subsections: (a), (b), (c)
- Paragraphs within subsections: (1), (2), (3)

## Definitions

- Define only terms that have special meaning in the bill or that are ambiguous
- Use "means" for exhaustive/complete definitions
- Use "includes" for partial/illustrative definitions
- Use "does not include" to explicitly exclude categories
- List definitions in **alphabetical order**
- Place definitions early in the bill (Section 3, after Declaration of Policy)
- A good test: substitute the definition for the term in any section — it should read correctly

## Amendment Drafting

When amending existing BAAs or laws:
- Identify the exact provision being amended by number and title
- Use the formula: "Section [X] of [Law] is hereby amended to read as follows:"
- Show inserted material clearly (underlined or in italics)
- Avoid general repealing clauses — repeal or amend must be precise and explicit
- Check all cross-references in the amended law and in other laws that reference it

## Sanctions and Penalties

Sanctions must be:
1. **Implementable** — within the capacity of enforcing institutions
2. **Cost-effective** — benefits of enforcement exceed costs
3. **Proportionate** — severity matches the offense
4. **Flexible** — range of sanctions, not just one extreme option
5. **Deterrent** — sufficient to discourage violations

Separate norms from sanctions in different sections. A substantive section should state what is required or prohibited; a penal section should state the consequences of violation.

## Pre-Drafting Checklist

Before writing a single section, the drafter should:

1. **Ascertain purpose** — what exactly does the requester want to accomplish?
2. **Check constitutionality** — does the bill conform to the 1987 Constitution and BOL?
3. **Research existing law** — what BAAs, RAs, IRRs already cover this topic?
4. **Search for conflicts** — will this bill contradict or duplicate any existing provision?
5. **Search for analogues** — has a similar bill been filed or enacted? Can it serve as a model?
6. **Outline the bill** — plan the section sequence before drafting
7. **Identify the implementing agency** — who has the mandate and capacity?
8. **Consider secondary legislation** — what should go in the IRR vs. the bill?
9. **Determine fiscal impact** — does this require appropriations?
10. **Plan transitional provisions** — what happens to ongoing situations?

## Quality Review Checklist

After completing a draft, review separately for each:

- [ ] **Arrangement** — sections in logical order, standard sequence followed
- [ ] **Style and grammar** — ABC principles applied, active voice, present tense
- [ ] **Specific words** — shall/may/must used correctly, no archaic terms
- [ ] **Definitions** — all defined terms used consistently, alphabetical order
- [ ] **Internal references** — all section cross-references are correct
- [ ] **Constitutional compliance** — no provision exceeds BOL authority
- [ ] **No conflicts** — does not contradict existing BAAs or national law
- [ ] **Title accuracy** — long title covers all substantive provisions
- [ ] **Closing sections** — separability, repealing, effectivity included
- [ ] **Gender-neutral language** — "he/she", "chairperson" (per BTA convention)

## The BTA Legislative Process

Understanding the process helps draft provisions that survive scrutiny:

1. **First Reading** — bill is filed, read by title, referred to committee
2. **Committee Level** — hearings, consultations, committee report
3. **Second Reading** — committee report presented, period of interpellation (Q&A with sponsor), period of amendments, approval on second reading
4. **Third Reading** — final vote, no amendments allowed
5. **Chief Minister** — bill presented for signing into law (becomes BAA)

The interpellation period is where weak provisions get exposed. Draft with interpellation in mind — every provision should withstand the question: "What exactly does this mean? Who enforces it? What happens if they don't comply?"
