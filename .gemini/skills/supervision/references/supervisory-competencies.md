# The 12 Supervisory Competencies

A comprehensive competency framework for supervisors and division chiefs in BARMM entities. Based on the MDN Supervisory Development Course, adapted for any government entity in the Bangsamoro region.

Each competency maps to the supervision cycle: **Planning, Monitoring, Evaluation, Improvement**.

---

## 1. Foundations of Supervision

**Definition**: Understanding the supervisor's role, the transition from individual contributor to leader, and the fundamental responsibilities of managing people and work through others.

**Supervision Cycle**: All phases

**Key Behaviors**:
- Recognizes the shift from "doing the work" to "ensuring the work gets done through others"
- Balances technical expertise with people management responsibilities
- Models the behavior expected of staff (integrity, punctuality, quality)
- Understands the dual accountability — upward to leadership, downward to staff
- Embraces the coaching mindset over the directing mindset
- Demonstrates Islamic leadership values: amanah (trustworthiness), adl (justice), shura (consultation)

**Development Activities**:
- Role transition workshop: "From Doer to Leader" exercise
- Shadow a senior supervisor for one week
- Reflective journaling on daily supervisory decisions
- Peer discussion: "What I wish I knew when I became a supervisor"
- Study the supervisory mandate within the entity's legal framework (BOL, BAAs, CSC rules)

## 2. Assessing Performance, Potential, and Readiness

**Definition**: The ability to accurately evaluate staff performance against standards, identify potential for growth, and determine readiness for expanded responsibilities.

**Supervision Cycle**: Evaluation

**Key Behaviors**:
- Distinguishes between performance (current results), potential (future capability), and readiness (preparedness for next role)
- Uses objective, measurable criteria rather than impressions or favoritism
- Applies the 9-Box Grid (performance vs. potential) to map team talent
- Documents assessment evidence consistently across all staff
- Provides assessment results to staff transparently with developmental framing
- Avoids common rating biases: recency, halo/horn, leniency, central tendency

**Development Activities**:
- Calibration sessions with peer supervisors to align rating standards
- Practice with case studies: rate hypothetical employees and discuss discrepancies
- Build a performance evidence log — note specific examples throughout the rating period, not just at year-end
- Train on the CSC Strategic Performance Management System (SPMS) and IPCR/OPCR forms
- Conduct a talent review of current team using the 9-Box Grid

## 3. Giving Effective Feedback

**Definition**: The ability to deliver feedback that is specific, timely, balanced, and actionable — feedback that changes behavior rather than just evaluating it.

**Supervision Cycle**: Monitoring, Evaluation

**Key Behaviors**:
- Uses the SBI model: Situation (when/where), Behavior (what was observed), Impact (the effect)
- Gives feedback close to the event, not weeks later
- Balances corrective feedback with recognition of strengths
- Prepares for feedback conversations: gathers evidence, anticipates reactions, plans key messages
- Handles defensive reactions calmly: acknowledges emotions, refocuses on observable behavior
- Separates feedback on work quality from feedback on character
- Follows up after feedback to check for behavior change

**Development Activities**:
- SBI feedback practice sessions with role-play scenarios
- "Feedback fishbowl" exercise: give feedback while peers observe and debrief
- Build a personal feedback log: track feedback given and its outcomes
- Practice the "feedback sandwich" alternative: positive-constructive-positive (use judiciously — experienced staff see through it)
- Study difficult conversation frameworks (Patterson's Crucial Conversations)

## 4. Coaching for Performance and Development

**Definition**: The ability to guide staff toward improved performance and professional growth through structured coaching conversations rather than simply telling them what to do.

**Supervision Cycle**: Improvement

**Key Behaviors**:
- Uses a coaching process: establish rapport, assess the situation, explore options, agree on action, follow up
- Adapts coaching style to the performer level:
  - **New/struggling performer**: more directive, frequent check-ins, clear structure
  - **Developing performer**: collaborative, ask before telling, stretch with support
  - **Strong performer**: empowering, focus on growth goals, delegate challenge
  - **Star performer**: mentoring, strategic exposure, leadership development
- Asks powerful questions rather than giving answers ("What have you tried?" "What would success look like?")
- Creates a safe space for staff to admit mistakes and ask for help
- Integrates coaching into regular supervision, not just formal sessions

**Development Activities**:
- GROW model practice: Goal, Reality, Options, Will (structured coaching conversations)
- Coaching triads: one coaches, one is coached, one observes and gives feedback
- Identify one staff member for a 90-day coaching engagement — set goals, meet biweekly, track progress
- Study coaching techniques specific to government context (where staff may fear authority)
- Build a coaching question bank for common supervisory situations

## 5. Setting Goals and Expectations

**Definition**: The ability to translate organizational objectives into clear, measurable individual and team goals, and to collaboratively set expectations that staff understand and commit to.

**Supervision Cycle**: Planning

**Key Behaviors**:
- Cascades organizational goals (BDP targets, ministry KPIs, office OPCR) down to individual targets (IPCR)
- Sets SMART goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
- Involves staff in goal-setting — collaborative rather than imposed
- Defines both quantitative targets (outputs, deadlines) and qualitative standards (quality, professionalism)
- Documents expectations in writing — verbal agreements lead to misunderstandings
- Revisits and adjusts goals when circumstances change (mid-year review)
- Aligns individual goals with career development aspirations

**Development Activities**:
- Goal cascade workshop: practice breaking ministry-level goals into division, section, and individual targets
- SMART goal writing exercise with peer review
- Conduct a goal-setting conversation with each direct report — document outcomes
- Study the OPCR-IPCR alignment process required by CSC
- Create a "first 30 days" expectation-setting template for new staff

---

## 6. Conducting Effective Check-Ins

**Definition**: The ability to conduct regular, purposeful one-on-one and team conversations that keep work on track, surface issues early, and maintain connection with staff.

**Supervision Cycle**: Monitoring

**Key Behaviors**:
- Prepares for check-ins: reviews task status, identifies discussion points, brings relevant data
- Follows a structured but flexible agenda: progress update, blockers, priorities, support needed, development
- Asks open-ended questions to draw out information staff might not volunteer
- Listens more than talks — the check-in is for the staff member, not the supervisor
- Documents action items and follows through on commitments made
- Adjusts frequency based on staff needs: weekly for developing staff, biweekly for experienced staff
- Uses check-ins for relationship building, not just task tracking

**Development Activities**:
- Design a check-in template for the entity (agenda, key questions, action item tracker)
- Practice check-in conversations using role-play scenarios
- Implement a 30-day check-in pilot with direct reports — collect feedback on what works
- Study the difference between check-ins (developmental) and status updates (transactional)
- Build a question bank for different check-in scenarios (new project, struggling staff, high performer)

## 7. Managing Underperformance

**Definition**: The ability to identify, diagnose, and address underperformance systematically — distinguishing root causes, providing support, and applying consequences when warranted.

**Supervision Cycle**: Evaluation, Improvement

**Key Behaviors**:
- Identifies underperformance early through monitoring (check-ins, output quality, deadline compliance)
- Conducts root cause analysis before jumping to corrective action:
  - **Capability gap**: staff lacks knowledge or skills (intervention: training, coaching, mentoring)
  - **Motivation gap**: staff has the skill but lacks the will (intervention: feedback, incentives, consequences)
  - **Systems gap**: organizational barriers prevent performance (intervention: fix the system, not the person)
  - **Personal issues**: health, family, financial stress affecting work (intervention: support, accommodation, EAP)
- Documents performance issues with specific examples, dates, and evidence
- Designs Performance Improvement Plans (PIPs) that are supportive, not punitive:
  - Clear performance standards to meet
  - Specific actions the staff member will take
  - Support the supervisor will provide
  - Timeline (typically 30-90 days)
  - Consequences if standards are not met
- Follows progressive discipline aligned with CSC rules: coaching, verbal warning, written warning, formal charges
- Maintains fairness and consistency — applies the same standards to all staff

**Development Activities**:
- Root cause analysis practice: given case scenarios, diagnose whether the issue is capability, motivation, systems, or personal
- PIP writing workshop: draft PIPs for hypothetical underperformers
- Study CSC Revised Rules on Administrative Cases (RRACCS) for due process requirements
- Role-play difficult performance conversations
- Review past underperformance cases in the entity — what worked, what failed

## 8. Developing Talent

**Definition**: The ability to identify staff potential, create development opportunities, and build a pipeline of capable people ready for expanded roles.

**Supervision Cycle**: Improvement

**Key Behaviors**:
- Creates Individual Development Plans (IDPs) with each staff member annually
- Identifies development interventions matched to needs:
  - **Formal training**: courses, certifications, workshops
  - **On-the-job**: stretch assignments, job rotation, project leadership
  - **Mentoring**: pairing with senior professionals inside or outside the entity
  - **Self-directed**: reading, online courses, professional communities
- Practices succession planning: for every critical role, at least one person is being developed as a potential successor
- Provides career pathing guidance: what competencies and experience are needed for the next level
- Balances staff development with operational demands — development is not a luxury, it is a supervisory responsibility
- Recognizes and nurtures diverse talents — not everyone develops on the same path

**Development Activities**:
- IDP workshop: practice writing development plans with staff
- Design a job rotation plan for the division/section
- Identify one high-potential staff member and create a 6-month development engagement
- Build a training needs analysis for the team
- Study succession planning frameworks adapted for government (where tenure and merit rules apply)

## 9. Leading Teams

**Definition**: The ability to build team cohesion, motivate diverse individuals toward shared goals, manage team dynamics, and coordinate across functional boundaries.

**Supervision Cycle**: All phases

**Key Behaviors**:
- Builds team identity: shared purpose, norms, and mutual accountability
- Understands team dynamics: forming, storming, norming, performing (Tuckman's model)
- Manages diverse personalities and working styles within the team
- Facilitates productive team meetings — clear agendas, inclusive participation, actionable outcomes
- Addresses interpersonal conflicts early before they escalate
- Coordinates with other divisions/sections for cross-functional work
- Celebrates team achievements and learns from team failures collectively
- Creates psychological safety — team members feel safe to speak up, disagree, and admit mistakes

**Development Activities**:
- Team assessment: use a team effectiveness survey to identify strengths and gaps
- Team-building activity design: purpose-driven activities, not just social events
- Practice conflict resolution using interest-based negotiation (Fisher and Ury)
- Cross-functional project leadership: lead or participate in a project spanning multiple units
- Study motivational theory adapted for government context (intrinsic motivation, public service motivation)

---

## 10. Managing Change

**Definition**: The ability to lead teams through organizational transitions — new policies, restructuring, technology adoption, leadership changes — while maintaining morale and performance.

**Supervision Cycle**: All phases

**Key Behaviors**:
- Communicates the "why" behind changes before the "what" and "how"
- Anticipates and addresses resistance: understands that resistance often stems from fear, uncertainty, or loss
- Serves as a bridge between leadership decisions and frontline implementation
- Maintains team stability during transitions — supervisors are the shock absorbers of the organization
- Adapts supervision practices to changed circumstances (new processes, new tools, new mandates)
- Monitors team morale and stress levels during change periods
- Follows the Kotter change model where applicable: urgency, coalition, vision, communication, empowerment, quick wins, consolidation, anchoring

**Development Activities**:
- Change readiness assessment: evaluate the team's capacity to absorb a specific change
- Communication planning exercise: draft change communication messages for different audiences
- Resistance mapping: identify potential resistors, understand their concerns, plan engagement strategies
- After-action review of past organizational changes: what worked, what failed, lessons learned
- Study BARMM-specific change dynamics: BOL transition, ministerial restructuring, BTA-to-elected government transition

## 11. Delegation and Empowerment

**Definition**: The ability to assign work effectively, grant appropriate authority, maintain accountability, and empower staff to take ownership of their responsibilities.

**Supervision Cycle**: Planning, Monitoring

**Key Behaviors**:
- Decides what to delegate based on task complexity, staff capability, and developmental value
- Uses authority levels to clarify the degree of empowerment:
  - **Level 1**: "Look into this and report back. I'll decide."
  - **Level 2**: "Look into this, recommend an option, and I'll decide."
  - **Level 3**: "Decide and inform me before acting."
  - **Level 4**: "Decide and act. Report what you did."
  - **Level 5**: "Decide and act. No need to report unless there's a problem."
- Provides clear delegation: task, authority level, resources, deadline, reporting expectations
- Resists the urge to take back delegated work (reverse delegation / "upward delegation")
- Holds staff accountable for delegated work without micromanaging
- Uses delegation as a development tool — stretch assignments build capability
- Maintains a delegation log to ensure fair distribution across the team

**Development Activities**:
- Delegation audit: list current tasks and categorize which should be delegated, to whom, at what authority level
- Practice delegation conversations using role-play
- Authority level exercise: for 10 common decisions in the entity, assign the appropriate authority level for each supervisory tier
- Study the difference between delegation (authority transfers) and assignment (authority stays)
- Build a delegation matrix for the division/section

## 12. Building a Culture of Accountability

**Definition**: The ability to create an environment where commitments are honored, standards are maintained, recognition flows for good performance, and consequences follow for poor performance — consistently and fairly.

**Supervision Cycle**: All phases

**Key Behaviors**:
- Sets clear standards and communicates them repeatedly — people cannot be accountable for unknown expectations
- Models accountability personally: meets own commitments, admits own mistakes, accepts responsibility
- Recognizes and rewards good performance publicly and promptly
- Addresses poor performance consistently — not selectively based on personal relationships
- Creates peer accountability: team members hold each other to standards, not just the supervisor
- Documents everything: commitments, deadlines, outcomes, conversations — memory-based accountability fails
- Uses accountability frameworks proportionately: informal for minor issues, formal for serious or repeated issues
- Integrates Islamic principles: amanah (trust), hisab (accountability before Allah), and the responsibility of leaders for those under their care

**Development Activities**:
- Accountability audit: assess current team culture — are commitments honored? Are consequences consistent?
- Recognition practice: implement a weekly recognition habit (public praise, commendation notes, team shoutouts)
- Build a team accountability charter: norms the team agrees to hold each other to
- Progressive discipline simulation: practice applying escalating consequences for repeated underperformance
- Study accountability best practices in high-performing government units within BARMM

## Competency Assessment Rubric

Use this 5-level scale for pre/post assessment of supervisors:

| Level | Label | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Limited** | Unaware of or does not practice the competency. Relies on instinct or authority rather than skill. |
| 2 | **Developing** | Understands the competency conceptually but applies it inconsistently. Needs guidance and reminders. |
| 3 | **Proficient** | Applies the competency consistently in routine situations. Can handle standard supervisory scenarios independently. |
| 4 | **Advanced** | Applies the competency in complex and ambiguous situations. Adapts approach to context. Coaches others. |
| 5 | **Expert** | Demonstrates mastery. Innovates and improves supervisory practices. Recognized as a resource by peers and leadership. |

### Assessment Method

For each of the 12 competencies, assess using multiple sources:
- **Self-assessment**: supervisor rates themselves (pre-training)
- **Supervisor assessment**: the supervisor's own supervisor rates them
- **Staff feedback**: anonymous upward feedback from direct reports (where culturally appropriate)
- **Behavioral observation**: during training exercises and simulations
- **Post-training reassessment**: 90 days after training to measure sustained change
