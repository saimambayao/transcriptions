# Supervisory Tools and Frameworks

Practical tools for supervisors and division chiefs. These supplement the 12 supervisory competencies (in supervisory-competencies.md) with concrete instruments, templates, and step-by-step protocols.

**Sources**: Supervisory Development Course materials (Leadership and Talent Development, OHR) + Asia Pacific Gateway Skills Table Supervisory Resource Guide

---

## 1. Performance-Potential-Readiness (PPR) Assessment

A 3-step framework for evaluating where each staff member stands before providing feedback or coaching.

### Step 1: Assess Performance (current role)
- Effectiveness in achieving goals and meeting expectations
- Includes both RESULTS (the "what") and BEHAVIORS (the "how")
- When to assess: when individuals are in their current role

### Step 2: Assess Potential (future capacity)
- Capacity to develop skills for a larger role
- Indicators: ability to solve complex problems, persistence through setbacks, driven to take on challenging work, improves based on feedback
- When to assess: only if person meets/exceeds current role expectations

### Step 3: Assess Readiness (for new assignment)
- Already has skills, knowledge, and abilities for the NEW role/project
- When to assess: when there's a specific future role or assignment in mind

### The 4-Quadrant Coaching Guide

| Quadrant | Performance | Potential | Coaching Focus |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low-Low | Low | Low | CHANGE results and/or behaviors. Revisit goals/expectations. Emphasize ownership. |
| Low-High | Low | High | CHANGE results and/or behaviors. Clarify goals, show behavior impact, establish periodic check-ins. |
| High-Low | High | Low | DEVELOP potential. Ask about interests/aspirations, assess development opportunities, keep engaged. |
| High-High | High | High | SUSTAIN, REINFORCE, RECOGNIZE. Help them become coach/mentor. Consider stretch assignments. |

## 2. "Get Ready, Get Set, Give Feedback" Framework

A 3-phase feedback delivery model using the SBI (Situation-Behavior-Impact) approach.

### Phase 1: Get Ready
- Check your biases — what contradicts your perceptions?
- Face your fear of delivering feedback — what happens if you don't?
- Motivate through connection to THEIR goals
- Build trust and emotional safety

### Phase 2: Get Set
Before giving feedback, check these timing questions:

| Situation | Should you give feedback? |
|---|---|
| Will they practice the behavior again? | If not, find ways to provide practice opportunities |
| Do they have the knowledge/skills for the desired behavior? | If not, coach to close gaps first |
| Have they had time to familiarize with the task? | If not, wait until they're familiar |
| Are they in the middle of the task? | If yes, wait until complete |

### Phase 3: Give Feedback (SBI Model)
- **S**ituation: Describe the specific context
- **B**ehavior: Describe the observable behavior
- **I**mpact: Explain the effect of the behavior

Example: "In our meeting today [S], you kept looking at your phone [B], which made it seem like you weren't engaged and slowed our decision-making [I]."

After giving feedback, shift to coaching to help apply it.

## 3. Coaching Process (5-Element Cycle)

```
Self-Awareness → Motivation → New Skill Development / Practice Opportunities →
Ongoing Feedback & Support → Assessment → Reflection → Application → Accountability
(loops back to Self-Awareness)
```

### Element 1: Self-Awareness
- Can the person identify their strengths, weaknesses, and how others perceive them?
- If not, coaching must start here — help build self-awareness first
- Sample questions: "What are your greatest professional strengths?" "How would others describe you?"

### Element 2: Motivation
- Is the person motivated to learn and change?
- Connect development to THEIR goals and broader mission
- Sample questions: "How motivated are you to make this change?" "How will you benefit?"

### Element 3: Ongoing Feedback and Support
- Tools: new skill training, practice opportunities, assessment, reflection
- Focus on deciding WHAT skills to develop and picking appropriate tools

### Element 4: Application
- Provide opportunities to apply and master skills on the job
- Start with low-stakes assignments, be available for support
- Provide room for mistakes

### Element 5: Accountability
- Recognize improvement, reward success
- Establish regular check-in discussions
- Sample questions: "What progress on your coaching goals?" "What incentives are meaningful to you?"

## 4. Goal Setting (5-Step Protocol)

1. **Ask for Input** — Goal-setting is collaborative. Ask what goals they suggest.
2. **Identify 3-5 Most Important Expectations** — Include both Results (what to achieve) and Behaviors (how to achieve).
3. **Connect to Broader Priorities** — Show how their goals serve the department/organization.
4. **Ensure Goals Are Challenging** — If accomplishing it wouldn't be a significant achievement, make it harder.
5. **Check-In Regularly** — Use ongoing flexible goals, update throughout the year. Don't set-and-forget.

### Good vs. Not Challenging Enough

| Not Challenging | Challenging |
|---|---|
| "Complete the process improvement project." | "The project will be successful if everyone understands benefits, concerns are addressed, and everyone adheres to the new process." |
| "Find a replacement for our data analysis tool." | "A replacement that provides specific capabilities for each team member, is within budget, implemented by September, and everyone is trained." |

## 5. Ongoing Check-In Protocol (4-Step)

Monthly check-ins at minimum. Covers:

1. **Discuss Roles and Responsibilities** — Supervisor communicates vision/strategy, clarifies expectations. Employee asks questions, seeks alignment, takes ownership.
2. **Review Goals and Assignments** — How does current work connect to annual goals? Flexible goal-setting for new projects.
3. **Evaluate Progress and Provide Coaching** — Consider self-awareness and motivation levels. Use SBI feedback. Provide positive feedback to reinforce good work.
4. **Determine Next Steps** — What will they do before next check-in? What support do they need from you?

## 6. Performance Evaluation Protocol (6-Step)

Annual formal evaluation. Should contain no surprises if check-ins were done well.

1. **Get Employee Input** — Have them summarize key accomplishments (Situation → Action → Results). Do NOT ask for self-ratings (creates defensiveness).
2. **Get Input from Others** — Peers, direct reports, partners. Use as learning/development input.
3. **Evaluate Results ("What")** — Context → Accomplishment → Impact. Use metrics where available, but account for differences in opportunities.
4. **Evaluate Behaviors ("How")** — Did they meet behavioral expectations? Focus on 5-10 most important competencies.
5. **Write the Review** — Use 5-7 point scale (each point defined). Provide ratings for both results AND behaviors. Write narrative explaining WHY each rating.
6. **Discuss with Employee** — Summarize check-in discussions. Share rating + narrative. Get employee's thoughts. Focus on development. Show empathy for surprises.

## 7. Supervisory Skills Framework (60 Skills)

From the Asia Pacific Gateway Resource Guide. Three domains, nine sub-areas, 60 specific skills.

### Domain 1: Managing Self (3 sub-areas)
1.1 Organization and Productivity: Time management, handling multiple tasks, delegating, computers, math/finance
1.2 Writing and Communication: Document preparation, phone communication, presentations
1.3 (All foundational — must be solid before managing others)

### Domain 2: Managing Others (2 sub-areas)
2.1 Interpersonal Relationships (13 skills): Communicating, conflict resolution, customer relations, trust-building, team-building, leading, coaching, motivating, managing employee relationships, problem-solving, training, meetings, change management
2.2 Human Resources Management (11 skills): Interviewing, orientations, training programs, performance evaluations, workplace rules, progressive discipline, grievance process, complaints, respect in workplace, documentation, roles/responsibilities

### Domain 3: Managing Systems (5 sub-areas)
3.1 Legal/Legislative: Labour standards, safety regulations, bylaws, environmental, emissions, vehicle inspection, privacy (PIPA)
3.2 Health and Safety: Access control, safe workplace, safe procedures, orientations, injury prevention, emergency response, first aid, incident investigation, return-to-work
3.3 Productivity and Performance: Company expectations, worker performance, shift transitions, process improvements, project management
3.4 Quality Management: ISO standards, quality plans, quality inspections, damage reporting
3.5 Asset Management: Equipment maintenance, tool tracking, loss control

### BARMM Adaptation Notes
- Replace "labour standards" with "BAA 82 (Labor Code)" and "CSC rules"
- Replace "PIPA" with "Data Privacy Act (RA 10173)"
- Replace "ISO standards" with "COA audit standards" and "BARMM reporting requirements"
- Add: Shari'ah compliance considerations (BOL Art. IX)
- Add: Moral governance principles (12-point agenda)
- The "Can Do / Can't Do / Not Sure" checklist format is directly usable as a supervisory assessment tool
