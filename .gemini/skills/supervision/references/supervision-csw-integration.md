# Supervision and CSW Integration Guide

How the `/supervision` skill (for supervisors) and the `/csw` skill (for staff) work together as complementary systems. This document bridges both frameworks so supervisors understand their role in enabling high-quality Complete Staff Work.

---

## The Core Relationship

**CSW is the work product. Supervision is the management system that ensures it gets done well.**

| `/csw` Skill | `/supervision` Skill |
|---|---|
| For **staff** who produce the work | For **supervisors** who manage staff |
| ADDRESS IT methodology (7 steps) | Supervision cycle (Plan, Monitor, Evaluate, Improve) |
| ACSCCRaPATFEE competencies (12 staff competencies) | 12 Supervisory Competencies |
| Output: CSW documents with transmittal | Output: Supervision systems, policies, training |
| Quality question: "Is this work complete and correct?" | Quality question: "Is my staff capable and supported?" |

These two skills are **complementary, not overlapping**. A supervisor who uses `/supervision` creates the conditions for staff to succeed with `/csw`. A staff member who uses `/csw` produces the work that the supervisor reviews and clears.

## The Supervisor's Role in the CSW Cycle

At each ADDRESS IT step, the supervisor has a specific touchpoint. When these touchpoints are missed, CSW quality suffers.

### Step-by-Step Supervisor Responsibilities

| ADDRESS IT Step | Staff Action | Supervisor Touchpoint | Failure Mode if Skipped |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Analyze Context** | Staff receives assignment, analyzes the context | Supervisor **assigns the task clearly** — states the purpose, desired output, deadline, and authority level (see delegation levels in supervisory-competencies.md) | Vague assignments lead to wasted effort and misaligned outputs |
| **2. Define and Deliberate** | Staff defines the problem statement | Supervisor **validates the problem statement** before staff proceeds to research — confirms scope, confirms the right problem is being solved | Staff solves the wrong problem; rework after submission |
| **3. Research** | Staff gathers information, analyzes data | Supervisor **checks in at midpoint** — provides access to resources, connects staff with subject matter experts, removes blockers | Staff hits dead ends, works in isolation, misses key sources |
| **4. Explore Options** | Staff generates and evaluates options | Supervisor **reviews options before recommendation** — adds institutional knowledge, flags political sensitivities, challenges assumptions | Staff presents options that are technically sound but politically or operationally unviable |
| **5. Submit Report** | Staff drafts the CSW document | Supervisor **reviews and clears** the document before it goes to the decision-maker — checks completeness, accuracy, alignment with assignment, quality of analysis | Poor-quality work reaches the decision-maker; supervisor's credibility is at stake |
| **6. Sustain Implementation** | Staff supports implementation after approval | Supervisor **monitors implementation** — tracks whether the approved recommendation is being executed, removes barriers, adjusts as needed | Approved decisions die on paper; no follow-through |
| **7. Iterate and Improve** | Staff captures lessons learned | Supervisor **conducts after-action review** — what worked, what failed, what to improve next time | Same mistakes repeat; no organizational learning |

### The Assignment Conversation

The single most important supervisor touchpoint is **Step 1 — the assignment**. A well-assigned task produces better CSW than a poorly assigned one, regardless of staff capability.

A complete assignment includes:
1. **What** — The specific task or question to address
2. **Why** — The purpose and context (who requested it, what decision it supports)
3. **Who** — The decision-maker who will receive the final output
4. **When** — The deadline, including any intermediate checkpoints
5. **How** — The expected output format (briefer, memo, proposal, analysis)
6. **Authority level** — How much latitude the staff member has (see delegation levels 1-5 in supervisory-competencies.md, Competency 11)
7. **Resources** — What data, contacts, or references the staff member can access

## Evaluating CSW Quality Using the ACSCCRaPATFEE Competencies

When a supervisor reviews a CSW document, they are implicitly assessing the staff member's ACSCCRaPATFEE competencies. Use this mapping to diagnose competency gaps from CSW quality issues.

### CSW Quality Issue to Competency Gap Mapping

| CSW Quality Issue | Likely Competency Gap | Supervisory Intervention |
|---|---|---|
| Context analysis is shallow or missing key factors | **Analytical and Critical Thinking** (A) | Coach on PESTEL analysis, stakeholder mapping; provide more context during assignment |
| Options are conventional, no creative solutions | **Creativity and Innovation** (C) | Encourage brainstorming before narrowing; expose staff to best practices from other entities |
| Recommendation does not align with strategic goals | **Strategic Thinking** (S) | Connect staff to the BDP, entity mandate, and leadership priorities; review at Step 4 |
| Document is poorly written, unclear, or disorganized | **Communication** (C) | Provide writing feedback using SBI model; share exemplar CSW documents; offer writing coaching |
| Staff worked in isolation, missed stakeholder input | **Collaboration and Teamwork** (C) | Assign collaborative tasks; require stakeholder consultation at Step 2 |
| Research is superficial or relies on single source | **Research and Analysis** (Ra) | Guide research methodology; provide access to databases and experts; check in at Step 3 midpoint |
| Problem definition is vague or addresses symptoms not causes | **Problem-Solving and Decision Making** (P) | Practice 5 Whys and fishbone diagrams; validate problem statement before proceeding |
| Errors in data, citations, formatting, or compliance | **Attention to Details and Quality** (A) | Implement a pre-submission checklist; require self-review before supervisor review |
| CSW is late or rushed | **Time and Attention Management** (T) | Help staff break the task into milestones; schedule intermediate check-ins; address workload balance |
| Staff cannot adapt when assignment parameters change | **Flexibility and Adaptability** (F) | Discuss change openly; reframe changes as learning opportunities; model adaptability |
| Staff reacts defensively to feedback or avoids accountability | **Emotional Intelligence and Accountability** (E) | Build psychological safety; give feedback using SBI; model vulnerability and accountability |
| Work contains unsupported claims or biased analysis | **Ethics and Integrity** (E) | Discuss intellectual honesty; require evidence for all claims; model ethical reasoning |

### Using CSW Quality as a Performance Indicator

CSW quality is one of the most concrete, observable performance indicators for knowledge workers in government. Supervisors can track:

| Metric | How to Measure | What It Tells You |
|---|---|---|
| **Rework rate** | Number of CSW documents returned for revision before clearance | Staff needs more support at Steps 1-4, or quality standards are unclear |
| **First-pass clearance rate** | Percentage of CSW documents cleared on first submission | High rate = strong staff capability and clear assignment; Low rate = gaps in competency or supervision |
| **Timeliness** | Percentage of CSW documents submitted by deadline | Time management capability; also reflects workload balance and assignment clarity |
| **Completeness** | Percentage of coded sections filled with substantive content (not placeholders) | Attention to detail; understanding of the template requirements |
| **Analysis depth** | Supervisor rating (1-5) on the quality of context analysis and options evaluation | Analytical thinking, research capability, strategic alignment |
| **Implementation follow-through** | Percentage of approved CSW recommendations that result in actual implementation | Steps 6-7 execution; also reflects supervisor monitoring |

Include these metrics in the staff IPCR as measurable performance targets.

## Common Supervision Failures That Lead to Poor CSW

When CSW quality is consistently low across a team, the problem is usually supervisory, not staff capability. These are the most common supervision failures and their CSW consequences.

### 1. Unclear or Incomplete Assignments

**What happens**: Supervisor gives vague instructions ("prepare something on this topic"), does not specify the decision-maker, output format, or deadline.

**CSW consequence**: Staff produces work that misses the mark — addresses the wrong question, uses the wrong format, or arrives too late to be useful. High rework rate.

**Fix**: Use the assignment checklist (What, Why, Who, When, How, Authority Level, Resources) for every task. Train supervisors on Competency 5 (Setting Goals and Expectations) and Competency 11 (Delegation and Empowerment).

### 2. No Feedback Loops

**What happens**: Supervisor reviews the final CSW document but provides no feedback at intermediate steps. Staff proceeds through all 7 ADDRESS IT steps without any supervisory input until submission.

**CSW consequence**: Errors compound. A wrong problem definition at Step 2 leads to irrelevant research at Step 3, which leads to unusable options at Step 4. The entire CSW may need to be redone.

**Fix**: Implement mandatory supervisor touchpoints at Steps 1, 2, and 5 (assignment, problem validation, final review). Add optional touchpoints at Steps 3-4 for complex CSW. Train supervisors on Competency 6 (Conducting Effective Check-Ins).

### 3. No Time Allocation for CSW

**What happens**: Supervisor assigns CSW tasks on top of routine operational duties without adjusting workload. Staff must squeeze CSW production into already-full schedules.

**CSW consequence**: Rushed, shallow work. Research is skimmed, options are not properly evaluated, the document has errors. Staff burns out.

**Fix**: When assigning a CSW task, the supervisor must explicitly deprioritize or reassign other tasks. Use the delegation matrix to distribute workload fairly. Track staff workload as a supervisory responsibility.

### 4. No Follow-Up on Implementation

**What happens**: CSW is approved, recommendation is accepted, but nobody monitors whether the approved action actually happens. The supervisor moves on to the next assignment.

**CSW consequence**: Staff learns that CSW is an academic exercise — the work does not lead to real outcomes. Motivation drops. Future CSW quality declines because staff does not believe the work matters.

**Fix**: Treat Steps 6-7 (Sustain and Iterate) as supervisory responsibilities, not just staff tasks. Include implementation monitoring in the supervisor's check-in agenda. Track implementation rate as a supervision health indicator.

### 5. Supervisor Reviews Only for Errors, Not for Development

**What happens**: Supervisor treats CSW review as a proofreading exercise — catches typos, formatting issues, and factual errors, but does not use the review as a coaching opportunity.

**CSW consequence**: Staff fixes surface-level issues but never improves their analytical, strategic, or communication capabilities. Competency growth stalls.

**Fix**: Use CSW review as a coaching conversation. Apply the SBI model: "In this analysis (S), you compared only two options when the assignment called for at least three (B), which limited the decision-maker's ability to choose the most cost-effective approach (I)." Train supervisors on Competency 3 (Giving Effective Feedback) and Competency 4 (Coaching for Performance).

### 6. No Quality Standards Communicated

**What happens**: Supervisor has implicit quality expectations but never communicates them. Staff submits work that the supervisor considers insufficient, but the staff had no way to know what "good" looks like.

**CSW consequence**: Staff is confused and demoralized by repeated rejection. Quality standards vary by supervisor, creating inconsistency across the entity.

**Fix**: Define and document CSW quality standards for the entity. Share exemplar CSW documents. Create a pre-submission checklist. Train supervisors on Competency 5 (Setting Goals and Expectations) and Competency 12 (Building a Culture of Accountability).

## The Transmittal Workflow from the Supervisor's Perspective

The transmittal memorandum is the formal handoff from staff to supervisor to decision-maker. Here is the supervisor's role at each stage.

### Stage 1: Staff Submits CSW to Supervisor

- Staff completes the CSW document following ADDRESS IT
- Staff attaches a transmittal memorandum addressed to the supervisor
- The transmittal memo includes: subject, reference assignment, brief description, recommended action, and attachments list

### Stage 2: Supervisor Reviews

The supervisor reviews the CSW against these criteria:

| Review Criterion | Check |
|---|---|
| **Assignment alignment** | Does the CSW address the original assignment? Is the scope correct? |
| **Problem definition** | Is the problem clearly defined and validated? Is the root cause identified? |
| **Research quality** | Are sources credible? Is the analysis evidence-based? Are all claims supported? |
| **Options analysis** | Were sufficient options explored? Is the evaluation methodology sound? |
| **Recommendation** | Is the recommendation logical, feasible, and aligned with organizational priorities? |
| **Completeness** | Are all coded sections filled? Are there any placeholders or gaps? |
| **Accuracy** | Are names, titles, dates, legal references, and data points correct? |
| **Writing quality** | Is the document clear, concise, and professionally written? |
| **Compliance** | Does the document comply with entity formatting standards and relevant laws/policies? |

### Stage 3: Supervisor Decides

| Decision | Action |
|---|---|
| **Clear** | Supervisor endorses the CSW and forwards to the decision-maker with their own transmittal memo |
| **Return for revision** | Supervisor provides specific, actionable feedback on what needs to change (using SBI model). Staff revises and resubmits. |
| **Reject** | Supervisor explains why the CSW cannot proceed (scope error, wrong assignment interpretation, fundamental analysis flaw). Restarts from the appropriate ADDRESS IT step. |

### Stage 4: Supervisor Endorses to Decision-Maker

- Supervisor writes their own transmittal memo addressed to the decision-maker
- The supervisor's memo adds: their endorsement, any caveats or additional context, and their recommendation on the recommended action
- The supervisor's endorsement carries weight — it signals that the work has been reviewed and meets quality standards

### Stage 5: Post-Decision

- Supervisor communicates the decision-maker's response to the staff
- If approved: supervisor monitors implementation (Step 6)
- If modified: supervisor explains the modifications and their rationale
- If rejected: supervisor discusses what can be learned and whether the CSW should be revised and resubmitted

## Integration Checklist for Entity Implementation

When implementing both supervision and CSW systems in a BARMM entity, ensure these connections are in place:

- [ ] Supervisor assignment protocol includes all 7 elements (What, Why, Who, When, How, Authority Level, Resources)
- [ ] Supervisor touchpoints are mapped to ADDRESS IT steps (at minimum: Steps 1, 2, and 5)
- [ ] CSW quality standards are documented and communicated to all staff
- [ ] CSW quality metrics (rework rate, first-pass clearance rate, timeliness) are included in staff IPCRs
- [ ] Supervisor CSW review uses the SBI feedback model, not just error-catching
- [ ] Transmittal workflow is documented with clear roles for staff, supervisor, and decision-maker
- [ ] Implementation monitoring (Steps 6-7) is included in the supervisor's check-in agenda
- [ ] ACSCCRaPATFEE competency assessment is used to diagnose CSW quality gaps
- [ ] Staff IDPs include CSW competency development goals based on quality data
- [ ] Supervision system M&E includes CSW quality as a supervision health indicator
