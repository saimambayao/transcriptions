## Classical School

### Frederick Taylor's Scientific Management (1911)

**Core Principle**: There is "one best way" to perform any task.

**Key Contributions:**
1. **Time and Motion Studies**: Analyze tasks to find optimal methods
2. **Standardization**: Develop standard procedures for each task
3. **Scientific Selection**: Match workers to jobs based on capabilities
4. **Training**: Train workers in the standard method
5. **Differential Piece Rate**: Pay workers based on productivity

**Principles:**
- Replace rule-of-thumb with science
- Scientifically select and train workers
- Cooperation between management and workers
- Equal division of work and responsibility

**Limitations:**
- Treats workers as machines
- Ignores social needs
- May lead to worker exploitation

### Henri Fayol's Administrative Principles (1916)

**Five Functions of Management:**
1. Planning (Prévoir)
2. Organizing (Organiser)
3. Commanding (Commander)
4. Coordinating (Coordonner)
5. Controlling (Contrôler)

**14 Principles of Management:**

| # | Principle | Description |
|---|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | Division of Work | Specialization increases efficiency |
| 2 | Authority & Responsibility | Right to give orders, obligation to accept responsibility |
| 3 | Discipline | Obedience and respect for agreements |
| 4 | Unity of Command | One supervisor per employee |
| 5 | Unity of Direction | One plan, one head for related activities |
| 6 | Subordination of Individual Interest | Organizational goals over personal |
| 7 | Remuneration | Fair compensation for work |
| 8 | Centralization | Optimal balance of centralization |
| 9 | Scalar Chain | Clear hierarchy from top to bottom |
| 10 | Order | Right place for everything and everyone |
| 11 | Equity | Fair and kind treatment |
| 12 | Stability of Tenure | Minimize turnover |
| 13 | Initiative | Encourage employee initiative |
| 14 | Esprit de Corps | Team spirit and harmony |

### Max Weber's Bureaucracy Theory

**Ideal Bureaucracy Characteristics:**

| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Formal Hierarchy** | Clear chain of command |
| **Division of Labor** | Specialized roles |
| **Formal Rules** | Written regulations |
| **Impersonality** | Decisions based on rules, not personalities |
| **Merit-Based Selection** | Hiring based on qualifications |
| **Career Orientation** | Professional managers, not owners |

**Six Principles:**
1. Formal hierarchical structure
2. Rules-based management
3. Functional specialty organization
4. Task specialization (division of labor)
5. Impersonal environment
6. Achievement-based advancement

**Strengths:**
- Predictable, consistent operations
- Clear authority and responsibility
- Protection against favoritism

**Weaknesses:**
- Rigidity and inflexibility
- Slow decision-making
- Red tape and bureaucratic inertia

## Behavioral/Human Relations School

### Elton Mayo & Hawthorne Studies (1924-1932)

**Key Finding**: Social and psychological factors affect productivity more than physical conditions.

**Hawthorne Effect**: Workers perform better when given attention.

**Conclusions:**
1. Work is a group activity
2. Social world of adults is organized around work
3. Recognition, security, and belonging matter more than physical conditions
4. Informal groups influence behavior
5. Effective supervisor understands human behavior

---

### Abraham Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (1943)

**Five Levels (bottom to top):**

```
         /\
        /  \  Self-Actualization
       /    \  (reaching potential)
      /──────\
     /        \  Esteem Needs
    /          \  (recognition, status)
   /────────────\
  /              \  Social Needs
 /                \  (belonging, love)
/──────────────────\
 Safety Needs (security, stability)
────────────────────────
 Physiological Needs (food, shelter)
```

**Management Implications:**
- Lower needs must be satisfied before higher needs motivate
- Different employees at different levels
- Match rewards to employee need levels
- Create opportunities for growth and development

---

### Douglas McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y (1960)

**Theory X Assumptions (Authoritarian):**
- Workers inherently dislike work
- Must be coerced, controlled, directed
- Prefer to be directed, avoid responsibility
- Seek security above all
- Little ambition

**Theory Y Assumptions (Participative):**
- Work is natural as play
- Self-directed when committed to objectives
- Seek responsibility
- Creativity widely distributed
- Intellectual potential underutilized

**Management Styles:**

| Aspect | Theory X | Theory Y |
|--------|----------|----------|
| Motivation | External (rewards/punishment) | Internal (achievement) |
| Supervision | Close, constant | Minimal, trust |
| Decision-making | Centralized | Participative |
| Leadership | Authoritarian | Democratic |

## Modern Approaches

### Systems Theory

**Organization as a System:**
- **Input**: Resources (human, financial, material, information)
- **Transformation**: Processes, activities
- **Output**: Products, services, results
- **Feedback**: Information for adjustment

**Key Concepts:**
- Subsystems are interdependent
- Changes in one part affect others
- Organization interacts with environment
- Holistic perspective required

---

### Contingency Theory

**Core Principle**: No single "best way" to manage; it depends on the situation.

**Key Contingency Variables:**
1. Organization size
2. Technology
3. Environment (stable vs. dynamic)
4. Strategy
5. People

**Implications:**
- Match management style to situation
- Flexibility is essential
- Diagnose before prescribing
- Multiple valid approaches

## Application Guidelines

### When to Apply Each Theory

| Situation | Recommended Approach |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Routine, repetitive tasks | Scientific Management |
| Large, formal organizations | Bureaucracy principles |
| Need for structure/clarity | Fayol's principles |
| High-skilled, motivated workers | Theory Y |
| Complex, dynamic environments | Contingency Theory |
| Cross-functional projects | Systems Theory |

### Context Considerations

**For Cooperatives:**
- Democratic principles override bureaucratic hierarchy
- Theory Y aligns with member participation
- Systems thinking for member-organization interaction

**For Social Enterprises:**
- Balance efficiency (Classical) with mission (Behavioral)
- Contingency approach for dual objectives
- Systems view of social impact

**For Government:**
- Bureaucracy provides accountability
- Balance with behavioral approaches
- Public service motivation differs from private
