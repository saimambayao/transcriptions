## Workflow 4: Managing Cooperatives

Practical guidance on how management principles specifically apply to cooperative organizations. This workflow bridges general management theory with cooperative governance requirements under RA 9520.

### How Cooperative Management Differs from Corporate Management

| Aspect | Corporate Management | Cooperative Management |
|--------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Accountability** | Manager reports to owner(s)/shareholders | Manager reports to elected Board (Art. 44) |
| **Authority Source** | Hierarchical control from top | Democratic mandate from General Assembly |
| **Decision Rights** | Concentrated at top | Distributed to members (one member, one vote) |
| **Governance** | Board appointed by shareholders | Board elected by members (Art. 39) |
| **Orientation** | Profit maximization | Member benefit + community service |
| **Participation** | Limited to shareholders | All members have voice in decisions |

**Key Differences to Emphasize:**

1. **Manager Reports to Elected Board (Not Owner)**
   - The Board of Directors is elected by the General Assembly (Art. 39)
   - Manager is hired by the Board to implement policies (Art. 44)
   - Manager has no ownership stake - purely professional relationship
   - Board represents member interests, not investor interests

2. **Democratic Oversight vs Hierarchical Control**
   - Members exercise control through General Assembly
   - Board provides oversight between GA meetings
   - Manager operates within policy framework set by Board
   - Transparency and member access to information required

3. **Member Participation in Organizational Processes**
   - Members participate in planning through GA and committees
   - Member feedback shapes service delivery
   - Democratic principle extends to organizational culture
   - Staff may also be members (dual relationship)

4. **Balancing Professional Management with Cooperative Values**
   - Efficiency must not override member welfare
   - Professional systems adapted to democratic culture
   - Business metrics balanced with social metrics
   - Manager must understand and respect cooperative identity

5. **Dual Accountability: To Board AND to Membership**
   - Direct accountability: Manager → Board of Directors
   - Indirect accountability: Manager → General Assembly (through Board)
   - Manager's performance ultimately judged by member satisfaction
   - Transparency to membership even when reporting to Board

### Manager-Board Relationship in Cooperatives

**Reference: RA 9520 Articles 44-47 (Officers)**

#### Separation of Governance and Management

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                     GENERAL ASSEMBLY                            │
│               (Supreme Authority - Art. 38)                     │
│                 Elects Board, Sets Policies                     │
└─────────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┘
                              │ Elects
                              ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    BOARD OF DIRECTORS                           │
│               (Governance - Art. 39-43)                         │
│   - Sets policy and strategic direction                         │
│   - Approves budgets and major decisions                        │
│   - Hires and supervises Manager                                │
│   - Accountable to General Assembly                             │
└─────────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┘
                              │ Hires & Supervises
                              ▼
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                        MANAGER                                  │
│                 (Management - Art. 44-47)                       │
│   - Implements Board policies                                   │
│   - Manages day-to-day operations                               │
│   - Supervises staff                                            │
│   - Reports to Board regularly                                  │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### Clear Roles: Board vs Manager

| Function | Board (Governance) | Manager (Management) |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Planning** | Approve strategic plan | Develop operational plans |
| **Policy** | Set policies | Implement policies |
| **Budget** | Approve budget | Prepare and execute budget |
| **Personnel** | Hire Manager | Hire and manage staff |
| **Oversight** | Monitor performance | Achieve targets |
| **Accountability** | To General Assembly | To Board of Directors |
| **Decision Level** | Strategic, policy | Operational, tactical |

#### Manager as Advisor (Not Decision-Maker)

The Manager serves as a professional advisor to the Board:

- **Provides Information**: Data, analysis, options for Board decisions
- **Recommends Actions**: Professional recommendations on operational matters
- **Implements Decisions**: Executes Board decisions faithfully
- **Does NOT Decide**: Major decisions remain with Board
- **Does NOT Vote**: Manager has no vote in Board meetings (unless member-elected)

#### Communication and Reporting Expectations

**Frequency:**
| Report Type | Frequency | Content |
|-------------|-----------|---------|
| Operational Report | Monthly | Activities, achievements, issues |
| Financial Report | Monthly/Quarterly | Income, expenses, cash flow |
| Performance Report | Quarterly | KPIs, targets vs actuals |
| Strategic Report | Annually | Progress on strategic plan |
| Special Reports | As needed | Significant issues, opportunities |

**Quality Standards:**
- Clear, concise, factual
- Highlights variances and exceptions
- Includes recommendations
- Transparent about challenges

#### Performance Evaluation of Manager by Board

**Evaluation Criteria:**
1. Achievement of operational targets
2. Financial management effectiveness
3. Staff management and development
4. Member service quality
5. Compliance with policies and regulations
6. Communication with Board
7. Alignment with cooperative values

**Evaluation Process:**
1. Board sets annual performance objectives
2. Quarterly progress reviews
3. Annual formal evaluation
4. 360-degree feedback (staff, members, committees)
5. Board decision on compensation and continuation

### POLC Framework Applied to Cooperatives

#### Planning in Cooperatives

| Standard Planning | Cooperative Planning |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| Top-down goals | Include member input (GA resolutions) |
| Shareholder value focus | Member benefit + community impact |
| Competitive strategy | Cooperative development + collaboration |
| Profit targets | Service quality + sustainability targets |

**Cooperative Planning Process:**
1. **Member Input**: Gather needs through surveys, forums, GA discussions
2. **Board Direction**: Strategic framework approved by Board
3. **Manager Development**: Operational plans aligned with Board direction
4. **Committee Input**: Specialized inputs from Audit, Education, Election committees
5. **GA Approval**: Major plans presented to General Assembly
6. **Alignment Check**: Ensure alignment with ICA Cooperative Principles

**Aligning with Cooperative Principles:**
- Principle 1: Voluntary membership affects recruitment plans
- Principle 2: Democratic control affects decision processes
- Principle 3: Member economic participation affects service pricing
- Principle 5: Education affects training and information plans
- Principle 6: Cooperation among coops affects partnership strategies
- Principle 7: Concern for community affects CSR/social activities

#### Controlling in Cooperatives

**Transparency Requirements:**

| Control Area | Transparency Measure |
|--------------|---------------------|
| **Financial** | Audited financial statements available to members |
| **Operational** | Regular reports to Board and GA |
| **Performance** | KPI dashboards accessible to members |
| **Decisions** | Minutes of Board meetings (non-confidential) |
| **Compliance** | Regulatory status disclosed |

**Member Oversight Mechanisms:**

1. **Audit Committee (Art. 48)**
   - Independent of Board and Management
   - Elected by General Assembly
   - Examines financial records
   - Reports directly to GA
   - Internal audit function

2. **General Assembly Rights**
   - Approve annual financial statements
   - Question Board and Management
   - Receive annual reports
   - Remove officers if necessary

3. **Committee Audits**
   - Credit Committee reviews loan quality
   - Education Committee reviews training effectiveness
   - Special committees for specific areas

**Performance Metrics for Cooperatives:**

| Category | Metrics |
|----------|---------|
| **Financial** | Profitability, liquidity, solvency, efficiency |
| **Membership** | Growth, retention, satisfaction, participation |
| **Service** | Quality, accessibility, responsiveness |
| **Social** | Community impact, education hours, employment |
| **Governance** | GA attendance, Board meeting frequency, compliance |

### Common Cooperative Management Challenges

#### 1. Board Micromanagement

**Symptoms:**
- Board involved in day-to-day decisions
- Manager seeks Board approval for routine matters
- Staff bypass Manager to consult Board members
- Board meetings dominated by operational details

**Causes:**
- Unclear separation of governance vs management
- Manager lacks confidence or competence
- Board members are former managers
- Lack of trust between Board and Manager

**Solutions:**
- Develop clear Board-Manager protocol
- Define decision authority levels
- Focus Board meetings on strategic/policy matters
- Train Board on governance role
- Regular Board-Manager dialogue sessions

#### 2. Manager Overreach into Governance

**Symptoms:**
- Manager making policy decisions unilaterally
- Manager controlling information to Board
- Manager influencing Board elections
- Manager treating cooperative as "their" organization

**Causes:**
- Weak or inexperienced Board
- Long-tenured Manager with accumulated power
- Lack of governance training for Board
- Poor accountability mechanisms

**Solutions:**
- Strengthen Board through training
- Establish term limits for Manager
- Regular performance evaluation by Board
- Rotate Board leadership
- Independent Audit Committee oversight

#### 3. Lack of Professional Management Skills

**Symptoms:**
- Poor financial management
- Inadequate record-keeping
- Weak internal controls
- Reactive rather than proactive management
- High error rates in operations

**Causes:**
- Manager promoted from within without training
- Limited budget for professional development
- Rural location limits access to training
- Preference for "cooperative-minded" over "professionally skilled"

**Solutions:**
- Invest in Manager training (invoke `/academy`)
- Partner with training service providers
- Hire management consultants for capacity building
- Consider shared management services with other coops
- Mentoring from experienced cooperative managers

#### 4. High Staff Turnover

**Symptoms:**
- Frequent resignations
- Difficulty attracting qualified staff
- Institutional knowledge loss
- Disruption in service delivery

**Causes:**
- Below-market compensation
- Limited career growth opportunities
- Poor working conditions
- Weak management/leadership
- Better opportunities elsewhere

**Solutions:**
- Review compensation competitiveness
- Create career development paths
- Improve workplace culture
- Strengthen management practices
- Implement retention strategies (training, recognition)

#### 5. Balancing Member Satisfaction with Efficiency

**Symptoms:**
- Members demand services that are unprofitable
- Pressure to keep prices low despite rising costs
- Difficulty saying "no" to member requests
- Operational inefficiencies tolerated for member convenience

**Causes:**
- Democratic principle creates pressure to please
- Short-term member demands vs long-term sustainability
- Lack of member understanding of business realities
- Insufficient member education

**Solutions:**
- Member education on cooperative economics
- Transparent financial communication
- Involve members in difficult decisions
- Balance services with sustainability
- Develop patronage refund system to share surplus

### Practical Scenarios

#### Scenario 1: Onboarding a New Professional Manager

**Situation:** A primary cooperative with 500 members is hiring its first professional manager. Previously, the Board Chair handled day-to-day operations. The Board wants to ensure a smooth transition.

**Recommended Approach:**

**Phase 1: Pre-Onboarding (Before Manager Starts)**
1. Finalize job description with clear scope
2. Define performance objectives for first year
3. Prepare orientation materials (bylaws, policies, history)
4. Identify Board liaison for transition period
5. Communicate to staff and members about new Manager

**Phase 2: First 30 Days - Learning**
1. Orientation to cooperative (history, culture, values)
2. Meet all staff individually
3. Review key documents (financials, plans, reports)
4. Attend committee meetings as observer
5. Visit service locations and member businesses
6. Meet key external stakeholders (CDA/CSEA, banks, partners)

**Phase 3: 30-60 Days - Understanding**
1. Assess current operations and systems
2. Identify quick wins and urgent issues
3. Build relationships with Board members
4. Begin regular reporting rhythm
5. Develop preliminary improvement recommendations

**Phase 4: 60-90 Days - Action Plan**
1. Present 100-day assessment to Board
2. Propose priority initiatives
3. Establish management team routines
4. Implement quick wins
5. Begin strategic planning process

**Key Success Factors:**
- Board Chair provides transition support
- Clear Board-Manager communication protocols
- Manager respects cooperative culture
- Staff given time to adjust
- Members informed of changes

---

#### Scenario 2: Improving Board-Manager Communication

**Situation:** A credit cooperative has experienced conflict between the Board and Manager. Board members complain they don't get enough information. The Manager complains the Board micromanages and doesn't trust her. Member confidence is declining.

**Recommended Approach:**

**Step 1: Diagnose the Issue**
1. Interview Board members individually (neutral facilitator)
2. Interview Manager separately
3. Review Board meeting minutes
4. Assess communication frequency and quality
5. Identify specific incidents and patterns

**Step 2: Develop Communication Protocol**
1. Agree on regular reporting schedule
2. Define content and format of reports
3. Establish between-meeting communication norms
4. Create escalation procedure for urgent matters
5. Set expectations for response times

**Step 3: Clarify Roles and Authorities**
1. Workshop on governance vs management
2. Develop decision authority matrix
3. Define what requires Board approval vs Manager decision
4. Document and formalize in policy

**Step 4: Build Relationship**
1. Informal Board-Manager dialogue sessions
2. Joint strategic planning retreat
3. Board appreciation for Manager achievements
4. Manager acknowledgment of Board oversight role
5. Regular check-ins between Board Chair and Manager

**Step 5: Monitor and Adjust**
1. Quarterly review of communication effectiveness
2. Anonymous feedback from Board and Manager
3. Adjust protocols as needed
4. Celebrate improvements

**Sample Decision Authority Matrix:**

| Decision Type | Manager Authority | Board Approval Required |
|---------------|------------------|-------------------------|
| Daily operations | Full authority | Not required |
| Staff hiring (below Manager) | Authority with reporting | Not required |
| Purchases below P50,000 | Authority within budget | Not required |
| Purchases P50,000-P200,000 | Recommend | Required |
| Purchases above P200,000 | Recommend | GA approval |
| Loans (per loan policy) | Authority | Not required |
| Loans exceeding policy | Recommend | Required |
| New policies | Recommend | Required |
| Strategic initiatives | Recommend | Required |

---

#### Scenario 3: Building Professional Management Capacity

**Situation:** A multipurpose cooperative in a rural area relies on the Manager who has been with the cooperative for 20 years. The Manager learned on the job and handles everything. The Board is concerned about succession and wants to professionalize management.

**Recommended Approach:**

**Phase 1: Assessment (1-2 months)**
1. Document current Manager's responsibilities
2. Assess current systems and processes
3. Identify gaps in professional practices
4. Evaluate staff capabilities
5. Benchmark against well-managed cooperatives

**Phase 2: Structure Development (2-3 months)**
1. Design appropriate organizational structure
2. Develop job descriptions for key positions
3. Create policies and procedures manual
4. Establish internal controls
5. Set up management reporting systems

**Phase 3: Capacity Building (6-12 months)**
1. Training program for current Manager
   - Formal management training (invoke `/academy`)
   - Exposure visits to model cooperatives
   - Mentoring from experienced cooperative Manager
2. Staff development
   - Cross-training to reduce single-point dependency
   - Skills training for specialized functions
3. System improvements
   - Management information system
   - Financial management software
   - Documentation of institutional knowledge

**Phase 4: Succession Planning (Ongoing)**
1. Identify potential successors (internal or external)
2. Development plan for successor candidates
3. Gradual transfer of responsibilities
4. Knowledge documentation and transfer
5. Board involvement in succession decisions

**Capacity Building Curriculum (invoke `/academy`):**

| Module | Duration | Topics |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Management Fundamentals | 2 days | POLC, management roles, skills |
| Financial Management | 2 days | Budgeting, analysis, reporting |
| Operations Management | 2 days | Processes, quality, efficiency |
| Human Resource Management | 1 day | Staffing, performance, development |
| Strategic Planning | 1 day | Vision, planning, implementation |
| Cooperative Governance | 1 day | Board-Manager relations, compliance |
| Leadership Development | 1 day | Leadership styles, communication |

## BARMM Context: Moral Governance Framework (BDP 2023-2028)

The Bangsamoro Development Plan 2023-2028 establishes **Moral Governance** as the centerpiece of BARMM's leadership and governance. This framework provides the context for all management practices within BARMM cooperatives, social enterprises, and government organizations.

### BARMM Vision

> "A Bangsamoro that is united, enlightened, self-governing, peaceful, just, morally upright, and progressive."

### BARMM Mission

> "Guided by moral governance and in pursuit of genuine and meaningful autonomy, the Bangsamoro government ensures the necessary conditions for enduring peace and sustained socioeconomic development suitable to the systems of life, needs, and aspirations of its people by providing services to communities, ensuring multi-stakeholder participation, and facilitating appropriate partnership."

### Overall Development Goal

**An empowered, cohesive, and progressive Bangsamoro:**
- **Empowered**: Improved capacity of communities through access to quality services and economic opportunities
- **Cohesive**: Improved relationships between stakeholders, reduced conflict, forged partnerships
- **Progressive**: Sustained ability to optimize resources in addressing development needs

---

### 12 Pillars of Moral Governance

The BDP 2023-2028 establishes 12 principles that define moral governance in BARMM. These principles should guide all management practices:

| Pillar | Description | Management Application |
|--------|-------------|------------------------|
| **1. Integrity** | Public servants should not bind themselves in any manner that influences execution of duties | Managers must avoid conflicts of interest; make decisions based on merit, not personal benefit |
| **2. Accountability** | Be open and transparent about all decisions and actions; document decisions | Regular reporting to Board/stakeholders; maintain comprehensive records |
| **3. Transparency and Honesty** | Declare private interests; resolve conflicts to protect public interest; make transactions public | Open books policy; accessible financial reports; disclosure requirements |
| **4. Sense of Patriotism** | Invest in way of life, achievements, rich history; maintain and protect reputation | Promote local products; support community development; uphold organizational reputation |
| **5. Striving for Excellence** | Uphold legitimacy of actions; prioritize process over results for the good of all | Quality management systems; continuous improvement; ethical processes |
| **6. Cultural Understanding and Tolerance** | Understand socioeconomics and differences between societies; appreciate diversity | Culturally-sensitive management; inclusive practices; respect for diverse stakeholders |
| **7. Unwavering Loyalty to God** | Public officials as leaders must follow rules and regulations set by authorities | Alignment with Islamic principles where applicable; ethical leadership |
| **8. Justice** | Serve without prejudice; equity without regard for personal relationships or alliances | Fair treatment of all staff and members; merit-based decisions |
| **9. Inclusivity** | Foster inclusive environment; free from discrimination based on religion, ethnicity, culture | Diversity and inclusion policies; accessible services; non-discrimination |
| **10. Objectivity** | Make merit-based decisions; nothing for personal gain | Evidence-based decisions; fair procurement; transparent selection processes |
| **11. Dialogue and Meaningful Engagement** | Ask community opinion; specify needs; achieve positive change for both parties | Stakeholder consultation; participatory planning; feedback mechanisms |
| **12. Balance Between Practical and Moral** | Balance practical duties with moral/ethical values in public interest | Sustainable business practices; ethical operations; values-driven management |

### Development Goal 1: Stable, Just, and Accountable Government

The BDP 2023-2028's first development goal directly relates to management and governance:

**Stable Government:**
- Continuity of service delivery even during emergencies and political events
- Effective and efficient execution of duties by ministries and offices
- Organizational resilience to internal and external shocks

**Just Government:**
- Government as primary agent of economic and social justice
- Prudent exercise of powers to advance public interest
- Leadership ensuring inclusive and pervasive growth

**Accountable Government:**
- Institutions, leaders, and staff held accountable for use of resources
- Accountability frameworks, mechanisms, systems, and processes
- Recognition for good performance; learning from lessons; stopping bad practices
