## Definition

> A cooperative is an autonomous association of persons united voluntarily to meet their common economic, social and cultural needs and aspirations through a jointly-owned and democratically-controlled enterprise.

**Key characteristics**:
- Autonomous (self-governing)
- Voluntary association
- Common needs and aspirations
- Jointly-owned
- Democratically-controlled

## Values

### Core Values

| Value | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Self-help** | Members help themselves through collective action |
| **Self-responsibility** | Members take responsibility for their cooperative |
| **Democracy** | Member control through democratic processes |
| **Equality** | Equal rights and opportunities for all members |
| **Equity** | Fair treatment and distribution of benefits |
| **Solidarity** | Members support each other and the community |

### Ethical Values

Members believe in:
- **Honesty** - Truthfulness in all dealings
- **Openness** - Transparency in operations
- **Social responsibility** - Concern for broader society
- **Caring for others** - Looking after fellow members and community

## The Seven Principles

### Principle 1: Voluntary and Open Membership

Cooperatives are voluntary organisations, open to all persons able to use their services and willing to accept the responsibilities of membership, without gender, social, racial, political or religious discrimination.

**Key points**:
- Membership is voluntary, not forced
- Open to all who can use services
- Members must accept responsibilities
- No discrimination of any kind

### Principle 2: Democratic Member Control

Cooperatives are democratic organisations controlled by their members, who actively participate in setting their policies and making decisions. Men and women serving as elected representatives are accountable to the membership.

**Key points**:
- One member, one vote (in primary cooperatives)
- Active member participation
- Elected representatives are accountable
- Democratic at all levels

### Principle 3: Member Economic Participation

Members contribute equitably to, and democratically control, the capital of their cooperative. At least part of that capital is usually the common property of the cooperative.

**Capital allocation**:
1. Developing the cooperative (reserves)
2. Benefiting members in proportion to transactions (patronage refund)
3. Supporting other activities approved by membership

**Key points**:
- Equitable capital contribution
- Limited compensation on capital subscribed
- Surplus belongs to members collectively

### Principle 4: Autonomy and Independence

Cooperatives are autonomous, self-help organisations controlled by their members. If they enter into agreements with other organisations, including governments, or raise capital from external sources, they do so on terms that ensure democratic control by their members and maintain their cooperative autonomy.

**Key points**:
- Self-governing organizations
- External agreements must preserve member control
- Independence from government and other entities
- Autonomy is non-negotiable

### Principle 5: Education, Training and Information

Cooperatives provide education and training for their members, elected representatives, managers, and employees so they can contribute effectively to the development of their co-operatives. They inform the general public – particularly young people and opinion leaders – about the nature and benefits of co-operation.

**Key points**:
- Education for all stakeholders
- Training for effective contribution
- Public awareness campaigns
- Youth engagement

### Principle 6: Cooperation Among Cooperatives

Cooperatives serve their members most effectively and strengthen the cooperative movement by working together through local, national, regional and international structures.

**Key points**:
- Inter-cooperative collaboration
- Federated structures
- Movement solidarity
- Collective strength

### Principle 7: Concern for Community

Cooperatives work for the sustainable development of their communities through policies approved by their members.

**Key points**:
- Community development focus
- Sustainability commitment
- Member-approved policies
- Local impact priority

## Historical Context

### Rochdale Pioneers (1844)

The modern cooperative movement traces its roots to 28 weavers in Rochdale, England who established the Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers in 1844. Their original principles formed the basis for today's cooperative principles.

### Manchester Congress (1995)

The current Statement on the Cooperative Identity was adopted at the ICA's Centenary Congress in Manchester in 1995, updating and consolidating the principles for the modern era.

### Guidance Notes (2016)

In 2016, the ICA Principles Committee released detailed Guidance Notes on the Cooperative Principles, providing practical application guidance for the 21st century.

## Application to Philippine Context

RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) explicitly adopts the ICA cooperative principles. Article 4 states that cooperatives shall be guided by these universally accepted cooperative principles.

**Philippine adaptations**:
- Training requirements for officers (Principle 5)
- Community Development Fund (Principle 7)
- Federation system (Principle 6)
- Democratic governance requirements (Principle 2)
