## Agency Theory

### Origin
Developed by Jensen & Meckling (1976), rooted in separation of ownership and control.

### Core Premise
Managers (agents) may prioritize personal interests over shareholder (principal) interests, creating the "agency problem."

### Key Mechanisms
| Mechanism | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| **Monitoring** | Board oversight of management |
| **Incentive Alignment** | Performance-based compensation |
| **Bonding** | Management commitments to act in owners' interest |
| **Transparency** | Regular financial reporting |

### Governance Implications
- Strong independent board oversight
- Separation of CEO and Chair roles
- Independent audit committee
- Performance-linked executive compensation
- Enhanced disclosure requirements

### Strengths
- Clear accountability structures
- Addresses real conflicts of interest
- Widely applicable framework

### Limitations
- Assumes opportunism as default behavior
- Can create excessive monitoring costs
- May undermine trust and collaboration

## Stewardship Theory

### Origin
Developed by Donaldson & Davis (1991) as alternative to agency theory.

### Core Premise
Managers are naturally motivated stewards who identify with organizational goals and act in the organization's best interests.

### Key Characteristics
| Characteristic | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Trust-Based** | Assumes managers are trustworthy |
| **Intrinsic Motivation** | Managers find fulfillment in organizational success |
| **Collaborative** | Emphasis on cooperation over control |
| **Long-Term Orientation** | Focus on sustainable value creation |

### Governance Implications
- Greater management autonomy
- Less emphasis on monitoring
- Unified leadership (combined CEO/Chair acceptable)
- Empowerment over control
- Focus on enabling structures

### Strengths
- Reduces monitoring costs
- Fosters innovation and initiative
- Builds organizational commitment

### Limitations
- May be naive about human nature
- Vulnerable to exploitation
- Less accountability mechanisms

## Stakeholder Theory

### Origin
R. Edward Freeman's "Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach" (1984).

### Core Premise
Organizations have responsibilities to all stakeholders, not just shareholders. Value creation should benefit employees, customers, suppliers, communities, and environment.

### Stakeholder Categories
| Category | Stakeholders |
|----------|--------------|
| **Internal** | Shareholders, employees, management |
| **External** | Customers, suppliers, creditors |
| **Social** | Community, government, environment |

### Governance Implications
- Stakeholder representation on boards
- Broader definition of organizational success
- Triple bottom line reporting (people, planet, profit)
- Corporate social responsibility integration
- Stakeholder engagement mechanisms

### Strengths
- Holistic view of organizational responsibility
- Long-term sustainability focus
- Ethical foundation for governance

### Limitations
- Complexity in balancing competing interests
- Difficulty measuring stakeholder value
- Potential dilution of accountability

## Resource Dependency Theory

### Origin
Pfeffer & Salancik (1978).

### Core Premise
Organizations depend on external resources for survival. Boards provide access to critical resources through director networks.

### Board Roles Under Resource Dependency
| Role | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Linking** | Connect organization to external resources |
| **Legitimizing** | Enhance organizational credibility |
| **Advising** | Provide expertise and counsel |
| **Signaling** | Communicate quality to stakeholders |

### Governance Implications
- Board composition based on resource needs
- Directors selected for networks and expertise
- Interlocking directorates
- Advisory board utilization

## Theory Application Guide

### When to Apply Each Theory

| Situation | Recommended Theory |
|-----------|-------------------|
| High agency costs, distrust | Agency Theory |
| Strong organizational culture, aligned values | Stewardship Theory |
| Multiple stakeholder interests, social mission | Stakeholder Theory |
| Need external resources, legitimacy | Resource Dependency |
| Cooperative or member-owned | Stakeholder + Stewardship |
| Social enterprise | Stakeholder + Stewardship |
| Publicly traded corporation | Agency + Stakeholder |

### Hybrid Approaches

Most effective governance combines elements from multiple theories:

**For Cooperatives:**
- Stakeholder theory (member focus)
- Stewardship theory (trust in elected leaders)
- Democratic governance principles

**For Social Enterprises:**
- Stakeholder theory (dual mission)
- Stewardship theory (mission-driven leaders)
- Agency theory (accountability to funders)

**For Corporations:**
- Agency theory (shareholder accountability)
- Stakeholder theory (ESG integration)
- Resource dependency (strategic board composition)
