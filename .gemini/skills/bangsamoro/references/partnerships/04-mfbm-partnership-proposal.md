# e-Governance Partnership Proposal

## For the Ministry of Finance, Budget and Management (MFBM)

> **Date**: January 2, 2026
> **Classification**: Strategic Partnership Proposal
> **Reference**: EGOV-2026-004

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This proposal presents a comprehensive Budget Portal designed to implement the complete Bangsamoro Budget System Act (BBSA) lifecycle in digital form. As the fiscal gatekeeper of the Bangsamoro Government, MFBM manages a PHP 114.07 billion annual budget across 15 ministries and numerous agencies. The proposed e-Governance Portal provides the visibility, control, and automation necessary to ensure this substantial public investment is managed with efficiency, transparency, and accountability.

Estonia's digital finance systems process 98% of tax returns in under five minutes and save an estimated 2% of GDP annually through administrative efficiency. For BARMM, similar capabilities would yield over PHP 2 billion in annual savings while dramatically improving fiscal accountability. The proposed Budget Portal brings these capabilities to MFBM through a platform specifically designed for BBSA compliance.

With an initial partnership investment of PHP 250,000 per month over six months, MFBM can pilot a platform that transforms budget management from periodic reporting to real-time fiscal command.

## 2. e-Governance and Its Value to the Bangsamoro Government

### 2.1 The Fiscal Management Imperative

Government financial management requires balancing competing demands: rapid disbursement to enable program delivery, rigorous controls to ensure accountability, transparent reporting for oversight bodies, and efficient processing to minimize administrative burden. Traditional paper-based systems struggle with these competing requirements, often achieving accountability only through cumbersome processes that delay service delivery.

Digital fiscal systems resolve this tension through automated controls embedded in workflows, real-time visibility that replaces periodic reporting, and audit trails generated automatically rather than through manual documentation.

### 2.2 Case Study: Estonia's Digital Finance

Estonia's financial management systems demonstrate what comprehensive digitalization achieves:

| System | Function | Outcome |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **e-Tax** | Tax administration | 98% of returns filed in under 5 minutes |
| **e-Business Register** | Financial reporting | Real-time company financial data |
| **Treasury System** | Government payments | Same-day processing of all transactions |
| **Digital Invoicing** | Procurement payments | Automated verification and payment |
| **Budget Management** | Appropriation tracking | Real-time execution visibility |

The financial impact is substantial:

- **2% of GDP saved annually** through digital signatures and reduced paperwork
- **EUR 820 per citizen** saved through digital efficiency
- **Zero fraud** in digital identity-based financial transactions
- **99.9% system availability** ensuring continuous operations

Most significantly, Estonia's digital treasury enables the Prime Minister and Finance Minister to monitor budget execution in real-time—a capability the proposed platform provides to MFBM leadership.

### 2.4 The E-Governance Act of 2025 (RA 12254)

On September 5, 2025, President Ferdinand R. Marcos Jr. signed Republic Act 12254, the **E-Governance Act**, institutionalizing the Philippines' transition to digital governance. This landmark legislation provides both the mandate and framework for BARMM's digital transformation.

#### Key Provisions

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Digital-First Mandate** | All government services must transition to digital platforms |
| **Chief Information Officers** | Every agency must designate a CIO to lead digital transformation |
| **E-Government Master Plan** | DICT must formulate comprehensive digital transformation blueprint |
| **Interoperability Framework** | Agencies must enable secure data sharing through APIs |
| **Cybersecurity Standards** | Mandatory security assessments and CERT establishment |

#### Compliance Timeline

| Timeline | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| **1 Year** | All cities and municipalities must establish digital portals or adopt eLGU system |
| **1 Year** | Government must complete transition to e-governance |
| **3 Years** | E-Government Master Plan reviewed and upgraded |
| **Annually** | E-Government maturity surveys and compliance reports |

#### E-Government Programs Mandated

1. **Citizen Frontline Delivery Services Platform** - Unified citizen service access
2. **Electronic Local Government Unit (eLGU) System** - Standardized LGU digital services
3. **Government Digital Payment Systems** - Integrated e-payment gateway
4. **Government Public Key Infrastructure** - Secure digital signatures
5. **Online Public Service Portal** - Single window for government services
6. **Philippine Digital Health System** - Integrated health information

#### Implications for BARMM

RA 12254 creates both **obligation and opportunity** for BARMM:

- **Compliance Requirement**: BARMM ministries and LGUs must meet digital governance standards
- **Funding Access**: Access to E-Government Interoperability Fund for implementation
- **Technical Support**: DICT cooperation for digital transformation roadmap
- **Leadership Opportunity**: Early adoption positions BARMM as compliance leader

The law addresses a critical gap—DICT now has authority to enforce digital integration across all government agencies, making digital transformation not optional but mandatory.

### 3.2 The Budget Portal: BBSA Lifecycle

The Budget Portal implements all four phases of the BBSA budget lifecycle:

**Phase 1: Budget Preparation**

| Function | Capability |
|----------|------------|
| **Budget Policy** | Ceiling determination, tier budgeting parameters |
| **MOA Submissions** | Digital Forms 100-500 (BBP Forms), proposal workflow |
| **BEP Development** | Budget Execution Plan with multi-year projections |
| **Line Item Management** | UACS-compliant classification and breakdown |
| **Consolidation** | Government-wide budget aggregation |

**Phase 2: Budget Authorization**

| Function | Capability |
|----------|------------|
| **Legislative Coordination** | Bill filing, committee referral, reading stages |
| **Budget Defense** | Hearing schedules, MOA preparation status, commitments |
| **Amendment Tracking** | Proposed changes, impact assessment, disposition |
| **GAAB Management** | Enacted appropriations, year-over-year comparison |

**Phase 3: Budget Execution**

| Function | Capability |
|----------|------------|
| **Allotment Release** | SARO/GAABAO processing, MOA allotment tracking |
| **Obligation Management** | ORS/OBRS workflow, procurement commitment tracking |
| **Disbursement** | NCA/NCAA processing, payment status monitoring |
| **Utilization Monitoring** | Real-time rates by expense class (PS/MOOE/CO) |

**Phase 4: Budget Accountability**

| Function | Capability |
|----------|------------|
| **Financial Reporting** | BFAR automated generation, variance analysis |
| **Performance Reporting** | Output-based budget monitoring, KPI tracking |
| **Audit Coordination** | COA finding management, action plan tracking |
| **Compliance Monitoring** | Submission status, deadline tracking, alerts |

## 6. Partnership Terms

### 6.1 Proposed Arrangement

This partnership recognizes MFBM's central role in BARMM's fiscal architecture:

1. **Portal Ownership**: MFBM as primary owner of the Budget Portal
2. **Standard Setting**: MFBM defines budget data formats and workflows
3. **Process Authority**: MFBM configures approval chains and controls
4. **Access Management**: MFBM approves MOA user permissions
5. **Reporting Authority**: MFBM generates official fiscal reports

### 6.2 Data Governance

| Aspect | Arrangement |
|--------|-------------|
| **Transaction Data** | MOAs input; MFBM approves and monitors |
| **Consolidation** | MFBM has government-wide visibility |
| **Controls** | System enforces MFBM-defined limits |
| **Audit Access** | COA receives read-only access as required |

### 6.3 Success Metrics

The pilot phase will be evaluated against:

- Transaction processing time improvements
- User adoption across MFBM and pilot MOAs
- Execution rate improvements in pilot agencies
- COA compliance rate achievements
- User satisfaction and system reliability

## 7. Conclusion

MFBM bears responsibility for the fiscal health of the Bangsamoro Government—a PHP 114 billion enterprise serving millions of citizens. Traditional paper-based processes cannot provide the visibility, control, and efficiency that this responsibility demands.

The proposed Budget Portal implements the complete BBSA lifecycle in digital form, providing MFBM with real-time command over government finances. The Fiscal Command Center delivers visibility that transforms fiscal management from periodic reporting to continuous monitoring.

Estonia demonstrated that digital fiscal systems generate substantial returns through efficiency and error prevention. Their success came from committed implementation of integrated platforms that embedded controls in workflows rather than adding them as administrative burden.

The initial investment of PHP 250,000 per month over six months provides a pathway to validate the platform's value. This represents less than 0.002% of the budget that MFBM manages—a minimal commitment for the potential transformation it enables.

We respectfully request MFBM's consideration of this partnership and welcome the opportunity to demonstrate the Budget Portal's capabilities in detail.
