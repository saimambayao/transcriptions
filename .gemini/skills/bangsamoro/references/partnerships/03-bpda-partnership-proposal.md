# e-Governance Partnership Proposal

## For the Bangsamoro Planning and Development Authority (BPDA)

> **Date**: January 2, 2026
> **Classification**: Strategic Partnership Proposal
> **Reference**: EGOV-2026-003

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This proposal presents a specialized digital platform designed to transform BPDA's role from periodic plan compilation to real-time strategic coordination. As the planning nerve center of the Bangsamoro Government, BPDA requires visibility into all ministry strategic plans, development programs, and resource allocations. The proposed e-Governance Portal provides this capability through a Government-Wide Planning Consolidation interface—a unique tool that no other agency in BARMM will possess.

Estonia's digital governance transformation demonstrated that data-driven planning accelerates development outcomes. Their integrated systems process 2.2 billion government transactions annually, enabling policy decisions based on real-time information rather than periodic reports. For BPDA, similar capabilities mean the difference between reactive plan compilation and proactive strategic coordination.

With an initial partnership investment of PHP 250,000 per month over six months, BPDA can pilot a platform that fundamentally enhances its capacity to fulfill its mandate under the Bangsamoro Organic Law.

## 2. e-Governance and Its Value to the Bangsamoro Government

### 2.1 The Planning Challenge in Modern Governance

Development planning in complex government structures faces inherent challenges. Multiple agencies pursue overlapping mandates, resources are allocated through fragmented processes, and coordination relies on periodic meetings and manual information exchange. The result is often duplicated programs, misaligned priorities, and delayed course corrections.

Digital governance resolves these challenges by creating shared information spaces where planning data flows automatically between agencies. Real-time visibility replaces periodic reporting. Automated analysis identifies gaps and overlaps. Planning becomes a continuous process rather than an annual exercise.

### 2.2 Case Study: Estonia's Data-Driven Government

Estonia's digital transformation created a government where policy decisions are informed by real-time data rather than historical reports. Key achievements relevant to planning functions:

| System | Function | Planning Impact |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| **X-Road** | Secure data exchange | 52,000+ organizations sharing data seamlessly |
| **Population Registry** | Citizen information | Real-time demographic data for planning |
| **Business Registry** | Enterprise data | Economic activity visibility for development planning |
| **State Portal** | Service delivery | Usage data informing service improvement |
| **Analytics Platform** | Cross-government analysis | Evidence-based policy formulation |

The economic impact of data-driven governance is substantial. Estonia saves an estimated 1,345 working years annually through digital efficiency—time redirected from administration to analysis and strategic work.

### 2.4 The E-Governance Act of 2025 (RA 12254)

On September 5, 2025, President Ferdinand R. Marcos Jr. signed Republic Act 12254, the **E-Governance Act**, institutionalizing the Philippines' transition to digital governance. This landmark legislation provides both the mandate and framework for BARMM's digital transformation.

#### Key Provisions

| Provision | Requirement |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Digital-First Mandate** | All government services must transition to digital platforms |
| **Chief Information Officers** | Every agency must designate a CIO to lead digital transformation |
| **E-Government Master Plan** | DICT must formulate comprehensive digital transformation blueprint |
| **Interoperability Framework** | Agencies must enable secure data sharing through APIs |
| **Cybersecurity Standards** | Mandatory security assessments and CERT establishment |

#### Compliance Timeline

| Timeline | Requirement |
|----------|-------------|
| **1 Year** | All cities and municipalities must establish digital portals or adopt eLGU system |
| **1 Year** | Government must complete transition to e-governance |
| **3 Years** | E-Government Master Plan reviewed and upgraded |
| **Annually** | E-Government maturity surveys and compliance reports |

#### E-Government Programs Mandated

1. **Citizen Frontline Delivery Services Platform** - Unified citizen service access
2. **Electronic Local Government Unit (eLGU) System** - Standardized LGU digital services
3. **Government Digital Payment Systems** - Integrated e-payment gateway
4. **Government Public Key Infrastructure** - Secure digital signatures
5. **Online Public Service Portal** - Single window for government services
6. **Philippine Digital Health System** - Integrated health information

#### Implications for BARMM

RA 12254 creates both **obligation and opportunity** for BARMM:

- **Compliance Requirement**: BARMM ministries and LGUs must meet digital governance standards
- **Funding Access**: Access to E-Government Interoperability Fund for implementation
- **Technical Support**: DICT cooperation for digital transformation roadmap
- **Leadership Opportunity**: Early adoption positions BARMM as compliance leader

The law addresses a critical gap—DICT now has authority to enforce digital integration across all government agencies, making digital transformation not optional but mandatory.

## 6. Partnership Terms

### 6.1 Proposed Arrangement

This partnership recognizes BPDA's central role in BARMM's planning architecture:

1. **Planning Authority**: BPDA defines planning data standards for the platform
2. **Consolidation Access**: Exclusive government-wide planning visibility
3. **Quality Assurance**: BPDA validates ministry planning submissions
4. **Reporting Authority**: BPDA generates official planning reports
5. **Steering Participation**: BPDA representation on e-Governance Steering Committee

### 6.2 Data Governance

| Aspect | Arrangement |
|--------|-------------|
| **Ministry Data** | MOAs input directly; BPDA views consolidated data |
| **BPDA Access** | Read-only consolidation (cannot modify MOA data) |
| **Data Standards** | BPDA defines planning data requirements |
| **Quality Control** | BPDA validates completeness and consistency |

### 6.3 Success Metrics

The pilot phase will be evaluated against:

- Completeness of MOA planning data in the system
- BPDA staff adoption and utilization rates
- Reduction in manual data gathering time
- Report generation efficiency improvements
- User satisfaction and capability assessment

## 7. Conclusion

BPDA's mandate requires comprehensive visibility into government planning across all ministries and agencies. Traditional approaches—manual data collection, periodic reporting, paper-based analysis—cannot provide the real-time, comprehensive view that effective coordination demands.

The proposed e-Governance Portal provides BPDA with unique capabilities that transform its role from periodic plan compilation to continuous strategic coordination. The Government-Wide Planning Consolidation interface delivers visibility that no other agency possesses, enabling BPDA to fulfill its mandate as the Bangsamoro Government's planning nerve center.

Estonia and Singapore demonstrated that digital planning systems accelerate development outcomes. Their success came not from superior resources but from committed implementation of integrated digital platforms. BPDA has an opportunity to establish similar capabilities for BARMM.

The initial investment of PHP 250,000 per month over six months provides a pathway to validate the platform's value for BPDA's specific needs. Success in this pilot establishes the foundation for government-wide planning integration that serves the Bangsamoro Development Plan's ambitious goals.

We respectfully request BPDA's consideration of this partnership and welcome the opportunity to demonstrate the planning consolidation capabilities in detail.
