## Workflow: Marketing in Cooperatives

This section provides practical guidance on how marketing applies specifically to cooperatives, distinct from corporate marketing approaches.

### How Cooperative Marketing Differs from Corporate Marketing

| Aspect | Corporate Marketing | Cooperative Marketing |
|--------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **Primary Audience** | Customers (external) | Member-owners (internal AND external) |
| **Value Proposition** | Product/service benefits | Membership benefits + products/services |
| **Brand Values** | Competitive differentiation | ICA Cooperative Principles (RA 9520 Art. 4) |
| **Customer Relationship** | Transactional | Patronage-based, lifetime membership |
| **Decision Making** | Top-down, executive | Democratic (GA approval for major initiatives) |
| **Success Metrics** | Profit, market share | Member satisfaction, patronage, social impact |
| **Community Role** | CSR as add-on | Community concern as core principle |

**Key Differences Explained:**

1. **Member-Owners as Primary Audience**: Unlike corporations where shareholders and customers are separate, cooperative members ARE the owners. Marketing must serve dual purposes: attract new members AND engage existing member-owners.

2. **Values-Based Marketing**: The 7 ICA Cooperative Principles (enshrined in RA 9520 Art. 4) are not just internal values but marketable differentiators:
   - Voluntary and Open Membership
   - Democratic Member Control
   - Member Economic Participation
   - Autonomy and Independence
   - Education, Training and Information
   - Cooperation Among Cooperatives
   - Concern for Community

3. **Cooperative Identity as Differentiator**: The cooperative model itself is a unique selling proposition. "Owned by members, not shareholders" is a powerful message for values-conscious consumers.

4. **Patronage-Based Relationships**: Members earn patronage refund based on their transactions with the cooperative, creating a fundamentally different customer loyalty model.

5. **Democratic Decision-Making**: Major marketing initiatives may require GA approval. Marketing plans must consider cooperative governance structures.

6. **Community Focus vs Profit Maximization**: The 7th Cooperative Principle (Concern for Community) means marketing should emphasize community impact, not just organizational growth.

#### 1. Marketing Cooperatives (RA 9520 Art. 23)

A distinct type of cooperative whose primary purpose is to collectively market members' products.

> **RA 9520 Article 23 - Marketing Cooperative**: "One which is organized to act as a marketing agent for the sale of the member's products..."

**Purpose**: Enable small producers (farmers, fisherfolk, artisans) to achieve collective bargaining power in the market.

**Key Functions**:
- Aggregate members' products for bulk sales
- Negotiate better prices through volume
- Establish quality standards and grading
- Develop collective branding
- Access markets unavailable to individual producers
- Provide market information to members

#### 2. Marketing FOR Cooperatives

Promoting any cooperative's products and services to consumers (external marketing).

**Focus Areas**:
- Product/service promotion
- Brand awareness
- Customer acquisition
- Sales growth
- Market expansion

#### 3. Member Recruitment Marketing

Attracting new member-owners to join the cooperative (internal growth marketing).

**Focus Areas**:
- Membership benefits communication
- Common bond outreach
- Conversion of customers to members
- Member retention and engagement

#### 4. Cooperative Identity Marketing

Promoting the cooperative difference to differentiate from corporate competitors.

**Focus Areas**:
- ICA Cooperative Marque usage
- Values-based messaging
- Community impact stories
- Trust and transparency

### Marketing Cooperatives (RA 9520 Art. 23) - Detailed Guide

#### Definition and Purpose

**Legal Basis**: RA 9520 Article 23

> "A marketing cooperative is one organized to act as a marketing agent for the sale of the member's products..."

**Primary Purpose**: To collectively market members' products, giving small producers access to markets and bargaining power they could not achieve individually.

#### Value Proposition for Farmer/Producer Members

| Without Marketing Coop | With Marketing Coop |
|------------------------|---------------------|
| Sell individually to traders | Collective selling at negotiated prices |
| Accept prevailing prices | Collective bargaining power |
| No quality standards | Standardized grading and quality |
| Limited market access | Access to larger buyers, exporters |
| Individual transportation costs | Pooled logistics and storage |
| No brand identity | Collective brand recognition |
| Price information asymmetry | Market intelligence shared |

#### Collective Bargaining Power

**How It Works**:
1. Members commit to sell through the cooperative
2. Cooperative aggregates products
3. Volume enables negotiation with larger buyers
4. Better prices flow back to member-producers
5. Patronage refund based on volume contributed

**Marketing Cooperative Success Factors**:
- Member commitment to sell through the coop
- Quality control and grading systems
- Reliable aggregation and logistics
- Strong buyer relationships
- Transparent pricing and patronage calculation

#### Quality Standards and Collective Branding

**Quality Management**:
- Establish grading standards (e.g., Grade A, B, C)
- Train members on quality requirements
- Implement quality inspections at collection
- Reject or downgrade non-compliant products
- Create traceability systems

**Collective Brand Development**:
1. Create unified brand identity for member products
2. Register trademarks for cooperative brand
3. Develop packaging and labeling standards
4. Build brand equity through consistent quality
5. Market the "cooperative produced" value proposition

#### Market Access and Distribution

**Channels Available to Marketing Cooperatives**:
- Direct contracts with processors/manufacturers
- Institutional buyers (schools, hospitals, government)
- Export markets (through cooperatives or traders)
- Retail partnerships (supermarkets, specialty stores)
- Online marketplaces (including Negosyo Bangsamoro platform)
- Coop-to-coop sales (Cooperation Among Cooperatives principle)

### Member Recruitment Marketing

#### Target Audience: Potential Members with Common Bond

**RA 9520 Art. 4** requires cooperatives to be "organized by persons bound by a common bond of interest."

**Common Bond Types**:
| Type | Example | Recruitment Focus |
|------|---------|-------------------|
| Occupational | Teachers' coop | Teachers in the municipality/region |
| Community | Barangay MPC | Residents of the barangay |
| Institutional | University coop | Students, faculty, staff |
| Associational | Women's coop | Women's organization members |
| Industry | Farmers' coop | Farmers growing same crops |

#### Membership Benefits Messaging

**Core Benefits to Communicate**:

1. **Patronage Refund**: "Earn back based on how much you use the coop"
2. **Democratic Participation**: "Your voice matters - one member, one vote"
3. **Access to Services**: Credit, insurance, training, markets
4. **Community Ownership**: "Be an owner, not just a customer"
5. **Cooperative Education**: Free training and seminars
6. **Network Benefits**: Connection to other cooperatives

**Sample Messaging Framework**:
```
WHY JOIN [Cooperative Name]?

Own It: Become a member-owner, not just a customer
Earn It: Receive patronage refund based on your transactions
Decide It: Your vote matters in the General Assembly
Learn It: Access free trainings and seminars
Connect It: Join a community of [X] members
```

#### Recruitment Channels

| Channel | Best For | Tactics |
|---------|----------|---------|
| **Member Referrals** | Trust-based recruitment | Referral incentives, member ambassadors |
| **Community Events** | Visibility, credibility | Barangay assemblies, fiestas, markets |
| **Local Radio** | Rural reach | Coop hour programs, testimonials |
| **Social Media (Facebook)** | Urban/youth reach | Success stories, membership drives |
| **Partnerships** | Institutional recruitment | LGU, schools, associations |
| **Outreach Missions** | Direct engagement | House-to-house, workplace visits |

#### Conversion: Membership Application Process

**Simplify the Member Journey**:
```
AWARENESS → INTEREST → APPLICATION → ORIENTATION → MEMBERSHIP
    |           |           |              |             |
 Learn of    Attend      Complete      Attend         Vote in
 the coop    info       application    PMES          first GA
             session    + share        seminar
                        capital
```

**Key Conversion Points**:
1. **Information Session**: Clear explanation of membership requirements and benefits
2. **Application Form**: Simple, accessible (consider literacy levels)
3. **Share Capital**: Affordable initial contribution with flexible payment
4. **PMES Seminar**: Required Pre-Membership Education Seminar (CDA MC)
5. **Board Approval**: Timely processing of applications

#### Member Retention and Engagement

**Engagement Ladder**:
```
MEMBER → ACTIVE USER → PARTICIPANT → COMMITTEE → LEADER
   |          |             |            |           |
 Joins    Uses coop    Attends GA    Joins      Runs for
          services     meetings      committee    BOD
```

**Retention Strategies**:
- Regular communication (newsletter, viber/messenger group)
- Member appreciation events
- Timely patronage refund distribution
- Responsive service quality
- Opportunities for participation
- Recognition programs

### Cooperative Identity Marketing

#### ICA Cooperative Marque Usage

The **ICA Cooperative Marque** is the global symbol identifying genuine cooperatives.

**Usage Guidelines**:
- Must be a registered cooperative (with CDA or CSEA in BARMM)
- Use alongside cooperative name/logo
- Follow ICA brand guidelines for sizing and placement
- Never alter the marque design

**Marketing Value**:
- Instant recognition as genuine cooperative
- Association with global cooperative movement
- Trust signal for values-conscious consumers
- Differentiation from non-cooperative competitors

#### "Owned by Members" Messaging

**Core Message Framework**:

```
NOT CORPORATE. NOT GOVERNMENT. MEMBER-OWNED.

[Cooperative Name] is owned by [X] members like you.
Every purchase supports your community.
Every member has a voice and a vote.
```

**Messaging Variations**:
| Context | Message |
|---------|---------|
| Product labels | "Cooperative-owned. Community-powered." |
| Storefront | "Member-owned since [year]" |
| Social media | "Owned by [X] member-families in [location]" |
| Recruitment | "Don't just buy. Own the business." |

#### Values-Based Differentiation

**Translating Cooperative Principles to Marketing Messages**:

| Principle (RA 9520 Art. 4) | Marketing Message |
|---------------------------|-------------------|
| Voluntary & Open Membership | "Everyone is welcome to join" |
| Democratic Member Control | "Your vote, your voice" |
| Member Economic Participation | "Profits shared with members" |
| Autonomy & Independence | "Locally owned, member-controlled" |
| Education, Training, Information | "We invest in our members" |
| Cooperation Among Cooperatives | "Part of a global movement" |
| Concern for Community | "Community first, always" |

#### Community Impact Stories

**Story Framework for Cooperative Marketing**:

1. **Member Success Stories**: How membership changed a member's life
2. **Community Projects**: What the coop has done for the community (CETF, CRCF)
3. **Economic Impact**: Jobs created, income generated, loans provided
4. **Before/After**: Community transformation through the cooperative

**Example Story Structure**:
```
THE CHALLENGE: [Problem the member/community faced]
THE COOPERATIVE: [How the coop provided solution]
THE IMPACT: [Measurable results]
THE FUTURE: [Ongoing commitment]
```

#### Transparency and Trust

**Trust-Building Tactics**:
- Publish annual reports publicly
- Share GA meeting outcomes with community
- Display patronage refund calculations
- Open-door policy for member queries
- Regular financial updates via social media
- Community accountability sessions

### Practical Scenarios

#### Scenario 1: Marketing Plan for a Marketing Cooperative

**Context**: Bangsamoro Farmers Marketing Cooperative (BFMC) has 500 farmer-members growing organic vegetables. They want to increase sales and attract more farmer-members.

**Marketing Objectives**:
1. Increase sales by 30% in 12 months
2. Recruit 100 new farmer-members
3. Establish brand recognition in Cotabato City

**Strategy Development**:

```
STP FOR BFMC

SEGMENTATION:
- B2B: Restaurants, hotels, supermarkets, institutions
- B2C: Health-conscious consumers, expat community
- Members: Existing farmers, potential farmer-members

TARGETING:
- Primary: Restaurants and hotels (volume, consistent orders)
- Secondary: Health-conscious urban consumers
- Membership: Small-scale vegetable farmers in adjacent barangays

POSITIONING:
"For restaurants and conscious consumers who want fresh, quality
vegetables, BFMC is the marketing cooperative that delivers
farm-fresh organic produce because we are owned by 500 local
farmers committed to sustainable agriculture."
```

**7Ps Marketing Mix**:

| P | Strategy |
|---|----------|
| **Product** | Certified organic vegetables, graded by quality, packaged with coop branding |
| **Price** | Premium pricing justified by quality + cooperative values; volume discounts for B2B |
| **Place** | Direct delivery to restaurants; weekend market stall; online orders via Negosyo portal |
| **Promotion** | Farm tours for chefs; sampling at restaurants; Facebook page with farm stories |
| **People** | Train farmers on quality; hire dedicated marketing officer |
| **Process** | Order-to-delivery within 24 hours; consistent quality grading |
| **Physical Evidence** | Branded crates, delivery vehicle signage, farmer ID cards |

**Member Recruitment Component**:
- Partner with LGU agriculture office for farmer list
- Conduct information sessions in adjacent barangays
- Offer "trial membership" for one season
- Showcase successful member-farmers as ambassadors

#### Scenario 2: Member Recruitment Campaign for a Credit Cooperative

**Context**: Bangsamoro Credit Cooperative (BCC) serves a municipality and wants to grow membership from 2,000 to 3,000 members in one year.

**Campaign: "Own Your Future"**

**Target Segments**:
1. Government employees (stable income, payroll deduction possible)
2. OFW families (remittance receivers, need savings/loan services)
3. Small business owners (capital needs)

**Recruitment Message by Segment**:

| Segment | Pain Point | Coop Solution | Message |
|---------|------------|---------------|---------|
| Gov't Employees | High-interest loans | Low-interest coop loans | "Borrow smarter. Own the lender." |
| OFW Families | Remittance fees, no savings | Remittance service, savings | "Your money works harder at BCC" |
| SME Owners | Bank loan rejections | Character-based lending | "We know you. We believe in you." |

**Campaign Tactics**:

1. **Government Employees**:
   - Partner with municipal HR for payroll deduction
   - Lunch-hour information sessions at municipal hall
   - Testimonials from member-employees

2. **OFW Families**:
   - Partner with local remittance centers
   - Orientation for OFW returnees
   - WhatsApp/Viber group for OFW member communication

3. **SME Owners**:
   - Chamber of commerce partnership
   - Business development seminars (free for members)
   - Showcase member-businesses that grew with coop loans

**Conversion Process**:
```
AWARENESS         INTEREST           APPLICATION
(Radio ad,     → (Info session,  → (Simple form,
Facebook post)    testimonials)     P500 share)
                                         ↓
MEMBERSHIP ← ORIENTATION (PMES) ← BOARD APPROVAL
(First GA       (Half-day           (Within
attendance)      seminar)           2 weeks)
```

**Success Metrics**:
- Information session attendees: 500/year
- Application conversion rate: 60%
- Time from application to approval: < 14 days
- First-year member retention: 90%

#### Scenario 3: Rebranding a Cooperative with Cooperative Identity

**Context**: Mindanao Multipurpose Cooperative (MMC) has operated for 20 years but looks "outdated." Members feel the brand doesn't reflect their modern services. They want to rebrand while emphasizing cooperative identity.

**Rebranding Process**:

**Phase 1: Brand Audit**
- Survey members on brand perception
- Analyze competitor positioning
- Review current brand touchpoints
- Assess alignment with cooperative principles

**Phase 2: Brand Strategy**

```
POSITIONING STATEMENT:

For Bangsamoro families and entrepreneurs seeking financial
empowerment, MMC is the member-owned cooperative that combines
modern services with community values, because for 20 years we've
grown together with our 5,000 member-owners.
```

**Brand Pillars**:
1. **Heritage**: 20 years of trusted service
2. **Member-Ownership**: Not a bank, owned by you
3. **Modern Services**: Digital, convenient, accessible
4. **Community Impact**: Investing in Bangsamoro development

**Phase 3: Visual Identity**

| Element | Old | New |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Logo | Generic building icon | Modern mark with community symbol |
| Colors | Faded blue | Vibrant Negosyo Blue (#0056D2) + accent |
| Tagline | "Serving the community" | "Member-owned. Future-ready." |
| Marque | None | ICA Cooperative Marque included |

**Phase 4: Rollout**

1. **Internal First**: Present to BOD, then GA for approval
2. **Member Launch**: Special GA with new brand reveal
3. **Public Launch**: Grand reopening event
4. **Touchpoint Update**: Signage, forms, website, social media

**Cooperative Identity Integration**:
- Include "Member-owned since 2005" in all materials
- Feature member testimonials in launch campaign
- Highlight democratic governance in messaging
- Emphasize patronage refund as member benefit
- Use ICA Cooperative Marque consistently

**Success Metrics**:
- Member awareness of rebrand: 90%
- Member pride score: +20% post-rebrand
- New member inquiries: +30% in 3 months
- Social media engagement: +50%
