# Writing Style Guide — The BARMM Guidebook Voice

This reference file defines the proven writing voice for all BARMM guidebooks. It was extracted from the Bill Drafting Guidebook (330 pages, 11 chapters) and codified here so every future guidebook maintains the same standard.

Read this file during Phase 3 (DRAFT) before writing any chapter.

---

## The Voice in One Sentence

A senior colleague coaching a competent professional -- authoritative, direct, practical.

---

## Core Rules

### Person
Direct second-person ("you", "your") throughout. The reader is a colleague, not a subordinate.

- Good: "You are writing law for the Bangsamoro people."
- Bad: "One should endeavor to draft legislation with due consideration..."
- Bad: "The drafter shall ensure compliance with..."

### Sentence Length
- **Average**: 15-20 words per sentence
- **Maximum**: 30 words (hard limit -- if it needs a semicolon, split it)
- **Exception**: Verbatim legal quotations in blockquotes may exceed 30 words

### Paragraph Length
- **Maximum**: 5 sentences per paragraph
- **One-sentence paragraphs**: Allowed and encouraged for emphasis
- **No wall-of-text**: If a paragraph feels dense, break it up

### Chapter Openings
Every chapter opens with WHY this matters to the reader's work right now. Not history. Not definitions. Not "This chapter explores..."

Good openings:
- "Every BAA you draft will be read by people who did not write it."
- "Someone has to implement this bill. Is the agency ready?"
- "What happens when this bill becomes law? Three things change."

Bad openings:
- "This chapter provides a comprehensive overview of the legislative drafting process..."
- "The history of bill drafting in the Philippines dates back to..."
- "In this section, we will explore the multifaceted dimensions of..."

### Instructions
Imperative mood. Always.

- Good: "Draft the summary before the hearing."
- Good: "Ask yourself: who implements this provision?"
- Bad: "The summary should be drafted prior to the hearing."
- Bad: "It is recommended that the analyst prepare a summary..."

### Examples
Every principle must be followed by a concrete BARMM example. No principle stands alone.

If you state a rule, immediately show what it looks like in practice -- a real BAA citation, a sample provision, a before/after comparison, or a filled-in template.

### Active Voice
80%+ of sentences must use active voice. Passive voice is acceptable only when the actor is genuinely unknown or irrelevant.

- Good: "The committee reviews the bill during first reading."
- Bad: "The bill is reviewed by the committee during first reading."

### Bold Key Terms
Bold the key terms in every section. A busy reader scanning the page should get the gist from bold text alone.

## Golden Paragraphs

These are exemplary passages from the Bill Drafting Guidebook that define the target voice. Use them as calibration benchmarks.

> "Legislative style is not about elegance. It is about precision. A novel can be ambiguous and still be great. A law that is ambiguous has failed."

> "You are writing law for the Bangsamoro people."

> "Put yourself in the implementer's position. You have just been handed this bill. What would confuse you? What would you need to look up? Fix those things before anyone else has to."

> "Every BAA you draft will be read by people who did not write it. Your job is to make sure they understand it the same way you do."

> "A good bill answers three questions on every page: What exactly must happen? Who exactly must do it? What happens if they do not?"

## Anti-Patterns

### Prohibited Phrases

These phrases signal AI-generated or academic writing. Never use them:

| Prohibited Phrase | Why It Fails | Better Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "It is important to note that" | Filler -- adds no information | Delete entirely, just state the thing |
| "In order to" | Wordy -- "to" does the same job | "To" |
| "It should be noted" | Passive filler | Delete or rewrite as direct statement |
| "With regard to" | Bureaucratic throat-clearing | "About" or "On" or delete |
| "In the context of" | Vague academic phrasing | Be specific about the context |
| "It is worth mentioning" | If it is worth mentioning, just mention it | Delete, state directly |
| "As previously mentioned" | Backward-looking filler | Use a cross-reference instead |
| "For the purposes of" | Legalistic filler | "For" or "To" |
| "In light of the foregoing" | Bureaucratic | "Given this" or "Because of this" |
| "The aforementioned" | Legalistic, stiff | Name the thing again or use "this" |
| "It is recommended that" | Passive, hedging | Use imperative: "Do X" |
| "Pursuant to the provisions of" | Legalistic pile-up | "Under [specific section]" |
| "Notwithstanding the foregoing" | Legal boilerplate | "Even so" or "Despite this" |
| "In furtherance of" | Bureaucratic | "To advance" or "To support" |
| "Furthermore" / "Moreover" | Academic connectors | Start a new sentence or paragraph |
| "It should be emphasized that" | If it needs emphasis, bold it | Bold the key phrase instead |

### Comparison Table

| Bad (academic/bureaucratic) | Good (BARMM guidebook voice) |
|---|---|
| "It is hereby recommended that legislative staff undertake a comprehensive review..." | "Review the bill before the hearing. Every provision. Every section." |
| "The implementation of the proposed measure necessitates..." | "Someone has to implement this bill. Is the agency ready?" |
| "In order to fully understand the implications of the proposed legislation..." | "What happens when this bill becomes law? Three things change." |
| "It is worth noting that the legislative calendar impacts the timeline for bill passage..." | "The calendar controls everything. Miss the deadline, and your bill waits another session." |
| "With regard to the budgetary implications of the proposed measure..." | "How much does this bill cost? Who pays?" |

## Audience Profiles

### Parliament Staff
- **Voice**: Peer-coaching. Assumes legislative literacy.
- **Terminology**: Use parliamentary terms directly (plenary, interpellation, first reading, committee report)
- **Urgency frame**: "Prepare for the hearing"
- **Example opening**: "Every BAA you draft will be read by people who did not write it."

### MOA Staff (Ministries, Offices, Agencies)
- **Voice**: Instructional, more step-by-step. Assumes operational focus.
- **Terminology**: Executive/administrative equivalents (approval chain, compliance deadline, COA findings)
- **Urgency frame**: "Meet the compliance deadline"
- **Example opening**: "When a directive lands on your desk, your first question should be: what exactly does this require my office to do?"
- **Key replacements**:
  - "your MP" becomes "your Director" or "your Minister"
  - "the floor" becomes "the approval chain"
  - "interpellation" becomes "review and feedback"
  - Examples from budget execution, program delivery, and COA findings instead of plenary proceedings

### Cross-Cutting Audience
- **Voice**: Coaching with context-setting for both audiences
- **Terminology**: Define parliamentary terms when used, bridge to operational equivalents
- **Example opening**: "Whether you work in the Parliament or a Ministry, the quality of your analysis determines the quality of the decisions your principal makes."

## Chapter Voice vs. Appendix Voice

### Chapter Voice
Explanatory, example-rich, conversational. The chapter coaches the reader through a concept or process.

"Here is why this matters and how to do it well."

Chapters use stories, scenarios, and extended examples. They address the reader directly and frequently. They explain the reasoning behind each instruction.

### Appendix Voice
Clinical but human. Appendices equip the reader with tools, not explanations.

Each template section gets a brief instruction block:
- **Purpose** (1 sentence -- why this section exists)
- **What to include** (2-4 bullets -- what goes here)
- **Sources** (where to find the information -- name specific BARMM institutions)

The instruction block speaks to the reader directly but briefly. The fill-in fields below it are clean and spacious.

**Instruction tone test:**
- Good: "Give the MP a complete snapshot of the bill -- what it does, where it stands, what action is needed next."
- Bad: "This section is intended to provide a comprehensive overview of the legislative measure under consideration."

If the appendix instruction sounds like it came from a bureaucratic form, rewrite it until it sounds like a note from a helpful colleague.

## Self-Check Checklist

Before finalizing any chapter, verify:

- [ ] Every chapter opens with a hook (scenario, question, or direct statement) within the first 50 words
- [ ] No sentence exceeds 30 words (except verbatim legal quotations)
- [ ] No paragraph exceeds 5 sentences
- [ ] "You/your" appears at least once per H2 section
- [ ] Every principle has a concrete BARMM example
- [ ] No prohibited phrases appear anywhere
- [ ] 80%+ of sentences use active voice
- [ ] Key terms are bolded for scannability
- [ ] Instructions use imperative mood ("Draft..." not "The draft should be...")
- [ ] Cross-references link to specific chapters, not vague gestures
