## Workflow: Cooperative-SE Relationship

This section provides practical guidance on the relationship between cooperatives and social enterprises, supporting consulting on structure selection, transitions, and partnerships.

### Cooperatives vs Social Enterprises: Comparison

| Aspect | Cooperative | Social Enterprise |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Legal Framework** | RA 9520 (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) | RA 11524 (BMBE Law) / Various (no dedicated SE law) |
| **Ownership** | Member-owned (at least 15 members for primary coop) | Founder-owned, investor-owned, or hybrid |
| **Governance** | Democratic (one member, one vote) - Art. 5, RA 9520 | Board-directed (may be democratic or not) |
| **Profit Distribution** | Patronage-based (Art. 86, RA 9520) | Mission-reinvestment (typically 50%+ to social purpose) |
| **Regulator** | CDA (national) / CSEA (BARMM) | Various agencies (DTI, SEC, etc.) |
| **Primary Goal** | Member economic benefit + community | Social/environmental impact |
| **Capital** | Member shares + statutory funds | Investor equity, grants, earned revenue |
| **Exit Rights** | Member withdrawal (refund of capital) | Varies by structure |

**Key Legal References:**
- **RA 9520** (Philippine Cooperative Code of 2008) - Governs all cooperatives
- **RA 11524** (BMBE Law) - Covers micro enterprises including some SEs
- **PRESENT Bill** (pending) - Proposed dedicated SE law in the Philippines

### When is a Cooperative a Social Enterprise?

Cooperatives inherently carry social enterprise characteristics through their foundational principles:

```text
COOPERATIVE AS SOCIAL ENTERPRISE

┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  COOPERATIVE PRINCIPLES (ICA) with SE CHARACTERISTICS           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                 │
│  Principle 7: CONCERN FOR COMMUNITY                             │
│  ───────────────────────────────────                            │
│  "Cooperatives work for the sustainable development of         │
│  their communities through policies approved by their members" │
│  → This IS social enterprise thinking embedded in coop DNA     │
│                                                                 │
│  Community Development Fund (Art. 86, RA 9520)                  │
│  ─────────────────────────────────────────────                  │
│  At least 3% of net surplus allocated to community projects    │
│  → Mandatory social impact funding                              │
│                                                                 │
│  Optional Fund (Art. 86, RA 9520)                               │
│  ────────────────────────────────                               │
│  10% of net surplus for social purposes (land, education, etc.)│
│  → Additional social investment vehicle                         │
│                                                                 │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Key Insight:**
- **All cooperatives have SE characteristics** (ICA Principle 7, statutory funds)
- **Not all SEs are cooperatives** (SEs can be corporations, foundations, etc.)
- A cooperative becomes a "full" social enterprise when social mission is primary, not secondary to member economic benefit

### CSEA Dual Mandate

The Cooperative and Social Enterprise Authority (CSEA) in BARMM regulates both cooperatives and social enterprises under a unified framework.

```text
CSEA DUAL MANDATE STRUCTURE

                    ┌─────────────────────┐
                    │        CSEA         │
                    │ (Cooperative and    │
                    │ Social Enterprise   │
                    │ Authority - BARMM)  │
                    └──────────┬──────────┘
                               │
           ┌───────────────────┴───────────────────┐
           │                                       │
           ▼                                       ▼
    ┌─────────────────┐                   ┌─────────────────┐
    │  COOPERATIVES   │                   │ SOCIAL ENTERPRISES│
    │  (RA 9520)      │                   │  (BTA Bill 210)  │
    └────────┬────────┘                   └────────┬────────┘
             │                                     │
             │         SHARED ELEMENTS             │
             │   ┌─────────────────────────┐       │
             └───┤ - FRAMES Compliance     ├───────┘
                 │ - Registration Portal   │
                 │ - Marketplace Access    │
                 │ - Training (MoroAcademy)│
                 │ - Impact Reporting      │
                 │ - Technical Assistance  │
                 └─────────────────────────┘
```

**CSEA Shared Services:**
1. **FRAMES Compliance** - Common compliance framework
2. **Negosyo Bangsamoro Marketplace** - Unified platform for coop and SE products
3. **MoroAcademy** - Shared training and capacity building
4. **Technical Assistance** - Business development support
5. **Impact Measurement** - Standardized reporting

### Choosing Between Cooperative and SE Structure

Use this decision framework when advising organizers:

```text
STRUCTURE SELECTION DECISION TREE

Start: What is the PRIMARY organizational driver?

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
    │                                                                 │
    │  MEMBER ECONOMIC BENEFIT              SOCIAL/ENVIRONMENTAL      │
    │  (mutual benefit for members)         MISSION (beneficiaries    │
    │                                       may not be members)       │
    │            │                                    │               │
    │            ▼                                    ▼               │
    │  ┌─────────────────┐                   ┌─────────────────┐      │
    │  │   COOPERATIVE   │                   │ SOCIAL ENTERPRISE│      │
    │  └─────────────────┘                   └─────────────────┘      │
    │                                                                 │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### Choose COOPERATIVE When:

| Factor | Cooperative Advantage |
|--------|----------------------|
| **Common bond exists** | Members share occupation, region, or association (Art. 4, RA 9520) |
| **Democratic governance valued** | One member, one vote regardless of capital |
| **Patronage-based benefits** | Returns based on member transactions, not capital |
| **Long-term member commitment** | Stable membership with withdrawal rights |
| **Tax incentives important** | RA 9520 tax exemptions (Art. 61) |
| **Government support** | CDA/CSEA programs for cooperatives |

#### Choose SE When:

| Factor | SE Advantage |
|--------|--------------|
| **Social mission is primary** | Beneficiaries may not be members/owners |
| **Flexible governance needed** | Can adopt board structures suited to mission |
| **Investor capital required** | Can offer equity to impact investors |
| **Rapid scaling planned** | More flexible growth mechanisms |
| **Hybrid revenue model** | Mix of earned income, grants, donations |
| **Innovation focus** | More agile structure for social innovation |

#### Hybrid Models

Some organizations combine both:
- **Cooperative running SE activities** - Coop uses Community Development Fund for social programs
- **SE with cooperative principles** - SE adopts democratic governance, patronage concepts
- **Multi-stakeholder cooperative** - Different member classes (workers, consumers, producers)

### Practical Scenarios

Use these scenarios for consulting guidance:

#### Scenario 1: Advising Organizers on Structure Choice

**Situation:** A group of 20 Bangsamoro artisans wants to formalize their business. They make traditional products and want to preserve cultural heritage while earning income.

**Consulting Approach:**

```text
STEP 1: Assess Key Factors
├── Common bond? → YES (artisans, Bangsamoro, same craft)
├── Member economic benefit primary? → YES (income for artisans)
├── Social mission? → YES (cultural preservation)
├── Governance preference? → Democratic (traditional consensus)
├── External investor needed? → NO (member capital sufficient)
└── Scale ambition? → Moderate (regional market)

STEP 2: Recommendation
├── PRIMARY: Form a COOPERATIVE (Art. 23, RA 9520 - Producer Cooperative)
├── ENHANCE: Use Community Development Fund (3%) for cultural programs
└── ALTERNATIVE: Register with CSEA for both coop and SE benefits

STEP 3: Implementation Path
├── Gather 15+ founding members (Art. 14, RA 9520)
├── Prepare: AOC, By-Laws, Economic Survey (Art. 15)
├── Register with CSEA (BARMM)
└── Apply for tax exemptions (Art. 61)
```

#### Scenario 2: Converting SE to Cooperative Structure

**Situation:** A social enterprise providing agricultural training has grown. The farmer-beneficiaries want ownership stake and democratic control.

**Consulting Approach:**

```text
STEP 1: Assess Conversion Feasibility
├── Are beneficiaries willing to become members? → YES
├── Can they contribute share capital? → YES (minimal)
├── Is democratic governance appropriate? → YES
├── Will mission be maintained? → YES (educational purpose)
└── Are 15+ members available? → YES (50+ farmers)

STEP 2: Conversion Path
├── Form Organizing Committee from beneficiaries
├── Conduct Pre-Membership Education Seminar (PMES)
├── Prepare cooperative formation documents
├── SE transfers assets to new cooperative
└── New cooperative registers with CSEA

STEP 3: Key Considerations
├── Asset transfer implications
├── Staff transition (become member-employees)
├── Governance transition period
└── Maintain SE activities under coop structure
```

#### Scenario 3: Building Coop-SE Partnership

**Situation:** A fishing cooperative wants to partner with a marine conservation SE for sustainable fishing practices.

**Consulting Approach:**

```text
STEP 1: Partnership Assessment
├── Coop strength: Fishing expertise, member network, market access
├── SE strength: Conservation knowledge, impact measurement, grant access
├── Shared interest: Sustainable fishing, community livelihood
└── Complementarity: HIGH (different capabilities, aligned mission)

STEP 2: Partnership Structure Options
├── Option A: Service Agreement
│   └── SE provides training, coop pays fee or in-kind
├── Option B: Joint Project
│   └── Co-apply for grant, share implementation
├── Option C: Value Chain Partnership
│   └── Coop supplies, SE markets "sustainable" products
└── Option D: Formal Alliance (MOU)
    └── Long-term collaboration framework

STEP 3: Implementation
├── Draft MOU defining roles, responsibilities, benefits
├── Establish joint steering committee
├── Define shared impact indicators
├── Create communication and reporting protocols
└── Build mutual capacity over time
```
