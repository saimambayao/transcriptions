# Enterprise Development Theories

Core theories and models for enterprise growth and organizational development.

## Enterprise Growth Stage Models

### Greiner's Growth Model (1972, revised 1998)

Six phases of organizational growth, each ending in a predictable crisis:

| Phase | Growth Through | Crisis Point | Coop/SE Application |
|-------|---------------|--------------|---------------------|
| 1 | Creativity | Leadership | Founder must professionalize |
| 2 | Direction | Autonomy | Staff need delegation |
| 3 | Delegation | Control | Systems needed |
| 4 | Coordination | Red-Tape | Avoid bureaucracy |
| 5 | Collaboration | Internal Growth | Team-based work |
| 6 | Alliances | Strategic | Partnerships |

**Key Insight**: Each phase duration varies by industry. For BARMM cooperatives:
- Phase 1→2: Professionalize management early
- Phase 2→3: Delegate to committees
- Phase 3→4: Avoid bureaucratic overhead

### Adizes Corporate Lifecycle (PAEI Model)

Ten stages with corresponding leadership role requirements:

**PAEI Roles:**
- **P** = Producer (results-oriented)
- **A** = Administrator (systems-oriented)
- **E** = Entrepreneur (innovation-oriented)
- **I** = Integrator (people-oriented)

| Stage | PAEI | Characteristics |
|-------|------|-----------------|
| Courtship | paEi | Idea stage, high enthusiasm |
| Infancy | Paei | Risk-taking, founder-dependent |
| Go-Go | PaEi | Rapid growth, weak systems |
| Adolescence | pAEi | Professional management |
| Prime | PAEi | Optimal balance |
| Stable | PAeI | Risk aversion begins |
| Aristocracy | pAeI | Form over function |
| Recrimination | -A-I | Internal blame |
| Bureaucracy | --A- | Systems paralysis |
| Death | ---- | Organization ceases |

**Cooperative Application:**
- Struggle often in Adolescence (founder → professional)
- Democratic governance provides built-in "I" capacity
- Goal: Reach and maintain Prime

### Cooperative-Specific: 4-in-3 Development Model

**Four Cornerstones:**
1. **Vision**: Articulation of founding group's hopes
2. **Talent**: People invested in success
3. **Capital**: Financial resources for all stages
4. **Systems**: Organized, integrated methods

**Three Stages:**
1. **Organizing**: Form initial group
2. **Feasibility & Planning**: Validate concept
3. **Implementation**: Launch and operate

**Six Phases of Formation:**
1. Agree on compelling problem/opportunity
2. Agree cooperative can address it
3. Reach adequate trust among members
4. Secure member commitment
5. Secure stakeholder commitment
6. Assemble staff and assets

## Organizational Development Theories

### Capacity Building Framework

**Multiple-Capitals Approach:**
- Individual level: Staff skills and knowledge
- Organizational level: Systems and processes
- Network level: Partnerships and alliances
- Community level: External relationships

**Core Capacity Assessment (CCAT) - 4 Capacities:**
1. **Leadership Capacity**: Vision, influence, decision-making
2. **Management Capacity**: Resources, systems, processes
3. **Technical Capacity**: Program delivery skills
4. **Adaptive Capacity**: Innovation, learning, change

**Three Approaches to Building:**
1. **Individual**: Training, mentoring, resources
2. **Organizational**: Tools, equipment, software
3. **Systemic**: Policy, governance, structure

### Leadership Development

**Level 5 Leadership (Jim Collins):**
- Personal humility + professional will
- Self-effacing, quiet, reserved
- Aspirational for organization, not self

**70-20-10 Development Framework:**
- 70% Challenging assignments
- 20% Developmental relationships
- 10% Coursework and training

**McKinsey 7-S Framework:**
1. Shared Values (center)
2. Strategy
3. Structure
4. Systems
5. Style
6. Skills
7. Staff

## Democratic Governance Integration

### Cooperative Governance Principles

**Key Considerations:**
- Democratic processes are participative
- Size and lifecycle stage influence processes
- Member participation crucial to survival

**Board Functions:**
- Policy creation
- Strategic planning leadership
- Annual budget setting
- Coordination between teams
- Manager oversight

**Member Voice Mechanisms:**
- Member facilitation structures
- Advisory panels
- Strategic planning consultations
- Long-range scenario planning

### Challenges and Solutions

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| Power concentrated at top | Multiple boards/councils |
| Low voting turnout | Technology for engagement |
| Rubber-stamp governance | Bi-directional communication |
| Information asymmetry | Regular member forums |

## Social Enterprise Adaptations

### Dual Mission Management

**Hybridity Definition:**
Social enterprises pursue financial sustainability AND social purpose simultaneously.

**11 Clusters of Resilience:**
1. Economic and social effects
2. Entrepreneurship
3. Financial sustainability
4. Hybridity management
5. Bricolage (resource recombination)
6. Social impact
7. Social network
8. Social innovation
9. Strategic orientation
10. Digital technology
11. Institutional support

### Bricolage Strategy

For resource-constrained SEs:
- Combine available know-how and resources
- Iterative piloting, evaluating, implementing
- "Zero investment improvement" alignment
- Network leverage for resource acquisition

## BARMM Enterprise Development Context (BDP 2023-2028)

### Development Goal for Economy

**Sectoral Goal:** Equitable, Competitive, and Robust Economy

The 2nd Bangsamoro Development Plan (2023-2028) establishes enterprise development as central to achieving economic transformation in the region.

### Macroeconomic Targets 2023-2028

| Indicator | Baseline | Target |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| GRDP Annual Growth Rate | 7.5% (2020-2021) | 8%-9% sustained |
| Industry/Services Growth | 7.9%/6.6% | 10%-12% double-digit |
| Gross Capital Formation | 16.91% of GRDP | 18%-20% of GRDP |
| Poverty Incidence | 29.80% (2021) | 20%-25% |
| Unemployment Rate | 3.80% (2021) | 3%-5% |

### Enterprise Development Strategic Framework

**Key Sectoral Objectives:**
1. Trade and industry, investments, and tourism increased and sustained
2. Access to decent employment improved
3. Functional cooperatives and social enterprises increased

**Sub-sector Objectives for Enterprise Development:**
- Competitive MSMEs with value-added products and profitability increased
- Economic corridors linking investments, freeports, production, and services developed
- Halal-compliant enterprises and industries established with wider market access
- Investments generated in BARMM increased
- Profitability of cooperatives increased
- Established social enterprises increased

### MSME Sector Assessment

**Current State (2021):**
- 8,427 MSMEs registered (161% increase from 2020)
- Total investments from MSMEs (2017-2021): Php 9.02 billion
- Jobs generated (2017-2021): 62,166
- BARMM accounts for only 0.92% of national BNR

**Key Challenges:**
- Most MSMEs operate in informal sector
- Limited access to production and processing facilities
- Limited access to credit facilities
- Limited digital connectivity
- Mismatch in skills and available resources

### Cooperative and Social Enterprise Context

**Registered Cooperatives (2010-2020):** 12,214
- Maguindanao: 38.24% (4,671)
- Lanao del Sur: 37.29% (4,554)
- Producer cooperatives: 37.58%
- Marketing cooperatives: 27.54%
- Agricultural cooperatives: 12.82%

**Compliance Rate:** Only 11.66% (1,424 cooperatives) are compliant

**Key Challenges:**
- Low compliance rate among registered cooperatives
- Lack of enacted Cooperative and Social Enterprise Code
- Inadequate LGU participation in cooperative development
- Limited Municipal Cooperative Officers (MCOs)

### Investment Climate

**Investment Generated (2010-2021):**
- Total: Php 25.3 billion
- Registered Business Enterprises: 46
- Employment Generated: 20,103

**By Province:**
| Province | Investment (Php) | Employment |
|----------|-----------------|------------|
| Maguindanao | 16.6 billion | 12,923 |
| Tawi-Tawi | 3.6 billion | 2,779 |
| Lanao del Sur | 997 million | 1,963 |
| Basilan | 1.8 billion | 1,620 |
| Sulu | 851 million | 170 |

**Investment Challenges:**
- Low business confidence due to peace/order concerns
- Underdeveloped halal products and services
- High cost of utilities
- Weak BIMP-EAGA linkages
- Lack of industry roadmap
- Poor implementation of ease of doing business

### Priority Enterprise Development Programs

**1. Bangsamoro MSME Development (BMSMED) Program**
- Regional Initiative for Sustainable Enterprise (RISE)
- Negosyo Center expansion
- Propelling Economic and Sustainable Opportunity (PESO)

**2. Growth Enhancement Approach (GEARED) Program**
- Industry Roadmaps (Coffee, Fishing, Weaving)
- Shared Service Facilities (SSF)

**3. Development of Cooperatives and SE Program**
- Profitability and Equity Support Oriented (PESO) Project
- Lakbay Aral sa Social Enterprise (LAKAS SE)

**4. Combatant Transitional Program**
- Organizing former combatants into cooperatives
- Financial and material support for business operations

### Halal Industry Development

The Bangsamoro Halal Board (BHB) leads halal industry development:
- Halal Implementing Rules and Regulations (M.C. 1 series 2021)
- Bangsamoro Standards and Halal Testing Laboratory (BHSTL)
- ISO/IEC 17025:2017 certification for testing services

**Strategic Investment Priorities unique to BARMM:**
1. Halal industry
2. Islamic banking
3. BIMP-EAGA-related investment enterprises

### Enterprise Growth Strategies for BARMM

**Strategy 14:** Improve MSME competitiveness through institutional building, value adding, product development, and market linkages

**Strategy 15:** Strengthen investment promotion at LGUs through capacity building and local investment code formulation

**Strategy 21:** Ensure cooperative profitability through technical/financial assistance, M&E systems, and human resource capacitation

**Strategy 22:** Institutionalize enabling policies for social enterprise establishment

### Development Opportunities

**Unique BARMM Advantages:**
- Bangsamoro Board of Investments (BBOI) for incentives
- BIMP-EAGA proximity and trade linkages
- Halal, Islamic finance, and e-commerce potential
- Young, trainable workforce (42% aged 15-39)
- Rich natural resources and tourism potential

**Public-Private Partnership Modalities:**
- LGU-private sector partnerships
- Inter-LGU partnerships
- National-local partnerships
- Grant funds (TIEZA, development partners)
