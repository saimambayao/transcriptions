## Aaker Brand Equity Model

David Aaker's framework defines brand equity as a set of assets linked to a brand's name and symbol.

### 5 Components of Brand Equity

| Component | Description | Key Question |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|
| **Brand Loyalty** | Strength of customer attachment | Would they switch if alternatives exist? |
| **Brand Awareness** | Recognition and recall ability | Do they know and remember us? |
| **Perceived Quality** | Customer perception of overall quality | Do they see us as quality? |
| **Brand Associations** | Mental connections to the brand | What do they think of when they hear our name? |
| **Proprietary Assets** | Patents, trademarks, channel relationships | What legal protections do we have? |

### Brand Loyalty Pyramid

```
           ┌─────────────────┐
           │   COMMITTED     │ ← Advocates (actively promote)
           │     BUYER       │
           ├─────────────────┤
           │    LIKES THE    │ ← Affection (emotional bond)
           │      BRAND      │
           ├─────────────────┤
           │   SATISFIED     │ ← Habitual (no switching cost)
           │     BUYER       │
           ├─────────────────┤
           │   HABITUAL      │ ← No reason to change
           │     BUYER       │
           ├─────────────────┤
           │   SWITCHERS     │ ← Price-sensitive, no loyalty
           └─────────────────┘
```

### Awareness Pyramid

```
           ┌─────────────────┐
           │ TOP OF MIND     │ ← First brand recalled
           ├─────────────────┤
           │  BRAND RECALL   │ ← Recalled without prompt
           ├─────────────────┤
           │ BRAND RECOGNITION│ ← Recognized when shown
           ├─────────────────┤
           │   UNAWARE       │ ← Never heard of brand
           └─────────────────┘
```

### Application for BARMM Organizations

| Component | Cooperative | Social Enterprise | Government |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|------------|
| **Loyalty** | Member patronage | Community support | Public trust |
| **Awareness** | Local recognition | Impact visibility | Program awareness |
| **Quality** | Product/service standards | Impact delivery | Service reliability |
| **Associations** | Values, community | Mission, impact | Competence, fairness |
| **Assets** | ICA Marque, .coop | Certifications | Official seals |

## Keller CBBE Model

Kevin Lane Keller's Customer-Based Brand Equity (CBBE) model builds brand from the ground up.

### CBBE Pyramid (4 Levels)

```
                    ┌───────────────────┐
                    │    RESONANCE      │ ← Deep loyalty
                    │  (Relationships)  │
                    ├─────────┬─────────┤
                    │JUDGMENTS│FEELINGS │ ← Response
                    ├─────────┼─────────┤
                    │PERFORMANCE│IMAGERY │ ← Meaning
                    ├─────────┴─────────┤
                    │    SALIENCE       │ ← Identity
                    │   (Awareness)     │
                    └───────────────────┘
```

### Building Blocks Explained

| Level | Block | Questions | Examples |
|-------|-------|-----------|----------|
| **1. Identity** | Salience | Who are you? | Recognition, recall, category identification |
| **2. Meaning** | Performance | What are you? | Features, reliability, style, price |
| **2. Meaning** | Imagery | What are you? | User profiles, purchase situations, values |
| **3. Response** | Judgments | What about you? | Quality, credibility, consideration, superiority |
| **3. Response** | Feelings | What about you? | Warmth, fun, excitement, security, social approval |
| **4. Relationships** | Resonance | What about you and me? | Loyalty, attachment, community, engagement |

### Brand Resonance Dimensions

| Dimension | Description | Indicators |
|-----------|-------------|------------|
| **Behavioral Loyalty** | Repeat purchase frequency | Purchase rate, share of wallet |
| **Attitudinal Attachment** | Deep emotional bond | Love for brand, feel special |
| **Sense of Community** | Connection with other users | Kinship, affiliation |
| **Active Engagement** | Investment beyond purchase | Time, energy, money invested |

## Brand Positioning

### Positioning Statement Formula

```
For [target segment], [brand] is the [category/frame of reference]
that [key benefit/point of difference] because [reason to believe].
```

### Example Positioning Statements

**Cooperative Example:**
```
For Bangsamoro farmers seeking fair prices, Cooperative Agriko
is the agricultural marketing cooperative that provides direct
market access because of our network of 50+ institutional buyers.
```

**Social Enterprise Example:**
```
For eco-conscious consumers, Maranao Weaves is the social enterprise
that preserves indigenous artistry while providing sustainable livelihoods
because every purchase supports 100 traditional weavers.
```

### Positioning Strategy Options

| Strategy | Description | Best For |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Benefit Positioning** | Focus on key benefit | Clear functional advantages |
| **User Positioning** | Define by target user | Niche markets |
| **Competitor Positioning** | Position against competitor | Market challengers |
| **Category Positioning** | Define/redefine category | Pioneers, disruptors |
| **Values Positioning** | Align with values | Mission-driven orgs |

### Points of Parity vs Difference

| Concept | Definition | Purpose |
|---------|------------|---------|
| **Points of Parity (POP)** | Shared with competitors | Legitimize category membership |
| **Points of Difference (POD)** | Unique to brand | Provide competitive advantage |

## Brand Architecture

### Architecture Types

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Branded House** | Master brand dominates | Google (Maps, Drive, Photos) |
| **House of Brands** | Individual brands dominate | P&G (Tide, Pampers, Gillette) |
| **Endorsed Brands** | Sub-brands with parent endorsement | Marriott Courtyard, Marriott Ritz-Carlton |
| **Hybrid** | Mix of strategies | Coca-Cola (Coke, Sprite, Fanta) |

### Architecture Decision Criteria

| Factor | Favors Branded House | Favors House of Brands |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Target overlap** | Same customers | Different customers |
| **Brand meaning** | Relevant to all | Category-specific |
| **Risk** | Low risk tolerance | High risk tolerance |
| **Resources** | Limited budget | Multiple budgets |
| **Synergy** | High synergy needed | Independence needed |

### BARMM Organization Architecture

| Org Type | Recommended | Rationale |
|----------|-------------|-----------|
| **Multi-purpose Coop** | Branded House | Leverage trust across services |
| **Multi-product SE** | Endorsed | Balance mission with product identity |
| **Government Agency** | Branded House | Unified government presence |

## Brand Portfolio Strategy

### Portfolio Roles

| Role | Purpose | Characteristics |
|------|---------|-----------------|
| **Flanker** | Protect core brand | Lower price, different segment |
| **Cash Cow** | Generate revenue | Mature, stable |
| **Fighter** | Combat competitors | Value positioning |
| **Prestige** | Elevate perception | Premium tier |
| **Entry Level** | Attract new customers | Accessible |

### Portfolio Optimization Questions

```
□ Does each brand serve a distinct segment?
□ Are there overlapping or competing brands?
□ Is there a clear hierarchy between brands?
□ Can resources be consolidated?
□ Are all brands actively contributing?
```
