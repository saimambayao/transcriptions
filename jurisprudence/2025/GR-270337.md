> The Court disagrees.
> The Constitution unequivocally safeguards every individual against
> unreasonable searches and seizures. As a general rule, no search or seizure
> may be conducted without a judicial warrant issued upon probable cause.
> This protection, enshrined in Article III, Section 242 of the 1987
> Constitution, serves as a bulwark against arbitrary intrusions by the State.43
> Nevertheless, the constitutional proscription against warrantless
> searches and seizures admits of certain exceptions, namely:
> 1. Warrantless search incidental to a lawful arrest;
> 2. Seizure of evidence in "plain view;"
> 3. Search of a moving vehicle;
> 4. Consented warrantless search;
> 5. Customs search;
> 6. Stop and frisk; and
> 7. Exigent and emergency circumstances.44
> Among the recognized exceptions to the constitutional requirement of
> a search warrant is the warrantless search incidental to a lawful arrest.
> Rule 113, Section 5 of the Rules of Court provides for instances when
> a lawful arrest may be effected without a warrant:
