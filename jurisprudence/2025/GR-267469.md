> A close perusal of the Acknowledgment reveals that there may be two
> interpretations: 1) petitioner’s actual interest over the subject property shall
> be determined by records and documents; and 2) petitioner’s interest is
> presumed to be at 50% and should the records and documents prove that the
> interest is more than 50%, then the latter will prevail.
>
> It may be argued that respondent acknowledged petitioner’s share for
> about 50% of the expenses in the acquisition of the subject property and in the
> constructions and improvements when petitioner inserted the phrase
> respondent had financed and paid for about 50% of the expenses in the
> acquisition, construction, and improvement of the subject property. On the
> other hand, it may also be argued that respondent only recognizes the expenses
> contributed by petitioner and to reimburse her for the amount she may have
> incurred for the renovation of the property, provided the same are supported
> by records and documents.
>
> Thus, an ambiguity results as to the share of petitioner in the acquisition
> of the subject property. In resolving this ambiguity, We apply the basic
> principle in the law of contracts, which states that.any ambiguity is to be taken
> contra| proferentem, that is, construed against the party who caused the
> ambiguity which could have avoided it by the exercise of a little more care.”
>
> Here, respondent, being the author of the Acknowledgment, any
> ambiguity in its provision must be taken against her. More, when different
> constructions of a provision are otherwise equally proper, that is to be taken
> which jis the most favorable to the party in whose favor the provision was
> made,>* which in this case is the petitioner. —
>
> We subscribed to the interpretation that petitioner is entitled to 50%
> share of the subject property. First, there is already an acknowledgment on
> the part of respondent that petitioner had financed and paid for about 50% of
> the expenses in the acquisition, construction, and improvement of the subject
> property. Second, taking the entirety of the Acknowledgment, the proviso
> “subject to the determination of the actual percentage of the aforesaid interest
> based on records and documents” simply means that should petitioner’s
> actual share exceed the 50% share she possesses, the excess must be proven

> Notably, the term “capacitated to marry each other” under Article 147
> of the Family Code pertains to the legal capacity of a party to contract
> marriage. The Family Code defines marriage as a union between a man and a
> woman.>® Considering that petitioner and respondent have the same sex when
> they cohabited, they are not capacitated to marry each other, and thus, Article
> 148 governs their property relations.
>
> Under Article 148 of the Family Code, the properties acquired during
> cohabitation can be considered the common property of petitioner and
> respondent if: (1) these were acquired during their cohabitation; and (2) there
> is evidence that the properties were acquired through their actual joint
> contribution of money, property, or industry. Such contributions and
> corresponding shares of the parties are prima facie presumed to be equal.
> However, for this presumption to arise, proof of actual contribution is
> required.>° Thus, if the actual contribution of a party is not proved, there will
> be no co-ownership and there can be no presumption of equal shares. Here,
> the Acknowledgment signed by respondent shows the actual contribution of
> petitioner. Respondent recognized that petitioner is entitled to 50% share of
> the subject property. Applying the doctrine of estoppel which is based on
> public policy, fair dealing, good faith and justice, respondent is now estopped
> to question the contribution of petitioner in the acquisition of the subject
> property. Having admitted the actual contribution of petitioner, their
> corresponding shares are prima facie presumed equal. Thus, with Article 148
> of the Family Code and the Acknowledgement executed by respondent,
> petitioner is a co-owner to the extent of 50% share of the subject property.
>
> Considering that there is co-ownership between petitioner and
> respondent, then each co-owner may demand at any time the partition of the
> thing owned in common, insofar as her share is concerned. Having rightful
> interest over the subject property, petitioner has the right to demand the
> division of the subject property.
