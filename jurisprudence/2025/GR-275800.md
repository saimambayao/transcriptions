> CONCURRING AND DISSENTING OPINIO
>
> CAGUIOA, J.:
>
> The ponencia dismisses the Petition for Mandamus, filed by petitioner
> Elroy John M. Hagedorn, which asks the Court to order the respondent House
> of Representatives (HoR) to issue a resolution certifying the existence of a
> permanent vacancy in the said chamber of Congress in light of the death of
> Edward S. Hagedorn, representative ofithe Third District of Palawan.
>
> I agree with the dismissal of the petition, not because of the reasons
> stated in the ponencia, but on the ground of mootness—considering that a
> special election for the said vacancy could no longer be held at this time. I
> thus fully disagree with the ponencia’s ruling that neither chamber of
> Congress is obliged to issue a resolution certifying to a permanent vacancy,
> should one arise, if order to trigger the duty of the Commission on Elections
> (COMELEC) to hold a special election.
>
> The ponencia anchors its ruling on its finding that COMELEC has the
> power and duty to call for a special election in case of a permanent vacancy
> at the HoR without need of a resolution from the said chamber certifying the
> existence of the permanent vacancy. The ponencia cites Section 4 of Republic
> Act No. 7166,! which it views as having amended Section 1 of Republic Act
> No. 6645.” The relevant provisions of both laws are provided below:

> Since Republic Act No. 7166 no longer mentions a resolution from the
> relevant chamber certifying the existence of a permanent vacancy, the
> ponencia dismisses the present petition for mandamus as it is of the view that
> neither chamber of Congress is required by law to issue anything for the
> COMELEC to call for a special election.
>
> While the conclusion of the ponencia may, at first blush, appear to be
> supported by the letter of Section 4 of Republic Act No. 7166, it is relevant to
> ask how—under the ponencia’s formulation—the COMELEC would have
> official notice of the permanent vacancy in a chamber of Congress. A
> permanent vacancy in a chamber of Congress may arise from various reasons,
> not just from death as in this case, but also from resignation, dismissal (by the
> relevant chamber), or guo warranto (by the electoral tribunal). Given these
> numerous ways by which a permanent vacancy may arise, the ponencia does
> not answer the question of how COMELEC would have official knowledge
> of the said vacancy. Even if the Court were to limit the discussions to
> permanent vacancies due to death, the ponencia still does not answer how
> COMELEC would have official notice of such death. If it is not the relevant
> chamber which has the duty to inform COMELEC, is it the heirs of the
> legislator or any of his or her constituents? May COMELEC rely on news
> reports, if any?
>
> It is thus my considered view that the Resolution referred to in Section
> 1 of Republic Act No. 6645 remains required so that COMELEC would have
> notice of the permanent vacancy. The repealing clause of Republic Act No.
> 7166 does not mention Republic Act No. 6645 despite expressly mentioning
>

> other laws. Thus, Section 1 of Republic Act No. 6645 and Section 4 of
> Republic Act No. 7166 could be harmonized—instead of ruling that the latter
> completely repeals the former—so that the Resolution will still be required
> but Republic Act No. 7166 governs as to when the special election must be
> held.
>
> This formulation is not new and is in fact confirmed by past practice.
> When then Senator Teofisto Guingona, Jr. became Vice-President upon
> nomination by then President Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo and confirmation by
> Congress, the Senate immediately issued (on the same day) a resolution
> certifying to the existence of a vacancy, and calling on COMELEC to fill the
> vacancy through a special election.‘ It is worthy to note that Republic Act No.
> 7166 was enacted in 1991, while the events relating to the vacancy left by
> Senator Guingona happened years after, in 2001. Thus, even with Republic
> Act No. 7166 as the governing law on vacancies in Congress, the chambers
> still understood that it is their duty to certify that a permanent vacancy exists
> therein.
>
> Having said that, I agree that a writ of mandamus cannot be issued in
>
> this case for being moot. The term of the position left by Edward S. Hagedorn
>
> will end in two months, and in fact, the next regular elections is scheduled in
> a month. As a special election for the position of representative of the Third
> District of Palawan is the ultimate objective of this litigation, and considering
> that the same is no longer possible, the present petition is thus already moot.
>
> Still, the aforementioned discussions on the import of Republic Act No.
> 6645 and Republic Act No. 7166 could be laid down by the Court as the issue
> in this case is “capable of repetition yet evading review.” Jurisprudence shows
> that the heart of this exception is that the “challenged action is in its duration
> too short to be fully litigated prior to its cessation or expiration.”° The said
> principle, allowing the Court to rule despite the mootness of the case, is
> primarily applied in election cases in the Philippines’ in light of the three-year
> terms for most elected positions in the government. Considering the relatively
> short time periods available to litigate the issue presented in this case, the
> exception allows the Court to rule that, moving forward, the relevant chamber
