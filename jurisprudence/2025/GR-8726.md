> In ascertaining whether respondent’s conduct was grossly immoral, this
> Court must determine whether her actions constituted bigamy.
>
> In Juni v. Atty. Juni,*' this Court held that disbarment cases are sui
> generis—neither purely civil nor purely criminal—as these involve an
> investigation by the court into the conduct of its officers. Substantial
> evidence, which refers to the relevant evidence that a reasonable mind may
> accept as adequate to support a conclusion, is the quantum of proof needed to
> establish guilt in administrative proceedings.” The evidence does not need to
> comply with the standard of evidence in civil cases, which is preponderant
> evidence, or criminal cases, which is proof beyond reasonable doubt.% As
> long as the quantum of proof | in disciplinary proceedings against lawyers 1S
> established, liability attaches."
>
> In this case, there is substantial evidence to hold respondent liable for
> grossly immoral conduct since her acts constituted the crime of bigamy.
>
> The elements of bigamy are as follows: (1) “that the offender has been
> legally married”; (2) “that the marriage has not been legally dissolved or, in
> case [their] spouse is absent, the absent spouse could not yet be presumed dead

> subsequent marriage”; and (4) “that the second or subsequent marriage has all
> the essential requisites for validity.”*
