> Ruling
> The Court resolves to adopt and approve in full the Resolution dated
> April 14, 2023 of the IBP Board of Governors.
> In its Extended Resolution 11 dated April 14, 2023, the IBP Board of
> Governors ruled that the complaint and its submissions are bereft of any
> evidence on the ownership of respondents of the Facebook accounts where
> the subject statements were posted. Thus, complainant failed to fulfill his
> burden of proof to establish his allegations against respondents by substantial
> evidence. Nonetheless, even assuming that the ownership of the subject
> accounts was proven, the statements made therein were within the sphere of
> the constitutional protection of freedom of expression. •
> We agree.
> First off, it is true that complainant was unable to prove, by substantial
> evidence, that the subject statements were made from the Facebook accounts
> owned by respondents.
> As ordained in Tan v. Alvarico 12 an attorney enjoys the legal
> presumption that he or she is innocent of the charges against him or her until
> the contrary is proved. As a result, the burden of proof is on the complainant
> to establish the allegations of his or her complaint, viz.:
> An attorney enjoys the legal presumption that he [o r she] is innocent
> of the charges against him until the contrary is proved, and that as an officer
> of the Court, he [o r she] is presumed to have performed his [o r her] duties
> in accordance with his [o r her] oath. In disbarment proceedings, the
> quantum of proof is substantial evidence and the burden of proof is on the
> complainant to establish the allegations in his [o r her] complaint.
> Here, the charges against respondents principally rest on the publication
> of the subject Facebook posts by certain accounts which were ostensibly

> But even assuming that the Facebook accounts were truly owned by
> respondents, we agree with the IBP Board of Governors that the subject posts
> fall within the constitutional protection of freedom of expression.
> First, the subject statements or utterances can be segregated into three
> categories: (a) posts regarding pending cases; (b) criticism against
> government officials; and ( c) statements made in pleadings.
> In Belo-Ife nares v. Guevarra, 16 the Court laid down the general
> standard for a proper invocation of freedom of expression as a defense in
> administrative cases:
> Time and again, it has been held that the freedom of speech and of
> expression, like all constitutional freedoms, is not absolute. While
> the freedom of expression and the right of speech and of the press are
> among the most zealously protected rights in the Constitution, every person
> exercising them, as the Civil Code stresses, is obliged to act with justice,
> give everyone his due, and observe honesty and good faith. As such, the
> constitutional right of freedom of expression may not be availed of to
> broadcast lies or half-truths, insult others, destroy lheir name or reputation
> or bring them into disrepute.

> As for the first category of statements, in the landmark case of
> Cabansag v. Fernandez, 17 way back in 1957, the Court ruled that a public
> utterance or publication should not be denied the constitutional protection of
> freedom of speech and press merely because it concerned a judicial
> proceeding still pending in the courts:
> Thus, speaking of the extent and scope of the application of this rule,
> the Supreme Court of the United States said "Clear and present danger of
> substantive evils as a result ofindiscriminate publications regarding judicial
> proceedings justifies an impairment of the constitutional right of freedom
> of speech and press only if the evils are extremely serious and the degree of
> imminence extremely high ..· .. A public utterance or publication is not
> to be denied the constitutional protection of freedom of speech and
> press merely because it concerns a judicial proceeding still pending in
> the courts, upon the theory that in such a case it must necessarily tend
> to obstruct the orderly and fair administration of justice. . . . The
> possibility of engendering disrespect for the judiciary as a result of the
> published criticism of a judge is not such a substantive evil as will justify
> impairment ·or the constitutional right of freedom of speech and press."
> (Bridges vs. California, 314 U.S. 252, syllabi) (Emphasis supplied)

> Neither can petitioner take refuge in the constitutional guarantee of
> freedom of speech and of the press. Although a wide latitude is given to
