> The ponencia explained that officials and personnel of international
> organizations (IOs) generally enjoy functional immunity. However, they may
> invoke diplomatic immunity if there is a treaty or some binding agreement
> which allows them to do so. Though sometimes used interchangeably,
> diplomatic immunity is different from functional immunity. Diplomatic
> immunity refers to those granted to diplomatic agents, as provided under the
> Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations. Diplomatic agents enjoy
> immunity from the civil, criminal, and administrative jurisdiction of the
> receiving state, subject to a few exceptions. However, IOs and their personnel
> only enjoy immunity as necessary for the fulfillment of the I Os' purposes.8
> The ponencia also observed that the question of the applicability of
> functional immunity requires an inquiry into the capacity in which the bank
> officer or personnel was acting. It recognized ADB 's organizational
> immunity, as found in Article 50 of its Charter, as being nearly absolute. It
> contrasted this with the functional immunity of ADB 's personnel, as found in
> Article 55 of the ADB Charter, which applies only to acts done in the
> personnel's official capacities. It declared that an ultra vires act by a bank
> personnel is not covered by any immunity. On this score, the ponencia held
> that in a proceeding where functional immunity of bank personnel is invoked,
> it is necessary to determine in what capacity the ADB personnel was acting.9
