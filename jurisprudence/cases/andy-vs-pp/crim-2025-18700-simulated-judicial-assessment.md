## I. FACTUAL PREMISES

This assessment assumes the following facts — representing the most challenging evidentiary scenario for the defense:

1. **Pre-talaq romantic relationship.** Andy and Charlie were in a romantic relationship before the talaq on January 23, 2025. CCTV footage and private videos showing romantic acts, including kissing, exist from the pre-talaq period. Boony suspected the relationship during the marriage.

2. **Former male partners.** Boony has located other males who were previously intimate with Andy and may present their testimony to establish a pattern of bisexual behavior during the marriage.

3. **Boony's brothers as witnesses.** Boony's brothers — including the brother who witnessed the talaq — may testify that they were "victimized" by Andy (per Boony's characterization), and may provide testimony unfavorable to Andy about his conduct during the marriage.

4. **The talaq.** Andy pronounced talaq on January 23, 2025. Boony accepted and voluntarily departed the apartment. The talaq has not been filed with the Shari'a Circuit Court under Art. 161, PD 1083. **Boony claims she was menstruating on January 23** — raising the question of whether the talaq was validly pronounced during *tuhr* (non-menstrual period) as required by Art. 46(1).

5. **February 12 incident.** Boony went to Andy's office, brought officemates, and proceeded to the apartment. Andy blocked the door. Boony did NOT enter the apartment — her Judicial Affidavit claim that she "slipped in" is false. She glimpsed Andy at the door.

6. **Financial support.** Andy sent P2,000 on February 10 (receipt exists) and P5,000 on February 23 (through Boony's brother). During the marriage, Boony had Andy's ATM card, wallet access, and unrestricted spending. Andy encouraged her to save; she chose not to.

7. **Andy's private statement.** Andy told his boss in a private message: "There is no 'Idda' yet as we are not yet divorced." This message is NOT known to Boony, the prosecution, or the Court.

8. **Boony's post-separation conduct.** Boony obtained a Barangay Protection Order (BPO) on Feb 13 and damaged Andy's property (video evidence; witnessed by barangay/police). She sent defamatory emails to Andy's boss on Feb 17 and Feb 20 (read by officemates). She took Andy's iPad mini without consent on Feb 18. Andy was forced to resign. She posted homophobic content on Facebook. She spread "immorality" claims to other offices, destroying Andy's livelihood.

## III. PER-ISSUE ANALYSIS

### Issue 1: Denial of Financial Support

**The Acharon Standard:** Under *Acharon v. People* (GR No. 224946, En Banc, November 9, 2021):

> "To be punishable by Section 5(i) of R.A. 9262, it must ultimately be proven that the accused had the intent of inflicting mental or emotional anguish upon the woman, thereby inflicting psychological violence upon her, with the willful denial of financial support being the means selected by the accused to accomplish said purpose."

**Prosecution evidence:** Boony's single sentence: *"Financially po hindi po talaga niya ako binibigyan ng personal allowance."*

**Defense evidence:** P2,000 receipt (Feb 10); P5,000 through brother (Feb 23); ATM card access; wallet access; unrestricted spending; Andy encouraging Boony to save; housing unit offered at April 6 settlement.

**Assessment:** The prosecution's evidence on this charge is fatally deficient. One unquantified sentence is contradicted by documented remittances AND unrestricted financial access during the marriage. The pre-talaq CCTV/video evidence and testimony from former partners do not affect this charge — financial support was provided regardless of any relationship with Charlie. **ACQUITTAL on this charge is certain.**

### Issue 2: Sexual Relationship as Psychological Violence — The Central Contest

This is the case's center of gravity. The Court must resolve four sub-questions.

#### 2A: Was the marriage dissolved before February 12?

**PD 1083 Art. 46(1):** "A divorce by talaq may be affected by the husband in a single repudiation..."

**Art. 56:** "'Idda is the period of waiting prescribed for a woman whose marriage **has been** dissolved..." — past perfect tense confirms dissolution precedes 'idda.

**Art. 34(1):** "The husband and the wife are obliged to... observe mutual respect and fidelity" — fidelity is incident to the marriage and terminates with it.

The talaq was pronounced January 23, 2025. Boony accepted and voluntarily departed. Both families were informed. The families met for *kawagib* (divorce settlement) on April 6, 2025. Boony negotiated settlement terms for seven months before withdrawing to press criminal charges.

**The prosecution's counter-argument:** The talaq has NOT been filed with the Shari'a Circuit Court under Art. 161. The Information describes Andy as "the lawful husband." Boony's affidavit proceeds as if the marriage subsisted.

**The Court's finding:** Art. 161 imposes no prescriptive period voiding the talaq for failure to file. The notice is evidentiary ("conclusive evidence that talaq has been pronounced"), not constitutive. The legal effect of talaq is determined by PD 1083, not by the parties' characterizations. Both parties' behavior — physical separation, SMS acknowledgment, settlement negotiations — confirms the divorce was real.

#### 2A-ii: The Menstruation Challenge — Was the Talaq During Tuhr?

**Boony's claim:** She was menstruating on January 23, 2025. PD 1083 Art. 46(1) requires that talaq be pronounced "during her non-menstrual period (tuhr) within which he has totally abstained from carnal relation with her." If she was menstruating, the talaq was not during *tuhr* and is potentially invalid.

**This is the prosecution's strongest attack on the talaq defense.** If the talaq is void, the marriage subsisted on February 12, GR 252739's presumption of intent applies, and the defense's primary shield collapses.

**The Court's analysis:**

**First — the evidentiary question.** How does Boony prove she was menstruating on January 23, 2025? This is a claim about a physiological state 14+ months ago. There are no medical records from that date. It is her word — and her word in a case where she has already made a provably false sworn statement (S15 — entering the apartment). The defense is entitled to challenge this unverified assertion. Menstruation on a specific date is not a judicially noticeable fact; it requires proof.

**Second — the Islamic legal question.** Even if Boony was menstruating, is talaq during menstruation void or merely irregular?

Under classical Sunni jurisprudence — which PD 1083 incorporates (Art. 5 recognizes the Hanafi, Hanbali, Maliki, and Shafi'i schools) — there is a near-unanimous position across all four schools:

**Talaq during menstruation is *haram* (sinful) but *valid* (effective).** This is known as *talaq al-bid'ah* (innovative/irregular divorce). The husband sins by pronouncing talaq outside of *tuhr*, but the divorce takes effect.

The foundational hadith is the case of Abdullah ibn Umar (may Allah be pleased with him), who divorced his wife during menstruation. The Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded him to take her back (*ruju*) — but when asked whether the divorce counted, Ibn Umar confirmed: "Yes, of course." The divorce was sinful but effective.

This is the overwhelming position of Islamic scholarship: Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Malik, Imam al-Shafi'i, and Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal all hold that talaq during menstruation is effective. Only a small minority (notably Ibn Taymiyyah and Ibn Hazm) hold it is void — a position considered heterodox.

**Third — the PD 1083 textual question.** Art. 46(1) says talaq "may be affected by the husband in a single repudiation of his wife **during her non-menstrual period (tuhr)**." The prosecution will argue the word "during" is mandatory — the talaq MUST be during *tuhr* to be valid.

The defense counters:
1. **PD 1083 Art. 5** directs that the Code be interpreted in light of Islamic law and the recognized schools. All four schools hold talaq during menstruation is effective. The Code should be read consistently with the classical position it codifies.
2. **The word "may"** — "A divorce by talaq **may be** affected... during her non-menstrual period" — is permissive, not mandatory. It describes the proper procedure, not a condition of validity. Compare with Art. 161(1): "Any Muslim male who has pronounced a talaq **shall**, without delay, file..." — the word "shall" is mandatory, yet non-filing does not void the talaq.
3. **No PD 1083 provision declares talaq during menstruation void.** If the Legislature intended to void such talaq, it would have included an express nullity clause — as it did for other situations (e.g., Art. 27 on prohibited marriages).
4. **Practical absurdity.** If talaq during menstruation were void, the husband would need to verify his wife's menstrual status before pronouncing — an impossible requirement in many cases, especially where the parties are already in conflict and not communicating.

**Fourth — estoppel by conduct.** Even if the talaq was technically irregular, Boony's conduct for 14 months after January 23 constitutes acceptance and ratification:
- She voluntarily left the apartment on January 23
- She sent the January 30 SMS acknowledging she caused the breakdown
- The families held a *kawagib* (divorce settlement) meeting on April 6
- She negotiated settlement terms for seven months (April–November 2025)
- She filed the complaint as a "wife" but never sought to challenge the talaq's validity in the Shari'ah Court
- She never returned to the apartment or attempted to resume cohabitation (*ruju*)

If Boony believed the talaq was invalid because of menstruation, she would have said so on January 23 — or at any point in the following 14 months. She did not. She accepted the divorce, departed, and conducted herself as a divorced woman. She cannot now, for the first time in the criminal proceeding, claim the talaq was void due to menstruation.

**Fifth — this is a question for the Shari'ah Court, not the RTC.** The validity of talaq under PD 1083 is a question of Muslim personal law within the exclusive original jurisdiction of the Shari'ah Circuit Court (PD 1083, Art. 143). The RTC, which has jurisdiction over the RA 9262 charge, is not the proper forum to adjudicate the validity of an Islamic divorce. If the prosecution wants to challenge the talaq's validity, the proper remedy is to file an action in the Shari'ah Circuit Court — which neither Boony nor the prosecution has done.

**The Court's finding:** The menstruation claim does not void the talaq. Under the prevailing Islamic legal position (all four Sunni schools), talaq during menstruation is sinful but effective. PD 1083 should be read consistently with this position. Even if the talaq were irregular, Boony's 14 months of post-talaq conduct constitutes acceptance. And the validity question properly belongs in the Shari'ah Court, not the RTC.

**On February 12, 2025, Andy and Boony were divorced.**

#### 2B: Does the pre-talaq CCTV/video evidence change the analysis?

The pre-talaq evidence of kissing establishes that a romantic relationship existed during the subsisting marriage. This is marital infidelity. Under *GR 252739* (En Banc, Hernando J., April 16, 2024):

> "In instances of marital infidelity, the requirement of specific criminal intent to cause mental and emotional suffering is already satisfied at the moment the perpetrator commits the act of marital infidelity."

This presumption is powerful. **However**, it applies to marital infidelity — conduct during a subsisting marriage. The critical question is: **can the prosecution use pre-talaq evidence to convict for post-talaq conduct?**

The Information charges acts "on or about **February 12, 2025** and sometime subsequent thereto." Under the Constitution (Art. III Sec. 14(2)), the accused has the right to be informed of the charges. Under the variance doctrine (Rule 120 Sec. 4), conviction must rest on the charged acts.

The pre-talaq CCTV/videos are admissible as **context** under Rule 130 (motive, intent, pattern). They show the relationship existed before the talaq. They explain why Boony experienced anguish on February 12. But they are not the acts charged in the Information.

**The two judicial paths:**

**Path A (ACQUITTAL):** The Information charges February 12. On that date, no marriage existed. Pre-talaq evidence is context, not basis for conviction. Andy's conduct on February 12 (blocking the door for privacy) is not an enumerated act of public ridicule or humiliation under Sec. 5(i).

**Path B (CONVICTION):** RA 9262 Sec. 7 treats VAWC as a "continuing offense." The infidelity-caused anguish began pre-talaq and continued through February 12. GR 252739 (En Banc) intent presumption applies to the pre-talaq infidelity and carries through. The judge reads the Information broadly.

**Path B is legally vulnerable on appeal:** the temporal scope, the variance doctrine, and GR 264870 all favor reversal.

#### 2C: Did Andy cause the "public humiliation" on February 12?

Section 5(i) requires that anguish be caused through **"acts of public ridicule or humiliation, repeated verbal and emotional abuse, denial of financial support... or similar such acts or omissions."** Under *Dinamling v. People* (GR 199522, 2015):

> "Psychological violence is the means employed by the perpetrator, while mental or emotional anguish is the effect caused to or the damage sustained by the offended party."

Under *GR 264870* (Leonen SAJ, April 21, 2025):

> "The acts complained of, which are the means of committing the offense, should be evaluated on whether they were done with the intention of causing mental or emotional anguish, public ridicule or humiliation to the woman or her child. It is when these acts are done with the intent to tilt the scales of power within the relationship against the woman, causing her to lose her autonomy does the State have a legitimate interest in punishing such acts."
>
> "To be sure, the effect on the victim alone, which is the second element, is not enough to sustain a conviction."

**On February 12:** Andy was in his apartment. Boony came uninvited with his officemates. Andy blocked the door — seeking privacy. Boony did NOT enter (her S15 claim is false). Andy did not post on social media. Andy did not flaunt the relationship publicly. Andy did not taunt, mock, or address Boony about Charlie.

**Contrast with conviction cases:** In *GR 270257* (2024), the accused moved his mistress next door, fathered an illegitimate child, and **flaunted the affair on social media** — deliberate public acts. None of these features are present here. The Caguioa concurrence explicitly stated: "marital infidelity per se is not punished by RA 9262."

**The "Boony facilitated" factor:** Boony knew Charlie and agreed to his role as Andy's assistant. They worked together at Andy's office. Boony herself repeatedly directed Andy to sleep at Charlie's apartment during marital conflicts. The prosecution's narrative of a clandestine affair discovered by a victimized wife is contradicted by the complainant's own facilitation of the relationship's proximity.

**Finding:** The "means" element of Sec. 5(i) is not met on February 12. Boony's anguish is credible — but under GR 264870, effect alone is insufficient. Andy did not cause public humiliation through his own acts on the charged date.

#### 2D: How do former male partners and Boony's brothers affect the analysis?

**Former male partners:**

The prosecution may present testimony from men who were previously intimate with Andy to establish a pattern of bisexual behavior during the marriage.

**Admissibility:** This is **character evidence** offered to prove conduct on a specific occasion — generally inadmissible under Rule 130, Sec. 54. The defense should object. However, the prosecution may argue it is admissible as evidence of "similar acts" showing motive, intent, or a behavioral pattern (Rule 130, Sec. 34).

**Even if admitted, the impact is limited.** These witnesses can establish that Andy had prior male relationships. They CANNOT establish:
- That any specific relationship caused Boony anguish through enumerated means under Sec. 5(i)
- That Andy used these relationships as tools of domination against Boony
- That Andy committed any act on February 12, 2025

The testimony proves Andy's sexual orientation — which is not a crime, not an element of any RA 9262 offense, and not legally relevant to whether Andy committed psychological violence against Boony on the charged date. It is emotionally prejudicial without being probative of the specific charge.

**The Court's assessment:** Admissibility is arguable. If admitted, it makes the Andy-Charlie relationship more believable but does not establish the statutory elements. The defense can counter that sexual orientation is constitutionally protected private information, and that pattern evidence of prior relationships does not prove psychological violence on a specific date against a specific person.

**Boony's brothers:**

Boony's brothers may testify to several things. The Court must assess each type of testimony:

| Testimony Type | Admissibility | Impact on the Case |
|---|---|---|
| **"We were victimized by Andy"** (general) | Vague and inadmissible unless specifying what "victimized" means. Hearsay if repeating Boony's characterization. | LOW — without specific acts, this is opinion testimony. The defense objects under Rule 130 (relevance). |
| **Testimony about Boony's anguish** (corroborating her emotional state) | Admissible — witnesses can testify to the complainant's observed emotional condition | MODERATE — strengthens the "effect" element. But under GR 264870, effect alone is insufficient. The "means" element must still be proven independently. |
| **Testimony about Andy's behavior during the marriage** (specific acts of mistreatment) | Admissible if relevant to the charged offense and within personal knowledge | VARIABLE — depends on what specific acts they describe. General complaints about Andy being a bad husband do not establish Sec. 5(i) elements. |
| **Testimony about the talaq** (particularly from the brother who witnessed it) | Highly admissible — goes directly to the talaq defense | **DANGEROUS FOR BOTH SIDES.** If the brother confirms the talaq was pronounced and Boony accepted it → strengthens the defense. If the brother testifies the talaq was "not serious" or "not valid" → undermines the defense. Boony may coach her brother, but the brother's testimony is under oath. |
| **Testimony that they were "harmed" by Andy's sexuality** | Admissibility questionable — the brothers' personal feelings about Andy's sexuality are not relevant to whether Andy committed Sec. 5(i) against Boony | LOW — the offense is against Boony, not her brothers. Their feelings are not a statutory element. |

**The talaq witness is the critical variable.** Boony's brother witnessed the January 23 talaq. If the prosecution calls him, his testimony can go EITHER way:

- **If he confirms:** "Andy pronounced talaq. Boony accepted. She left." → This is the defense's strongest corroboration of the talaq.
- **If coached to deny or minimize:** "It wasn't serious. They always said talaq and then made up." → This attacks the talaq's validity.

**The defense's counter to a coached denial:** Multiple prior talaqs followed by reconciliation (ruju) are contemplated by PD 1083 Art. 46(2) — they do NOT invalidate the January 23 talaq. Each prior talaq followed by ruju is a separate, valid pronouncement. The January 23 talaq was different: Boony accepted it, voluntarily departed, sent the January 30 SMS acknowledging the breakdown, and the families negotiated divorce settlement terms. No ruju was exercised. The conduct of BOTH parties for 14+ months confirms the talaq was real and final.

### Issue 3: Charlie's Liability

Under *Go-Tan v. Spouses Tan* (GR 168852, September 30, 2008), conspiracy under RPC Art. 8 applies suppletorily to RA 9262 via Sec. 47.

The prosecution's evidence: Charlie was present in the apartment on February 12. Nothing more.

Even with pre-talaq CCTV evidence and former partner testimony, there is NO evidence Charlie acted *in concert with Andy* to inflict psychological violence ON BOONY. Charlie did not communicate with Boony. He did not engage in public acts. He was Andy's known assistant whom Boony agreed to and worked alongside.

If Andy is acquitted, there is no underlying principal offense for conspiracy.

**ACQUITTAL. This charge fails regardless of what additional evidence is presented.**

**The prosecutor's own Case Evaluation Form (Document 7)** found:

- Sufficiency of allegations: **NO** — "Acts analyzed; insufficient to constitute psychological abuse/welfare"

### Issue 5: Boony's Credibility

The prosecution's case rests primarily on Boony's Judicial Affidavit. Her credibility is compromised by:

1. **S15 — False entry claim:** "Nakapasok po ako dahil maliit po ako na babae." She did NOT enter the apartment. This is a provably false material statement in a sworn document. Under Rule 132, Sec. 11, this is impeachable by prior inconsistent statement.

2. **S18 — Contradicted financial claim:** "Hindi po talaga niya ako binibigyan ng personal allowance." Contradicted by ATM card access, wallet access, P2,000 receipt, P5,000 through brother, and unrestricted spending during the marriage.

3. **Omission of talaq:** The affidavit proceeds as if the marriage subsisted on February 12. Boony does not mention the January 23 talaq, her acceptance, her voluntary departure, or her January 30 SMS acknowledging the breakdown. This omission of a fundamental fact raises questions about the completeness and candor of her testimony.

4. **S16 contradicts cohabitation narrative:** Boony herself says Charlie came to the apartment only "dalawang beses lang" (twice). This undermines any suggestion of cohabitation.

5. **BPO contradiction:** Boony claims she entered the apartment on February 12 (S15), but she obtained a BPO on February 13 to enter the apartment. If she could freely enter on the 12th, why did she need a BPO on the 13th?

**Assessment:** The trial court must weigh Boony's testimony in light of these deficiencies. A witness who made a provably false material claim about entering the apartment — the foundational act from which she claims to have seen Charlie inside — presents serious credibility concerns on all other material facts.

### The Contested Charge: Detailed Judicial Analysis

The pre-talaq CCTV/video evidence of kissing, combined with testimony from former partners and Boony's brothers, creates a factual environment significantly more favorable to the prosecution than Boony's affidavit alone. The Court must now choose between two legally defensible paths:

**PATH A — ACQUITTAL (legally stronger):**

1. The Information charges acts "on or about February 12, 2025." On that date, no marriage existed (talaq January 23). Pre-talaq evidence is context, not basis for conviction.
2. On February 12 itself, Andy committed no enumerated act of public ridicule or humiliation — he blocked the door for privacy while Boony brought the audience.
3. Under GR 264870 (Leonen SAJ, April 2025): effect alone is insufficient; the means must show intent to tilt power against the woman. Boony facilitated the relationship's proximity.
4. Former partner testimony is character evidence (Rule 130, Sec. 54) — inadmissible or, if admitted, does not establish Sec. 5(i) elements on February 12.
5. Brothers' testimony on "victimization" is vague and irrelevant to Andy's conduct on the charged date. Corroboration of Boony's anguish strengthens the "effect" element, but effect alone is insufficient under GR 264870.
6. Boony's S15 false statement impeaches her credibility as a witness.

**PATH B — CONVICTION (legally questionable):**

1. Pre-talaq CCTV/video of kissing establishes marital infidelity during the marriage. GR 252739 (En Banc, 2024) — technically higher authority than GR 264870 (Division) — presumes intent from the act of infidelity.
2. RA 9262 Sec. 7 "continuing offense" — the anguish began pre-talaq and continued through February 12.
3. Former partner testimony shows a pattern, making the Andy-Charlie relationship more credible and the anguish more severe.
4. Brothers' testimony corroborates Boony's emotional suffering and the family's collective anguish.
5. The judge interprets the Information's "on or about February 12 and sometime subsequent thereto" to encompass the continuing anguish from pre-talaq infidelity.

**Path B is legally vulnerable on appeal because:**
- The Information's temporal scope cannot be expanded without formal amendment (Rule 110 Sec. 14; Constitution Art. III Sec. 14(2))
- A Division decision (GR 264870) that is more recent and more specifically on point is being overridden by an older En Banc decision
- The variance doctrine (Rule 120 Sec. 4) prohibits conviction on uncharged acts
- An affidavit containing a provably false material claim (S15) is being credited entirely
- Character evidence from former partners is inadmissible under Rule 130 Sec. 54

### What Concerns the Court

1. **The talaq is under multi-pronged attack.** The prosecution can challenge: (a) the talaq was strategic (to be with Charlie); (b) Boony was menstruating on January 23 (talaq not during *tuhr*); (c) the talaq was never filed under Art. 161. The defense must counter: (a) multiple prior talaqs show marital dysfunction predating Charlie; Boony's SMS confirms mutual breakdown; (b) menstruation claim is unproven and, even if true, all four Sunni schools hold talaq during menstruation is sinful but valid (*talaq al-bid'ah*); Boony's 14 months of post-talaq conduct = estoppel; (c) Art. 161 filing is evidentiary not constitutive — file immediately for the docket number.

2. **The judge has seen kissing videos and cannot unsee them.** Regardless of the legal analysis, the emotional weight of visual evidence affects judicial perception. The defense must keep the argument on legal grounds.

3. **GR 252739 (En Banc) vs. GR 264870 (Division).** The prosecution will argue the En Banc decision controls. The defense must argue: (a) GR 264870 is more recent (April 2025 vs. April 2024); (b) GR 264870 is more specifically on point (acquittal for infidelity alone); (c) the Caguioa concurrence in GR 270257 (August 2024) explicitly stated "marital infidelity per se is not punished by RA 9262" — confirming the doctrinal direction.

4. **Boony's brother as talaq witness.** If Boony coaches her brother to minimize or deny the talaq, the defense must be ready with corroborating evidence: the physical separation, the January 30 SMS, the April 6 settlement meeting, the seven months of settlement negotiations. The brother's coached denial must be measured against BOTH parties' conduct for 14 months.

5. **Pre-arraignment Information amendment.** Arraignment has NOT occurred. The prosecution can amend as a matter of right (Rule 110, Sec. 14) to cover pre-talaq dates. This would drop acquittal probability to 45-50%. **The defense must not alert the prosecution to this option.**

6. **Andy's private "not yet divorced" statement.** Not in the prosecution's possession — it is a private message to his boss. It will only surface if Andy testifies, the boss testifies, or the message is discovered. The defense must avoid all three scenarios.

7. **Social prejudice.** The same-sex nature of the relationship, combined with former partner testimony, may affect the trial judge's sympathies. The defense must frame the case entirely on legal grounds, not identity.

### Strategic Observations

**For the prosecution (what the Court notes they should do but have not done):**
- **Amend the Information before arraignment** to cover "September 2024 through February 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto." This closes the temporal scope defense and brings GR 252739's presumption into full force. This is the prosecution's most powerful available move — and they appear not to have considered it.
- **Move to discharge Charlie as state witness under Rule 119, Section 17.** Charlie, as co-accused, currently cannot be compelled to testify. But if discharged, he loses his right to remain silent and becomes a prosecution witness who can testify from inside the relationship itself — about the pre-talaq relationship, the nature of his relationship with Andy, and what happened in the apartment. The requirements for discharge are arguable: (a) absolute necessity for testimony, (b) no other direct evidence available, (c) testimony substantially corroborable, (d) not the most guilty, (e) no moral turpitude conviction. The defense can counter on (a) and (b) — the prosecution has Boony's affidavit, CCTV, and other witnesses. But this remains the prosecution's nuclear option for breaking the co-accused silence strategy.

## V. THE COURT'S CONCLUSION

Based on the totality of the evidence — including pre-talaq CCTV/video evidence, potential testimony from former partners and Boony's brothers — the applicable law, and the controlling jurisprudence:

**This Court would ACQUIT both accused, but the margin is narrow.**

On the denial of financial support: the *Acharon* standard is not met. This charge fails regardless of any additional evidence about Andy's relationships.

On the sexual relationship as psychological violence: the pre-talaq evidence establishes that a romantic relationship existed during the marriage. Boony's anguish is credible and corroborated. **However:**

1. The Information charges acts "on or about February 12, 2025." On that date, no marriage existed. Conviction for uncharged pre-talaq acts violates the accused's constitutional right to be informed (Art. III Sec. 14(2)) and the variance doctrine (Rule 120 Sec. 4).

2. On February 12, Andy committed no enumerated act of public ridicule or humiliation. He was in his apartment. He blocked the door. Boony brought the audience.

3. Under GR 264870 — the most recent SC pronouncement — "the effect on the victim alone is not enough to sustain a conviction." The means must show intent to "tilt the scales of power against the woman." Boony's own facilitation of the relationship — knowing Charlie, agreeing to his role, working alongside him, directing Andy to sleep at his apartment — runs counter to this test.

4. Former partner testimony is character evidence that does not establish the statutory elements on the charged date. Brothers' testimony corroborates anguish (the "effect") but does not establish the "means."

5. Boony's Judicial Affidavit contains a provably false material claim (S15), undermining her credibility.

On Charlie's liability: no proof of conspiracy, no underlying offense.

**Estimated probability of acquittal: 55-65%** (depending on the strength of the menstruation claim and witness testimony). The legal defenses are sound but the cumulative emotional impact of pre-talaq videos, former partner testimony, brothers' testimony, and the menstruation challenge to the talaq creates a 35-45% risk of conviction by a judge who follows the GR 252739 (En Banc) path and credits the talaq invalidity argument. **If convicted, appeal prospects are strong** — the Information's temporal scope, the talaq defense (all four Sunni schools hold talaq during menstruation is effective), GR 264870, the variance doctrine, the S15 impeachment, and the estoppel from Boony's 14 months of post-talaq conduct all provide grounds for reversal.
