## II. THE INFORMATION

The Information dated June 26, 2025 charges ANDY with committing psychological abuse upon his wife BOONY by:

1. **Denying financial support** to the private complainant; and
2. **Indulging in a sexual relationship with another male named CHARLIE** inside their dwelling at [ADDRESS 1], Canton City.

The Information alleges these acts occurred **"on or about February 12, 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto."**

Charlie is named as co-accused, charged presumably as a co-conspirator in the psychological violence.

### The Actual Text of the Information

From the case file (Document 1):

> The undersigned public prosecutor accuses **ANDY** of committing the crime of violation of **Section 5 (i) of Republic Act No. 9262 or the Anti-Violence Against Women and Children**, committed as follows:
>
> That on or about February 12, 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto, in Canton City, and within the jurisdiction of this Honorable Court, the above-named accused, being the lawful husband of private complainant **BOONY**, did then and there inflict willfully, unlawfully cause psychological abuse, public ridicule or humiliation to his wife, by denying financial support to the said private complainant and indulging in a sexual relationship with another male named **CHARLIE** inside their dwelling at [Address Redacted], Canton City.
>
> **CONTRARY TO LAW.**

Two observations from the actual text:

- The Information describes Andy as "the **lawful husband**" — the prosecution never considered the talaq. The entire case is built on the assumption the marriage subsisted. This is not a minor drafting choice; it is the factual predicate on which the charge rests. If the marriage was dissolved on January 23, 2025, the characterization of Andy as "lawful husband" on "or about February 12, 2025" is factually incorrect.
- The Information charges acts at "[Address Redacted], Canton City" — but this was Andy's apartment from which Boony had voluntarily departed 20 days earlier. The dwelling is characterized as "their dwelling" in the body of the Information when, by that date, it was exclusively Andy's under the complete separation of property regime.

**Critical observations on the Information:**

**First**, the temporal scope. The phrase "on or about February 12, 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto" does two things: (1) "on or about February 12" covers the specific date with minor leeway (days, not months); (2) "sometime subsequent thereto" extends forward in time, covering ongoing or continuing conduct after February 12. The phrase does **not** extend backward to cover acts committed before February 12 — including acts committed during the subsisting marriage before the January 23 talaq.

**Second**, the characterization of the dwelling. The Information states Andy was "indulging in a sexual relationship with another male named CHARLIE inside **their dwelling**." This is factually misleading. Andy had been renting the apartment under the complete separation of property regime. It was Andy's exclusive property. Boony had voluntarily left the apartment on January 23, 2025 — twenty days before the charged incident. The dwelling was not "theirs" on February 12, 2025. It was his.

**Third**, the Information does not explicitly charge "marital infidelity" as an element. It charges psychological violence through "indulging in a sexual relationship with another male" — framing this as a means of causing mental or emotional anguish. The prosecution's theory is that this conduct caused Boony "mental or emotional anguish, public ridicule or humiliation." The distinction matters because the prosecution must prove the statutory elements, not merely a moral transgression.

**Fourth**, the Certification states the preliminary investigation was conducted "in accordance with Rule IV of DOJ Department Circular No. 15, s. 2024" and that the accused was "informed of the complaint and of the evidence submitted against him/her and was given an opportunity to submit controverting evidence." Andy's affidavit directly contradicts this — he states he "never received the subpoena issued in relation to this complaint" and that the Resolution was issued "without his having been informed or heard." This due process issue is addressed in Section X.

**Fifth**, the Certification (Document 2) recommends bail at **P36,000.00** and certifies under oath that "the accused was informed of the complaint and of the evidence submitted against him/her and was given an opportunity to submit controverting evidence." This is the specific certification statement that Andy disputes — he maintains he was never informed and never received any subpoena. The gap between the prosecutor's sworn certification and Andy's sworn denial is a factual dispute that goes to the regularity of the entire preliminary investigation.

## III. STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. Undisputed Facts

Andy and Boony were married on May 20, 2017, with a marriage contract issued by the Shari'ah Court on August 16, 2022. During their marriage, they experienced recurring quarrels and emotional incompatibility. Andy had pronounced talaq on separate prior occasions, but the parties reconciled each time.

During the marriage, Andy had repeatedly asked Boony to hire a house helper because she could not consistently manage household chores. Boony did not do so. In September 2024, Andy found and hired Charlie — a 19-year-old male with educational and employment background in housekeeping — as his assistant, and found him a boarding house in Cotabato. **Boony knew of and agreed to Charlie's role as Andy's assistant**, though she was uncomfortable having a male helper in the apartment. Charlie entered the apartment only once for this reason. On multiple occasions during periods of marital conflict, **Boony herself directed Andy to go sleep at Charlie's apartment** — proactively sending her husband toward Charlie as a response to their marital tensions.

On **January 23, 2025**, Andy pronounced talaq. Boony accepted the divorce and physically departed the apartment, moving to her brother's house. Both parties treated the pronouncement as an effective dissolution of the marriage bond.

On January 28, 2025, Andy traveled to Manila for work. On January 30, 2025, Boony sent Andy SMS messages in which she admitted her mistakes and shortcomings in the marriage, stated she was no longer good for him, acknowledged causing him pain and damage, and stated she had failed the trust he gave her — leading to the divorce.

Andy returned to Cotabato on February 9, 2025. On February 8, 2025, Andy sent P2,000 to Boony. On February 23, 2025, Andy sent an additional P5,000 to Boony.

On April 6, 2025, both families met to discuss and agree on the terms and consequences of the divorce (*kawagib*). There was no discussion of reconciliation or revoking the talaq. A divorce settlement was reached, including Andy's agreement to provide Boony a housing unit.

Between September and October 2025, additional rounds of settlement meetings were held. Boony agreed to the terms and communicated her acceptance through her aunt, requesting finalization of the settlement after her graduation on October 15, 2025. On November 3, 2025, Boony's aunt communicated that the family was no longer interested in pursuing the divorce settlement and would continue pressing the criminal charges.

### B. The February 12 Incident (Disputed)

**Prosecution version (Boony's Judicial Affidavit):**

At about 4:30 p.m. on February 12, 2025, Boony went to Andy's office at the BTA. His boss informed her that Andy had returned to Cotabato on February 9. The boss later offered that his driver bring her to her residence, and they agreed to pass by the apartment first so she could retrieve documents and belongings. Around 7:30–8:00 p.m., when they reached the apartment, Andy blocked the door and refused to let her enter. She slipped in because she is small, and inside she saw Charlie and his things. She states Andy refused her entry because his male lover was inside.

**Defense version (Andy's Affidavit):**

On February 12, 2025, Boony went to his office to seek mediation. As he was not there, she and some officemates proceeded to his apartment. He and Boony argued outside the apartment about her sudden visit. She did not enter the apartment but created a scandalous scene outside in the presence of his officemates and other people, causing him public ridicule and humiliation. She said she did not care if he lost his job.

**Key factual context:** Charlie was not a stranger. He was Andy's assistant — hired by Andy after Boony repeatedly failed to get a house helper despite Andy's requests. Boony knew of and agreed to Charlie's role. She was uncomfortable with a male helper in the apartment, so Charlie entered the apartment only once. Despite this discomfort, Boony herself repeatedly directed Andy to sleep at Charlie's apartment during periods of marital conflict. The "discovery" on February 12 was not the uncovering of a secret life; it was, at most, the confirmation of what Boony had long facilitated and likely suspected. The distinction between discovering a *secret* and confirming a *suspicion one had helped create* is legally significant.

**Critical impeachment material:** Boony did **NOT** enter the apartment. She glimpsed Andy at the door, but Andy blocked entry and she did not pass through. Her judicial affidavit claim that she "slipped in because she is small" and saw Charlie and his things inside is **false**. If Boony had entered, Andy would have had to physically instruct or grab her to leave, which would have further escalated the situation — she was already making a scene outside.

**How to execute the impeachment in court:**

1. **Legal basis:** Rule 132, Section 11 of the Revised Rules on Evidence — a witness may be impeached by evidence of prior inconsistent statements. Section 13 permits impeachment by evidence of bad character for truthfulness.

2. **Cross-examination procedure (laying the foundation):**
   - **Step 1:** During cross-examination, confront Boony with her judicial affidavit. Read the specific paragraph where she states she "slipped in because she is small" and saw Charlie and his things inside. Ask her to confirm this is her sworn statement.
   - **Step 2:** Establish the physical details — ask Boony to describe the apartment door, the layout, where Charlie allegedly was, what things she saw. Lock her into the specific details of her claim.
   - **Step 3:** Introduce contradicting evidence — Andy's testimony (or witnesses) that she did not enter; the fact that Andy blocked the door; that she was making a scene outside with officemates present who can testify she did not enter.
   - **Step 4:** Confront the inconsistency — "You testified under oath that you entered the apartment. But you did not enter. Why did you make a false statement in your judicial affidavit?"

3. **Effect on credibility:** Under Rule 133, Section 1, the credibility of a witness may be impeached by any party, including the party who called the witness. Once the "slipped in" fabrication is established, the defense argues: if Boony fabricated a material fact in her judicial affidavit — her physical entry into the apartment, which is the foundation of her claim to have seen Charlie inside — her testimony on all other material facts (anguish, humiliation, denial of support) is unreliable. The trial court must weigh all of her testimony in light of this proven falsehood.

4. **Optional: Perjury referral.** A false statement in a judicial affidavit constitutes perjury under RPC Article 183. The defense may file a separate complaint or request the court to refer the matter to the prosecutor. This serves as leverage — Boony faces her own criminal exposure — and further undermines her credibility as a witness.

5. **Tactical sequencing:** Do NOT reveal the impeachment evidence during pre-trial or in pleadings. Reserve it for cross-examination. The maximum impact of impeachment comes from the witness committing to the false version on the stand, then being confronted with the contradicting evidence. If Boony's counsel learns of this line of attack before trial, Boony may "correct" her testimony — losing the impeachment opportunity.

**The reconciliation context:** Andy was willing to reconcile — he was open to exercising *ruju* during the *'idda* period. Andy asked Boony to draft a Reconciliation Agreement in her own language and on her own terms, so she would not feel the reconciliation was being imposed on her. **Boony refused to write the agreement.** Instead, she tried to force reconciliation by ambushing Andy at his office and then going to the apartment unannounced with his officemates. This fact has two implications: (1) Andy was empowering, not dominating — asking Boony to write her own reconciliation terms is the opposite of coercion and passes the GR 264870 "tilt the scales of power" test in Andy's favor; and (2) Boony was the coercive party — she refused structured reconciliation and forced confrontation at Andy's workplace.

### C. Post-February 12 Conduct

The events following February 12 demonstrate a pattern of escalation by Boony, not by Andy:

- **February 13:** Boony obtains a Barangay Protection Order to enter the apartment and retrieve belongings. Andy alleges she ransacked the apartment and damaged his personal property in the presence of barangay officials and police.
- **February 17:** Boony sends a letter to Andy's office email complaining of unjust treatment, including alleged failure to provide financial support and forcible eviction from the apartment. The email was read by Andy's officemates.
- **February 18:** Andy alleges Boony forcibly entered the apartment and took his iPad mini and other devices without his consent. Andy receives a call from their Administrative and Finance Officer asking him to resign because of Boony's harassment of the office.
- **February 20:** Boony sends a second email to the office pressing it to act on her complaint.
- **April 16:** Andy discovers, on a smartphone where Boony's Facebook account was previously open, posts and messages in which she allegedly slandered and made homophobic remarks against him.

This post-incident timeline is relevant to assessing who was causing "public ridicule or humiliation" to whom.

### D. Financial Arrangement

**During the marriage:** Boony handled the household budget entirely. She had unrestricted access to Andy's wallet and ATM cards — she kept one of Andy's ATM cards, withdrew money freely, and paid for everything including restaurant bills from Andy's wallet. Boony could buy for her personal needs using Andy's money without restriction. Andy actively encouraged Boony to keep money for herself for emergencies. **Boony chose not to save** — this was her decision, not Andy's restriction. Boony's "no personal allowance" claim amounts to a preference about money organization (wanting a separate cash allocation on top of already having unrestricted spending access), not denial of support.

**After the talaq (January 23, 2025):** Boony left the apartment — Andy's wallet and ATM cards naturally stayed with him as a physical consequence of separation, not a deliberate cutoff of financial access. Andy sent **P2,000 on February 8, 2025** (receipt exists) and **P5,000 on February 23, 2025** (handed physically through Boony's brother, who can corroborate). On **April 6, 2025**, Andy offered Boony a housing unit he had paid for and built as part of the divorce settlement. Boony ultimately withdrew from the settlement on November 3, 2025 and pursued the criminal case. The offer may not be enforceable, but it demonstrates willingness to provide — the opposite of willful denial.

### E. Boony's Email Campaign and Andy's Forced Resignation

On **February 17, 2025**, Boony sent an email to Andy's boss containing false accusations: denial of support, forcible eviction, prohibition of work, and references to a "male partner." On **February 20, 2025**, Boony sent a second email pressing the office to act on her complaint. Both emails were read by officemates.

On **February 18, 2025**, Andy was instructed by the Administrative and Finance Officer to tender his resignation because of Boony's harassment of the office. Andy had to resign. The head of office (the MP/boss) has not been reappointed, and the office no longer exists. Andy lost his livelihood directly because of Boony's conduct.

Boony did not stop at Andy's office. She spread "immorality" and "crime" claims to other offices, destroying Andy's ability to do business. As of the date of this analysis, Andy has no regular employment.

**Defense implications:** Andy is the actual victim of "public ridicule and humiliation" — not Boony. The very harm that Section 5(i) requires the offender to cause was inflicted by the complainant upon the accused. The email campaign, workplace harassment, forced resignation, and reputation destruction constitute a pattern of conduct by Boony that reverses the victim-offender narrative.

### F. Key Admissions from Boony's Judicial Affidavit (Document 10)

Boony's own sworn statements in her Judicial Affidavit contain admissions that undermine the prosecution's narrative. The following passages are quoted verbatim from the affidavit (in Tagalog, with English translations):

**1. S16 — Charlie came to the apartment only twice:**

> *"Dalawang beses lang po nakapunta si Charlie sa bahay namin noong may meeting ako sa labas. Naghugas lang po siya ng pinggan pero hindi naglinis."*
> [Charlie came to our house only twice, when I had meetings outside. He only washed dishes but didn't clean.]

This is Boony's own testimony — Charlie visited the apartment only twice, and only when Boony was away. This directly contradicts any narrative of cohabitation between Andy and Charlie at the apartment. The prosecution's theory that Charlie was living with Andy "inside their dwelling" collapses against the complainant's own sworn statement.

**2. S17 — Andy slept at Charlie's boarding house:**

> *"Madalas po sila magkasama ni Charlie kahit hating gabi. Minsan at madalas doon na po siya natutulog sa boarding house ni Charlie."*
> [Andy and Charlie were often together even at midnight. Sometimes and often, Andy would sleep at Charlie's boarding house.]

Boony frames this as evidence of a romantic relationship. But this statement CONFIRMS the defense fact that Andy slept at Charlie's place — not the other way around. The defense position is that Boony herself directed Andy to go to Charlie's boarding house during their marital conflicts. Boony's own affidavit corroborates that Andy was at Charlie's place, not that Charlie was living at the conjugal apartment.

**3. S15 — The false entry claim:**

> *"Nakapasok po ako dahil maliit po ako na babae. Doon ko po nakita si Charlie at iyong mga gamit niya."*
> [I was able to enter because I'm a small woman. There I saw Charlie and his things.]

This is the statement the defense identifies as FALSE. Boony claims she physically entered the apartment and saw Charlie and his belongings inside. Andy maintains she did NOT enter — he blocked the door, and the confrontation happened outside in the presence of his officemates. If Boony fabricated this material fact, her credibility on all other material assertions is impeachable (see Section III.B, impeachment analysis).

**4. S18 — The "no personal allowance" claim:**

> *"Financially po hindi po talaga niya ako binibigyan ng personal allowance."*
> [Financially, he really did not give me a personal allowance.]

This general assertion is contradicted by the defense evidence: Boony had unrestricted access to Andy's ATM card, withdrew money freely, used Andy's wallet for purchases, and received remittances of P2,000 (February 8) and P5,000 (February 23) after the separation. The "no personal allowance" claim conflates a preference for a separate cash allocation with actual denial of financial support.

**5. S7 — No children:**

> *"Wala po kaming anak ni Andy."*
> [We have no children, Andy and I.]

This confirms the case is purely about spousal support, not child support. There are no minor children whose welfare is at stake. The entire RA 9262 claim rests on psychological violence against Boony alone, without the added dimension of harm to children that strengthens many VAWC prosecutions.

**6. Absence of any talaq reference:**

Boony does NOT mention the talaq anywhere in her Judicial Affidavit. She testifies throughout as if the marriage subsisted on February 12, 2025 — referring to Andy as "asawa ko" (my husband) and never acknowledging the January 23 pronouncement or her voluntary departure from the apartment. This silence is consistent with either: (a) the prosecution deliberately framing the case to avoid the talaq issue, or (b) Boony not considering the talaq legally significant. Either way, the affidavit's complete omission of the talaq — and the prosecution's corresponding characterization of Andy as "lawful husband" in the Information — reveals that the entire case was built without confronting the Muslim divorce question.

## IV. THE TALAQ DEFENSE

### A. Foundational Definition: Divorce by Talaq

Article 45 of Presidential Decree No. 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws of the Philippines) provides:

> *"Divorce is the formal dissolution of the marriage bond in accordance with this Code to be granted only after the exhaustion of all possible means of reconciliation between the spouses. It may be effected by: (a) Repudiation of the wife by the husband (talaq)..."*

Talaq constitutes a unilateral right of the husband to dissolve the marriage bond through repudiation. This form of divorce does not require judicial intervention or the wife's consent, distinguishing it from other forms of divorce under the Code.

### B. Mechanics and Immediate Effect

Article 46(1) of PD 1083 establishes the procedural requirements and immediate consequences:

> *"A divorce by talaq may be affected by the husband in a single repudiation of his wife during her non-menstrual period (tuhr) within which he has totally abstained from carnal relation with her. Any number of repudiations made during one tuhr shall constitute only one repudiation and shall become irrevocable after the expiration of the prescribed 'idda."*

The provision states that talaq "may be affected" — language indicating the divorce takes effect upon pronouncement. The phrase "shall become irrevocable after the expiration of the prescribed *'idda*" reveals that the divorce exists in a state of legal effect immediately upon pronouncement, but this effect can be reversed during the *'idda* period.

### C. The *'Idda* Period

Article 56 of PD 1083 defines *'idda*:

> *"'Idda is the period of waiting prescribed for a woman whose marriage has been dissolved by death or by divorce the completion of which shall enable her to contract a new marriage."*

The language "**has been dissolved**" — past perfect tense — unambiguously establishes that dissolution occurs **before** the *'idda* period begins, not after its expiration. The *'idda* follows dissolution; dissolution precedes *'idda*.

Article 57(1)(b) specifies the duration:

> *"Every wife shall be obliged to observe 'idda as follows: ... (b) In case of termination of marriage by divorce, for three monthly courses..."*

### D. The Right of Revocation (*Ruju*)

Article 46(2) clarifies the husband's right during the waiting period:

> *"A husband who repudiates his wife, either for the first or second time, shall have the right to take her back (ruju) within the prescribed 'idda by resumption of cohabitation without need of a new contract of marriage. Should he fail to do so, the repudiation shall become irrevocable (Talaq bain sugra)."*

The husband possesses a unilateral right to restore the marriage during *'idda* through *ruju* — resumption of cohabitation. The absence of any requirement for a new marriage contract during this period distinguishes revocable talaq from irrevocable talaq, where remarriage would necessitate a completely new marriage contract with all attendant formalities.

### E. Additional Provisions Confirming Dissolution Upon Pronouncement

**Article 29(2):**

> *"Should a repudiated woman and her husband reconcile during her 'idda, he shall have a better right to take her back without need of a new marriage contract."*

The use of "take her back" (*ruju*) — rather than language suggesting continuation of an existing marriage — indicates that the marital bond has been severed, albeit in a manner that permits restoration without formalities.

**Article 96(1):**

> *"The husband who divorces his wife shall have mutual rights of inheritance with her while she is observing her 'idda. After the expiration of the 'idda, there shall be no mutual rights of succession between them."*

The retention of mutual inheritance rights during *'idda* demonstrates that certain incidents of marriage persist during this transitional period, while the marriage itself has been dissolved.

**Article 161(1):**

> *"Any Muslim male who has pronounced a talaq shall, without delay, file with the Clerk of Court of the Shari'a Circuit Court of the place where his family resides a written notice of such fact and the circumstances attended thereto, after having served a copy thereof to the wife concerned. The talaq pronounced shall not become irrevocable until after the expiration of the prescribed 'idda. The notice filed shall be conclusive evidence that talaq has been pronounced."*

This provision distinguishes between pronouncement of talaq and its irrevocability. The talaq is pronounced and takes effect immediately; it remains in a revocable status during *'idda*. The filing requirement treats the pronouncement as a completed act requiring registration, not a pending or conditional act awaiting validation.

### F. Termination of Fidelity Obligations

Article 34(1) of PD 1083 provides:

> *"The husband and the wife are obliged to live together, observe mutual respect and fidelity, and render mutual help and support in accordance with this Code."*

The obligation to "observe mutual respect and fidelity" is incident to the marriage. It terminates along with the marriage bond. While support obligations continue during *'idda* under Article 67, the fidelity obligation does not. A divorced person — even during *'idda* — is no longer bound by marital fidelity because there is no marriage to be faithful to.

### G. Effects of Irrevocable Talaq

Article 54 enumerates the effects:

> *"A talaq or faskh, as soon as it becomes irrevocable, shall have the following effects: (a) The marriage bond shall be severed and the spouses may contract another marriage in accordance with this Code; (b) The spouses shall lose their mutual rights of inheritance; (c) The custody of children shall be determined in accordance with Article 78 of this code; (d) The wife shall be entitled to recover from the husband her whole dower in case the talaq has been affected after the consummation of the marriage, or one-half thereof if effected before its consummation; (e) The husband shall not be discharged from his obligation to give support in accordance with Article 67; and (f) The conjugal partnership, if stipulated in the marriage settlements, shall be dissolved and liquidated."*

While this provision addresses irrevocable talaq, the language "the marriage bond shall be severed" indicates that even during revocable talaq, the fundamental marital relationship has already been disrupted. The subsection recognizing that divorced persons "may contract another marriage" affirms their liberty to form new intimate relationships post-divorce.

### H. Application to Andy's Case

Andy pronounced talaq on **January 23, 2025**. Boony accepted the divorce, physically left the apartment, and moved to her brother's house. Both parties treated the pronouncement as an effective dissolution.

Under Article 57(1)(b), Boony's *'idda* period (three monthly courses) ran from approximately **January 23 to April 23, 2025**.

The events of **February 12, 2025** — the date charged in the Information — occurred **twenty days** after the pronouncement of talaq, during the *'idda* period. During this time:

- Andy and Boony were **divorced** (Art. 46(1): divorce takes effect upon pronouncement)
- The divorce was **revocable** (Art. 46(2): Andy retained the right of *ruju* during *'idda*)
- **But absent exercise of *ruju*, they stood divorced** — neither party took steps toward resumption of cohabitation
- The **fidelity obligation had terminated** (Art. 34(1): fidelity is incident to marriage, which had been dissolved)
- **Support obligations continued** (Art. 67)
- **Mutual inheritance rights persisted** (Art. 96(1))

**The legal status on February 12, 2025: Andy and Boony were divorced.** The concept of "marital infidelity" is a legal impossibility after divorce. Infidelity requires fidelity, and fidelity exists only within marriage. Once Andy pronounced talaq and Boony accepted it, no marital fidelity obligation remained. Any relationship Andy formed after January 23, 2025 occurred outside the context of marriage and cannot constitute "marital infidelity" in the legal sense.

### I. Talaq Documentation Status

**Witnesses to the January 23, 2025 talaq:**
- **Boony's brother** — was living with Andy and Boony at the time; witnessed the pronouncement
- **Andy's siblings** — were informed of the talaq
- **Boony's family** — were informed that Andy and Boony had separated

### L. Boony's Menstruation Claim — Attack on Talaq Validity

**The attack:** Boony claims she was menstruating on January 23, 2025. PD 1083 Art. 46(1) requires that talaq be pronounced "during her non-menstrual period (tuhr) within which he has totally abstained from carnal relation with her." If she was menstruating, the talaq arguably was not during *tuhr* — and the prosecution will argue the talaq is invalid, the marriage subsisted on February 12, and GR 252739's presumption of intent applies.

**This is the prosecution's strongest attack on the talaq defense.** If it succeeds, the defense's primary shield collapses.

**Five defense layers:**

**First — Evidentiary.** How does Boony prove she was menstruating on January 23, 2025? This is a claim about a physiological state 14+ months ago. There are no medical records from that date. The claim rests entirely on her word — and her word comes from a witness who has already made a provably false sworn statement (S15 — claiming she entered the apartment when she did not). Menstruation on a specific date is not a judicially noticeable fact; it requires proof. The defense is entitled to challenge this unverified, uncorroborated assertion.

**Second — Islamic law.** All four Sunni schools recognized by PD 1083 Art. 5 (Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) hold that talaq during menstruation is *haram* (sinful) but **VALID** (effective). This is known as *talaq al-bid'ah* (innovative/irregular divorce). The husband sins by pronouncing talaq outside of *tuhr*, but the divorce takes effect.

The foundational hadith is the case of Abdullah ibn Umar (may Allah be pleased with him), who divorced his wife during menstruation. The Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded him to take her back (*ruju*) — but when asked whether the divorce counted, Ibn Umar confirmed: "Yes, of course." The divorce was sinful but effective.

This is the near-unanimous position of classical Islamic scholarship. Only a small minority (notably Ibn Taymiyyah and Ibn Hazm) hold talaq during menstruation is void — a position considered heterodox by mainstream Sunni jurisprudence.

**Third — PD 1083 textual.** Art. 46(1) says talaq "**may be** affected by the husband in a single repudiation of his wife during her non-menstrual period (tuhr)." The phrase "may be" is permissive, not mandatory — it describes the proper procedure, not a condition of validity. No PD 1083 provision declares talaq during menstruation VOID. Compare with Art. 161(1): the word "**shall**" is mandatory ("Any Muslim male who has pronounced a talaq **shall**, without delay, file..."), yet non-filing does not void the talaq. Compare also with Art. 27 (prohibited marriages), where the Legislature included express nullity clauses. The absence of an express nullity clause for talaq during menstruation indicates the Legislature did not intend invalidity.

**Fourth — Estoppel by conduct.** Even if the talaq were technically irregular, Boony's conduct for 14 months after January 23 constitutes acceptance and ratification:

- She voluntarily left the apartment on January 23
- She sent the January 30 SMS acknowledging she caused the breakdown
- The families held a *kawagib* (divorce settlement) meeting on April 6
- She negotiated settlement terms for seven months (April-November 2025)
- She filed the criminal complaint as a "wife" but never sought to challenge the talaq's validity in the Shari'ah Court
- She never returned to the apartment or attempted to resume cohabitation (*ruju*)

If Boony believed the talaq was invalid because of menstruation, she would have said so on January 23 — or at any point in the following 14 months. She did not. She accepted the divorce, departed, and conducted herself as a divorced woman. She cannot claim invalidity for the first time in a criminal proceeding after 14 months of post-talaq conduct.

**Fifth — Jurisdictional.** Talaq validity is a question of Muslim personal law within the exclusive original jurisdiction of the Shari'ah Circuit Court (PD 1083, Art. 143). The RTC, which has jurisdiction over the RA 9262 charge, is not the proper forum to adjudicate the validity of an Islamic divorce. Neither Boony nor the prosecution has challenged the talaq in the proper forum.

**Impact on probability:** If the menstruation claim is raised and credited by the trial judge, acquittal probability drops by approximately 5 percentage points. This is factored into the probability table in the judicial assessment.

### M. The PD 1083 Hierarchy

Article 3(1) of PD 1083 establishes:

> *"In case of conflict between any provision of this Code and laws of general application, the former shall prevail."*

This hierarchy requires that R.A. 9262 — a law of general application — be interpreted consistently with PD 1083 where the two intersect on matters of Muslim marriage and divorce. When PD 1083 treats divorce by talaq as severing the marriage bond and terminating fidelity obligations, RA 9262 cannot impose criminal liability for conduct that PD 1083 permits.

## V. THE LEGAL FRAMEWORK: R.A. 9262 AND CONTROLLING JURISPRUDENCE

### A. Elements of the Offense — Section 5(i), R.A. 9262

R.A. 9262 provides:

> SECTION 5. *Acts of Violence Against Women and Their Children.* — The crime of violence against women and their children is committed through any of the following acts:
>
> ...
>
> (i) Causing **mental or emotional anguish**, public ridicule or humiliation to the woman or her child, including, but not limited to, repeated verbal and emotional abuse, and **denial of financial support** or custody of minor children or access to the woman's child/children.

Section 3(C) defines psychological violence:

> "*Psychological violence*" refers to acts or omissions causing or likely to cause mental or emotional suffering of the victim such as but not limited to intimidation, harassment, stalking, damage to property, public ridicule or humiliation, repeated verbal abuse and **marital infidelity**.[^1] It includes causing or allowing the victim to witness the physical, sexual or psychological abuse of a member of the family to which the victim belongs, or to witness pornography in any form or to witness abusive injury to pets or to unlawful or unwanted deprivation of the right to custody and/or visitation of common children.

[^1]: **Note on statutory text:** The lawphil.net transcription of RA 9262 Section 3(C) reads "mental infidelity." The Official Gazette version and consistent Supreme Court usage (Dinamling, Araza, GR 252739, GR 264870) employ "marital infidelity." This analysis follows the prevailing jurisprudential usage.

As enumerated by the Supreme Court in **Dinamling v. People (G.R. No. 199522, June 22, 2015)**, the elements are:

> (1) The offended party is a woman and/or her child or children;
>
> (2) The woman is either the wife or former wife of the offender, or is a woman with whom the offender has or had a sexual or dating relationship, or is a woman with whom such offender has a common child. As for the woman's child or children, they may be legitimate or illegitimate, or living within or without the family abode;
>
> (3) The offender causes on the woman and/or child mental or emotional anguish; and
>
> (4) The anguish is caused through acts of public ridicule or humiliation, repeated verbal and emotional abuse, denial of financial support or custody of minor children or access to the children or similar such acts or omissions.
>
> — *Dinamling v. People*, G.R. No. 199522, June 22, 2015

The Court in *Dinamling* further clarified:

> *"Psychological violence is the means employed by the perpetrator, while mental or emotional anguish is the effect caused to or the damage sustained by the offended party."*

Both elements — the **means** (psychological violence) and the **effect** (mental or emotional anguish) — must be proven beyond reasonable doubt. The means requirement demands proof of specific enumerated acts; the effect requirement demands the victim's personal testimony establishing actual anguish.

### B. The Critical Distinction: Emotional Pain vs. Psychological Violence

The statutory language reveals a distinction the prosecution and courts sometimes conflate: emotional pain is **not** synonymous with psychological violence under R.A. 9262.

Section 5(i) requires not merely that the victim experience "mental or emotional anguish" but that such anguish be caused "through acts of public ridicule or humiliation, repeated verbal and emotional abuse, denial of financial support... or similar such acts or omissions."

The provision establishes both a **harm requirement** (mental or emotional anguish) and a **means requirement** (specific acts or omissions enumerated in the statute). Both must be proven beyond reasonable doubt. The law does not criminalize causing emotional pain generally — it criminalizes causing such pain through specific means.

### D. The Convergence of GR 264870 and the Caguioa Concurrence in GR 270257

Two decisions from the same judicial term (2024-2025) converge on the same principle:

**GR 264870 (Leonen, April 2025)** held that the State must prove the accused's conduct was done with intent to "tilt the scales of power within the relationship against the woman." The prosecution failed this test — infidelity alone, without proof of domination or coercion, does not constitute criminal psychological violence.

**GR 270257 (Caguioa concurrence, August 2024)** expressly stated:

> [M]arital infidelity per se is not punished by Republic Act No. 9262. XXX270257 is guilty in this case because his acts of abandoning his conjugal home with AAA, moving CCC into his mother's house in full view of AAA and their three children, siring a son with CCC, and publicizing on social media his new family were clearly indicative of his reckless disregard of AAA's welfare, amounting to intent to inflict emotional violence upon her.

The conviction rested not on the affair itself but on its deliberate publicization — distinguishing conviction on the basis of *public flaunting* + *abandonment* + *cohabitation in full view of the legitimate family*.

Together, these articulate the current direction of the jurisprudence: **infidelity alone is insufficient; deliberate, public acts of humiliation directed at the victim are required.**

### E. Constitutional and Privacy Considerations

The 1987 Constitution protects fundamental liberties including the right to privacy, liberty of abode, freedom of association, and liberty under the Due Process Clause. These protections extend to divorced persons who have lawfully dissolved their marriages.

When divorce occurs, the legal relationship fundamentally transforms. Rights and obligations incident to marriage terminate, except those specifically preserved by law. Criminalizing a divorced person's conduct not prohibited by law merely because it causes emotional distress to a former spouse raises serious constitutional concerns. The State cannot generally prohibit lawful behavior solely because others find it emotionally distressing. Such a principle would grant former spouses veto power over their ex-partners' personal lives indefinitely after divorce.

R.A. 9262 **criminalizes violence, not morally questionable conduct**. The conduct must cause psychological violence through specific means enumerated in the statute. Post-divorce relationships, even if they cause emotional pain to the former spouse, do not automatically constitute criminal psychological violence.

The same-sex nature of the alleged relationship introduces an additional dimension. Same-sex conduct is not criminalized in the Philippines. Same-sex relationships, while not legally recognized through marriage, are not illegal. The prosecution should be scrutinized as to whether it targets the relationship itself (impermissible) or its alleged effect on Boony (proper, but requiring proof of the statutory elements).

### F. Andy Should Not Testify

Andy has the **absolute constitutional right to remain silent** (1987 Constitution, Art. III, Sec. 17). This right should be exercised.

**Risk of testifying:** If Andy takes the stand, the prosecution cross-examines with CCTV footage, witness testimony, messages from the iPad, and Boony's documentary evidence. Any affirmative claim Andy makes — about when the relationship started, about the talaq, about Charlie's role — becomes a target for contradiction. If the prosecution can impeach Andy on any factual claim, his credibility is destroyed on ALL facts, including the talaq itself. The risk is catastrophic.

**Defense strategy without testimony:** The defense presents its case through:
1. **Documents** — talaq notice filing and Shari'a Court docket stamp, January 30 SMS screenshots, Barangay Protection Order, P2,000 transfer receipt, divorce settlement document, Boony's February 17 email to the office
2. **Witnesses** — Boony's brother (witnessed the talaq), Andy's siblings (informed of talaq), Boony's other brother (corroborates P5,000 remittance)
3. **Legal arguments** — talaq defense, Information scope, GR 264870 elements standard, Acharon on financial support

The burden of proof is on the prosecution. The defense does not need to prove innocence — it needs to create reasonable doubt. Andy's silence preserves the legal framework and prevents the prosecution from turning a legal defense into a credibility contest.

### G. Constitutional Right Not to Testify and the Charlie State Witness Risk

**Constitutional and procedural basis:**

**1987 Constitution, Article III, Section 17:** *"No person shall be compelled to be a witness against himself."*

**Rule 115, Section 1(e), Revised Rules of Criminal Procedure:** The accused has the right *"to be exempt from being compelled to be a witness against himself."*

**Andy's right is absolute.** He cannot be compelled to take the witness stand. The prosecution cannot comment on his silence or invite the court to draw adverse inferences from his decision not to testify. This right is self-executing and requires no invocation — Andy simply does not take the stand.

**Charlie has the same right as co-accused.** Under the same constitutional and procedural provisions, Charlie cannot be compelled to testify against himself. As a co-accused, his interests are aligned with Andy's: silence protects both from cross-examination that could destroy the legal defense framework.

**THE CHARLIE STATE WITNESS RISK (Rule 119, Section 17):**

This is the prosecution's nuclear option. Under Rule 119, Section 17 of the Revised Rules of Criminal Procedure, the prosecution can move to **discharge Charlie as accused and convert him to a state witness.** The court may grant the discharge if:

> (a) There is absolute necessity for the testimony of the accused whose discharge is requested;
> (b) There is no other direct evidence available for the proper prosecution of the offense committed, except the testimony of said accused;
> (c) The testimony of said accused can be substantially corroborated in its material points;
> (d) Said accused does not appear to be the most guilty; and
> (e) Said accused has not at any time been convicted of any offense involving moral turpitude.

**If Charlie is discharged as state witness, he LOSES his right to remain silent and CAN be compelled to testify against Andy.** This transforms Charlie from a co-accused protected by the right against self-incrimination into a prosecution witness who must answer questions under oath — including questions about the pre-talaq relationship, the nature of his relationship with Andy, what happened inside the apartment on February 12, and whether Andy ever discussed Boony with him.

**Why this is dangerous:** Charlie provides direct testimony from INSIDE the relationship itself. Unlike Boony (who has a provably false S15 claim) or former male partners (whose testimony is character evidence), Charlie can testify to specific facts, conversations, and acts that occurred between him and Andy. The defense's "never deny or confirm" strategy becomes much harder to sustain when the prosecution has a cooperative insider witness.

**Defense against the discharge motion:**

The defense should object on grounds (a) and (b):

1. **No absolute necessity.** The prosecution has other evidence: Boony's affidavit, CCTV footage, testimony from former male partners, testimony from Boony's brothers. Charlie's testimony is cumulative, not absolutely necessary.
2. **Other direct evidence available.** The prosecution can prove its case — or attempt to — through Boony's testimony, the CCTV/video evidence, and corroborating witnesses. The requirement under (b) is that there is "no other direct evidence available." The prosecution has direct evidence from multiple sources.
3. **Argue the elements remain unmet regardless.** Even if Charlie testifies that he and Andy were in a romantic relationship before the talaq, this does not establish the means element under GR 264870 (intent to tilt power against Boony) or the public humiliation element (Boony created the public dimension).

**Charlie must retain independent counsel.** His interests may diverge from Andy's if the prosecution offers a deal. If the prosecution approaches Charlie with a discharge offer, Andy's counsel cannot advise Charlie — that would be a conflict of interest. Charlie needs his own lawyer who can assess whether the discharge offer is in Charlie's interest (it may be — Charlie faces a weak conspiracy charge, and discharge means acquittal on that charge in exchange for testimony).

**If Charlie flips:** The defense must pivot to arguing that Charlie's testimony, even if credible, does not establish the statutory elements. The relationship existed — the defense does not deny what can be proven. But the existence of the relationship does not prove: (1) that Andy used it to dominate Boony (GR 264870); (2) that Andy caused public humiliation through his own acts (Boony brought the audience); or (3) that the relationship constituted an enumerated means under Sec. 5(i) on the charged date (February 12, post-talaq). The legal shields survive even with Charlie's testimony.

## VI. PROCEDURAL DEFENSE: SCOPE OF THE INFORMATION

### A. The Constitutional and Procedural Framework

**1987 Philippine Constitution, Article III, Section 14(2):**

> *"In all criminal prosecutions, the accused shall be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation against him."*

This constitutional guarantee means the Information is not merely a procedural document — it defines the jural reality of what the accused must answer for. An accused cannot be convicted of an act not charged.

**Rule 110, Section 8 (Revised Rules on Criminal Procedure):**

The Information must "aver the acts or omissions constituting the offense" with sufficient particularity. The date alleged is material to the accused's ability to prepare his defense.

**Rule 110, Section 11:**

> *"It is not necessary to state in the complaint or information the precise date the offense was committed except when it is a material element of the offense. The offense may be alleged to have been committed on a date as near as possible to the actual date of its commission."*

"On or about" gives some flexibility for the specific date — but only reasonably proximate to the date alleged, not months before.

**Rule 120, Section 4 (Variance Doctrine):**

When there is variance between the offense charged and that proved, the accused can only be convicted of the offense charged if the proved offense is included in it. A conviction cannot rest on acts entirely outside the scope of what was charged.

### B. The "On or About February 12, 2025" Issue

As analyzed in Section II, the phrase "on or about February 12, 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto" extends only forward from February 12, not backward. If the prosecution argues that the psychological violence actually began before January 23, 2025 (i.e., infidelity during the subsisting marriage), it asserts a different factual predicate than what is alleged in the Information. This would:

1. **Violate the accused's constitutional right** to be informed of the charges (Art. III, Sec. 14[2])
2. **Run afoul of the variance doctrine** under Rule 120
3. **Constitute an amendment** to the Information requiring leave of court after arraignment (Rule 110, Sec. 14)

### C. Can Pre-Talaq Evidence Be Used?

**As basis for conviction:** No. Pre-February 12 acts cannot form the basis of conviction under this Information.

**As contextual or corroborating evidence:** Possibly. Under Rule 130 (Rules of Evidence), evidence of prior acts is admissible to show motive, intent/knowledge, or pattern of behavior. However, this evidence goes to the weight and credibility of the charged acts — it cannot serve as independent acts of psychological violence forming the basis for conviction.

### D. The RA 9262 "Continuing Offense" Doctrine

Section 7 of RA 9262 treats VAWC as a "continuing offense" for purposes of venue — it may be filed where committed or where the victim resides. Jurisprudence (e.g., *Go-Tan v. Tan*) also treats VAWC as a continuing offense for purposes of protection order mechanisms.

**However**, the continuing offense doctrine does not mean the prosecution can convict an accused for acts not alleged in the Information. The doctrine addresses **where to file** and **when to apply for protection orders**, not what acts the accused can be tried for in a specific criminal Information.

If the prosecution wanted to charge the full continuum of psychological violence — including acts before the talaq — it should have filed an Information alleging acts from, for example, "September 2024 through February 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto." It did not. The Information is limited to "on or about February 12, 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto."

**Strategic observation:** If the prosecution believed the strongest theory was pre-talaq infidelity during the subsisting marriage, it should have filed the Information alleging pre-talaq dates. Under the En Banc ruling in GR 252739 (2024), marital infidelity during a subsisting marriage carries a *presumption of intent* — a much stronger prosecutorial position. By filing an Information focused on February 12 (post-talaq), the prosecution inadvertently stepped into the minefield of the talaq defense.

## VII. ANALYSIS — CHARGE 1: DENIAL OF FINANCIAL SUPPORT

### A. The Acharon Standard

Under *Acharon v. People* (GR No. 224946, En Banc, November 9, 2021), denial of financial support is **NOT** criminal per se. The prosecution must prove that Andy *willfully denied* support with the specific intent to cause mental or emotional anguish — that the denial was a tool of psychological violence, not mere financial difficulty, inadvertence, or dispute over amounts.

The Court in *Acharon* held:

> In order for criminal liability to arise under Section 5(i) of R.A. 9262, insofar as it deals with "denial of financial support," there must, therefore, be evidence on record that the accused willfully or consciously withheld financial support *legally due* the woman for the purpose of inflicting mental or emotional anguish upon her. In other words, the *actus reus* of the offense under Section 5(i) is the willful denial of financial support, while the *mens rea* is the intention to inflict mental or emotional anguish upon the woman. Both must thus exist and be proven in court before a person may be convicted of violating Section 5(i) of R.A. 9262.

Both the *actus reus* and *mens rea* must be proven beyond reasonable doubt.

### B. The Evidence

The undisputed evidence on financial support:

| Date | Amount | Source |
|------|--------|--------|
| February 8, 2025 | P2,000 | Andy's affidavit (four days BEFORE the charged incident) |
| February 23, 2025 | P5,000 | Andy's affidavit (eleven days AFTER the charged incident) |

Boony's claim, in her own words from the Judicial Affidavit (S18):

> *"Financially po hindi po talaga niya ako binibigyan ng personal allowance."*
> [Financially, he really did not give me a personal allowance.]

This is the sole basis for the financial support charge — a single sentence in the affidavit. The claim is general and unquantified; it does not specify what amount was due, what was requested, or what was refused. It is directly contradicted by the defense evidence: unrestricted ATM card access during the marriage, wallet access, and specific remittances of P2,000 (February 8) and P5,000 (February 23) after the separation.

### C. Analysis

The fact that Andy sent money on February 10 and February 23 directly contradicts the charge of "denial" of financial support. A total of P7,000 was remitted during the relevant period. While P7,000 may be argued as inadequate financial support for a former wife during *'idda* — and the support obligation under PD 1083 Article 67 is a civil obligation that could independently be enforced — the question for **criminal** purposes under *Acharon* is whether Andy **willfully** withheld support **as a means of psychological abuse**.

Boony's January 30 SMS messages — in which she admitted her shortcomings, stated she was no longer good for Andy, and acknowledged causing him pain — suggest a mutual deterioration of the relationship rather than Andy's deliberate campaign to financially starve her.

The prosecution presents:
- No evidence of a **specific demand** for support that Andy refused
- No evidence of a **pattern of financial withholding** during the marriage as a tool of control
- No evidence that the P2,000 and P5,000 remittances were inadequate **relative to their arrangement** rather than the general cost of living

The support obligation during *'idda* under Article 67 of PD 1083 is a **civil** obligation — its enforcement belongs in the Shari'ah Circuit Court, not in a criminal prosecution under RA 9262 absent proof of willful denial as psychological abuse.

### D. Verdict on Charge 1

**ACQUITTAL.** The prosecution failed to prove beyond reasonable doubt that Andy *willfully denied* financial support as a means of psychological violence under the *Acharon* standard. The fact of remittances on February 10 and February 23 creates reasonable doubt as to willful denial. Even if the amounts were inadequate, inadequacy of support is a civil matter, not a criminal one, absent proof of the specific criminal intent required by *Acharon*.

## VIII. ANALYSIS — CHARGE 2: SEXUAL RELATIONSHIP AS PSYCHOLOGICAL VIOLENCE

This is the weightier and more complex charge. The analysis requires five sub-inquiries.

### A. Does Boony Have Standing as a "Former Wife"?

**Yes.** Section 3(a) of RA 9262 defines the protected class to include women who are "former wives" of the offender. The talaq does not strip Boony of RA 9262 protection. Even as a divorced woman, she remains within the law's protective scope.

This finding establishes standing only. It does not determine whether the charged conduct constitutes psychological violence. The prosecution must still prove the statutory elements.

### B. Does "Marital Infidelity" Exist Post-Talaq?

**No.** The concept of marital infidelity is definitionally tied to a subsisting marriage. *Infidelity* requires *fidelity*, and fidelity obligations flow from the marriage bond.

Under PD 1083:
- Talaq was pronounced on January 23, 2025 and accepted by Boony (Art. 46(1))
- The divorce takes effect upon pronouncement; "become irrevocable" presupposes the divorce already exists in effective form
- *'Idda* follows dissolution; dissolution precedes *'idda* (Art. 56: "marriage **has been** dissolved")
- The obligation to "observe mutual respect and fidelity" is incident to the marriage; it terminates with the marriage bond (Art. 34(1))

From January 23, 2025 onward, Andy had **no fidelity obligation** to Boony. The relationship with Charlie — whatever its nature and whenever it began — cannot constitute "marital infidelity" in the context of February 12, 2025, because there was no marriage to be unfaithful to.

The prosecution's framing as "marital infidelity" is legally incorrect as of January 23, 2025. The proper question is: **whether Andy, a former husband, committed psychological violence against Boony, a former wife, through his relationship with Charlie, which she discovered on February 12, 2025.**

Additionally, traditional concepts of marital infidelity or adultery under the Revised Penal Code (Art. 333) require a subsisting marriage as an essential element. The RPC provisions on adultery and concubinage are further inapplicable because: (1) the marriage was dissolved before the alleged relationship; (2) Muslim marriages are governed by PD 1083, which prevails over general laws under Article 3(1); and (3) the alleged relationship is same-sex, while RPC provisions address only heterosexual conduct.

### C. Was There "Public Ridicule or Humiliation" Caused by Andy?

Section 5(i) requires that the mental or emotional anguish be caused *through* specific means — including "public ridicule or humiliation." The **offender** must be the source of that publicity and humiliation. The "means" requirement cannot be satisfied by the victim's emotional reaction alone.

**The critical question: Who created the public dimension?**

**Prosecution theory:** Andy humiliated Boony by having Charlie inside the apartment and blocking her entry, exposing his relationship in a way visible to officemates.

**Defense theory:** Boony created the public scene by going to Andy's office, involving his boss and officemates, and bringing them to Andy's private apartment.

The undisputed facts favor the defense:

1. **Boony initiated the public exposure.** She went to Andy's office — his place of work — and involved his boss and co-workers.
2. **Boony brought the audience.** She and the officemates traveled *together* to Andy's apartment. The confrontation occurred in their presence because she brought them there.
3. **Andy attempted to prevent exposure.** He blocked the door — conduct consistent with someone who wanted privacy, not someone seeking to humiliate.
4. **The dwelling was Andy's exclusive property.** Under the complete separation of property regime, the apartment was Andy's. Boony had voluntarily left twenty days earlier. Andy did not invite Boony or her companions.
5. **Boony already knew Charlie.** Her own affidavit states she noticed co-workers had suspected the relationship. She knew of and agreed to Charlie's role as Andy's assistant. Boony and Charlie often worked together at Andy's office providing assistance to Andy. She had repeatedly sent Andy to Charlie's apartment during marital conflicts. The February 12 encounter was not the revelation of a hidden secret — it was an uninvited confrontation at a private residence.

**Contrast with GR 270257 (August 2024):** In that case, the conviction rested in part on the accused *flaunting* his affair on social media — a deliberate, public act of humiliation directed at the victim. That element is entirely absent here. Andy did not post about Charlie. He did not invite people to witness his private life. He attempted to *block* Boony's entry.

**The "Boony directed Andy to Charlie" factor:** This fact is devastating to the prosecution's humiliation theory. Boony herself — on multiple occasions during the marriage — told Andy to go sleep at Charlie's apartment. She placed Andy in Charlie's domestic space as a routine response to marital conflict. She cannot now claim that Andy's relationship with Charlie was a deliberate tool of humiliation against her when she was the one who repeatedly directed Andy toward Charlie. The prosecution cannot frame Charlie's relationship with Andy as an instrument of abuse against Boony when Boony herself facilitated that proximity.

**The post-February 12 conduct reinforces this analysis.** After February 12, it was Boony — not Andy — who generated the public dimension: obtaining a BPO (Feb 13), sending emails to Andy's office (Feb 17, Feb 20), forcibly entering the apartment and taking devices (Feb 18), and reportedly making homophobic social media posts (discovered Apr 16). The pattern of public exposure runs from Boony, not from Andy.

**The public dimension of this situation was substantially the result of Boony's own actions.** The law requires that the *offender* cause the public ridicule or humiliation — not that the victim, through her own choices, generate the public exposure and then attribute it to the offender.

### D. The Controlling Jurisprudence: Which Doctrine Applies?

Two Supreme Court decisions pull in opposite directions:

**GR 252739 (En Banc, Hernando J., April 16, 2024):** Marital infidelity is psychological violence; intent to harm is presumed; the affair itself is sufficient. The Court held:

> [I]n instances of marital infidelity, the requirement of specific criminal intent to cause mental and emotional suffering is already satisfied at the moment the perpetrator commits the act of marital infidelity. This finds basis in the fact that marital infidelity is inherently immoral and depraved under prevailing societal, cultural, and religious norms.

**GR 264870 (Second Division, Leonen SAJ, April 21, 2025):** Marital infidelity per se is **NOT** criminal. The Court held:

> The acts complained of, which are the means of committing the offense, should be evaluated on whether they were done with the intention of "causing mental or emotional anguish, public ridicule or humiliation to the woman or her child." It is when these acts are done with the intent to tilt the scales of power within the relationship against the woman, causing her to lose her autonomy does the State have a legitimate interest in punishing such acts. This Court finds that the prosecution failed to prove this element of the offense beyond reasonable doubt. To be sure, the effect on the victim alone, which is the second element, is not enough to sustain a conviction.

Emotional pain alone is insufficient; R.A. 9262 must not be used as a State instrument to punish moral transgressions or intrude into the private realm of human relationships.

**Which controls here?**

**First**, even under GR 252739, the presumption of intent applies to *marital* infidelity — conduct occurring during a *subsisting marriage*. Andy's relationship with Charlie on February 12 occurred post-talaq. The presumption that attaches to marital infidelity does not automatically extend to post-divorce conduct where no fidelity obligation exists.

**Second**, GR 264870 (April 2025) is the most recent Supreme Court pronouncement on the issue. While it is a Second Division decision that does not formally overturn GR 252739 (En Banc), it represents the current articulation of the doctrine. Under the principle that courts should apply the most recent pronouncement, GR 264870's stricter elements standard should govern.

**Third**, the Caguioa concurrence in GR 270257 (August 2024) — decided by the Third Division between the two decisions — explicitly states that "marital infidelity per se is not punished by RA 9262," distinguishing conviction on the basis of deliberate public flaunting. This reinforces the GR 264870 direction.

Under GR 264870, the prosecution must prove:
1. That the relationship with Charlie was part of a **pattern of domination or coercion** intended to cause emotional harm to Boony; and
2. That it was this pattern — not merely Boony's emotional response to the discovery — that caused the psychological violence.

The prosecution has not met this burden. The evidence shows:
- Andy and Boony's marriage failed due to mutual incompatibility (her own SMS of January 30 confirms this)
- The talaq was the culmination of recurring conflicts, not a sudden act of cruelty
- There is no evidence that Andy manipulated Boony using Charlie as a tool of domination
- Boony's discovery of Charlie occurred because she went uninvited to Andy's private home with his co-workers — Andy did not orchestrate this exposure
- **Boony herself directed Andy toward Charlie** during the marriage — the direction of influence runs opposite to what the prosecution would need to prove

### E. The Constitutional Liberty Defense

The post-divorce context raises fundamental constitutional questions. Andy, as a divorced person, possesses the constitutional right to privacy, liberty of abode, freedom of association, and general liberty under the Due Process Clause. Article 54(a) of PD 1083 explicitly recognizes that divorced persons "may contract another marriage" — affirming their liberty to form new intimate relationships.

Criminalizing Andy's post-divorce private conduct — in his own apartment, with a person his former wife knew and had repeatedly directed him toward — merely because it causes emotional distress to his former wife would effectively grant Boony veto power over Andy's personal life after divorce. The State has no business interfering in private experiences of heartbreak that do not involve coercion or abuse of power.

### F. The "Boony Directed Andy to Sleep at Charlie's Apartment" — Specific Legal Consequences

This fact introduces a concept that cuts deeply against the prosecution: the complainant's own facilitation of the conditions she now claims caused her harm.

**1. Absence of intent to harm.** If Boony was the one directing Andy toward Charlie, Andy cannot plausibly be said to have used the Charlie relationship as a *weapon* against Boony. The weapon theory requires that Andy deployed Charlie to hurt Boony. But Boony was handing Andy the "weapon" herself.

**2. Negation of the domination/coercion pattern under GR 264870.** Coercion implies the victim is being controlled against her will. Andy cannot have been coercing Boony through his relationship with Charlie when Boony was telling Andy to go to Charlie's place. The coercion analysis runs entirely backward.

**3. Undermined credibility of anguish testimony.** Under RA 9262 and *Dinamling*, the victim's testimony is both sufficient and necessary to establish mental and emotional anguish. But credibility is assessed against the totality of the evidence. A witness who told the man she is accusing to repeatedly go to his alleged lover's apartment will face serious credibility challenges in establishing that she experienced the kind of sustained, acute psychological anguish that Section 5(i) requires.

**4. The proximate cause question.** Section 5(i) requires that the *offender* cause the anguish through specific acts. If Boony repeatedly sent Andy to Charlie, and the relationship that developed is partly a product of that arrangement, Boony's own conduct is a proximate cause of the situation she now claims caused her anguish. The law does not permit the State to criminalize a man for a relationship his wife directed him into.

**5. Collapse of the "clandestine affair" narrative.** The prosecution's theory depends on a narrative of concealment — Andy secretly conducting a relationship with Charlie, hidden from Boony, which caused her humiliation upon discovery. The facts collapse this narrative: Boony knew and agreed to Charlie's role as Andy's assistant; she and Charlie often worked together at Andy's office; and she repeatedly sent Andy to sleep at Charlie's apartment during marital conflicts. There was no concealed affair. Boony knew who Charlie was and had been directing Andy toward him as a matter of routine.

### G. Pre-Talaq CCTV and Video Evidence — Defense Response

**Assumed fact:** CCTV footage and private videos exist showing Andy and Charlie in romantic acts, including kissing, before the talaq on January 23, 2025. Boony suspected the relationship and the February 12 discovery confirmed her suspicion.

**The defense does NOT deny this evidence.** Instead, it reframes:

1. **Admissibility:** The evidence is admissible as CONTEXT under Rule 130 (motive, intent, pattern) — but it depicts events BEFORE February 12, 2025 and therefore cannot serve as the independent basis for conviction under this Information.

2. **Legal framing:** "The nature and timing of any relationship is not the legal question before this Court. The question is whether the prosecution has proven, beyond reasonable doubt, that the accused committed the acts charged — acts on or about February 12, 2025 — through the means enumerated in Section 5(i)."

3. **Mitigating context:** Even with pre-talaq evidence, the following facts remain:
   - Charlie was Andy's known assistant whom Boony agreed to and worked alongside
   - Boony repeatedly directed Andy to sleep at Charlie's apartment during marital conflicts
   - Boony's own conduct facilitated the proximity from which the relationship developed
   - The talaq was the culmination of mutual dysfunction confirmed by Boony's own January 30 SMS

4. **The talaq reframing:** The prosecution may argue Andy divorced Boony to be with Charlie. The defense counters: Andy's affidavit cites "emotional incompatibility and recurrent quarrels" — not Charlie. Boony's own SMS confirms she caused Andy pain. Multiple prior talaqs followed by reconciliation show a pattern of marital breakdown, not a strategic divorce.

5. **Probability assessment:** With pre-talaq kissing video evidence, the probability of acquittal drops from ~95% (without evidence) to **~65-70%** — provided Andy does NOT testify and the defense does NOT make affirmative claims about the relationship timeline.

### H. Pre-Talaq Romantic Relationship — Assumed Fact and Defense Strategy

**FACTUAL ASSUMPTION:** The romantic relationship between Andy and Charlie began before the talaq on January 23, 2025. CCTV footage and private videos showing romantic acts, including kissing, exist from the pre-talaq period. Boony suspected the relationship during the marriage. The February 12, 2025 discovery confirmed her suspicion.

**This is the factual reality the defense must prepare for.** The defense strategy does not depend on denying the pre-talaq relationship — it depends on the legal shields that survive even when pre-talaq intimacy is proven.

When the prosecution presents this evidence:

**Shield LOST:**

**Shields that STILL HOLD:**

### I. Former Male Partners and Boony's Brothers as Witnesses

**1. Former male partners — character evidence objection.**

Boony has located other males who were previously intimate with Andy and may present their testimony. This is **character evidence** under Rule 130, Section 54 — offered to prove conduct on a specific occasion. It is generally **inadmissible**.

**Defense objection:** Rule 130, Sec. 54 prohibits the prosecution from proving the accused's character (in this case, bisexuality and prior male relationships) to establish that he committed the charged offense on February 12. The defense should object to this testimony's admissibility.

**Even if admitted** — as "similar acts" evidence showing motive, intent, or behavioral pattern (Rule 130, Sec. 34) — the impact is limited. Former partners can establish Andy's orientation. They CANNOT establish:
- That any specific relationship caused Boony anguish through enumerated means under Sec. 5(i)
- That Andy used these relationships as tools of domination against Boony (GR 264870 standard)

**2. Does NOT apply the Acharon standard for financial support.** The Resolution does not cite or discuss *Acharon v. **(b)** Andy caused public ridicule or humiliation through his own acts — the public element was generated by Boony's decision to visit Andy's office, involve his colleagues, and proceed with them to his private residence; Andy attempted to prevent entry, consistent with a desire for privacy;

**(c)** Andy's post-divorce relationship with Charlie constitutes a pattern of domination or coercion — Boony knew and agreed to Charlie's role, worked alongside him at Andy's office, and directed Andy to sleep at Charlie's apartment during marital conflicts; the direction of influence runs opposite to what the prosecution must prove under GR 264870;

**(d)** The statutory means requirement is met — the "public ridicule or humiliation" element was substantially generated by Boony's own conduct, and the law requires the offender, not the victim, to cause the publicity.

## IX. ANALYSIS — CHARLIE'S LIABILITY

### A. The Go-Tan Conspiracy Doctrine

Charlie is named as co-accused. The Information charges him as a co-conspirator in the psychological violence.

Section 3(a) of RA 9262 defines the offender as a person in a marital, former marital, or sexual/dating relationship with the victim. Charlie does not fall within this relational nexus. However, in *Go-Tan v. Spouses Tan* (G.R. No. 168852, September 30, 2008), the Supreme Court held:

> [T]he principle of conspiracy under Article 8 of the RPC may be applied suppletorily to R.A. No. 9262 because of the express provision of Section 47 that the RPC shall be supplementary to said law. Thus, general provisions of the RPC, which by their nature, are necessarily applicable, may be applied suppletorily.
>
> Thus, the principle of conspiracy may be applied to R.A. No. 9262. For once conspiracy or action in concert to achieve a criminal design is shown, the act of one is the act of all the conspirators, and the precise extent or modality of participation of each of them becomes secondary, since all the conspirators are principals.

Under *Go-Tan*, persons who act *in conspiracy* with the primary offender can be charged even if they lack the relational nexus required by Section 3(a).

*Go-Tan* involved in-laws who conspired with the husband to commit psychological and economic violence. Whether the same doctrine applies to a paramour or sexual partner of the accused has not been directly ruled upon in any verified local jurisprudence. The application to paramours is a logical extension of *Go-Tan* but remains **inferential**.

### B. Why Charlie's Charge Fails on the Merits

The conspiracy theory requires proof that Charlie acted *in concert with Andy* to inflict psychological violence on Boony. The prosecution's evidence is that Charlie was present in Andy's apartment on February 12 — nothing more. Mere presence does not establish conspiracy.

Moreover:

1. **If the charges against Andy fail — as analyzed above — there is no underlying offense to which Charlie can be a co-conspirator.** The conspiracy must attach to a proven principal offense. If Andy is acquitted, Charlie's derivative liability collapses.

2. **Charlie's presence in Andy's apartment is not criminal.** He was Andy's known assistant whom Boony herself had agreed to.

3. **There is no evidence Charlie individually committed any act of psychological violence against Boony.** He did not send her messages, publicize the relationship, or take any act directed at causing her anguish.

4. **Boony herself knew Charlie and facilitated his proximity to Andy.** She directed Andy to sleep at Charlie's apartment during marital conflicts. The prosecution cannot frame Charlie's relationship with Andy as a conspiracy against Boony when Boony herself facilitated that relationship.

### C. Verdict as to Charlie

**ACQUITTAL.** No proof of conspiracy. No underlying principal offense (Andy acquitted). Charlie's mere presence in Andy's apartment — as a known assistant whom Boony herself had agreed to and worked alongside — does not constitute an act of psychological violence.

**D E C I S I O N**

The accused Andy stands charged with causing psychological violence upon his former wife BOONY through: (1) denial of financial support; and (2) indulging in a sexual relationship with another male, CHARLIE, inside their dwelling on or about February 12, 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto. Charlie is charged as co-accused.

After careful review of the evidence, the Information, the relevant provisions of R.A. 9262, P.D. 1083, the 1987 Constitution, and the controlling jurisprudence, this Court **ACQUITS** both accused on the following grounds:

**On Charge 1 (Denial of Financial Support):**

The prosecution failed to prove beyond reasonable doubt that Andy *willfully denied* financial support to Boony as a means of inflicting psychological violence. The undisputed evidence shows that Andy remitted P2,000 on February 8, 2025 and P5,000 on February 23, 2025 — four days before and eleven days after the charged incident. Under *Acharon v. People* (GR No. 224946, En Banc, November 9, 2021), the failure to provide financial support is not criminal per se; the prosecution must prove deliberate withholding as a tool of psychological abuse. No such proof was presented. The prosecution's evidence of "denial" is general and rebutted by the specific acts of remittance. The civil support obligation during *'idda* under Article 67 of PD 1083 is a matter for the Shari'ah Circuit Court, not a criminal prosecution absent proof of the specific criminal intent required by *Acharon*. **This charge is DISMISSED.**

**On Charge 2 (Sexual Relationship with Charlie as Psychological Violence):**

The Information charges acts occurring on or about February 12, 2025. On January 23, 2025 — twenty days prior — Andy pronounced *talaq* and Boony accepted it, moving out of the apartment. Under P.D. 1083, Articles 46, 56, and 57, the marriage was dissolved upon pronouncement of *talaq*; the *'idda* period that followed was the waiting period *after* dissolution, not a continuation of the marriage bond.

**Marital infidelity cannot exist where there is no marriage.** The alleged relationship with Charlie, discovered by Boony on February 12, 2025, occurred in the context of a dissolved marriage. The charge of psychological violence through "marital infidelity" is factually inapplicable to the period after January 23, 2025. The fidelity obligation under Article 34(1) of PD 1083 terminated with the marriage bond.

While RA 9262 extends protection to "former wives," the prosecution must still prove that Andy caused Boony public ridicule or humiliation *through his own conduct*. The Court finds that the public dimension of the February 12 incident was substantially generated by Boony's own decision to visit Andy's place of work, involve his colleagues, and proceed with his officemates to his private residence. Andy did not arrange this exposure. He attempted to prevent Boony's entry — conduct consistent with a desire for privacy, not a desire to humiliate.

The Court further notes that Charlie was not a stranger introduced clandestinely into Andy's life. He was Andy's assistant — hired after Boony repeatedly failed to obtain a house helper despite Andy's requests. Boony knew of and agreed to Charlie's role. She and Charlie often worked together at Andy's office providing assistance to Andy. Despite her stated discomfort with having a male helper in the apartment, Boony herself, on multiple occasions during periods of marital conflict, *directed* Andy to sleep at Charlie's apartment. This conduct by the private complainant has the following legal consequences:

*First*, it eliminates any factual foundation for the "clandestine affair" theory. Boony knew Charlie. She facilitated his proximity to Andy. No element of deception or concealment — central to the prosecution's humiliation theory — can be established on these facts.

*Second*, it negates the "pattern of domination or coercion" required under *G.R. No. 264870* (Leonen SAJ, April 21, 2025). A former husband cannot be said to have used a relationship to dominate or coerce his former wife when she herself directed him into that relationship's proximity.

*Third*, it raises a serious credibility issue for Boony's testimony on mental and emotional anguish. A witness who voluntarily and repeatedly placed the accused in proximity with the alleged third party cannot credibly testify that the resulting relationship caused her the kind of acute, sustained psychological suffering that Section 5(i) requires.

*Fourth*, Boony's own conduct — directing Andy to Charlie's apartment — is itself a proximate cause of the situation she now assigns as criminal. The law does not permit a complainant to set the stage for a relationship and then invoke the criminal law against the actor when the relationship develops in a direction she finds painful.

This Court applies the doctrine in *G.R. No. 264870*: the prosecution must prove that the accused's conduct was part of a pattern of domination or coercion intended to cause emotional harm — not merely that the former wife experienced emotional anguish upon discovering his new relationship. No such pattern has been proven. The evidence shows the opposite: a mutual deterioration confirmed by Boony's own communications, a lawful divorce, and a post-divorce relationship with a person Boony herself had directed Andy toward. **This charge is DISMISSED.**

**On the Prosecution's Possible Pre-Talaq Theory:**

To the extent the prosecution seeks to establish that the psychological violence was committed *before* January 23, 2025 — during the subsisting marriage — this Court holds that such evidence cannot support a conviction under this Information, which charges acts "on or about **February 12, 2025** and sometime subsequent thereto." The accused's constitutional right to be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation (Art. III, Sec. 14[2], 1987 Constitution) prohibits conviction for acts not alleged in the Information. Pre-February 12 evidence may be considered as contextual background, but cannot serve as the independent basis for conviction.

If the prosecution believes a criminal offense was committed during the marriage, the remedy is to file a separate Information alleging such acts, not to expand the scope of this Information beyond what it charges.

**On Charlie's Liability:**

The conspiracy theory under *Go-Tan v. Spouses Tan* (GR 168852, September 30, 2008) requires proof that Charlie acted in concert with Andy to inflict psychological violence on Boony. The prosecution's evidence is Charlie's presence in Andy's apartment — nothing more. Charlie was Andy's known personal assistant and house helper. Boony herself knew Charlie and directed Andy to sleep at Charlie's apartment during marital conflicts. There is no proof of conspiracy, no underlying principal offense (Andy being acquitted), and no independent act of psychological violence by Charlie.

**WHEREFORE**, finding the prosecution to have failed to prove guilt of both accused beyond reasonable doubt, the Court hereby renders judgment:

**ACQUITTING ANDY and CHARLIE** of the charge of violation of Section 5(i) of R.A. 9262 as charged in Crim. Case No. 2025-18700. Their bail bonds are ordered CANCELLED and returned. Any hold departure orders issued in connection with this case are LIFTED.

The civil aspect of this case — specifically, the support obligation under Article 67 of P.D. 1083 which continues during the *'idda* period — is **RESERVED** for prosecution in the appropriate Shari'ah Circuit Court, which has jurisdiction over matters arising under the Code of Muslim Personal Laws.

**SO ORDERED.**

### Critical Note on GR 264870 (Leonen SAJ, Second Division, April 21, 2025)

This case carries the most analytical weight for Andy's acquittal. Key clarifications from the verified primary source:

1. **It is a Second Division decision, not En Banc.** Under *stare decisis*, the more recent En Banc ruling (GR 252739, April 2024) technically has higher precedential weight — but GR 264870 articulates a stricter elements analysis and was decided almost exactly one year later.

2. **The actual holding is precise.** The means element (psychological violence) must be evaluated on whether the acts were done with the intent to "tilt the scales of power within the relationship against the woman, causing her to lose her autonomy." The prosecution failed to prove this intent.

3. **The recantation was critical.** The private complainant's Affidavit of Recantation — stating she was not hurt, they had an understanding about outside relationships, and the case was filed against her better judgment — created reasonable doubt on the anguish element.

4. **The Caguioa concurrence in GR 270257 converges.** Both decisions confirm the direction of the jurisprudence: infidelity alone is insufficient; deliberate, public acts of humiliation are required for conviction.
