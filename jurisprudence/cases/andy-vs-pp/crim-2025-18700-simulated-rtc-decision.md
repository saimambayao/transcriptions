## II. STATEMENT OF THE CASE

Upon arraignment, both accused pleaded not guilty. Pre-trial and trial ensued. The prosecution presented private complainant Boony as its principal witness, supported by her Judicial Affidavit, Marriage Certificate, and Incident Report Form. ## III. VERSION OF THE PROSECUTION

The prosecution's evidence, drawn principally from the Judicial Affidavit of private complainant Boony, establishes the following narrative:

Boony and Andy were married on May 20, 2017, with a marriage contract issued by the Shari'ah Court on August 16, 2022. They have no children.

In September 2024, Andy hired Charlie, a 19-year-old male, ostensibly as a household assistant, and secured a boarding house for him in Canton City. Boony testified that Charlie came to the apartment only twice when she was away at meetings, and that Charlie only washed dishes but did not clean (*"Dalawang beses lang po nakapunta si Charlie sa bahay namin noong may meeting ako sa labas. Naghugas lang po siya ng pinggan pero hindi naglinis."* S16, Judicial Affidavit).

Boony further testified that after Charlie's arrival, Andy's behavior changed. Andy and Charlie were frequently together, even at midnight, and Andy often slept at Charlie's boarding house (*"Madalas po sila magkasama ni Charlie kahit hating gabi. Minsan at madalas doon na po siya natutulog sa boarding house ni Charlie."* S17, Judicial Affidavit).

On February 12, 2025, at approximately 4:30 p.m., Boony went to Andy's office and was told Andy had returned to Canton City. Later that evening, she proceeded to the apartment. Andy blocked the door and refused to let her enter. Boony claims she "slipped in because she is small" and saw Charlie and his things inside (*"Nakapasok po ako dahil maliit po ako na babae. Doon ko po nakita si Charlie at yong mga gamit niya."* S15, Judicial Affidavit).

Boony testified to experiencing severe emotional anguish: *"Sobrang sakit po sa part ko bilang babae, asawa yung ginawa ng asawa ko sa akin. Na ginawa niya akong tanga at pinapalit sa lalake. Nagkasakit na din po ako physically at emotionally."* (S18, Judicial Affidavit). She also alleged denial of financial support: *"Financially po hindi po talaga niya ako binibigyan ng personal allowance."* (S18, Judicial Affidavit).

The prosecution additionally presented CCTV footage and private videos purportedly showing Andy and Charlie in romantic acts, including kissing, captured before January 23, 2025. Testimony from Boony's brothers was offered to corroborate Boony's emotional condition and the circumstances of the marital breakdown. The prosecution also offered testimony from males who claimed to have been previously intimate with Andy, to establish a pattern of bisexual relationships during the marriage.

## IV. VERSION OF THE DEFENSE

The defense's evidence establishes the following:

On **January 23, 2025**, Andy pronounced talaq (divorce by repudiation) under Presidential Decree No. 1083 (Code of Muslim Personal Laws of the Philippines). The pronouncement was witnessed by Boony's own brother, who was living with Andy and Boony at the time. Boony accepted the talaq and voluntarily departed the apartment, moving to the residence of her other brother.

On **January 30, 2025**, Boony sent Andy SMS messages acknowledging her shortcomings, admitting she caused him pain, and stating she was "no longer good for him" -- consistent with acceptance of the divorce, not the conduct of a wife who believes her marriage subsists.

On **February 8, 2025** (corrected from February 10 in the record), Andy sent Boony P2,000 through electronic transfer. A receipt for this remittance was presented as documentary evidence.

On **February 12, 2025**, Boony came to Andy's office uninvited, brought officemates, and proceeded to the apartment. Andy blocked the door. The defense maintains Boony did NOT enter the apartment. The confrontation occurred outside, in the presence of Andy's officemates and other persons, and it was Boony who created the public scene.

On **February 23, 2025**, Andy sent Boony an additional P5,000 through Boony's brother. The brother can corroborate this remittance.

On **April 6, 2025**, both families met for a *kawagib* (divorce settlement) meeting. There was no discussion of reconciliation or revoking the talaq. Andy offered Boony a housing unit as part of the settlement.

During the marriage, Boony had unrestricted access to Andy's wallet and ATM cards. Andy actively encouraged Boony to set aside money for herself. Boony chose not to do so.

The defense further established that on **February 13, 2025**, Boony obtained a Barangay Protection Order (BPO) and entered the apartment, during which she damaged Andy's personal property -- an incident witnessed by barangay officials and police, and recorded on video. On **February 17 and 20, 2025**, Boony sent emails to Andy's boss making accusations that were read by Andy's officemates, directly causing Andy to be asked to resign from his employment on **February 18, 2025**. Boony subsequently posted homophobic content about Andy on social media and spread allegations of "immorality" to other offices, destroying Andy's ability to find employment.

The defense raised the talaq under PD 1083 as an affirmative defense, arguing that the marriage was dissolved on January 23, 2025 -- twenty days before the acts charged in the Information -- and that no marital fidelity obligation existed on February 12, 2025.

## VI. THE COURT'S RULING

### A. PRELIMINARY MATTER: THE TALAQ AND THE STATUS OF THE MARRIAGE

#### 1. The Legal Framework Under PD 1083

This case requires the Court to consider, as a threshold matter, the effect of a talaq pronouncement under Presidential Decree No. 1083 on the marital status of the parties. No published Supreme Court decision has addressed whether talaq under PD 1083 constitutes a defense to a charge under R.A. 9262. This Court must therefore reason from the statutory text and from the principles of Islamic personal law that PD 1083 codifies.

PD 1083 Art. 3(1) establishes the hierarchical supremacy of the Code over general laws:

> "In case of conflict between any provision of this Code and laws of general application, the former shall prevail."

R.A. 9262 is a law of general application. PD 1083, by contrast, is a special law governing Muslim personal relations -- including marriage and divorce -- with an express supremacy clause. Where PD 1083 provides for the dissolution of a Muslim marriage by talaq, its provisions prevail over any general law that would treat the marriage as subsisting.

Article 45 of PD 1083 defines divorce:

> "Divorce is the formal dissolution of the marriage bond in accordance with this Code to be granted only after the exhaustion of all possible means of reconciliation between the spouses. It may be effected by: (a) Repudiation of the wife by the husband (talaq)..."

Article 46(1) prescribes the mechanics:

> "A divorce by talaq may be affected by the husband in a single repudiation of his wife during her non-menstrual period (tuhr) within which he has totally abstained from carnal relation with her. Any number of repudiations made during one tuhr shall constitute only one repudiation and shall become irrevocable after the expiration of the prescribed 'idda."

Article 56 defines *'idda*:

> "'Idda is the period of waiting prescribed for a woman whose marriage **has been** dissolved by death or by divorce the completion of which shall enable her to contract a new marriage."

The use of the past perfect tense -- "has been dissolved" -- unambiguously establishes that dissolution occurs upon pronouncement, not upon the expiration of *'idda*. The *'idda* follows dissolution; it does not cause it.

Article 34(1) imposes marital obligations:

> "The husband and the wife are obliged to live together, observe mutual respect and fidelity, and render mutual help and support in accordance with this Code."

The obligation of "mutual respect and fidelity" is incident to the marriage bond. It terminates with the dissolution of that bond. While Article 67 extends the wife's right to support during *'idda*, no parallel provision extends the fidelity obligation beyond the point of dissolution.

#### 2. The Evidence of the Talaq

The evidence establishes that Andy pronounced talaq on January 23, 2025. The following facts are established in the record:

**(a) The pronouncement was witnessed.** Boony's own brother -- who was living with Andy and Boony at the time -- witnessed the talaq pronouncement. The prosecution has not presented evidence contradicting the occurrence of the pronouncement.

**(b) Boony accepted and departed.** Boony voluntarily left the apartment on January 23, 2025, and moved to her other brother's residence. This physical separation is undisputed.

**(c) Boony's January 30 SMS messages.** Seven days after the talaq, Boony sent Andy messages acknowledging her faults in the marriage, stating she caused him pain, and declaring she was "no longer good for him." These messages are the words of a woman who has accepted the dissolution of her marriage, not of a wife who believes her marriage continues.

**(d) The April 6 settlement meeting.** Both families met for *kawagib* -- divorce settlement discussions. No discussion of reconciliation or revoking the talaq occurred. The very fact of *kawagib* negotiations presupposes a completed divorce; one does not negotiate divorce settlement terms for a subsisting marriage.

**(e) Seven months of settlement negotiations.** From April through November 2025, the parties negotiated settlement terms. Boony agreed to terms and communicated acceptance through her aunt, requesting finalization after her October 15 graduation. This seven-month course of conduct is incompatible with the prosecution's characterization of Andy as Boony's "lawful husband."

**(f) The criminal complaint was Plan B.** On November 3, 2025, Boony's aunt communicated that the family was withdrawing from the divorce settlement and would pursue criminal charges. The timing establishes that the criminal case was initiated only after the settlement path collapsed -- not as an immediate response to the alleged psychological violence of February 12.

**(g) Boony's Judicial Affidavit omits the talaq entirely.** Boony testifies throughout as if the marriage subsisted on February 12, 2025, referring to Andy as "asawa ko" (my husband) and making no mention of the January 23 pronouncement, her acceptance, her voluntary departure, or the settlement negotiations. This wholesale omission of a material fact raises serious questions about the completeness and candor of her testimony.

#### 3. The Prosecution's Challenge: Non-Filing Under Art. 161

The prosecution contends that because Andy has not filed the written notice of talaq with the Shari'ah Circuit Court as required by Article 161(1) of PD 1083, the talaq cannot be recognized by this Court.

Article 161(1) provides:

> "Any Muslim male who has pronounced a talaq shall, without delay, file with the Clerk of Court of the Shari'a Circuit Court of the place where his family resides a written notice of such fact and the circumstances attended thereto, after having served a copy thereof to the wife concerned. The talaq pronounced shall not become irrevocable until after the expiration of the prescribed 'idda. The notice filed shall be conclusive evidence that talaq has been pronounced."

This Court finds that the non-filing does not void the talaq. **First**, Article 161(1) provides that the notice "shall be conclusive evidence that talaq has been pronounced" -- it creates an evidentiary presumption, not a constitutive requirement. **Second**, Article 161(1) states that the talaq "shall not become irrevocable until after the expiration of the prescribed 'idda" -- not that it shall not take effect without filing. The talaq's effectivity and its irrevocability are distinct legal events. **Third**, no provision of PD 1083 declares a talaq void for failure to file under Art. 161. Had the Legislature intended non-filing to void the talaq, it would have included an express nullity clause -- as it did for prohibited marriages under Article 27. **Fourth**, Article 161(1) imposes no prescriptive period that would nullify the talaq for delayed filing.

The non-filing means that the defense cannot invoke the "conclusive evidence" presumption of Art. 161(1). However, the talaq may be proven by other competent evidence -- including the testimony of the witness (Boony's own brother), the physical separation, the SMS messages, and the settlement negotiations. The defense is not deprived of its right to prove the talaq merely because the administrative notice was not filed.

#### 4. The Menstruation Challenge

Boony claims she was menstruating on January 23, 2025. Article 46(1) provides that talaq may be effected "during her non-menstrual period (tuhr)." The prosecution argues that talaq during menstruation is invalid.

This Court addresses this challenge on four independent grounds.

**First, the evidentiary question.** Boony's claim that she was menstruating on January 23, 2025, is an assertion about a physiological state more than fourteen months prior to trial. No medical records from that date have been presented. The claim rests entirely on Boony's uncorroborated testimony -- testimony from a witness whose credibility is compromised by the provably false claim of entering the apartment (addressed in Section VI.E below). Menstruation on a specific date is not a judicially noticeable fact. It requires proof. This Court cannot credit an uncorroborated, self-serving assertion of a physiological state from over a year ago.

**Second, the Islamic legal question.** PD 1083 Article 4(1) directs that the Code shall be construed by reference to "the primary sources of Muslim law." Article 6(2) recognizes the Hanafi, Hanbali, Maliki, and Shafi'i schools. The near-unanimous position of all four Sunni schools is that talaq during menstruation -- known as *talaq al-bid'ah* (innovative or irregular divorce) -- is sinful (*haram*) but effective (*sahih*). The husband sins by pronouncing outside of *tuhr*, but the divorce takes legal effect. This is founded on the hadith of Abdullah ibn Umar (may Allah be pleased with him), who divorced his wife during menstruation. The Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded him to take her back, but when asked whether the divorce counted, it was confirmed: the divorce was effective. This position is held by Imam Abu Hanifa, Imam Malik, Imam al-Shafi'i, and Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal. Only a small minority hold that talaq during menstruation is void -- a position considered heterodox by mainstream Sunni jurisprudence.

**Third, the PD 1083 textual analysis.** Article 46(1) says talaq "**may be** affected... during her non-menstrual period (tuhr)." The phrase "may be" is permissive, not mandatory. It describes the proper procedure, not a condition of validity. No provision of PD 1083 declares talaq during menstruation void. Compare with Article 161(1), where the word "**shall**" is mandatory -- "Any Muslim male who has pronounced a talaq **shall**, without delay, file..." -- yet non-filing does not void the talaq. The absence of an express nullity clause for talaq during menstruation, when the Legislature included such clauses for other situations, indicates that invalidity was not intended.

**Fourth, estoppel by conduct.** Even assuming *arguendo* that the talaq was technically irregular, Boony's conduct for fourteen months after January 23 constitutes acceptance and ratification: she voluntarily departed the apartment; she sent the January 30 SMS acknowledging the breakdown; the families held *kawagib* discussions on April 6; she negotiated settlement terms through November 2025. She never sought to challenge the talaq's validity in the Shari'ah Circuit Court. She never attempted to resume cohabitation. She cannot now, for the first time in a criminal proceeding, claim the talaq was void due to menstruation -- particularly when the proper forum for challenging talaq validity is the Shari'ah Circuit Court (PD 1083 Art. 143), not this Court.

#### 5. Finding on the Talaq

Based on the totality of the evidence, this Court finds that Andy pronounced talaq on January 23, 2025; that Boony accepted the talaq and voluntarily departed; that both parties and their families treated the pronouncement as an effective dissolution of the marriage bond; and that the talaq took legal effect upon pronouncement in accordance with PD 1083 Art. 46(1). **On February 12, 2025 -- the date charged in the Information -- Andy and Boony were divorced.**

The characterization of Andy as "the lawful husband" in the Information is factually incorrect as of the charged date. The marriage bond had been dissolved twenty days prior. While this finding does not divest this Court of jurisdiction over the R.A. 9262 charge (which applies to "wife or former wife" under Sec. 3(a)), it fundamentally alters the legal framework within which the charged acts must be assessed -- particularly with respect to the concept of "marital infidelity" and the obligations incident to marriage.

### B. CHARGE 1: DENIAL OF FINANCIAL SUPPORT

#### 1. The Controlling Standard: *Acharon v. People*

The elements of violation of Section 5(i) of R.A. 9262, insofar as it deals with denial of financial support, were authoritatively established by the Supreme Court En Banc in *Acharon v. People* (G.R. No. 224946, November 9, 2021, Caguioa, J., En Banc):

> "In other words, to be punishable by Section 5(i) of R.A. 9262, it must ultimately be proven that the accused had the intent of inflicting mental or emotional anguish upon the woman, thereby inflicting psychological violence upon her, with the willful denial of financial support being the means selected by the accused to accomplish said purpose."

The *Acharon* Court further clarified:

> "[T]he mere failure or one's inability to provide financial support is not sufficient to rise to the level of criminality under Section 5(i), even if mental or emotional anguish is experienced by the woman. In other words, even if the woman were to suffer mental or emotional anguish due to the lack of financial support, but the accused merely *failed* or was *unable* to so provide support, then criminal liability would not arise."

Under *Acharon*, the prosecution must prove two things: (1) the *actus reus* -- willful denial of financial support legally due the woman; and (2) the *mens rea* -- the intent to inflict mental or emotional anguish through such denial.

#### 2. The Prosecution's Evidence

The prosecution's evidence on this charge consists of a single sentence from Boony's Judicial Affidavit:

> *"Financially po hindi po talaga niya ako binibigyan ng personal allowance."* (S18)
> [Financially, he really did not give me a personal allowance.]

No amount of support allegedly due was specified. No evidence of demand and refusal was presented. No evidence of financial capacity and willful withholding was adduced. No evidence of intent to cause anguish through financial deprivation was offered.

#### 3. The Defense's Evidence

The defense presented the following evidence rebutting the denial of financial support:

**(a)** During the marriage, Boony had unrestricted access to Andy's wallet and ATM cards. She kept one of Andy's ATM cards and withdrew money freely. She paid for everything, including restaurant bills, from Andy's wallet. She could purchase items for her personal needs without restriction.

**(b)** Andy actively encouraged Boony to save money for herself. Boony chose not to do so. Her "no personal allowance" claim reflects a preference about the *form* of financial support (a separate cash allocation), not a denial of support itself.

**(c)** After the talaq, when Boony physically departed and Andy's wallet and ATM cards naturally remained with him, Andy sent **P2,000 on February 8, 2025** -- documentary evidence of this remittance was presented in court. Andy sent an additional **P5,000 on February 23, 2025**, through Boony's brother.

**(d)** On April 6, 2025, Andy offered Boony a housing unit he had paid for and built, as part of the divorce settlement.

#### 4. Finding on Charge 1

The prosecution has failed to establish the elements required by *Acharon*. The evidence shows the exact opposite of willful denial: unrestricted financial access during the marriage, documented remittances after the talaq, and a housing unit offered as settlement. The single uncorroborated statement *"hindi po talaga niya ako binibigyan ng personal allowance"* is contradicted by the documentary evidence and by the undisputed fact that Boony had unrestricted access to Andy's financial resources throughout the marriage.

There is no evidence of *actus reus* (willful denial of support legally due). There is no evidence of *mens rea* (intent to inflict anguish through such denial). The prosecution has failed to prove this charge beyond reasonable doubt.

**ACCUSED ANDY IS ACQUITTED of the charge of denial of financial support.**

### C. CHARGE 2: SEXUAL RELATIONSHIP AS PSYCHOLOGICAL VIOLENCE

This is the central contest of the case. The prosecution alleges that Andy committed psychological violence by "indulging in a sexual relationship with another male named CHARLIE inside their dwelling." The Court must resolve several interlocking legal questions.

#### 1. The Elements Under *Dinamling v. People*

The elements of Section 5(i) were laid down in *Dinamling v. People* (G.R. No. 199522, June 22, 2015, Peralta, J., Third Division):

> "(1) The offended party is a woman and/or her child or children;
> (2) The woman is either the wife or former wife of the offender, or is a woman with whom the offender has or had a sexual or dating relationship, or is a woman with whom such offender has a common child;
> (3) The offender causes on the woman and/or child mental or emotional anguish; and
> (4) The anguish is caused through acts of public ridicule or humiliation, repeated verbal and emotional abuse, denial of financial support or custody of minor children or access to the children or similar such acts or omissions."

**Element (1):** Established. Boony is a woman.

**Element (2):** Established. Boony is either the wife or former wife of Andy. As this Court has found above, she was Andy's former wife (by talaq) on the charged date. This satisfies Element (2) regardless of the talaq finding, because Section 5(i) covers "former wife."

**Element (3):** This Court credits Boony's testimony that she experienced mental and emotional anguish. Her testimony on this point was emotionally compelling and is corroborated by the testimony of her brothers regarding her observed emotional condition. The discovery (or confirmation) of Andy's relationship with Charlie caused her genuine pain.

**Element (4): This is the contested element.** The anguish must be caused through enumerated *means* -- "acts of public ridicule or humiliation, repeated verbal and emotional abuse, denial of financial support... or similar such acts or omissions."

The prosecution's theory is that Andy's sexual relationship with Charlie constitutes a "similar act" causing mental or emotional anguish. This brings the Court to the critical doctrinal question.

#### 2. Reconciling G.R. No. 252739 and G.R. No. 264870

Two Supreme Court decisions bear directly on whether a sexual relationship, standing alone, satisfies Element (4). The prosecution and defense invoke different decisions, and this Court must resolve the apparent tension between them.

##### (a) G.R. No. 252739 -- *XXX v. People* (En Banc, April 16, 2024, Hernando, J.)

The Supreme Court En Banc held:

> "In instances of marital infidelity, the requirement of specific criminal intent to cause mental and emotional suffering is already satisfied at the moment the perpetrator commits the act of marital infidelity. This finds basis in the fact that marital infidelity is inherently immoral and depraved under prevailing societal, cultural, and religious norms."

This creates an intent presumption: in cases of marital infidelity, the *mens rea* of Section 5(i) is conclusively presumed from the act of infidelity itself.

##### (b) G.R. No. 264870 -- *XXX264870 v. People* (Second Division, April 21, 2025, Leonen, SAJ)

One year later, the Supreme Court Second Division held:

> "The acts complained of, which are the means of committing the offense, should be evaluated on whether they were done with the intention of causing mental or emotional anguish, public ridicule or humiliation to the woman or her child. It is when these acts are done with the intent to tilt the scales of power within the relationship against the woman, causing her to lose her autonomy does the State have a legitimate interest in punishing such acts."
>
> "To be sure, the effect on the victim alone, which is the second element, is not enough to sustain a conviction."

The opening line of the decision states: **"Marital infidelity per se is not an act of violence criminalized under Republic Act No. 9262."**

The prosecution argues G.R. No. 252739, being En Banc, controls. The defense argues G.R. No. 264870, being more recent and more specifically on point, should apply. This Court holds that **both decisions are compatible**, and that their proper reconciliation leads to acquittal in this case.

##### (c) The Reconciliation

G.R. No. 252739 and G.R. No. 264870 address **different factual scenarios** and establish **different aspects** of the same legal framework. They are compatible, not conflicting.

**G.R. No. 252739** establishes that in cases of *marital infidelity* during a *subsisting marriage*, the prosecution need not independently prove specific criminal intent to cause anguish -- the intent is presumed from the inherently immoral character of the act. This addresses the *mens rea* element: it removes the prosecution's burden to prove intent separately from the act.

**G.R. No. 264870** establishes that the prosecution must still prove the *means* element (Element 4 of *Dinamling*) -- that the anguish was caused through acts done with the intent to "tilt the scales of power within the relationship against the woman." This addresses the *actus reus* element: even with the intent presumption, the prosecution must show that the specific acts complained of were directed at causing the woman to lose her autonomy.

The two holdings are complementary: G.R. No. 252739 addresses *intent* (presumed from infidelity); G.R. No. 264870 addresses *means* (the acts must show domination or coercion, not merely the existence of a relationship). A Division decision cannot overturn an En Banc decision, and G.R. No. 264870 does not purport to do so. It refines the framework by holding that even where intent is presumed, the prosecution must prove acts directed at the woman -- not merely the existence of a relationship that causes her pain.

This reconciliation is confirmed by Associate Justice Alfredo Benjamin S. Caguioa's Concurring Opinion in *XXX270257 v. People* (G.R. No. 270257, August 12, 2024, Third Division):

> "In this case, the evidence presented by the prosecution established beyond reasonable doubt that XXX270257 intended to cause mental or emotional anguish and public ridicule or humiliation upon AAA. He did so, **not by engaging in marital infidelity per se, but by flaunting the very same extramarital relationship in full view of AAA, his legitimate children, and the public.**"
>
> "I wish to emphasize as I did in my Dissenting Opinion in the En Banc case of XXX v. People of the Philippines (G.R. No. 252739, April 16, 2024 [Per J. Hernando, En Banc]) that **marital infidelity per se is not punished by Republic Act No. 9262.**"

In G.R. No. 270257, the accused was convicted because his acts went beyond mere infidelity: he moved his mistress into his mother's house adjacent to the complainant's residence, sired an illegitimate child, and publicized his new family on social media. These were deliberate acts of public humiliation directed at the complainant.

The doctrinal framework is therefore: (1) marital infidelity, if proven during a subsisting marriage, gives rise to a presumption of intent (G.R. No. 252739); but (2) the prosecution must still prove that the accused's specific acts constituted public ridicule, humiliation, or similar acts directed at the woman (G.R. No. 264870; Caguioa, J., concurrence in G.R. No. 270257); and (3) the effect on the victim alone is not enough to sustain a conviction (G.R. No. 264870).

#### 3. Application to Andy's Case

Applying this reconciled framework, the prosecution's case on Charge 2 fails on multiple independent grounds.

##### (a) The G.R. No. 252739 Intent Presumption Does Not Apply to the Charged Acts

G.R. No. 252739's presumption applies to "marital infidelity" -- infidelity during a subsisting marriage. As this Court has found, the marriage between Andy and Boony was dissolved by talaq on January 23, 2025. The Information charges acts "on or about February 12, 2025." On that date, there was no marriage in existence and therefore no marital fidelity obligation.

"Marital infidelity" is a legal concept predicated on the existence of a marital bond. Infidelity requires fidelity, and fidelity exists only within marriage. As PD 1083 Art. 34(1) provides, the obligation to "observe mutual respect and fidelity" is incident to the marriage -- it terminates with dissolution. After the talaq, Andy's relationship with any person was no longer a breach of marital fidelity.

The pre-talaq CCTV/video evidence of romantic behavior between Andy and Charlie is admissible as context -- it establishes the background of the relationship and explains why Boony experienced anguish. However, these pre-talaq acts are not the acts charged in the Information. The Information charges acts "on or about February 12, 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto." Under Article III, Section 14(2) of the Constitution, the accused has the right "to be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation against him." Under the variance doctrine, conviction must rest on the facts alleged in the Information. Pre-talaq acts cannot form the basis for conviction when the Information charges post-talaq conduct.

##### (b) The Means Element Is Not Satisfied on February 12

Even assuming *arguendo* that the intent presumption applied, the prosecution must still establish the *means* element under G.R. No. 264870: that Andy's acts constituted "public ridicule or humiliation" or "similar acts" directed at Boony with the intent to "tilt the scales of power within the relationship against the woman."

On February 12, 2025, the evidence shows:

- Andy was in his apartment -- an apartment from which Boony had voluntarily departed twenty days earlier.
- Boony came uninvited with Andy's officemates.
- Andy blocked the door -- seeking privacy, not engaging in public display.
- Andy did not post on social media. He did not flaunt any relationship publicly. He did not taunt, mock, or address Boony about Charlie.
- Boony created the confrontation and the public dimension.

**Contrast this with the conviction cases.** In G.R. No. 270257, the accused moved his mistress next door, sired an illegitimate child, and publicized his new family on social media. In G.R. No. 252739, the husband maintained a mistress and sired a child over four years, then confronted his wife with the fact when discovered. These cases involved deliberate, sustained, public acts directed at the complainant.

Here, Andy's conduct on February 12 was passive: he was in his apartment and blocked the door when Boony arrived uninvited. There is no evidence that Andy intended to publicly humiliate Boony or to "tilt the scales of power" against her. The "public humiliation" -- such as it was -- was generated by Boony's own decision to bring officemates to the apartment and to create a scene outside.

As G.R. No. 264870 holds: "the effect on the victim alone, which is the second element, is not enough to sustain a conviction." Boony's anguish is credible and real. But the law does not criminalize causing emotional pain; it criminalizes causing pain **through enumerated acts of psychological violence directed at the woman**. The pain here was generated by Boony's own confrontation, not by Andy's acts.

##### (c) The "Continuing Offense" Theory Does Not Cure the Deficiency

The prosecution may invoke Section 7 of R.A. 9262, which provides that VAWC offenses may be considered "continuing." The argument would be that the infidelity-caused anguish began pre-talaq and continued through February 12, making the anguish a continuing offense within the scope of the Information.

This Court rejects this theory for two reasons. **First**, the "continuing offense" provision relates to the *venue* of prosecution and the accrual of the offense for prescriptive purposes -- it does not expand the temporal scope of a specific Information beyond the facts alleged. The Information charges acts "on or about February 12, 2025." The accused's constitutional right to be informed (Art. III, Sec. 14(2)) requires that conviction rest on the acts so charged. **Second**, even treating the anguish as continuing, the *means* element must be established for the specific acts charged. The pre-talaq romantic relationship may have been the *cause* of Boony's continuing anguish, but on February 12, no *act* of Andy directed at Boony constituted public ridicule or humiliation.

##### (d) Boony's Facilitation of the Relationship's Proximity

The evidence establishes that Boony herself facilitated the conditions that led to Andy and Charlie's proximity:

- Boony knew of and agreed to Charlie's role as Andy's assistant.
- Boony and Charlie worked together at Andy's office providing assistance to Andy.
- On multiple occasions during periods of marital conflict, **Boony herself directed Andy to go sleep at Charlie's apartment.**
- Boony's own testimony confirms Charlie entered the apartment only "dalawang beses lang" (twice) -- undermining the prosecution's narrative of cohabitation.

The prosecution's narrative of a clandestine affair discovered by a victimized wife is contradicted by the complainant's own facilitation of the relationship's proximity. The law does not criminalize a relationship the complainant helped create. This context negates the claim that Andy used the relationship as a tool of domination against Boony.

#### 4. Assessment of Pre-Talaq CCTV/Video Evidence

The prosecution presented CCTV footage and private videos showing Andy and Charlie in romantic acts, including kissing, from the period before January 23, 2025. This evidence is admissible as context to establish the background of the relationship and to explain Boony's emotional state.

However, this evidence cannot form the basis for conviction for three reasons:

**First**, the Information charges acts "on or about February 12, 2025." Pre-talaq acts are outside the scope of the Information. Conviction based on uncharged acts would violate the accused's constitutional right to be informed of the nature and cause of the accusation against him (Art. III, Sec. 14(2)).

**Second**, the pre-talaq evidence establishes that the relationship existed during the marriage. But the question is not whether the relationship existed -- it is whether Andy committed enumerated acts of psychological violence under Section 5(i) on the charged date. The relationship's existence is a fact; it is not itself an act of public ridicule or humiliation.

**Third**, even treating the pre-talaq evidence as establishing "marital infidelity" under G.R. No. 252739, this creates only a presumption of intent -- it does not supply the missing means element under G.R. No. 264870. The prosecution must still show that Andy directed acts of public humiliation at Boony on February 12. The pre-talaq videos do not establish what Andy did on February 12.

#### 5. Assessment of Former Male Partners' Testimony

The prosecution offered testimony from males who claimed to have been previously intimate with Andy, to establish a pattern of bisexual relationships during the marriage.

This Court rules that this testimony is **inadmissible character evidence** offered to prove conduct on a specific occasion. Under the Revised Rules on Evidence, Rule 130, Section 54: "Evidence of a person's character or a trait of character is not admissible for the purpose of proving action in conformity therewith on a particular occasion."

Even if admitted under an exception (such as Rule 130, Section 35 on similar acts showing intent or plan), the testimony does not establish the statutory elements of Section 5(i) on February 12. The testimony proves Andy's sexual orientation and prior relationships -- neither of which is a crime, an element of any R.A. 9262 offense, or relevant to whether Andy committed psychological violence against Boony on the charged date. The testimony is emotionally prejudicial without being probative of the specific charge.

#### 6. Assessment of Boony's Brothers' Testimony

Boony's brothers offered testimony of various types. This Court assesses each type:

**(a) Corroboration of Boony's emotional anguish.** Admissible and credited. Boony's brothers observed her emotional condition after the events. This strengthens Element (3) -- mental or emotional anguish. However, under G.R. No. 264870, "the effect on the victim alone is not enough to sustain a conviction." Element (3) is not the contested element.

**(b) Testimony about the talaq.** Boony's brother who witnessed the talaq was called by the prosecution. His testimony is directly relevant and highly credible as an eyewitness to the January 23 pronouncement. To the extent his testimony confirms the talaq was pronounced and that Boony accepted, it corroborates the defense's primary factual claim.

**(c) General claims of "victimization."** Inadmissible absent specific facts. General assertions that the brothers were "victimized" by Andy, without specifying what acts constituted victimization, are vague opinion testimony. The brothers' personal feelings about Andy's conduct are not elements of the offense, which is against Boony, not them.

#### 7. Finding on Charge 2

The prosecution has established that Boony experienced mental and emotional anguish (Element 3). The prosecution has not established that Andy caused that anguish through enumerated acts of "public ridicule or humiliation, repeated verbal and emotional abuse, denial of financial support... or similar such acts or omissions" on the date charged in the Information (Element 4).

On February 12, 2025, the marriage had been dissolved by talaq. The G.R. No. 252739 intent presumption, which applies to "marital infidelity" during a subsisting marriage, does not apply to Andy's post-talaq conduct. Even if applied, the means element required by G.R. No. 264870 is unmet: Andy committed no act of public ridicule or humiliation directed at Boony on February 12. He was in his own apartment. He blocked the door. Boony brought the audience and created the public scene. Pre-talaq evidence is admissible as context but cannot form the basis for conviction on a post-talaq Information without violating the accused's constitutional right to be informed.

The prosecution has failed to prove this charge beyond reasonable doubt.

**ACCUSED ANDY IS ACQUITTED of the charge of indulging in a sexual relationship with Charlie as a mode of psychological violence.**

---

### D. CHARLIE'S LIABILITY AS CO-CONSPIRATOR

The prosecution charges Charlie as a co-conspirator under the theory that he participated in the psychological violence against Boony. Under *Go-Tan v. Spouses Tan* (G.R. No. 168852, September 30, 2008), conspiracy under Article 8 of the Revised Penal Code applies suppletorily to R.A. 9262 via Section 47.

Conspiracy requires proof that two or more persons came to an agreement to commit a felony and decided to commit it. The prosecution's evidence against Charlie consists solely of his presence in the apartment on February 12 and his role as Andy's assistant.

There is **no evidence** that Charlie:
- Communicated with Boony on February 12 or at any time;
- Engaged in any public act directed at Boony;
- Agreed with Andy to inflict psychological violence on Boony;
- Knew of or participated in any plan to cause Boony anguish.

Charlie was Andy's known assistant, hired with Boony's knowledge. He was present in the apartment -- an apartment that was Andy's exclusive residence after Boony's voluntary departure twenty days earlier. His presence alone does not establish conspiracy.

More fundamentally, conspiracy is derivative: it requires a principal offense. As this Court has found that Andy is not guilty of the underlying charge of psychological violence, there is no principal offense for which Charlie can be held liable as co-conspirator.

**ACCUSED CHARLIE IS ACQUITTED.**

### E. CREDIBILITY OF PRIVATE COMPLAINANT BOONY

This Court makes the following specific findings regarding Boony's credibility, which independently support the acquittals:

#### 1. The False Entry Claim (S15)

Boony testified under oath that she "slipped in" to the apartment on February 12 "because she is small" and saw Charlie and his things inside (*"Nakapasok po ako dahil maliit po ako na babae. Doon ko po nakita si Charlie at yong mga gamit niya."* S15). The defense has established that this statement is **false**: Boony did not enter the apartment. Andy blocked the door, and the confrontation occurred outside in the presence of his officemates.

This finding is material. Boony's claim to have entered the apartment and seen Charlie inside is the foundational factual predicate of the prosecution's narrative -- it is the act from which the "discovery" of the sexual relationship is alleged. If Boony did not enter and did not see what she claims to have seen, the prosecution's central narrative is built on a fabrication.

Under the Revised Rules on Evidence, a witness whose testimony on a material point has been shown to be false may be discredited in her entirety. This Court finds that Boony's provably false statement on a material point casts doubt on the reliability of her testimony on all other material assertions.

#### 2. The Contradicted Financial Claim (S18)

Boony's assertion that Andy "really did not give her a personal allowance" is contradicted by the documentary evidence of P2,000 remitted on February 8, P5,000 through her brother on February 23, unrestricted access to Andy's wallet and ATM cards during the marriage, and the housing unit offered at settlement. This is not a case where the prosecution's evidence is simply weak; the complainant's specific factual claim is affirmatively contradicted by documentary evidence.

#### 3. The Omission of the Talaq

Boony's entire Judicial Affidavit proceeds as if the marriage subsisted on February 12. She refers to Andy as "asawa ko" throughout. She never mentions the January 23 talaq, her acceptance of it, her voluntary departure, or the January 30 SMS in which she acknowledged the breakdown. This wholesale omission of a fundamental fact -- the dissolution of the marriage twenty days before the charged incident -- is incompatible with a candid account of events.

#### 4. S16 Contradicts the Cohabitation Narrative

Boony's own testimony establishes that Charlie came to the apartment only "dalawang beses lang" (twice). This directly undermines the prosecution's characterization of Charlie's presence "inside their dwelling" as cohabitation or as an ongoing sexual relationship conducted in the marital home.

#### 5. The BPO Contradiction

On February 13 -- one day after the alleged incident -- Boony obtained a Barangay Protection Order to enter the apartment to retrieve belongings. If Boony could freely enter the apartment on February 12 (as she claims in S15), there would be no need for a BPO on February 13. The fact that she sought formal legal authorization to enter the following day corroborates the defense's position that she did not enter on the 12th.

### F. CONTINGENCY: THE "NOT YET DIVORCED" STATEMENT

The defense's case file references a private message from Andy to his employer stating: "There is no 'Idda' yet as we are not yet divorced." This statement is **not in evidence before this Court**. It is a private communication that has not been presented by either party.

However, this Court addresses the statement in contingency, as it may surface on appeal or in subsequent proceedings.

**If the statement were admitted into evidence**, it would not alter the result for the following reasons:

**First**, the legal effect of talaq under PD 1083 is determined by the statute, not by the parties' subjective understanding. Article 46(1) provides that talaq "may be affected" by pronouncement. Article 56 defines *'idda* as the waiting period for a woman "whose marriage **has been** dissolved" -- past perfect tense confirming dissolution precedes *'idda*. Andy's misunderstanding of PD 1083's timing provisions -- confusing irrevocability (which occurs after *'idda* under Art. 46(2)) with effectivity (which occurs upon pronouncement under Art. 46(1)) -- does not undo the legal effect of the talaq.

**Second**, Andy's own behavior contradicts the statement: he did not exercise *ruju* (right to take back the wife during *'idda*); he did not resume cohabitation; he asked Boony to draft a Reconciliation Agreement (which presupposes a separation); and the families held *kawagib* discussions.

**Third**, the statement was made to Andy's employer in the context of minimizing a workplace disruption caused by Boony's complaints. It is a statement about a social situation, not a legal declaration regarding the technical status of the marriage under PD 1083.

### G. THE TALAQ AS A DEFENSE TO R.A. 9262: A QUESTION OF FIRST IMPRESSION

This Court acknowledges that no published Supreme Court decision has ruled on whether talaq under PD 1083 constitutes a defense to a charge under R.A. 9262. The Court's analysis on this question of first impression proceeds from the following principles:

**First**, PD 1083 Art. 3(1) establishes the supremacy of the Code over laws of general application. Where PD 1083 provides that a Muslim marriage is dissolved by talaq, a law of general application -- even one as important as R.A. 9262 -- cannot treat that marriage as subsisting.

**Second**, the concept of "marital infidelity" under R.A. 9262 presupposes a subsisting marriage. Section 3(C) defines psychological violence as including "mental infidelity." While R.A. 9262 uses the term "mental infidelity" rather than "marital infidelity," the concept of infidelity-as-psychological-violence presupposes a subsisting intimate relationship. After divorce, there is no marriage to which fidelity is owed and no infidelity within the meaning of the statute possible.

**Third**, R.A. 9262's protection extends to "former wives" (Sec. 3(a)). A former wife may still be a victim of psychological violence. But the specific form of violence at issue here -- "marital infidelity" causing anguish -- is unavailable as a theory after the marriage's dissolution. Other forms of Section 5(i) violence (public ridicule, verbal abuse, denial of support) remain available regardless of marital status.

**Fourth**, PD 1083 Art. 34(1) imposes the fidelity obligation as incident to the marriage. Art. 67 extends support obligations during *'idda*. Art. 96(1) extends inheritance rights during *'idda*. The Legislature knew how to extend specific marital incidents into the post-divorce period and chose to extend support and inheritance -- but not fidelity. This omission is intentional and dispositive.
