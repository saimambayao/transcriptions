## I. CASE BACKGROUND

### A. The Main Case: PP v. Andy (Crim. Case No. 2025-18700)

On May 21, 2025, Boony filed a criminal complaint against Andy for violation of Section 5(i) of R.A. No. 9262 (Anti-Violence Against Women and Their Children Act of 2004). The Information, filed June 26, 2025, charges Andy with psychological abuse through: (1) denying financial support, and (2) indulging in a sexual relationship with another male named Charlie inside the dwelling. The charged acts allegedly occurred "on or about February 12, 2025 and sometime subsequent thereto."

Andy was arrested on September 9, 2025 and posted bail on September 11, 2025. The case is pending before the RTC, 19th Judicial Region, Canton City. Arraignment has not yet occurred.

### B. How the Counter-Complaint Arises

The charges Andy now files against Boony arise from Boony's own independent criminal conduct during February through April 2025. These are not retaliatory charges — they are distinct offenses committed by Boony against Andy's person, property, livelihood, and reputation:

1. **February 13, 2025** — Boony ransacked Andy's apartment and damaged personal property under the pretext of a Barangay Protection Order (BPO).
2. **February 18, 2025** — Boony forcibly entered the apartment and took Andy's iPad mini and other devices without consent.
3. **February 12–20, 2025 and continuing** — Boony conducted a systematic campaign of workplace harassment that forced Andy to resign and destroyed his professional reputation.
4. **February 17, 2025** — Boony sent a defamatory email to Andy's boss containing false accusations, which was read by Andy's officemates.
5. **On or about April 16, 2025** — Boony published homophobic and defamatory posts on Facebook and Messenger messages about Andy.

These acts are independently criminal regardless of the pending RA 9262 case. Indeed, the pattern of harm documented here demonstrates that Boony — not Andy — was the aggressor in this relationship.

### C. Timing of Filing — Not Retaliatory

Andy is filing this counter-complaint in April 2026, approximately 13-14 months after the acts were committed. A retaliatory filing would have been filed within days or weeks of Andy being charged (September 2025). The passage of time demonstrates that Andy's filing is based on deliberate assessment of evidence and legal rights, not on vindictive impulse.

## II. CHARGE 1: MALICIOUS MISCHIEF (Art. 327, RPC)

### A. The Law

> **Article 327, Revised Penal Code — Who are liable for malicious mischief.** — Any person who shall deliberately cause to the property of another any damage not falling within the terms of the next preceding article shall suffer:
>
> 1st. The penalty of *prision correccional* in its minimum and medium periods, if the value of the damage caused exceeds 1,000 pesos;
> 2nd. *Arresto mayor*, if such value is over 200 pesos but does not exceed 1,000 pesos;
> 3rd. *Arresto menor*, if such value does not exceed 200 pesos.

### B. Elements of the Offense

Per Art. 327, RPC and *Grana v. People* (G.R. No. 202111, November 25, 2019, per J. Hernando):

> The elements of Malicious Mischief have been duly proven in this case, *viz*.:
>
> 1. Petitioners admitted in their "kontra salaysay" that Teofilo deliberately destroyed the fence and its cement foundation, and made diggings in the subject property;
>
> 2. The destruction did not constitute arson or other crime involving destruction; and
>
> 3. The act of damaging another's property was committed merely for the sake of damaging it.

The Court further found: "Evidently, petitioners' actions were made out of hatred, revenge or evil motive." The motive element distinguishes malicious mischief from damage to property through reckless imprudence, which is a quasi-offense conceptually incompatible with the intentional element of malicious mischief. Generalized, the elements are:

1. **The offender deliberately caused damage to the property of another.**
2. **Such act does not constitute arson or other crimes involving destruction** (i.e., does not fall within Art. 326, RPC).
3. **The act of damaging another's property was committed merely for the sake of damaging it** (i.e., out of hate, revenge, or other evil motive).

### C. Facts

**Date:** February 13, 2025

**Narrative:** On February 13, 2025, Boony obtained a Barangay Protection Order (BPO). The BPO authorized Boony to retrieve her personal belongings from Andy's apartment. Instead of limiting herself to retrieving her belongings, Boony ransacked the apartment and deliberately damaged Andy's personal property.

**Key details:**
- The BPO was specifically limited in purpose — retrieval of Boony's personal belongings. It did not authorize the destruction of Andy's property.
- Boony exceeded the scope of the BPO by ransacking the apartment.
- Barangay officials and police officers were present during the BPO enforcement. They witnessed Boony's destructive conduct and **told Boony she was violating the purpose of the BPO**.
- The damage was deliberate — not incidental to retrieving belongings.

**Motive:** Boony's destructive conduct was motivated by anger and revenge following the talaq pronouncement of January 23, 2025 and the confrontation at Andy's apartment on February 12, 2025. The malice element is satisfied by the fact that Boony did not merely retrieve her belongings — she deliberately damaged Andy's property in the process, against the instructions of the barangay officials and police present.

**Strength: MODERATE TO STRONG.**

*Favorable factors:*

## III. CHARGE 2: QUALIFIED THEFT (Arts. 308 & 310, RPC)

### A. The Law

> **Article 308, Revised Penal Code — Who are liable for theft.** — Theft is committed by any person who, with intent to gain but without violence against or intimidation of persons nor force upon things, shall take personal property of another without the latter's consent.

> **Article 310, Revised Penal Code — Qualified theft.** — The crime of theft shall be punished by the penalties next higher by two degrees than those respectively specified in the next preceding article, if committed by a domestic servant, or with grave abuse of confidence, or if the property stolen consists of motor vehicles, mail matter or large cattle, or consists of coconuts taken from the premises of the plantation, or fish taken from a fishpond or fishery, or if property is taken on the occasion of fire, earthquake, typhoon, volcanic eruption, or any other calamity, vehicular accident or civil disturbance.

### B. Elements of the Offense

**Simple Theft (Art. 308)** — per *Teologo and Delos Santos v. People* (G.R. No. 238383, per J. Kho, Second Division):

> The essential elements of theft are: (1) the taking of personal property; (2) the property belongs to another; (3) the taking away was done with intent of gain; (4) the taking away was done without the consent of the owner; and (5) the taking away is accomplished without violence or intimidation against person or force upon things.

**Qualifying circumstance (Art. 310) — Grave abuse of confidence:**
Per the same decision: "Theft becomes qualified when the taking under Article 308 is committed under any of the circumstances enumerated in Article 310 of the RPC, i.e., with grave abuse of confidence."
- The offender had a relationship of trust and confidence with the victim (domestic/marital relationship).
- The offender exploited this relationship to facilitate the taking.

### C. Facts

**Date:** February 18, 2025

**Narrative:** On February 18, 2025, Boony forcibly entered Andy's apartment and took Andy's iPad mini and other devices without Andy's consent. As of April 2026, the iPad mini remains in Boony's possession and has not been returned despite demand.

**Key details:**
- The iPad mini is Andy's personal property — it was purchased by Andy, used by Andy, and contained Andy's personal data and communications.
- Boony did not have Andy's consent to take the device.
- The taking was with intent to gain — Boony has retained possession of the iPad for over 14 months.
- Boony and Andy had a domestic/marital relationship (they were married on May 20, 2017, though by this date, the talaq had already been pronounced on January 23, 2025). The familiarity from this relationship is what gave Boony access to the apartment and knowledge of where the devices were located. This constitutes **grave abuse of confidence** under Art. 310.

**Additional concern — RA 4200 (Anti-Wiretapping Act):**

If Boony accessed the private messages stored on the iPad mini — particularly any communications between Andy and Charlie — this constitutes a separate violation of R.A. No. 4200 (Anti-Wiretapping Law), which prohibits any person from secretly intercepting or recording any private communication or spoken word of another without authorization.

> **Section 1, R.A. 4200.** — It shall be unlawful for any person, not being authorized by all the parties to any private communication or spoken word, to tap any wire or cable, or by using any other device or arrangement, to secretly overhear, intercept, or record such communication or spoken word...

Accessing stored electronic messages on a device taken without consent falls within the ambit of unauthorized interception of private communications. If evidence emerges that Boony read, copied, or used any private messages from the iPad, a separate charge under RA 4200 should be filed.

### E. Strength Assessment

**Strength: MODERATE.**

*Favorable factors:*
- The taking is undisputed — the iPad is in Boony's possession over 14 months later.
- The domestic relationship establishes the qualifying circumstance of grave abuse of confidence.
- The continued refusal to return the property negates any claim of temporary borrowing.

*Risk factors:*
- Boony may claim the iPad was community property or that she had a right to take it (though the marriage was under complete separation of property, and the talaq had been pronounced).
- If the property regime was separation of property, Andy must establish that the iPad was acquired from his exclusive funds.
- The "forcible entry" element needs corroboration beyond Andy's testimony alone.
- If Boony used force to enter, the charge may be robbery (Art. 293) rather than theft — but force upon things (not persons) still qualifies as theft if not accompanied by violence or intimidation of persons.

*Mitigation of risks:*
- Andy should gather purchase receipts or digital records (Apple ID purchase history, credit card statements) to establish exclusive ownership.

- Even if Boony argues shared use, "shared use" does not equal "shared ownership." A spouse who borrows the other's device with permission does not become a co-owner.

## IV. CHARGE 3: UNJUST VEXATION (Art. 287, RPC)

### A. The Law

> **Article 287, Revised Penal Code — Light coercions.** — ...Any other coercions or unjust vexations shall be punished by *arresto menor* or a fine ranging from 5 pesos to 200 pesos, or both.

Unjust vexation is a catch-all provision that penalizes any human conduct which, although not productive of some physical or material harm, would unjustifiably annoy or vex an innocent person. The paramount question to be considered is whether the offenders act caused annoyance, irritation, torment, distress or disturbance to the mind of the person to whom it is directed. (*Maderazo v. People*, G.R. No. 165065, September 26, 2006, per J. Callejo, Sr., citing *Boleros, Jr. v. People*, 518 Phil. 175 [2006])

### B. Elements of the Offense

Per *Maderazo v. People* (G.R. No. 165065, September 26, 2006):

> [T]he second paragraph of the Article is broad enough to include any human conduct which, although not productive of some physical or material harm, could unjustifiably annoy or vex an innocent person. Compulsion or restraint need not be alleged in the Information, for the crime of unjust vexation may exist without compulsion or restraint. However, in unjust vexation, being a felony by dolo, malice is an inherent element of the crime. Good faith is a good defense to a charge for unjust vexation because good faith negates malice. The paramount question to be considered is whether the offenders act caused annoyance, irritation, torment, distress or disturbance to the mind of the person to whom it is directed.

From the foregoing, the elements are:

1. **The offender performed an act or series of acts** — compulsion or restraint need not be alleged, for unjust vexation may exist without compulsion or restraint.
2. **The act caused annoyance, irritation, torment, distress, or disturbance to the mind of another** — this is the paramount question; the offended party need not be present when the act was committed.
3. **The act was done with malice (without good faith or legal justification)** — unjust vexation, being a felony by dolo, requires malice as an inherent element; good faith negates malice.

### C. Facts

**Dates:** February 12–20, 2025 and continuing thereafter

**Narrative:** Boony conducted a systematic pattern of workplace harassment targeting Andy that caused him severe professional and personal harm, culminating in the loss of his employment and the destruction of his ability to earn a livelihood.

**Chronology of vexatious acts:**

| Date | Act | Effect on Andy |
|------|-----|----------------|
| **February 12, 2025** | Boony went to Andy's office and involved his boss and coworkers in the marital dispute. She then brought them to Andy's apartment, creating a public spectacle. | Public embarrassment before colleagues and superiors; professional boundaries violated. |
| **February 17, 2025** | Boony sent a formal complaint email to Andy's boss via the office email system, containing accusations about Andy's personal life. The email was read by Andy's officemates. | Public humiliation in the workplace; officemates became aware of intimate marital allegations. |
| **February 18, 2025** | Andy received a call from the Administrative and Finance Officer instructing him to tender his resignation because of Boony's harassment of the office. Andy was instructed to resign. | **Loss of employment.** Andy lost his livelihood directly because of Boony's workplace harassment. |
| **February 20, 2025** | Boony sent a second email to the office pressing it to act on her complaints. | Continued workplace harassment even after Andy had already been pushed out. |
| **Post-February 2025** | Boony spread claims of "immorality" and "crime" to other offices and professional contacts. | Destroyed Andy's ability to do business and earn a livelihood. Andy has had no regular employment since. |

**The cumulative result:**
- Andy was forced to resign on **February 18, 2025** — the day after Boony's first email.
- The head of office (the MP/boss who supervised Andy) was not reappointed, and the office no longer exists.
- As of April 2026, Andy has no regular employment.
- Boony's campaign of spreading "immorality" and "crime" claims to other professional contacts has made it impossible for Andy to rebuild his career.

**Note on RA 9262 irony:** Boony charged Andy with causing *her* "public ridicule and humiliation" under Sec. 5(i). Yet the documented evidence shows it was **Boony** who caused **Andy** public ridicule and humiliation — at his workplace, before his officemates, and in the broader professional community. The direction of harm runs from Boony to Andy, not the reverse.

*Mitigation of risks:*

- The legal argument is that workplace harassment is not a legitimate means of seeking redress for marital grievances. Boony had access to barangay mediation, the police, and the prosecution — she used all of those AND ALSO harassed Andy's employer, which is unjustified.

## V. CHARGE 4: CYBER LIBEL — EMAIL (Sec. 4(c)(4), RA 10175)

### A. The Law

> **Article 353, Revised Penal Code — Definition of libel.** — A libel is a public and malicious imputation of a crime, or of a vice or defect, real or imaginary, or any act, omission, condition, status, or circumstance tending to cause the dishonor, discredit, or contempt of a natural or juridical person, or to blacken the memory of one who is dead.

> **Section 4(c)(4), R.A. No. 10175 (Cybercrime Prevention Act of 2012).** — The unlawful or prohibited acts of libel as defined in Article 355 of the Revised Penal Code, as amended, committed through a computer system or any other similar means which may be devised in the future.

Cyber libel carries a penalty one degree higher than ordinary libel under Sec. 6, RA 10175. Since ordinary libel under Art. 355, RPC is punishable by *prision correccional* in its minimum and medium periods, cyber libel is punishable by *prision correccional* in its maximum period to *prision mayor* in its minimum period. The constitutionality of Section 6 (one-degree-higher penalty for cybercrimes) was upheld in *Disini v. Secretary of Justice*, G.R. No. 203335, February 11, 2014, where the Court held:

> Section 6 merely makes commission of existing crimes through the internet a qualifying circumstance. As the Solicitor General points out, there exists a substantial distinction between crimes committed through the use of information and communications technology and similar crimes committed using other means. In using the technology in question, the offender often evades identification and is able to reach far more victims or cause greater harm. The distinction, therefore, creates a basis for higher penalties for cybercrimes.

**CRITICAL NOTE ON PRESCRIPTIVE PERIOD:** In *Causing v. People*, G.R. No. 258524, October 11, 2023, the Supreme Court ruled that **cyber libel prescribes in ONE (1) YEAR from discovery by the offended party**, not 15 years:

> [W]hat governs the prescription of Cyber Libel is paragraph 4, *not* paragraph 2, of Article 90 of the RPC. Hence, the crime of Cyber Libel prescribes in one year.
>
> *First,* paragraph 4, Article 90 of the RPC must be given its literal and plain meaning: the crime of Libel shall prescribe in one year. This provision must therefore determine the prescriptive period of Cyber Libel...
>
> [P]aragraph 2 thereof is a general provision on prescription of crimes punishable by afflictive penalties, while paragraph 4 specifically governs Libel or other similar offenses. Applying the foregoing rule on statutory construction, paragraph 4, Article 90 of the RPC indisputably prevails over paragraph 2 thereof in setting the prescriptive period of Cyber Libel.

The earlier *Tolentino v. People* (unsigned resolution holding 15 years) was effectively superseded. The 1-year period runs from the date of discovery per Article 91, RPC. This must be factored into the filing strategy — see Section IX.

### B. Elements of Cyber Libel

Per Art. 353, RPC in relation to Sec. 4(c)(4), RA 10175, as discussed in *Disini v. Secretary of Justice* (G.R. No. 203335, February 11, 2014) and *Causing v. People* (G.R. No. 258524, October 11, 2023):

1. **Imputation of a discreditable act or condition to another** — a crime, vice, defect (real or imaginary), or any act, omission, condition, status, or circumstance tending to cause dishonor, discredit, or contempt.
2. **Publication** — the imputation was communicated to at least one third person, made through a computer system or similar means. Sec. 4(c)(4) of RA 10175 defines cyber libel as "the unlawful or prohibited acts of libel as defined in Article 355 of the Revised Penal Code, as amended, committed through a computer system or any other similar means which may be devised in the future."
3. **Identity of the person defamed** — the person defamed must be identifiable.
4. **Malice** — malice is presumed from every defamatory imputation (Art. 354, RPC). The presumption is rebutted only if the statement falls within privileged communication under Art. 354. If not privileged, the burden shifts to the accused to prove good motives and justifiable ends.

### C. Facts

**Date:** February 17, 2025

**The defamatory publication:** Boony sent a formal complaint email to Andy's boss via the office email system. The email contained the following false and defamatory statements:

#### Statement-by-Statement Analysis:

**Statement 1: "Does not give me personal allowance."**

- **Truth:** Boony had unrestricted access to Andy's wallet and ATM cards throughout the marriage. She handled the household budget entirely. She withdrew money freely. Andy actively encouraged her to keep money for herself. After the talaq, Andy sent P2,000 on February 10 (receipt exists) and P5,000 on February 23 (through Boony's brother, who can corroborate).
- **Assessment:** FALSE. Boony's claim reframes her own choice not to keep separate savings as Andy's denial of support. The documentary and testimonial evidence directly rebuts this claim.

**Statement 2: "Prohibits me from working."**

- **Truth:** Andy purchased Civil Service exam reviewers for Boony and personally mentored her preparation. Boony applied for a position in 2021 but lacked the CS eligibility. The reviewers are physically present in the apartment and can be photographed.
- **Assessment:** FALSE. Andy actively supported Boony's employment prospects. The physical evidence (CS reviewers) and testimonial evidence (mentorship) rebut this claim.

**Statement 3: "Forcibly evicted me."**

- **Truth:** Boony left the apartment voluntarily after accepting the talaq on January 23, 2025. She moved to her brother's residence of her own accord. She subsequently obtained a BPO to retrieve her belongings (February 13, 2025) — which itself proves she no longer had free access, having voluntarily left.
- **Assessment:** FALSE. Boony's voluntary departure is confirmed by: (a) Boony's own January 30 SMS acknowledging the breakdown, (b) her family's participation in divorce settlement negotiations, (c) the fact that she needed a BPO to return.

**Statement 4: "Allowed his male partner to live there."**

- **Truth:** Charlie was Andy's known assistant, hired because Boony could not consistently manage household chores. Boony knew of and agreed to Charlie's role. Boony and Charlie often worked together at Andy's office. Charlie entered the apartment only once (because of Boony's discomfort). On multiple occasions, Boony herself directed Andy to "go sleep at Charlie's apartment" during quarrels.
- **Assessment:** MALICIOUS IMPUTATION OF IMMORALITY. By referring to Charlie as Andy's "male partner" in a communication to Andy's employer, Boony imputed an immoral or criminal act (a same-sex relationship during marriage) to Andy. The framing was designed to cause dishonor and discredit at Andy's workplace.

**Statement 5: "Improper Idda process" / misrepresentation of Islamic law.**

- **Truth:** The talaq was pronounced in the presence of Boony's brother as witness. Boony accepted the talaq and left. The *'idda* period ran from January 23 to approximately April 23, 2025. Andy offered Boony the opportunity to write her own Reconciliation Agreement on terms she was comfortable with — Boony refused.
- **Assessment:** MISREPRESENTATION. Boony's characterization of the talaq and *'idda* process as improper is a distortion of Islamic law designed to cast Andy as an oppressor.

### D. Publication

The email was sent to **Andy's boss via the office email system**. It was **read by Andy's officemates**. This satisfies the publication element — the defamatory imputation was communicated to third persons.

The use of email (electronic communication via computer system) satisfies the "committed through a computer system" requirement of Sec. 4(c)(4), RA 10175, making this cyber libel rather than ordinary libel.

### E. NOT Privileged Communication

Boony may raise the defense that her email was a privileged communication under Art. 354, RPC, which exempts from the presumption of malice:

> **Article 354, RPC.** — ...
> 1. A private communication made by any person to another in the performance of any legal, moral, or social duty; and
> 2. A fair and true report, made in good faith, without any comments or remarks, of any judicial, legislative, or other official proceedings which are not of confidential nature...

**This defense fails.** The email was sent to Andy's **employer** — not to a court, prosecutor, police officer, or barangay official. An employer has no legal, moral, or social duty to adjudicate marital disputes between employees and their spouses. The email was not a report of judicial proceedings. It was a unilateral complaint to a private party designed to damage Andy's professional reputation and force his employer to punish him.

Boony had proper legal channels available — and she used them (she filed a police complaint on May 21, 2025, obtained a BPO, and eventually filed the criminal case). Her simultaneous campaign to Andy's employer was not a good-faith exercise of a legal right but an extrajudicial punishment.

### F. Direct Consequence

On **February 18, 2025 — the day after the email was sent** — Andy received a call from the Administrative and Finance Officer instructing him to tender his resignation. The causal link is immediate and direct: Boony's defamatory email caused Andy to lose his job.

### G. Strength Assessment

**Strength: STRONG.**

*Favorable factors:*
- The email is documentary evidence — its contents are fixed and provable.
- The false statements are specifically rebutted by documentary evidence (P2,000 receipt, CS exam reviewers, Jan 30 SMS, BPO itself).
- Publication is clear: the email was sent to a third party (the boss) and read by others (officemates).
- The causal link to Andy's forced resignation is immediate (email Feb 17 → resignation instruction Feb 18).
- Malice is presumed because the communication is NOT privileged (Art. 354 does not apply to complaints sent to employers about marital disputes).
- The "male partner" language demonstrates deliberate intent to impute immorality.

*Risk factors:*
- Boony may argue good faith — that she genuinely believed Andy denied her support and evicted her.
- The court may give weight to Boony's subjective perception even if objectively wrong.
- Under settled libel jurisprudence, "publication" requires communication to at least one third person — there is no requirement of broader dissemination. An email sent to a single recipient other than the person defamed satisfies the publication element. This is consistent with Art. 353, RPC, which defines libel as a "public and malicious imputation" — "public" in this context means communicated to a person other than the one defamed, not that it must reach the general public. Defense counsel may argue de minimis publication, but this argument has no basis in the statute or case law.

*Mitigation of risks:*
- Good faith is negated by the falsity of the specific claims and Boony's awareness of the true facts (she had ATM access, she left voluntarily, she knew Charlie was an assistant she worked alongside).
- Even if Boony subjectively believed her claims, she made no effort to verify them before publishing to Andy's employer — this constitutes reckless disregard for truth.

## VI. CHARGE 5: CYBER LIBEL — SOCIAL MEDIA (Sec. 4(c)(4), RA 10175)

### A. The Law

Same as Charge 4: Art. 353, RPC (definition of libel) + Sec. 4(c)(4), RA 10175 (cyber libel through computer systems).

### B. Elements

*Favorable factors:*

- The posts are consistent with the pattern established in Charges 3 and 4 (systematic campaign to damage Andy's reputation).

*Risk factors — evidentiary:*
- **Evidence acquisition method.** Andy discovered the posts through Boony's Facebook account that was still logged in on a smartphone. Boony may challenge the admissibility of screenshots taken from her own account without her authorization. However, Andy was using a device in his possession where Boony's session was still active — he did not hack into her account or use unauthorized credentials.
- If Boony deleted the posts before a preservation order can be obtained, the screenshots become the only evidence. Their authenticity may be challenged.
- The exact content of the posts and messages must be specified in the complaint-affidavit — Andy should transcribe the relevant portions from the screenshots.
- The admissibility of screenshots from a social media account accessed on a shared or inherited device session is a developing area of Philippine cyber evidence law. The screenshots may be admitted as electronic evidence under the Rules on Electronic Evidence (A.M. No. 01-7-01-SC), provided authenticity is established through testimony of the person who captured them, metadata corroboration, and showing the chain of custody. Andy's discovery was incidental (Boony's session remained active on his device), which is distinguishable from unauthorized access or hacking. [NOTE: No binding Supreme Court decision directly addresses this specific scenario as of April 2026. The argument for admissibility rests on the Rules on Electronic Evidence and the principle that evidence obtained without constitutional violation is admissible.]

*Risk factors — Boony's anticipated substantive defenses:*

| Boony's Defense | Counter-Argument |
|---|---|
| **Truth (Art. 361, RPC):** Boony argues her posts are true — Andy IS in a same-sex relationship | Art. 361 requires truth published with **good motives and justifiable ends**. Homophobic attacks on social media are not published for justifiable ends — they are published to shame and damage. Even if the underlying facts are true, the publication must serve a legitimate public interest, not private revenge. |
| **Opinion, not factual imputation:** Boony claims the posts are her personal opinion about Andy's character | Libel requires imputation of a "discreditable act, condition, status, or circumstance" (Art. 353). Homophobic language that attributes sexual orientation as a defect IS a factual imputation — it asserts a specific condition about a specific person, not a general opinion. |
| **Qualified privileged communication (Art. 354):** Boony argues she was exercising her right of self-expression or commenting on a matter of public concern | Andy's sexual orientation and private relationships are NOT matters of public concern under Art. 354(2). Boony's posts are directed at a private individual about his private life, not commentary on a public figure or public policy. |
| **No malice — posted in emotional distress:** Boony claims she posted while in anguish, negating malice | Malice is **presumed** from defamatory publications under Art. 354. The burden shifts to Boony to prove good motives and justifiable ends. Emotional distress may explain the posts but does not establish justifiable ends or negate the presumption of malice. |

*Mitigation of evidentiary risks:*
- File immediately for a **preservation order under RA 10175 Sec. 14** to prevent Boony from deleting the Facebook data.
- The screenshots, combined with metadata (timestamps, account identification), are prima facie evidence of the posts' existence.
- Andy's discovery was incidental (Boony's account was logged in on his device), not the result of unauthorized access — distinguishable from hacking.

### Unjust Vexation Prescriptive Period — Detailed Analysis

The 2-month prescriptive period for *arresto menor* offenses (Art. 90, RPC) is the shortest in the Revised Penal Code. However, this charge is timely for three independent reasons:

**1. Continuing offense.** Boony's vexatious conduct is not a single discrete act but a pattern that continued well beyond February 2025. As of April 2026, Andy cannot do business with other offices because Boony has spread "immorality" and "crime" claims — this is ongoing harm. The prescriptive period runs from the **last act** in the continuing pattern, not from the first. The November 3, 2025 withdrawal from settlement (combined with continued spreading of claims) is the most recent identifiable act in the pattern. Two months from November 3, 2025 = January 3, 2026. If Andy can document any vexatious act by Boony after January 2026, the filing in April 2026 is timely.

**2. The criminal complaint (May 21, 2025) is itself a vexatious act.** If the RA 9262 case is ultimately determined to be unfounded (as the defense analysis projects), the filing of the complaint — which caused Andy's arrest, detention, and bail — is an extension of the harassment pattern. The prescriptive period would run from the last act connected to the prosecution (most recent hearing date or court action).

**3. Alternative: subsume within Cyber Libel charges.** The workplace harassment (emails, spreading of claims) that constitutes unjust vexation substantially overlaps with the cyber libel charges (Charges 4-5). **NOTE: However, the cyber libel charges themselves have a 1-year prescriptive period from discovery (see *Causing v. People*, G.R. No. 258524), not the 15 years previously assumed.** If the unjust vexation charge faces a prescriptive challenge AND the cyber libel charges are timely filed, the same conduct may be captured by the cyber libel counts. The unjust vexation charge adds the non-cyber dimensions (office visits, in-person spreading) but the core harm is covered regardless — provided the cyber libel charges themselves are filed within the 1-year prescriptive window.

**Recommendation:** Include the unjust vexation charge in the filing but be prepared to argue the continuing offense doctrine. Andy should document the most recent instance of Boony spreading claims to third parties — date, to whom, what was said — to anchor the prescriptive period to a date within 2 months of filing.

### Cyber Libel Prescriptive Period — URGENT Analysis

**WARNING: A common misconception holds that the prescriptive period for cyber libel is 15 years (based on the penalty of *prision mayor* under the general rule of Art. 90, par. 2). This is WRONG.** The Supreme Court in *Causing v. People* (G.R. No. 258524, October 11, 2023) definitively ruled that cyber libel prescribes in **one (1) year from discovery** by the offended party, the authorities, or their agents (Art. 90, par. 4, and Art. 91, RPC).

The earlier *Tolentino v. People* (unsigned Supreme Court resolution) had applied paragraph 2 of Art. 90 (the general rule based on penalty) and held 15 years. But *Causing* explicitly held that paragraph 4 of Art. 90 is a **specific provision** that prevails over paragraph 2, and that the Legislature never amended the 1-year prescriptive period for libel when it enacted RA 10175.

**Impact on Charges 4 and 5:**

| Charge | Publication/Discovery Date | 1-Year Deadline | Filing in April 2026 |
|--------|---------------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Charge 4 (Email)** | Feb 17, 2025 (Andy was aware of the email on or shortly after this date) | On or about Feb 17, 2026 | **LIKELY PRESCRIBED** unless Andy can argue a later discovery date |
| **Charge 5 (Facebook)** | On/about Apr 16, 2025 (Andy's discovery date) | On or about Apr 16, 2026 | **EXTREMELY TIGHT** — filing must occur before this date |

**Strategies to preserve the cyber libel charges:**

1. **Argue discovery, not publication.** Under Art. 91, RPC, the prescriptive period runs from *discovery*, not publication. For Charge 4 (email), if Andy can establish that he did not become aware of the *full defamatory content* of the email until a later date (e.g., when officemates disclosed it to him), the discovery date may be later than Feb 17, 2025. For Charge 5 (Facebook), the discovery date is already the date Andy found the posts (April 16, 2025), which gives until approximately April 16, 2026.

2. **File the complaint-affidavit IMMEDIATELY.** Under Art. 91, the prescriptive period is interrupted by the "filing of the complaint or information." Filing with the Prosecutor's Office constitutes the "complaint" that interrupts prescription. For Charge 5, Andy must file before April 16, 2026 — any delay risks prescription.

3. **Charge 4 may be prescribed.** If Andy's discovery of the email was on or about February 17, 2025, then the 1-year period expired on or about February 17, 2026. Filing in April 2026 would be too late. Andy must establish a discovery date *after* the filing date minus one year. If this cannot be established, Charge 4 should still be filed — prescription is an affirmative defense that Boony must raise, and the issue of the exact discovery date is a factual question for the court.

4. **Alternative: file ordinary libel for Charge 4.** If the email was sent from a non-computer device or if the "computer system" element is arguable, consider filing ordinary libel under Art. 355, RPC. Ordinary libel also prescribes in 1 year from discovery, so this does not help with the prescriptive period. However, it removes the Sec. 6 penalty enhancement issue.

**Summary:** Charges 1 and 2 are clearly within their prescriptive periods. Charge 3 (Unjust Vexation) is within period under the continuing offense analysis. **Charge 4 (Cyber Libel — Email) is at serious risk of having prescribed.** **Charge 5 (Cyber Libel — Social Media) is within period but EXTREMELY TIGHT — filing must occur IMMEDIATELY (before approximately April 16, 2026).**
