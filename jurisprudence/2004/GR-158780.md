>
> R E S O L U T I O N
>
> **QUISUMBING, J:**
>
> On March 24, 2003, public respondent Special Division of the SandiganôÙbayan denied petitionerôs **Motion for Three Days Hearing Per Week** whereby the Office of the Special Prosecutor sought to hasten the trial beôÙfore said court of Criminal Cases Nos. 26558, 26565, and 26905, entiôÙtled "People of the PhilipôÙpines v. Joseph Ejercito Estrada, et al." Public respondent also deôÙnied petitionerôs motion for reconsiôÙderation. Hence, petitioner through the Office of the SpeôÙcial Prosecutor now comes to this Court in this petiôÙtion for certiorari and manôÙdamus to assail the denial of its petition and to compel pubôÙlic respondent to conôÙduct three hearings of the cited cases each week.
>
> Originally, public respondent conôÙducted trial hearings twice a week, from 9:00 a.m. to 12:00 noon.1 Later, on February 26, 2002, pubôÙlic respondent issued a **Resolution** modifying this six-hours-per-week schedule. PubôÙlic respondent ordered that starting March 18, 2002, the cases would be heard thrice a week, every MonôÙdays, WednesôÙdays and Fridays, from 9:00 a.m. to 12:00 noon.2
>
> Although this schedule could have expedited the proceedings, it was never implemented. Shortly after the order was issued, private reôÙspondents forôÙmer President Joseph Estrada and Jose "Jinggoy" Estrada dismissed their counsel de parte. Counsel de oficio had to be apôÙpointed and trial did not resume until April 17, 2002.3
>
> Determined to expedite the prosecution of the cases, the Office of the Special Prosecutor started insisting on additional hearing days. The newly-appointed counsel de oficio, however, needed time to study the cases so the Office of the Special Prosecutor opted instead to agree to private respondentsô proposition that hearings be exôÙtended to five hours a day. The parties agreed that starting May 8, 2002, hearings shall be from 8:00 a.m. to 1:00 p.m. twice a week or for a total of ten hours per week. CrimiôÙnal Case No. 26558, for Plunder, and CrimiôÙnal Case No. 26565, for Illegal Use of Alias, would be heard every MonôÙday while CrimiôÙnal Case No. 26905, for Perjury, was to be heard every WednesôÙday. On April 22, 2002, pubôÙlic respondent issued an **Order** adopting the agreement as new trial schedule. Thereafter, this schedule was consistently followed starting May 8, 2002.
>
> When the longer hearings still did not result in expedited proceedôÙings, the Office of the Special Prosecutor filed on March 21, 2003, the aboveôÙmenôÙtioned Motion for Three Days HearôÙing Per Week.4 The Office of the Special Prosecutor asked pubôÙlic respondent to implement the schedule proôÙvided in the February 26, 2002, Resolution.
>
> On March 24, 2003, public respondent denied the motion.5
>
> The Office of the Special Prosecutor moved for reconsideration of the denial,6 citing this Courtôs ruling in A.M. No. 01-12-01-SC and A.M. No. SB-02-10-J that "[t]he setting of the hearing of the plunder case three times a week is in orôÙder, not only because the case is of naôÙtional concern, but more importantly, because the accused are presently deôÙtained."7
>
> On May 13, 2003, this **motion for reconôÙsideration** was likewise denied.8 Hence, this petition.
>
> The Office of the Special Prosecutor relies on the following grounds:
>
> Public respondent Sandiganbayan clearly acted with grave abuse of disôÙcreôÙtion amounting to lack or excess of jurisdiction in issuing the quesôÙtioned Order dated March 24, 2003 and Resolution dated May [13], 2003 in CrimiôÙnal Cases Nos. 26558, 26565, and 26905, considôÙerôÙing that:
>
> A. The Honorable Court, in its Decision dated January 16, 2003 in A.M. No. 01-12-01-SC and A.M. No. SB-02-10-J has alôÙready manôÙdated that holding of three-day-per-week hearôÙings in the Plunder case.
>
> B. The Honorable Court, in its earlier Resolution dated January 21, 2002 in A.M. No. 02-1-07-SC entitled "Re: Request of Accused Through Counsel for Creation of a Special Division to Try the PlunôÙder Case (SB Crim. Case No. 26558 and related cases)" has previôÙously mandated the speedy trial of the Plunder and related cases and has further mandated that said cases be heard, tried, and deôÙcided with dispatch.
>
> C. Public respondent Sandiganbayan, in issuing the questioned Order dated March 24, 2003 and Resolution dated May [13], 2003, has not complied with the clear mandates issued by the Honorable Court to hold three-day-per-week hearôÙings in the Plunder and reôÙlated cases and to hear, try and deôÙcide with disôÙpatch said cases.
>
> D. The law mandates continuous trial especially in detention cases.
>
> E. National interest requires the speedy resolution of the PlunôÙder case.
>
> F. Public respondent Sandiganbayanôs questioned Order of March 24, 2003 and Resolution dated May [13], 2002 would add to the unôÙdue delay caused by private respondent EsôÙtradas in Plunder and reôÙlated cases.
>
> Essentially, for our resolution is the question, whether public respondent Sandiganbayan, Special Division, committed grave abuse of discretion in ordering two trial days per week instead of three.
>
> The Office of the Special Prosecutor argues that this Court has manôÙdated in A.M. No. 01-12-01-SC and A.M. No. SB-02-10-J that CrimiôÙnal Case No. 26558, for Plunder, be heard three times a week. The Office of the Special Prosecutor likewise stresses that in A.M. No. 02-1-07-SC, this Court has directed public respondent "to hear, try and deôÙcide with disôÙpatch" the Plunder and all reôÙlated cases against former President Estrada and his co-accused. Considering that the consolidation of CrimiôÙnal Case No. 26905, for Perjury, and Criminal Case No. 26565, for IlleôÙgal Use of Alias, encroached into the hearing days for the Plunder case, public responôÙdent should not have refused to order more hearôÙings days per week. That pubôÙlic respondent refused to order more hearings per week was grave abuse of discretion, accordôÙing to the Office of the Special ProsecuôÙtor.9
>
> The Office of the Special Prosecutor likewise laments public respondentôs failure to conôÙsider that counsels for private respondents had been employing every bit of dilatory techôÙnique they could imagôÙine.10
>
> The petition is devoid of merit.
>
> "Grave abuse of discretion," required as the sole ground for petiôÙtions for certiorari under Rule 65 of the Rules of Court, has a deôÙfined meaning. It is the arbitrary or despotic exercise of power due to passion, prejudice or perôÙsonal hostility or the whimsical, arbitrary, or capricious exercise of power that amounts to an evasion or refusal to perôÙform a positive duty enôÙjoined by law or to act at all in contemplaôÙtion of law.11
>
> For an act to be struck down as having been done with grave abuse of disôÙcretion, the abuse of discretion must be patent and gross.12 Grave abuse of discretion cannot be made gratuitously, and the Office of the Special Prosecutor does a disservice to the fair and prompt administration of justice when that Office fails to substantiate its charge.
>
> The excerpts of this Courtôs decision in A.M. No. 01-12-01-SC and A.M. No. SB-02-10-J -- on which the Office of the Special ProsecuôÙtor relies -- cannot support its conôÙtentions. The issue discussed in the cited portion of A.M. No. SB-02-10-J was limited to whether Justices AnaôÙcleto D. Badoy and Teresita LeoôÙnardo-De Castro were administraôÙtively liable for misconôÙduct for setôÙting the hearing of the plunôÙder case three times a week, at one oôclock in the afternoon, withôÙout prior consultation with the defense counôÙsel.
>
> On that limited issue, this Court ruled as follows:
>
> The setting of the hearing of the plunder case three times a week is in orôÙder, not only because the case is of national concern, but more imporôÙtantly, because the accused are presently detained. Contrary to comôÙplainôÙantsô assertions, the continuous trial is in accorôÙdance with the manôÙdate of the law. This Court, in AdministraôÙtive Circular No. 3-90 dated January 31, 1990, ordered all trial courts to adopt the mandatory conôÙtinuôÙous trial sysôÙtem in accorôÙdance with AdministraôÙtive Circular No. 4 dated September 22, 1988 and Circular No. 1-89 dated January 19, 1989. It was adopted precisely to minimize delay in the processing of cases. This deôÙlay was attributed to the common practice of piecemeal trial wherein cases are set for trial one day at a time and thereafter the hearôÙing is postôÙponed to another date or dates until all the parôÙties have finished their presentaôÙtion of evidence. SecôÙtion 2 of Rule 119 of the ReôÙvised Rules on Criminal Procedure provides:
>
> SEC. 2 Continuous trial until terminated; postponeôÙments.ôTrial once commenced shall continue from day to day as far as practiôÙcable until terminated. It may be postôÙponed for a reasonôÙable period of time for good cause.
>
> The court shall, after consultations with the prosecutor and deôÙfense counsel, set the case for continuous trial on weekly or other short-term trial calendar at the earliest possiôÙble time so as to ensure speedy trial. In no case shall the entire period exceed one hundred eighty (180) days from the first day of trial, except as otherwise authorôÙized by the Supreme Court.
>
> Corollarily, the "consultations" referred to in the foregoing proôÙviôÙsionsdoes not necessarily mean that the court has to secure first from the prosecuôÙtion and defense their approval before it can set the date of hearôÙing. To rule otherwise is to subject our trial sysôÙtem to the control of the parties and their counsel.
>
> Complainants also assail respondentsô act of setting the hearing at one oôclock in the afternoon. Again, there is nothing irregular in it. The scheôÙdule of hearing is regarded as a matter necessarily at the discretion of the trial judge. As a matter of fact, a court may even hold night sesôÙsions, and a court of review will not interfere unless it clearly appears that there has been an abuse of the power of the judge and that injustice has been done. This is because the good of the service demands more toil and less idleôÙness, and the limiôÙtations imposed by law are aimed to cut indolence and not the other way around.13
>
> In so declaring the thrice-a-week schedule proper, this Court did not lay down an inexorable trial schedule for public respondent to folôÙlow under all circumstances. In fact, contrary to the contentions of the Office of the SpeôÙcial ProsecuôÙtor, nowhere in said decision was it imôÙplied that the Court had fixed for public respondent the number of hearôÙing days to three per week. The question of how many trial days per week should be set was not beôÙfore the Court, and the Court clearly did not make any pronouncement on that question.
>
> Even if taken in the light of this Courtôs directive to public responôÙdent "to hear, try and decide with dispatch" the cases against priôÙvate responôÙdents, the decision cannot be given the strained interpretaôÙtion the Office of the Special Prosecutor gives it. The direcôÙtive did not remove from public respondent the discretion to schedule trials in such number and at such times as may be proper. NotaôÙbly, in the same **Resolution**14 containing the directive, this Court granted pubôÙlic respondent the power to promulôÙgate its own rules to emôÙphasize public respondentôs inherôÙent power to control the trial of the cases beôÙfore it.
>
> Likewise it does not appear that in setting the cases to be heard twice a week, public respondent violated Section 2, Rule 119 of the Rules of Court,15 which sets the limits to the discretion granted to trial courts on the matter of trial dates. The requirement of conôÙtinuous trial is satisfied if trial continôÙues from day to day, is held on a weekly or other short-term trial calenôÙdar, and, except as otherwise authorized by this Court, is completed within 180 days from the first day of trial.
>
> The Court notes that the trial schedule under the **Order** of April 22, 2002, allots a total of ten hours per week, while the Resolution of February 26, 2002, which the Office of the Special Prosecutor insists on, only allots a total of nine hours. The assailed trial schedule was adopted with the express consent of the Office of the SpeôÙcial Prosecutor. The Court cannot see how the assailed Order may cause material injury to petitionerôs cause throughout subsequent proôÙceedings. Nor can the Court agree that the assailed Order can constitute an evasion on public respondentôs part, or refusal to perôÙform its positive duty enôÙjoined by law. Denial of the MoôÙtion for Three Days Hearing Per Week, of itself, is not proof that public respondent exercised its power arôÙbitrarily or despotically by reason of passion, prejudice or perôÙsonal hosôÙtility.
>
> The determination of how many hearing days shall be devoted to trial rests within the sound disôÙcretion of the trial court. There is no justifiable reason to interfere with a trial courtôs schedulôÙing of trial dates unless it clearly appears that the judge abused its power and that injusôÙtice has been done.16 Having failed to show that public respondent has been guilty of grave abuse of discreôÙtion or that injustice would result from the adopôÙtion of a schedule of ten hours per week, the writ of certiorari sought by the Office of the Special Prosecutor canôÙnot be granted.
>
> The writ of mandamus likewise cannot issue. The Office of the Special Prosecutor has not sufficiently shown that public respondent has the imôÙperative duty to conôÙduct three hearôÙings per week. Mandamus is employed to comôÙpel the performance, when refused, of a ministerial duty.17 It does not lie to control or review the exôÙercise of discretion.18 It is unavailable to direct the exôÙercise of judgment or disôÙcreôÙtion in a parôÙticular way or the retraction or reverôÙsal of an action already taken in the exercise of either. Of course, this rule admits of exôÙceptions as when there is grave abuse of discretion,19 maniôÙfest injustice20 or palpaôÙble exôÙcess of authority.21 But, as discussed above, none has been shown thus far in this case. Worth stressing, the Office of the Special ProsecuôÙtor has not shown how the assailed Order of public respondent can constiôÙtute an evasion or refusal to perôÙform a positive duty enjoined by law.
>
> **WHEREFORE,** the instant petition for certiorari and mandaôÙmus is **DISMISSED** for lack of merit. The assailed **Order** dated March 24, 2003, and the **Resolution** dated May 13, 2003, of the public respondent Sandiganbayan, Special Division, are **AFFIRMED**.
